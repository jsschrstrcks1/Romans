# Cultural Marxism and Scripture
## Romans 10:12–13 *(Baptist Faith and Message Series)*

---

Today is sort of a part two of last week. The next article in the Baptist Faith and Message talks about communion and baptism, and since next week is communion Sunday, it seemed appropriate to talk about that article then. So we spent most of last week talking about history and Scripture. This week will mostly be philosophy and Scripture.

We're going to be reading a number of verses. We'll start with a verse in Romans: 10:12–13.

While you're turning there, let's talk about our people group of the week.

---

**Dhund Abbasi — Northern Pakistan**

The Dhund Abbasi people are said to have come to ancient Pakistan from Egypt around 850 AD, as traders and merchants in commodities. It is likely that they are descended from the Abbasid dynasty. This dynasty governed for 500 years from Baghdad, Iraq. The rule of the Abbasi extended eastwards across Afghanistan into the South Asian subcontinent, covering the eastern part of modern-day Pakistan. A conflicting account is that the Dhund Abbasi are descendants of Abbas, an uncle of their prophet Mohammad. Either way, there are nearly a million of these people, and to the best of our knowledge not a single one is a believer in Jesus Christ. Muslims who can trace their lineage back to Mohammad tend to be especially proud of this and more devoted as a result. This makes ministering in their midst especially challenging. Who will pray for the Dhund of northern Pakistan?

---

Let's read Romans 10:12–13:

> For there is no distinction between Jew and Greek; for the same Lord is Lord of all, bestowing his riches on all who call on him. For "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved."
> — Romans 10:12–13 (ESV)

---

There is so much more to talk about when it comes to the church and the current issues facing our denomination. One of the leaders of our denomination — Albert Mohler, the president of The Southern Baptist Theological Seminary in Kentucky — has been calling out what we are seeing as the invasion of cultural Marxism. That sounds like a complicated term, but it's really not. Anyone know what Marxism is? It's a political system where everything you own is taken away by a group of people at the top, to be redistributed by them to whomever they want for whatever reasons they deem important.

Can anyone define culture? Culture is a reflection of a people's achievements, aspirations, art, and intellect.

Cultural Marxism, then, seeks to suppress any cultural thing with which it disagrees, and support any cultural thing with which it agrees. Think in terms of what's been happening with southern culture recently — people have been tearing down statues, burning flags, encouraging and discouraging elements of our collective heritage. Have you picked up on this at all? I'm sure we're all aware of this happening to some extent even here in our own area — there was a statue pulled down in Brooksville because it didn't agree with a certain cultural narrative. It isn't just the radicals on the left who are guilty of this. If you're familiar with the alt-right, they use these same tactics to oppress cultural things produced by ethnic groups they dislike. Suppressing anyone or anything simply because it was thought of, painted by, or written by a person of a different group than you is racism. Full stop.

The way this is manifesting in our denomination right now — and especially through some of the seminaries — is that... well, let's do it this way.

Let's say Dottie is from Albania, and let's say Greg is a white Protestant American male.

Let's say Dottie writes a book on philosophy. It's good overall. It's not a subject she's passionate about, and it shows through in her work. However, she is an excellent writer, and that makes up a lot of the difference — making it a worthwhile book to read. Then let's say Greg writes a book on the same topic. Except let's say that for Greg this subject is his passion. He's studied it his whole life. He wakes up thinking about it; it's the last thing he thinks about before he goes to bed. He eats this subject, he drinks this subject. He's read every book on the subject that has been published since the invention of the printing press. Not only that, but he has the same writing ability as Dottie — and he has a depth and consideration on this topic that few in the world could match. So he produces a book that is groundbreaking, completely shaking up the world of philosophy, redefining the subject for everyone. He basically writes THE book that everyone is going to be studying for the next century whenever this topic comes up.

If you decided to teach a class on the subject, which book would you choose? Greg's or Dottie's? Based on those descriptions, we would all choose Greg's.

The form of cultural Marxism we are facing ignores the merit of Greg's book and says that because Dottie is Albanian — even though her book isn't as good, or as groundbreaking, or as important to the subject — we should eliminate Greg's book and only allow everyone to read from Dottie's book. Primarily because Dottie was born with certain genes.

The definition of racism is prejudice, discrimination, or antagonism directed against someone of a different race. You don't have to believe you're better than someone to be a racist. Hitler believed blonde-haired, blue-eyed white people were superior. He himself was not blond — his hair was dark brown — and he fell well short of the tall, fair Nordic ideal he demanded of others. He knew he did not measure up to his own standard, and yet he hated people based on their ethnicity alone. Hitler was no less a racist for not fitting his own ideal.

People today who are prejudicial, discriminatory, or antagonistic based simply on race are no less racists than Hitler was.

It isn't Albanians that are the focus of this invasion. And we love everyone who walks through those doors — regardless of race, politics, or gender.

The practice of cultural Marxism can best be expressed by a guy I went to school with. He wrote: "White people need to quit their jobs, and stop publishing their books, and let people that are not white have them, and write them. Schools need to fire their white professors and give the jobs to people that are not white. Schools need to purge their libraries of books that were written by white professors. Professors need to purge their syllabi of any books that were written by white people. Even if those professors, or books, are objectively better than the books written by any person of color." Let me ask you this: does this discriminate based on race? Does it antagonize based on race? Is it prejudicial based on race?

This most recent convention of the Southern Baptists had a resolution that talked about critical race theory and intersectionality. The idea is that... well, let me quote David Brooks, who wrote in the *New York Times* ("Liberal Parents, Radical Children," November 27, 2018):

> "Two great belief systems are clashing here. The older liberals tend to be individualistic and meritocratic. A citizen's job is to be activist, compassionate, and egalitarian. Boomers generally think they earned their success through effort and talent."

But —

> "The younger militants tend to have been influenced by the cultural Marxism that is now the lingua franca in the elite academy. Group identity is what matters. Society is a clash of oppressed and oppressor groups. People who are successful got that way through some form of group privilege and a legacy of oppression."

If you are comfortable, they seem to argue, you are comfortable because you took advantage of people to get to that point. If you are uncomfortable, it is because the world, your boss, some other race is taking advantage of you. Coupling this with the theory of intersectionality — the idea that the more ways in which you are a minority (gender, race, sexual orientation), the greater degree to which you have been taken advantage of — if you are a white male, you are at the top of the heap. White female, you're a close second. Male of color, you're below that depending on the color you happen to be. And if you're a female of color, you're lower still. A base assumption, then, is that all men are always taking advantage of all women, all the time. All white men are taking advantage of all people of color, all the time. And every ethnicity takes advantage of people of less dominant ethnicities.

This is nonsense. Think about your own actions and this idea of intersectionality: are you actively trying to take advantage of everyone below you on that list, always? Knowing most of you pretty well, I don't think any of you are the type to be actively trying to take advantage of anyone.

It goes at least one step further when it starts talking about truth. When you and I evaluate the truth of something, what do we compare it to? Scripture. Under this worldview, experience is the arbiter of truth — especially the experience of those who have been the victims of white privilege. The more subjugated you are, the more truth you have experienced; the more truth you have experienced, the more valuable your opinions are. This is similar to the error many Catholics make when they elevate the words of the Pope or tradition above Scripture. What happens when people do that? Nothing but trouble. The Jehovah's Witnesses do this, and they are a cult. The Mormons do this, and they are also a cult. It is a sign of deep trouble, and bears the odor of hell. If a person finds ultimate truth outside of Scripture, they are walking down a dangerous path.

What does the Bible say about all of this? Like we read earlier:

> ...for in Christ Jesus you are all sons of God, through faith. For as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ. There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus. And if you are Christ's, then you are Abraham's offspring, heirs according to promise.
> — Galatians 3:26–29 (ESV)

*(Someone open to Acts 10:34–35)*

> "Truly I understand that God shows no partiality, but in every nation anyone who fears him and does what is right is acceptable to him."
> — Acts 10:34–35 (ESV)

*(Someone open to Colossians 3:11)*

> Here there is not Greek and Jew, circumcised and uncircumcised, barbarian, Scythian, slave, free; but Christ is all, and in all.
> — Colossians 3:11 (ESV)

*(Someone open to Acts 10:28)*

*(Someone open to Revelation 5:9–10)*

> For there is no distinction between Jew and Greek; for the same Lord is Lord of all, bestowing his riches on all who call on him. For "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved."
> — Romans 10:12–13 (ESV)

This is the clear and consistent testimony of Scripture:

> For the LORD your God is God of gods and Lord of lords, the great, the mighty, and the awesome God, who is not partial and takes no bribe.
> — Deuteronomy 10:17 (ESV)

Those who adhere to this worldview forget that our citizenship is not on earth. We are citizens of heaven, here just for a short while. This whole worldview forgets that we are just passing through, and that what matters most isn't the things of earth, but the things of heaven. Is your citizenship in heaven? Then a wonderful eternity awaits. If your citizenship is of earth, then you have no hope.

One day we are all going to be standing in front of God. What race we are doesn't matter. What culture we are doesn't matter. What color shoes, or what watch, or what car we drive isn't going to matter. The one thing that will matter is whether or not we have a relationship with Jesus Christ.

In Christ we are all equal. Slaves bought back off the auction block. Sinners condemned and unclean — except for the work of Christ. None of us are better or more deserving. But for the grace of God, we would all be spending eternity in hell apart from Christ.

As the praise team comes up and sings a final song, I want you to ask yourselves: where is your citizenship? Are you a citizen of heaven? Or does your heart belong to the things of earth? If you aren't a citizen of heaven, come talk to me after the service, and I'll show you in the Bible how you can have eternal life.

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Prophetic Cultural Warning That Uses the Gospel as a Landing Strip Rather Than the Runway"**

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A courageous and locally relevant address on cultural Marxism and racial ideology that marshals genuine biblical authority but uses Romans 10:12-13 as a springboard rather than its governing text, relegating the gospel to a closing invitation rather than preaching it as the sermon's gravitational center.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Result: Conditional Pass — with significant reservation.**

**Scopus Test**: Romans 10:12-13 is read early and then largely set aside. The sermon's actual argument is: cultural Marxism is an ideology that divides by race; Scripture unifies in Christ; therefore the church should reject this ideology. This is a real biblical argument, and several texts support it. But the main point of Romans 10:12-13 — that universal gospel access for Jew and Gentile flows from Christ's lordship and the necessity of calling on him for salvation — is not the main point of this sermon. The sermon uses the text's *conclusion* (no racial distinction before God) while skipping the text's *engine* (Christ is Lord of all, and calling on his name is the only path to salvation for all). The passage is a springboard to the cultural topic, not the source of the sermon's argument.

**Authority Test**: Pass. The preacher does not soften or edit Scripture. He is willing to say hard things about race and ideology to a congregation that likely has complex opinions on these subjects. No passage is quoted selectively to avoid offense.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**No full fatal flags triggered — but two are close:**

- **Near-trigger: Fatal Flaw #1 (No clear gospel presentation)**: The gospel is gestured at but not preached. The closing lines — "come talk to me after the service, and I'll show you in the Bible how you can have eternal life" — are a gospel deferral. The congregation hears that their citizenship matters, that they need a relationship with Jesus Christ, and that heaven awaits the true citizen. But no articulation of *how* to be saved is given in the body of the sermon. What is Christ? What did he do? What does "relationship with Jesus Christ" actually mean? These are not answered. The gospel is assumed, not proclaimed.

- **Near-trigger: Fatal Flaw #4 (Application is entirely generic)**: The closing application — "where is your citizenship?" — is the right question but stated abstractly. No differentiated address, no specific call to a specific hearer, no cornering of a conscience. The sermon ends with a feeling of urgency but without the specificity that makes urgency actionable.

Neither fully triggers the cap because the sermon's doctrinal trajectory and multiple Scripture references produce some applicatory content. However, the gospel deficit is serious and the score will reflect it without a cap.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is deferred, not proclaimed** (closing section): "Come talk to me after the service" is a pastoral invitation, not a sermon. By the time the congregation is told their citizenship in heaven matters, they have not been told *how* to become citizens. The verse quoted — Romans 10:12-13 — contains the answer: "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved." But the call to *call on the name* never comes from the pulpit. The sermon quotes the gospel and then defers it.

2. **The primary text is treated as a topical peg** (throughout): Romans 10:12-13 is the announced text, but the sermon's argument does not flow from it. If the preacher had asked, "What is Paul actually arguing in Romans 10:12-13, and why?" — the answer would have forced the sermon in a completely different and more powerful direction: the universal necessity of calling on Christ's name, the Jewish-Gentile barrier that the gospel demolishes, the urgency of preaching that flows from v.14-17. The text contains everything the sermon needs; none of it is used.

3. **The application invites but never summons** (closing section): "Ask yourselves: where is your citizenship?" is the right question but requires a specific answer from the preacher, not just the hearer. What must a person *do* whose citizenship is not in heaven? That answer is in the text — call on the name of the Lord. The sermon never says it.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's structure communicates an implicit theology: the cultural problem is primary; the gospel is the solution addended at the end. This inverts the biblical priority. The gospel is not the answer to cultural Marxism — cultural Marxism is one symptom of the deeper problem the gospel addresses. A sermon that leads with cultural analysis and ends with a gospel invitation has accidentally told the congregation that the cultural argument is what matters most.

Additionally, the preacher may not see that the argument from Scripture for racial equality — while entirely true — could be made by any liberal theologian, Christian or otherwise. The *scandal* of the gospel in this text is not that God is impartial (most people believe that) but that *calling on Christ's name* is the *only* path through which his impartiality becomes operative for salvation. That is offensive. That is what would give offense in a mosque or synagogue. That offense is entirely absent.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon *feels* strong because it is engaging, locally specific, and culturally brave. The congregation will likely respond to it energetically because it addresses things they are thinking about. But a sermon that successfully engages a cultural moment while failing to preach the gospel has mistaken relevance for faithfulness. The cultural analysis is competent; the preaching is incomplete.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Romans 10:12-13 sits at the hinge of Paul's most anguished argument in all his letters — the lament over Israel's rejection of Christ, the proclamation of universal Gentile access through faith, and the commissioning of every believer to go and proclaim (vv.14-17: "How then will they call on him in whom they have not believed? And how are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard?"). The passage is a missionary text. It is a preaching text. It is a text about the urgency of declaring Christ to people who have not heard — including the Dhund Abbasi of northern Pakistan, mentioned at the sermon's opening. The connection between the people group segment and the primary text is already there: the Dhund Abbasi need to hear the gospel precisely because "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved." The sermon never makes this connection.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach Romans 10:12-13 by preaching its argument, not just its conclusion.** The text answers the sermon's own question. "No distinction between Jew and Greek" — *why?* Because "the same Lord is Lord of all, bestowing his riches on all who call on him." The unity is not moral or cultural; it is soteriological. Everyone can call on Christ. No one is excluded by race. No one is included by race. The only category is *those who call on the name of the Lord.* That is the text's argument, and it is far more powerful than "God doesn't play favorites."

2. **Move the gospel proclamation from a post-service invitation to the sermon's climax.** The final paragraph is the material: "Slaves bought back off the auction block. Sinners condemned and unclean — except for the work of Christ." Expand this. What did Christ do? Why does his blood cross every racial barrier? How does one call on the name? Make the gospel the conclusion to which the cultural analysis has been building.

3. **Connect the people group segment to the primary text explicitly.** The Dhund Abbasi have nearly a million people who have not called on the name of the Lord — *and Romans 10:14-17 is the commissioning text for why this congregation should care.* "How are they to hear without someone preaching?" Say it. The cultural sermon and the missionary heart belong together.

4. **Give the closing question a specific answer from the pulpit.** "Where is your citizenship?" must be followed by: "If it is not in heaven — if you have never called on the name of the Lord — here is what that means and here is what you must do." Do not defer it to a parking lot conversation.

5. **Consider re-titling and recentering the sermon around Romans 10:14-17 rather than 10:12-13.** The missionary imperative — "how are they to hear without someone preaching?" — makes the cultural critique a subplot of the main story rather than the main story itself. The sermon's pastoral instincts are right; its textual orbit needs adjustment.
