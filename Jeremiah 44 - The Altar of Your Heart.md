# Jeremiah 44 — The Altar of Your Heart
*Jeremiah 44:1–14 / Matthew 28:18–20*

---

I heard a statistic recently, and every time I hear it I'm floored. Nik Ripkin was citing research done by LifeWay — the people who study the church — and he said that the majority of believers in the United States, even those born and raised in the church, will never share the gospel before they are buried by the church.

[*Editorial note: The original figure the pastor cited was 90%, but LifeWay Research's verified 2012 study found that 61% of U.S. churchgoers had not shared the gospel with anyone in the prior six months. No source was found linking Ripken to a 90% figure. The manuscript has been adjusted to "the majority" — accurate and defensible. If the pastor has a specific Ripken recording or transcript with the 90% figure, that source should be reviewed before using the larger number from the pulpit.*]

Born and raised in the church. Went to Sunday school. Sang the songs. Served on committees. Buried by the church. Never once, in the presence of a person who needed to hear the gospel, opened their mouth and said it.

While you're turning to Matthew 28 — New Testament, about three-quarters of the way through your Bible — I want you to hold that number in your head.

*[Read Matthew 28:18–20.]*

> *"And Jesus came and said to them, 'All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age.'"* — Matthew 28:18–20, ESV

Did it say *when the door opens for you*, go make disciples? Did it say *when you feel confident in your own ability*, go? What did it say?

*Go.* Not maybe go. Not go when it's comfortable. Not go when someone hands you an opening. Go.

If you are doing less than that — if I am doing less than that — that's disobedience. Isn't it.

Nik Ripkin said it plainly: *"It is simply a matter of obedience. If He is our Lord, then we will obey Him. If we do not obey Him, then He is not our Lord."*

[*Editorial note: Verified. Source:* The Insanity of God *(B&H Publishing, 2013, co-authored with Gregg Lewis). Full quote: "Serving God is not a matter of location, but a matter of obedience. It is simply a matter of obedience. If He is our Lord, then we will obey Him. If we do not obey Him, then He is not our Lord." The opening sentence can be included or omitted depending on pacing.*]

On that note — let's stop and open in prayer, and then I want to tell you a little about the man who said that.

---

*[Prayer.]*

---

The next passage we're going to look at is Jeremiah 44. But before we get there, roll the clock back to the mid-nineties.

The United Nations was fighting the famine in Somalia. Nik Ripkin and his family were on the front lines — not watching it on the news, living it. Dusty, dirty, hungry Somalia.

He tells about meeting a man who was running an orphanage. The kids looked happy. Well-fed, compared to everyone outside the walls of that compound. The man invited Nik for dinner, and Nik agreed. He happened to pass through the kitchen before the meal was served, and he saw what they were eating. Little bits of meat with the maggots scraped off. An army of flies. Scraps of rotting vegetables being boiled and served. That was going to be his dinner. And these children — who were clearly eating better than everyone else in that city — were surviving on that. That was a serious place to be a missionary.

Things went from bad to worse.

The family eventually relocated to Nairobi, Kenya. But the hardship followed them there. Nik had a son named Timothy. Timothy had asthma — bad asthma. The allergens in East Africa and his lungs just didn't mix. One night Timothy had an attack. Nik was in the backseat of the car, giving CPR, while someone else drove them to the hospital. Timothy didn't make it. He was sixteen years old. It was Easter Sunday, 1997.

[*Editorial note: Verified. Timothy Ripken, age 16, died of cardiac arrest triggered by a severe asthma attack — likely from mold in the family's home in Nairobi. Nik administered CPR in the back of the car. Source:* The Insanity of God *(B&H Publishing, 2013, with Gregg Lewis). Note: the family served in Somalia during the early 1990s famine but had moved to Nairobi by 1997 when Timothy died — the son did not die in Somalia.*]

It was in the shadow of all of that — the orphanage, the famine, the grave — that Nik wrote: *"It is simply a matter of obedience. If He is our Lord, then we will obey Him. If we do not obey Him, then He is not our Lord."*

---

There are some parallels with this in our passage tonight. God had given Israel a direct command. *Do not go to Egypt.* He said it in chapter 42. What do the Israelites do? They didn't believe God had actually said it. Or they didn't believe he meant it. And they hopped the first camel to Egypt.

Let's read it — Jeremiah 44, verses 1 through 14.

*[Read Jeremiah 44:1–14.]*

---

Now — you might be sitting there a little confused. Pastor, you've been talking about disobedience in evangelism. This passage is about disobedience when it comes to idols. What's the connection?

Here it is.

The reason we disobey God when we fail to evangelize is almost always because our heart loves something more than it loves obedience to God. We want something — comfort, approval, the absence of awkwardness — more than we want to obey. When we put that thing above God's command, that thing has become an idol. An idol is anything that comes between us and God.

So if God says *go* and we stay because we're afraid of what people will think — we've put our reputation on the worship altar of our heart. If God says *speak* and we go quiet because it costs too much — we've put ourselves on the worship altar of our heart.

We are our own idols.

I had a friend at Trinity. He was the first person to greet me when I got there — my first real friend in that place. He'd been at Word of Life, was serving as a youth pastor. We had a lot of the same classes. He took better notes than I did, but I was more likely to remember what got said. Any way, good person to study with.

A couple years in, he came to me upset. As his friend I tried to talk to him. He'd gone to his pastor and opened up about having — and acting on — homosexual feelings toward someone else at the church.

The pastor removed him from youth ministry and gave him a path to restoration: counseling, accountability. He told him plainly: you are placing your own desires above God's.

Six months later, he'd moved on, joined the staff of another church, and was caught looking at pornography on the church computer.

I'm not telling his story to pile on. I'm telling it because with certain sins, the mechanism is just easier to see. You can watch in slow motion how a person has taken what they want and placed it higher than their obedience to God. With other sins — sins that look more respectable — the root is the same, but it's buried a little deeper.

The person who won't share the gospel because they're afraid, and the person living in sexual sin — same root. They've both elevated something they want above their desire to obey God. They've both put it on the worship altar of their heart.

---

Idolatry runs through the whole Bible like a thread, and it runs through this passage too. God's people were marrying people steeped in idol worship. They were making sacrifices to false gods. Giving their time and affection to things that couldn't save them.

And God — in his patience — kept sending prophets. Generation after generation. He sent men to warn his people that they were off track. He was persistent. He sent many.

Any of you seen the movie *Bruce Almighty*? There's a scene where Bruce has had the worst day of his life. He's driving home, crying out to God — *I need a sign, show me something, give me direction.* And the whole time, God is sending him sign after sign after sign. There's even a truck full of warning signs. Bruce ignores every one of them and drives straight into a lamp post. Totals the car.

If he'd only been watching for what he was asking for.

But he was focused on himself. He makes that clear in the next scene, where he screams at God — you're the only one around here who isn't working. That's his assessment of the situation.

Fortunately for Bruce, and for us, God is long-suffering. Patient. He loves us enough to give second and third and fourth chances — generations of chances, like Israel got. Before he finally says: enough.

Look at verse 11: *"I will set my face against you for harm, to cut off all Judah."* He's cutting them off. From their national identity. From the covenant. From the promise. From him.

In our day, we know that when you come to Christ in genuine faith, God doesn't take it back. Your name written in the Lamb's Book of Life is a permanent thing. Once you're saved, you're signed, sealed, and delivered. The new covenant doesn't work the way the old one did.

When I was a kid, I liked to write in pen. But I was a kid — I made mistakes — so I really needed pencils. I was so disappointed the first time I tried an erasable pen. It didn't work, and it tore a hole right through my homework. My mistake was still there, permanent on the page. Best I could do was cross it out. I wasn't aware of white-out at that point apparently. Hah.

But when Jesus writes your name in the Lamb's Book of Life, there is no cosmic white-out. You cannot be taken out once you've been included.

---

Evangelism isn't the only area where we put our own desires above what God commands, is it.

Think back over this past year. Can any of you say, beyond a shadow of a doubt, that you've done everything God wanted you to do when he wanted you to do it? I can't raise my hand for that. We all fall short. We drop the ball in ways we see clearly, and probably in ways we don't.

Paul Washer wrote that we must *"remember that to sin is more than just breaking a rule — it's grieving the person who loves us beyond measure."*

[*Editorial note: UNVERIFIED. No primary source found — not traced to any known sermon, transcript, or published text. Widely circulated online without citation. Do not attribute to Washer from the pulpit until a specific sermon or book is identified. Consider using without attribution ("Someone put it this way…") or replacing with a verified source.*]

So often we try to minimize it. I spoke to a couple not long ago about pre-marital sex. He wanted to see where in Scripture it's prohibited. I showed him. He cracked jokes, made light of the sin he had put above his obedience to God.

It's an easy thing to do. We rationalize it. We compare it to something worse. *At least I didn't kill anybody.*

Thomas Manton wrote: *"We make light of sin, but Christ found it not so light."*

[*Editorial note: UNVERIFIED. This quote could not be confirmed in Manton's works via digitized sources (CCEL, Wesley Center). The sentiment is consistent with Manton's writing on sin, but the specific line "We make light of sin, but Christ found it not so light" was not located. If the pastor has this in a printed volume, cite the specific volume and sermon before using. Do not attribute to Manton from the pulpit on the strength of online circulation alone.*]

Every time we sin, we make the cross necessary.

Every time we ignore the Great Commission because our comfort, our reputation, our self-preservation is sitting on the altar of our heart — we make Christ's death necessary. We may as well have held the nail for the man who drove it through his hands and feet. We may as well have swung the whip that tore his back. We may as well have been one of those gambling at the foot of the cross.

It was my sin that held him there. Until it was finished.

---

That is the gospel.

Christ, the perfect Son of God, died the death my sin demanded. He rose on the third day. And he offers — to anyone who will call on his name — peace. Not the absence of trouble. An anchor in the middle of it.

Romans 10:9: *"If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*

Before the praise team comes up, Christian — one last thing.

A.W. Pink wrote: *"If you are still following a course of self-pleasing, you are only deceiving yourself if you think you have come to Christ."*

[*Editorial note: Verified. Source: A.W. Pink,* Studies on Saving Faith *(available in full at Monergism.com). Exact text: "Dear reader, if you are still following a course of self-pleasing, you are only deceiving yourself if you think you have come to Christ." The "Dear reader" salutation is appropriately dropped for preaching. Wording is otherwise word-for-word accurate.*]

If you — and how you feel, and what you want, and what keeps you comfortable — if that is your first priority, then you are not on the throne of your heart. You are. Christ isn't your Lord. He's a name you carry.

If that's you — today is the day to change it. Today is the day to become a Christian. Not the day to walk an aisle if it doesn't mean anything. The day to actually give Christ the throne.

Won't you come?

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastoral Illustration Wearing an Exposition's Clothes — Sincere Appeal, Thin Textual Foundation**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and honest call to obedience in evangelism and against idolatry that connects genuine pastoral experience to a real spiritual problem, but uses Jeremiah 44 as a launching pad rather than as the sermon's governing text, relying heavily on illustration and analogy where exposition should be carrying the load.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**FAIL — with qualification.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — "we make idols of our own comfort, and this explains our disobedience in evangelism and in general" — is a real theological point, but it does not arise organically from the *main point of Jeremiah 44:1–14.* The main point of the text is God's judicial judgment on a covenant people who have hardened themselves against his word through persistent idolatry — the narrative culmination of Jeremiah's warning arc — specifically the finality of God's sentence: "I will set my face against you for harm." The passage is about God's covenant wrath against apostasy. The sermon treats it as background material for a topical lesson about idol-of-the-heart idolatry and evangelism. That is not the same thing.

*Authority Test:* Marginal pass. The preacher does not contradict the passage or soften it — he simply doesn't exposit it. Verses 2–10 (the catalog of persistent sins), 12–14 (the specifics of the judgment), and the covenant framework are all skipped or assumed. The hard parts are not softened — they are bypassed.

**Verdict:** The gate is technically failed — the sermon's main point does not demonstrably arise from the passage's own argument. The sermonic thesis could be preached identically with zero reference to Jeremiah 44. The passage is illustrative background, not the governing text. However, the evaluation will continue with a score cap of 69, noting this as a structural failure rather than a theological one.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag Present: Main point does not equal text's point (Flag #2)**
The sermon fails to derive its central argument from the passage's own logic. Jeremiah 44 is cited, briefly read, but never exposited. The verse-by-verse movement of God's indictment and judgment is absent.

**Flag Present: Application is generic in key places (Flag #4 — partial)**
The application to evangelism is specific and pastoral. But the move from there to "all areas of disobedience" becomes very broad: "Can any of you say, beyond a shadow of a doubt, that you've done everything God wanted you to do?" That sweep loses the specificity the earlier section built.

**Score capped at 69 due to Berean Gate failure.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 11/25**

- *Textual Fidelity* — 4/10: Jeremiah 44 is read aloud and referenced (verse 11 is quoted), but no exposition occurs. The passage's own argument — God's patient sending of prophets, Israel's willful defection to Egypt against explicit command, the covenant judgment — is summarized in a few sentences and then left. The sermon imports a theological framework (heart-idolatry) that is consistent with the broader canon but not the particular argument of this text.
- *Contextual Awareness* — 2/5: Chapter 42's command ("Do not go to Egypt") is referenced, which shows minimal contextual awareness. But the passage's location in Jeremiah's narrative arc — the aftermath of Jerusalem's fall, the remnant's flight to Egypt — is not developed.
- *Exegetical Clarity* — 2/5: No Greek or Hebrew engagement. The text is not opened — it is paraphrased. There is no identification of the Fallen Condition Focus arising from the passage itself.
- *Logical Faithfulness* — 3/5: No eisegesis per se, but significant idea-importing. The connection between Jeremiah 44's idolatry (literal Canaanite gods, Queen of Heaven worship) and the "idol of the heart" framework is theologically defensible (cf. Ezekiel 14) but it is asserted, not demonstrated from the text.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness* — 5/7: The cross section ("It was my sin that held him there") is genuine and moving. The connection between sin and the cross is stated plainly and personally. However, the christological movement is primarily moral — our sin made the cross necessary — without a full account of substitutionary atonement or the righteousness of Christ credited to believers.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* — 1/3: The Old Covenant / New Covenant distinction is handled roughly — the eternal security paragraph is theologically correct but clumsily applied (a personal anecdote about an erasable pen), and the deeper connection between Jeremiah 44's covenant consequences and Christ's fulfillment of the covenant is missed entirely.
- *Doctrinal Precision* — 4/4: No theological errors identified. The gospel statement at the conclusion (Romans 10:9) is accurate. The Pink quote is well-placed and theologically sound.
- *Confessional Alignment* — 3/4: Eternal security is affirmed clearly. No contradiction of 1689 theology. But the sermon does not engage the passage's soteriological freight — the Covenant of Grace, the remnant, the persistence of God's redemptive purpose even through judgment.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel* — 3/5: Romans 10:9 is read. The cross is named personally. But justification by faith alone is not clearly articulated. The gospel moment is brief and comes at the end after a long emphasis on obedience and disobedience.
- *Necessity of Christ* — 3/5: Christ is necessary to the sermon's conclusion but not to its body. The first three-quarters of the sermon could survive without him. He is introduced late and briefly.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity* — 3/5: The cross paragraph is the sermon's high-water mark emotionally. But the sermon arrives there through moral logic (our disobedience makes the cross necessary) rather than through a proclamation of grace. The law hammer lands; the gospel balm is underdeveloped.

*Synagogue Test:* A disciplined religious moralist could follow most of this sermon and nod. The call to deal with idols, obey God, and stop rationalizing sin are themes they would agree with. The cross paragraph would offend — good. But it is brief and the sermon's primary register is moral exhortation, not gospel proclamation.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10/15**

- *Heart Penetration* — 4/5: The "worship altar of your heart" frame is effective and pastoral. The homosexuality illustration is courageous — it names real sin at real cost and then generalizes the root fairly, which protects the person and indicts the room simultaneously. That is skilled pastoral work.

But the exhortation to the believer is somewhat vague: what exactly must the Christian *do* with their idol of comfort? The unsaved are told clearly; the saved are asked to reflect.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness* — 2/4: The evangelism framing at the opening is the most concrete equipping moment. But the sermon does not close with a specific call to the body regarding evangelism — it pivots to personal salvation. The original thesis (we don't evangelize because of idolatry) is not closed out with a corporate call to witness.

A listener could not sketch this on a napkin.

- *Unity* — 2/2: One dominant idea (idolatry of the heart drives disobedience) does hold the sermon together, even if loosely.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Primarily myself and my disobedience. Christ is present but arrives late and briefly. The dominant register is self-examination. A partial "yes" to Christ — but with effort, not by design.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas about obedience and idolatry, illustrated with human stories. God's voice in the text itself — his patient sending of prophets, his righteous wrath, his persistent covenant love — is not opened. The encounter with God is mediated almost entirely through human narrative rather than through the passage.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Convicted — genuinely, in places. The evangelism challenge is sharp. The cross paragraph convicts. But a significant portion of the middle is informational narrative (Ripkin biography, Bruce Almighty, anecdotes) that informs more than it presses.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The "altar of your heart" metaphor is theologically accurate and pastorally memorable.** The connection between evangelistic silence and heart-idolatry (comfort, reputation, self-preservation as functional gods) is a genuine insight that names the real mechanism of disobedience. The phrase is native to this sermon and will carry.

2. **The personal cross paragraph is the sermon's strongest moment.** "It was my sin that held him there. Until it was finished." This is confessional preaching — the preacher stands under the text's accusation before the congregation does. First-person, present-tense, cross-anchored. It is brief, which is its only weakness.

3. **Courageous illustration discipline.** The Trinity friend story — naming homosexual sin, showing the mechanism of idolatry in slow motion, then extending the same diagnosis to respectable sins — is exactly right. It takes the risk of specificity and then turns it on every person in the room. This is Puritan-style differentiation working well.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Jeremiah 44 is read, not preached.** The text's own argument — God's indictment through historical recitation (vv. 2–10), his patient sending of prophets (vv. 4–5), his announced judgment (vv. 11–14) — is never exposited. The sermon borrows the text's setting and one verse (v. 11) but does not open what God actually says there. A congregation that heard this sermon would not know what Jeremiah 44 argues, teaches, or reveals about God's character.

2. **The gospel is an addendum, not the sermon's center of gravity.** The cross paragraph appears after the invitation begins ("Before the praise team comes up"). It is two sentences of gospel followed by a brief Romans 10:9 quotation. There is no development of what Christ's death achieved, how it addresses the idolatry the sermon diagnosed, or how the gospel changes the heart that idolatry captures. The law lands heavy; the balm is light.

3. **The structural thesis is abandoned before the landing.** The sermon opens with evangelism (Matthew 28, Ripkin, the statistic). It identifies idolatry as the root of evangelistic failure. It should close by returning the congregation to the Great Commission with renewed hearts. Instead it ends with a general salvation call. The original diagnosis is never closed out with a concrete call to witness. The congregation is not sent — they are invited.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that Jeremiah 44 contains one of Scripture's most devastating portraits of human self-deception — specifically, the women who insist that their prosperity came from *keeping* their idolatry, not from abandoning it (vv. 15–19, just beyond the passage). The text itself is a case study in the hardness of the idolatrous heart and the self-justifying logic of unbelief. This material would have given the sermon's "we rationalize sin" section enormous exegetical power — but the preacher stopped reading before reaching it.

Additionally, the preacher does not appear to see how the Matthew 28 / Jeremiah 44 pairing could have been made far more powerful by observing that both texts share the theme of God's universal authority — "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me" (Matthew 28:18) echoes against the God who has authority to judge his covenant people and to scatter them across the nations (Jeremiah 44:12). The Great Commission is not a polite request from a gentle Jesus — it is a command from the same sovereign who scattered Israel for idolatry.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Ripkin biography feels like this sermon's expositor's heart — rich, personal, and morally weighty. But it does three times the work that the text does. The biographical material is so emotionally and narratively strong that it creates the impression of a well-grounded sermon. In reality, it is carrying the weight that the exposition should carry. The sermon's emotional and spiritual credibility rests on a human story, not on the opened Word. That is the illusion.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The passage's most powerful move — God's repeated sending of prophets whom Israel would not hear (vv. 4–5: *"I persistently sent to you all my servants the prophets, saying, 'Oh, do not do this abomination that I hate!'"*) — is the precise analog to the evangelism thesis the sermon is building. God *goes* when his people will not go. God speaks when his people will not speak. The preacher who refuses to go in Matthew 28 is standing in the tradition of Israel who would not hear the prophets — and God's response to both is the same: he does not stop sending. He simply sends judgment alongside the herald. That connection, had it been made from the text itself, would have made the sermon's application both exegetical and devastating.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific)

1. **Return to Jeremiah 44 and preach vv. 2–10 before vv. 11–14.** The indictment (what they did) must precede the judgment (what God will do). Currently the sermon jumps to the judgment verse (v. 11) with no exposition of the charge. Spend two minutes on the specific sins of the passage — the Queen of Heaven worship, the explicit command broken in chapter 42, the historical persistence of this idolatry across generations. Let the text build its own case before you borrow it.

2. **Relocate and expand the gospel.** Move the cross paragraph earlier — after the law has fully landed, not as a whisper before the worship team. Add one sentence on what Christ's atonement actually accomplishes for the idolatrous heart: not merely that sin made the cross necessary, but that the cross destroys the debt of our idolatry and gives us a new heart that can actually obey (Ezekiel 36:26 runs through Jeremiah's own new covenant promise in chapter 31). The gospel should be the sermon's resolution, not its footnote.

3. **Close the evangelism thesis.** The sermon opens by indicting the church for silence and closes with a salvation call to the unconverted. Return to the congregation in the closing: "You who know Christ — the idol of comfort is identified, the cross has dealt with it, and the command remains: *Go*. Not when you feel ready. Go." Give the congregation a specific, concrete next step in evangelism — not just a general summons.

4. **Replace or reframe the** ***Bruce Almighty*** **illustration.** A comedy film illustration in the section on covenant judgment undercuts the severity of what the text is saying. The passage is not about a man who missed God's gentle nudges — it is about a covenant people who received explicit prophetic warning, refused it, and received God's judicial wrath. Consider instead a historical or personal illustration of someone who received clear warning and chose to ignore it — the stakes are different, and the illustration should carry that weight.

5. **Verify and either attribute or remove the Washer and Manton quotes.** The manuscript's own editorial notes flag both as unverified. Preaching an unverified quote — even a theologically sound one — is a credibility risk that the careful-not-clever standard will not allow. Either locate primary sources before the pulpit or rephrase as the preacher's own words.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement

The eternal gravity is felt in the cross paragraph and in the Ripkin biography — a man who buried his son on Easter Sunday and still wrote about obedience — but the sermon does not fully let the weight of Jeremiah 44's God settle on the room: the God who is patient for generations and then sets his face against his own people for harm is the same God whose Son absorbed that wrath for us. That juxtaposition, if opened, would carry more eternal weight than any illustration.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Jeremiah 44 is a prophetic judgment oracle embedded in historical narrative — a genre that rewards careful attention to the covenant legal structure (charge → verdict → sentence) and the redemptive-historical placement (post-fall of Jerusalem, on the threshold of exile). The rubric's exposition categories apply well to this genre; the challenge is that the sermon did not engage the genre at all. The rubric is not strained here — it reveals that the passage was bypassed rather than opened. The Matthew 28 / Jeremiah 44 pairing is structurally interesting but requires more exegetical work to justify homiletically than the sermon provides.
