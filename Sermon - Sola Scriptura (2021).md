## Opening: People Group of the Week — The Buryat

It's October — my second favorite month, after December. Many are excited about pumpkins and candy. People have fun dressing up. I'm happy for them, so long as they aren't dishonoring their Savior. But there is a far more important event that happened on the same day — one that should be celebrated by Protestants worldwide.

Does anyone know what day that is? Reformation Day.

Before we get into that, open your Bible to 2 Timothy 3. While you're turning there, let me talk about our people group of the week: the **Buryat**.

*[Note: The pastor spells it "Buriat." The standard modern spelling is Buryat. The older transliteration "Buriat" appears in some historical sources.]*

The Buryat people live in an area straddling China, Mongolia, and Russia. They have different languages and trade languages in each of those countries. The people are being pulled in two directions religiously: roughly half toward animism and Buddhism, while the other half are drawn toward the nominal Christianity of Russia. Less than four tenths of one percent are believers in Jesus Christ — basically four in a thousand — mostly because there is almost no one there sharing the gospel. They are, as far as we know, completely unreached and unengaged.

Who will pray for the Buryat of China, Russia, and Mongolia?

## Reformation Day: What Happened and Why It Matters

Reformation Day marks the day a little-known, unimportant Catholic monk called the church to repentance.

I have read quite a bit about the Protestant understanding of what happened, and recently have begun reading the Catholic perspective as well. It seems 504 years later they still don't understand what the issues were — and part of that is Luther's fault. He wrote 95 theses — 95 points of contention with the Catholic Church — and nailed them to the door of the Wittenberg church.

*[Note: Tradition holds that Luther nailed his Ninety-Five Theses to the door of the Castle Church (Schlosskirche / All Saints' Church) in Wittenberg on October 31, 1517.]*

In doing so he caused an earthquake in the church that eventually shattered much of Rome's power across the world.

Of the 95 theses, most of them were about the same two or three issues — and the majority were about the Catholic Church essentially extorting the laity. Luther wanted the church to stop taking advantage of the elderly, the sick, and the poor. He wanted to re-align the church to the Bible. The most important ways he did that were: unpinning authority from men and repinning it to Scripture, and reclaiming justification by faith alone.

Things had gotten bad — even by the Catholics' own admission. People had substituted superstition for the Word of God.

Here is not an exact example, but something like what was going on: the Bible says not to lie, but perhaps a local priest was teaching that wrong was right — that lying on Tuesday after seven o'clock, after a black cat had crossed your path, could be good for the soul.

Further, the church was the biggest bank around. If you needed money, you borrowed from the church. Suppose Dottie wanted to start a business and took out a loan for a million dollars. Before she could open her bookstore, she is hit by a meteor and dies. She has spent almost all of the money and hasn't made any yet. The Catholic Church would then go to families in situations like this and tell them their loved one was stuck in a state not far from hell until they could pay back all the money — plus additional payments to the pope to pray for the debtor's soul, so that the person might move higher and higher in this transitionary near-hell state until one day they might escape to glory. It was predatory on the grieving, and it was offensive.

Not only that — corrupt priests would sell indulgences. I once found one, and I wish I still had it. It belonged to an elderly couple who had to earn one from the Catholic Church.

The Catholic Church outlawed the *sale* of indulgences in 1567 — but did not change their fundamental beliefs and practices regarding them. Instead of a set price, they now require people to earn them through charitable donations and other means.

*[Note: Pope Pius V formally abolished all indulgences involving financial transactions in 1567. The Council of Trent (concluding December 1563) had already condemned "all evil gains for the obtaining of them." Indulgences as a concept were retained; only their commercialization was abolished. The pastor's characterization is essentially accurate.]*

I encountered an elderly couple — poor and dying of cancer — who had paid approximately twenty thousand dollars, nearly everything they had, to earn an indulgence from their local Catholic diocese. It left them unable to afford their two-bedroom apartment in a rough neighborhood. That's how I met them — they were being evicted. It is a predatory and corrupt practice. It is how they funded growth.

If you have ever seen the great cathedrals of Europe — I saw Notre Dame in Paris more than a decade ago — its existence was probably owed in no small part to corrupt priests extorting the sick, the elderly, and the grieving. As were so many of the great cathedrals of Europe.

The corruption didn't stop at the parish level. There were those skimming off the top, purchasing for themselves more power. It turned out there was very little you couldn't buy an indulgence for. Kings, queens, and popes paid with gold. At one point even the papacy itself was for sale to the highest bidder.

*[Note: This refers to the crisis of the 10th–11th centuries. The three-way simultaneous papacy (three claimants at once) historically involved **Benedict IX, Sylvester III, and Gregory VI** — all in 1044–1046. The pastor listed "John the 19th, and Gregory the 4th, Sylvester the third AND Benedict the 9th" as all pope at the same time. John XIX and Gregory IV were not contemporaneous with Sylvester III and Benedict IX: John XIX was pope 1024–1032 (before Sylvester III and the triple papacy crisis), and Gregory IV was pope 827–844. This list conflates popes from different eras. The actual simultaneous triple papacy was Benedict IX, Sylvester III, and Gregory VI.]*

Speaking of Benedict IX — he is accused of having inappropriate relationships with men, women, and animals.

*[Note: These accusations come primarily from St. Peter Damian's polemic "Liber Gomorrhianus" (Book of Gomorrah, c. 1049) and other hostile contemporaries. The general historical record does acknowledge Benedict IX as one of the most scandalous and corrupt popes in Church history. The specific accusations are from adversarial sources and may involve some exaggeration.]*

Looking back, the Catholic Church of Luther's era felt more like the Italian Mafia than it resembled the church.

*[Note: The transcript reads "the catholic church of 1504." In context, this appears to be a slip — the pastor is speaking about the era of the Reformation (1517), not 1504 specifically. 1517 is the year Luther nailed his theses; "1504" may be a transcript or speech error.]*

## The Reformers Before Luther

In the midst of all this corruption — extortion, theft, vice — Martin Luther was one of a handful of men willing to stand up and speak out. But before Luther, there was a man named **Jan Hus**.

*[Note: The pastor spells the name "Jon Huss." The standard spelling is Jan Hus. "Hus" is indeed derived from the Czech word for "goose." His surname came from his birthplace, Husinec — literally "Goosetown." The pastor says "check language" — this should be Czech.]*

Hus preached against indulgences and against the ways the Roman Catholic Church had abandoned Scripture. It ultimately led to his death.

He was tied to a stake, a great pile of wood built up around him, prepared for burning. Before they lit it they offered him the chance to recant. He said:

*"I would gladly recant of the things I never said, and the things I did say — if I could be convinced from Scripture."*

They could not convince him from Scripture. So as they set him on fire, Martin Luther — who lived roughly a century later — reports him as having said this:

*"They will roast a goose now, but after a hundred years they will hear a swan sing, and him they will endure."*

He then began reciting the Psalms from memory.

*[Note: The historicity of the "goose and swan" prophecy is debated. The quote does not appear in contemporary eyewitness accounts of Hus's execution in 1415. Its clearest source is Luther himself, writing in the 1530s. Luther attributed the "swan" prophecy to Hus, and the version the pastor cites ("They will roast a goose now, but after a hundred years they will hear a swan sing, and him they will endure") closely matches Luther's own written account. The legend was later transmitted through Lutheran hagiographers including Johannes Mathesius (1566) and John Foxe's Book of Martyrs. Whether Hus actually spoke these words at the stake — or whether the prophecy grew from his prison letters (in which he used bird metaphors for future reformers) and was later shaped by Luther — is historically uncertain. The pastor correctly attributes the report to Luther's writing, which is accurate to the sources.]*

*[Note: The recantation statement attributed to Hus — "if I could be convinced from Scripture" — represents the spirit of Hus's defense at the Council of Constance accurately: he consistently appealed to Scripture over papal authority and refused to recant what he believed the Bible taught. However, the specific phrasing is strikingly similar to Luther's famous declaration at the Diet of Worms (1521): "Unless I am convinced by the testimony of the Scriptures or by clear reason... I cannot and will not recant anything." The exact wording at the stake is not confirmed in primary sources; this may reflect a convergence of both men's documented positions.]*

We come from a proud tradition of people who stood strong for the faith in the teeth of persecution.

Before Hus was a man named **John Wycliffe**. You've heard of Wycliffe Bible Translators? They are named for him. It was Wycliffe's teachings that inspired Hus to preach the gospel, and Hus who inspired Luther. Luther even called himself a Hussite.

*[Note: Luther declared "I am a Hussite!" (*Ich bin ein Hussite!*) at the Leipzig Disputation in 1519, after reading Hus's writings during a noon break. He later wrote: "We are all Hussites without knowing it" (c. February 1520). Both statements are historically confirmed.]*

Wycliffe taught against indulgences and was famous for saying:

*"The pope is but a man, subject to sin; but Christ is the Lord of Lords, and this kingdom is to be held directly and solely of Christ alone."*

*[Note: Confirmed authentic. Source: Wycliffe, "On Civil Dominion" (De Civili Dominio), 1376. The fuller quote is: "England belongs to no pope. The pope is but a man, subject to sin; but Christ is the Lord of Lords, and this kingdom is held directly and solely of Christ alone."]*

True words. The pope is but a man, subject to sin. One pope would say one thing; the next would say something contradictory. And the papacy was available to the highest bidder.

Because of the centuries of corruption Wycliffe witnessed, he wanted people to read God's Word for themselves. Popes, priests, bishops, and cardinals could not be trusted. The only thing the average person could trust was the Word of God. Wycliffe wrote:

*"The Scripture is a light and sheweth us the true way, both what to do and what to hope for: and a defense from all error, and a comfort in adversity."*

*[Note: This quote is attributed to Wycliffe in numerous Protestant sources, and the archaic spelling is consistent with Middle English. However, an exact primary-source citation has not been verified. It may derive from Wycliffe's sermons or treatises, but the specific location could not be confirmed in this session. Flagged for future verification.]*

This threatened the power of the papacy. They responded, saying the pearl of the Bible was being thrown into the swine pen. They seemed especially offended that women would read it — which tells you something about what was really going on.

The Roman Catholic Church was in a dark and terrible place. Not just for a few years — for five hundred years before Luther nailed his theses to the door of the Wittenberg church.

On top of all the rest, the Roman Catholics had lost the plot in other key ways. Salvation could be purchased. Which was an overflow of all the other corruption the Church had allowed in.

The most important thing to come from the Reformation was this: **salvation is by grace alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone**.

Remember the Five Solas?

- *Sola Scriptura* — Scripture alone
- *Sola Fide* — faith alone
- *Solus Christus* — Christ alone
- *Sola Gratia* — grace alone
- *Soli Deo Gloria* — for the glory of God alone

That is what they were fighting to recover — against a foe that would burn or otherwise put to death any it could catch.

## What We Owe the Reformers

We owe much to the men and women of our armed forces. Their noble sacrifice is why we are free today. But the task of these reformers was no less noble, and in some ways even larger. In the same way we remember D-Day, Veterans Day, and Memorial Day, we should remember Reformation Day — perhaps even more. We owe as much to a handful of men for freedom from corrupt religious expression as we owe to all our armed forces for freedom from political tyranny.

Sola Scriptura was a hard-fought, costly birthright that we must continue to fight to protect.

## Sola Scriptura and Authority

I'm not one to get into politics — other than to point out that all things are subject to God's sovereignty — but Sola Scriptura has political implications.

The Bible is the Word of God written down for us. It is the greatest accumulation of God's commands, instructions, and wisdom. As such, it is the greatest representative of God's authority on earth. Any other authority — kings, presidents, pastors, city council members, teachers, professors, school administrators, bosses, popes, cardinals, bishops — is secondary to Scripture. All authority comes from God through Scripture. Any other authority on earth is secondary.

The donkey and the elephant aren't sovereign — God is. The pope isn't sovereign — God is. The pope doesn't speak on behalf of God any differently than any pastor does on Sunday morning. Speaking on behalf of God is the Bible's job.

Our passage today said:

> *"All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness, so that the servant of God may be thoroughly equipped for every good work."*
> — 2 Timothy 3:16–17

Teaching. Rebuking. Correcting. Training. Fully equipping. These are some of the things Scripture does for us.

Where we are weak, it strengthens. Where we have wandered and strayed, it rebukes and corrects. Where we are ignorant, it trains. So that we can be fully equipped.

A Christian who doesn't spend time in the Word is like an Alaskan wilderness hiker who forgets to pack food.

## Carl McCunn: A Parable of the Unprepared

In 1981, a man named Carl McCunn wanted to hike and photograph a remote section of the Alaskan wilderness. He was generally regarded as a pretty good planner, but he had his absent-minded moments. He planned for everything — as a photographer he made sure he had all the film he needed, and the ability to develop it. He planned for animal attacks and hunting. He thought he had it all covered. He even had 1,400 pounds of supplies.

But it turned out he never confirmed his plans for a flight out with the pilot who had dropped him off. The pilot thought Carl had other arrangements; Carl thought the pilot was coming back. By November he was fainting. By December he was dead — a lonely figure in the middle of the Alaskan wilderness. One of the last things he wrote was a note to his father.

*[Note: Carl McCunn's story is confirmed. He was an American wildlife photographer who flew into the wilderness near the Coleen River, northeast of Fairbanks, in March 1981 with 1,400 pounds of supplies. He died around late November/early December 1981 after failing to confirm a pickup with his bush pilot. His final journal entries included a letter to his father about developing his film. The pastor says "one of the last things he wrote was a plea for God to forgive him his mistakes" — the primary sources document his letter to his father and diary entries, but a specific "plea for God to forgive him his mistakes" has not been confirmed in accessible sources. Flagged.]*

For the believer, the Bible is more than enough to meet almost every need — short of physical hunger and fellowship, it meets all our needs. It is our authority, and a love letter from God.
