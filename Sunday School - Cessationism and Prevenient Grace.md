## Part One: Cessationism — Have the Sign Gifts Ceased?

### The Last Recorded Miracle

Does anyone know what the last recorded miracle in Scripture is, in terms of year — and where to find it?

Acts 28:7–10.

> *"In the neighborhood of that place were lands belonging to the chief man of the island, named Publius, who received us and entertained us hospitably for three days. It happened that the father of Publius lay sick with fever and dysentery. And Paul visited him and prayed, and putting his hands on him, healed him. And when this had taken place, the rest of the people on the island who had diseases also came and were cured. They also honored us greatly, and when we were about to sail, they put on board whatever we needed."*
> — Acts 28:7–10 (ESV)

*[Note: The pastor dates Acts 28:7–10 to 58 AD. The scholarly consensus places Paul's shipwreck on Malta and these healings during the winter of 59–60 AD, as part of the voyage to Rome. The 58 AD date is off by approximately one to two years. See: BibleStudy.org Book of Acts Timeline; BibleRef.com Acts 28.]*

From that point until roughly 90 AD, when the last book of the New Testament was written on the island of Patmos, there are no further miracles recorded.

*[**CORRECTION — IMPORTANT**: The pastor said "until roughly 90 AD when **Paul** writes the book of Revelation on the island of Patmos." This is a significant factual error. The Book of Revelation was written by the **Apostle John**, not Paul. John explicitly identifies himself as the author (Revelation 1:1, 1:4, 1:9). Paul died approximately 64–67 AD under Nero. Revelation is traditionally dated to approximately 95 AD during the reign of Emperor Domitian. These two men, two deaths, and two dates should not be confused. This error should not be repeated in preaching or teaching.]*

In fact, the only New Testament books that record miracles are the Gospels, Acts, and 1 Corinthians. First Corinthians is among the earlier books written.

*[Note: The claim is largely accurate but should be qualified. 2 Corinthians 12:12 also references Paul's miraculous works: "The signs of a true apostle were performed among you with utmost patience, with signs and wonders and mighty works" (ESV). Hebrews 2:4 similarly references God "bearing witness by signs and wonders and various miracles." These books do not record miracles in narrative form, but they do reference them.]*

Nothing in the rest of the New Testament records miracles happening on demand — no men going around to sickbeds, healing everyone they touched and prayed for. Why is that?

### 1 Corinthians 13:8–10 — When the Gifts Cease

> *"Love never ends. As for prophecies, they will pass away; as for tongues, they will cease; as for knowledge, it will pass away. For we know in part and we prophesy in part, but when the perfect comes, the partial will pass away."*
> — 1 Corinthians 13:8–10 (ESV)

I had to dig into the Greek here to make sure I was being precise. There are three gifts mentioned as coming to an end, and each has its own Greek word describing *how* it ends.

**Tongues** (ESV: "will cease") — the Greek word is *pauō* in the middle voice. The middle voice indicates the action happens to itself — tongues stop of their own accord. This is the distinctive grammatical argument cessationists point to: unlike prophecy and knowledge, which are said to be *abolished*, tongues *cease themselves*.

*[Note: The pastor says this means "stop on their own, and never begin again." The middle voice of pauō does support "stop of themselves" — this is an accurate and standard cessationist exegetical point. However, "never begin again" goes beyond what the grammar strictly states; that is a theological inference from the passage, not a lexical meaning of pauō.]*

**Knowledge** (ESV: "will pass away") — the Greek is *katargeō*. The pastor describes this as meaning "erode" or "be slowly washed away."

*[Note: This characterization is imprecise. Katargeō means "to render inoperative," "to abolish," "to bring to nothing" — a more decisive cessation than "erode." However, the pastor's broader point is theologically interesting and not without basis: he argues that accumulated theological and biblical knowledge has diminished over the centuries since the apostolic era, and will only be fully restored in glory. That is a defensible theological claim; it just doesn't rest on the lexical meaning of katargeō itself.]*

**Prophecy** (ESV: "will pass away") — also *katargeō*. This is sometimes rendered "be done away with," "abolished," or "rendered inoperative." "Condemned into inactivity" (as the pastor puts it) is a fair characterization of the force of the word.

### "When the Perfect Comes"

> *"When the perfect comes, the partial will pass away."*
> — 1 Corinthians 13:10 (ESV)

There are two partials: we *know in part* and we *prophesy in part*. So the "perfect" (*teleion* in Greek — complete, mature, whole) has a twofold fulfillment.

**Regarding prophecy**: When was prophecy complete? When the last book of the New Testament was finished — Revelation, written approximately 90–95 AD. At that point we had the full, complete revelation God was giving us on earth. The canon closed.

**Regarding knowledge**: That same point represents the high-water mark of apostolic knowledge. Over the subsequent centuries, theological understanding has been imperfectly preserved and transmitted. In glory, what has been obscured will be fully recovered, and what God wants us to know that was not given to us here will be revealed.

Something worth noting: in heaven, I believe we will all understand one another perfectly. So tongues — in the sense of communication across barriers — may be present there as well.

*[Note: The interpretation of "the perfect" as referring to the completion of the biblical canon is the standard cessationist position. Other mainstream interpretations include: (1) the Second Coming of Christ; (2) the maturation or full growth of the church. This is a genuinely contested exegetical point, and thoughtful Christians hold different views. The cessationist reading is defensible and consistent with the rest of the session's theology.]*

### Miracles Today — Soft Cessationism

I've seen miracles. My car making it to Miami — that was a miracle, absolutely. We are all aware of some miracles that have happened.

But that wasn't a result of me. God didn't give me the "magical mechanic's touch." I can't go around touching broken cars and have them start working. Every other time my vehicle has broken down, I've had to pick up the wrenches myself — or pay someone else to fix it.

What I mean is this: God is capable of doing *anything*. He could make me speak in tongues right now if he wanted. He could speak in my ear as he did with the prophets of old and give me a new book of Proverbs.

But those macro-level gifts — the *sign* gifts — don't seem to be distributed anymore. When he acts, it is *him* working, not me. It is not a gift I possess. Individual miracles are gifts God gives in specific situations — rare, precious, and always with a purpose.

Consider my mother's arthritis. Could you imagine her carrying another twenty years of arthritis damage while helping raise three grandchildren? Or my car — I was struggling and questioning whether I should go to Haiti. God wanted to reassure me I was following his path, so he kept my car running when it shouldn't have been able to. He gave a gift for a purpose.

This is what I would describe as the "fourth wave" position: God is capable of doing anything he chooses. He could still distribute the sign gifts if he wanted. But based on Scripture, history, and what we see around us, he doesn't seem to be giving those gifts in the way he gave them to the apostles.

*[Note: "Fourth wave" is not a standard theological classification. The recognized movements are generally: First Wave (classical Pentecostalism, early 1900s), Second Wave (Charismatic Renewal, 1960s), Third Wave (Signs and Wonders movement, 1980s–90s, associated with C. Peter Wagner and John Wimber). The position described here — God can do anything, but the apostolic sign gifts are not routinely distributed today — is more precisely called **soft cessationism** or **open-but-cautious cessationism**. The theological-map.md in this project identifies this congregation's position as soft cessationist.]*

God heals my mother of arthritis. Does that mean Bob has the gift of healing? If Bob had that gift, wouldn't everyone he prayed for be healed? Wouldn't Lois have had her lungs healed? But that wasn't God's will. We asked God for a miracle, and God said no. We accepted it. God can still heal. We should absolutely ask. But it depends on God — not on Bob, or Will, or Greg, or Ken.

## Part Two: Eternal Security — Against Arminianism

One more difference between our theology and Tozer's: Tozer was what you would call an Arminian. He believed that through effort and diligence, a person could by their own power continue in God.

*[Note: This characterization of Tozer is somewhat reductive. Tozer was theologically aligned with the Christian and Missionary Alliance, which has Wesleyan-Arminian leanings. However, Tozer strongly opposed any notion that human effort alone was sufficient — he was deeply concerned about the shallowness of easy-believism and cheap grace. A more precise statement is that Tozer held to conditional security: that genuine believers could, through persistent and willful apostasy, forfeit salvation. The description of Arminianism as believing one can "in your own power" follow God would be rejected by most Arminian theologians, who also insist on the necessity of grace.]*

If we were capable of that in our own power, we wouldn't have needed Christ to die. And if God gives us such a massive gift, wouldn't he sustain it?

The Arminian says: be careful, or you can lose it.

But Scripture says:

> *"My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand."*
> — John 10:27–28 (ESV)

What does "perish" mean there? Is it talking about physical death? It has to mean hell. So he gives us eternal life, we will never go to hell, and no one can pluck us out of God's hand.

Unless you are stronger than God, once you are saved, you remain saved — until the day you stand face to face with Christ and he no longer needs to maintain it for you.

Of course no one is stronger than God. No one, and nothing, is stronger than the one who saved us and is actively sustaining our salvation until we reach glory.

Now we should put in effort — not because we fear damnation without it, but out of love and gratitude for what Christ did. It is not duty. It is not obligation. It is *joy* to follow Christ's example. Not a chore — an opportunity to identify with him. Because we love God, we want to be different. God will help us in that. But our effort does not save or damn us. Our efforts, when directed toward God and commanded by God, are supported by God — and we are drawn closer to him.

### On Tozer

By the end of Tozer's life, he is quoted as saying there wasn't a church left on the continent he hadn't offended. He wasn't far off the mark. Whenever he saw something he believed was contrary to Scripture, he confronted it — hard and publicly. He died loving Christ and disillusioned with the church.

*[Note: This is a widely circulated description of Tozer's latter years, but the exact source of the "hadn't offended" quote has not been verified. It may be paraphrased from a secondary source. Flagged for future verification.]*

## Part Three: Chapter 1 — Following Hard After God

*(Tozer,* The Pursuit of God*, Chapter 1)*

Did you catch what Tozer said on the first page — the idea of prevenient grace? Here is what he wrote:

> *"Christian theology teaches the doctrine of prevenient grace, which briefly stated means this, that before a man can seek God, God must first have sought the man. Before a sinful man can think a right thought of God, there must have been a work of enlightenment done within him; imperfect it may be, but a true work nonetheless, and the secret cause of all desiring and seeking and praying which may follow. We pursue God because, and only because, He has first put an urge within us that spurs us to the pursuit."*
> — A.W. Tozer, *The Pursuit of God*, Chapter 1 ("Following Hard After God")

*[Note: Quote confirmed accurate. Source: A.W. Tozer, The Pursuit of God, Chapter 1 — verified across Project Gutenberg (gutenberg.org/files/25141), World Invisible (worldinvisible.com), and the Society of Evangelical Arminians. The pastor paraphrased the central definition faithfully: "before a man can seek after God, God must have first sought the man."]*

Does Scripture back this up? Can you think of any verses?

The first one that comes to mind:

> *"We love because he first loved us."*
> — 1 John 4:19 (ESV)

It is clear that God does the pursuing. This is part of why the church is called Christ's Bride — the bridegroom pursues the bride. Tozer put it this way: we have the *capacity* to follow Christ, but not the *power* to do so.

### 1 Corinthians 2:14–16 — The Natural Man

> *"The person without the Spirit does not accept the things that come from the Spirit of God but considers them foolishness, and cannot understand them because they are discerned only through the Spirit. The person with the Spirit makes judgments about all things, but such a person is not subject to merely human judgments, for 'Who has known the mind of the Lord so as to instruct him?' But we have the mind of Christ."*
> — 1 Corinthians 2:14–16 (NIV)

*[Note: Quoted from the NIV. ESV renders v. 14: "The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned."]*

Another translation says "the things of the Lord are foolishness to the natural man." The *natural man* is the man apart from Christ — as God made us by nature, fallen and without the Spirit. Christ's prevenient love means he loved us first, rescued us first, did a work in us so that we could even have a chance to respond to him.

It is as if we have blinders on, and Christ is light — but we cannot see the light because the blinders block it. So Christ, because of his great love, removes those blinders so we can truly see and follow.

### Ephesians 2:1–10 — Dead Men Don't Choose

> *"As for you, you were dead in your transgressions and sins, in which you used to live when you followed the ways of this world and of the ruler of the kingdom of the air, the spirit who is now at work in those who are disobedient. All of us also lived among them at one time, gratifying the cravings of our flesh and following its desires and thoughts. Like the rest, we were by nature deserving of wrath. But because of his great love for us, God, who is rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ even when we were dead in transgressions — it is by grace you have been saved. And God raised us up with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms in Christ Jesus, in order that in the coming ages he might show the incomparable riches of his grace, expressed in his kindness to us in Christ Jesus. For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith — and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God — not by works, so that no one can boast. For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do."*
> — Ephesians 2:1–10 (NIV)

*[Note: Quoted from the NIV. The congregation should be aware the ESV renders "For we are God's handiwork" as "For we are his workmanship."]*

What can a dead man do? What can he see? What can he choose? But for the prevenient grace of God, we would still be that natural man — that dead man. That corpse.

### The Longing God Puts in Us

Tozer was once asked: if he had an hour to share the gospel, what would he say? His answer was that he would spend fifty-five minutes explaining original sin — the weight of our sin debt, how lost and hopeless things are apart from Christ — and then in the final five minutes, give the gospel.

*[Note: This anecdote is widely attributed to Tozer but **has not been verified**. A similar "fifty-five minutes / five minutes" framework is more reliably attributed to **Francis Schaeffer** — though in Schaeffer's version, the emphasis is on *listening* to the person rather than on explaining original sin. The attribution of this specific version to Tozer has not surfaced in a verifiable primary or secondary source. Flagged — should not be repeated in preaching as a confirmed Tozer quote.]*

Scripture says so much about this. We could go into Romans 8 and 9 — but that is another time.

We only pursue God because he gave us the urge to do so. I always think of David when I think of a true, visceral longing for God. He wrote:

> *"O God, you are my God; earnestly I seek you; my soul thirsts for you; my flesh faints for you, as in a dry and weary land where there is no water."*
> — Psalm 63:1 (ESV)

*[Note: The pastor did not cite a specific Psalm here, only quoting from memory ("He wrote in the psalms"). This is Psalm 63:1 — a psalm of David, written when he was in the wilderness of Judah. The ESV wording is confirmed.]*

And Tozer himself quotes Psalm 42:

> *"As a deer pants for flowing streams, so pants my soul for you, O God."*
> — Psalm 42:1 (ESV)

*[Note: ESV confirmed. The KJV, which Tozer used, reads: "As the hart panteth after the water brooks, so panteth my soul after thee, O God."]*

Have you ever felt that longing? That pull — like a moth to a flame, or iron to a magnet? When God tugs on a soul, there is no resisting it. There are days I feel it more than others. Sometimes I allow the complexities of life to crowd it out — my stresses, my interests, my concerns consume my focus.

But does that honor God? In light of what God has done for us, where should our focus be?

I've noticed: the more focused on the Lord I become, the less these other things concern me. There have been times when small things rocked me badly, and times when large things passed with peace — and the difference was always where my focus was. When fixed on Christ, the world around us fades, because of the sheer greatness of Christ.

It is a little like Gothic architecture. You remember Notre Dame? When you walk in, the sheer scale of the building is designed to make you feel small — to remind us how vast God is, and how small we are.
