Parable of the Lost Sheep (Luke 15)

Were going to be in Luke 15 this week, luke 15. Ifyou've brought your bibles, and I hope you have. Please turn there.

While youre turning there, Our people group of the week, is our denomination, her leadership, and pastors.

I watched our denominations annual meeting this week, and was a little discourage, and a little encouraged. It was discouraging because some philosophies that are against scripture being supported and embraced. We have churches and pastors blind to certain sins, claiming to be the only ones really able to see. We took a couple of good steps – made resolutions against Critical race theory, intersectionality, and abortion that while non-binding, send a clear message of where we stand. So that part was encouraging.

I'm concerned about the new president of our denomination. He's either weak on inerrancy or hasn't thought things through fully. Both are concerning for their own reasons.

Someone asked me this week if things were bad enough that we needed to find a new denomination. But I don't think they are. The SBC is a formed in such a way that it can drift a little, and be pulled back to its scriptural foundations. Its happened before, and worse than any perceived drifting we have had this time, and a group of Godly men formed a group that fought hard to pull us back towards orthodoxy. The did a thing we now call the conservative resurgence that pulled us back.

So who would like to take a moment and pray for the southern Baptist convention?

Any way, it made me think a little bit of the parable of the lost sheep, lets read. Luke 15, starting in vs:1-7

Its important to point out that a Shepherd in this time period was responsible for each individual sheep in his care. If one turned up missing, or was eaten by a predator. If one grew sick by some preventable cause, the shepherd was responsible. Your average sheep was worth about the same as a shepherds daily wages. So it was a loss, but not an insurmountable loss. But still. A shepherd had to be on guard. A number of years ago, I brought home four young lambs that were really neat looking. They had spots kind of like a cow might. Some were black and white, while some where white and brown. Either way they were quite cool. This was back when I had Grace. She was incredibly protective and loyal. She'd protect me or die trying. Her intensity made my extended family anxious. But she was a great dog for me. Any way. I lifted the sheep out of the dog crate in the bed of the truck, and set them down on the ground. Grace was there watching, but was up under the truck. I couldn't see her warnings. Any way. After I set each of the four sheep down, I turned around, and closed the tailgate, and by the time I turned back around all four sheep were bleeding out on the ground. She had protected me from the sheep. Point is, a single predator can devastate a flock in no time at all. In less than a minute, if I were a bible times shepherd, in less than a minute more than half a weeks pay would be gone. Just one dog. Not even a wolf. Not a bear. Not a lion. Just a dog. So shepherds had to be exceptionally watchful. They had to sleep lightly and listen to their flock or a single days pay, could turn into a week, or several weeks worth of pay. Every sheep has its own unique personality. We have this one – anna – that comes up to you to have her head scratched every time you go in the pasture. Others at 200lbs will still try and jump in your feed bucket.

We have one we called Trouble, that wants to be near you, but doesn't want to be touched by you. She has no problem nipping you if you turn your back though, and then trying to pretend it wasn't her.

By contrast we had one, Razzle, who was incredibly bold and playful. His life goal was to play with you, like he would play with another ram – by running full speed into your backside. Occasionally he was successful as my uncle can attest. But most of the time we were able to manage that crazy ram by keeping a fence between us and him. Another sheep we had at this point in time was called frazzle. She was really friendly, but didn't like to be touched. So this one particular day, she had gotten into my mothers tomato garden. A sheep can make quick work of a tomato garden. So we decided to catch her. She wouldn't leave. But she wouldn't let us near her either. So we grabbed a section of rope, and held it at about her chest height. So when she'd try to run between us she would crash into the rope, and maybe we could catch her. However, she willed free first, and then was wary to our methods, and wouldn't come near either of us or the rope again.

Took us an hour to catch her. point is that the shepherd was also seeking to restore the relationship with this sheep as much as he was desiring not to lose his days pay. The closest analog I can think of is losing a cat or dog – something with a unique personality, that you'd cared for its entire life – perhaps months or years. So it is with us and Christ. When one of Christ's sheep is lost, he (19:10) has come to seek and save the lost. Once we caught frazzle the sheep for the third and final time, we rejoiced. Certainly not for the same reasons Christ rejoices after finding one of his lost sheep, but still we were rejoicing. Out of breath, soaked with sweat and rejoicing.

Lets read our passage. Luke 15:1-7.

I want you to take notice of a certain noun in this passage. Whose translation says the 99 were left in the wilderness? Can you read that for me? That's a poor translation. In the greek, the word used is "ἔρημος". When I think of wilderness, I think of the unmanaged parts of starkey park. I think of the amazon rainforest. I think of the jungles of Vietnam. Unpassable, unfriendly, unkempt places loaded with predators, snakes, spiders and other things that want to kill or eat you. At this point in the story my mind always wanders to the 99 being left in peril. In my mind, the 99 were always in more danger than the 1 that wandered off. However, Jesus took care of them by his word choice. If we spoke Hebrew we would have understood that they weren't being left in wilderness the way we think of it. The sheep were being left in a safe place. You see ἔρημος means "to be in a state of isolation, desolation, or deserted. To be in an isolated area, unfrequented by visitors, abandoned, empty & desolate. We know that based upon its use, this area is suitable for sustaining sheep. Frequently in the new testament sheep are left in ἔρημος. If my sheep are any indicator, the 99 were sitting in this empty field that is relatively safe, heavily laden with grass munching to their little hearts content. This calls to mind the first part of the 23rd psalm: "The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want. He makes me lie down in green pastures. He leads me beside still waters. He restores my soul. He leads me in paths of righteousness for his name's sake." The shepherds made them lie down because the sheep weren't smart enough to stop eating the grass on their own. They can founder if given too much fresh lush grass. So when the shepherds would find themselves in a river delta, or some other place with especially nice grass, they would have to make the sheep stop eating for fear of founder, or another disease called bloat. Both of which can now be treated (Sometimes), were once totally fatal. The point is that the good shepherd protects his sheep from their own stupidity. Many of you have been that sheep – God has sought you and protected you when you were intentionally doing things you now find to be foolish. We have heard each other's testimonies and we know most or maybe all of us have been in this situation. Drinking or drugs, living for anything but the Lord; but he sought us and brought us to himself. This first story represents then the salvation of the wayward brother. You may have heard this, but when a shepherd has to hunt down a wayward sheep, sometimes in order to protect the sheep he has to break their leg, and carry the sheep until she imprints on him and follows him around gladly. The sheep of course choose this for itself. Is not a broken leg better than being eaten by a coyote? Or mauled and toyed with by a lion (then eaten), or falling into a crack or crevase where one would slowly die of dehydration or starvation? In the end having your leg broken is a mercy if it teaches us to follow more closely to our savior.

We have a bottle baby named angel. Her mother rejected her the day she was born. Left her to die. So my mother took angel in, and bottle fed her. Kept her alive, and thriving for months. Now shes back to living outside, but she

Another thing to point out is a twofold point. Firstly its Jesus that is taking the initiative in seeking the Lost. Calvinism gets a bad reputation from those who argue that because Jesus seeks the lost, they don't have to. I attended a church like that for years. We all know someone who is an overbalanced Calvinist; and yet on this point Calvinism has a point: Jesus seeks the Lost. He came to earth born into humble circumstances, in a feed trough surrounded by barn animals. Lived by meager means, never owned a home, never had a 401k or health insurance. He eventually died a death he did not deserve to save a people who couldn't save themselves. Further, today, apart from God moving in our hearts we don't even have an interest in the things of the Lord. Scripture says in 1st Corinthians 2:14 "The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him". Natural man, when you study its context you will find that the phrase means one for whom the holy spirit does not interpret things of the Lord. This is nothing new though, this doctrine is in our Baptist faith and message. This is a contrast to the beliefs of the Pharisees of the day. You see they (in general) believed that they shouldn't proselytize non jews to their sects. Some did thus the exhortation from Jesus in Mt 23 about Pharisees who were aggressively proselytizing their sect, for the purpose of growing their sect, and not for the purpose of expanding the kingdom of God.
Secondly, The apogee of the story then is two fold: the return of the sheep, and the triumphant rejoicing in its rescue. Jesus is saying both in parable and by way of direct statement that it pleases God to seek and receive sinners. Something they had been criticizing him for earlier in the chapter.

Based on the rest of the passage, you might liken the feeling a shepherd has for his lost sheep, to the feeling you have when you lose your wedding ring. Any of you ever done that?. I was working with my brother-in-law for a property management company, and at the end of this stagnant, smelly, nasty canal there was a busted dock, and what seemed to be sandbags in the water. He and I took turns dealing with nasty things like this, and so in order to "clean" the property the dock and sand bags needed pulled out of the water, and hauled off to the dump. In order to take care of these two things one or both of us needed to get in the awful smelling muck swamp of a dead end canal. It was his turn, but we tag teamed the dock, and he offered to get the sandbags out. So he donned his gloves and tried to lift. He couldn't get a good enough grip from above the water, and so in the interest of doing a good job, he ducked his head under water and picked up the nasty bag of stinky filth. Still it had sat long enough that it was heavier than he, or as it happened, he and I could lift out. He told me years later that he ended up getting really sick from this particular job, and in hindsight it was the last job we ever did together. Any way We eventually used a 4x4 as a fulcrum and used the truck to lift the bag out of the water. We left it on the ground until we were ready to go home (hoping the more the bag dried, the more manageable the bag would be). But even then it was too heavy. I ended up driving onto some of our families land, tying the bag to one of those "this road has been adopted by" signs and driving off out from underneath it. Any way, the point is the following day, I get a call from my brother-in-law. He was frantic. "Did you find my wedding ring any where?". I hadn't. He and I drove back out to the work site, and began searching everywhere. Not in the house, or the garage, or the yard… Guess that means we have to go back in the canal. I sat on the bank (so as not to disturb the water), he hopped in and grabbed his gloves from the day before along with a mask and snorkel. He donned the mask, and started putting his gloves on when he found the ring. It had been in his glove all along. Relieved and stinking of bog water, I made him ride home in the back of the truck.

Sheep were a shepherds future security. They provided his food, food for his family, and income. So to lose a sheep was to lose part of what safe guarded a shepherd and his families future. Thus he was quite motivated to recover his lost sheep. The Sheep you see represents us, we are something of value – indeed as the church we are betrothed to Christ, selected by God the Father, pursued & purchased by God the son, and indwelt by God the Holy Spirit. While we didn't have any value on our own, God's purchasing of us, through the life and death of his son, gave those of us his son saves immeasurable value. Our heavenly father spent the blood of his son, to vanquish sin and Satan. Giving us salvation, freedom from sin, eternity in heaven, and the indwelling of the Holy Spirit. We lowly sinners are a messy lot, of poor stock from poor families, we deserved hell, and we were given heaven. We deserved death, but were given life. Praise God we didn't get what we deserved. Instead of a bag of feed, we gain eternal life. Instead of being left to fend for ourselves, and die on our own in the wilds we are as a church betrothed to the King of Kings and Lord of Lords. Instead of eternity in Hell, we can have eternal life.

It strikes me that we aren't told why the sheep is lost. Maybe it runs off. Maybe a predator chased it. Maybe it just didn't hear what was going on. Maybe the conditions that lead to it becoming lost were no faut of the sheep. The convention this week might be a stumbling block. One way or the other. You and I need to make sure we aren't the cause of a little sheep being separated from the flock. A dear friend of mine used to tell a story about how he showed up at a church one time, because one of his friends told him it would be a good place to meet girls. He shows up wearing a hat, and the first thing someone does was shame him into taking it off.

Now on the one hand, its true that the bible does say for men to not wear their hats in church (1 cor 11:7), but its equally true that it was more important for my lost friend to hear the gospel, than whether he wore a hat or not. After he was confronted, he barged out of the church, and went home. He didn't darken the door of a church again for years and years.
Likewise, I knew two young ladies One of whom struggled with a medical condition that caused her hands to ache. She was young – maybe 21 or 22 at the time. This one particular Sunday a hurricane was coming, or had passed through and her hands were particularly achey. Her younger sister was present this particular morning, which was unusual as her sister lived several hours away. During the service, the pain of the older sisters hands was causing tears in her eyes. The younger sister was moved with compassion and began to rub her older sisters hands and wrists. Discretely, low in the pew so as not to be a distraction. At the end of the service, an older woman came up to the two girls – whom she had never met before, and didn't know whether they were lost or not – both of whom were sitting next to me and declared that "God" had told her to tell these two that they were a horrible distraction, and shame on them for behaving in such an ungodly way in church. Needless to say that was the last time either of them attended that church. Be careful not to be a force for division when it comes to the gospel. Because afterall where in scripture does it say a non believer can't wear a hat? Where does it say that we MUST wear our suits and ties to church or in the pulpit? Let me tell you it doesn't! Those things are constructs of culture and not of Christ. I'm not saying that we should go to church in hole filled pajamas, or immodestly – but I am saying that people should come as they are, and expect to find no judgement for having done so! Heh. That reminds me of another story. I met this one particular woman I had been invited out to lunch with a group of people, and most of them I knew, but not all of them. Any way. This one woman seated across from me starting going on about how she attended a church this one time and the preacher had the audacity to preach in denim jeans. She was appalled. She felt like it was being too cavalier with the pulpit, that it was a sign of disrespect to God. A dear friend and former pastor was dining with us, and he asked her "what did Jesus wear to preach?" She was at a loss for words. Jesus of course preached in clothes that were culturally relevant to the day. May have even been a little behind the fashion curve. I pointed out gently that I preach in blue jeans nearly every time I preach, and not out of disrespect for God, but out of concern for the lost. If wearing blue jeans makes someone off the street feel more comfortable, and because of that they stay through a sermon, and hear the Gospel, then I am all for wearing blue jeans. Likewise if a tie is required for the gospel, then, lets all wear ties. Its not what one wears, or eats that defiles a man, its what stems from his heart. While seeking to honor God is not sinful, it can become sinful if we project our own hang ups onto other people. If you want to honor God good – you do well. But let other brother wear his blue jeans, or his t-shirt, and let him honor God in his own way. Don't be a stumbling block like the lady was to my dear friend, and don't be the one that drives a young man away from church over a hat. God readily receives sinners, whatever their state, their dress, their sins. Far better it is for us to be the one being put out for the gospel. That's why we spend hundreds of hours preparing for things like VBS. If even one person is impacted for the gospel, then its all worth while. I'm sure that's part of why all of you do the things you do for the church. That's part of why willie mows, and part of why dottie does all the secretarial stuff. Its part of why Greg sings, and Cindy teaches the kids. Its part of why all of you that bring food, bring food. For the fun and fellowship yes, but to hopefully spark conversations that lead to the gospel. People hearing the gospel is one of the most important things we do.

The rest of this chapter, I could preach the same message, and just change the details. The lost coin was likely a brides wedding coin – like a wedding ring in our culture. Or the lost son. Or a denomination that has drifted off its course. Its worth saving. Its worth fighting for. Its worth the hunt to get it back.

Maybe you are like that lost sheep. Run, or been driven off. Alone in the wilds of the world. My friend your savior is here waiting. Today is the day! You may think it was your plan to be here Today, but indeed God directs our steps. God has brought you here Today, maybe you even are aware of that, maybe its been going on for a long time. If you have never prayed and asked Jesus into your heart Today is the day. Come see me , or my dad who leads the singing, or Dottie, or Carol or any of us.

Gospel.

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A raw transcript with genuinely excellent pastoral illustration and a textually honest reading of ἔρημος, but two fatal structural failures — an incomplete gospel and an unfinished draft — prevent this from functioning as a sermon.

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test:** PASS. The sermon's thesis — Christ seeks and saves the lost — arises from the parable's own argument. The connection to Luke 19:10 ("to seek and to save the lost") is organic and appropriate.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**TWO FLAGS TRIGGERED. Score is conceptually capped at 69, though the raw score falls below that threshold independently.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 15/25**

The ἔρημος exegesis (the 99 were left in an *isolated safe place*, not a dangerous wilderness) is the sermon's exegetical jewel — genuinely illuminating, properly sourced, and pastorally productive (leads to Psalm 23). The Luke 19:10 connection is apt. But: the context of Luke 15 (the Pharisees' complaint that Jesus receives sinners — the passage's own reason for existing) is barely acknowledged. The rejoicing in heaven (vv. 6-7, the actual climax of the parable) receives almost no treatment. The sermon is textually honest but incomplete in its coverage of the passage's argument.

FCF: Named but undeveloped — the lost sheep represents the wayward sinner whom Christ seeks. This is stated rather than searched.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18**

The Trinitarian statement near the end ("selected by God the Father, pursued and purchased by God the Son, indwelt by God the Holy Spirit") is strong and confessionally precise. 1 Corinthians 2:14 on the natural man is well applied to the doctrinal brief about Calvinism. The shepherd-as-Christ connection to Luke 19:10 is organically derived. However: no atonement theology; the "blood of his son" appears once without development; the "broken leg" illustration (controversial in the tradition) is presented without qualification.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 5/15**

The gospel is not proclaimed — it is placeholder. Scattered gospel-adjacent language ("we deserved hell and were given heaven") appears but is applied to existing believers' testimonies, not proclaimed as good news to the lost. The one evangelistic moment at the end ("Today is the day") has no theological content attached. *Synagogue Test: This sermon could be preached with minimal offense in a Jewish context — the cross is absent.*

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15**

The stumbling-block section (hat story, hand-rubbing story, blue jeans argument) is specific, vivid, and addresses a real failure in church culture. The SBC opening frames the sermon as a corporate application. But: the conscience is mostly addressed corporately (don't drive sheep away) rather than individually (are *you* the lost sheep?). The "maybe you are like that lost sheep" at the very end is the only direct personal address, and it comes after the sentence has already begun to trail off.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8**

"Come see me, or my dad." That is the full exhortation. No specific call to the believer. No call to repentance with content. The invitation exists; the response it calls for has no theological anchor.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 4/9**

The SBC convention material opens the sermon and occupies considerable space before reaching the text. The sheep farm illustrations are numerous, vivid, and sometimes run past the point they're serving. The unfinished sentence about "angel the bottle baby" breaks the manuscript's coherence. The sermon does not build toward the rejoicing-in-heaven climax that the text itself builds toward.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

The sheep farming illustrations are the sermon's undeniable strength. Grace the dog and the four lambs. Razzle the ram, Frazzle the runaway ewe, Trouble the nipper, Anna who wants her head scratched. These are native — no preacher without a sheep farm could have preached them. The broken-leg-to-imprint illustration is striking and theologically interesting. The wedding ring/canal story is vivid, though long. These illustrations *will not leave the congregation.*

**8. Sermonic Force — 2/5**

Raw transcript. The voice is authentic — this is a man who knows sheep, loves his people, and preaches out of lived experience. But the manuscript reads as dictation, not sermon. Unfinished sentences. No cadence variation. The preacherly moments are present in the sheep stories but not in the theological turns.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The ἔρημος exegesis** (p. 2 of transcript). Genuinely good Greek work. The point that the 99 were left in a *safe pasture*, not a dangerous wilderness, resolves a common misreading and leads naturally into Psalm 23. This is the kind of exegetical unlock that congregations remember.

2. **The sheep farm illustrations.** Razzle, Frazzle, Grace, Trouble, Anna — these are not borrowed illustrations. They are native to the preacher's life, specific in detail, and theologically productive. The broken-leg-to-imprint illustration is striking. These carry the sermon.

3. **The stumbling-block application.** The hat story and the hand-rubbing story are specific, locally grounded, and directly derived from the parable's context (Pharisees criticizing Jesus for receiving sinners). The application "don't be a Pharisee who drives sheep away" lands because it has a face on it.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **No gospel.** "Gospel." is a single word at the end of the manuscript. The cross does not appear. Substitution is not named. Repentance is not given theological content. A lost sheep cannot be found by a sermon that never delivers the news. *This is the fatal wound.*

2. **The parable's climax is ignored.** Luke 15:6-7 — "Rejoice with me, for I have found my sheep that was lost. Just so, I tell you, there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety-nine righteous persons who need no repentance." This is the text's own conclusion. The sermon never gets there. The pastoral application wanders to blue jeans and denominational politics before the text's own triumphant note is struck.

3. **The SBC opening is a distraction.** The congregation is praying for the SBC convention before they know what passage they're in or why it matters. The opening creates a two-topic sermon (denominational drift + lost sheep) that are never fully integrated.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher does not seem to see that the stumbling-block section — while true and well-illustrated — functionally shifts the sermon's burden from *the lost sheep's own lostness* to *the church's responsibility for the sheep's lostness*. The parable doesn't exonerate the sheep. Neither the hat-wearer nor the girl rubbing her sister's hands are the *lost sheep* of Luke 15:1-7. The parable is about a sheep that has wandered, not a sheep that was driven away by a bad church experience. The preacher's pastoral instinct is right — don't be a stumbling block — but it is doing more work here than the text supports.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sheep farm illustrations *feel* like exposition because they are so textually grounded in the world of sheep. They are genuinely illuminating. But they are not exposition — they are illustration. The sermon spends far more time in the sheep pen than in the text, and the illustrations can mask the absence of the text's own argument.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The parable's narrative climax — the returning shepherd who *calls his friends and neighbors together to rejoice* (Luke 15:6) — is a window directly into the nature of God's joy over the saved. The heavenly party. This is the text's own emotional peak, and the sermon never arrives there. The ἔρημος exegesis opens something beautiful (the 99 safe in their pasture), but the sermon doesn't follow the narrative all the way to its end.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Write the gospel.** Remove the placeholder. Proclaim: Christ is the shepherd who left heaven's glory, came to earth, found us lost and dying, and bore the penalty our wandering deserved — on the cross. That is not a sentence; it is a paragraph. Write it. The lost person in the pew deserves more than "Come see me."

2. **End with the rejoicing.** Luke 15:7 should be the sermon's final emotional beat. "There will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents..." — the preacher's own joy at catching Frazzle the sheep three times is a beautiful bridge to this. Use it. End there.

3. **Move or tighten the SBC material.** It may work as a brief opening frame ("This week I watched something that reminded me of this parable..."), but it cannot occupy the space it does now without fragmenting the sermon.

4. **Finish the bottle-baby sentence.** Angel is a potentially powerful illustration — the lamb whose mother rejected her, fed by human hands, now living outside. That is an obvious gospel image and it's left hanging mid-sentence.

5. **Consult the polished manuscript.** The file header notes that "Sermon - Lost and Found.md" is the polished version. This evaluation should be read alongside that document. The raw transcript's value is primarily in the illustrations it preserves and the exegetical instincts it reveals.
