So any way that morning, since he wouldn't call an ambulance (other than Stroke, about the only other thing he managed to say was "don't call those pirates, I won't pay 1000$ to be driven across the street". So I helped bring him down the stairs, his balance was bad, and he kept listing to the right. We got him in the car, and Mom drove him across the street to bayonet point.

I had to stay behind, the kids needed an adult, and mom needed to be with Dad. So I went back in the house, and began thinking about all the things I knew about strokes. All the people Ive known that have had strokes, and began to be upset. We've known and know several that are really struggling with the after effects – even some who have a really hard time forming sentences years after the stroke. I realized pretty quickly that I was focusing on ME, rather than on the Lord in the midst of the storm, and so I prayed one more time, and decided to try and distract my self before I went and had to face the kids.

About that time I got a Facebook notification, and opened it on my phone and it was a post — a quote by Charles Spurgeon that says this: *"It is well to praise the Lord for His mercy when you are in health, but make sure that you do it when you are sick, for then your praise is more likely to be genuine."* *(Verified: Spurgeon, Sermon #2483, "The Objective of Christ's Death," Metropolitan Tabernacle Pulpit Vol. 42, 1896.)*

I know Brenda and Carol saw it. But it really hit home in that moment, It was as though God were saying "Will you still praise me, if I take your father, or worse, allow him to become a vegetable?".

I wish I could tell you that I started worshipping God right then, but I didn't. However I was hit with an overwhelming sense of peace that pushed out all the anxiety, and worry that I had been feeling up until that point. I knew come what may, God was in control, and whatever we faced we would be ok. That God equips you for whatever he brings you to. There is a song that I introduced dad to a few months back, that he's been practicing by a band called "Shane and Shane" the song is titled "though you slay me" and that's a line that comes right from Job 13:15, "though you slay me, still I will hope in you". *(Note: the song adapts Job 13:15 to second-person address. ESV reads: "Though he slay me, I will hope in him." The song lyric is faithful in spirit; clarify when quoting if presenting as Scripture.)* This was Job, who lost his family, who lost his belongings, his house, and basically had everything stripped away from him, and in that momen, Job said to God "though you slay me, still I will hope in you".

I'd like to take a moment and read that song to you it says "I come, God, I come, I return to the Lord The one who's broken The one who's torn me apart You struck down to bind me up You say You do it all in love That I might know You in Your suffering Though You slay me Yet I will praise You Though You take from me I will bless Your name Though You ruin me Still I will worship

In that moment, I felt that peace that passes all understanding (Philippians 4:7). I was able to pull it together and go take care of the kids.

Just before all of this, last Sunday in fact, I was talking with Dottie, and it occurred to me that there were something like ten people that either attend, or who are loved by those who do, that were having surgery or something major medical. Kathy's shoulder, dads shoulder, bills shoulder, Donna's surgery, and on and on and on. You all know who I'm talking about. We are a small body of people, but yet looking back across the year, there has been a ton of things that have happened. It kind of made me think of Paul a little bit. Open with me to 2 Corinthians 11. We will start in verse 24.

Paul has been through a lot of things. Hopefully most of us can't relate to a lot of these things, being hungry, and thirsty, afraid for your life, and the recipient of a death penalty by a cat of 9 tails multiple times. Not to mention being stoned, ship wrecked, left for dead, and lowered over the city walls covertly to escape with his life in a basket.

In light of all of that, let's read 1 Thessalonians 5:12 and following.

> 12 We ask you, brothers, to respect those who labor among you and are over you in the Lord and admonish you, 13 and to esteem them very highly in love because of their work. Be at peace among yourselves. 14 And we urge you, brothers, admonish the idle, encourage the fainthearted, help the weak, be patient with them all. 15 See that no one repays anyone evil for evil, but always seek to do good to one another and to everyone. 16 Rejoice always, 17 pray without ceasing, 18 give thanks in all circumstances; for this is the will of God in Christ Jesus for you. 19 Do not quench the Spirit. 20 Do not despise prophecies, 21 but test everything; hold fast what is good. 22 Abstain from every form of evil. 23 Now may the God of peace himself sanctify you completely, and may your whole spirit and soul and body be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ. 24 He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it.
> — 1 Thessalonians 5:12-24

IN light of all that Paul says "Rejoice always". I think this must have been what Spurgeon had in mind when he wrote the quote from the start. That we should be praising God no matter what comes. No matter the storm. No matter if you've been beaten within an inch of your life, or stoned and left for dead, or shipwrecked, or left to drift at sea for two days. No matter if your dad has a stroke and might be a vegetable for the rest of his life, no matter if your kids are struggling, or hateful, or lost. No matter if your shoulder was broken by a dog, or your child dies, or you've been diagnosed with cancer, or any of a hundred other things that are represented just by the people sitting here in this room today.
I think when our enemy shuts our mouth about how Good God is, we've lost the battle. Ultimately we know God wins the war. But in that moment, we've lost the battle. The way I see things, we are mere mortals caught up in a war between Immortal eternal God and His enemy, and his enemies armies.

We are living in the dominion of our enemy. We are stuck behind enemy lines. In a lot of way we are like the French in June of 1940. The Nazi's occupied their country, their political leaders had yielded to the germans in exchange for continuing in power, and had largely convinced the French people to capitulate as well. The French for all intents and purposes had been defeated. Until June 18 1940. An exiled 2 star general called Charles spoke to the BBC passionately, and rejected the collaboration between the French government, and the Nazis, and pleaded with the French military to reject their leaders capitulation and begin to fight back.

In the days that followed there were a few sparks of resistance. Some were met with success, and some failure. There were sparks, and those sparks ignited the nation. Within just four short years, instead of being puppets of Germany, they became a free people once more due in part to the near millions of brave men and women who fought a war of shadows for their freedom. Many of them never fired a shot, but would print patriotic newspapers and deliver them in secret, or would drag around tank tracks to make it seem like the British, or americans had come and driven across this town, or through that field in the night. Many brandished their proverbial swords too. In 1943 everyone they thought even might possibly be tied to French resistance was rounded up, and either publicly executed, or shipped off to a worse fate at the concentration camps.

But by the war's end, there were more than a million French directly fighting under de Gaulle — the fourth-largest Allied army in Europe — and Paris was retaken, and the Nazi occupation was over. *(Corrected: original draft said "3 million" — actual figure ~1.3 million. Also removed "Communists were defeated" — the French Communist Party was a core part of the Resistance, not the enemy.)*

Right now in this world we are trapped behind enemy lines, fighting a war of information against our enemy. We can give up, and go home. Or we can stand and fight. We can sit quietly by while those around us go to hell, or we can rise in resistance, and do something that rattles the very gates of hell, we can worship God. de Gaulle wrote that France would have to go through great peril, to give her shirt, her dress, her house and her soul, France would have to give up her habits and cautions, and be transformed as a nation into something new. As Christians we have been transformed into something new. I want to spend the rest of the service not lamenting our struggles – a thing we could do, we have plenty of struggles, instead I want us each to find some things to thank God for, in spite of our trials. I want us to be the resistance. To stand and fight through worship, through holiness, through reading the bible, through prayer and through evangelism.

So for the next ten minutes or so I want to spend time doing just that. Lets thank the Lord for our struggles. Lets thank him for our sicknesses. Lets thank him for the purifying effects trials of various kinds have upon our very soul. Lets thank God for allowing us to know him in his suffering.

Close in prayer.

Announcements:
Clubs are a maybe, food pantry will still happen. Praise Practice is up to dottie this week. Leaving directly for the hospital, please lock up. Dads in room 3780, and out of the ICU so visiting is a lot easier now.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### Verdict Title
**"A Tender Pastoral Heart with a Hollow Gospel Center and an Unfinished Sermon"**

---

### One-Sentence Summary
A warmly personal opening of 1 Thessalonians 5:12–24 that magnified congregational solidarity and resilient praise in suffering while leaving the gospel unarticulated, the text largely unexposed, and the sermon structurally unresolved.

---

### A. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test:** The sermon's thesis — that Christians must rejoice and give thanks to God regardless of suffering, and that this worship is itself spiritual warfare — is broadly consistent with 1 Thessalonians 5:16–18. However, the sermon never surfaces the *passage's own argument*. Verses 12–15 (church order, leadership, mutual care) and verses 19–24 (Spirit, prophecy, sanctification, God's faithfulness) receive no exposition at all. The scopus is extracted from three verses inside a twelve-verse passage, and those three verses are treated as standalone imperatives without grounding in Paul's argument or the letter's occasion.

**Authority Test:** The preacher is not editing the text or standing above it as a critic. He submits to it personally, and the introduction demonstrates genuine faith under pressure. But the preacher does not yet *open* the text — he uses it as a frame for his experience and then pivots to an extended illustration. The Word governs the preacher's heart here, but it does not yet govern the sermon.

**Result: Conditional Pass.** The main point (rejoice always, even in suffering) is present in the text. The sermon passes the gate narrowly — but only on the three-verse core. The passage's own argument is almost entirely unexplored.

---

### B. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation:** TRIGGERED. The sermon references "God equips you for whatever he brings you to," the Shane and Shane lyrics, and Philippians 4:7, but never articulates the gospel. Christ's death, resurrection, and justifying work are absent. The sermon cannot be used by an unbeliever to understand how to be saved. The Synagogue Test passes without offense — a devout Jew or Muslim could agree with virtually everything in this sermon.

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic:** TRIGGERED. "Let's thank God for our struggles" and "be the resistance through worship, holiness, Bible reading, prayer, and evangelism" are sincere but undifferentiated. No specific hearer is addressed. No conscience is cornered. The application is a general call to attitude, not a specific summons.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation:** TRIGGERED (borderline). The sermon's ending ("Let's spend ten minutes thanking the Lord") is participatory, which is a pastoral instinct worth keeping — but it is not a "therefore" moment. The congregation is invited into worship, which is good, but they are never told *why the cross makes rejoicing possible*, or *what it costs to refuse*, or *what it means for the self-righteous versus the grieving*. The "go" is soft and directionless.

**Score is capped at 69.**

### F. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Honestly, neither. It makes me think of the preacher's father, of Paul's suffering, and of the French Resistance — all in the service of a call to praise. Christ appears at the edges.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** The personal testimony creates proximity to God. But the sermon does not press the congregation into encounter — it invites them to follow the preacher's experience.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Comforted — sincerely and pastorally. Not convicted. The self-righteous, the hardened, and the unbeliever are not cornered.

---

### G. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The personal introduction is genuinely pastoral and emotionally honest.** The preacher's confession that he was "focusing on ME, rather than on the Lord" and the subsequent moment of peace is the most theologically rich moment in the sermon — and it's entirely real. This is not performance. This earns the congregation's trust.

2. **The French Resistance illustration is the most vivid thing in the manuscript.** The image of a defeated people who found that worship, printed pamphlets, and dragged tank tracks through fields were acts of war — this is creative, memorable, and theologically defensible. It will be remembered.

3. **The sermon has genuine unity.** One idea — rejoice always, no matter what — governs the entire sermon from first sentence to last. That is not a small thing.

---

### H. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is absent.** Not thin. Absent. There is no articulation of what Christ did, why his cross is the ground of rejoicing, or how a person enters the life of praise the sermon calls for. The unbeliever has no bridge. The believer has no reminder. This is the sermon's defining failure.

2. **The text is almost entirely unexplored.** Twelve verses are read; three are preached. Verses 12–15 (community, leadership, mutual care) and 19–24 (the Spirit, sanctification, God's covenant faithfulness in verse 24 — "He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it") receive zero treatment. Verse 24 alone — *"he will surely do it"* — is the very ground of rejoicing Paul is pointing to. It was skipped entirely.

3. **The sermon ends without ending.** The final paragraph calls for ten minutes of corporate thanksgiving, which is a good pastoral instinct — but the manuscript then pivots to announcements and logistics. The sermon has no sermonic conclusion. A listener who needed to be summoned by the Word is instead dismissed. The last spiritual word before the announcements is "Lets thank God for allowing us to know him in his suffering" — a sentiment, not a declaration.

---

### I. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how far the French Resistance illustration has traveled from the text by the time it concludes. What began as a supporting illustration effectively became the sermon's argument — the text illustrates the illustration rather than the other way around. The illustration is good enough to stay; it needs to be reoriented so that the text governs it.

The preacher also likely does not see verse 24 as the fulcrum of the passage. Paul's reason for "rejoice always, pray without ceasing, give thanks in all circumstances" is not willpower or warfare — it is God's faithfulness. "He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it." This is the gospel ground Paul provides, and it is the sermon's missing center.

---

### J. Illusion of Strength

The introduction *feels* like the sermon's best moment — and in a pastoral, relational sense, it is. But its emotional power may mask the fact that the sermon never moved from the preacher's experience to the congregation's. The introduction is an on-ramp that never connects to the highway. The preacher's transparency creates the feeling of depth without delivering textual depth. This is not a criticism of the transparency — it is a call to ensure the text catches up to the testimony.

---

### K. Missed Opportunity

Verse 24 — *"He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it"* — is Paul's own answer to why Christians can rejoice always. Paul does not ground the command to rejoice in willpower, in warfare imagery, or even in the nobility of resistance. He grounds it in God's covenantal faithfulness and sovereign completion of what he began. This verse is the theological engine of the passage, and it was never mentioned. The sermon could have been rebuilt around it: *We rejoice not because we are strong enough to praise God in the storm, but because the God who called us is faithful — and he will surely do what he has promised.*

---

### L. Surgical Fixes

1. **Restructure the sermon around verse 24.** Make "He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it" the climax, not a skipped verse. The command to rejoice (v.16–18) is only sustainable because of the promise of v.24. Build to it.

2. **Preach the gospel explicitly.** One paragraph. Why can we rejoice in suffering? Because Christ bore our worst suffering — the wrath we deserved — so that nothing we now face is punitive. Our suffering is not hell; it is refining. That distinction requires the cross. Add it. Place it just before the call to corporate worship.

3. **Expose verses 12–15 briefly.** The community context is not accidental — Paul's call to rejoice comes after calling the church to care for one another. "Be patient with them all" (v.14) and "rejoice always" (v.16) are connected. Corporate rejoicing is grounded in corporate covenant life.

4. **Give the French Resistance illustration a hard boundary.** End it with the theological point — worship is resistance — and return immediately to the text. Do not let the illustration run longer than the exposition.

5. **Write a real conclusion.** A "therefore" sentence. One clear declaration. Something like: *"The God who called you is faithful. Come what may — stroke or cancer, grief or failure — he will finish what he started. That is why Paul says: Rejoice always. Pray without ceasing. Give thanks in all circumstances. Go and do it."*

6. **Address at least two categories of hearer.** The grieving (already addressed well by the introduction). Add one word to the self-sufficient — those in the room who have not yet suffered enough to feel desperate for God. "Some of you haven't been desperate yet. You will be. The question is whether you will have built the habit of praise before the storm comes."

---

### M. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered. This sermon is not cold. It is warm, genuine, and alive in pastoral voice. The problem is not dead orthodoxy — it is thin gospel and thin exposition. The fire is present. The Word has not yet caught.

### N. Weight of Glory Assessment

There is genuine unction in the introduction — a man standing before his congregation while his father is in the hospital, calling them to praise God in suffering, with tears probably closer than the manuscript can show. That carries weight. It does not yet carry the weight of the Word.
