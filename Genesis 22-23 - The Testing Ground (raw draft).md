# Genesis 22-23 — The Testing Ground

**Primary Text**: Genesis 22:1–23:20
**Supporting Texts**: Genesis 15:6; James 2:21; Romans 4:1-5; Galatians 3:8, 16; Hebrews 11:17-19; John 1:29; Romans 8:32; 2 Chronicles 3:1
**Themes**: Faith and works; testing not tempting; Isaac as type of Christ; Abrahamic covenant; "seed" = Christ (Gal 3:16); Abraham as sojourner in the promised land; substitutionary atonement; Jehovah-jireh
**Format**: Expository sermon — covers Genesis 22 and 23
**Status**: Raw draft — polished from preaching notes, March 2026
**Note**: Distinct from `Genesis 22 - Binding of Isaac (raw draft).md` (evening/midweek sermon, persecution/obedience theme) and `Genesis 22 - There and Back Again (raw transcript).md` (study notes, Christological parallels). This version has a different doctrinal entry point — James/Romans justification tension — and covers Genesis 23 (death and burial of Sarah), which neither prior file addresses.

## Part I — The Tension That Isn't (James 2:21 and Romans 4)

Before we read, I want you to sit with a question that has bothered people for centuries.

James 2:21 says this: *"Was not Abraham our father justified by works when he offered up his son Isaac on the altar?"*

Justified by works. That's James. That's in your Bible.

Now go to Romans 4. Paul says: *"What then shall we say was gained by Abraham, our forefather according to the flesh? For if Abraham was justified by works, he has something to boast about, but not before God. For what does the Scripture say? 'Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness.'"*

Justified by faith. That's Paul. Also in your Bible.

Which is it?

And most people walk right past it. Paul is quoting Genesis 15. Genesis 15 — which is years, maybe decades, before Genesis 22. Abraham was credited righteous by God because he believed — long before he ever climbed Mount Moriah. Long before Isaac was placed on an altar. Long before any of this.

So what is James saying? He is not contradicting Paul. James is saying that what lived in Abraham's heart in Genesis 15 — genuine faith in God — became visible in Genesis 22. Works do not produce faith. But real faith produces works. The Akedah — the binding of Isaac — is not the cause of Abraham's justification. It is the proof of it.

Faith without works is dead. This passage is the evidence.

Paul shows you what justification looks like under grace — Genesis 15. James shows you what it looks like lived out — Genesis 22. They are not in conflict. They are the same man, the same faith, thirty years apart.

Now — let's read.

## Part II — The Test (Genesis 22:1-2)

*"After these things God tested Abraham."* — Genesis 22:1

Not tempted. **Tested.**

The King James version used the word "tempt" here, and that caused all manner of confusion. J. Vernon McGee was right to press on this. *Tested* is the better translation, and the ESV gets it right. James 1:13 is clear: God cannot be tempted with evil, and he himself tempts no one. What God does is test. There is a difference. Temptation is an invitation to sin. A test is a proving ground.

So why this test? Why now?

Abraham is a man who has walked with God for decades at this point. He has left his homeland — that was the first test. He parted with Lot and gave up the better land — the second. He let Hagar go into the wilderness when Sarah demanded it — the third. And now the fourth. The hardest. The one that will cost him the most.

*"Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love, and go to the land of Moriah, and offer him there as a burnt offering on one of the mountains of which I shall tell you."*

Your son. Your only son. The one you love.

The text is deliberate. God does not say "a son." He says *your only son*. Abraham had Ishmael. But God had already made clear that Ishmael was not the covenant son. Isaac — born to a barren woman and a hundred-year-old man — Isaac was the only son that mattered for the promise. And God says: give him back.

We ought to ask an honest question here. Was it possible that Abraham loved Isaac more than he loved God? The test seems designed to expose exactly that. You do not test a man for what you do not suspect. God was asking Abraham: *Where does Isaac rank? Above me? Below me? Beside me?*

The only way to answer that question was to act.

Some of you have an Isaac. You know exactly what it is. You know exactly why this text makes you uncomfortable.

## Part III — Three Days to Moriah (Genesis 22:3-8)

Abraham got up early the next morning.

No bargaining. No extended prayer meeting. No "let me pray about this and get back to you." He got up early and went. That kind of obedience only comes from one place — settled trust in God. Abraham had no idea how God would resolve this. But he trusted that God would.

How do we know? Look at verse 5. He tells his servants: *"Stay here with the donkey; I and the boy will go over there and worship and come back to you."* We will come back. Both of us. Abraham was so confident in God's provision that he told his men Isaac was coming home. Hebrews 11:19 tells us why: he figured God was able to raise him from the dead if necessary.

That is not wishful thinking. That is faith that has been forged over decades.

The walk to Moriah was three days. Three days carrying what this command cost him. Three days of silence between a father and his son. And on the third day — God would restore what had been given up.

Now look at verse 6. *"And Abraham took the wood of the burnt offering and laid it on Isaac his son, and he took in his hand the fire and the knife."*

Isaac carried the wood. Abraham carried the fire and the knife.

The wood is the instrument of death — and Isaac bore it on his own back, up the hill. The fire speaks of judgment. The knife is the execution of that judgment. The father carried the means of his son's death. The son carried the weight of the sacrifice.

You do not need to force this typology. It is there in the text. The gospel is already in this story. Christ carried his own cross up to Golgotha. The Father, in some way we cannot fully understand, bore the weight of what was done there. 2 Chronicles 3:1 tells us that Solomon built the temple on Mount Moriah. Golgotha — where Christ was crucified — is on the same mountain ridge, a short walk away from the temple site. Same mountain. Different hill. Same God. Different generation. The pattern was already in the earth before Jesus ever walked it.

## Part IV — The Lamb Not Yet Come (Genesis 22:7-14)

Isaac knows something is wrong. Verse 7: *"Behold, the fire and the wood, but where is the lamb for a burnt offering?"*

Abraham answers: *"God will provide for himself the lamb for a burnt offering, my son."*

He said **lamb**. God provided a **ram**.

God provided a ram that day — a ram caught by its horns in a thicket. Notice: the ram's head was caught in thorns. The substitution was real, immediate, sufficient for that moment. But it was not a lamb. Abraham said lamb. And it would be roughly 1,900 years before God made good on that word in full.

John the Baptist saw him coming. *"Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!"* — John 1:29.

Abraham said "God will provide for himself the lamb." He did not know the name. He did not know the date. But he trusted the promise. And the promise was exactly right.

This is what the burnt offering meant. A burnt offering was a dedication — an ascent — an act of complete surrender to God. The ram substituted for Isaac. The life of the animal stood in for the life of the son. Life for life. That is the logic underneath every sacrifice in the Old Testament — and underneath the cross. Substitution. One standing in the place of another.

And notice: Isaac could have fought back. He was likely in his thirties — born to Sarah when she was ninety, and by the time of Sarah's death in chapter 23, he would have been thirty-seven years old at the latest. *(See Integrity Log — age is not stated in the text; various traditions place him between 25 and 37; the thirty-something range is well-supported.)* Abraham was old. Isaac was young and strong. He knew what was happening when his father bound him and laid him on the altar. He went anyway. Like Christ before his Father's will. Not because he had no choice. Because he chose to trust.

Abraham named that place: *"The LORD will provide."* *(ESV — the Hebrew Yahweh-yireh; traditional transliteration: Jehovah-jireh.)* And the text says — *as it is said to this day, "On the mount of the LORD it shall be provided."* The name became a proverb. It still is.

## Part V — By Myself I Have Sworn (Genesis 22:15-24)

The angel of the LORD calls from heaven a second time. And what follows is not a quiet comfort — it is an oath.

*"By myself I have sworn, declares the LORD, because you have done this and have not withheld your son, your only son, I will surely bless you, and I will surely multiply your offspring as the stars of heaven and as the sand that is on the seashore."* — Genesis 22:16-17

God swore by himself because there is no one greater to swear by. That oath — once made — cannot be broken. The writer of Hebrews says that's exactly the point: God wanted to show that his promise was unchangeable, so he confirmed it with an oath.

But look at the language. Abraham had not withheld his only son. God would not withhold his own Son.

Romans 8:32 is the echo: *"He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all."*

God did not ask Abraham to do anything He was not willing to do Himself. He stayed Abraham's hand. He did not stay his own.

And then this word — *seed*. *Offspring*. In your offspring shall all the nations of the earth be blessed.

Paul drives this point into the text of Galatians 3:16 with a precision that is almost mathematical: *"Now the promises were made to Abraham and to his offspring. It does not say, 'And to offsprings,' referring to many, but referring to one, 'And to your offspring,' who is Christ."*

One. Christ. The blessing of every nation flows through one man — the seed of Abraham, the son of David, the Lamb of God, Jesus Christ. Paul says in Galatians 3:8 that God *preached the gospel beforehand to Abraham*. Abraham may have understood more of what was coming than we might expect. He saw it from a distance. He believed it. And it was counted to him as righteousness.

Faith leads to action. The action at Moriah proved what was true at Genesis 15. God waited on Abraham's actions to show — to the record, to the angels, to us — what God already knew in his heart.

After this, Abraham and his servants returned to Beersheba. And there comes news from home — his brother Nahor has had children. The family line is moving forward. The promise is still unfolding.

## Part VI — The Cave at Machpelah (Genesis 23)

Chapter 23 is often skipped. Don't skip it.

Sarah died at Hebron. She was 127 years old — the only woman in Scripture whose age at death is recorded. She was ninety when Isaac was born. And the text gives us her years with the same gravity it gives to Abraham's. She mattered.

Abraham mourned her and wept for her.

And then he rose from beside his dead wife and went to buy land.

Not any land. A cave. A burial place. In the land God had promised him. The land he did not yet own.

Here is what is quietly devastating about this. Abraham is standing in the land God gave him — and he cannot bury his wife in it without buying a piece of it first. He is a sojourner in his own inheritance. He has the promise. He does not yet have the possession. Not an acre. Not a field. Not a cave.

He told the Hittites plainly: *"I am a sojourner and foreigner among you."* — Genesis 23:4

But then notice what they called him: *"You are a prince of God among us."* — Genesis 23:6

The world recognized something in this man that his legal status did not explain. He had no land. No deed. No city. But he had the weight of a man who had walked with God, and the Hittites could feel it.

Ephron the Hittite offered him the cave freely — the field and the cave both. In the culture of that day, you named a price so the person could decline. Abraham did not decline. He insisted on paying. He paid four hundred shekels of silver — weighed out according to the merchant's standard — and made the transaction public and formal in the hearing of all the Hittites at the gate.

The cave of Machpelah in Hebron. This became the burial site for Sarah, and eventually for Abraham, Isaac, Rebekah, Jacob, and Leah. Today the Ibrahimi Mosque stands over that location in Hebron. You can visit it. The patriarchs are under it.

Why does this matter? Because Abraham paid for a burial plot in a land he already owned by promise. He was content to die owning only a hole in the ground — because he believed the promise would outlast him. Hebrews 11:13 says this explicitly: *"These all died in faith, not having received the things promised, but having seen them and greeted them from afar."*

He was a sojourner. He knew it. And he was at peace with it.

That is the testimony of Genesis 23. Not conquest. Not possession. A tomb. And unshakeable trust in the God who would bring it all home.

## Part VII — What This Requires of Us

Some of you have been asked to put something on the altar.

Not Isaac. But something that matters to you the way Isaac mattered to Abraham. A dream. A relationship. A child you've made your identity. A marriage you've stopped trusting God with. A plan for how your life was supposed to go.

And the question God is asking — the same question he asked Abraham — is not *"Will you obey a rule?"* The question is: *"Do you trust me with this?"*

Kierkegaard wrote about this story — he called it a *teleological suspension of the ethical*. A moment where God asks something that seems to violate every normal moral category. He wasn't wrong about that. Abraham could not have explained to his neighbors what he was doing. There was no category for it. Except this one: God said so.

That is the absurdity of faith. Not irrational — but beyond rational. Faith that trusts God when there is no visible path.

And God met that faith.

He does not always remove the knife. But he always meets the faith.

This is before the Ten Commandments. This is before the law of Moses. This is before the temple, the priesthood, the sacrificial system. What Abraham had was a promise and a God who had never once failed to keep his word. That was enough. It still is.

One more thing. Child sacrifice was practiced throughout the ancient Near East — Canaan, Phoenicia, Carthage. What God was doing here was not inviting Abraham to join those practices. He was doing the opposite. He was stopping the sacrifice, providing a substitute, and revealing through the whole story that the real sacrifice would come from God's own side. The nations around Abraham sacrificed their children to appease their gods. God would sacrifice his own Son to redeem ours.

That is the gospel in Genesis 22. That is the word Abraham heard in the promise of Genesis 15. That is what he believed. That is what was counted to him as righteousness.

---

## Gospel Close

You are not standing at Moriah. But you are standing somewhere.

And Christ has already climbed the hill. He already carried the wood. The Father did not stay his own hand. The knife fell. The Son cried out. And on the third day — God brought him back.

There is a substitute. His name is Jesus. His blood is sufficient. His resurrection is real.

If you have never trusted him — not just agreed about him, but *trusted him* — that is what the gospel is asking of you today. Not a performance. Not a self-improvement plan. Faith. The same faith Abraham had. The empty hand that takes what God provides.

Come.

### Corrections Applied

1. **"EEPROM offered the cave free"** → corrected to **"Ephron"** (Genesis 23:10-11). *EEPROM* is a type of erasable programmable read-only memory chip — a transcription error/mishearing. The Hittite who offered the cave was Ephron (עֶפְרוֹן, *'Efron*).

2. **"400 shackles of silver"** → corrected to **"400 shekels of silver"** (Genesis 23:15-16 ESV). A *shekel* (שֶׁקֶל) is a unit of weight/currency. A *shackle* is a metal restraint device.

3. **"Macpilah"** → corrected to **"Machpelah"** (Genesis 23:9 ESV: "the cave of Machpelah"). The Hebrew is מַכְפֵּלָה (*Makhpelah*).

4. **"thine only son"** → changed to **"your only son"** throughout. ESV uses "your," not "thine" (KJV). See Genesis 22:2, 12, 16 ESV.

5. **"God 'tempted'"** — the original notes flag this correctly. Genesis 22:1 ESV reads: *"After these things God tested Abraham."* The ESV uses "tested" not "tempted." The KJV used "tempt" (נָסָה, *nasah* — to test/prove). J. Vernon McGee's observation is correct and has been incorporated.

6. **"James 2:21 was not Abraham justified by works (under the law)"** — the parenthetical "under the law" reflects a legitimate framing device (contrasting law-works with faith-works), but requires care. James is not referring to Mosaic law-keeping. He is referring to works that flow from genuine faith — demonstrative works. Paul's "under the law" in Romans 4 refers specifically to the Mosaic covenant. The two apostles are addressing different questions: Paul addresses the *grounds* of justification (faith alone); James addresses the *evidence* of justification (faith that produces works). They do not contradict each other. This nuance is woven into Part I of the sermon body.

### Factual Claims — Verified

- **Moriah = Temple Mount**: 2 Chronicles 3:1 confirms it. ✓
- **Moriah / Golgotha on same mountain ridge**: The Temple Mount (Moriah) and both traditional Golgotha locations (Church of the Holy Sepulchre ~450m; Gordon's Calvary ~250m) are on the same limestone ridge north of the City of David. The claim is geographically defensible. ✓ — but present as sermonic tradition, not established archaeology; neither site is on the Temple Mount itself.
- **Abraham's four trials (family, Lot, Hagar, Isaac)**: Associated with J. Vernon McGee's *Thru the Bible* commentary. This is a traditional homiletical framework, not stated as such in Genesis, but it tracks with the narrative arc. Present as McGee's observation. ✓
- **Ibrahimi Mosque / Cave of Machpelah at Hebron**: The Cave of Machpelah in Hebron is the traditional and historically supported site. The Ibrahimi Mosque (Sanctuary of Abraham) was built over it by the Mamluks (c. 1266-1516), built on a Herodian enclosure. ✓
- **Kierkegaard on the "absurd"**: Søren Kierkegaard's *Fear and Trembling* (1843, pseudonym Johannes de Silentio) argues that Abraham's obedience required a "teleological suspension of the ethical" — transcending the universal moral order by virtue of an absolute relationship with God. ✓
- **Child sacrifice in the ancient Near East**: Documented in Canaanite, Phoenician, and Carthaginian practice (*topheth* / *tophet* sites; Punic Sardinia; Hinnom Valley; Molech references). The Abraham/Isaac narrative operates against this cultural background. ✓

### Flags — Investigate Before Reuse

⚠️ **Isaac's age at the Akedah (30-33 vs. 37)**: The notes suggest "30-33 years old." The text does not state Isaac's age at Genesis 22. The *Seder Olam Rabbah* (rabbinic tradition) calculates 37 based on linking Sarah's death at 127 (Gen 23:1) to the Akedah — Sarah was 90 at Isaac's birth, so Isaac was 37 when she died, and tradition connects the two events. However, J. Vernon McGee and other Christian commentators estimate the late twenties to early thirties based on general narrative context, and some traditions infer 30-33 from Christological typology (matching Christ's age at the crucifixion). Neither is stated in the text. The sermon uses "likely in his thirties" with an asterisk directing to this log. Josephus (*Antiquities* 1.13.2) places Isaac at 25 — a third tradition. None is authoritative.

⚠️ **"Burnt offering was an offering for sin"**: The burnt offering (*olah*, עֹלָה) is a general dedication offering — an ascent offering, consumed entirely by fire. It does carry an atoning dimension (Lev 1:4: "it shall be accepted for him to make atonement for him"), but the *specific* sin offering is the *chattat* (חַטָּאת). Calling the burnt offering "an offering for sin" is broadly accurate in a homiletical sense but imprecise in a technical sense. The point of substitutionary logic (life for life) still stands and has been preserved in the sermon body.
