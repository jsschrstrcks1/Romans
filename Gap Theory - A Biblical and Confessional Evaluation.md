# Gap Theory — A Biblical and Confessional Evaluation

## What Is the Gap Theory?

The Gap Theory (also called the "Ruin-Reconstruction Theory") teaches that between Genesis 1:1 and Genesis 1:2 there exists a vast — possibly billions of years — gap of time. During this gap, an original creation existed, was judged and destroyed (usually connected to Satan's fall), and then God started over with the six-day creation account beginning in Genesis 1:3.

Some versions go further and posit an entire race of pre-Adamic beings — people or humanoid creatures who lived before Adam and Eve. This is the version that was brought up today.

The theory gained popularity through Thomas Chalmers in the early 1800s and was later embedded in the Scofield Reference Bible (1909), which gave it wide distribution in dispensational circles. It was an attempt to harmonize the Genesis account with the emerging claims of an old earth in secular geology.

---

## The Claim, Stated Plainly

The Gap Theory asserts:

1. Genesis 1:1 describes an original, complete creation.
2. Something catastrophic happened — Satan fell, judgment came, the earth was ruined.
3. Genesis 1:2 ("the earth was without form and void") describes the aftermath of that destruction.
4. Genesis 1:3 onward describes a *re-creation*, not the original creation.
5. Some versions claim pre-Adamic humans or beings existed in that first creation.

---

## Where It Falls Apart: The Exegetical Problems

### 1. "Was" Does Not Mean "Became"

The entire theory hangs on reading Genesis 1:2 as "the earth *became* without form and void" rather than "the earth *was* without form and void."

The Hebrew verb is הָיְתָה (*hayetah*), the qal perfect of הָיָה (*hayah*). This is the standard copulative verb — "was." To force it to mean "became" requires a reading that the grammar does not support in this construction. When *hayah* means "became," it is followed by the preposition *le* (לְ). That preposition is absent here.

This is not a minor grammatical quibble. It is the load-bearing wall of the entire theory, and it does not hold.

### 2. "Without Form and Void" Is Not Judgment Language

The phrase תֹהוּ וָבֹהוּ (*tohu wabohu*) — "without form and void" — describes the earth in its *unfinished* state, not in a *judged* state.

Gap theorists point to Isaiah 45:18, which says God "did not create [the earth] *tohu*." But Isaiah's point is that God did not create the earth to *remain* empty and purposeless — he formed it to be inhabited. That is exactly what Genesis 1 shows: God takes the unformed earth and shapes it, fills it, orders it. Isaiah 45:18 *supports* the standard reading. It does not undermine it.

The same phrase appears in Jeremiah 4:23: "I looked at the earth, and behold, it was without form and void." But Jeremiah is using Genesis language *metaphorically* to describe the devastation of judgment on Judah. He is borrowing the imagery of the pre-creation state to depict what judgment will feel like — a return to chaos. He is not providing a commentary on what Genesis 1:2 "really" meant.

### 3. The Six Days Include Everything

Exodus 20:11 settles this directly:

> "For in six days the LORD made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that is in them, and rested on the seventh day." (ESV)

*Heaven and earth* — that is Genesis 1:1. *The sea, and all that is in them* — that is everything in the six days. Moses, under inspiration, tells us plainly that the totality of creation happened in six days. There is no room for a prior creation, a prior destruction, and a do-over. Six days. Heaven, earth, sea, and everything in them.

Exodus 31:17 reinforces this: "In six days the LORD made heaven and earth, and on the seventh day he rested and was refreshed."

If there were a prior creation of billions of years, these statements are misleading at best and false at worst. God does not mislead.

### 4. Death Before the Fall Is Theologically Catastrophic

This is where the Gap Theory does its deepest damage.

Romans 5:12 — the very book we are preaching through — states:

> "Therefore, just as sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men because all sinned." (ESV)

Death entered through Adam's sin. Not before it. If there were pre-Adamic beings who lived and died — entire civilizations destroyed in judgment before Genesis 1:3 — then death preceded sin. And if death preceded sin, then Paul's argument in Romans 5:12–21 collapses. The entire Adam-Christ typology depends on Adam being the first man through whom death entered the world. Remove that, and the logic of the gospel itself is undermined.

1 Corinthians 15:21–22 says the same thing:

> "For as by a man came death, by a man has come also the resurrection of the dead. For as in Adam all die, so also in Christ shall all be made alive." (ESV)

The symmetry is exact: one man brought death, one Man brings life. The Gap Theory shatters that symmetry by introducing death before the one man.

Romans 8:19–22 tells us creation itself groans under the curse that came through Adam's sin. The whole creation was subjected to futility — not in some prior age, but as a consequence of the Fall described in Genesis 3.

### 5. Adam Is Called the First Man

1 Corinthians 15:45:

> "Thus it is written, 'The first man Adam became a living being'; the last Adam became a life-giving spirit." (ESV)

Adam is the *first* man. Not the first man of the second creation. Not the first man after a gap. The first man, full stop. Paul does not qualify this. The Holy Spirit does not qualify this. Adam is the beginning of humanity.

If pre-Adamic beings existed, Paul is wrong. And Paul is not wrong.

### 6. Genesis 1 Is a Sequence, Not a Restart

Read Genesis 1 as it presents itself. There is no break, no judgment, no destruction between verses 1 and 2. Verse 1 is the summary statement: "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth." Verse 2 describes the initial state of the creation: unformed and unfilled. Verses 3–31 describe how God formed and filled it over six days.

The structure is chiastic and intentional:
- Days 1–3: Forming (light/dark, sky/sea, land)
- Days 4–6: Filling (luminaries, birds and fish, animals and man)

This is a carefully constructed, ordered, progressive account. It reads as creation moving from initial state to completion — not as reconstruction after catastrophe.

## What the 1689 Confession Says

**Chapter 4, Paragraph 1** — "Of Creation":

> "In the beginning it pleased God the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, for the manifestation of the glory of his eternal power, wisdom, and goodness, to create or make the world, and all things therein, whether visible or invisible, **in the space of six days**, and all very good."

"In the space of six days." The confession leaves no room for a prior creation or a gap. The world and *all things therein* were made in six days. The framers of the 1689 — men who knew their Hebrew, who studied under the weight of the Reformation's recovery of Scripture — understood Genesis to teach a straightforward six-day creation. Not a reconstruction. Not a second attempt. A creation.

"And all very good." God's assessment in Genesis 1:31 covers everything he made. If there had been a prior creation full of death, judgment, and destruction lying beneath the surface, "very good" would be a strange thing to say about the rebuilt version sitting on top of a graveyard.

## Why This Matters Pastorally

### It Protects the Gospel

The Gap Theory seems harmless — just a way to account for the age of the earth, right? But it introduces death before sin. And once death exists apart from sin, the gospel's internal logic unravels. Christ died to defeat death — the death that came through Adam's sin. If death was already there, what exactly did Adam's sin introduce? And what exactly did Christ's death reverse?

We do not need to harmonize Genesis with secular geology. We need to let Genesis say what Genesis says.

### It Protects the Authority of Scripture

The Gap Theory arose not from careful exegesis but from cultural pressure. Geologists in the 1800s said the earth was old. Some Christians panicked and looked for a place to stuff millions of years into the text. Genesis 1:1–2 seemed like a convenient hiding spot.

But we do not read Scripture to find places to hide secular conclusions. We read Scripture to hear from God. When the text says "in six days the LORD made heaven and earth," we believe it — not because we are anti-science, but because we trust the One who was there when it happened.

### It Protects Pastoral Clarity

When someone in the congregation hears "there were people before Adam and Eve," it does not just raise a question about Genesis. It raises a question about whether the Bible can be trusted at all. If the first two verses of the Bible need to be decoded with a theory the text never mentions, what else might be hidden? What else might not mean what it plainly says?

We owe our people clarity. Genesis is not a puzzle to be decoded. It is a declaration to be believed.

---

## What to Say When Someone Brings This Up

You do not need to be unkind. The person who mentioned this probably heard it from someone they trust. But you can say plainly:

1. **The Hebrew does not support it.** "Was" means "was," not "became." The grammar is clear.
2. **Exodus 20:11 rules it out.** God made heaven, earth, sea, and everything in them in six days. That leaves no room for a prior creation.
3. **Romans 5:12 forbids it.** Death came through Adam. Not before Adam. If death existed before sin, Paul's argument — and the gospel — falls apart.
4. **Adam is the first man.** Not the first man of a second creation. The first man (1 Corinthians 15:45).
5. **The 1689 Confession affirms six-day creation.** "In the space of six days, and all very good." Our confessional heritage stands against this theory.
6. **The theory was born from cultural pressure, not exegesis.** It was invented to accommodate secular geology, not to faithfully handle the text.

---

## A Note on Charity

There are sincere believers who hold to some form of the Gap Theory. We are not questioning their salvation. We are questioning their exegesis. A person can be genuinely saved and genuinely wrong about Genesis 1:2. But the pastoral task is to gently, firmly bring people back to what the text actually says — not what we wish it said, not what would make the Bible easier to defend in a secular university, but what it says.

We let the text speak. We trust the God who wrote it. And we hold fast to the confession that summarizes it.

> "The authority of the Holy Scripture, for which it ought to be believed, depends not upon the testimony of any man or church, but wholly upon God, who is truth itself; the Author thereof; therefore it is to be received because it is the Word of God." — 1689 2LBCF, Chapter 1, Paragraph 4
