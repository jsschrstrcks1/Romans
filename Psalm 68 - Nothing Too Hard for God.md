# Psalm 68 — Nothing Too Hard for God

**Text: Psalm 68:1–4**

---

If you've brought your Bibles, and I hope you have.

A couple of months ago, an article came out of the University of Nebraska's medical center talking about the coronavirus. According to that article, worldwide there are still over 10,000 people dying from COVID every week. The article also noted that many places aren't reporting official numbers anymore — some cities, whole states, even entire countries have quit tracking COVID deaths. So the real number is likely higher. As a percentage of the world's population, it's not large. But it's still a striking number.

There's certainly a lot going on in the world right now. Iraq. Afghanistan. Israel and Palestine. Ukraine. Russia. COVID. And here's something else that caught my attention — scientists are watching a tick-borne illness in the Ukraine region that produces something like hemorrhagic fever. Deadly. And they've said it would take fewer mutations than COVID has already been through this year alone to become transmissible the way COVID is. That's worth thinking about.

I was talking to someone this week. I won't say who — I don't think most of you know this person, but some might, so I'll leave the name out. We were talking about the pandemic and everything surrounding it, and this person said: *"There are some things that are beyond even God's ability to handle."*

Do you think that's true?

Those of you who have been here a while probably already know what I think. There's an old Sunday school song — I won't sing it, but the lyrics were *"My God is so big, so strong and so mighty, there's nothing my God cannot do."* That's not theology I've grown out of. It's theology I've grown into.

Our psalm this morning starts right there. The power of God. Enemies scattered. Blown away like smoke. Melted like wax.

That's a powerful God.

---

*[READ: Psalm 68:1–4 — ESV]*

> God shall arise, his enemies shall be scattered;
>     and those who hate him shall flee before him!
> As smoke is driven away, so you shall drive them away;
>     as wax melts before fire,
>     so the wicked shall perish before God!
> But the righteous shall be glad;
>     they shall exult before God;
>     they shall be jubilant with joy!
> Sing to God, sing praises to his name;
>     lift up a song to him who rides through the deserts;
>     his name is the LORD;
>     exult before him!

---

The biblical writers didn't have words for Apache attack helicopters, machine guns, or defense satellites. I was reading about the testing of satellites designed to attack other satellites — or to shoot down missiles from orbit. Future battles could be fought on land, at sea, in the air, and in space. But David, if he could have conceived of those things, would have named those too. And God's Word accounts for tomorrow's weapons. God is omnipotent, omnipresent, and omniscient. Would an all-knowing, truthful God speak in Scripture in ways that would someday stop being true? Certainly not.

So when our Bible says God can melt his enemies like wax in a fire, he means it. He means attack helicopters and fighter jets. Missiles and machine guns. He means the weapons we haven't imagined yet. God is more powerful than a nuclear bomb, more powerful than COVID-19, more powerful than ticks with hemorrhagic fever, more powerful than every war on the map right now. There is nothing on that list that changes the equation.

---

Scripture is full of passages like this one. Exodus 15:6 says —

> "Your right hand, O LORD, glorious in power, your right hand, O LORD, shatters the enemy."

The enemies of God are like a pane of glass to God's hammer. He moves, and they shatter.

That actually reminds me of something. Probably twenty-five, thirty years ago now. Christmas morning. We needed to turn on the Christmas tree lights. This was before Alexa. My parents now call their tree Bob and just ask Alexa to turn Bob on or off. But back then we didn't have one of those foot pedal switches. If you wanted the tree on, you reached around behind it, very carefully without knocking it over, and plugged it into the wall.

This particular year, the tree was right next to a window. So there was the tree to navigate, and the window right there beside it. Christmas morning, my turn. I pressed my body against the wall, reached back and around and down to find the outlet.

And I heard glass shatter. I'd pressed just a little too hard and caught the window.

Glass is fragile like that. And that's exactly the image in Exodus 15. God's right hand is glorious in power. His enemies — all the strength of all mankind, all the collective force of every enemy God has ever faced — are like that window pane on Christmas morning. Shattered. Ruined.

There is no enemy we face that is greater than the devil himself. And even the devil is like that pane of glass on Christmas morning. Weak. Frail. Shattered by God with one hand, as the saying goes, with the other tied behind his back.

---

Before I go any further — I need to give you an update on Chuck.

You remember Chuck. His wife passed away just last week. His daughter Denise usually sits back there in the corner. Well, Friday morning the doctors told the family Chuck had no hope. I wasn't there for that conversation, but I was there a little later when Chuck's hospice nurse was trying — very gently — to tell Denise not to get her hopes up. Denise said, "I know the doctors are saying twenty-four to forty-eight hours." And then, with every bit of fierceness and faith she possessed, she looked at that nurse and said: *"But you don't know my God."*

We prayed. People got on the phone and talked to him. He'd try to speak but it wasn't clear, and he wasn't opening his eyes. His hands and feet were growing cold.

And then, all of a sudden — he woke up. Started playing his guitar. Once his tongue wasn't stuck to the roof of his mouth anymore, he spoke just like Chuck normally does. Ate a full meal. Tried to joke with the nurses.

I fully believe God spared his life that day.

We serve a powerful God.

---

Let's look at one more passage.

1 Chronicles 29:11 —

> "Yours, O LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the victory and the majesty, for all that is in the heavens and in the earth is yours. Yours is the kingdom, O LORD, and you are exalted as head above all."

I love this verse. It reminds me, actually, of Plato. Have you heard of Plato? He's famous for a number of things, but the one I want to focus on is his concept of ideal forms. Here's how it works. There are thousands of different chairs in the world, right? All kinds. But Plato would argue that somewhere there is *the* chair. The ideal chair. The perfect chair that all others are lesser copies of. A heavenly chair. All the others — while still chairs, maybe even good chairs — are just fainter versions of that one perfect original.

Apply that to this verse.

Yours, O LORD, is *the* greatness. *The* power. *The* glory. *The* victory. *The* majesty.

Not *a* greatness. *The* greatness. All other greatnesses on earth are frail copies of the one true original, which belongs to God alone.

What's the opposite of great? Small. Tiny. Insignificant. If God possesses the ideal form of greatness, then the greatness of mankind — the best we have ever achieved — is inconsequential by comparison. A candle flame next to a hurricane.

I've been reading about that solar flare not long ago — the coronal mass ejection. Depending on which system of measurement you use, it was either the largest since the 1930s or the largest since the 1950s. That got me curious. How much material gets ejected in one of these things? Turns out what hit us was actually seven large coronal mass ejections in sequence. They're still working out the total mass, but based on average CME figures, the number comes out to something like two and a half quadrillion pounds of plasma. Quadrillions. That's thousands of trillions of pounds of superheated material from the sun hitting the earth all at once.

But the sun didn't notice. It isn't any dimmer. It doesn't provide any less warmth. It didn't register.

And even *that* image sells things short. Us compared to God is a dim little candle next to the sun. God's enemies are no better. Brittle. Easily shattered. What can stand as great before him?

The same is true of power. God possesses the ideal form of power. Every earthly power is a fainter copy. Human power compared to his is like a paper fan in a hurricane.

You know the fans I mean — small, handheld, floral or Chinese design, ladies would use them to fan themselves. That's us compared to the hurricane of God.

A few years back, leading up to Hurricane Irma, there were jokes circulating on Facebook about how to stop the storm. People said if we all go outside and shoot at Irma at the same time, we can repel it. Or plug in a box fan on your porch — if everyone does it at once, the hurricane can't come ashore. Alarmingly, people actually did shoot into the storm. And funnily enough, people really did plug in box fans and point them at the hurricane. I remember reading that a significant number of people in Miami-Dade County had their box fans blown right back through their windows by Irma.

A box fan in a hurricane is not resistance. It's asking for trouble. That's human power compared to God's. And even that image sells things short.

Even majesty — the beauty of God. What's the most beautiful thing you think mankind has ever made? A painting? A sculpture? Something that stops you cold when you stand in front of it?

Garbage. Compared to a single flicker of God's majesty, our most breathtaking art is a smudge.

Moses asked to see God's glory. God told him no man could see his face and live. So he put Moses in a cleft in the rock, covered him with his hand, and let his glory pass by — allowing Moses to see only his back, not his face. Moses came off that mountain with his face shining so bright the people couldn't look at him directly. He had to wear a veil. He caught one passing glimpse — not God's face, not his glory in full — just the passing of it. And it changed him for the rest of his life.

The best we can do, as human beings, is maybe make someone cry. Make them smile. A moment. A brief point in time. God's majesty doesn't just move people. It *changes* them. Permanently.

---

And because God possesses the ideal form of greatness, power, and majesty — he also possesses the ideal form of victory.

Ever had a hollow victory?

You won — sort of. I heard a story this week. An acquaintance made a product. Good product, met a real need, took years to develop. Over the next decade he built a real business — sales staff, manufacturing, the whole operation. Things were going well. Then one day his sales manager called him demanding to know why they'd released a new version with more features without telling him. My friend had done no such thing.

Turns out a competitor had copied almost everything — his marketing, slogan, colors, even an eerily similar company name. What they didn't have was a product that worked. They were capitalizing on his name and years of hard work without even a functioning product.

He had no choice but to sue for an injunction. The court heard the case, compared the products, and ruled in his favor in a single day. Seventy-five thousand dollars in legal fees.

But there was no enforcement agency. With delays and excuses and dragging of feet, it took nearly five more years before the other company had to shut down — which they did, for about a day, before the exact same people came back as a new company with a new name, same colors, same marketing, same broken product.

My friend won. He was right, the other side was wrong. He was the victim and the victor. But there was no joy in it because nothing really changed.

So many of our victories are hollow like that.

Think about World War One. It was called the war to end all wars. The genuine belief — after the horror of trench warfare, of chemical weapons, of that scale of industrial killing — was that no nation would ever go to war again. Something like seventeen million people paid the ultimate price. After this, they said, mankind is done with war.

Then came World War Two. Something like eighty-five million dead. Then Korea. Cuba. Vietnam. The Dominican Republic. Lebanon. Beirut. Panama. The first Gulf War. Somalia. Haiti. Bosnia. Kosovo. Libya. Afghanistan. Iraq. Syria. ISIL.

We have been at war for most of the last century. The victory in the war to end all wars was hollow. Not unnecessary — the wars we've fought have often been, many of them, for genuinely noble causes. I am grateful for every man and woman who has put their life on the line to stop ethnic cleansing, to protect the weak, to maintain freedom in the world. Laudable goals. But our power is finite. Our victories are finite too.

Even this virus. A vaccine against COVID-19 was developed in under a year — a genuine scientific achievement. And there are already variants that resist what the vaccine does by up to thirty percent. In another year our victory over COVID may be hollow again. They think they can update the vaccine every nine months if necessary. But even that is a hollow victory, won at great cost in both lives and economy.

The best victories mankind can create are incomplete. They don't last. We defeat this dictator, and another rises. We solve this disease, and a new one emerges. Our power is finite. Our victories are, too.

But God's victories are not.

One day, when we are with him in glory — do you think we'll be worrying about hurricanes? Do you think there will be ethnic cleansing and human rights violations in the presence of God? Do you think we'll be standing before our Savior in masks, social distancing from each other?

No.

---

I'm reminded of the account of Abraham and Sarah.

God had promised them descendants more numerous than the grains of sand on the seashore, more than the stars in the sky. And here they are — Abraham closing in on a hundred years old, Sarah turning ninety, well past the season of bearing children. God tells them they'll have a son. Next year.

They laugh.

And God says — Genesis 18:14 —

> "Is anything too hard for the LORD? At the appointed time I will return to you, about this time next year, and Sarah shall have a son."

Sure enough, the next year, Sarah has a baby. At ninety years old.

Can you imagine that, ladies? Being ninety and becoming a mother for the first time? She'd have been close to a hundred and ten by the time Isaac was grown. Kids are hard when you're young. Some of you are in your eighties right now. Imagine having a toddler in a couple of years. Makes me shudder.

Nothing is outside the scope of God's power. Nothing.

---

Ephesians 3:20–21 says it this way —

> "Now to him who is able to do far more abundantly than all that we ask or think, according to the power at work within us, to him be glory in the church and in Christ Jesus throughout all generations, forever and ever. Amen."

Not only are we less powerful, less holy, less glorious, less majestic than God. Not only are we incapable of standing against his power — box fans in a hurricane. Not only all of that. We are so far beneath him in every way that we can't even *conceive* of what he is able to do. He can do the things we ask. But so much more. More than our wildest imagination. More than we could ever hope for.

In six days, God made more than our most brilliant minds have been able to fully understand in the six thousand years since.

Do you think COVID surprised him?

Certainly not. Being all-knowing, he knows exactly how much of this the world needs to accomplish his purposes. He knows every person he will bring home, and every person who will face judgment, throughout the full course of this pandemic — and whatever follows it. He has reasons for everything that happens. We may never understand them this side of eternity. But that's not where we're left.

We are left leaning into him when things are scary. When things are hard.

We know God is good. We know God is able to do abundantly more than we could ever ask or imagine. We know these things. We need to be sure we are *living* them.

Don't let fear — fear of COVID, fear of anything — keep you from sharing the gospel. Don't let it slow you down from what God is calling you to do. Maybe you'll get sick. Maybe not. But obedience to the one who is able matters more than safety.

Now — be smart about it. The Bible says the battle belongs to the Lord, but saddle your horse anyway (Proverbs 21:31). Take precautions. Use wisdom. But don't let fear make the call.

---

Let me ask you something.

Say you know someone. They may or may not have COVID — you don't know, they don't know. But God keeps bringing them to mind. He keeps putting them on your heart. You know they aren't saved. And God is pressing you — not with words, but with that restlessness you know all too well — *go share the gospel with this person.*

But COVID is scary.

What do you do?

Do you disobey, or do you obey?

I'd prepare my chariot — mask, distance, whatever precautions make sense — and go. Maybe I'll get COVID. Maybe I'll die. But God won't bring me to something he isn't prepared to bring me through.

This question reminds me of a story I read in high school. A young man in a country very hostile to Christianity got saved. Was discipled. Felt called by God to start a house church. A few months in, the secret police battered down the door and held everyone at gunpoint. One by one, the policeman put his gun to each church member's head. "Denounce Jesus right now, I'll let you live. Say you're a follower of Christ, and I'll send you to the back of the room — and when I'm done, you'll be shot. Right between the eyes. While the rest of this church watches."

One at a time, people made their choices. Some left. Some walked to the back and sat down.

The pastor sat with the ones in the back and prayed with them. He said a peace settled over him. He knew he was going to die. His children were at school. He knew God would take care of them.

Finally, the policeman sent his officers to follow those who had left and begin surveillance. Then he called the pastor forward. The pastor was the last one. The real believers who remained were singing.

The policeman asked him to denounce Christ.

The pastor said: *"I believe in Jesus Christ — maker, sustainer, creator of all things. And I believe that if you do not bend your knee to him, you will spend eternity apart from him in punishment. I fear my Savior more than I fear your gun. Your gun can only take my life. God is responsible for my eternity. Yours too. And if you repent right now, Jesus can forgive anything you have done."*

And then he quoted Martin Luther: *"Here I stand. I can do no other."*

The policeman put his gun down and wept. He said: "I had to be sure you were really who you said you were. Because of my job. Because of my family. I needed to be certain." He then asked the pastor to start the service.

So the story goes — what remained of that little church was safe for the rest of that period of persecution.

---

COVID is like that gun. It can do no worse than take your life. It has no power over God. It has no influence that God did not account for and plan for.

I'd like to think that if a man came in here with a gun, most of us would stay.

Don't let COVID — or fear, or whatever is on your list — keep you from obedience to God.

And for those of you who would leave: not just hypothetically, but in the deeper sense — those of you who are not yet believers, who aren't ready to stand on that ground — this next part is for you. It's time to become a follower of Jesus Christ.

*[Gospel presentation]*

## Source Notes

*Items flagged for verification before pulpit or print use.*

**COVID statistics (opening)** — "Over 10,000 people still dying from COVID" sourced to a University of Nebraska medical publication. The specific journal, article title, and date need to be identified before citing publicly. COVID death tracking methodology varied significantly by jurisdiction and reporting period; the "undercount" caveat is well-documented generally but should be tied to a verifiable source.

**Tick-borne hemorrhagic fever / Ukraine** — The disease referenced is likely Crimean-Congo hemorrhagic fever (CCHF), which is endemic in parts of Eastern Europe including Ukraine. The claim about mutation requirements for airborne transmissibility comes from the same unidentified article. Needs independent verification and specific citation before public use.

**"War to end all wars" — corrected in manuscript** — The phrase originated with H.G. Wells in 1914 and was applied to World War One, not World War Two. The draft applied it to WWII; corrected in the manuscript to WWI. The rhetorical point is actually strengthened by the correction: the war that was supposed to end all wars was followed within a generation by an even larger one. WWI death estimates are typically 17–20 million; WWII estimates range from 70–85 million. Manuscript uses "something like seventeen million" for WWI and "something like eighty-five million" for WWII — verify preferred figures.

**"80 people in Miami-Dade had box fans blown back through windows"** — Specific figure attributed to a news article during Hurricane Irma. The broader phenomenon (viral social media posts urging fans or firearms against the storm, people actually complying) is well-documented. The specific number of people who had fans blown back through windows was not independently verified; manuscript softened to "a significant number" to avoid citing an unverified figure.

**CME / solar flare data** — The event referenced is likely the May 2024 geomagnetic storm (G5 event, the strongest since 2003 or 2005 depending on the index used). The "two and a half quadrillion pounds" figure is an estimate based on average CME mass multiplied by seven ejections. This figure is in the right ballpark for a large CME event but should be verified against a specific published source before citing as a factual number. The broader illustration (sun loses quadrillions of pounds and doesn't notice) is rhetorically sound.

**Abraham and Sarah's ages — corrected in manuscript** — Draft said "in their 80s." Genesis 17:17 places Abraham at 99 and Sarah at 90 at the time the promise was made; Isaac was born when Abraham was 100 (Gen 21:5) and Sarah was 90. Corrected to "Abraham closing in on a hundred, Sarah turning ninety."

**Genesis 18:14 — corrected in manuscript** — Draft paraphrased as "Do you really think there is anything too difficult for me?" ESV reads: "Is anything too hard for the LORD? At the appointed time I will return to you, about this time next year, and Sarah shall have a son." Updated in manuscript.

**Isaiah 64:6 ("filthy rags")** — Draft uses the familiar KJV/NIV rendering. ESV has "all our righteous deeds are like a polluted garment." The Hebrew (*עִדִּים*) refers to cloth used during menstruation, which is the background for the older rendering. Manuscript references the concept without quoting; citation should be Isaiah 64:6.

**Proverbs 21:31** — Draft paraphrased as "the battle is ours but make your chariots ready." ESV: "The horse is made ready for the day of battle, but the victory belongs to the LORD." The manuscript uses this as a pulpit paraphrase with the citation noted.

**Martin Luther — "Here I stand"** — The declaration at the Diet of Worms (1521) is widely attributed as: *"Here I stand; I can do no other. God help me."* Some historians note that the phrase does not appear in the earliest documentary records of the trial and may have been added to the published account shortly after. The quote is culturally established and widely accepted; flag it as traditional attribution.

**Secret police / house church story** — Presented as something the pastor read in high school. This type of persecution narrative circulates widely, often through organizations like Voice of the Martyrs or Richard Wurmbrand's writings. The specific story's origin should be identified if possible before citing it as a documented account rather than an illustrative composite.

**Moses and God's glory** — Draft used "Moses saw the shadow of God's glory pass by." The Exodus 33:18–23 account has God placing Moses in a cleft of the rock, covering him with his hand, and allowing Moses to see his "back" as he passes — not his face. "Shadow of glory" is interpretive language not in the text. Manuscript describes the event in keeping with Exodus 33 language without using "shadow."

**"My God is so big, so strong and so mighty"** — Traditional children's Christian song, author unknown. Wide circulation, no specific attribution needed for pulpit use.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm and Winsome Omnipotence Sermon That Stalls at the Gospel Gate**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A generally accessible and pastorally engaged opening of Psalm 68:1–4 that magnifies divine omnipotence with real illustrative energy, while stopping short of a genuine gospel presentation and failing to press the conscience with the weight the text's own doxology demands.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS (conditional)**
The sermon's thesis — "God is omnipotent; nothing is beyond his power, therefore do not fear and do not disobey" — arises from Psalm 68:1–4. The text declares God's power over enemies, the gladness of the righteous, and an invitation to exult before him. The sermon's main burden is God's supreme power. That connection holds.

**Authority Test: PASS (with caution)**
The preacher does not editorialize the text's claims away. However, the sermon treats only verses 1–4 of a 35-verse psalm, and the martial/eschatological weight of even those four verses is substantially softened into a comfort-against-current-events framework. The preacher does not contradict the text, but he does considerably narrow it.

**Gate Status: PASS — proceed to scoring.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag #1 — No clear gospel presentation: TRIGGERED**
The sermon ends with the stage direction *[Gospel presentation]* — a placeholder, not an actual presentation. The gospel is not preached in the manuscript as it stands. Christ's person, finished work, substitutionary atonement, and the call to repentance and faith are entirely absent from the delivered content. This is not a marginal omission. The final paragraph explicitly sets up an evangelistic appeal and then replaces it with a bracket. A bracketed gospel is not a preached gospel.

**Flag #3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary: TRIGGERED**
Jesus is never named. The cross is never referenced. The resurrection is never mentioned. The sermon could be delivered in a synagogue, a mosque, or by a theistic motivational speaker without alteration. The omnipotence of God is affirmed with energy — but the omnipotent God who sent his Son, who defeated sin and death at the cross, who is the fulfillment of the very victory-language of the Psalms, is nowhere present. The Christological connection is not missing because the text doesn't allow it — Psalm 68 is richly Christological (Paul quotes v.18 in Ephesians 4:8 regarding the ascension). It is missing because the preacher did not go there.

**Score cap: 69 maximum.**

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 16 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10 pts): 6/10* — The sermon draws genuine energy from Psalm 68:1–4, but four verses of a 35-verse psalm are treated as though the passage is complete at v.4. The text's own structure — the triumphal procession, the ascent of God to Zion, the redemptive-historical freight of the ark narrative (Num 10:35 background), the explicit eschatological horizon — is entirely bypassed. The sermon reads from the text but does not follow the text's argument.
- *Contextual Awareness (5 pts): 3/5* — The immediate context of the Numbers 10:35 background, the superscription, and the psalm's place in the Psalter's movement toward God's ultimate victory are ignored. The surrounding canonical witness (1 Chron 29:11, Eph 3:20–21, Gen 18:14, Exod 15:6) is deployed well as corroborating testimony, though the connections are topical rather than covenantal.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5 pts): 3/5* — No Hebrew work, no attention to the psalm's genre as a victory hymn, no treatment of what "enemies of God" means covenantally. The images (smoke, wax, fire) receive illustration but not exegetical unpacking.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5 pts): 4/5* — No significant eisegesis. The Plato illustration is philosophically creative and theologically serviceable, though it owes more to Platonic metaphysics than to the text's own idiom.

**FCF (Fallen Condition Focus):** Partially identified — fear in the face of threats. But the deeper FCF of the psalm (the people of God surrounded by enemies who appear stronger than their God, requiring a vision of the ascended, triumphant God) is not named.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7 pts): 2/7* — Christ is absent. The triune God is not distinguished. The omnipotence being preached is generic theism; it could belong to any monotheistic system. This is the sermon's most serious theological failure.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3 pts): 1/3* — No placement of Psalm 68 in the redemptive-historical narrative. The connection to the Exodus (the psalm's own background), the ark, the Sinai theophany, and ultimately to the ascension of Christ (Eph 4:8) is never approached.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4 pts): 4/4* — Within its scope, the theology is accurate. Divine omnipotence, omniscience, omnipresence, and immutability are handled soundly. No doctrinal errors.
- *Confessional Alignment (4 pts): 4/4* — Nothing contradicts confessional theology. The soteriological categories are simply not engaged.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 3 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (5 pts): 1/5* — A placeholder bracket where the gospel should be. One sentence near the end gestures toward unbelievers needing to "become a follower of Jesus Christ," but the content of that following is never defined.
- *Necessity of Christ (5 pts): 1/5* — Christ is structurally unnecessary to this sermon. The argument proceeds identically without him.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (5 pts): 1/5* — The sermon orbits entirely around omnipotence as comfort against present threats. The cross, the greatest demonstration of God's power (1 Cor 1:18, 24), is absent.

**Synagogue Test: TRIGGERED.** A devout Jewish person could hear this sermon and not be offended by the necessity of Christ's blood. The sermon preaches the God of Abraham — but not the God who sent his Son to be the final and ultimate victory over God's enemies.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration (5 pts): 4/5* — The Chuck story and the house church narrative are genuinely pastoral and carry real emotional weight. The question "Do you disobey or do you obey?" reaches the conscience. The direct address to those who "would leave" is honest.
- *Specificity (5 pts): 3/5* — The COVID/fear application is concrete and timely. The evangelism exhortation is pointed. But the application stops at fear management and evangelism courage — two notes, both good, but narrow relative to the text's own scope.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (5 pts): 2/5* — Perkins' taxonomy: the sermon addresses the believers (category 5) with encouragement and the unbeliever (category 2) with an implicit appeal. The self-righteous (category 3), the humbled-but-not-yet-believing (category 4), the backslider (category 6), and the hardened (category 1) receive no differentiated address. The differentiation is essentially binary.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5 / 8**

- *Call to Response (4 pts): 3/4* — The evangelism exhortation is specific: go share the gospel despite fear, take precautions, but go. The call is clear and concrete.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (4 pts): 2/4* — The sermon equips for witness courage. It does not equip for the deeper corporate life of the church, nor does it send the believer into any domain beyond evangelism.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6 / 9**

- *Clarity of Outline (4 pts): 3/4* — The sermon moves logically: power of God → illustrations of power → real-life testimony (Chuck) → more illustrations → specific exhortation. It is traceable, though not tightly outlined.
- *Progression (3 pts): 2/3* — The middle section (Plato, CME, Hurricane Irma) is rich in illustration but produces some congestion. Three large illustrative sequences in a row slow the progression.
- *Unity (2 pts): 1/2* — Two dominant ideas compete without full resolution: "God is all-powerful" and "therefore don't let fear stop your obedience/witness." These are connected but the sermon doesn't formally unify them under a stated thesis.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4 / 5**

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Ephesians 4:8 quotes Psalm 68:18 — "When he ascended on high he led a host of captives, and he gave gifts to men." Paul's use of this psalm is one of the clearest Christological appropriations of the Psalter in the New Testament. The enemies scattered in Psalm 68:1 are the same enemies defeated at the cross and put under Christ's feet in the ascension. The victory that human victories can never achieve — the final, complete, unhollowed victory — is not merely God's abstract omnipotence but Christ's specific, historical, once-for-all triumph. The preacher set up the hollow-victory illustration brilliantly, then failed to deliver its deepest payoff: the only unhollowed victory in history is the resurrection and ascension of Jesus Christ.

That move — from hollow human victory to Christ's final victory, mediated through Psalm 68's own canonical fulfillment — would have been the sermon's best moment and its true climax. It was sitting in the passage the entire time.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Write out the gospel.** Replace *[Gospel presentation]* with actual content: Christ's life, death, resurrection; the problem of sin and wrath; justification by faith alone; the call to repentance and trust. Even a paragraph. The bracket must go.

2. **Introduce Christ through the Ephesians 4:8 connection.** After the hollow-victory sequence, transition: "But there is one victory that is not hollow. One victory that doesn't unravel. Psalm 68 points forward to it — Paul tells us so in Ephesians 4:8. When Jesus ascended on high, he led captivity captive. He triumphed over the very enemies this psalm describes: sin, death, the devil. His victory does not erode." This requires approximately ninety additional seconds and transforms the sermon from a theistic omnipotence talk into a genuinely Christian proclamation.

3. **Add a direct address to the knowledgeable-but-unmoved hearer.** The sermon reaches the fearful believer and (gesturally) the unbeliever. It does not reach the person who intellectually affirms God's omnipotence and is utterly unchanged by it — who could recite this sermon's thesis and nod along, and still live in practical functional atheism. Consider: "Some of you believe everything I've said this morning — you've believed it for years — and your life shows almost none of it. You know God is powerful. You just don't live like it matters. That's not faith. That's information. And information without trust is not Christianity."

4. **Trim one illustration from the middle section.** The CME sequence, the Plato sequence, and the Hurricane Irma sequence are all strong individually. Together they produce drag. Remove one (Plato or CME — the Irma illustration is the most culturally accessible) and give the recovered time to the Christological pivot in Fix #2.

5. **Name the Fallen Condition Focus explicitly.** The FCF here is not merely "fear of COVID" but something deeper: the tendency to look at visible threats and conclude that God's power is insufficient or his purposes are beyond his management. Name it more starkly, early: "There is a lie that fear tells — it says that what you can see is more real than what God has promised."
