## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date:** 2026-03-21
**Sermon:** Psalms 24
**Primary Text:** Psalm 24

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Searching Application on Idolatry Grounded in an Honest Gospel Deficit — Conviction Without Cross"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A genuinely searching sermon on clean hands, pure hearts, and the idols that disqualify us from ascending God's holy hill — pastorally honest and personally vulnerable — that ends at precisely the moment it should begin, gesturing toward the gospel without proclaiming it.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**PASS — with a significant reservation.**

**Scopus Test:** The sermon's thesis — "Only the person of clean hands, pure heart, who does not lift his soul to idols and has integrity can ascend the hill of the LORD — and all of that starts with the gospel" — arises from Psalm 24:3-5. The sermon's primary concern (what qualifies a person for God's presence, and what disqualifies them) is genuinely the psalm's own concern. The application sections on idolatry are directly derived from v.4's prohibition on lifting the soul to what is false.

**Authority Test:** The preacher does not soften the hard parts. The requirements of vv.3-4 are not minimized — the sermon honestly confronts the congregation with the impossibility of meeting God's standard in their own strength. However, the second half of the psalm (vv.7-10, the entrance of the King of Glory) is largely unexplored, which means the sermon's resolution stops short of the text's own climax.

The gate passes. The main point of the sermon is the main point of the text's first half (who may ascend). The sermon earns a conditional pass while noting the significant theological weight of vv.7-10 that is absent.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 triggered:** "All of that starts with the gospel" is not a gospel presentation. It is the sentence that should introduce a gospel presentation, and the manuscript ends there. The gospel is named as the starting point but the starting point is never reached. Score cap: maximum 69.

**Flag 5 triggered:** No exhortation is given beyond "pray about it." The congregation is given no specific call to believe, repent, or act from the text's own imperative logic.

**Score cap applies: maximum 69.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 15/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (6/10):* The sermon works primarily from vv.3-4 with good focus: who shall ascend (v.3), the qualifications (v.4 — clean hands, pure heart, no idolatry, no deceit). The Hebrew word study on "clean hands" (guiltless, innocent) is helpful and text-faithful. The verse 4 content is organized around integrity, which is a legitimate synthetic reading. However, vv.7-10 — the entrance of the King of Glory, the opening of the ancient gates — receive no treatment. This is a serious gap: the psalm's own structure moves from "who may ascend?" (vv.3-4) to "who is the King?" (vv.7-10) as its answer to the human qualification problem. The psalm's resolution is the King's arrival, not human effort. Omitting this half of the psalm omits the psalm's own answer to the problem it raises. The FCF is well-identified: idolatry — the tendency to place anything before God — is exactly what the psalm diagnoses.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5):* The ancient Near Eastern context of sacred places with entry requirements is developed usefully (Yom Kippur, the high priest, the holy of holies). This contextual work opens up the psalm's stakes. The sermon does not develop the psalm's setting as a processional psalm likely used at the entry of the ark of the covenant into Jerusalem (2 Sam 6), which would illumine the King of Glory section.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5):* The Hebrew "clean hands" study is accurate and clearly presented. The John Piper quote on imputed righteousness is apt and helpfully cited. The sermon's use of Isaiah 64:6 (filthy rags) is textually accurate.
- *Logical Faithfulness (2/5):* The primary logical gap is that the sermon raises the qualification problem (who can ascend?) without providing the psalm's own answer (the King of Glory). The sermon ends with "all of that starts with the gospel" — which is directionally correct — but the logic of the text is not followed to its destination. The psalm's answer to "who can ascend?" is not primarily "a person of clean hands" — it is "the King of Glory enters." The psalm is not fundamentally about human moral qualification; it is about the arrival of the King who has won the right to enter.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7):* The Piper quote on imputed righteousness is the strongest theological moment in the sermon. "The perfect righteousness of Christ imputed to us" is named and attributed accurately. But the Christological fulfillment of vv.7-10 — Jesus Christ as the King of Glory who has defeated sin and death and now enters in triumph — is entirely absent. This is the psalm's own Christology, and it is the most obvious missed connection in the batch.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* Some awareness. The Yom Kippur section develops the old covenant background appropriately. The imputed righteousness point is redemptive-historical in character. But the psalm's own redemptive-historical movement — from human failure to the King's arrival — is not traced.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4):* Nothing theologically incorrect. The imputed righteousness point is precise. The idolatry sections are theologically sound.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4):* The Piper quote on imputed righteousness aligns with confessional soteriology. The idolatry material is consistent with Reformed teaching. The gospel gesture at the end is directionally confessional.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 5/15**

- *Necessity of Christ (2/5):* The Piper quote on imputed righteousness names Christ's necessity. Isaiah 64:6 (filthy rags) names the human problem. But the bridge between the two — the specific work of the cross that accomplishes imputation — is not stated. Christ is necessary in the theology that is gestured toward; He is not present in the sermon's actual argument.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5):* The sermon builds toward the gospel but does not arrive. The imputed righteousness point is the closest the sermon comes to the cross, and it is cited as supporting material for the application rather than as the sermon's climactic proclamation.

1. **Psalm 24:7-10 (the King of Glory entrance) is entirely absent:** The psalm's own answer to "who may ascend?" is not "a person of clean hands" — it is "the King of Glory." The human qualification section (vv.3-5) ends with an impossibility ("our righteousness is as filthy rags") and the psalm resolves that impossibility by the arrival of the King who has won the right to enter. ### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher has an excellent pastoral instinct for idolatry diagnosis. The sermon works only one half of the psalm's own argument. The result is a sermon that creates conviction without providing the gospel relief that the text itself provides. The preacher may not realize that the King of Glory section of Psalm 24 is not a separate topic — it is the psalm's own answer to everything the first section raises.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The idolatry application is strong enough that it can feel like the sermon has done its work — the congregation has been convicted and searched, and the writing exercise has created genuine engagement. This creates the impression of a complete homiletical arc. But conviction without the gospel is law without grace, and the sermon's final sentence ("all of that starts with the gospel") is a road sign pointing to a destination the sermon never reaches.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Psalm 24:7-10 — "Lift up your heads, O gates! And be lifted up, O ancient doors, that the King of Glory may come in. Who is this King of Glory? The LORD, strong and mighty, the LORD, mighty in battle!" — is the supreme missed opportunity of this sermon, and one of the great missed opportunities in the batch. The psalm's own structure answers the question "who can ascend?" with the arrival of the King of Glory who does not need clean hands to earn entrance — He is the one before whom the gates must lift their heads. Jesus Christ, entering Jerusalem on Palm Sunday, enters as this King. He who is the King of Glory descends to where we are, earns clean hands on our behalf, dies for our filthy-rags righteousness, rises in triumph, and now the ancient doors are opened before Him. The sermon raises the qualification problem and then gestures toward the gospel — when the gospel was already in the text, six verses away.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Develop vv.7-10 as the sermon's climax and the gospel's arrival:** "The psalm raises a problem no one in this room can solve on our own: Who can ascend? Nobody. Our righteousness is filthy rags. And then the psalm does something extraordinary — it doesn't answer the question by telling us to try harder. It answers by announcing an arrival. 'Who is the King of Glory? The LORD, strong and mighty.' The one who can ascend is not you with clean hands. The one who ascends is the King who has won the battle. And Jesus Christ is that King."

2. **Connect the imputed righteousness point explicitly to the King of Glory:** "The only way our hands are clean before God is because of the King who has clean hands — and who imputes His righteousness to everyone who trusts in Him. John Piper is right. The perfect righteousness of Christ is imputed to us. And that imputation is what makes the psalm's promise available: the King of Glory enters — and He brings His people in with Him."

3. **Return to the writing exercise at the conclusion:** "You wrote something down at the beginning of this sermon. 'God, I'll do anything except...' The King of Glory, strong and mighty, won the right to enter — and He came for you specifically, to deal with the exact idol you wrote down. He didn't come for people with clean hands. He came for people whose hands were dirty. Will you open the ancient door?"

4. **Replace the final sentence with a gospel paragraph:** Do not end on "all of that starts with the gospel." Preach the gospel. Two to three sentences minimum: "Here is where the gospel arrives in Psalm 24: you cannot ascend on your own. You cannot clean your own hands. But the King of Glory has already won the battle — He went to the cross with our filthy rags and rose in triumph. And now He offers His righteousness to anyone who will open the door. That is where it starts. Have you opened the door?"
