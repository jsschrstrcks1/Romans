INTRODUCTION — THE FISH THAT TALKED TOO MUCH

An Indian legend about how man learned to fish. A man is sitting under a tree, and a fish comes
up and starts talking to him. They become fast friends and soon talk every day — about what it's
like to be a fish, and what it's like to be a person. The man learns what fish eat, what they
look for in a place to eat, how they think — basically what life as a fish is like. After a
week, the fish turns up to their meeting place and sees a red worm wiggling in the water.
Without thinking, the fish bites, and so man learned to fish.

The fish, in many ways, was caught in a trap of his own making. He perhaps trusted too easily,
and the man took advantage of this. I think sometimes we too get caught in traps of our own
making. We too like things we shouldn't. Some of us speed. Some of us worship the TV or other
things with our lives that we shouldn't. Anyway, that story fits pretty well with what we're
going to talk about tonight.

THE CONTEXT: ROMANS 1

As we talked about last week, Romans opens up with Paul talking about the Gospel. He references
some Old Testament verses regarding Christ. But then in verse 16, he starts talking about
salvation and righteousness. "The righteous will live by faith," Paul says. Paul then launches
into a discussion of the attributes of God that are obvious to all mankind — even the lost. In
fact, it says in verse 20 that they are "without excuse."

The people who were not Christians in Paul's day — and frankly today — accepted pretty much
universally that there was a God. Today there are a lot of people who think they are atheists
but who are not in fact atheists. You see, you can't prove a negative. You can't prove that a
person has never gone to some place or done a certain thing, because all you can say is that
while you were looking, that person has not done X, Y, or Z. An atheist cannot prove that God
does not exist; they can only say they have never knowingly had an experience with God — which
of course presupposes that a God exists, making them not an atheist but an agnostic. The
quickest way to convert an atheist to an agnostic is to ask them to disprove the existence of
God. If they are intellectually honest with you, they will concede the point.

The concept of atheism would have been foreign to the Romans — and even the Greeks, who had a
god for everything, and even a temple to gods they didn't yet know about (see Acts 17:23). So
while they knew that there was a God, they didn't honor him as God.

It's like the guy who goes walking down the beach and finds a watch — but instead of thinking
"huh, there must be a watchmaker," says "must've been a snail." So while they knew someone or
something made the watch, they attributed the watch's creation to a lump of wood or a great big
rock, rather than the true and living God who was making an effort to speak to them through the
beauty of creation.

THE FINGERPRINTS OF GOD IN CREATION

The earth is a remarkable feat of engineering. If we were much closer to the sun, we'd fry;
much further and we'd freeze. If we were located anywhere else in the Milky Way we would either
be so protected we couldn't study the cosmos, or so exposed to meteoroids and comets that life
would have gone extinct right away. We live in what's called the Goldilocks zone — meaning all
the conditions for life to be possible are met, and not only that: we live in an ideal spot to
be able to study our surroundings. How can someone look on the beauty of a sunset and not
imagine God at work with a paintbrush, or look at the complexity of a seashell, or our own
cells, and not see that God exists? God's orderly and creative fingerprints are all over
creation.

I've used this illustration before, but I want to share it again. My grandmother had this one
particular painting of Indian children. She had her kids and grandkids put their names on things
of hers that they wanted, so there would be no arguing, and so that before she passed she could
imagine us using these things of hers. So she had this painting and no one would speak up saying
they wanted it. A few months before she passed she offered it to me, saying she had poured so
much of herself into her paintings that she wanted them to go to someone who would appreciate
them. Of course I jumped at the chance. But the point is that the artist displays a bit of who
they are in their craft. Much the same way as grandma poured herself into her paintings, God
poured himself into the world to benefit us.

As is pointed out to Job in the Old Testament — and in the Chris Tomlin song "Indescribable" —
who else could have hung the stars in the sky? Told the lightning bolts where to go, built
storehouses in the heaven full of snow? Who else could have designed the Milky Way galaxy in
such a way that we would be protected enough from things crashing into us, but not so much that
we couldn't stand in awe of the power and majesty of our creator God?

Isaiah 46:10 — God says: "My purpose will stand, and I will do all that I please."
[NOTE: This is the NIV rendering. ESV reads: "my counsel shall stand, and I will accomplish all
my purpose." See correction log item 2.]

AW Pink wrote on this verse: "Subject to none, influenced by none, absolutely independent; God
does as He pleases, only as He pleases, always as He pleases."
[SOURCE VERIFIED: A.W. Pink, *The Attributes of God*, Ch. 6. Also in *The Sovereignty of God*
(1918), Ch. 1. See correction log item 3.]

Some people think that makes God capricious and unaccountable. However, a God who can design a
universe to work better than a fine Swiss watch — or even the human body, trillions of cells all
working together to repair themselves, to keep going, to be a reflection of God himself in a way
— even the tiniest living part of our body, the cell, reflects an incredible amount of
craftsmanship.

IRREDUCIBLE COMPLEXITY

Have you ever heard of the idea of irreducible complexity? In Darwin's day they had relatively
recently discovered the cell and were aware of its foundational nature as part of our bodies.
This was before DNA, and before they could see the smaller structures of the cell itself.
However, Darwin posited that if the cell was so complex that pieces couldn't be removed and the
cell still survive and serve its function, then evolution would be false.

A biologist and author — Michael Behe — started studying the cell and came to the conclusion
that it was indeed irreducibly complex. That is, if any parts are cut off, the cell either dies
or ceases to function in its expected way. Damaged cells are something we are all aware of —
cancer, for instance, is one of the things that can happen when a cell becomes damaged. Behe
went on to compare the cell to a mousetrap.

A mousetrap has been refined over the years to basically its most simplistic version. Behe
argued that in order for a mousetrap to work you need a platform, a spring, a retaining bar, a
catch, and a hammer. You can reconfigure those parts or combine them, but in the end you still
have those five functions being served. If you try to remove any of the parts, the trap ceases
to be a trap. Remove the spring — you might use it as a paperweight, but not a trap. Remove the
catch or the retaining bar — without the ability to store and release power against the mouse
the trap ceases to function. Without a base, the device would be unable to store and release the
power of the spring against the neck of the mouse. Truly, with any fewer parts serving any
fewer functions, the device loses purpose.

Behe argues that a cell is like the trap — in order for it to survive there are something like
thirteen systems required, or the cell dies. I suspect that as we gain devices of increased
sensitivity we might learn there are even more things going on that we simply can't see yet.
[SOURCE VERIFIED: Michael Behe, *Darwin's Black Box* (1996). The mousetrap analogy and
five-component description are accurately represented. VERIFIED.]

[NOTE: The sermon then says "by Darwins own admission, the evolutionary model is flawed and
impossible." This overstates Darwin's position. Darwin wrote a conditional falsification
criterion: "If it could be demonstrated that any complex organ existed which could not possibly

down" (*Origin of Species*, 1859). This is a hypothetical test, not an admission. See

In any case, by Darwin's own admission, the evolutionary model — if a cell is truly irreducibly
complex — breaks down. I used to have a T-shirt that said on the front, "I believe in the Big
Bang Theory." On the back: "God spoke and — Bang — it happened."

Just in the things we've studied these last couple years: we know that God is sovereign over
human will — look at Jonah. He's sovereign over where we go and why. He's sovereign over the
wind and waves (remember Peter?). He's sovereign over the birds (Elijah), the brook, the rain,
the tree, the worm. He is sovereign over all creation. How can we doubt he'd be sovereign over
us too?

IDOLATRY AND THE DARKENED HEART

So in light of the fingerprints of God being manifest upon all creation, some people refuse to
acknowledge God as God. Romans 1:22 — "Claiming to be wise, they became fools." It says their
foolish hearts were darkened. They worshiped idols.

And before you think we don't have trouble with idols today, let me point something out. I had
a professor say one time that if you show me your checkbook, I'll show you your priorities. In
America we may not worship statues of wood — though some do. Instead we worship our
independence, we worship our comfort, we worship money. In a practical sense Americans are not
much different than those who used to worship Baal — instead we're worshiping consumerism.
Consider this past week: how much time have you spent watching TV, or playing video games, or
pursuing your own entertainment? How much money do you spend on such things? Now think about
how much time you've spent reading your Bible? In prayer?

We can be worshippers of idols. We can do like Esau did — substitute the best thing we have
going for something that might satisfy for a moment but leaves you suffering for an eternity.

So these people Paul was talking about were wrapped up in idols, with wicked, foolish, and
darkened hearts. In verse 24 it says they were given up to the lusts of their hearts. Then it
specifically mentions a few things — impurities, dishonorable passions, dishonoring their
bodies — and finally it mentions homosexuality. Then it describes these people as being full of
all manner of unrighteousness, evil, covetousness, malice, envy, murder, strife, deceit,
maliciousness, gossip, slander, hatred of God, insolence, haughtiness, boastfulness, inventors
of evil, disobedience to parents, foolishness, faithlessness, heartlessness, ruthlessness. They
know what they do is wrong — and not only continue doing these things themselves but give
approval to, and encourage others to do the same. Ephesians 2 describes these people as
"children of wrath." In fact earlier in this chapter, verse 18 says: "The wrath of God is
revealed against" these very things and more.

THE WRATH OF GOD

We talked about hell a while back, and I'm not sure we can fully understand or come to grips
with its depths. When a God can speak galaxies full of millions of stars into existence in an
instant, what then will the place of punishment be like?

Jonathan Edwards said we dangle as from a string over a fiery pit, and then the string catches
fire.
[NOTE: This is a paraphrase/summary of *Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God* (1741), not a
direct quotation. The actual sermon uses the image of a spider held over a fire. Present in
future use as a paraphrase of Edwards. See correction log item 4.]

Life is so short. I have this mental picture of people marching blindfolded off a cliff. Those
of us who believe can see what's happening — billions of people actively moving towards their
eternal death. Those of us who can see our friends and family members marching off the cliff
need to be helping them remove the blindfolds. What man in this room wouldn't lay his life down
in a heartbeat to protect his kids from a gunman? I have no doubt that any of us would do so
in an instant to save their kids and grandkids. Yet how many of us have loved ones facing
eternal mortal danger with whom we've never shared the gospel? I know I have people like that
in my life. I think we probably all do. Have you shared the gospel with someone this week?

GOSPEL CLOSE

You have to know Jesus to be able to share him.

Maybe you are sitting there tonight and you've never come to know Jesus as your Savior. If you
haven't, or if you aren't sure, this part is for you.

Scripture says in Romans 3:23 — for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God. Everyone
everywhere has done things that displease God. I have. You have. Everyone has. It's a universal
truth. Because of this sin in our lives, we are incapable of reaching God's standard. It's as
though everyone is supposed to be wearing pure white clothes, and every time we sin, it's like
we've gone outside and rolled in the mud — skidded in the grass and ruined the clothes. Now
instead of white and clean and pure, our clothes are black, wet, and green. Clearly without help
we can't be clean. In many ways it's like we're a rock on the ground and God's standard is way
up here. The rock can't jump up to my hand on its own. It needs to be picked up. The same is
true of us.

Romans 6:23 says that the wages of sin is death. So we spend our whole lives working — trying
to earn a wage. Except that apart from God, the best of what we can come up with is basically
filthy rags. Because of our sin, the wage we have earned is death. On your own, handed a
screwdriver and a ratchet, you'd never be able to build the Brooklyn Bridge. Likewise we could
never earn our way into heaven.

Scripture says in Ephesians 2:8-9 that by grace we have been saved, through faith — it is the
gift of God, lest no man should boast.
[NOTE: Paraphrase. ESV: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your

When God created us with free will, he gave Adam the ability to reject God, to reject God's
ways, to reject eternity in heaven with God. He gave Adam, in a sense, the ability to adopt for
himself a sin nature. This is a doctrine the Westminster Confession of Faith and our own 1689
London Baptist Confession hold in common. A Reformed theologian put it this way:

Possible sources: Robert Haldane (*Exposition of the Epistle to the Romans*), R.L. Dabney

"God is not the author of sin. The creature is: and therefore can originate an act which is not
God's. We must, therefore, make the distinction between God's permitting and causing the acts

Man, then, is the author of sin. God merely permitted us to author it. Adam then created for us
a sin nature — one imputed to us, though one which we would all (like Adam) have willingly
adopted on our own.

Sin then is a trap of our own creation, that we readily and greedily run into with abandon. A
trap incapable of being unlocked from the inside. We — like the fish — could never escape on
our own. And so we needed a Savior.

Maybe tonight you identify with the fish. Maybe you feel like your sin is under your control —

If you have never come to a point in your spiritual life where you've been saved, then in a

If you have accepted Christ but your life looks more like the person described in Romans 1 than

If you're looking for a church to join — come talk to me. One of the things I love about this
