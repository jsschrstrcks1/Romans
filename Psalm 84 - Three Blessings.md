## The Sons of Korah

We're going to be in Psalm 84 today as we continue to read through the Bible.

While you're turning there, I want to talk about the authors of this psalm. See right there by the chapter heading — *A Psalm of the Sons of Korah.*

Korah was a famous rebel in Scripture. Back under Moses, he led a coalition of 250 men of renown to try to overthrow Moses's leadership. Korah had the gall to say that leading the Israelites out of Egypt was no big deal — anyone could have done it. He went further, essentially accusing Moses of lying: claiming to speak on behalf of God when really, Moses was just speaking for himself.

Ultimately, Moses ordered Korah and the other families involved to separate themselves from the congregation and their belongings lest they be consumed in the judgment. Moses prayed, and the ground opened up and swallowed Korah, the others, and everything they owned.

David Platt, former president of the International Mission Board, has said this would have been something every Israelite remembered for generations — that Korah was Israel's most famous rebel. He might have been their Benedict Arnold. That name is synonymous with traitor. We all know it.

And yet — what a testament to God's forgiveness. Korah angered God to the point where he made the earth open up and swallow him. But Korah's kids — literally the sons of Korah — become the gatekeepers of the temple. They served by standing at the doors. They made bread for the priests. Scripture says they lodged near the house of God, charged with watching over it.

I think this is a mark of God's complete forgiveness. He doesn't hold the father's sin over the children's heads. He doesn't hold grudges. And the sons of Korah — they love their job.

It strikes me that there's something unique in this passage. Three promised blessings.

Let's go ahead and read Psalm 84. Watch for the blessings — that's where we're going to camp out today.

## Blessing One — Dwelling in God's House (v. 4)

What does it mean to dwell in the house of the Lord?

For the sons of Korah, it was literal. They slept there. Their job was there. Their whole life centered around the house of the Lord — and they loved it.

Verse 10 says *a day in your courts is better than a thousand elsewhere.* Let's sit with that for a moment.

At this point in Israel's history, the house of the Lord was the place where God's presence dwelt in a particular, powerful way. He was still God everywhere — in heaven, on earth, in everything. But the temple was a visible, physical reminder of that presence. A place you could actually go to be near God. The Bible says *draw near to God and he will draw near to you.* The sons of Korah got to live there.

One thousand days is roughly three years. The sons of Korah are saying: better is a single day near the presence of God than three years in any other position. Better a day in God's courts than three years as king of Israel. Better a single day near God than three years as a wealthy man who doesn't have to work. Better to be near God's presence than to spend three years as a billionaire adventurer doing whatever you want.

What's your dream? Living with family, in peace and prosperity? Living on an island with servants? Some of you want all the cats. Someone else wants all the dogs. Fine. All of that sounds good.

The sons of Korah would say — no, that's fine, but I'd rather spend a single day near the presence of the Lord than a thousand days fulfilling that dream.

What are some practical ways we can dwell in God's house today? What are ways we can draw near to God right now?

*[Congregational response — worship, reading God's word, fellowship with other believers, sharing the gospel with someone.]*

That's right. Worship. Reading God's word. Fellowship with other believers. Telling people about Jesus. All good ways to draw near to God. All good ways to dwell in the house of the Lord.

Before we move on, look at the end of verse 4 again: *ever singing your praise.*

And verse 2 — *my heart and flesh sing for joy to the living God.*

This son of Korah loved being in God's presence, and he loved to sing God's praise. His heart and his flesh sing for joy. One commentator has said that "sing for joy" is actually a weaker translation than the Hebrew warrants — that the original language here describes something closer to a loud cry. It's a longing for God that overflows and pours out of the psalmist, not just in song but in shouts.

This isn't lazy, going-through-the-motions Christianity. This is exuberant, joyful, alive worship of God.

When was the last time you felt that way?

If you're not enjoying the presence of God, it's usually — not always, but usually — because of idolatry in the heart. We come to church and we're more focused on the fight we had with our spouse before we left, or the argument with the kids, or what's on TV tonight, or some game. We miss the joy of the Lord because, in John Calvin's words, our hearts are factories of idolatry. We allow our desires, our hopes, our joys to be stolen by these idols, and where we should be finding joy in the Lord, we go through empty motions.

Idols are vampires. They drain the joy out of our interaction with the One who matters. An idol can be anything — and it doesn't have to be something bad. It can be something good. Your kids. A hobby. A relationship. Anything that takes our focus from God and puts it somewhere else. Even good things — things that ought to be celebrated — can take the place of God in our hearts and become an idol.

If you were to write down everything you spent time on this week, it would be a reflection of your priorities. Is God even on the list? The average person watches three hours of TV per day and another three hours on social media. But the average churchgoing person doesn't come close to spending that much time in the Bible or in prayer. Our relationship with God is more than showing up once or twice a week. If you did that to your spouse, what kind of marriage would you have? And yet that's exactly what we do to God. We allow idols to steal time, energy, and attention that rightfully belongs to him.

Keep your focus on God, and off your idols, and you'll be blessed.

## Blessing Two — Strength in the Lord (v. 5)

When we're consumed by idols, are we strong in the Lord? Not a chance.

But God is right there, waiting for us to turn back to him. Let me have some of you look up a few verses.

**Psalm 46:1-3** — *God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble. Therefore we will not fear, though the earth gives way and the mountains be moved into the heart of the sea, though its waters roar and foam, though the mountains tremble at its swelling.*

**Proverbs 18:10** — *The name of the Lord is a strong tower; the righteous man runs into it and is safe.*

**Nehemiah 8:10** — *Do not be grieved, for the joy of the Lord is your strength.*

**Isaiah 41:10** — *Fear not, for I am with you; be not dismayed, for I am your God; I will strengthen you, I will help you, I will uphold you with my righteous right hand.*

**Exodus 15:2** — *The Lord is my strength and my song, and he has become my salvation; he is my God, and I will praise him, my father's God, and I will exalt him.*

God is our refuge, our strength, our help in trouble, the one who takes away our fear. Even when the world crumbles away — when the mountains fall into the sea and the waters roar — he is shelter. He is our strength.

Don't gloss over that. The God of the universe — and we've talked about how staggering the universe is — takes time to pay attention to *you* in your trial. To uphold you with his righteous right hand. He gives us victory.

In this room right now are stories of how God has brought people victory. Victory over alcohol. Victory over drugs. Victory over situations that could have crushed you. God does that. But the deepest victory here is the one we have over sin — over our own flesh, over the desire for idols. We can't do that on our own. We need to be upheld by his hand. We need shelter in that storm.

And for that we should praise him.

Actually, it creates a feedback loop. Ask the sound guys about feedback — get the microphone too close to the speaker and you get that loud, building squeal. The mic picks up the sound from the speaker and sends it back through, over and over, louder and louder. With the sound board that's something to avoid. With God, it's something to pursue.

*The joy of the Lord is our strength. The Lord is our strength and our song.* Worshipping God gets us through things. It gives us strength when we are weak, and it gives us more to worship God for. If you lack strength, start there — start with worship. God is our refuge and our strength. Of whom shall we be afraid? Even when the earth gives way and the mountains fall into the sea, when our joy is in the Lord, what do we have to fear? *If God is for us, who can be against us?*

## Blessing Three — Trust in the Lord (v. 12)

Finally — *blessed is the one who trusts in you.*

This is all about the gospel.

One more passage. Psalm 42:1-2 and 5.

*As a deer pants for flowing streams, so pants my soul for you, O God. My soul thirsts for God, for the living God. When shall I go and appear before God?*

And verse 5:

*Why are you cast down, O my soul, and why are you in turmoil within me? Hope in God; for I shall again praise him, my salvation and my God.*

All of these blessings come inside a relationship with God. The joy of the Lord comes from *knowing* the Lord. Joy in the Lord leads to strength in the Lord. And all of it flows from trust in the Lord.

## Source Notes — Verify Before Print Use

**CRITICAL — "ten thousand days" vs. the text:** Psalm 84:10 ESV reads "a day in your courts is better than **a thousand** elsewhere." The Hebrew is מֵאָלֶף (*mē'ālep*) — "than a thousand." This is consistent across ESV, NIV, NASB, and KJV. The sermon uses "10,000 days" throughout. That number is not in the text — it's off by a factor of ten. 1,000 days ≈ 2.7 years, not 27. The rhetorical comparison ("better than 30 years as Elon Musk") does not hold with the actual verse. Needs correction before preaching or print — either adjust the illustration to ~3 years, or use a different number for the comparison without claiming it comes from the text. Corrected to 1,000 / ~3 years above, but flagging for pastoral review before use.

**Korah's rebellion (Numbers 16):** The characterization of Korah's accusation ("anyone could have done it," "Moses was speaking for Moses") is a homiletical expansion. Numbers 16:3 records the actual accusation: "You have gone too far! For all in the congregation are holy, every one of them, and the Lord is among them. Why then do you exalt yourselves above the assembly of the Lord?" (ESV). The expansion is reasonable but not a direct quote from the text; label accordingly if preaching closely.

**"Moses ordered Korah and two others families":** The two other principals were Dathan and Abiram (Numbers 16:1). Numbers 16:23-33 records Moses separating the congregation, then the ground opening. Numbers 26:11 confirms "the sons of Korah did not die." History is accurate.

**David Platt / Benedict Arnold comparison:** Platt is correctly identified as former president of the International Mission Board (SBC, 2014–2019). The Benedict Arnold / Korah comparison is attributed to Platt without a source. Needs specific citation (sermon title, series, book) before print attribution.

**"Allowed to live in the priests' quarters":** 1 Chronicles 9:26-27 says the chief gatekeepers "lodged around the house of God" and were charged with watching over it. The phrase "priests' quarters" is stronger than the text supports. Consider softening to "lodged near the house of God" or "lodged around the temple complex."

**"One commentator said" (the Hebrew means a loud cry):** Anonymous. Needs attribution before print use. The Hebrew רָנַן (*ranan*, as in the broader genre) or the specific word used in Psalm 84:2 should be checked. The claim that ESV's "sing for joy" understates the original is not uncommon in Psalm scholarship — but an anonymous attribution is not citable.

**Calvin quote — "factories of idolatry":** The common attribution is *Institutes of the Christian Religion* 1.11.8. Calvin's Latin has been translated various ways: "the human mind is a perpetual forge of idols," "the human heart is an idol factory," "the human mind is a factory of idols." Exact wording varies by translator. Do not quote in print without citing a specific translation and edition. The paraphrase "our hearts are factories of idolatry" is consistent with the substance but needs a footnoted source.

**Scripture translations — "strength" section:** The five verses quoted in the strength section appear to follow NIV rather than ESV. Specific differences: Psalm 46:1 ("ever-present" NIV vs. "very present" ESV); Isaiah 41:10 ("So do not fear" NIV vs. "fear not" ESV); Exodus 15:2 ("he has given me victory" NIV vs. "he has become my salvation" ESV). All five corrected to ESV above.

**Psalm 42:1-2, 5:** Quoted text follows NIV. ESV used above. Key differences: "As a deer pants" vs. "As the deer pants"; "flowing streams" vs. "streams of water"; "When shall I go and appear before God?" vs. "When can I go and meet with God?"; "Hope in God" vs. "Put your hope in God"; "my salvation" vs. "my Savior." ESV used above.

**1 Corinthians 11:23-29:** Quoted text follows NIV. ESV used above. Key differences include "passed on" (NIV) vs. "delivered" (ESV); "whenever you drink it" (NIV) vs. "as often as you drink it" (ESV); "guilty of sinning against" (NIV) vs. "guilty concerning" (ESV); "everyone ought to examine themselves" (NIV) vs. "let a person examine himself" (ESV); "without discerning the body of Christ" (NIV) vs. "without discerning the body" (ESV).

**Romans 8:31 paraphrase:** "If God is for us, who can be against us?" — quoted from memory at the end of the strength section. ESV Romans 8:31: "If God is for us, who can be against us?" — exact match. Fine to use.
