Thankfulness is hard when you're down. But it's appropriate.

I heard a story this week about a pastor whose little two-year-old daughter was diagnosed with terminal leukemia. Of course that reminded me of Bob Clark and his son Rob, who faced a terminal diagnosis of leukemia in Rob. If you know Bob, you know that God spared Rob, and he went on to get a Master's degree in counseling, and he's practicing in Texas. That story could have gone wildly differently but for the grace and mercy of God.

Likewise this little girl was diagnosed with terminal cancer, but at a much younger age — two years old. Worse, normally you have your immune system to help fight cancer, but in this case the baby was born with the cancer already growing in her little body. This meant that perhaps his wife had leukemia as well, and just hadn't discovered it yet. Testing was done the same day, while they were sitting there weeping with their little toddler given this death sentence. Come to find out, the mother had it too. Both were going to be a difficult match for donor marrow, both needed the same type. No one in their family was a match — none of the other four children, nor the brothers or sisters or parents were suitable donors. They dosed both with chemotherapy. They dosed both with radiation, trying to extend their lives long enough to find a match. Even with a match they were told that if both lived another five years, both would most likely make it an additional fifteen, short of some other tragedy happening. The chemo had limited effect. The radiation seemed to slow it, but not stop it. Things kept deteriorating.
