## People Group of the Week: The Tarkahn of Pakistan

Today we are going to spend some time in prayer for the **Tarkahn of Pakistan**. They are part of the Punjabi people of western Pakistan. As best we can tell, there are approximately three million Tarkahn in Pakistan — and not a single known believer in Jesus Christ among them.

Reaching them is complicated by the closed nature of Pakistan as a country, and by difficult terrain inside it. The Tarkahn are largely comprised of carpenters and other skilled artisans. But they have also made a name for themselves in fighting and warfare. A straightforward mission effort isn't going to work. Because of Pakistan's restrictions on foreign entry, someone wanting to reach these people would need to find a genuine need — clean water, western medicine — and meet it first. Short of something along those lines, they are not going to let one of us in.

So we need to pray. Pray that God would raise up people who can meet both the physical and spiritual needs of the Tarkahn. That those workers would be successful in sharing the gospel. That we would see many lost people won to Christ.

## The Baptist Faith and Message, Article XI

Our Baptist Faith and Message says this about Evangelism and Missions:

> *It is the duty and privilege of every follower of Christ and of every church of the Lord Jesus Christ to endeavor to make disciples of all nations. The new birth of man's spirit by God's Holy Spirit means the birth of love for others. Missionary effort on the part of all rests thus upon a spiritual necessity of the regenerate life, and is expressly and repeatedly commanded in the teachings of Christ. The Lord Jesus Christ has commanded the preaching of the gospel to all nations. It is the duty of every child of God to seek constantly to win the lost to Christ by verbal witness undergirded by a Christian lifestyle, and by other methods in harmony with the gospel of Christ.*

## I. What Missions Is — and What It Isn't

I've heard about an odd trend lately. There are "missions" trips being advertised to the financially comfortable — trips to work in an orphanage, or do something genuinely philanthropic but not really missions-related.

Hear me: if you are going to feed the hungry, that is good. But it is not missions. If you are going to build walls for a school, that is good. But it does not make you a missionary any more than sitting in a garage makes you a truck.

A missionary once told me that being a missionary means living the way you should live anyway — but in another country. And that necessarily involves evangelism. The gospel has to go somewhere in it, or it's humanitarian work. Which we need. But let's call it what it is.

I was watching Southeastern's Convocation this week. They asked a packed chapel: *whoever has shared the gospel in the last four months, please stand.* Almost directly in front of the camera were three young men who I suspect were regretting their choice of seats. Pretty much everyone else in the room stood, while those three remained seated.

Being active in sharing the gospel is practically a requirement for seminary admission. As part of the application you have to discuss how often you share the gospel, with whom, and how it went. The school's expectation that students be involved in evangelism is explicit. I admired those young men's honesty. I also noticed Danny Akin from his perch on the stage, looking to see who wasn't standing. I suspect they got called to his office for a conversation later.

But before you start thinking evangelism is only for pastors or missionaries — it isn't. Evangelism is for everyone.

## II. The Great Commission — Five Times

Who here knows the Great Commission off the top of their head?

It is in the Bible five times. I'll take any of the five.

We're going to briefly look at all five this morning. Open your Bibles to Matthew 28:18–20.

---

> **Matthew 28:18–20** — And Jesus came and said to them, "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."

> **Mark 16:15–18** — And he said to them, "Go into all the world and proclaim the gospel to the whole creation. Whoever believes and is baptized will be saved, but whoever does not believe will be condemned. And these signs will accompany those who believe: in my name they will cast out demons; they will speak in new tongues; they will pick up serpents with their hands; and if they drink any deadly poison, it will not hurt them; they will lay their hands on the sick, and they will recover."

> **Luke 24:45–49** — Then he opened their minds to understand the Scriptures, and said to them, "Thus it is written, that the Christ should suffer and on the third day rise from the dead, and that repentance and forgiveness of sins should be proclaimed in his name to all nations, beginning from Jerusalem. You are witnesses of these things. And behold, I am sending the promise of my Father upon you. But stay in the city until you are clothed with power from on high."

> **John 20:21–23** — Jesus said to them again, "Peace be with you. As the Father has sent me, even so I am sending you." And when he had said this, he breathed on them and said to them, "Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven them; if you withhold forgiveness from any, it is withheld."

> **Acts 1:8** — But you will receive power when the Holy Spirit has come upon you, and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the end of the earth.

---

Before we dig into these passages, let's figure out who Jesus is talking to.

Matthew says "Jesus came and said to them." Whose *them*? The disciples.

Mark 16:15 — "he said to them." Whose *them*? The disciples.

Luke 24:45 — "he opened their minds and said to them." Whose *them*? The disciples.

John 20:21 — "Jesus said to them again." Whose *them*? The disciples.

Acts 1:8 — "you will receive power when the Holy Spirit has come upon you." Who receives the power, and when? The disciples, when the Holy Spirit comes upon them.

Now — what is a disciple? A follower of Christ. A mature Christian is one who has been discipled and is capable of making disciples of their own. And the Holy Spirit enters the believer at the moment of salvation. So every believer has already had the Holy Spirit come upon them.

I think it is safe to say that this Great Commission — one of the last recorded commands of Jesus before his ascension — applies to us today.

So the next time you want us to pray for your lost friend, we will pray for your lost friend. But we need to be praying for you too. Because maybe — just maybe — God put you in that lost friend's life so that *you* could share the gospel with them.

Maybe you are the only Bible your lost friend has ever read.

Maybe you are everything they know about Christianity.

## III. The Privilege of Going — and the Ease of Neglect

Evangelism is a tremendous privilege and blessing to those who participate. It is also terribly easy to neglect.

Most people, when asked, say they don't know how to share the gospel.

Have you heard of a man named Francis Schaeffer? He was a thinker who did more than almost anyone in the twentieth century to call the church back to what Scripture actually says. He ran a retreat center in Switzerland called L'Abri, where he would bring pastors and others in for rest, discipleship, renewal — real engagement with the Word. He deeply impacted almost everyone who was in active ministry in the generations preceding mine. The missions organization I served with for a time — it seemed like everyone had been directly touched by him personally, by his books, or by his daughter who now teaches in his place.

Someone once asked Schaeffer: if you had an hour to share the gospel with someone, what would you say?

⚠️ *[Verification flag — see top of document. This quote is widely attributed to Schaeffer but unverified to a primary source. Use with appropriate pastoral discretion.]*

He said: *"I would spend the first fifty-five minutes explaining man's bondage to sin and his state before a holy God — before explaining the gospel itself in the remaining five minutes."*

Think about that. Who can tell me about man's bondage to sin? What do we bring to our salvation? What are we, apart from Christ? What chapter and verse spells this out most plainly?

Ephesians 2.

Read it. The whole chapter is extraordinary. That is the foundation. That is what Schaeffer is pointing at — we have to understand the depth of our condition before the good news of the gospel means anything at all.

Then you share the gospel. I like to work through Romans 3:23, Romans 6:23, Romans 5:8–9, John 3:16, Ephesians 2:8–9 — though the whole chapter is brilliant — and I close with Romans 10:9–10.

---

## IV. What God Is Already Doing

It is not the words of a prayer that saves someone. It is that God has been at work in their heart — and they want to follow him. The changes God has already begun to work in their heart and their life are what make salvation possible.

Here's the good news about that.

If God is already at work in someone's heart, there is nothing you can say that will stop that process. And if he hasn't started that work yet, all you can do is be obedient and share the gospel anyway. What they are rejecting when they say no is not you. It's Jesus. What they are fighting against when they say they don't want to come to church isn't you. It's Jesus.

But it is still important that they hear the gospel.

For one thing, you and I cannot know in whom Christ is already at work. You never know. Secondly, we have been commanded by our Savior to be involved in this ministry of finding those lost people in whom he's already begun working. We have to find them. We have to tell them about Jesus.

---

## V. He Said Go

Let me give you a different angle on this.

You all have kids — or have had kids at some point. How would you feel about a child you had told to do something five times, who just kept sitting there on the couch and disobeying? How are we any different when we neglect or refuse to share the gospel?

Jesus himself said, *Go therefore.*

He didn't say, "By the way, if you have time." He didn't say, "When it's convenient." He didn't say, "When you feel like it." He didn't say, "If work isn't too hard, would you mind doing something for me?" He said *Go.*

He didn't say Go after work. He said Go. You need to be telling people about Christ at work.

He didn't say Go if you're healthy. You need to be telling everyone you encounter about Christ. Every illness is an opportunity to share the gospel with someone you might not encounter any other way. If you are in the hospital, maybe you're there to share the gospel with your nurse. With your doctor. Maybe your surgeon. Maybe your sickness isn't about you — maybe it is about sharing the gospel with a lost person. Maybe God is calling you to be a missionary at that hospital. Maybe that is why you are sick.

He didn't say *maybe*. He said Go.

He didn't say Go while you're young. You should go while you're young — but in the Greek you can look at verbs and see what they're doing. Some verbs describe a short, completed action. Some verbs describe something that started and is ongoing — that you begin and keep doing without stopping. When Jesus said *Go*, which kind of verb do you think he used?

How long does he expect us to keep going?

We're talking about heaven and hell. If God says *Go*, isn't that more important than anything else in our lives?

---

## Closing

If I were to ask the same question they asked at that convocation chapel — how many of you have shared the gospel intentionally with someone recently — what would happen?

Would you be like those three boys in front of the camera?

Or would you be able to stand, because you have been obedient to Jesus's direct command:

> *"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."*

As the praise team comes up, I want you to think about the people in your life. Those you know are saved — and those you know are not.

Do the people you know who are saved share the gospel? If not, part of the Great Commission is to *teach them to obey* all that Christ commanded. You have a duty and a privilege toward those people. Go to them.

Think about the lost people you know — or those you aren't sure about. You have the privilege of telling them the best news they will ever hear. But you have to go find them and say it.

Normally, at this point in a service, I say: won't you come.

But today — I plead with you.

*Won't you go.*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title

**"An Earnest Commission Sermon Weakened by a Thin Gospel Center and a Missing Cross"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A warm and pastorally energetic call to evangelism that correctly establishes the universality of the Great Commission but relies on command and guilt more than the indicative power of the gospel as the fuel for obedience.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**PASS — with a significant caveat.**

The main point of the sermon (every believer is commanded to go and share the gospel) does broadly arise from the Matthew 28 text. The Scopus Test passes: "Jesus commands every disciple to make disciples of all nations, and that command extends to us today." The preacher submits to the text's imperative.

However, the sermon is built on five Great Commission passages treated primarily as parallel commands, not as a unified proclamation. The authority of Christ in Matthew 28:18 — the theological hinge on which the entire commission swings — receives almost no development. The gate passes, but narrowly.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 4 triggered — Application is largely generic.**

The application is earnest but falls short of specific. "Go tell people about Jesus" is the sum of it. The sermon does not press into the concrete conditions of this congregation's evangelistic cowardice or comfort. It names seminary students who weren't standing, but doesn't name the specific sins or fears that keep this congregation seated.

**Flag 5 is borderline.** The closing *"Won't you go"* is a genuine exhortation, and the specific list of gospel texts (Romans 3:23, 6:23, 5:8–9, etc.) is practical. But the sermon never moves to a clear "therefore, this week, here is what you will do." The exhortation is impressionistic rather than surgical.

**Score is not capped — Flag 4 is present but the application is not entirely absent, merely underdeveloped. This is noted as a significant weakness, not a hard cap trigger. Evaluator judgment: no cap applied, but this weakness substantially depresses scores in Categories 3, 4, and 5.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 14/25**

- Textual Fidelity: 6/10 — The five Great Commission passages are surveyed, but surveying is not the same as expounding. Matthew 28:18 ("All authority has been given to me") — the christological foundation of the entire commission — is read but not unpacked. The sermon uses the texts as authorization for a conclusion it already holds, rather than letting the texts drive the argument. The *therefore* (v. 19) hinges entirely on that authority claim; without developing it, the commission floats free of its theological anchor.
- Contextual Awareness: 3/5 — Some awareness (the disciples as original recipients, bridging to all believers). The resurrection context of Matthew 28 and the ascension context of Acts 1 are not developed.
- Exegetical Clarity: 2/5 — The note on the Greek verb (present imperative / continuous action) is the sermon's best exegetical moment — simple, accurate, and memorable. But it is the only one. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) — the specific condition this passage addresses — is gestured at (neglect, fear, busyness) but never named concretely.
- Logical Faithfulness: 3/5 — The move from "disciples received this command" to "all believers receive this command" is valid but too quick. No eisegesis detected, but the handling is thin.

**Category 2 — Theology & Doctrinal Integrity: 11/18**

- Christ-Centeredness: 4/7 — Christ appears as commander but not as Savior in any developed sense. The resurrection — the ground of Christ's authority and the content of the gospel witness — is mentioned in Luke 24 ("the Christ should suffer and on the third day rise") but not proclaimed. The Christology stays at the surface.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3 — There is no development of where the Great Commission sits in the redemptive arc. The connection between God's promise to Abraham (Genesis 12 — all nations blessed), the prophetic vision of the nations (Isaiah 49), and Christ's commission is entirely absent. This is a significant missed depth.
- Doctrinal Precision: 3/4 — The discussion of what we bring to salvation (Ephesians 2 reference) is sound. No errors detected.
- Confessional Alignment: 3/4 — The sermon is broadly orthodox. The note that God's prior work in the heart is what makes salvation possible gestures toward sovereign grace, but the confessional precision stops there.

**Category 3 — Gospel Centrality & Force: 7/15**

This is the sermon's most significant weakness. The sermon is *about* the gospel's spread without *being* a gospel proclamation. The list of Romans passages (3:23, 6:23, 5:8–9, etc.) is offered as a how-to toolkit, not preached. The cross is mentioned only in the quoted Luke 24 passage — "the Christ should suffer." Justification by faith is not articulated. There is no moment where the hearers themselves are confronted with their need for Christ and the provision of his blood.

- Clarity of the Gospel: 2/5 — The mechanics of the gospel are named but not preached.
- Necessity of Christ: 3/5 — Christ is essential to the commission structure but his necessity as the only Savior is assumed, not proclaimed.
- Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5 — The sermon orbits the command to share rather than the cross itself.

The Synagogue Test: A devout non-Christian could sit through most of this sermon without being scandalized by the blood of Christ. That is a diagnostic failure for a sermon explicitly about proclaiming the gospel.

**Category 4 — Conscience & Applicatory Force: 8/15**

- Heart Penetration: 3/5 — The illustration of the three seminary students is effective and honest. The "maybe you are the only Bible they've ever read" line has genuine pastoral force. The hospital illustration presses creatively. But the sermon does not reach the heart of the person sitting comfortably in the pew who has not shared the gospel in three years — it describes that person without cornering them.
- Specificity: 2/5 — The application is church-generic. It does not address the specific congregation's context, culture, or specific patterns of evangelistic neglect.
- Spiritual Differentiation (Perkins): 3/5 — The sermon addresses two categories meaningfully: the believer who has not been obedient, and the believer who doesn't know how. It does not address the backslider, the one who tried and was wounded, the one who is theologically uncertain about evangelism's urgency, or the one who has made peace with neglect. The closing paragraph about teaching the saved to share is a good differentiating move.

**Category 5 — Exhortation & Response: 5/8**

- Call to Response: 3/4 — The "Won't you go" closing is genuine and earned. The specific mention of going to the saved to teach them, and to the lost to tell them, is concrete. The closing could be sharpened into a specific personal commitment.
- Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 2/4 — The Romans road toolkit is helpful. But the sermon does not equip deeply — it lists verses without modeling how to actually use them in a conversation. The Schaeffer point (55 minutes on sin's bondage) is the most practically useful element, but it is handled as an illustration rather than a teaching.

**Category 6 — Structure & Logical Flow: 6/9**

- Clarity of Outline: 3/4 — The five sections are traceable. The movement from "What missions is" → "The five commission texts" → "The privilege and neglect" → "What God is doing" → "He said Go" is coherent.
- Progression: 2/3 — Section IV (What God Is Already Doing) slightly disrupts the flow. It introduces a comfort that slightly softens the imperative edge just before the climactic "He said Go" section.
- Unity: 1/2 — There is a slight tension between the missions-education tone of the early sections and the evangelism-imperative of the later sections. The sermon is one idea, but it sometimes feels like two audiences — those who confuse missions with humanitarianism, and those who are simply not evangelizing.

**Category 7 — Illustration & Imagination: 4/5**

- Relevance: 2/2 — The seminary convocation story is well-chosen. The hospital illustration is vivid and specific. The "sitting in a garage doesn't make you a truck" line is memorable.
- Clarity: 1/2 — The hospital illustration (your illness might be so you can witness to your surgeon) is bold and pastorally interesting, but it needs one clarifying move — acknowledging that God doesn't cause suffering only for evangelism purposes, lest it sound like God made you sick as a missions assignment.
- Memorability: 1/1 — "Won't you go" will land. The truck/garage line will be repeated.

**Category 8 — Sermonic Force: 3/5**

- Reads as Preaching: 2/2 — Yes. Oral cadence is present throughout. The rhetorical repetition in Section V ("He didn't say... He said Go") is the sermon's strongest homiletical moment.
- Conviction on the Page: 1/2 — The preacher's personal conviction is evident, but the text-grounding density is low. Many assertions float without anchoring back to the passage.
- Cadence and Breath: 0/1 — The "He said Go" section is the only place where sentence compression and climactic rhythm appear. Most of the sermon is even-paced, which flattens the moments that should punch.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily myself — my obedience, my neglect, my commission. Christ is the commander more than the Savior here. Honest answer: more of myself.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about mission. The sections on the Tarkahn and on prayer come closest to a felt weight of God's purposes. But the sermon as a whole is motivational in register, not doxological.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Mildly convicted. The seminary illustration lands. The "won't you go" close moves. But conviction is not sustained at depth because the cross that makes the command urgent and joyful is not preached.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The "He said Go" rhetorical sequence (Section V)** is the sermon's most powerful moment. The anaphoric repetition — "He didn't say Go after work... He didn't say Go if you're healthy..." — builds genuine urgency and the closing climax ("He said Go") earns its force. This is real preaching on the page.

2. **The Greek verb note** is simple, clear, and exactly the right kind of exegetical moment for a general congregation — it illuminates without overwhelming, and it extends the command's reach across a lifetime rather than a morning. This is Greek used as an unlock, not a decoration.

3. **The Tarkahn section and the closing application** — the movement from global (three million with no believers) to personal (the lost people in your life) is effective pastoral structure, and the closing differentiation between going to the saved to teach them and going to the lost to tell them is a genuinely pastorally useful distinction.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is described but not preached.** The sermon lists Romans 3:23, 6:23, 5:8–9 as a toolkit but never opens any of them. There is no moment where the congregation hears the good news — Christ's substitutionary death, his resurrection, justification by faith alone — proclaimed with force. A sermon about evangelism that does not itself evangelize is internally inconsistent. The greatest argument for sharing the gospel is a sermon that demonstrates how the gospel sounds when preached with power.

2. **Matthew 28:18 is the theological key that goes unused.** "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me" is the resurrection-vindication of the crucified Christ — the basis on which the entire commission rests. The sermon reads past it to get to "Go." But the authority is the reason the going matters. Without this development, the commission is reduced to a moral command rather than a resurrection imperative. The commission's power is its Christological foundation — and that foundation is not built.

3. **The conscience is described but not searched.** The sermon names the problem (people don't evangelize) but does not press into the specific heart-sins that produce evangelistic cowardice: the fear of man, the love of comfort, the unspoken belief that our social standing matters more than their soul. These are not merely motivational failures — they are theological ones. The sermon needed a moment that named what it actually costs a comfortable person to obey, and then named the grace that makes that cost bearable.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that this sermon makes the same error it warns against: it motivates by command and example (stand if you've shared the gospel, those seminary students, won't you go) rather than by the indicative power of the gospel. The BFM quote itself names it correctly — "The new birth of man's spirit by God's Holy Spirit means the birth of love for others" — but the sermon never returns to that ground. The new birth as the regenerate source of evangelistic love is stated in the quote and then abandoned. The sermon argues from duty when it could have argued from delight.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

Section V ("He Said Go") feels like the sermon's climax and is preached with real energy, but it is primarily law-work — command-stacking without relief. The repetition is rhetorically effective, but the accumulation of imperatives without the gospel indicative as their foundation creates pressure without power. The sermon feels bold at this point but is actually motivationally thin — command without enabling grace is the structural form of moralism, regardless of how earnestly it is delivered.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Luke 24:45–49 is the richest of the five commission texts and was barely touched. In it, the risen Christ opens the disciples' minds to understand the Scriptures, and the commission flows directly out of that illumination: "Thus it is written, that the Christ should suffer and on the third day rise from the dead, and that repentance and forgiveness of sins should be proclaimed in his name to all nations." This is the commission *with its gospel content embedded*. The message the disciples are commissioned to preach is spelled out in the passage: Christ's death, resurrection, and the offer of repentance and forgiveness. Developing Luke 24 would have given the sermon both its commission and its content — closing the gap between "why go" and "what to say when you get there."

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (4)

1. **Preach the gospel in the sermon itself.** Before listing the Romans Road as a toolkit, open one passage — Romans 5:8 is ideal ("God demonstrates his own love for us in this: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us") — and proclaim it. Let the congregation hear what evangelism sounds like. This closes the sermon's central internal contradiction and models the very thing it commands.

2. **Develop Matthew 28:18 before verse 19.** Spend two minutes on "All authority has been given to me." This is the resurrection claim. This is why the commission is not mere moral instruction but eschatological mandate. The *therefore* of verse 19 only makes sense if verse 18 has been established. Without it, the commission is thin.

3. **Add a conscience-searching passage addressed to evangelistic cowardice.** Name the fear of man by name. Use Proverbs 29:25 or John 12:42–43 (the leaders who believed but would not confess because they feared the Pharisees, loving the glory of men more than the glory of God). Corner the specific sin, then offer the specific cure: the love of Christ constrains us (2 Corinthians 5:14) — not the command of Christ alone, but the love of Christ.

4. **Return to the BFM's own sentence as a closing theological move.** "The new birth of man's spirit by God's Holy Spirit means the birth of love for others." This is the indicative foundation for the imperative. If the congregation is not going, the question is not just "will you obey?" — it is "has the new birth produced love in you?" That is a far more searching question than the one the sermon currently closes with, and it naturally leads to both examination and the gospel.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The exposition score does not reach the 20+ threshold. The sermon is not dead orthodoxy — it is more warm-but-thin than correct-but-bloodless.

The sermon carries genuine pastoral love for the lost and a real conviction about the urgency of eternity, but the weight of glory is muffled because the cross itself — the glory of God displayed in the face of Jesus Christ — is not the sermon's center of gravity; the command is.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This sermon is preached from a topical-series framework (Baptist Faith and Message, Article XI) using five texts as parallel supports, not as a single expounded passage. The rubric's exposition categories press hardest against single-passage expository sermons; applied to a topical sermon, the textual fidelity scoring appropriately penalizes breadth-without-depth rather than penalizing the topical form itself. The rubric fits this genre without distortion, though a topical sermon will inherently score differently in contextual awareness than a single-text exposition. The evaluation reflects that context.
