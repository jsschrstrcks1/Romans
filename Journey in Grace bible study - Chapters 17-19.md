Chapter 17 – The Danger of Pride: When Doctrine Inflates Instead of Humbling

Summary:

As Belcher's journey unfolds, a subtle but serious danger emerges — one that strikes closest to the men most committed to truth: pride disguised as orthodoxy.

The doctrines of grace were given to crush boasting. Yet in sinful hands, they can become fuel for superiority. Some men move from "I am saved by grace alone" to "I understand grace better than others." That is not Reformed theology. That is old-fashioned flesh wearing theological clothing.

Paul anticipated this. In a letter soaked in the doctrines of grace, he asked a question that should silence every theological ego:

"What do you have that you did not receive? If then you received it, why do you boast as if you did not receive it?" (1 Corinthians 4:7)

If total depravity is true, then we were no better.
If election is true, then we were not smarter.
If effectual calling is true, then we did not awaken ourselves.

Right doctrine should leave a man stunned, not smug.

True Calvinism produces:

* Humility — because we contributed nothing
* Gratitude — because we deserved nothing
* Compassion — because we were no different
* Evangelistic urgency — because God uses means

False or "over-balanced" Calvinism produces:

* Coldness toward the lost
* Intellectual arrogance over the uninformed
* Fatalism that excuses disobedience
* Withdrawal from gospel witness

The difference is not in the doctrine itself — it is in the heart that receives it.

This danger is not hypothetical. It lives and breathes in the academy. When the doctrines of grace first click for men in seminary — when election, effectual calling, and particular redemption suddenly come into focus — entirely too many of them grow arrogant. Rudely condescending. They walk out of a classroom on the doctrine of God's sovereignty and immediately lord it over everyone who hasn't caught up yet. They seem to think they have the whole Bible figured out, and everyone else is dumb, or backwards, or beneath serious conversation.

That is not the fruit of the Spirit. That is the flesh wearing a Geneva gown.

A man who truly understands total depravity should be the most humble man in the room — because he knows what he was. A man who truly understands unconditional election should be the most grateful man in the room — because he knows he did nothing to deserve it. And a man who truly understands effectual calling should be the most patient man in the room — because he remembers that God had to drag him to the light.

If your theology makes you look down on other believers, your theology hasn't gone deep enough. It has lodged in your intellect but hasn't reached your knees.

And Paul warned the Corinthians precisely about this: "This 'knowledge' puffs up, but love builds up" (1 Corinthians 8:1). A man can be right about every point of doctrine and still be useless to the kingdom if his knowledge has made him proud rather than pastoral.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Paul systematically dismantled human pride across thirteen chapters of Romans. Romans 1–3 proved that every mouth is stopped — Jew and Gentile alike stand guilty. Romans 3:27 asks the question that should echo in every man's conscience: "Then what becomes of our boasting? It is excluded." Romans 9 pressed further: salvation "depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy" (Romans 9:16). Romans 11:18 warned the Gentile believers: "do not be arrogant toward the branches." Romans 11:20 drove it home: "So do not become proud, but fear." And Romans 12:3 brought it into daily life: "not to think of himself more highly than he ought to think, but to think with sober judgment, each according to the measure of faith that God has assigned."

Our sermon series has traced this theme from midnight to morning — and at every stage, pride is what keeps men asleep in the dark. In Romans 14, we saw how pride wears church clothes: the strong despise the weak, the weak judge the strong, and both assume a role God never assigned them. The doctrines of grace don't create pride; they expose and destroy it — unless we twist them into trophies instead of receiving them as gifts.

Key Scriptures:

* 1 Corinthians 4:7 — "What do you have that you did not receive? If then you received it, why do you boast as if you did not receive it?"
* Romans 3:27 — "Then what becomes of our boasting? It is excluded."
* James 4:6 — "But he gives more grace. Therefore it says, 'God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble.'"
* 1 Corinthians 8:1 — "This 'knowledge' puffs up, but love builds up."

Chapter 18 – Unconditional Election: The Justice and Goodness of God

Summary:

Here Belcher presses into one of the most difficult and glorious doctrines — and the question every honest man eventually asks: "How is it fair for God to choose some and not others?"

Scripture reframes the question entirely: How would it be just for God to choose any?

Justice would condemn all. Mercy rescues some. And that mercy flows not from any foreseen goodness in man, but from God's own purpose, wisdom, love, and glory.

Election is not arbitrary — as if God flipped a coin. Nor is it reluctant — as if God wished He could save all but something prevented Him. Election is the free, purposeful, and loving act of a sovereign God who set His heart on a people before the foundation of the world. It flows from the depths of who God is, not from anything found in us.

R.C. Sproul wrote:

"If there is one single molecule in this universe running around loose, totally free of God's sovereignty, then we have no guarantee that a single promise of God will ever be fulfilled."

Election does not shrink God's goodness. It magnifies it. Because it means God's saving work depends entirely on God's saving character. And that is the only foundation that will never crack.

The real objection to election is not intellectual — it is moral. Men resist it because it strips away the last vestige of human contribution. And nothing offends pride like being told you had nothing to do with your own rescue.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Romans 9 was some of the most challenging ground we covered in our sermon series. Paul's answer to the fairness objection was immediate and absolute: "Is there injustice on God's part? By no means!" (Romans 9:14). He did not soften the doctrine or apologize for it. He appealed to God's own words to Moses: "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" (Romans 9:15).

The potter-and-clay argument of Romans 9:19-21 was not designed to silence questions with raw power, but to humble us before a wisdom we cannot fully comprehend. Paul's anguish for Israel (Romans 9:1-3) proved that belief in election does not kill compassion — it deepens it. A man who weeps for the lost while trusting God's sovereignty has found the biblical center.

And the doxology of Romans 11:33-36 — "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways!" — was not the conclusion of a man who had explained everything, but of a man overwhelmed by a God too great to explain. That is where doctrine is meant to land: not in a neat system, but on your knees.

Key Scriptures:

* Romans 9:14-16 — "Is there injustice on God's part? By no means! For he says to Moses, 'I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion.' So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy."
* Ephesians 1:4-5 — "He chose us in him before the foundation of the world, that we should be holy and blameless before him. In love he predestined us for adoption to himself as sons through Jesus Christ."
* Deuteronomy 29:29 — "The secret things belong to the LORD our God, but the things that are revealed belong to us and to our children forever, that we may do all the words of this law."
* Romans 11:33 — "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways!"

Chapter 19 – The Danger of Extremes: Hyper-Calvinism and Man-Centered Theology

Summary:

Belcher now exposes two opposite errors that threaten every man who takes theology seriously — and both are alive in our churches today.

Extreme 1 — Hyper-Calvinism (the "Over-Balanced" Error):

"If God wants to save the lost, He can do so on His own; I don't have to be involved."

That is not a caricature. It is a sentence that has been spoken from real pulpits, by real pastors, to real congregations. And it has done real damage — not just to the lost who never heard the gospel, but to the doctrine of sovereignty itself. Because when men twist God's sovereignty into an excuse for laziness, they hand the world a reason to reject what the Bible actually teaches.

This error has become, for many people, synonymous with Calvinism. Not because it is true of those who faithfully believe what Scripture teaches about election and grace — but because a few men across the centuries twisted what the Bible said to support their own passivity. And the reputation has stuck.

The hyper-Calvinist has so over-balanced his theology toward sovereignty that he has lost all urgency for the lost. He reasons: "If God has already chosen His elect, why preach? Why plead? Why weep?" He takes the doctrines of grace and builds a fortress — not for the glory of God, but for the comfort of his own system.

History gives us the clearest example of this error — and its cure.

In the late 1700s, before the modern missionary movement began, many Particular Baptists in England believed strongly in God's sovereignty — but some had drifted into exactly this kind of passivity. A young cobbler-turned-preacher named William Carey began studying Scripture and geography, and he became convinced that the Great Commission still applied — that Christians were obligated to take the gospel to the nations.

At a ministers' meeting in 1786, Carey raised the question of overseas missions. According to later missionary accounts, an older minister — often identified as John Ryland Sr. — reportedly rebuked him, saying something like: "Young man, sit down. When God pleases to convert the heathen, He will do it without your aid or mine."

Historians debate the exact wording — we don't have a transcript of the meeting. But whether those precise words were spoken or not, that sentiment absolutely reflected the attitude present in parts of the church at the time.

Carey did not reject God's sovereignty. He embraced it.

But he also saw something others had neglected: the same sovereign God who ordains the salvation of His elect also ordains the preaching of the gospel as the means.

So Carey wrote his famous pamphlet: An Enquiry into the Obligations of Christians to Use Means for the Conversion of the Heathens. The key phrase there is "use means." God uses means. He uses preaching. He uses prayer. He uses sending. He uses sacrifice.

In 1792, Carey helped found the Baptist Missionary Society. In 1793, he sailed to India. And from that small beginning, the modern missionary movement was born.

Carey did not believe missions were unnecessary because God is sovereign. He believed missions were unstoppable because God is sovereign.

That is the difference between hyper-Calvinism and historic Reformed theology.

Hyper-Calvinism says: "If God wants to save them, He will do it without me."
Biblical Calvinism says: "Because God has His people, I will go — and I will preach with confidence."

And history proved which vision bore fruit.

Extreme 2 — Man-Centered Arminianism (Dr. Bloom's Error):

"God has done His part; now it depends on man." This leads to pressure-driven evangelism, fragile assurance, and subtle pride in human decision. Dr. Bloom means well — he wants to honor human dignity and moral agency. But in doing so, he has made the Creator subordinate to the creature and salvation contingent on the very will that Scripture says is enslaved to sin.

Both errors distort biblical balance. Both fail the text. Both fail the lost.

Historic Reformed theology affirms: God ordains the ends — and the means. He ordains salvation. He ordains preaching. He ordains prayer. He ordains missions. The Great Commission was not optional. It was commanded by a sovereign King who told Paul in Corinth: "Do not be afraid, but go on speaking and do not be silent, for I am with you... for I have many in this city who are my people" (Acts 18:9-10) — and then sent Paul to find them through the means of preaching.

Spurgeon captured this balance with characteristic clarity in his Defense of Calvinism:

"That God predestines, and yet that man is responsible, are two facts that few can see clearly. They are believed to be inconsistent and contradictory to each other. If, then, I find taught in one part of the Bible that everything is fore-ordained, that is true; and if I find, in another part of the Bible, that man is responsible for all his actions, that is true; and it is only my folly that leads me to imagine that these two truths can ever contradict each other."

Biblical Calvinism produces bold evangelism, deep prayer, patient discipleship, and confidence in God's power. It never produces passivity. A man who truly believes God has His elect will preach more urgently, not less — because he knows the Word will not return void. And Paul himself endured everything — shipwreck, prison, beatings — "for the sake of the elect, that they also may obtain the salvation that is in Christ Jesus with eternal glory" (2 Timothy 2:10). Election did not make Paul passive. It made him unstoppable.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Paul himself modeled this balance throughout Romans. In Romans 9, he taught unconditional election with unflinching clarity — the potter and clay, the sovereign choice of Jacob over Esau. Then in Romans 10:1, without pausing to reconcile the tension, he wrote: "Brothers, my heart's desire and prayer to God for them is that they may be saved." The same apostle who wrote "it depends not on human will or exertion" also asked: "How then will they call on him in whom they have not believed? And how are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard? And how are they to hear without someone preaching?" (Romans 10:14).

Paul never resolved the tension philosophically — he lived it pastorally. Romans 11 ends not with a systematic chart but with a doxology: a man on his knees before a God whose ways are "inscrutable." That is where theology is meant to land — not in a system that explains everything, but in worship before a God who is greater than our systems.

Key Scriptures:

* Romans 10:14-15 — "How then will they call on him in whom they have not believed? And how are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard? And how are they to preach unless they are sent?"
* Acts 18:9-10 — "Do not be afraid, but go on speaking and do not be silent, for I am with you... for I have many in this city who are my people."
* 2 Timothy 2:10 — "Therefore I endure everything for the sake of the elect, that they also may obtain the salvation that is in Christ Jesus with eternal glory."
* Isaiah 55:8-9 — "For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways, declares the LORD."

Closing Reflections — Strong Theology, Soft Hearts

Summary Truths:

1. Pride — The sin that turns doctrine into a trophy instead of a gift. Sovereignty should humble us, not harden us.
2. Election — The doctrine that proves God's justice and magnifies His mercy. It should fuel evangelism, not freeze it.
3. Balance — The wisdom to hold sovereignty and responsibility together without collapsing either. Doctrine should produce worship, not pride.

Spurgeon, in his Defense of Calvinism, reminds us:

"The old truth that Calvin preached, that Augustine preached, that Paul preached, is the truth that I must preach to-day, or else be false to my conscience and my God."

Our theology must always serve our worship, not replace it. Our convictions must produce compassion, not contempt. And our confidence must rest in Christ, not in our system's ability to answer every question.

The balanced Reformed man is:

* Humble under grace
* Bold in witness
* Deep in prayer
* Tender in heart
* Steadfast in truth
