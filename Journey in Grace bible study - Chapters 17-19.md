Providence, Justice, and Grace Under Fire

A Reformed Baptist Study for Men Walking in Truth and Grace

Chapter 17 – A Pressing Invitation: Personality, Doctrine, and the Providence of God

Summary:

This chapter moves on two tracks — one external (a pressing invitation from the First Baptist Church pulpit committee) and one internal (lessons about the relationship between personality and doctrine).

Externally, the pulpit committee chairman insists that Ira come preach a weekend at First Baptist Church. Ira raises every objection he can: his age, his lack of a college degree, his need for seminary, his single status. The chairman dismisses them all. Finally, Ira pulls out his ultimate argument: Dr Bloom. The chairman's response is telling — he is shocked, angry at Dr Bloom's behavior, and makes a promise: 'You let us take care of Dr Bloom!'

Ira agrees to the visit, partly hoping to end the pursuit once and for all. But in the back of his mind, two theological questions are growing: Whose definition of justice can be used to test God's actions? And when did God's decree of election take place in relation to man's Fall?

Meanwhile, Ira runs into Jim Mitchell, the former pastor at Lime Creek — the man whose unbalanced Calvinism had caused so much damage to the church. Mitchell is still complaining about not getting credit for the work others finished. He even asks Ira to recommend him to another church. When Ira presses him on whether he would repeat the same mistakes, Mitchell storms out of the shop without a word.

This encounter produces one of the most important insights in the book. Ira realizes that Mitchell's extreme form of Calvinism was not his main problem. His main problem was one of personality. The ministry can attract men who want to be the centre of attention, who crave authority over others, or who thrive on the thrill of importance. Couple that personality weakness with an unbalanced view of any theology — including Calvinism — and it could create a monster capable of incalculable damage to a church.

But Ira is careful not to overcorrect. Not every church problem is a personality problem. Personality problems can exist in the pews as easily as in the pulpit. And some problems are genuinely doctrinal, even when personalities are under control. The honest answer is that most church conflicts involve both — and a problem in personality can make a legitimate doctrinal problem even greater, and vice-versa.

This is a chapter about learning to see clearly. The invitation forces Ira to trust God's leading when he cannot see the outcome. The Mitchell encounter forces him to distinguish between a man's theology and a man's character — and to recognize that the deadliest ministry failures often come from the second, not the first.

Key Scriptures:

* 1 Corinthians 8:1 — "This 'knowledge' puffs up, but love builds up."
* James 4:6 — "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble."
* 1 Corinthians 4:7 — "What do you have that you did not receive? If then you received it, why do you boast as if you did not receive it?"
* Proverbs 15:1 — "A soft answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger."

⸻

Reflection Questions

1. Ira tried to end the committee's pursuit, but God kept the door open. When have you resisted something God was clearly leading you toward — and what did that resistance reveal about your heart?
2. Jim Mitchell's theology was sound on paper, but his personality made it toxic in practice. Have you seen good doctrine distorted by a proud or difficult personality — in yourself or in others?
3. Belcher concludes that the ministry can attract men who crave attention, authority, or importance. How do we guard against those motives in our own desire to lead, teach, or serve?
4. Ira says most church problems involve both personality and doctrine. In your experience, which is harder to confront — and why?
5. What is the difference between theological confidence and theological arrogance? Where is the line?

⸻

Group Discussion

* Read 1 Corinthians 8:1 together: "This 'knowledge' puffs up, but love builds up." How does Jim Mitchell illustrate this? How can we make sure our growing understanding of the doctrines of grace builds up rather than puffs up?
* Ira doesn't question Mitchell's call to preach — but he questions his fitness. How do we speak honestly to brothers whose character undermines their doctrine, without becoming the very thing we're warning against?
* The pulpit committee pursued Ira relentlessly. Has God ever used persistent, even unwelcome circumstances to guide you toward His will? How do we distinguish persistence from pressure?
* Discuss: What does a healthy, balanced Calvinist look like in practice — in preaching, in relationships, in how he handles disagreement?

⸻

Prayer Focus

"Lord, guard me from the pride that turns right doctrine into wrong attitudes. Teach me to distinguish between conviction and arrogance, between courage and stubbornness. Give me the humility of a man who knows everything he has was received, and the wisdom to follow Your leading even when I cannot see where it goes."

Chapter 18 – Is It Fair? The Justice of God in Unconditional Election

Summary:

This is a doctrine chapter — and one of the most important in the book. Terry and Ira sit down on a Friday evening and tackle the hardest question unconditional election raises: Does it make God unjust?

If God chose the elect on the basis of his sovereign will alone, without consideration of anything in man, then would not God be unjust in choosing one and bypassing another?

They reach their answer through two lines of argument.

First, the order of the decrees. If God's decree of election followed the decree of the fall of man — a view called infralapsarianism — then all men were already seen as fallen and undeserving at the moment of election. All were deserving of eternal hell. None had any basis to claim God's grace or love. Therefore, how could anyone accuse God of being unjust for choosing to save some from a mass that was already condemned? Grace, by definition, is undeserved. You cannot call it unfair that someone receives what no one earned.

Second, the dictionary test. Even if we refused to consider the order of the decrees — even if election came before the Fall (a view called supralapsarianism) — the charge of injustice still fails. Terry and Ira consulted a dictionary to examine the very words men use to accuse God: arbitrary, despot, tyrant, dictator. They discovered something critical: every one of these words describes the relationship between equals. A tyrant is an equal who seizes unjust power over other equals. A despot is a man who lords it over fellow men.

But God and man are not equals. God is God — the eternal sovereign. Man is man — the limited creature. To press upon God an equality with man, and then judge Him by human standards of justice, is to commit the very category error these words expose. The potter-and-clay metaphor of Romans 9 makes the same point: the issue is not whether God is fair by our definitions, but whether we are in any position to define fairness for Him.

Their conclusion: unconditional election is not only biblical, but defensible — and the very nature of grace makes the "why" question illegitimate. Grace, by its pure definition, cannot be demanded or explained. You can ask what grace is, what it produces, how it changes a man — but you cannot ask why God bestows it on one and not another. The moment you demand a reason for grace, you have turned it into something earned — and it is no longer grace.

They spent the rest of the evening in awe. That is where sound doctrine should always land a man — not in smug satisfaction, but in worship.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Paul anticipated this exact objection. Romans 9:14 asks, "What shall we say then? Is there injustice on God's part?" and answers, "By no means!" He then appeals to God's own words: "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" (Romans 9:15). The potter-and-clay argument of Romans 9:20-21 makes the same move Belcher's characters make with their dictionary: it exposes the category error of judging the Creator by the standards we apply to fellow creatures.

And Romans 11:33 is where both Paul and Ira's study evening end — in doxology: "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways!" That is not the conclusion of men who have explained everything. It is the response of men overwhelmed by a God too great to explain.

Key Scriptures:

* Romans 9:14-16 — "What shall we say then? Is there injustice on God's part? By no means! For he says to Moses, 'I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion.' So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy."
* Romans 9:20 — "But who are you, O man, to answer back to God?"
* Romans 11:33 — "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways!"
* Deuteronomy 29:29 — "The secret things belong to the LORD our God, but the things that are revealed belong to us and to our children forever, that we may do all the words of this law."

⸻

Reflection Questions

1. Belcher walks through infralapsarianism (election follows the Fall) and supralapsarianism (election precedes the Fall). Under either view, why does the charge of injustice against God fail?
2. The dictionary exercise is striking: words like "tyrant" and "despot" describe equals abusing equals. Why is it a fundamental category error to apply these words to God?
3. Ira and Terry concluded that you cannot ask "why?" about grace — because the moment you demand a reason, it ceases to be grace. How does that insight change the way you think about your own salvation?
4. When you first encountered the doctrine of election, what was your honest reaction? Has it changed over time?
5. The chapter ends with two men sitting in awe. When was the last time a doctrine left you in worship rather than in argument?

⸻

Group Discussion

* Read Romans 9:14-16 aloud. Paul's answer to the fairness question is blunt: "By no means!" He doesn't apologize or soften. Why is Paul so direct here — and what does that tell us about how we should handle the "fairness" objection?
* Discuss: If strict justice were applied to every man, what would every man receive? How does that reality reframe the entire "fairness" conversation?
* Belcher's characters use a dictionary to test their assumptions. What would happen if we tested more of our instinctive objections to Scripture the same way — examining whether our categories even apply?
* The chapter ends in awe, not in a neat system. Read Romans 11:33 together. How do we hold firm doctrinal conviction while also maintaining genuine humility before mysteries we cannot fully resolve?

⸻

Prayer Focus

"Sovereign Lord, I bow before mysteries I cannot solve. Thank You that Your justice is not measured by my standards, and Your mercy is not limited by my understanding. I deserved nothing, and You gave everything. Silence the pride that demands explanations, and give me the humility to worship where I cannot comprehend. Let election produce in me not coldness, but awe."

Chapter 19 – A Difficult Meeting: False Accusations and Grace Under Fire

Summary:

This is one of the most painful chapters in the book — and one of the most instructive for men who have ever faced opposition for their convictions.

Ira arrives at First Baptist Church of Collegetown for the weekend the pulpit committee arranged. The services are poorly attended. Dr Bloom is present at all three — sitting silently, speaking to no one, vanishing at the end of each service "almost as if magically leaving by some supernatural power at some uncommon exit."

Then comes the Sunday afternoon meeting. Ira was told it would be the pulpit committee only. Instead, it has become an open church meeting — and Dr Bloom is front and center. The chairman who promised to "take care of Dr Bloom" has not taken care of anything. (Ira later learns it was Dr Bloom who insisted on the setup.)

The chairman gives his opening remarks and then opens the floor. Dr Bloom rises immediately: "I have serious concerns about this young man." What follows is a devastating public attack. He accuses Ira of holding a theology that will destroy their commitment to foreign missions. He calls Ira narrow, bigoted, unteachable, rebellious against authority, and non-Baptistic in doctrine. He accuses Ira of pursuing the church by reverse psychology. He urges the congregation and pulpit committee to give up their pursuit entirely.

As Dr Bloom sits down, Ira's first thought is: "Well, Mr Chairman, you really did take care of Dr Bloom! You let him make every false accusation against me in the book!"

But here is where the chapter turns — and where every man in this room should pay attention.

Ira does not defend himself point by point. He does not match accusation with accusation. Instead, he smiles and lets the fruit speak: "If I were all those things Dr Bloom accuses me of being, I wonder how I ever was ordained; or how Lime Creek Baptist Church ever called me; or how we ever saw such a great revival and salvation of souls in the church this spring; or how I ever am allowed to stay in college!"

Then he draws a boundary: "I am your guest, and the discussion of these issues, I feel, is not only unnecessary at this point, but out of order."

And then — remarkably — he hands the outcome to God: "Yet even in God's providence, he may have used Dr Bloom to close the door to any possibility for me to pastor here, which is fine with me, if that be God's will. I will take it all as an answer to my prayer for direction."

He leaves that afternoon certain the door is closed, sighing, "I'm glad that's over!" and asking himself: "Anyway, who wanted to be the pastor to fifty people in a building that seated a thousand?"

This chapter is about what a man does when he is publicly attacked, when promises are broken, and when the people who should have defended him do not. Ira's response is a model: let the fruit of your life speak louder than the accusations of your critics, set gracious boundaries, and trust God's providence to open or close the doors He wills.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Ira's response in this meeting is Romans 12:17-19 in action: "Repay no one evil for evil, but give thought to do what is honorable in the sight of all... Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God." He does not retaliate. He does not escalate. He gives a soft answer — and then he hands the result to the God who governs all outcomes.

And his trust in God's providence, even when the door seems to close, reflects Romans 8:28: "And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose." Even Dr Bloom's ambush, even the chairman's failure, even the humiliation of a public attack — all of it is under God's sovereign hand.

Key Scriptures:

* Romans 12:17-19 — "Repay no one evil for evil, but give thought to do what is honorable in the sight of all. If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all. Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God, for it is written, 'Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord.'"
* 1 Peter 3:15 — "In your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy, always being prepared to make a defense to anyone who asks you for a reason for the hope that is in you; yet do it with gentleness and respect."
* Romans 8:28 — "And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose."
* Matthew 5:11-12 — "Blessed are you when others revile you and persecute you and utter all kinds of evil against you falsely on my account. Rejoice and be glad, for your reward is great in heaven."

Reflection Questions

1. Ira chose not to defend himself point by point. Instead he pointed to the fruit of his life and ministry. Why is that often more powerful than a detailed rebuttal — and when is it the wrong approach?
2. The chairman promised to "take care of Dr Bloom" but failed completely. When has someone who should have defended you failed to do so? How did you respond — and how do you wish you had responded?
3. Ira says the discussion was "out of order" — a gracious but firm boundary. How do we set boundaries in the church without being defensive or divisive?
4. Ira interprets Dr Bloom's attack through the lens of God's providence: "if that be God's will." How does trusting God's sovereignty change the way you process opposition and closed doors?
5. The chapter ends with: "Who wanted to be the pastor to fifty people in a building that seated a thousand?" What does that line reveal about Ira's heart at that moment — relief, disappointment, or something else?

⸻

Group Discussion

* Read Romans 12:17-19 together. Ira lived this passage in that meeting. Discuss a time you were falsely accused or publicly opposed. How did you respond? What would Romans 12 have looked like in that moment?
* Dr Bloom called Ira narrow, bigoted, unteachable, rebellious, and a danger to missions — all false. How do you determine which accusations to answer and which to leave with God? What criteria should guide that decision?
* The chairman's broken promise is one of the most frustrating moments in the book. Discuss: How do we handle it when church leaders fail to protect or support us — without becoming bitter or cynical?
* Ira's response was gentle but not weak. He smiled, pointed to fruit, set a boundary, and trusted God. How is that different from passivity? How is it different from fighting back? What makes it harder than either one?

Prayer Focus

"Father, when men oppose me falsely, give me the grace to respond with truth and gentleness. When those who should defend me stay silent, give me the faith to trust that You have not. Guard my heart from bitterness, my tongue from retaliation, and my spirit from despair. Let me rest in Your providence — knowing that no man can close a door You intend to open, and no accusation can undo what Your hand has done."

Closing Reflections — What These Chapters Teach Us Together

Three chapters. Three lessons that every man needs.

1. Chapter 17 — See clearly. Good doctrine in a bad personality is dangerous. But so is the reverse — good character without sound doctrine. Learn to tell the difference, and guard against both in yourself.

2. Chapter 18 — Think carefully. The hardest questions about God's sovereignty have real answers. The "fairness" objection fails when we understand what grace actually is — and when we stop judging the Creator by the standards we apply to fellow creatures.

3. Chapter 19 — Respond faithfully. When false accusations come — and they will — let the fruit of your life speak, set gracious boundaries, and hand the outcome to the God who governs all things.

The thread running through all three: trust God's providence. In the pressing invitation, in the hard doctrine, in the painful meeting — God is at work. Even when we cannot see it. Even when the door seems to close. Even when the people who should help us don't.
