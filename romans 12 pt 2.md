Alright, with that in front of us, let’s start with verse 3 and think about a gospel-shaped mind.
Let’s look again at verse 3:
“For by the grace given to me I say to everyone among you not to think of himself more highly than he ought to think, but to think with sober judgment, each according to the measure of faith that God has assigned.”
**A. The first thing the gospel changes is how you think about **
**you**

Paul has just said, “Be transformed by the renewal of your mind.”
Now he shows us the first area that renewal has to touch: **self-perception**.
In Greek, he piles up the same word for “think” four times. If we woodenly reflected that, it would sound like:
“Don’t *over-think* yourself, but think rightly with sober thinking…”
It’s as if he’s saying,
“Let the gospel get into the way you think about yourself—your place, your importance, your role.” A renewed mind doesn’t just think new thoughts about God; it thinks new thoughts about **self**. Cussing is low hanging fruit, but when we are truly seeking Jesus our internal dialog starts changing, and that affects the kinds of things that come out of our mouth.
**B. Paul speaks “by the grace given to me”**
“For by the grace given to me I say…” Even as an apostle, Paul locates his authority in **grace**.
He doesn’t say, “As someone who has worked harder than all of you, listen up,” but, “As someone who has been given grace, I’m going to talk to you about grace.”
His authority in humility sets the tone.
The whole conversation about how we see ourselves is wrapped in grace from the beginning:
Grace saved Paul.
Grace commissioned Paul.
Grace now speaks through Paul to us.
Which means this isn’t a “try harder to be humble” verse. It’s, “Let grace tell you the truth about yourself.” And let me ask you this, if you were to encounter a lost person at work, would the lost person be able to tell the difference between you and another lost person? If not, perhaps you are not as saved as you think you are, or perhaps you’re living in rebellion.
**C. He speaks “to everyone among you”**
“For by the grace given to me I say to **everyone among you**…” No one in the Roman church gets to duck this. Older believers, new believers, leaders, followers, Jews, Gentiles—“everyone among you.”
Same here for us today. If you’re among us today, this applies to you too.
We all have some version of the same struggle:
Some of us are tempted to quietly think too **highly** of ourselves.
Some of us are tempted to quietly think too **lowly** of the grace God has given us.
Verse 3 is aimed at both.
**D. The negative: Don’t think “more highly than you ought”**
“not to think of himself more highly than he ought to think…”
“More highly” is literally “over-thinking” yourself—thinking above what is true.
The world around us is constantly whispering:
“You’re the main character.”
“Trust your heart.”
“You do you.”
And if we’re not careful, that seeps into the church:
“This church really needs *me*.”
“If I’m not happy, something’s wrong with *them*.”
“My preferences, my comfort, my spot.”
The gospel doesn’t flatten us; it **rights** us.
Romans has already told us:
“None is righteous, no, not one.” (3:10)
“All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God.” (3:23)
“While we were still weak, at the right time Christ died for the ungodly.” (5:6)
So when Scripture says, “Don’t think more highly than you ought,” it’s not calling you to false modesty; it’s calling you to **reality**.
A prideful mind says: “They need my gifts; I’m doing God a favor by being here.”
A gospel-shaped mind says:
“If God had not had mercy on me, I’d be lost. Even the best things in me are His gifts, not my achievements.”
This matters in a small church because subtle pride can quietly fracture a body.
It makes us impatient with others’ weaknesses.
It makes us compare our gifts to theirs.
It makes us more aware of how we’re not being served than how we might serve.
So the first question verse 3 presses on us is:

“Where am I thinking *over* myself—treating my perspective, my comfort, my way as more important than it really is?”

**E. The positive: “Think with sober judgment”**
“…but to think with **sober judgment**…” If the first half says, “Don’t live in a fantasy about yourself,” the second says, “Live in **clarity** about yourself.”
Sober judgment is:
Not self-hatred (“I’m worthless; I have nothing to offer”),
Not self-inflation (“The church revolves around me”),
But clear-headed, steady evaluation:
“Who am I, really, in light of the gospel?”
The gospel lets you say two things at once:
“I am a sinner. Left to myself I have nothing to boast in.”
“I am loved, chosen, forgiven, indwelt by the Spirit, and placed in a body on purpose.”
Sober judgment is being able to hold both without wobbling.
Very practically, that means:
You can admit your sins and weaknesses without collapsing.
You can acknowledge your gifts without bragging.
You can receive correction without falling apart,
because your identity rests in Christ, not in always being right.
**F. “According to the measure of faith that God has assigned”**
“…each according to the measure of faith that God has assigned.” That little phrase keeps grace right at the center. Whatever “measure” of faith you have— your capacity, your maturity, your present strength— it has been **assigned** by God.

That does two things:
It kills envy.
If God has given someone else a different measure, that’s His wisdom, not their superiority.
You don’t have to resent what He’s given to others.
It kills despair.
You are not a random extra in God’s story.
If you are in Christ, He has measured out to you a real share in the faith and a real place in the body.
Sometimes that “measure” feels large: You’re in a season where you have energy, clarity, strength to serve. Sometimes it feels small: You’re tired, grieving, foggy. The best you can manage is to get here and stay awake. Verse 3 covers both.
It doesn’t say, “Think with sober judgment *if* your faith is strong.” It says, “Think with sober judgment… **according to the measure** God has assigned.”
In other words: “Receive the reality of where you are right now as from the hand of God, and be honest about it—without pride and without shame.”nIf you’re in a low-capacity season, that doesn’t make you less a part of the body. If you’re in a high-capacity season, that doesn’t make you more important. It just means God has entrusted different measures in different moments.
**G. Bringing Point 1 home: A renewed mind in a small church**
So before Paul says anything about “one body” and “many members,” before he lists a single spiritual gift,
he says: “Let grace teach you how to think about yourself.”
For us, that means asking some honest, gentle questions:
Am I quietly assuming my way is best, my preferences should prevail, my comfort is central?
Am I shrinking back as if I have nothing to offer, even though God says He’s given me a measure of faith and some gift for the body?
When I think about this church, do I see myself mainly as a **consumer** or as a **member**?
A gospel-shaped mind starts by saying:
“I am here by grace.
Whatever I have is from God.
Whatever I lack, He knows, and has planned around.
I am not the center of this body, but I am truly part of it.
So I want to see myself the way He sees me—no higher, no lower.”
That’s the soil Paul wants to plant the rest of this passage in.
Only when we stop over-thinking ourselves and start thinking soberly by grace are we ready to hear what comes next: “For as in one body we have many members…”
That’s where we’ll turn next—what it means to belong to one another as a gospel-shaped body.
**2. A Gospel-Shaped Belonging (vv. 4–5)**
Alright—verse 3 dealt with how we think about *ourselves*. Now Paul widens the lens to how we think about **each other**. “For as in one body we have many members, and the members do not all have the same function, so we, though many, are one body in Christ, and individually members one of another.” (vv. 4–5) He gives us a picture we all know: the human body.
**A. One body, many members **“**For as in one body we have many members…**” One body—singular. Many members—plural. When you see a person walk into a room, you don’t say, “Here comes a collection of organs and bones.” You say, “Here comes *someone*.” There is a real unity and a real diversity bound together. Paul says: *that* is what the church is like. If you are in Christ, you are not just a free-floating Christian. You are one member in **a body**.
You still have your own story, your own personality, your own burdens and joys. But you are no longer only “you.” You are part of an “us.”
**B. Different functions by design **“…**and the members do not all have the same function…**” Hands do what hands do. Eyes do what eyes do. They’re not interchangeable. So we should expect differences in Christ’s body:
Different temperaments,
Different strengths,
Different ways of showing love,
Different capacities in different seasons.
That’s not a problem; that’s a design feature. The church is not healthiest when everyone looks the same and acts the same. It’s healthiest when every God-given difference is harnessed for a shared good.
**C. One body **
**in Christ … members of one another **“**so we, though many, are one body in Christ, and individually members one of another.**”
Notice the order:
We are **in Christ** first. United to Him by faith.
Because we are in Christ, we are now united to **each other**.
“Members one of another” is strong language. It means:
*We belong to Christ, and therefore, we belong to each other.*
Now—this would have landed differently on Paul’s first hearers than it does on us.
In Paul’s world, people thought in terms of **family, clan, village**—*we* before *I*. Your identity was wrapped up in your people. Putting the group first was normal. Here in modern America, we swim in the opposite stream.
We are taught from childhood to think mainly in terms of: “my rights, my dreams, my truth, my space.” Yet, in Pauls day it was more like “our rights, our dreams, our truth, our space, our people first”.  So when the Bible says, “**We are one body in Christ… individually members one of another,**” it is pushing against our default settings. It is saying:
“In Christ, you are not just an individual consumer of religious goods;
you belong to a people. Their good is now your concern.”
That’s a big shift.
**D. The unity this creates**
This is where **unity** comes in. The world teaches us to divide— to exclude, to suspect, to look down on anyone different from us. But notice what Paul does here: he doesn’t slice the church into competing groups. He doesn’t line us up as Jew versus Gentile, insider versus outsider, “super-spiritual” versus “ordinary.” He simply says: **“we”**. “We, though many, are one body in Christ…” In Christ, the old labels fall away. In His body, the world’s divisions don’t get to rule.
Christ teaches us:
Not to hoard ourselves, but to *belong*.
Not to push people out, but to *include*.
Not to posture above others, but to *embrace and serve*.
In His body, no one who is in Christ is an outsider. All who are in Him are fellow pilgrims in the same mercy, headed toward the same home.

We are one new people in Christ. And the local church is meant to be the first place where the old walls start to come down.
**E. What this looks like in a small room**
Now, in a little room like ours, this doesn’t just live in theology books. It looks very ordinary—and very precious. Some here have buried a wife after many years. Some have said goodbye to a parent. Some carry grief that’s not simple or neat, that’s hard to even put into words.
“**Members one of another**” means:
Your joys are not just *yours*; they’re ours to rejoice in with you.
Your sorrows are not just *yours*; they’re ours to shoulder with you.
You are not a project to fix; you are part of us.
When one part of us hurts, we all hurt. When one grieves we all grieve. When one celebrates, we all celebrate. Which reminds me, happy birthday Curt! Glad we get to celebrate 7 more birthdays with you .
Let me step to the side for a moment and speak into that grief element particularly, and then we’ll come back to the flow of the text.

He didn’t cry because He lacked strength.
He didn’t cry because He was powerless.

If the Son of God can feel sorrow openly, then so can you—without shame.
Not everyone will cry, and that too is ok. We all grieve differently.
It doesn’t mean we loved them any less; it just means we are wired differently.

The most important part is this:

**Jesus sees you.**
**He carries what you cannot.**
**And He gives beauty for ashes even when the ashes come from the people we love most.**
That’s His heart. And often, He shows that heart through **His body**—through a church family that knows it is “members one of another.”
So when Paul says we are one body and members of each other, part of what that means is:
“When your heart is heavy, you don’t have to pretend.
You don’t have to walk through it alone.
Because in Christ, we really do belong to one another.”
Then Paul’s going to show us *how* God has equipped that kind of body—with different gifts, all given by grace, so that this unity and this shared life can actually work itself out in practice.
That’s where we’ll go next:
**Gifts of grace** in verse 6.
**3. Gifts of Grace (v. 6a)**
We’ve seen **how** we’re to think (v.3),
we’ve seen **who** we are together (vv.4–5),
now Paul turns to **what** God has given us inside this body. Verse 6, just the first part:
“Having gifts that differ according to the grace given to us…” That little phrase is loaded. Three key words: **gifts**, **differ**, **grace**.

**A. “Having gifts…”**

First, **“having gifts…”** The word is *charismata*—grace-gifts. Paul doesn’t say: “Some of you have gifts, and the rest of you just sort of sit and watch.” He assumes something: If you are in Christ, you **have** a gift. God has entrusted something to you for the good of others.
Gifts here are not:
Your worth,
Your identity,
A reason to boast.
They are **tools** in the hands of a gracious God, given so that you can help build up the body. Some of those tools are more visible—teaching, leading. Some are quieter—serving, mercy, generosity. But the starting point is: “Having gifts…” Not “*if* you somehow manage to develop a gift.” But “Because God is gracious, you *have* one.” That alone pushes back on the “consumer Christian” mindset: “I come, I listen, I go home.”
Romans 12 says: “No, if you’re in Christ, you are part of the body, and the body has placed something in your hands.”
**B. “Gifts that differ…”**
Second, **“gifts that differ…”** Paul makes sure we don’t flatten this. The gifts are not all the same:
Not everyone is wired to stand and teach.
Not everyone is wired to quietly notice every practical need.
Not everyone is wired to move toward hurting people first.
Not everyone is wired to organize and lead.
And that’s *good*. If the whole body were an eye, where would be the hearing? If the whole body were a mouth, who would do the listening?
“Gifts that differ” means:
You are not meant to be a clone of the person sitting next to you.
You don’t have to feel guilty that your strengths aren’t theirs.
You don’t get to despise someone because they don’t do things the way you would.
It also means:
A healthy church will feel a little bit like a toolbox. Not a drawer full of identical screwdrivers, but a mix of tools—each one designed for a particular kind of work. Or a sewing closet – my grandma used to have one that smelled of ceder and old leather. Inside of it she had hundreds of kinds of material. She had denim, and leather, and lace. Each chosen because she loved it, each valuable for its own purposes. If my denim had developed a hole, she would patch it with other denim. She’s not going to patch the seat of a pair of denim pants with lace, it’s a waste of good lace, and it spoils a mans pants. Neither would she patch the veil of a wedding dress with denim. It would be silly. But she has the right material, the right thread, the right needle, the right sewing machine for the right task. The body of Christ is like that too.

Another pastor friend was more like a scalpel, careful cutting near delicate things so that healing could happen. A third pastor friend reminds me of a coach. When the hammer strikes our thumb it hurts.
The person who is always thinking about people, and not systems…
The person who is always thinking about systems, and not feelings…
The person who wants to dive deep into doctrine…
The person who wants to sit quietly with the one who’s hurting…
But Romans 12 says: “Those differences are precisely where God’s wisdom shines.” The question is not, “Why aren’t you like me?” The question is, “Lord, how do these differences work together for the good of Your people?”
**C. “…according to the grace given to us” **Third, **“…according to the grace given to us.” **We’re back to that word again: **grace**. Grace saved you Grace united you to Christ. Grace put you in a body. And now grace **assigns** your gifts.
That does at least two things in the heart.
**1. It kills pride**
If what I have is “according to the grace given to me,” then I can’t boast in it.
Because I didn’t earn it. I didn’t generate it. I didn’t audition and get the part because I was more impressive. God, in His wisdom and kindness, handed out different portions:
Different personalities,
Different stories,
Different strengths,
Different spiritual gifts.
So if I teach, I can’t say, “Look at me.” If I serve, I can’t say, “No one else does it like I do.” Grace puts us all on level ground:
“Whatever I am that is useful is a gift.
The Giver is the One to be praised.”
**2. It kills despair**
On the other side, some of us quietly feel: “I don’t bring anything. I just sit here. I’m not gifted like so-and-so.” But Paul doesn’t say, “Having *some* gifts among you…”. He says, “**Having gifts** that differ according to the grace given to **us**…” Not “them”—**us**.
If you are in Christ, there is *some* way God has wired you to bless His people:
It might be more word-based,
It might be more hands-on,
It might be more relational,
It might be more behind-the-scenes.
You may be in a season where that feels small:
You’re tired.
You’re grieving.
Your capacity is low.
But even then, grace is at work. In some seasons, the way grace flows *through* you is very active.
In other seasons, the way grace flows *toward* you is by letting others use their gifts on your behalf—while you simply show up and receive.
Both are part of life in the body. So “according to the grace given to us” means:
“God knows exactly what He’s doing with me and with us.
He has not overlooked me, and He has not made a mistake in how He has wired me.”
**D. What this means for  our little church**
Let’s bring it down to where we sit.
We are a small room.
We do not have all the gifts we’d *like* to have.

We had a need, we were covering it, but not well, and one Sunday she showed up, and before long she was one of us, and serving in the capacity we had been struggling with. It was and is a breath of fresh air.
We may not be the biggest church in town.
But Scripture says: “Having gifts that differ according to the grace given to us…”
Which means:
Whatever this little church **does** have is because God, in His mercy, put it here.
The mix of personalities here is not an accident.
The people who can teach, the people who can serve, the people who can encourage, the people who can give, the people who can show mercy—
all of that is **grace**.
And that also means:
The gaps we feel—the things we *don’t* have—are not reasons to despair;
they are invitations to trust that God knows what this body needs in this season.
So before we ever get to the list—prophecy, service, teaching, exhortation, giving, leading, mercy— Paul wants us to hear this:
“You, as a church, are not empty-handed.
God has given you what you need for the work He’s called you to do *right now*.
And if you are in Christ individually, you are part of that gift-mix for the good of others.”
**E. A gentle question**
So the searching question of verse 6a is simple: “Do I see what God has given me as **grace** for others, or do I see it as **mine** for my own comfort or reputation?”
And right behind it:
“Am I quietly believing the lie that I have nothing to give,
when God says He has given gifts of grace to *us*?”
Paul’s going to move next into specifics:
“If prophecy…”
“If service…”
“If teaching…”
“If exhortation…”
“If giving…”
“If leading…”
“If mercy…”

And with each one he’s going to say some version of: “Then **do** it. Use it. Lean into it—for God’s glory and the body’s good.” That’s where we’ll go in the next section: how **gifts in action** look when a church actually lives like a body on the altar.
**4. Gifts in Action (vv. 6–8)**
We’ve seen:
**How** we’re to think (a gospel-shaped mind),
**Who** we are (a gospel-shaped belonging),
**What** we’ve been given (gifts of grace).
Now Paul says: *use them.*
“Having gifts that differ according to the grace given to us, **let us use them**:
if prophecy, in proportion to our faith;
if service, in our serving;
the one who teaches, in his teaching;
the one who exhorts, in his exhortation;
the one who contributes, in generosity;
the one who leads, with zeal;
the one who does acts of mercy, with cheerfulness.” (vv. 6–8)
Each gift gets a little “how” attached to it—a flavor, a posture.
Let’s walk through them.**A. Prophecy — “in proportion to our faith”**

“if prophecy, in proportion to our faith…” Whatever view you take on prophecy in the New Testament, the principle here is clear: Any speaking “for God” must be tethered to **the faith**—to what God has actually revealed.
For us:
When we teach,
When we give counsel,
When we say, “Here’s what God says…”
it must be in line with Scripture, not our guesses or personal opinions dressed up in Bible language.
“In proportion to our faith” is like a guardrail:
“Stay within the bounds of what God has made known.
Don’t outrun His Word, and don’t shrink back from it either.”In a small room with real joys and real sorrows, that matters.
People don’t need our theories; they need God’s word.
**B. Service — “in our serving”**
“if service, in our serving…” Service is practical help. Some of you naturally see tasks that need doing and quietly move toward them:
Setting up chairs,
Bringing a meal,
Giving someone a ride,
Helping fix something that’s broken,
Checking in on a practical need.
Paul’s instruction is wonderfully simple: “If your gift is serving, **then serve**.”
Don’t despise it as “less spiritual.” Jesus said, “Whoever would be great among you must be your servant.”
In any church—but especially a small one—this gift is part of what keeps us functioning with warmth and stability.

**C. Teaching — “in his teaching”** “the one who teaches, in his teaching…” Teaching is the Spirit-given capacity to explain and apply God’s Word clearly and faithfully.
It happens:
From a pulpit,
Around a table,
In a Bible study,
Over coffee with an open Bible.
If you’re wired to love clarity, to love making connections in the text, to help others understand—this might be you.
Paul says: “If that’s you, be faithful **in your teaching**.” To a point that is all of us. Everyone is teaching those around them what their priorities are by the way they live their life. so
Stay anchored in the Word. Point people to Christ, not to yourself. Aim not just for information, but for transformation.

**D. Exhortation — “in his exhortation”** “the one who exhorts, in his exhortation…” Exhortation is coming alongside to encourage, urge, steady, sometimes gently correct. Some people are especially tuned for this. They naturally move toward others and say things like:
“Don’t give up.”
“Lift your eyes to Jesus.”
“Let me walk with you through this.”

If that’s you, Paul says: “Do it—**in your exhortation**. Don’t bury that impulse.” A short, well-timed word of encouragement can carry a weary person much farther than you realize.
**E. Giving — “in generosity”**
“the one who contributes, in generosity…”
Some believers have a particular grace to give:
They have resources,
They see needs,
They enjoy meeting those needs quietly.
Paul’s instruction:
“If that’s you, give **with generosity**.” The word has the idea of simplicity, open-handedness—not manipulative, not stingy, not drawing attention.
In a small church, that might be:
Helping with a bill,
Covering a cost someone is anxious about,
Supporting ministry that others benefit from.
Whatever form it takes, generosity reflects the heart of a God who “did not spare His own Son but gave Him up for us all.”
**G. Mercy — “with cheerfulness”**
“the one who does acts of mercy, with cheerfulness.” Mercy is compassion in action—moving toward those who are hurting, weak, or on the margins. Some of you are especially sensitive to this:
You notice who’s alone,
You remember who’s grieving,
You’re drawn to visit, to check in, to sit with someone who’s struggling.
Paul says: “If that’s you, show mercy **with cheerfulness**.”
That doesn’t mean fake smiles. It means a willing, glad heart: “I’m not annoyed that you’re still hurting. I’m glad to walk with you.” Mercy with cheerfulness says to the other person:
“You are not a burden. You are loved.”
And that, in a room where some wounds are still fresh and some are long and heavy, is a priceless gift. **H. The picture as a whole**

Put it all together and what do you see? Not a church where a few people do everything and everyone else watches… …but a body where:
Minds have been humbled by the gospel,
Hearts have been bound together in Christ,
And every grace-gift God has given is being quietly brought to the table for the good of the others.
Prophecy / teaching / exhortation — **speaking gifts** used faithfully.
Service / mercy / giving — **hands-on gifts** used gladly.
Leadership — **guiding gifts** used diligently.
And all of it under the banner we started with:
**Because of God’s mercy, we are a humble, united, gifted body—called to serve one another in love.**
But all of that starts with following Jesus, which begins with the gospel.

# Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

## 1. Verdict Title
**Warm Pastoral Fabric, Frayed Gospel Thread**

## 2. One-Sentence Summary
A generous and personally rooted opening of Romans 12:3–8 that illuminates the community life of the body with genuine warmth, while stopping short of proclaiming the gospel with the clarity and force the passage—and the congregation—require.

## 3. Berean Gate
**PASS**

- **Scopus Test**: The sermon's thesis—"Because of God's mercy, we are a humble, united, gifted body, called to serve one another in love"—arises directly from Romans 12:3–8's own argument. Pass.
- **Authority Test**: The preacher submits to the text's plain claims without softening them. He stays within the passage's orbit throughout. Pass.

## 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**FLAG RAISED — Score cap at 69**

**Flag #1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The sermon invokes "the gospel" and "God's mercy" repeatedly as atmosphere, but never lands on Christ's finished work in a way that explains *what* that mercy consists of. An unbeliever sitting in the room would hear that mercy is the foundation without being told what mercy *is* or how it is received. Justification by faith is not articulated. The cross is referenced once in passing ("did not spare His own Son") without being proclaimed.

**Flag #4 — Application is entirely generic in places**: While there are moments of genuine specificity (the birthday aside, the prayer answered through a new member, grief in the room), the applicatory structure defaults to questions directed at self-perception ("Am I quietly assuming my way is best?") without pressing the conscience toward a concrete, differentiated response. The sermon applies to any congregation in any city in any decade — it lacks the specificity Puritan application demands.

**Note**: Flag #5 (no actual exhortation) is borderline. The sermon does summon the congregation to *use their gifts*, which is a genuine call. However, the exhortation does not move from explanation to a "therefore" moment that corners sinners or calls anyone to repentance. The call to serve is present; the call to believe, to repent, to seek Christ for the power to serve — is absent.

## 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 20/25**
- *Textual Fidelity*: 8/10 — The sermon follows the passage's argument faithfully. Each section of the text (v.3, vv.4–5, v.6a, vv.6–8) is addressed in order, with the text read aloud and explained. The Greek play on "think" (φρονεῖν × 4) is noted and helpfully used.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 4/5 — Good backward connection to Romans 1–11's doctrinal spine. The "therefore" of 12:1 and the "hinge" of the letter is named clearly. The flow from Romans 12:1–2 (last week) to 12:3–8 is well-handled.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 4/5 — The Greek observation is well-deployed without being pedantic. "Charismata" is explained. "Sober judgment" is unpacked with pastoral clarity.
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 4/5 — No eisegesis detected. The sermon does not import ideas foreign to the text. The one mild drift is the lengthy political/identity aside in Point 2D, which while not wrong, is not generated by the passage.
- *Fallen Condition Focus*: Identified as pride (thinking too highly of self) and its mirror, despair (thinking too lowly of grace). Both are named concretely. Solid.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness*: 4/7 — Christ is present but not central. He appears as the basis of unity ("in Christ") and is named as the goal of teaching ("Point to Christ"). The organic connection to Christ as the one through whose body these gifts flow is stated but not developed. A single passing reference to the cross is insufficient for a text that roots everything in "the mercies of God."
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness*: 2/3 — The connection from Romans 1–11 to 12:1–8 is named, but the redemptive-historical placement of the body-of-Christ as a new-covenant reality is not developed.
- *Doctrinal Precision*: 4/4 — No sloppiness detected. Gifts are correctly framed as grace, not merit. The "measure of faith" is handled without veering into works-righteousness.
- *Confessional Alignment*: 3/4 — No contradiction of confessional theology. However, where the sermon invokes "the mercies of God" as the ground for ecclesial life, it does not surface the Covenant of Grace as the frame — an organic opportunity that passes unused.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel*: 1/5 — Justification by faith is not articulated. The sermon says "gospel" and "mercy" frequently, but neither term is unpacked for a hearer who does not already know what they mean.
- *Necessity of Christ*: 3/5 — Christ is referenced as the head of the body and the basis of unity, but He is not presented as *necessary* in the sense that His blood purchased these mercies and without Him there is only wrath.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity*: 3/5 — The sermon does not orbit morality, which is good. But it orbits ecclesiology without ever dropping anchor at the cross. The one Christological moment ("did not spare His own Son") is deployed briefly in the context of generosity rather than as the ground of everything being said.
- *Synagogue Test*: A devout Jewish believer in God who practices communal life and moral discipline could sit through this sermon and feel largely at home. The necessity of Christ's atonement is not pressed. **Moralistic drift flag raised.**
- *Law/Gospel Dialectic*: The hammer never falls. There is no moment where the law strips the hearer of self-trust before the gospel provides the only hope. The sermon begins in grace and stays in grace — which sounds good but, without first naming what we are saved *from*, produces a gospel without weight.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9/15**
- *Heart Penetration*: 3/5 — The sermon has genuine warmth and does press on pride and self-inflation. The pastoral aside on grief is among the strongest moments of the manuscript — personal, specific, pastorally courageous. However, the affective register stays largely at the level of encouragement rather than searching conviction.
- *Specificity*: 4/5 — The small-church particularity is real and valuable. The birthday aside, the answered-prayer illustration, the grief references, the men's and women's Bible studies — all of these root the sermon in a specific room rather than generic churchmanship. This is a genuine strength.
- *Spiritual Differentiation*: 2/5 — The sermon addresses believers almost exclusively. The unbeliever in the room is mentioned only at the close ("But all of that starts with following Jesus, which begins with the gospel") — which is a sentence, not a differentiated address. The backslider is not addressed. The knowledgeable-but-unmoved is not cornered. The Perkins taxonomy is largely unused; the sermon speaks to believers in community without distinguishing among them.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5/8**
- *Call to Response*: 2/4 — There are gentle exhortatory questions throughout, and the final section ("use them") is a genuine call to active service. But the sermon never drives to a clear "therefore" moment — a summons with weight. The congregation is encouraged but not summoned.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness*: 3/4 — The sermon does equip the church for internal community life, mutual service, and gift-deployment. Outward witness is mentioned but not developed.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 7/9**
- *Clarity of Outline*: 3/4 — The four-point outline (mind, belonging, gifts, gifts in action) is clean, memorable, and stated up front. A listener could sketch it.
- *Progression*: 3/3 — Builds logically from self-perception to community to gift-reception to gift-use. Good theological sequence.
- *Unity*: 1/2 — The political/identity aside in Point 2D, while not wrong, competes with the main idea. It could be removed without loss, and its removal would tighten the sermon considerably.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**
- *Relevance*: 2/2 — The sewing closet, the toolbox, the hammer/scalpel/coach pastoral types, the birthday aside — these are native to a small congregation and land in the room they were preached in.
- *Clarity*: 1/2 — The sewing closet illustration is the sermon's best. The "circles" illustration in Point 2D is overlong and wanders into political territory that, while earnest, muddies the point being made about church unity.
- *Memorability*: 1/1 — The sewing closet will be remembered.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**
- *Reads as Preaching*: 1/2 — The manuscript moves between oral cadence (direct address, asides, congregation-specific references) and an essayistic register. The pastoral aside on grief is the most "preached" section of the manuscript. The gift-list section (4A–4G) reads more like a structured summary than a proclamation.
- *Conviction on the Page*: 1/2 — The sermon asserts truth but rarely roars. The closest it comes to sermonic force is the grief section. The close is quiet when it should be climactic.
- *Cadence and Breath*: 1/1 — Sentence variation is present and natural. The manuscript breathes.

## 6. Cognitive Load Modifier
**0** — The sermon is accessible and well-paced. A single mother or new believer could follow the main argument and take home one clear truth. No penalty applied.

## 7. Total Score
20 + 13 + 7 + 9 + 5 + 7 + 4 + 3 = **68/100**

Interpretation Band: **Concerning Gaps** — The sermon explains but does not fully preach. Significant revision needed, specifically in gospel proclamation and conscience differentiation.

## 8. Core Override Questions
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* — Primarily of how we relate to one another in the body. Christ is present but not magnified.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* — Ideas about Him, generously and warmly presented. The weight of God's holiness and the scandal of the cross are absent.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* — Comforted, yes — especially in the grief aside. Convicted, only mildly. Informed, substantially.

## 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Pastoral particularity in the grief aside (Point 2E)**: "Jesus stood at the tomb of someone He loved and wept. He didn't cry because He lacked strength. He didn't cry because He was powerless. He cried because He loved." This is the strongest moment in the manuscript — personal, textually anchored, pastorally courageous, and congregation-specific. It earns trust.
2. **The sewing closet illustration (Point 3B)**: Native to the sermon, native to the congregation, memorable, and genuinely illuminating of the "gifts that differ" point. The contrast of denim, leather, and lace mapped onto the body's diversity is well-constructed.
3. **Structural clarity**: The four-point outline is stated early, followed throughout, and coherent. A listener can track the argument. This is a genuine expositional discipline.

## 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The gospel is invoked but never proclaimed (throughout)**: The sermon uses "gospel," "mercy," and "grace" as atmospheric terms without ever defining them for a hearer who doesn't already know them. What is the gospel? What are the mercies of Romans 1–11 that ground this call? The cross must be proclaimed, not assumed. An unbeliever in the room receives no clear answer to "How do I get in on this mercy?"
2. **The Synagogue Test fails (Point 3, Gospel section)**: The sermon could be preached in a Jewish community center that believes in God, practices communal life, and honors mutual service — without giving offense at the necessity of Christ's blood. That is the diagnostic. Christ's atonement is not the hinge this sermon turns on; community values are.
3. **Spiritual differentiation is nearly absent (Points 1–4)**: The sermon speaks almost entirely to converted, committed believers. The conscience of the self-righteous is not cornered. The backslider is not addressed. The unbeliever present is mentioned in one closing sentence. A sermon on Romans 12 in a mixed congregation must at minimum ask: "Who in this room is trying to build community without being in Christ — and does this text address them?"

## 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely does not see that repeating "gospel" and "mercy" without defining them creates a private vocabulary that excludes the very people most in need of the sermon's message. He is preaching to the saints without noticing that some in the room may not yet be saints — and the text's ground (Romans 1–11's declaration of justification) is being assumed rather than proclaimed. The political/identity aside also reveals a blind spot: the preacher is more comfortable with ecclesiology than with soteriology when the pressure is on.

## 12. Illusion of Strength
The repeated use of "grace" and "gospel" creates the impression of gospel-centered preaching. But citation of the word is not proclamation of the thing. A sermon can say "grace" forty times and never explain what grace secured, at what cost, and for whom. This sermon is gospel-flavored but not gospel-driven. The warmth and specificity of the congregational references further masks this gap — the emotional resonance substitutes for theological weight.

## 13. Missed Opportunity
The sermon's own thesis—"Because of God's mercy"—is a doorway to Romans 1–11 that the preacher never fully enters. Paul's argument in Romans 12:1 is that the imperatives of communal life rest on the indicatives of chapters 1–11: wrath, condemnation, justification by faith alone, union with Christ, the gift of the Spirit, adoption. Each of those doctrines is the "mercy" Paul means. The sermon names the mountain ("eleven chapters of rich doctrine") but does not mine it for the gold that would make the application urgent. *Why* is humility possible? Because justification is by faith, not by performance. *Why* can we show mercy with cheerfulness? Because we ourselves received mercy we did not deserve at infinite cost. This is the missing engine.

## 14. Surgical Fixes
1. **Insert a gospel declaration between the close of Point 1 and the opening of Point 2**: After establishing that "the gospel changes how you think about yourself," drive one clear nail: "Here is the gospel — Christ took the condemnation your pride deserved, so that you stand before God not on the basis of your humility but on the basis of His righteousness. *That* is the mercy of Romans 1–11. *That* is why you can think soberly about yourself." Two to three sentences. Specific. Christologically anchored.
2. **Differentiate the conscience in Point 1D**: After "A prideful mind says / A gospel-shaped mind says," add a direct address to the person who is self-righteous about their service — the one who has been serving longest and is quietly resentful that others haven't noticed. Corner that person. The text is written for them.
3. **Cut or sharpen the political/identity aside in Point 2D**: The aside is three paragraphs long and wanders into political identity in a way that competes with the ecclesiological point. Either cut it to two sentences ("We are one body in Christ — that means every circle we inhabit must be evaluated against this primary belonging") or anchor it more directly to the text's argument.
4. **Close with a gospel-centered exhortation**: The current close ("But all of that starts with following Jesus, which begins with the gospel") is a sentence, not a summons. Write a closing paragraph that names what is at stake, drives the hearer to Christ as the source of power for everything just described, and calls for a specific response: "If you are trying to be a generous, humble, mercy-showing member of this body in your own strength, you will fail and resent it. The power for all of this is not in you — it is in Christ, who gave Himself for you. Come to Him. The rest follows."
5. **Add a direct address to the unbeliever**: Even one moment — "If you're here today and you're not sure you're in Christ, the passage we've been reading is a picture of what Christ purchases for you — a community, a place in a body, gifts given by grace. But you enter through the door of faith in Him, not through trying harder to be a good member." This closes the Perkins gap.

## 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — The sermon does not score high enough in exposition or theology individually to trigger this flag. The warmth and pastoral specificity also distinguish it from bloodless orthodoxy. The concern here is gospel absence, not cold correctness.

## 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The grief aside carries genuine unction — the moment where personal suffering becomes pastoral authority — but the sermon as a whole does not yet carry the eternal gravity that comes from standing under the full weight of what Romans 12:1–8 is built on: that God did not spare His own Son, and therefore these mercies are purchased at infinite cost.

## 17. Rubric Fit Note
The rubric fits this passage well. Romans 12:3–8 is hortatory-doctrinal prose, and the rubric's weighting (exposition, gospel centrality, conscience force, exhortation) maps naturally onto Paul's own movement from indicative to imperative. No genre strain. The rubric does not penalize the sermon for being an ethical text — it simply asks whether the ethical imperatives are grounded in gospel indicatives, which is Paul's own standard.
