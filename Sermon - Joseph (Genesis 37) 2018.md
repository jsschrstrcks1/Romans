### Source Notes

- **Primary source**: `Other-Sermons/Sermons-txt/Sermon 2 gen 37 joeseph.txt` — submission matches exactly.
- **Estimated date: Summer 2018** — "All those peoples lives upended by the volcano" in Hawaii; the Kilauea lower East Rift Zone eruption began May 3, 2018, destroying approximately 700 homes. The present-tense urgency of "right now" suggests the eruption was active at the time of delivery. The "through the bible in a year" commitment and this being "Sermon 2" in a Genesis/Joseph series is also consistent.
- **Typological structure**: This is a typological sermon (Joseph as a type of Christ). The typological parallels between Joseph and Jesus are well-established in Christian theology and are faithfully handled throughout.
- **"He was persecuted — mocked, humiliated, beaten, tempted, sold as a slave, and finally exalted"**: In the pre-reading list, the referent of "he" is ambiguous — the list blends things that happened to Christ (mocked, beaten, tempted) with things that happened to Joseph (sold as a slave). In context the preacher is previewing parallels between the two, but a reader encountering this in isolation may find the referent unclear. "Sold as a slave" refers to Joseph's experience (Gen 37:28); for Christ the typological parallel is Judas's betrayal for silver (not literal enslavement). The distinction could be clarified in future delivery.
- **Joseph's coat and the double portion**: "That coat of Joseph's also entitled him to a double share — the firstborn's share of the inheritance." The text of Genesis 37 does not explicitly state the coat conveyed a double inheritance share. Jacob's eldest son and legal firstborn was Reuben (Gen 29:32; 35:23). Joseph's line receives a double portion later, through Jacob adopting his two sons Ephraim and Manasseh as his own (Gen 48:5–6), giving Joseph's descendants two tribal allotments. The coat as a symbol of intended primary heirship (and thus the birthright/double portion) is a traditional interpretive inference supported by the cultural context of the text, but it is not stated explicitly. Recommend a phrase like "many commentators believe" or "the coat appears to have signified" to distinguish interpretation from explicit text.
- **Bill Gates illustration**: "It would be like Bill Gates if he had any kids." Bill Gates has three children: Jennifer Katharine (b. 1996), Rory John (b. 1999), and Phoebe Adele (b. 2002). The illustration contains a factual error. Consider updating to a figure without children, or adjust the illustration accordingly.
- **"Twenty pieces of silver"**: Genesis 37:28 specifies Joseph was sold for twenty shekels of silver. The sermon says "a few bits of silver" without specifying the amount. Worth noting the unstated typological parallel: Judas betrayed Jesus for thirty pieces of silver (Matthew 26:15). The difference in amount (20 vs. 30) is sometimes noted in typological commentaries.

**Joseph**

It strikes me that yet again we see the same old problem popping up. It happens over and over and over through Scripture, and often when we see it, it's a tip-off that something else is going on — that perhaps this text is also going to point to Christ, like so many of the others we've talked about over the years.

I like finding these passages where one of the people is a pointer to Christ. Before we open our Bibles, let's take a minute and describe some of the things that happened in Christ's life. Obviously all of these aren't going to carry over — it's a correlation, not an exact copy. I made a small list.

For one thing, **God loved Christ dearly**. John 15:9–10 says:

> "As the Father has loved me, so have I loved you. Abide in my love. If you keep my commandments, you will abide in my love, just as I have kept my Father's commandments and abide in his love."

Jesus loved us enough to die on the cross for our sins. If God loves Jesus as much as Jesus loves us, that's a pretty fierce love — a love that will go to death for you, will go to the ends of the earth for you as well. That means anything Jesus says about God's love for us is also true of God's love for Christ. That makes me think of music — we all know love songs where a guy swears he'd climb any mountain, cross any river, do anything he had to for the woman he loves. The difference here is that God through Christ accomplished more than those songs and songwriters could have ever imagined. They may have promised those things, but Christ did them and more.

Another thing that occurred to me — and I'm trying to think of things perhaps you guys wouldn't have — **God speaks to Christ publicly, declaring his affection**. There are a few moments, but the one that sticks out in my mind is when Jesus was baptized. Matthew 3:16–17:

> "As soon as Jesus was baptized, he went up out of the water. At that moment heaven was opened, and he saw the Spirit of God descending like a dove and alighting on him. And a voice from heaven said, 'This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased.'"

Another aspect — **Jesus threatened the established leaders' power**. The Pharisees had brokered an uneasy peace with Rome. They were allowed to retain semblances of control and power, but they had to keep their people in line. When their people started following Christ instead, they began to lose their grip, and thus their authority in the eyes of Rome, and thus their income, their prestige, their influence — and maybe even their lives. The country had divided into two camps — the Jesus Party and the Pharisee Party. But your average Roman with a sword isn't going to know the difference, isn't going to care. Caiaphas knew that when Rome came, many lives would be lost. Which is why he says in John 11:50:

> "You do not realize that it is better for you that one man die for the people than that the whole nation perish."

This Gospel truth, spoken in the midst of a conspiracy to kill Jesus, was never more true than when Christ died on the Cross — once and for all, defeating sin and death and hell and Satan. He rose again three days later and brought salvation to the world. Caiaphas was more right than he knew: it was better for us that Christ should die, but through Christ, we could all have life.

There are a few more, and then we'll get to the text. See if you recognize any of these — or any others — in our story tonight.

**Because of jealousy and the threat to their power, people tried to kill him.** John 11:53 says: "So from that day on they plotted to take his life."

**Christ was sent to save the very people that rejected him.** Psalm 118 and again in Matthew 21 address this. Jesus is the stone that the builders rejected, that became the cornerstone. Someone read Matthew 21:42. Who were the builders — the Pharisees? Yet through his life, death, burial, and resurrection he became the cornerstone of the salvation of everyone.

**He was persecuted — mocked, humiliated, beaten, tempted — and finally exalted at the right hand of the Father.** *(Note: this list previews parallels between Christ and Joseph; "sold as a slave" is Joseph's specific experience (Gen 37:28), while the typological parallel for Christ is Judas's betrayal for silver — see source notes.)*

Now let's read today's passage: Genesis 37:1–4. Keep your finger there — I'm going to need you to do some more reading for me this morning.

*(Read Genesis 37:1–4.)*

Already we see some parallels, do we not?

The first thing we talked about with Christ was that his Father loved him very much. Likewise, Joseph's father loved him very much — to the point where everyone knew Joseph was the favorite. It's hard to deal with parental favoritism. I knew a girl at school who had to deal with this. She grew up in Hawaii — which is a place we need to keep in our prayers right now; all those people's lives upended by the volcano. Anyway, this girl grew up in Hawaii. She had a brother about a year older than her. For his sixteenth birthday, her parents bought a 2004 Ford F-150, King Ranch edition — leather seats, heat and air, premium sound, high-end rims, and an aftermarket cup warmer installed so his coffee would be warm on his way to high school every morning. They even bought seat warmers. For Hawaii. How does that even make sense?

Anyway, as my friend's sixteenth birthday rolled around, she tried to downplay her excitement. She didn't figure she'd get a brand-new car, but maybe a used beater — an old beat-up pickup, or a worn-out Honda. She even made sure she had her license before her birthday so she could drive whatever they got her right away. She was pretty excited the morning of her birthday. They handed her a small envelope. She thought for sure it would contain a car key. When she opened it, there was a braided hemp bracelet with a garage sale sticker price of $1. Needless to say, she was disappointed. Fortunately, she didn't try to kill her brother like what we'll see in our passage this morning. But imagine how that favoritism would feel — a lavish, ornate cloak of authority for him, while you're left cleaning up after the sheep.

The cloak was interesting because the style of the day for such a garment would have been cut impractically for work. You couldn't wear this thing and do any real labor. It was a public declaration of affection from Jacob to Joseph — like God did for Christ at the baptism. And that caused jealousy in his family.

*(Read Genesis 37:11.)*

So his brothers were jealous and hatched a plan. Any similarities to Christ so far? Good.

*(Read Genesis 37:17–18.)*

Joseph's brothers were so jealous and so threatened by him that they decided to conspire to kill him. Sound familiar? If Joseph is like Jesus in this story, who are his brothers like? The Pharisees.

That coat of Joseph's also appears to have carried the significance of a double share — the firstborn's portion of the inheritance. *(Many commentators connect the coat to intended heirship; the double portion is formally established later when Jacob adopts Joseph's two sons Ephraim and Manasseh as his own, giving Joseph's line two tribal allotments — Genesis 48:5–6.)* Jacob was a ridiculously wealthy man — something like a modern billionaire. He could have written each son a check for a fortune and not blinked. They could have lived wealthy, comfortable lives on a single portion of that inheritance. But they were greedy — and greed is wanting more. They were jealous — jealousy is saying "I want yours." And they were envious — envy is saying "I don't want you to have what's yours." They apparently didn't want the cloak itself. They just didn't want Joseph to have the power, the authority, and the wealth it symbolized.

And those three sins weren't the only ones. They wanted to murder their brother, conspired together to do it, and then ultimately sold him into slavery for a few pieces of silver. *(Gen 37:28 specifies 20 shekels; the unstated typological echo is Judas's 30 pieces of silver, Matthew 26:15.)*

*(Read Genesis 37:23–24.)*

Joseph was stripped of his robe, stripped of his rightful treatment, stripped of his inheritance, and tossed into a pit empty of water.

It mentions the pit was empty of water — but it doesn't mention being empty of other critters. Seems to me that in a hot desert like that, things might want to get into the shade a dry well would offer during certain parts of the day. If I were a snake or a scorpion, I might be inclined to spend part of my day in the cool shade of that pit.

*(Read Genesis 37:28–34.)*

They add lying to their sins. To sell the lie, they killed one of their father's grazing animals, poured its blood over Joseph's coat, and took it to their father to identify. I love their approach. They act unsure — "Maybe, could it be? Is it possibly the coat we've hated and loathed for so long?" They knew! They just wanted to deceive their father.

---

So, any more parallels between Christ and Joseph come to mind? Good.

It strikes me that all this strife traces back to Jacob's father-in-law's deception — and to Jacob allowing the tension and rivalry in his home to continue. We never hear of any trouble between Leah and Rachel before they married the same man. Afterward, all we see is strife, a house divided. How much grief could have been avoided if the tension between the two women had been settled?

How much did Leah share with her children about how her sister made her feel? How much of this trouble went back to Leah poisoning the proverbial well? It's hard to say. But we've all known people who would do that — people who try to spin their web, draw others into their side of the argument.

But the word of God says blessed are the peacemakers (Matthew 5:9). Proverbs 16:28 says:

> "A perverse person stirs up conflict, and a gossip separates close friends."

Godly leadership in the home matters. How do you know you're leading your family in a godly way? By reading your Bible. By praying, by going to church. Scripture says as iron sharpens iron, so one person sharpens another (Proverbs 27:17). By spending time together we are better equipped to face the tensions of the world. By spending time with the Lord we are better equipped to fight the evil advances of our enemy. Without it, we're like the soldier who goes to war without his sword and shield. The Spartans had a saying — "Come back with your shield or on it." It meant: be prepared to fight or die trying. How silly to think of a soldier going into battle unprepared. But how are we any different when we don't spend time with the Lord on a daily basis?
