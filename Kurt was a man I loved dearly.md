## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Pastoral Eulogy with Gospel Instincts But No Sermonic Scaffolding

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A grief-saturated funeral message that carries genuine gospel instincts and a moving personal commission from the dying man, but never develops its stated text into a sermon and leaves the congregation informed rather than cornered before Christ.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**CONDITIONAL PASS — with significant qualification.**

The sermon does reference 2 Corinthians 5:1-10 and gestures at its content (the earthly tent destroyed, present with the Lord, the judgment seat of Christ). The main point — that Christians face death assured of presence with Christ and will one day stand before the judgment seat — does arise from the passage. The scopus test passes narrowly.

However, the Authority Test exposes a problem: the passage is quoted but never opened. The preacher does not stand under the text to explain it, submit to it, and let it govern the sermon's argument. The text is present as emotional scaffolding, not as the sermon's authority structure. This is the difference between a pastoral address that uses a text and an expository sermon governed by one. For a funeral context, this is understandable — but it must be named.

**Gate: Conditional Pass — the main point arises from the passage, but the passage is not expounded.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score cap: 69 — multiple flags triggered.**

- **Flag 4: Application is entirely generic.** The invitation to "follow Jesus" and "find him" is the only application given, and it remains at the level of a slogan rather than a specific call. No differentiation of hearers. The grieving widow, the hardened unbeliever, the nominal church member, and the genuine believer all receive the same undifferentiated word.
- **Flag 5: No actual exhortation.** The sermon points toward the gospel (Romans 3:23 is referenced at the end) but the text cuts off before any exhortation lands. The manuscript ends mid-thought. Even reading it generously as a complete draft, the call is implied but never issued. The congregation is moved but never summoned.
- **Flag 2 (borderline): Main point drifts from the passage's argument.** 2 Corinthians 5:1-10 carries a strong argument about confidence in the face of death, the groaning of the present body, the desire to be clothed with the heavenly dwelling, the walk by faith, and the judgment seat of Christ. ### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

**Weakness 1: The text is cited but never opened.**
2 Corinthians 5:1-10 is announced and quoted but never expounded. Paul's argument — the groaning of the present body, the confidence of the believer, the walk by faith not sight, the ambition to please the Lord, the judgment seat — is touched at best, ignored at worst. A congregation that hears this sermon cannot reconstruct what Paul is arguing in 2 Corinthians 5. The text is a launchpad, not the sermon's governing authority.

**Weakness 2: The gospel is gestured toward but never proclaimed.**
The manuscript ends at Romans 3:23 — the text is cited, the gospel presentation is about to begin, and then the sermon stops. Whether this is an incomplete draft or a transcript that cuts off, the result is a funeral where the congregation that most needs the gospel — the unbelieving family members, the lapsed churchgoers who came out of respect — did not receive it in full. Kurt's dying request was "you gotta get em there too." The sermon sets it up and then stops before delivering.

**Weakness 3: Hearers are not differentiated with any specificity.**
The room at Kurt's funeral almost certainly contained: a grieving widow who needed comfort and hope, adult children at different points of faith, military brothers who respect toughness but distrust religion, grandchildren, and nominal church members. Not one of these is specifically addressed. The sermon speaks to "those who are not Christians" as a single undifferentiated mass. Perkins' taxonomy is entirely absent. The sermon misses the grieving, the hardened, the already-believing who needs to be stabilized, and the doubter who came hoping to find something real.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how much the sermon's emotional weight depends on Kurt rather than on the Word. The affection is real and the pastoral instincts are genuine — but the risk is that in a room where not everyone knew and loved Kurt, the sermon has less purchase. The text, if opened, would carry weight in any room, with any widow, at any funeral. The personal tribute carries weight only where Kurt was known. The preacher's blind spot is assuming that the emotional power of the relationship transfers to the gospel claim.

There is also a blind spot around the incomplete manuscript. The sermon ends at Romans 3:23 — whether the preacher preached more or the transcript is incomplete, the evaluation must work with what exists. If more was preached, it needs to be captured in the manuscript.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The crucible illustration *feels* like exposition because it connects to themes in the text (purification, suffering, transformation). But it is not exposition — it is a thematic parallel drawn from outside the text. The preacher may feel that because the illustration aligns with the passage's *themes*, it is functioning as *exposition*. It is not. Theme-alignment is not textual argument. The congregation came away with a powerful image (crucible, pure gold) that does not directly arise from Paul's words in 2 Corinthians 5. The illustration is strong — but it creates the illusion that the text has been opened when it has only been approached.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

2 Corinthians 5:6-8 contains one of the most pastorally powerful statements in the New Testament for a funeral: *"We are always confident, knowing that, while we are at home in the body, we are absent from the Lord... We are confident, yes, well pleased rather to be absent from the body and to be present with the Lord."* This is not a vague comfort — it is an argued confidence. Paul does not merely say "it gets better." He says we *know* something. He grounds the confidence in faith ("we walk by faith, not by sight") and in the character of the One who is waited for. The preacher touched this but did not mine it. A dying man's family needed to hear not just that Kurt is with the Lord, but *why* Paul says we can be *confident* of this — what grounds the confidence, what it is grounded in, and what it produces in those who are still walking by faith in the body.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3-5)

1. **Open the text before the illustration, not after it.** Read 2 Corinthians 5:1-10 and walk through Paul's argument in at least three structural movements: (1) the destroyed tent vs. the heavenly dwelling (vv. 1-5), (2) the confident walk by faith (vv. 6-9), (3) the judgment seat (v. 10). Let the text govern the sermon's movement.

2. **Complete the gospel presentation.** Romans 3:23 is the right hinge. Follow it with Romans 6:23 and Romans 5:8 (or the clear proclamation of Christ's substitutionary death and resurrection). Issue the specific call: *This is how you find Kurt. This is how you stand before the judgment seat and hear "well done." Believe on the Lord Jesus Christ.* Kurt asked for this. Finish it.

3. **Add direct address to at least three categories of hearers after the gospel is presented.** Speak to: (a) the widow and immediate family who are grieving and need comfort rooted in the Word; (b) the unbeliever in the room who respected Kurt but has never believed — name them specifically and gently corner them; (c) the lapsed believer who walked away and needs to be called back. This fulfills Kurt's commission with pastoral precision.

4. **Integrate the crucible illustration with the text's own language.** Paul uses the image of groaning (vv. 2, 4) — this is not far from the crucible's heat. The illustration can stay, but anchor it explicitly to Paul's words: "Paul says we groan in this tent — Kurt groaned. The crucible was not just boot camp. It was every hard year." This moves the illustration from thematic parallel to textual illumination.

5. **End the sermon with a doxology or benediction that carries the congregation out on the text's note, not the eulogy's note.** The sermon currently moves toward Kurt's commission and then reaches for the gospel. It needs a closing that unifies tribute, text, and commission — landing on the surpassing weight of glory (2 Cor. 4:17, the immediate context) and sending the congregation out with worship, not merely sadness.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This rubric is designed for Sunday expository preaching and strains significantly against a funeral homily. A funeral address serves a different genre with different constraints: a mixed room of believers and unbelievers, extreme emotional vulnerability, limited tolerance for extended exposition, and the pastoral necessity of honoring the deceased. The rubric's categories for exposition, structure, and differentiation of hearers apply with modification — the preacher is not expected to deliver a 40-minute expository sermon at a graveside. What the rubric *does* legitimately demand even in a funeral context is: (1) that the text be actually opened, even briefly; (2) that the gospel be actually proclaimed, not merely gestured at; (3) that a specific response be called for, not merely implied. On those three counts, the rubric's pressure is valid regardless of genre. The lower scores in exposition and structure should be read against the funeral context — they are not failures of pastoral judgment so much as they are the natural limits of the genre. The gospel and exhortation gaps, however, are genre-independent failures that the preacher himself recognized and began to address (Romans 3:23 was the next move) — the sermon simply stops before he gets there.
