A tale of two men.
If you've brought your bibles, and I hope you have, were going to be in Gen 13 tonight. Starting in vs1. While you're turning there, I want to talk about Israel for a moment.
I think we all have a picture in our heads of what Israel must be like. We've all seen the Charlton Heston movies ("let my people go") that show a vast inhospitable desert. Most of us that have read the bible, or even those of us who have spent any time at church have probably read about desert, and famine, and Jesus and the disciples having dusty feet and needing to have them washed. But is that really what it was like? Was it all desert all the time?

You guys know me, I did some research. Turns out the climate of Israel is not so different from ours – except in a few key points. Their summers are hot – 80s to 90s, the winters are mild. They get an abundance of rain. The key difference is that they have a dry heat, where we have a lot of humidity. Their rain comes mostly in winter. So I dug a little deeper. It struck me that the book of Exodus describes the promised land, or the land of Israel as a land flowing with milk and honey. In order for that to be true a couple of other things must also be true. For one, the land must be exceptionally fertile. I don't know if they had kept cows back in Abrahams day, but I know they kept sheep. I have sheep as well at home, and know a little about them as well. For instance grass fed sheep produce more milk than grain fed sheep. I know that at this point in history, they would have been raising wool sheep. Wool sheep require 300 pounds of feed to produce a single pound of wool. Which means most of their feed goes into producing wool. So in order for them to produce an abundance of milk, they are going to need a huge amount of food. Because that food first goes to the wool. So there had to have been an abundance of exceptional pasture land. It is interesting to point out that in Israel the average milk animal to this day produces more milk than the average milk animal any where else in the world.

Secondly flowing with honey may have had two meanings. The first one seems the most obvious – bee honey. But Jewish scholars argue that while there may be a lot of bee honey, they believe the scriptures were talking about Date pollen. Apparently when Dates are living in the right climate, and in exceptionally fertile ground their pollen flows out of their flowers like honey. In either case, their would need to be an abundance of highly fertile soil (not desert), and a bunch of plants. Israel at this point in history would have been pretty lush, with a comfortable dry climate with plenty of lush pastures. It was quite beautiful. Hilly, but beautiful. That brings us to our passage tonight.

Read with me Gen 13:1-13

I love how God set the stage for this decision. He had blessed both Lot and Abraham so much that the land couldn't support both of them at the same time. Verse 7 says that there was so much strife, that the herdsmen – the shepherds were fighting over grazing land. I've been told that wool sheep will tear the grass up by the roots leaving nothing but dirt behind, and so they require a lot of management. I imagine with the amount of possessions they had, that their shepherds were having to walk further and further away each day to keep the ground from becoming barren. This would cause strife.

Imagine if your job, the job that you walk to moved further and further away each day. In time Lots men, and Abrams men would be arguing over the few choice places that were left. Or worse, lack of communication might have caused them to walk all the way out to where they had planned to feed the sheep, only to find that it had been completely grazed for miles already, and now you have to walk miles more to find a place that was suitable for the sheep. This would have been a pretty big deal. Because you can only walk so far out before you can't make it back in the same day. They had to make it back because they were leading around everyone's dinner, because back at the camp was the shelter for the sheep, the fenced structure that would have held everyone's sheep, and protected them from any predators that would seek to harm the animals.

This was no small procession. I think today when we think about people traveling like this we think well there was sarah, abram, and lot. Three people traveling together like we might. A couple days worth of clothes in a backpack in the trunk of the car. But it wasn't like that was it. They lived off the land, the land fed the animals, the animals fed the family. But it wasn't just the family was it? There were shepherds vs 7, there were servants of all kinds. Each additional mouth meant more animals, more animals meant more servants. They would have had mounts that they rode. But those animals needed to eat, so they would have had grain for their camels or horses. That grain needed to be carried everywhere they went which would likely have meant donkeys. Someone had to feed the donkeys. Someone had to feed the horses and camels. That someone likely had a wife, and kids. By the time everything shakes out, there would have likely been several thousand or more people traveling with Abraham and Lot. This was no small group of people traveling together. It was more like a small city moving around. There would have been people traveling with them for safety, for trade, and so forth. There would have been tent makers and menders, there would have been shop keepers of other kinds, there would have been people to make and mend clothing, there would have been people to protect the wealth of the family. There would have been people to hunt down wild animals that threaten the camp, and all of those men would have likely had wives, and at some point kids.

Needless to say, at some point conflict was bound to arise. Which is exactly what happened.

You ever have friends or family over that you love to see come, and you love to see go? We had a situation one time where a family came to stay with my parents for a hurricane. It was supposed to hit the west coast of Florida, but ended up coming up the east coast and hitting us. It wasn't a bad storm, but we lost power, and ended up spending our time outdoors, under my parents stilt home where the wind kept things cool, but we were able to stay relatively dry as well. Any way after a few days of their presence (and we dearly love them) they decided to drive home. In the middle of the storm. It sort of followed them back to their house. A pastor I once knew said family is like fertilizer. Its incredibly important, valuable, and to be treasured, but too much of it in one spot can make things stink. They were ready to leave, and we were ready for that. So were things with Abram and Lot. They still loved each other, were happy to have the other visit. But they needed to part company to keep the situation from devolving into something much more unpleasant.
So we have looked at Israel and how it was flowing with milk and honey, not exactly the desert one expects, we looked at the separation, now lets look at the decision.

Knowing they had to part ways, and seemingly without praying, Abram puts the decision in Lots hands. Lot again without praying looked east and west, then made up his mind. To the east he saw foothills, and mountains. Fertile land its true, but farming the grain they would need would require terraced gardens. It wasn't as well watered, and frankly, valleys tend to have the better of the soil. He looked east and west and he saw on one hand a good land, but one that required more work, and to his west he saw what seemed to be a great land, and bonus – it was close to a couple major cities. But I can't emphasize this enough, when facing this major life change – not only for them but for all those following them, neither one of them spent any time on their knees in prayer. Abram just got in trouble last week in Egypt for this, and so rather than praying, Abram believes that no matter what Lot chooses, God will take care of him. Lot rather than praying picks the choicest of the land leaving his uncle with the leftovers. Because of God's providence here and his sovereignty none of the land was bad. But there were in lots mind advantages to living in the valley. A famous preacher and writer named Calvin once said "even as he thought he was living in heaven, he had already sunk almost into hell".

Here is the crux of the whole story. You and I like Lot are faced with decisions every day. For most of us, they don't involve where a few thousand people are going to live, however they are important in our own lives, and the lives of our kids and loved ones.

One major decision coming up is the election.

I talked to some of you while we were passing out flyers and for some of you the decision may be regarding what church to go to.

Maybe some of you are thinking about getting married, or whether or not to have kids.

The absolute most important decision you can personally make in your entire life is whether or not to follow Jesus.

Following God was the question at the heart of the decision Lott had to make. Its interesting to me that at the bottom of the Jordan Valley, is the Jordan river, and does anyone know where the Jordan river leads? Straight to the dead sea. It's waters are beautiful, but its so salty no vegetation can survive around it. Nothing can survive by drinking only its waters.

In choosing to go the direction that looked best to Lot, Lot chose to start a downward slide towards sin that nearly resulted in his physical death, and I dare say his eternal separation from God. It is just like the decisions you face today. Do I do what I want, or do I do what God wants.

Do I follow Jesus, or do I do what makes me feel good for a few minutes.

Today like Lot you are standing at an important cross roads. To your left is following Jesus, and to your right following yourself, following whatever feels good to you in the moment, following the lusts of your eyes.

If you go right ultimately Mathew 7 says that path leads to destruction, and many will find it.

If you decide to follow Jesus, you have eternal life with Jesus in heaven. There is no third option.
