### 3. Berean Gate Result
**FAIL — Partial**

The Scopus Test strains: the sermon's working thesis appears to be "make God-directed decisions rather than self-directed ones," which is adjacent to the text's content but does not arise from Genesis 13's own argument. Genesis 13's main point is God's sovereign faithfulness to Abraham in the covenant — Abram's faith-driven deference and God's covenant renewal in vv. 14–18 are the climax. The sermon treats the passage as a decision-making morality tale, omitting the covenant renewal entirely (vv. 14–18 receive zero treatment). The Authority Test also reveals drift: the sermon softens the passage into generic decision-making wisdom, bypasses God's speech to Abram, and ends with an exhortation framework ("left vs. right") that is the preacher's own construction, not the text's. The gate does not result in a full stop because the sermon is manifestly early-stage (raw transcript, community grand opening, appears to be an early homily), and there is a genuine effort to handle the passage. However, the structural failure is real and must be named.

The preacher appears unaware that **Genesis 13:14–18 exists homiletically** — that the chapter's resolution is not the separation of Abram and Lot, but God's speech of covenant faithfulness afterward. The sermon ends where the drama peaks but before God speaks. This suggests the preacher may be reading the narrative as a human story (Abram and Lot making decisions) rather than as a theological story (God keeping his covenant despite human failure).

There is also a likely blind spot around **the difference between application and exhortation**. The sermon makes applications (decisions matter, follow Jesus), but application is not exhortation. ### 13. Missed Opportunity

**The biggest missed opportunity is the entirety of vv. 14–18.** After Lot departs, God comes to Abram and says: *"Lift up your eyes and look from the place where you are, northward and southward and eastward and westward, for all the land that you see I will give to you and to your offspring forever."* This is God redeeming the moment. Lot lifted his eyes and saw what he wanted (v. 10). God now tells Abram to lift his eyes — and what he sees is not given by clever choice but by unconditional promise. The contrast between Lot's self-directed sight and God's covenant-directed word to Abram is the entire sermon. The preacher walked up to the cliff edge of this and turned back.

Additionally, the covenantal connection to Christ — that Abram's offspring who receives this land promise is ultimately fulfilled in Christ (Galatians 3:16, 29) — is the New Testament grounding that would have given the "follow Jesus" appeal actual theological content instead of a placeholder.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific)

1. **Preach vv. 14–18.** Make God's covenant renewal speech the climax, not vv. 11–13. Structure the sermon so that everything before v. 14 is tension (strife, separation, Lot's self-directed choice), and God's speech in vv. 14–18 is the resolution. The sermon's main point becomes: *God's covenant faithfulness is more certain than our best choices.* This arises from the text; the current main point does not.

2. **Prepare and deliver an actual gospel presentation.** The placeholder must be replaced with a proclaimed word: Christ as the seed of Abraham in whom all the nations are blessed (Galatians 3), the cross as the fulfillment of covenant promise, justification by faith as the only ground of standing before God. This is not a section to be improvised — write it out, word for word, until it can be delivered with clarity and force.

3. **Cut the agricultural research by two-thirds and relocate what remains.** One sentence establishing the lushness of the land is sufficient context. The sheep-lactation economics and date-pollen discussion consume perhaps a quarter of the sermon and deliver nothing the text requires. The time belongs to vv. 14–18.

4. **Apply the Perkins taxonomy to the conclusion.** After the gospel is presented, address at least three categories of hearer distinctly: (a) the person who, like Lot, has already made the self-directed choice and is experiencing its consequences — speak to them with hope; (b) the person standing at the decision right now — speak with urgency; (c) the believer who is already following Jesus but following him prayerlessly, like Abram — speak with pastoral challenge. "Follow Jesus or follow yourself" does not reach any of them with specificity.

5. **Replace the two-path diagram with an exhortation grounded in God's promise.** The sermon currently calls people to make the right choice. The text calls people to trust God's covenant word. These are different invitations. The exhortation should be: *God has spoken. He has promised. The question is not which path looks better to you — it is whether you will lift your eyes at God's word or at your own assessment of the landscape.* That is an exhortation with a gospel backbone.
