My nieces love to swim at my parents' house.

You know the place — on the canal. Spring-fed, clear water, gulf access. It's a nice place to swim in the summer, and the girls love it. Except every year, they have to be reminded just how much they love it.

When I was a kid I remember one summer being absolutely convinced that an alligator was going to come bursting out from behind the rocks under the dock and eat me before I could get back to the stairs. The next summer a cousin pointed out how remarkable it was that a bull shark hadn't found its way in from the gulf and eaten all the noisy children. The following summer I was convinced there was an alligator and a bull shark waiting for me under the dock. I may be overstating the case slightly. But the first time you jumped in each summer was a little nerve-racking — even when you knew, you knew, you knew that it was completely irrational.

So I could sympathize with the girls a little bit this past week when they were reluctant to get in. I jumped in first. Lilly was going to be right behind me — she got to the edge and stopped. I told her it was safe. She ran to jump a second time, got scared before she reached the edge, and stopped again. Finally she asked if she could catch my hand as she jumped.

I agreed. She ran. She jumped — right in front of me — and caught my hand mid-air, and held it all the way into the water.

There's something about holding an adult's hand. When you're a kid and the lights are out and there's a monster in the closet, what do you do? You go get an adult. You hold their hand while you go investigate. There's peace in knowing that whatever might be lurking in that closet, the adult can handle it.

Keep that in your mind. We'll come back to it.

---

Tonight we're in John chapter 20. While you're turning there — John is in the New Testament, about three-quarters of the way through your Bible — let me tell you a little about the man we're going to spend time with tonight.

His name is Thomas. People have been quick to give him a bad reputation, but he was a good man who loved Christ fiercely. Before we read tonight's passage, I want to show you something from earlier in the same gospel — chapter 11.

You remember the story of Lazarus? The man Jesus raised from the dead? Just before that, when Lazarus had died, Jesus said he wanted to go to Judea — which was the place Lazarus lived, and also the place where people were actively plotting to stone Christ. The other disciples were unsettled. They were trying to work out how to talk him out of going.

Thomas didn't hesitate.

*"Let us also go, that we may die with him."* — John 11:16

That's Thomas. He didn't fully understand everything about Jesus yet. But he knew he wanted to be with him. Whatever it costs — even if it costs me my life — I'll go. John 15:13 says: *"Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends."* Thomas wasn't giving his life for anyone just yet. But he was willing to walk into the mouth of the lion to follow Christ. No hesitation. No doubt.

---

I'm reminded of a girl I heard about who had that same kind of faith.

She was a student at Columbine High School on April 20, 1999. Her name was Valeen Schnurr. When the shooting started in the library, she took cover. She was shot — twelve wounds from a shotgun blast. As she lay bleeding on the floor, she cried out, "Oh God, oh God."

One of the gunmen heard her. He came back over and asked her why she was saying that. Did she believe in God?

She said yes.

He asked her why.

"Because I believe," she said. "And because my parents raised me that way."

He reloaded and walked away. She survived.

She had every reason to deny it. She was bleeding. There was a loaded weapon to her head. She said yes anyway.

I want to tell you another story. This one I heard from a missionary I know who was there.

He was in his twenties at the time. They were in Uzbekistan — one of the Central Asian countries, not far from Afghanistan, Pakistan, Tajikistan. In that part of the world, converting away from Islam marks you as a traitor. So this house church met at 2am. People arrived in staggered intervals to avoid drawing attention. My friend had to walk two miles, jumping fences in the dark, to get there by 1:30.

About twenty-seven people in a small room. They shared a meal — draal soup, a thick okra soup — around 2am. By 2:30, people were putting their plates away when an armed man burst in, fired a few rounds into the ceiling, and ordered everyone back to their seats.

In Uzbekistan, it is against the law to be a believer in Christ.

One by one, he called each person to the front. Gun to their head. Demanded they declare their allegiance — Jesus or Allah. If they said Allah, they could leave with their family.

The first person, a deacon, said Allah and was sent out. The second, a Sunday school teacher, said Allah and was sent out.

Then a thirteen-year-old girl named Umida was called up.

She looked the man in the face with his gun against her head and said: *"I believe in the one true triune God, who is three and yet one, whose Son lived a perfect life and died on the cross for my sins. I believe in Jesus. Taking my life will only put me in the presence of my Lord that much faster."*

He struck her in the head with the butt of the rifle and put her against the wall.

It continued down the line. Same threats. Same punishments. My friend declared for Christ and was struck in the face. When they finally got to the pastor, he did much the same — tried to share the gospel with what he thought were his last breaths.

The soldier knelt down and wept.

He said he had to know they really believed. He was the commander of the unit tasked with persecuting Christians in that region. But Jesus had appeared to him in a dream. Showed him the holes in his hands. Asked him: *"Muhammad, why do you persecute me? Seek me and you will find me."*

Muhammad knew he needed to find people who could tell him about Christ. So the next time he found out about a gathering, he stole the file from the police and went himself.

Twenty-two of the twenty-seven did not recant.

---

Can you say the same? If it came down to it — gun to your head — would you follow Christ?

Thomas said yes to that question before he ever had to answer it.

Now let's read tonight's passage. Five verses. John chapter 20, starting at verse 24.

*[Read John 20:24–29.]*

---

Thomas's words in verse 25 struck me. *"Unless I see in his hands the mark of the nails, and place my finger into the mark of the nails, and place my hand into his side, I will never believe."*

Here is the man who was willing to walk into a stone-throwing crowd to die with Jesus — and now he's on the floor. He's been gutted by grief. The man he loved, the man he pledged his life to, is dead. And he missed it when the disciples saw him risen.

I've heard it said — and I think there's something in it — that God often teaches things experientially. You've heard the warning about praying for patience, because the answer to that prayer is usually a long line of opportunities to exhibit it. In college the courses go 101, 102, 201, 202. In seminary they started at 1000, worked up to 2000, 3000, on up to 10000 at the doctoral level. The questions get harder. The depth expected increases.

The death and resurrection of Jesus was a PhD-level course in faith for Thomas. And he very nearly failed it — partly because he wasn't there.

One commentator observes that Thomas had forsaken the assembly. While the other disciples huddled together behind locked doors — grieving, yes, but together — Thomas was somewhere else, alone in his grief. So when the women came back from the empty tomb, when Jesus appeared to the disciples, when their spirits lifted and everything clicked — Thomas wasn't there. He was left to wallow in his grief longer than the others.

We do that. When life falls apart, does it drive you toward God or away from him? Do you open your Bible more? Do you come to church? Do you press into prayer? Or does the grief make you pull back?

Most people's first instinct is to isolate. We put the Bible down. We skip church. *I don't feel like it. I'll go next week.* Some people tell me they're too ugly inside to come into the presence of God. Friend — that is a lie. What drives out darkness but light? What relieves pain but the joy that comes from the Lord?

Psalm 23 — which David wrote in the middle of his own wilderness — says God leads his people to green pastures. Restores their soul. Walks with them through the valley. Comforts them. Fills their cup. Do you think that's the God who wants you to run off when things get hard?

Psalm 37:23–24 says: *"The steps of a man are established by the LORD, when he delights in his way; though he fall, he shall not be cast headlong, for the LORD upholds his hand."*

The Lord upholds his hand. Not in spite of the fall. In the fall.

We have sheep. You know this. About seventeen of them right now. The babies move in one fuzzy clump — they stick together. But the mothers are a different story. We let them out to graze the yard. Some of them will wander, drink from the canal, test the fence line. We had one baby get separated on the other side of the canal. Dad had to go after it — kayak or swimming, I like to picture him swimming — and bring it back.

Sheep without a shepherd are in serious danger. A sheep that gets separated doesn't have to be attacked to die. A bite, a thicket, a sore hoof, heat — any of it can cause a sheep to give up. Jesus said: *I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep.* Luke 15 says he leaves the ninety-nine to go find the one.

He went after the one. That's Thomas.

---

Look again at verse 26. Christ comes through locked doors and stands among them. And his first word to Thomas — after the doubt, after the absence, after the declaration that he would never believe — is peace.

*"Peace be with you."*

Not: where have you been, Thomas? Not: I poured three years into you — where is your faith? Not: do you remember what you said about dying with me? Just — peace.

He then offered himself for examination. *Here are the holes. Put your finger here. Put your hand in my side.* Whatever you need, Thomas. Ask the question. Look at the evidence. I'm not threatened by your doubt.

Maybe you've been running something like Thomas's question. Maybe there's something you've wrestled with for a long time. I want you to know that in Christ are the answers to every skeptic's question. He's not waiting to punish you for asking. His first word is peace.

Thomas saw the hands. And what had been the greatest doubt became the greatest declaration in the entire gospel:

*"My Lord and my God."*

---

So let me ask you — what's your knee-jerk reaction to hardship?

When things fall apart, do you run to Christ? Or do you try to manage it on your own like Thomas did — alone in a locked room somewhere, grief compounding, the assembly gathering without you?

And if you don't know him — if you don't have the kind of faith that holds on when the lights go out — I want to offer you what Thomas found. Peace. Not the absence of trouble. An anchor in the middle of it. A hand to hold mid-jump.

Romans 10:9: *"if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*

Come find me after the service if you want to talk about it.

Pray. Hold on. The good shepherd is not confused about where you are.

Just like Lilly coming off the dock — you reach out, he's already there.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm Pastoral Heart with an Underbuilt Gospel Center and a Missing Exhortation Edge**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A tenderly pastoral opening of John 20:24–29 that rehabilitates Thomas with genuine care and illustrative warmth, while stopping short of fully proclaiming the gospel, pressing the conscience of distinct hearers, or calling for a specific obedient response.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS (with qualification)**
The sermon's thesis — that Thomas's doubt arose from isolation and grief, and that Christ meets doubters with peace — is derivable from the passage. The main point is defensible as a reading of John 20:24–29. However, the sermon's true center of gravity drifts toward isolation/grief management rather than the passage's own climactic emphasis: the declaration "My Lord and my God" (v. 28) and Christ's beatitude over those who believe without seeing (v. 29). The passage's own climax is under-preached.

**Authority Test: PASS (with caution)**
The preacher submits to the text's claims and does not edit out its demands. The uncomfortable word — Thomas's absence from the assembly — is pressed. However, v. 29 ("Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed") receives no treatment, and this is the interpretive hinge of the whole pericope. Its omission tilts the sermon toward pastoral comfort while softening the passage's implicit exhortation to faith without sight.

**Gate Verdict: PASS** — but the sermon preaches the approach to the climax more fully than the climax itself.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 — Partial: Main point drifts from text's point.** The text's main point is the resurrection appearance to Thomas, culminating in the greatest Christological confession in the gospel ("My Lord and my God") and the beatitude on believing without seeing (v. 29). The sermon's main point is "don't isolate in grief — run to God and the assembly." This is derived from the passage, but it is a secondary theme elevated above the primary one. Score cap is not invoked at the full level, but this represents meaningful drift that is noted.

**Flag 4 — Partial: Application is largely generic.** The application ("when things fall apart, do you run to Christ?") is not specific to the congregation's actual situation. No differentiated address: the doubter, the backslider, the new believer, the unbeliever, and the long-time saint all receive essentially the same word. The application is real, but thin and undifferentiated.

**Flag 5 — Triggered: No actual exhortation.** The sermon closes with "Pray. Hold on." and an invitation to speak with the preacher. These are gestures, not exhortations. The sermon never summons the congregation to a specific, named act of obedience or faith. There is no "therefore" moment where doctrine becomes demand.

**Score cap: invoked at 69** due to Flag 5 (no actual exhortation) being clearly present.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17/25**
- *Textual Fidelity*: 7/10. The sermon handles what it chooses well — vv. 24–28 are treated with care. However, v. 29 is entirely absent. This is not a minor omission; v. 29 is the passage's capstone beatitude, the hinge between the Thomas narrative and every subsequent believer. Skipping it leaves the exposition materially incomplete.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 4/5. The use of John 11:16 to establish Thomas's character is excellent — it reframes the man before the passage is read, which is good hermeneutical instinct. The connection to the resurrection narrative is present.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 3/5. No Greek engagement. The text's structure is not explained (why did Thomas make that specific conditional statement? what does it signal theologically about the disciples' situation?). The meaning is stated but not unpacked.
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 3/5. The isolation point (Thomas forsook the assembly) is drawn from "the other disciples were gathered" — an inferred point that may be more homiletical than strictly exegetical. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is identified as grief-driven isolation, which is plausible, but the passage's own FCF may better be named as: the tendency to demand proof before trusting the testimony of the resurrection — a condition addressed by Christ's gracious self-offering and then by the beatitude that pronounces blessing on those who believe the testimony alone.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness*: 5/7. Christ is genuinely central and not decorative. His "peace" greeting is handled well. His pursuit of Thomas as the Good Shepherd is organic. However, the theological weight of "My Lord and my God" — a direct identification of Jesus with Yahweh — is not unpacked. This is one of the most significant Christological statements in the NT, and it passes in a sentence.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness*: 2/3. The resurrection context is present, but the sermon does not locate this passage within the redemptive arc (post-resurrection appearances confirming the finished work; the disciples being sent as the Father sent the Son, v. 21). The commission of vv. 21–23 is not treated.
- *Doctrinal Precision*: 3/4. The gospel citation (Romans 10:9) is accurate. No theological errors are present. But the precision is thin — justification, atonement, the nature of saving faith are not defined.
- *Confessional Alignment*: 3/4. No contradictions of confessional theology. The sermon is broadly orthodox.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel*: 3/5. Romans 10:9 is cited near the end, which is the clearest gospel statement in the sermon. But it arrives as a reference, not as a proclamation. The nature of what Christ accomplished, why his resurrection matters for the condemned sinner, and what "believing in your heart" entails are not explained. A brand-new unbeliever would not have enough to understand the mechanism of salvation.
- *Necessity of Christ*: 3/5. Christ is necessary to the sermon's argument — but primarily as an example of gracious welcome and a source of peace. His role as atoning Savior, the one whose wounds are wounds borne for sin, is not developed. Thomas placing his hand in the wound is mentioned but the theological weight of those wounds is not pressed.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity*: 3/5. The cross is background, not foreground. The resurrection is appropriately central (the passage demands it), but the resurrection is preached primarily as emotional comfort rather than as the vindication of Christ's atoning work. The indicative weight of "he is risen" is not fully deployed before the imperatives arrive.

**Synagogue Test**: A devout Jew or Muslim could receive most of this sermon without offense. The peace Christ offers, the shepherd imagery, the call to not isolate — all of these survive without the scandal of the cross. Only the Romans 10:9 citation carries teeth, and it arrives at the end without development. This is a soft flag — not a full failure, but a warning.

**Law/Gospel Dialectic**: Law is present in the form of "don't isolate, come to church." Gospel is present in "Christ meets you with peace." But the law is not sharp enough (it does not expose the sinfulness of unbelief or the danger of the soul apart from Christ), and the gospel is not specific enough (it does not name what Christ's death accomplished and for whom). The hammer taps; the balm soothes; but neither lands with full weight.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15**
- *Heart Penetration*: 3/5. The question "when things fall apart, do you run to Christ or manage it alone?" is a genuine applicatory move. It lands. The acknowledgment that "some people tell me they're too ugly inside to come to God" is pastorally specific and shows real pastoral observation.
- *Specificity*: 2/5. The application is real but generic. No moment where a specific type of sinner is cornered. No address to the person who has intellectually rejected the resurrection, or the person who has been disappointed by Christ's apparent absence, or the proud person who doesn't feel needy enough to reach for a hand.
- *Spiritual Differentiation*: 3/5. The sermon acknowledges believers and unbelievers ("if you don't know him"). It addresses the doubter loosely. But it does not distinguish the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled, the backslider, or the person who is in church every week and has become spiritually numb. Perkins' seven categories of hearers are largely collapsed into one.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8**
- *Call to Response*: 1/4. "Come find me after the service" and "Pray. Hold on." are invitations and encouragements, not exhortations. There is no clear moment where the preacher looks the congregation in the eye and names what they must now do. The sermon closes warmly but without imperative force. No "therefore" moment.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness*: 2/4. The implicit instruction (come to church, don't isolate) has some equipping value. The Uzbekistan story has great witness-value as illustration. But the sermon does not send the church out equipped; it gathers them in and comforts them in place.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**
- *Clarity of Outline*: 3/4. The sermon moves: introduction (dock/hand) → Thomas's character → two martyr illustrations → the text → the isolation problem → Christ's gracious return → application/gospel. The line is followable.
- *Progression*: 2/3. The sermon builds well to verse 26–28 but then dissipates rather than culminates. The two illustrations at the front (Valeen Schnurr, Umida) are powerful but their connection to Thomas becomes somewhat tenuous — they illustrate bold faith under threat, while the sermon ultimately pivots to doubt and isolation. The illustrations set up a sermon about courageous faith that was never quite preached.
- *Unity*: 1/2. Two ideas compete: (1) Thomas was a courageous man of faith who crumbled in grief, and (2) don't isolate when things fall apart. These are related but not the same, and the sermon never resolves the tension by tying them to the passage's dominant idea.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**
- *Relevance*: 2/2. The dock illustration is native to the preacher's life and feels real. The sheep illustration is native to the congregation's context. Both work.
- *Clarity*: 1/2. The martyr stories (Valeen Schnurr and Umida) are stunning but they function ambiguously. They set up a question about bold faith under fire ("would you follow Christ?") that the sermon then partially answers by pivoting to Thomas's doubt. The contrast is more implicit than explicit, and the Umida story in particular is so powerful that it risks eclipsing the text.
- *Memorability*: 1/1. The hand mid-jump, the sheep in the canal, the soldier weeping — these will be remembered.

**8. Sermonic Force — 4/5**
- *Reads as Preaching*: 2/2. Oral cadence is strong throughout. The language is spoken, not written. Direct address ("Friend — that is a lie") appears where needed.
- *Conviction on the Page*: 1/2. The manuscript carries genuine pastoral conviction, but the sermonic climax ("My Lord and my God") passes in a phrase when it deserved a sustained stop. The force is diffused at the top of the passage's arc.
- *Cadence and Breath*: 1/1. Sentence variation is good. The short sentences at pressure points work.

---

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Mostly of Christ, though the closing application redirects attention to the self (how am I responding to hardship?). The balance is reasonable but tilts toward anthropology in the final third.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas, primarily — though the illustrations carry emotional weight that approaches encounter. The sermon tells you about a gracious Christ without fully placing you in his presence.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Mostly comforted, with some mild conviction at the isolation point. The sermon does not press hard enough to produce deep conviction, but it does offer genuine comfort.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Rehabilitation of Thomas before the passage is read.** The use of John 11:16 to establish Thomas as a man of fierce loyalty is a masterstroke of pastoral framing. It resists the lazy "Doubting Thomas" caricature and correctly reads the text's character with nuance. This earns the congregation's attention and sets up the pathos of verse 25 with real power.

2. **Contextually native illustrations.** The dock, the canal, the sheep, the father going after the lamb in the kayak — these images are drawn from the preacher's own life and the congregation's world. They do not smell borrowed. The Umida illustration is genuinely remarkable and will not be forgotten.

3. **Pastoral voice on the shame of coming to God.** The moment where the preacher names the feeling — "some people tell me they're too ugly inside to come into the presence of God" — is the sharpest piece of direct pastoral address in the sermon. It names a real spiritual deception with precision and gentleness.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Verse 29 is completely absent.** "Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed" is the hinge of the entire pericope and the word that transforms this from a story about Thomas into a word for every believer in every generation. The sermon reaches the climax (Thomas's confession) and then closes without ever preaching the benediction Christ pronounces over the congregation sitting in the room. This is the primary exegetical failure of the sermon.

2. **The exhortation never arrives.** The sermon builds emotional energy through two powerful martyr stories, raises the question ("if it came down to it — gun to your head — would you follow Christ?"), and then never answers it in terms of what the congregation must actually do. The closing line is pastoral ("Pray. Hold on.") but not exhortatory. The congregation is comforted but not summoned.

3. **The wounds of Christ are theologically unworked.** Thomas's request to place his fingers in the nail holes and his hand in the side is the most theologically loaded gesture in the passage. Those wounds are the wounds of substitutionary atonement. The sermon notes them in passing ("whatever you need, Thomas") but does not press their meaning. Why are the holes there? What do they say about what Christ bore? Why is Thomas's faith triggered not by the face of Jesus but by the wounds? This is the gospel's entry point in this passage, and it is walked past.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the two opening illustrations (Valeen Schnurr, Umida) are mismatched to the sermon's actual thesis. They illustrate bold confessional faith under lethal pressure — which is Thomas's *pre-crisis* character, not his *post-crisis* lesson. The sermon uses them to set up a question about courageous faith and then pivots to a sermon about not isolating in grief. The congregation may feel a subtle disconnect they cannot name: *We just heard about people who would die for Christ — now why are we talking about coming to church?* The illustrations deserve either a different sermon (one that directly addresses bold witness) or a different bridge into Thomas's failure that makes the contrast explicit.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The illustration weight creates the feeling of a powerful sermon. The Uzbekistan story is so emotionally and spiritually arresting that it fills the room with weight — and that weight is then carried forward as if it belongs to the theological argument. But the argument does not earn it. The sermon feels stronger in the listening (or reading) than it is in the structure. The emotional debt of the Umida story is never paid in theological coin.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Verse 29 is the sermon's unpreached gift.** "Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed" is not a consolation prize for people who missed out on seeing Jesus. It is a direct beatitude addressed to every person in the congregation — to every generation of believers who have trusted the testimony of the apostles without standing in that room. Thomas needed the wounds. You have the Word. And Christ pronounces you *blessed* for believing it. This is the passage's greatest pastoral word, and it was left unpreached. The congregation came to hear John 20 and went home without the benediction.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach verse 29.** Add a full movement at the end: "Thomas needed to touch the wounds. You and I will never touch them. And Jesus says — *blessed are you for it.* You believed without seeing. That is not a lesser faith. That is the faith Christ pronounced blessed." This closes the loop on the dock illustration too — Lilly had to hold the hand mid-jump; we believe the testimony and jump anyway.

2. **Name what the wounds mean.** Before "whatever you need, Thomas," add two to three sentences: "Those holes — do you know what they cost? Every wound was a sin. Yours. Mine. Thomas had pledged to die with Christ, and instead Christ died carrying his betrayal, his doubt, his grief. That's what Thomas was putting his finger into."

3. **Add a "therefore" exhortation before the close.** After "Peace" and the gospel offer, add: "So here is what I am calling you to tonight. You who have been isolating — come back to the assembly. You who have not believed — put your name on the testimony of these witnesses and confess what Thomas confessed. You who believe but are wavering — hear Christ's first word to you: peace. Now go live like it."

4. **Tighten the illustration-to-text transition.** Between the Umida story and the text, add an explicit contrast: "Thomas had that same courage in him — 'Let us also go, that we may die with him.' And then he didn't. Grief and absence did what a stone-throwing mob could not. He crumbled. And Jesus came looking for him anyway." This makes the arc explicit.

5. **Address the unbeliever with more specificity near the gospel offer.** "If you don't know him" is the correct move, but Romans 10:9 without explanation is a key without a lock. Add: "To believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead is to trust that his resurrection vindicates his death — that he actually took your guilt, actually satisfied the justice you owe, actually made peace between you and God. That is what you are trusting."

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon is neither bloodless nor formally correct. It carries genuine pastoral warmth and real affection for the congregation. The fire is present, if diffused. The issue is not dead orthodoxy but structural incompleteness — the sermon is alive but does not yet arrive.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries genuine pastoral unction — particularly in the Uzbekistan narrative and the sheep-and-shepherd section — but that weight dissipates before the close rather than culminating in it; the congregation is left warmed rather than undone.

**Bonus: +2/5**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This is a gospel narrative passage (John 20) with strong apologetic and Christological content. The rubric applies without significant strain. The narrative genre rewards illustration and imagination (scores well here), and the rubric's weight on exposition appropriately flags the missing verse 29. The Conscience and Exhortation categories are the correct pressure points for this sermon's weaknesses. No genre-based rubric adjustment is needed.
