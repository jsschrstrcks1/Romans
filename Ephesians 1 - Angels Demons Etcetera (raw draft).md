# Angels, Demons, Etcetera
## Ephesians 1:3–14

**Primary Text**: Ephesians 1:3–14
**Supporting Texts**: 1 John 4:4; Colossians 1:13–14
**People of the Week**: Bahdai of India — carpenters, trace lineage to Vishwakarma (Hindu architect god), largely Muslim today. ~600,000, no known believers. *(⚠️ verify current Joshua Project / IMB data)*
**Themes**: Angels, demons, dualism heresy, pseudepigrapha, spiritual authority, riches of God
**Quoted**: Chuck Hastings (⚠️ unverified source) on cultural angel obsession
**Context**: Preacher's week of cascading breakdowns — 4 of 5 vehicles down, pest issues, banking problems, sick kids and adults, broken lawn equipment, dad's back, sheep escaping fence

---

I want to tell you a little bit about my week.

Four of our five vehicles went down. Not one. Not two. Four. The lawn mower quit. We had a bug situation. The bank did something that cost us money I didn't expect to lose. Two of the kids were sick. My dad threw his back out. And the sheep got out of the fence — again.

I don't know what I did to deserve that particular combination of events, but there it was.

Now, I'll tell you what some people said when I started talking about this. "The devil's attacking you, pastor." "Satan doesn't want you preaching on Sunday." "Spiritual warfare, brother."

Maybe.

Or maybe the mower was fourteen years old and had a lot of hard hours on it. Maybe I let the fence go too long without checking it. Maybe the kids caught what was going around school.

That question — what actually caused my terrible week — is exactly what we're going to talk about today. Because a lot of people have strong opinions about angels and demons and spiritual forces, and a lot of those opinions don't come from where they think they come from.

Open your Bibles with me to Ephesians 1.

---

**People of the Week**

The Bahdai of India. They are a carpenter caste — craftsmen by trade and by lineage. They trace their ancestry back to Vishwakarma, the divine architect and craftsman of the Hindu pantheon, the god said to have built the palaces of the gods. Over centuries, many of the Bahdai converted to Islam. Today they number around 600,000. As far as anyone can determine, there are no known believers in Jesus Christ among them. *(⚠️ Verify current numbers against Joshua Project or IMB data before reuse.)*

Six hundred thousand souls. Carpenters. The Son of God spent thirty years working with his hands in a carpenter's shop. I don't think that's an accident. Let's pray for them — that God would open a door, that someone would go, and that the Bahdai would meet the one true craftsman who made them.

---

Now — let's talk about angels.

Someone once wrote — I believe it was Chuck Hastings, though I'd want to verify that before putting it in print — that angels appear on coffee mugs, t-shirts, checkbooks, license plates, soap dispensers, and something called a Bubble Angel Barbie. *(⚠️ Hastings quote unverified — confirm source before citing in print.)* And he's right. Our culture is fascinated with angels. Not the God of the angels. The angels themselves.

I saw a clip once of a TV preacher standing behind a podium with a mountain of credit card statements stacked up in front of him. He laid his hands on that stack of paper, started muttering words that didn't mean anything in any language I've ever heard, and declared that he was binding the "demon of debt."

I'll give him credit for one thing. At least he acknowledged the debt existed.

But here's the thing — where did he get the idea that you could bind a demon with made-up words? That's not in your Bible. I promise you, that's not in your Bible. So where does it come from?

---

You need to know there are three categories of ancient books.

The first is the Canon. Sixty-six books. Tested by the church over centuries, verified by internal consistency, historical accuracy, predictive prophecy, manuscript evidence, and the testimony of the Holy Spirit to the hearts of believers. These are your Scriptures.

The second category is the Apocrypha. Our Catholic brothers and sisters include these books in their Bible — texts like Tobit, Judith, First and Second Maccabees, Sirach. Protestants and Catholics have argued this one since the Reformation, and I'm not going to settle that today. The church has had ongoing debates about these, which is itself significant.

The third category is the Pseudepigrapha. These are books that claim to be Scripture but are verifiably not. "Pseudepigrapha" means "falsely attributed writings" — books that claim to be written by Paul, or Enoch, or Solomon, but aren't.

And here's how you know. Language dates things.

If I hand you a document today that claims to be a letter from George Washington, and it uses the word "groovy," you know it's fake. Washington didn't talk like that. That word didn't exist yet. Same thing with "bad" meaning good — that's an 80s thing. "Booyah," "psych," "fly" — that's 90s. "Bae," "lit," "snowflake" — 2010s. Language leaves fingerprints. You can date it.

So when someone hands you a document that claims the Apostle Paul wrote it, but the language, the style, the vocabulary, and the theological concerns all point to a period several centuries later — it's fake. We can tell. Scholars can tell. The early church fathers could tell, which is why they rejected these books.

The problem is people read them anyway. And then they build their theology out of them.

---

Let me give you two examples.

The Book of Tobit is in the Apocrypha — Catholics include it; Protestants don't. In it, there's a woman named Sarah. She's been married seven times. Every single husband died on the wedding night, killed by a demon named Asmodeus before the marriage could be consummated. A man named Tobias wants to marry her, but understandably he's a little nervous about the track record.

An angel named Raphael tells him what to do. He says burn the liver and heart of a fish, and the smoke will drive the demon away. Tobias does it. It works. He marries Sarah. No dead husband this time.

Now — it's a story. It may have historical elements. But it is not Scripture. And yet that idea — that you can drive away demonic influence through specific ritual acts using specific substances — that's the root of a whole tradition of demon-binding and spiritual formulas. People just don't know where it came from.

The Testament of Solomon is a pseudepigraphal text — no one in the church has ever accepted it as Scripture. In it, Solomon is given a magic ring by the archangel Michael, and he uses it to summon demons one by one and interrogate them. Each demon has a name and a weakness — a specific word or substance or angel that defeats it. One demon, Orniskelos — and I'm going from memory here on the spelling, so verify that against a real edition of the text — is said to cause sore throats, and flees when Raphael is invoked. Another, something like Hellorath, causes intestinal problems, and flees if you mutter what sounds like "aiarhea." *(⚠️ Demon names from Testament of Solomon — spellings vary across translations; verify exact names and attributes against a standard critical edition.)*

Solomon's ring eventually breaks when he gets involved with female demons. Which, if you know how Solomon's story actually ends in 1 Kings, is a surprisingly accurate footnote to his real downfall — just dressed up in pseudepigraphal nonsense.

But here's what happened. People read that. They built theology out of it. They convinced themselves that if you could just learn the right words, the right names, the right formulas — you could control the spiritual world. That you could bind demons with syllables. That the key to spiritual authority was knowing the secret names.

That TV preacher muttering over a stack of credit card statements? That's the Testament of Solomon, not the New Testament. He just doesn't know it.

---

So what does your Bible actually say?

Let's read Ephesians 1:3–14.

> Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who has blessed us in Christ with every spiritual blessing in the heavenly places, even as he chose us in him before the foundation of the world, that we should be holy and blameless before him. In love he predestined us for adoption to himself as sons through Jesus Christ, according to the purpose of his will, to the praise of his glorious grace, with which he has blessed us in the Beloved. In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of his grace, which he lavished upon us, in all wisdom and insight making known to us the mystery of his will, according to his purpose, which he set forth in Christ as a plan for the fullness of time, to unite all things in him, things in heaven and things on earth.
>
> In him we have obtained an inheritance, having been predestined according to the purpose of him who works all things according to the counsel of his will, so that we who were the first to hope in Christ might be to the praise of his glory. In him you also, when you heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation, and believed in him, were sealed with the promised Holy Spirit, who is the guarantee of our inheritance until we acquire possession of it, to the praise of his glory.
> — Ephesians 1:3–14 (ESV) ✓

Did you catch that? Verse 13. Sealed with the promised Holy Spirit.

Sealed.

That is a legal, permanent, ownership word. When you believed the gospel — when you heard the word of truth and trusted Christ — God sealed you. Marked you. The Holy Spirit took up residence in you, and that sealing is the guarantee of everything that's coming.

Now. With that in mind, listen to what John says:

> He who is in you is greater than he who is in the world.
> — 1 John 4:4 (ESV) ✓

Greater. Not roughly equal. Not fighting for position. Greater.

And listen to Colossians 1:13–14:

> He has delivered us from the domain of darkness and transferred us to the kingdom of his beloved Son, in whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins.
> — Colossians 1:13–14 (ESV) ✓

Delivered. Transferred. Past tense. Done.

The battle is over for the believer. You were rescued from the domain of darkness. You were moved. You are now under a different king, in a different kingdom, sealed by a different Spirit. A demon cannot possess someone the Holy Spirit already occupies and owns. That's the consistent teaching of Scripture. That's settled.

Now — angels are real. Demons are real. The spiritual world is real and active. I'm not telling you otherwise. But the hero of the spiritual world is God. Not the angels. Not the demons. Not you, if you can memorize the right binding words.

I'll use an analogy here. During World War II, something strange happened on a number of Pacific islands. American military forces flew in supplies by cargo plane — food, medicine, equipment — to remote islands that had never seen anything like it. After the war, American forces left. And some of those island peoples, trying to get the cargo to come back, built replica airstrips out of straw and bamboo. They carved wooden headphones. They stood on mock airstrips waving homemade signal paddles. They were worshipping the plane instead of the people who sent it.

Anthropologists call these cargo cults.

Now — God does use angels. That's real. That's in the Bible. Angels are real messengers, real warriors, real servants of God. But when they become the focus — when people start trying to invoke angels, catalog angels, bind demons by name — they've built a bamboo airstrip. They're worshipping the plane. They've stopped asking about the one who sent it.

Don't worship the boat. Worship the God who steers it.

---

Now I want to talk about the other end of the error, because this one is closer to home.

There's a theological problem called dualism — the idea that the world is basically a cosmic standoff between good and evil, light and dark, angels and demons, more or less equal forces fighting it out. A galactic struggle. It sounds biblical on the surface — there is spiritual warfare, after all. But dualism fails because it leaves out one thing. A sovereign God who is not in a struggle. A God who has already won. A God who is not anxiously watching the scoreboard.

That's not the God of Scripture. That's a smaller God.

But here's what dualism does that I really want you to think about. It removes personal responsibility. It gives you someone else to blame.

This is not a new problem.

Go back to the Garden. God confronts Adam: "Did you eat from the tree I told you not to eat from?" And Adam says — and I want you to hear this — he says, "The woman you gave me, she gave me the fruit, and I ate." He blamed Eve. He actually blamed God for giving him Eve.

Eve's turn. "The serpent deceived me, and I ate."

At least she didn't blame God. But the pattern is already set. Someone else is responsible. Something outside of me made me do it.

We've been doing this ever since.

Some of you have been calling your choices spiritual warfare for so long the two feel the same to you. Some of you are here today still holding someone else responsible for a decision you made years ago. That's not a demon. That's Adam in the Garden.

And I hear it dressed up in spiritual language all the time. "The devil's been attacking my marriage." "There's a spirit of addiction on my family." "I've got a demon of lust, a demon of anger, a demon of laziness." Now look — spiritual warfare is real. I'm not dismissing that. But most of the time, when people invoke spiritual warfare language, what they're actually doing is the same thing Adam did in the Garden. They're distributing the blame somewhere besides themselves.

Let me tell you about my week again.

The devil did not break my lawn mower. That machine was fourteen years old and I haven't maintained it like I should have. The devil did not break down four vehicles. Some of those have deferred maintenance problems I've known about and not addressed. The devil did not give me a bug problem. I let some things go around the house. The devil did not cause my banking issue — that was a combination of a vendor error and my own inattention.

The devil didn't make you sign up for that credit card with the interest rate you didn't read. The devil didn't make you eat the thing, drink the thing, look at the thing, say the thing. The devil didn't make you walk away from your marriage, your church, your integrity.

You chose that.

You are responsible for that.

And I know that's a harder word than "spiritual warfare." But it's also the word that leads somewhere. Because if the devil did it, you're stuck. You're waiting for someone to mutter the right formula over your life. But if you did it — if you are a responsible moral agent who makes choices — then repentance is available. Change is possible. Grace is waiting. The same God who sealed you with his Spirit is the God who, when his children confess their sin, is faithful and just to forgive it and cleanse them from all unrighteousness.

That's the good news. You're not a victim of forces beyond your control. You're a sinner with access to the most powerful grace in the universe.

---

Go back to verse 7. The riches of his grace. Verse 11 — we have obtained an inheritance. Verse 14 — the guarantee of our inheritance.

I read an article a while back about lottery winners. Not what you'd expect. The thesis was that winning the lottery was, for most of the winners they profiled, one of the worst things that happened to them. They spent it. Shysters found them. Relatives they'd never heard of appeared. They took on debt to match the new lifestyle. A surprising number of them ended up more broke and more alone than before they won.

Now think about what it means that God is rich.

Every dollar in circulation, every euro, every pound, every yen — it belongs to him. The cattle on a thousand hills. The silver and gold. The house you live in is God's house that he lets you live in. That's not a metaphor. He owns it. He lets you manage it. Every resource you think you have is a stewardship from a God of infinite supply.

But that's just the material side, and that's the small part.

The riches Paul is talking about go deeper. Perfect goodness. Perfect grace. Patience that never runs out. Wisdom that never reaches its limit. Mercy that is new every morning. These are treasures. And they are costly — every one of them.

You only want to ask God for patience if you're ready to encounter situations that require it. That's how he grows it. That's the cost of the treasure.

But look at what Paul says he does with these riches. Verse 8 — he *lavished* them on us. That's not a careful word. That's not a measured, calibrated, just-enough word. Lavished. Poured out. More than needed. More than asked for.

And then the phrase that stops me: *in all wisdom and insight.*

He is not extravagant without discernment. He doesn't just pour riches out blindly. Every blessing, every trial, every gift, every season of want and every season of abundance — all of it comes from a God who is lavishing in accordance with his wisdom. He knows what you need. He knows what will make you holy rather than just comfortable. He knows the treasure that is worth the cost.

That's the God we belong to. Sealed to him. Transferred into his kingdom. Not fighting for position against some equal and opposite darkness.

His.

Let's pray.
