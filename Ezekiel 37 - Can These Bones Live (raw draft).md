## Doctrine: Total Depravity

While you're turning there I want to tell you the theological term for the doctrine we're going to be studying today.

You remember that quote often attributed to Jonathan Edwards? The only thing we contribute to salvation is — what?

The sin that made it necessary.

Well, the theological term for that is called *total depravity*.

A lot of people hear that phrase and misunderstand it. I think they could have called it something better. When we hear *total depravity* we automatically think it means everyone is as bad as Hitler. Anyone with eyes to see knows that isn't true. We can look at the world and see lost people trying to do things for the homeless, or orphans, or widows. Those same people aren't operating gas chambers. They aren't like Hitler, are they.

So what does total depravity mean? In short, it means you're going to be as bad as you're going to be. Maybe for this person it's becoming an alcoholic; for that person, they become a murderer; and for another person, they struggle with the sin of arrogance. Everyone is different. Everyone is going to reach a different depth of evil. But to the extent that you are able to — because of your genetics, your upbringing, and due to God's mercy keeping you out of trouble — you're going to be evil in your natural state.

R.C. Sproul likes to call it *radical corruption*, because compared to how we were created to be, because of Adam's sin, we are radically corrupt. This corruption, according to Scripture, is completely pervasive throughout us apart from Christ.

## The Corpse and the Slave

Ephesians 2 talks about this. Anyone remember what Ephesians 2 says about us apart from Christ?

Dead.

It says we are corpses. Literally dead in our trespasses and sins. By nature, children of wrath.

> *"And you were dead in the trespasses and sins..."* (Ephesians 2:1)
> *"...and were by nature children of wrath, like the rest of mankind."* (Ephesians 2:3)

Jesus himself said in John 8:34, *"everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin."* Paul echoes it in Romans 6 — the whole chapter is one long argument that apart from Christ, sin is your master.

Think about that a minute. What can a corpse do on its own? Anything? Certainly not.

What about a slave? If you're a slave, and your slave master is always standing there watching you — is there anything you can do that your slave master doesn't permit? If your slave master is sin and the devil, what kinds of things are you going to do? Everything possible that the Lord's mercy doesn't keep you out of.

What kinds of decisions are we able to make apart from the Lord? If we're slaves to sin — sons of disobedience, children of wrath, and walking corpses — what kind of decisions are we going to be able to make?

> *"...the spirit that is now at work in the sons of disobedience"* (Ephesians 2:2)

Say it again. What kind of decision can an enslaved corpse make?

That's right. None.

## The Dalmatian Problem

The Catholics viewed sin as a virus. Viruses infect every cell of your being.

That reminds me of a man named David Ishee who was trying to use a virus to change dogs on a fundamental level. There's a gene in Dalmatians that causes them to develop kidney stones in the wrong places, which ultimately causes the dogs to die young. But Ishee wanted to use a virus to deliver new genetic code that changes all that — to help the dog properly process the elements that form kidney stones, so they won't form in places like the urinary tract.

> ⚠️ **FLAG — David Ishee / Dalmatian story**: This is a known biohacker working on Dalmatian genetic modification. The general details are accurate. The specific mechanisms should be verified before use in print.

In order for that to work, the virus has to infect every cell of the dog embryo, then cut the DNA and insert the new DNA.

As humans, we have a sin nature. It's pervasive and affects every single fiber of our being. It causes radical corruption in us that dooms us to hell apart from Christ. It is so pervasive it might as well be part of our genetic code — and it's present from birth. But Christ did for us what Ishee is trying to do for Dalmatians.

Dalmatians have no control over their genetic code. They can't fix it or make it better. They just have to deal with the consequences of things that happened before they were ever born. Like us. Sin nature has been imputed to us from Adam. Adam and Eve sinned, and we have had to deal with the consequences. We know from Ephesians 2 there is nothing we can do about that — just like Dalmatians can do nothing about their own problem.

So along comes David Ishee, who has spent years of his life and tons of his treasure trying to find a cure — designing this virus that would totally cure every single fiber of the dog. Just like Christ, who gave everything He had to execute our cure.

## The Glasses Problem

Like the Dalmatians, we cannot fix ourselves. In part, because apart from Christ we can't even begin to understand the things of the Lord. 1 Corinthians 2:14-16 says:

> *"The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned. The spiritual person judges all things, but is himself to be judged by no one. 'For who has understood the mind of the Lord so as to instruct him?' But we have the mind of Christ."*

So who is the natural person? That's us — apart from Christ. Without the work of God in our heart and mind, the best we can come up with is believing that Jesus is folly. That God is folly. That God's Word is folly. Because we can't understand them.

It's like going through life with dirty glasses. You ever know someone like that? I was that person as a kid. The glasses would slowly get dirty during the day, and by that evening they were disgusting and tainted my view of everything. They would almost be grey from the amount of junk they'd pick up. Nothing was crisp or clear — everything was muddled and confused. And I didn't even realize how dirty my glasses had become. I had to be told. Frequently they were so dirty I had to have help cleaning them up.

That's our view of the world apart from Christ. Completely tainted by sin. Even if we — as corpses — could understand, we would reject Christ as foolishness. Which is why God had to begin working in us first.

*We love God because He first loved us.* He loved us enough to begin working in us, so that when the time came for us to hear the Gospel, we would be able to respond to Him the way we ought. He loved us enough to send His Son. He loved us enough to make a way for us to be with Him.

Apart from God doing this work in our hearts, every single human being would end up in hell. Because we chose to reject Him. Because we see Him as folly apart from the work of Christ. Because we are dead in our trespasses and sins.

With that context, let's go ahead and read Ezekiel 37.

## First Observation: The Dry Bones

The first thing I want you to notice is the dry bones. What are they symbolic of?

They are symbolic of the nation of Israel. And they are symbolic of the lost today.

When it comes to Ezekiel, again and again in this book, God lifts Ezekiel up off the ground. Ezekiel falls on his face before the glory of the LORD, and the Spirit sets him back on his feet — because there is work to be done, a word to be delivered, a people who need to hear.

A lot of people feel like Ezekiel was the key prophet to the exiles — preaching hope and judgment to a people sitting in Babylon, who felt completely cut off from God and from their land.

If you look at the Hebrew, you'll see a word with a kind of interesting double meaning. It's used in two ways: one is for a literal valley, and the other is for a place on a battlefield where soldiers have forced a breach. Many translators felt like this was a double entendre — intending to mean both a valley and a place where soldiers have forced a breach. A valley battlefield, if you will. Which would explain the bones.

> ⚠️ **FLAG — Hebrew valley/breach double entendre**: Cannot verify this interpretation. The Hebrew word (בִּקְעָה, *biq'ah*) primarily means valley or plain. The "place of a forced breach" interpretation is unusual and needs to be confirmed against a Hebrew lexicon or commentary before it is preached as settled.

Israel had been fighting a war against idols for a long time by the time we get to this passage. Remember the fight against the Baals? The Asheroth? And the other idols God's people worshiped? In fact the idolatry was so bad that God calls His people whores — whoring after false gods. But in the end they chose to follow the false gods, and that left them spiritually dead. They are corpses. Merely bones exposed to the elements after having been dealt a devastating spiritual defeat as a nation. They chose defeat by choosing to worship idols.

And if we could survey the world around us today — if we could see with God's eyes — I think we would see an ocean of dead bones. Many of us would have neighbors who were dead bones. Many of us would have family who were dead bones. Those of us who are believers have been these dead bones.

But God loves us too much to leave us that way. Amen?

## What the Text Shows

The bones start coming together. Sinews. Skin. But these corpses are still corpses. Better-looking corpses — but corpses nonetheless.

It's not until God commands Ezekiel to prophesy about the breath returning to their lungs that the corpses come alive.

Ultimately, at the moment of salvation, our dead rotting corpse has new life breathed into it. We're infected with the virus of salvation. Every cell is made new. That virus of sin is killed off. The damage it did begins to be repaired.

You can't become a Christian and remain unchanged.

## The Encounter That Changes Everything

There are two outcomes when you come face to face with God.

Look at Moses — what happened to him when he encountered even the shadow of God's glory? He glowed. He reflected the glory of God. He was changed on a fundamental level.

Truly encountering God will have the same effect on you.

My laundry had an accident this past week. Someone blesses me by washing it if I bring it to her. It's a wonderful thing during school — a major blessing, and I appreciate it. Anyway, I took her my laundry as per usual, and we sorted it: jeans here, whites there, reds in another pile. Started with some whites. Added a little bleach — and the bottle of bleach fell and landed on my blue jeans. She caught it quick. Only a pair or two were damaged. But blue jeans cannot encounter bleach and remain unchanged. It had a profound effect on them. Instead of being warm and blue, they became blotchy white.

Jeans cannot encounter bleach and remain unchanged. And a human cannot have a salvation encounter with the true and living God and remain unchanged.

We become different on a fundamental level. We go from dead to alive. We go from dry bones to bodies covered in flesh and breathing. We're given new life through Christ. The old becomes new. The dead becomes alive.

You remember the passage about old wineskins and new wine? Scripture says you wouldn't fill an old wineskin with new wine. Old wineskins are already stretched out, and new wine still contains CO₂ that would cause the skin to stretch some more. A new wineskin can handle that — but the old wineskin would burst and spill the wine all over the ground.

When we are saved, all becomes new. We become the new wineskins. We are filled with the new wine of the Holy Spirit.

## The Block Party

When I was in St. Louis, we had a series of evangelistic block parties. We had balloons, and cotton candy, and popcorn, food cooked by Baptist relief, music, giveaways, and all sorts of things — all for the purpose of gathering the community to give a few of us the opportunity to share the gospel with them.

Anyway, we were preparing, and I was sent with another guy to get some more helium. While we were there, we invited the guys who sold us the helium. One of them asked if we had enough booze — offered to bring a keg of beer. The guy who was with me was quick-witted and told him there would be plenty of Spirit present — meaning the Holy Spirit.

The guys came. And one of them prayed to accept Christ before the day was out.

God sent His Son Jesus to pay our sin debt.
