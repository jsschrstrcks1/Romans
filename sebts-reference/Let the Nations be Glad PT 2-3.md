Let the Nations be Glad PT 2-3

Ken Baker 000210914

I think the part that resonated the most with this writer was the parts about suffering in missions. It might be easy to think one is overly excited by being a martyr – and that could (rightfully) cause red flags to go up. In truth no one relishes the thought of dying young, or of experiencing deep suffering. However most can identify with suffering of some sort and can really appreciate the ways in which God uses this tool in other people's lives to see the lost reached. In 2001 a professor at Trinity College of Florida bullied from the pulpit "count the cost" he was preaching on missions; and wanted his audience to come to grips with the fact they may die, they may be maimed, they may have to watch their wife, children, or teammates die, or be dismembered. But Praise God for his faithfulness. Praise God that when He asks us to make the ultimate sacrifice: 1. The request is reasonable – It is nothing he would and hasn't asked His son to go through, and 2. Frequently surrounding such a request many people are saved. As for this writer, should even one individual come to know the Lord through my life, death, or any other circumstance that the Lord ordains – then it was all worth it. Obviously as a single man with no kids perhaps this is easier to say. But if circumstances were different, I think I could come to a point where I could praise God for allowing me to identify with Him in that small way. It would definitely take time, but I think I would get there.

### 9. Strengths (3)
1. **Honest cost-counting**: The writer resists cheap missional enthusiasm. The acknowledgment that "no one relishes the thought of dying young" is more honest than most pep-talk missions rhetoric. This is the beginning of genuine cost-counting, not romanticized martyrdom.
2. **Theological grounding of suffering**: The instinct that God's request for sacrifice "is reasonable — it is nothing he would and hasn't asked His son to go through" is a Christologically-grounded theodicy of suffering. This is theologically sound and worth developing.
3. **Self-aware qualification**: The writer notes his status as a single man makes it "easier to say" — a sign of intellectual and pastoral self-awareness that is often absent from younger students engaging missions rhetoric.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (3)
1. **No Scripture cited**: The entire reflection floats theologically without a single biblical anchor. The claims made about God's purposes in suffering are true — but they are asserted from personal conviction and Piper's framework, not from the text of Scripture. A seminary student should show the biblical reasoning, not just receive it second-hand from Piper.
2. **Underdeveloped argument**: The paper is a single paragraph. The ideas gestured at — theodicy of martyrdom, identification with Christ's suffering, the fruit of missionary death — are each rich enough for a full paper. None are developed. This is a series of assertions, not an argument.
3. **Passive theological register**: Phrases like "I think I could come to a point" and "It would definitely take time, but I think I would get there" indicate tentative, ungrounded confidence. The theology of suffering in Philippians 1, Romans 8, or 2 Corinthians 4 would transform this from "I think I'd get there eventually" into a settled doctrinal conviction. The writer doesn't yet know *why* he'd get there.

### 11. Blind Spots
The writer assumes the emotional resonance with suffering ("most can identify with suffering of some sort") does the theological work that only Scripture can do. Identifying emotionally with suffering and being theologically equipped to endure missionary suffering and loss are different things. The paper does not yet distinguish between empathy and endurance, or between sentiment and settled conviction.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The anecdote about the Trinity College professor who preached cost-counting from the pulpit appears as strong supporting evidence. But the professor is unnamed, the sermon is unreferenced, and the theological substance is unreported. The anecdote lends emotional weight but provides no actual theological content. It feels like evidence — it functions as atmosphere.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Piper's own argument in *Let the Nations Be Glad* Part 2–3 is grounded in specific texts — Romans 15, Isaiah 52–53, Acts 13–14, and others. The writer could have engaged Piper's exegetical case for suffering as a missionary strategy and evaluated it biblically. Instead, the paper stays entirely at the level of personal emotional response. The exegetical and theological substance of Piper's argument goes completely unexamined.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (if this were to be developed)
1. **Anchor every claim in a text**: Choose one passage — Romans 8:17–18, 2 Corinthians 4:7–12, or Philippians 1:29 — and show how it grounds the theology of missionary suffering being described.
2. **Distinguish empathy from endurance**: Clarify that what makes missionary suffering bearable is not emotional solidarity with others who have suffered, but the promises and presence of God in the text.
3. **Develop the Christological argument**: The sentence "it is nothing he would and hasn't asked His son to go through" is the strongest theological move in the paper. Expand it. What did Christ's suffering accomplish? How does union with Christ transform the meaning of missionary death?
4. **Engage Piper's actual argument**: Summarize what Piper argues in the assigned sections and interact with it critically. What does Piper claim? What is his evidence? Do you agree? Where does his argument need qualification?
5. **Move from "I think I'd get there" to "Here is why the promises of God make that possible"**: Replace tentative emotional confidence with textually-grounded doctrinal assurance.
