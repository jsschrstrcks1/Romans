## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Warm Pastoral Heart, Undisciplined Architecture — A Sermon That Finds the Gospel at the End Instead of the Center

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A sincere and pastorally earnest opening of Colossians 3:1–17 that correctly targets mortification, idolatry of politics, and the new creation — but whose strengths are undermined by an inverted structure, an absent formal gospel proclamation, and application that is more exhortive sentiment than conscience-searching specificity.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with reservations.**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's thesis can be stated: Christians who have been raised with Christ and hidden in Him must put to death the sins of the old life and put on the virtues of the new. This does arise from the passage's own argument. Pass.

*Authority Test*: The preacher largely submits to the text's claims. He does not soften the warnings of wrath (v. 6). He applies the text against himself at points ("I fell short here this week"). The authority test is passed — though there are moments where the preacher's own concerns (particularly Christian nationalism and politics) extend beyond what the text explicitly argues. This is not disqualifying, but it is worth noting.

**Berean Gate: PASS.** Proceed to scoring.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**One flag is triggered:**

- **Flag 4 — Application is substantially generic**: While the political application is specific and bold, the broader application pattern defaults to motivational language ("keep putting one foot in front of the other," "try to do better tomorrow than you did today") without pressing the conscience with the specificity the text demands. The virtues in vv. 12–17 — compassion, meekness, patience, forgiveness — receive almost no concrete applicatory treatment beyond a single driving anecdote.
- **Flag 5 — Exhortation is weak at the structural level**: The sermon ends with the words "Gospel presentation." — indicating the gospel proclamation was not yet written into the manuscript. The sermon's call to response trails off rather than culminating in a clear summons. The exhortation is embedded in encouragement rather than delivered as a command.

**Score is not hard-capped** — neither flag is severe enough to cap at 69 — but both are present and must be named. The evaluation proceeds without capping, but both flags are carried.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 15/25**

The sermon engages Colossians 3 but does so in a fragmented and sometimes inverted manner. The preacher begins with a lengthy personal anecdote (seminary, sports, visualization) that is tangentially related to "setting your mind on things above" (v. 2) but does not arise from the text — it precedes the reading of the text. The sermon then works through the "do not" list (vv. 5–9) with genuine engagement, including a useful Greek etymology of *porneia*. However:

- Verses 1–4 (the indicative foundation — hidden with Christ, raised with Christ, appearing in glory) are not exposited until halfway through the sermon. The sermon inverts the text's own logic: Paul grounds the imperatives in the indicatives (vv. 1–4 before vv. 5–17), but the sermon works through the imperatives first and returns to the indicatives as a conclusion.
- Vv. 10–11 (the "new self" being renewed in knowledge, the erasure of ethnic/social distinctions) receive almost no treatment.
- Vv. 12–17 (the "put on" virtues) are read aloud but not meaningfully exposited — only illustrated briefly with a driving anecdote.
- The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is implicitly the problem of continued old-self living among those who have been raised with Christ — but it is not named clearly. The specific tension Paul is addressing is never articulated.
- Greek use is accessible and appropriate (porneia/pornography connection).

Score: **15/25** — Genuine textual engagement, but structural inversion, significant omissions, and underdeveloped exposition of the passage's theological core.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

The sermon's theology is mostly sound. The preacher correctly connects sin to the wrath of God (v. 6), correctly treats mortification as the work of the new man, and the Puritan quote ("be killing sin, or sin will be killing you") is apt. The "hidden with Christ" theology is touchingly expressed if not fully developed.

Weaknesses:
- The relationship between indicative and imperative — the engine of Pauline ethics — is sensed but never clearly articulated. The sermon says Christians change because of Christ, but it does not explain the theological mechanism: union with Christ, the new birth, the Spirit's work, the definitive break with sin at conversion.
- The election reference (v. 12 — "God chose you") is correct but underdeveloped and could leave hearers with a vague sense of being special rather than a robust understanding of election's motivating power.
- The note that "earning God's wrath" (on account of these things, v. 6) applies to believers is presented with no clarification about the nature of that wrath for the covenant community versus the unregenerate. This is not heresy, but it is imprecise enough to create confusion.
- Confessional alignment is acceptable — no 1689 contradictions found. Doctrines of grace are gestured at.

Score: **12/18** — Sound instincts, imprecise mechanics.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9/15**

The gospel is genuinely present in this sermon — more present than in many. The hymn ("How Deep the Father's Love For Us") is a genuine gospel moment, and the cross-reference is specific: "our porneia required him to carry the cross." This is not moralism — Christ is not merely an example, he is the price paid.

However:
- The gospel proclamation is structurally displaced to the end ("Gospel presentation." at the close of the manuscript — and that section is not written). The sermon's main architecture is law-heavy and closes with motivation rather than proclamation.
- The law has time to land (the wrath section is clear), but the gospel is not proportionately developed as its relief.
- The Synagogue Test is borderline. A devout moralist could follow most of this sermon and agree with the main lines of argument without being confronted with the necessity of Christ's atoning work. The cross appears clearly — but late, and primarily as motivation for obedience rather than as the only ground of acceptance.

Score: **9/15** — Gospel is present and sincere; displaced rather than absent; force is weakened by structural relegation to the conclusion.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9/15**

The political application is the sermon's most courageous moment. The indictment of Christian nationalism — "I fear the American church has lusted for power, and saw Trump as a convenient way to gain it" — is specific, concrete, and targets a real pattern in the likely congregation. This is real conscience-pressing. The statistics on gospel sharing versus political talking are well-deployed.

Weaknesses:
- The application of vv. 12–17 is almost entirely absent. Compassion, kindness, humility, meekness, patience, forgiveness — these receive one driving anecdote and a vague aspiration ("it's hard to live this out"). The hearer is not pressed on any of them.
- Perkins' categories: The sermon addresses believers primarily, touches on the backslider implicitly, and gestures at the unconverted at the end. The ignorant-but-teachable, the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, and the knowledgeable-but-unmoved are not distinctly addressed. The application stays in the believer-exhortation lane throughout.
- The differentiation is limited: political idolaters are addressed specifically; everyone else receives general encouragement.

Score: **9/15** — One bold specific application; the rest is broad encouragement.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

The closest thing to a clear exhortation is: "Keep your eyes on the prize. Keep Christ as your focus." This is encouragement, not exhortation — it lacks the imperative force of "Therefore, repent of this. Do this now. Bring this to the Lord's table. Go this week and tell someone about Christ."

The sermon ends with the promise of a gospel presentation that is not there. The "therefore" moment — where truth becomes demanded obedience — is gestured at but never fully articulated.

Score: **4/8** — Present but not landed.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**

The structure has a significant architectural problem: the personal introduction (seminary/marathon) sets up a frame (setting your mind on the prize), but this frame is not tied cleanly to the text's opening argument. The sermon then inverts Paul's own order — working through vv. 5–11 before returning to vv. 1–4, which Paul puts first as the doctrinal foundation for everything that follows. The conclusion circles back to vv. 1–4, which is satisfying emotionally but exegetically backward — the hearers would have been better served by understanding the foundation before receiving the commands.

The sermon is nonetheless followable. There is a traceable logic: old life sins (vv. 5–11) → cross motivation → indicative foundation (vv. 1–4) → new life virtues (vv. 12–17) → encouragement. But this logic is the preacher's logic, not Paul's logic.

Score: **5/9** — Followable but structurally inverted from the text's own argument.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**

The porneia/pornography Greek connection is effective and memorable — this will stick. The "be killing sin" t-shirt update is useful. The Ernie Haase mosquito quote is charming and memorable. The driving anecdote is relevant.

The seminary/marathon introduction is the weakest illustration — it floats untethered from specific textual grounding and resembles motivational speech more than expository illustration.

Score: **3/5** — Several effective moments; the opening illustration works against the sermon.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

The manuscript reads as a spoken document — direct address is present ("Some of you all"), the preacher references himself ("I fell short"), the register is pastoral and colloquial. The sentence "Change because you know those words are part of the reason Jesus had to be nailed to that cross" has genuine homiletical weight.

However, the sermon also contains passages that read as discursive meandering rather than focused proclamation. The political section is long and at points feels more like a pastoral editorial than the focused proclamation of a text. Filler is present ("Anyway," "Heh," "Right?"). The final paragraph dissolves into encouragement rather than climaxing.

Score: **3/5** — Genuine pastoral voice, but lacks the compression and climactic force that sermonic manuscripts require.

### 6. Cognitive Load Assessment
**−1** — The sermon covers significant ground (mortification, political idolatry, porneia, blasphemy/slander, new creation, election, the virtues of vv. 12–17) across a medium-length manuscript. The structure, while somewhat inverted, is followable. The average congregant can track this. A single further concept would tip it toward overload. The political section in particular, while bold, carries enough length and nuance that it may displace tracking of the rest of the passage.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* Primarily Christ — particularly in the hymn section and the cross-connection to sin. The motivational opening and the closing encouragement edge toward self-focus. Net: leans toward Christ, not decisively.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* The hymn section and the wrath section create genuine encounters. The rest is largely informational and motivational. Partial encounter.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* The political idolatry section genuinely convicts. The closing encouragement comforts. The bulk of the middle informs. All three are present; conviction is the weakest of the three despite being the most needed given the text's imperatives.

### 9. Strengths (3)
1. **Courageous political application**: The indictment of Christian nationalism and political idolatry is specific, well-grounded in the text's own warning about passion and evil desires, and aimed at a real and present sin in the congregation. The statistics on gospel-sharing versus political talking are effective and press the conscience with concrete data. This is the most Puritan moment in the sermon — a specific sinner is named and cornered.
2. **Cross-connected motivation**: The sermon's best theological sentence — "Change because you know those words are part of the reason Jesus had to be nailed to that cross" — and the surrounding hymn create a genuine gospel moment. The cross is not decorative here; it is offered as the only sufficient motivation for mortification. This is theologically right.
3. **Pastoral self-disclosure**: The preacher applies the passage to himself ("I fell short here this week with this lady driving insanely," "I myself included at times") in a way that is neither false modesty nor pulpit confession-for-effect. It reads as genuine, and it creates a sermon that does not preach down to the congregation.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (3)
1. **Inverted indicative/imperative structure**: Paul builds Colossians 3 on a foundation: you have been raised with Christ, you have died, your life is hidden in God (vv. 1–4). The imperatives of vv. 5–17 flow from this foundation. The sermon spends the first two-thirds on imperatives and returns to the foundation as a conclusion. This is not wrong in the sense of teaching heresy, but it is structurally backward and produces a sermon that feels morally demanding before it is theologically grounding. The congregation is called to mortify before they are told what they have already been made.
2. **Vv. 12–17 receive no real exposition**: The second half of the passage — compassion, humility, patience, forgiveness, the peace of Christ ruling, the word of Christ dwelling richly, doing all in the name of Christ — is read aloud and then essentially abandoned. One driving anecdote and a vague aspiration do not constitute exposition or application of six verses. The "put on" virtues are at least as important as the "put off" vices, and they receive a fraction of the treatment.
3. **The gospel presentation is not written**: The manuscript ends with the annotation "Gospel presentation." This is a placeholder, not a proclamation. The sermon cannot be evaluated as complete without it, and no sermon on Colossians 3 — which grounds the imperatives in the gospel of union with Christ — should end with a gospel presentation tacked on rather than woven through.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher may not see how much doctrinal weight the unwritten gospel section is expected to carry. The sermon's middle section is morally demanding and the closing encouragement is emotionally warm, but the justifying, regenerating, indwelling work of God that actually enables the obedience being called for is assumed rather than proclaimed. The congregation is told they need Jesus at every step — but the mechanism (union with Christ, the Spirit's work of mortification and vivification, the completed work of justification freeing them to obey without earning) is never explained. The "hidden in Christ" language is used devotionally without its doctrinal freight being unpacked.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The political application section feels like the sermon's strongest moment — it is specific, bold, and creates energy. But its length and prominence mean it functions as the sermon's de facto main point, displacing what Paul's actual main point is (the new creation reality that makes new obedience both possible and demanded). The sermon feels strongest when it is most topical, and weakest when it is most textual. This is the illusion of strength: the boldest application is not the most expository section, and the congregation may leave remembering the Trump indictment more than the "hidden with Christ" foundation.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Colossians 3:3–4 — "For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God. When Christ who is your life appears, you also will appear with him in glory" — is one of the most pastorally rich verses in the New Testament for a congregation struggling with identity, politics, fear, and mortality. The eschatological force of "you will appear with him in glory" as the ground of present obedience, and the security of being "hidden" in the one place the world cannot reach, are barely touched. This is the text's own pastoral heart for anxious, politically overinvested Christians — and the sermon drives past it to get to the sin lists.

### 14. Surgical Fixes
1. **Restructure around vv. 1–4 as the foundation**: Open with the indicative (you have died, you have been raised, you are hidden, you will appear in glory). Establish what God has already done before issuing a single imperative. The sermon will gain theological grounding and the application will land harder for it.
2. **Develop vv. 12–17 with the same specificity as the political application**: Choose one or two virtues — forgiveness (v. 13) or the peace of Christ as umpire (v. 15) — and press them with the same concreteness used for political idolatry. Who in this congregation is holding a grudge right now? Who has let political division fracture a friendship? The text addresses this directly.
3. **Write the gospel presentation and integrate it, not append it**: The gospel should not appear at the end as a standalone addendum. It should be woven into the indicative foundation (vv. 1–4) and the cross-motivation moment. The sermon already has the raw material (the hymn, the cross connection) — it needs to be structured so the gospel is not the final element but the central animating reality.
4. **Add a clear "therefore" exhortation before closing**: After the gospel moment, the sermon needs a direct summons — not encouragement but command: "Therefore, this week: repent of the political idol. Identify one person you have never told about Christ. Put on forgiveness in the specific relationship you have been avoiding. Return to this passage and let it search you." Encouragement is not exhortation.
5. **Tighten the introduction**: The seminary/marathon/sports visualization opening is the weakest section and the longest. Cut it to three sentences or replace it entirely with an opening that arises from the text's own stakes — the tension between who Paul says you are (raised with Christ) and how you are living (still walking in the old patterns).

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — but the inverse pattern is present. The rubric flags dead orthodoxy when exposition scores high but conscience and applicatory force score low. This sermon has the opposite problem: pastoral warmth and genuine conviction exceed the theological precision and expository depth. The fire is present; the doctrinal structure needs to be stronger to sustain it. This is not dead orthodoxy — it is living warmth in need of deeper rootage.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries genuine pastoral gravity — especially in the cross-and-hymn section — but it does not yet carry the full weight of glory that comes when a congregation is made to feel, simultaneously, the weight of what God has done and the lightness of being hidden in the One who has done it; that moment of weight is glimpsed but not sustained.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Colossians 3:1–17 is a Pauline paraenetic passage with a clear indicative/imperative structure and explicit ethical content — it is well-suited to this rubric. The framework applies naturally. The categories of exposition, conscience-pressing, and exhortation are all directly relevant to what this text demands. The only rubric-fit note is that the passage's "put on" section (vv. 12–17) is more communal and corporate than the rubric's applicatory categories naturally surface — the peace of Christ ruling "in one body" and the word of Christ dwelling in the community point to corporate ecclesial application that the rubric's individual-conscience categories do not fully capture. The sermon itself does not develop this corporately, which is itself a missed opportunity.
