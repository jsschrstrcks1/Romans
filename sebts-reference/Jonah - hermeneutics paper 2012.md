Background and Introduction.
The author of the palistrophe entitled Jonah remains anonymous so we can’t say for certain who wrote it. However, in light of details that ONLY Jonah could know, it is quite reasonable to assume that Jonah himself authored this book. Some of you may say “but Jonah was written in the third person”, according to John MacArthur this was not an uncommon practice – he goes on to cite Exodus 11:3, and 1 Samuel 12:11 as instances where Old Testament passages were written in this particular voice. Jonah was born in the Northern Tribes during the time of King Jeroboam II. This was a prosperous time; Israel was about the same size as it had been under David and Solomon. In short, things were good in Israel financially. Spiritually was quite a different story. This timeline would make him a contemporary of Amos who preached against similar themes in his book, especially empty worship (Amos 5). In the mean time, a growing threat came in the form of Assyria. They were increasing in strength and would go on to take Israel into captivity in 722BC. Nineveh of course was the capital of Assyria (which would later fall to the Babylonians) and is hailed as one of the largest cities of the ancient world. Jonah mentions it took him three days to preach his way across Nineveh. Its interesting to point out the city is only two miles across, but due to the density of population, things were likely crowded, and no doubt Jonah did not want to be discovered by God shirking his duties a second time. Finally, I would like to point out one more controversy regarding the historicity of Jonah. Liberals and secularists almost universally argue that Jonah is simply an allegory, that no one could really be swallowed by a fish and live for three days. However, a student of scripture is always best served by interpreting a particular verse based upon its genre. IF the scripture indicates it is to be taken metaphorically; then do so, but when it is recounting a particular story as though it were historical fact, then of course we should accept it as historical fact. The biggest argument for why the Christian ought to believe the historicity of Jonah is that Jesus believed it was historical. In fact, we can see Jesus exposition of parts of Jonah in Mathew 12:39, 16:4, Luke 11:29, John 1:42 and John 21:15. “…however grandiose and miraculous the events may have been, the narrative must be viewed as historical. Centered on an [sic] historically identifiable OT prophet who lived in the 8th century B.C. the account of whom has been recorded in narrative form, there is no alternative but to understand Jonah as Historical” (MacArthur 1293). Leading up to Jonah’s ministry in Nineveh, they had experienced two plagues (765, and 759) and a solar eclipse (763bc). At this point, a rather stinky Jonah (whose name means Dove, a symbol of peace) was vomited out on the shore of the city. Ninevites believed that a fish God had founded the city, so this means of Jonah’s delivery had special meaning for them. Another interesting point in this book is that up until this point in Israel’s history, they had been a magnet for the lost (in the OT sense) – they were at the crossroads of the known world, and so word of the One True Hebrew God spread far and wide around the world. However, in the case of Jonah, God specifically directed him to go out (in contrast with Israel’s history) to a growing threat, to share the Gospel, to secure their salvation. It’s reminiscent of the time after the Iron Curtain fell, there was a spiritual vacuum, and they desired to know the truth – and many were hesitant to go because they were our enemy. Those who traveled to these countries (Bill Viehl for instance, NAMB missionary who did puppet ministry in what is now Ukraine) witnessed an amazing harvest. This of course Jonah saw too – once he overcame his rebelliousness. I mentioned that palistrophe is used in the book – let me explain: each chapter within the book contains a palistrophe, in addition chapter 1-2, and 3-4 are palistrophes (independently), as is the entire book as a whole. That is to say that Chapter 1 mirrors 2, chapter 3 mirrors 4, and chapters 1-2 mirror 2-4 in a palistrophic way. In conclusion, it is important to note that eventually the Babylonians overran Nineveh. The city was effectively sacked and burned. We could interpret that as the Ninevites having returned to their wicked ways, but the truth is we are just speculating. Some of the artifacts of Nineveh have been recovered and are on display at the British Museum in London – a display I had the privilege to see in 2009. The reliefs depicted slaves being tortured. However, we know the city existed, and can even see some of the artifacts that demonstrate that Nineveh existed, and that the Biblical account of its destruction is true. 
MIT Outline      
Provisional Title: Disobedience and a Big Fish; Jonah Swallowed to Further the Gospel.
Jonah 1
MIT:  Jonah flees from God’s call, and inadvertently shares the Gospel.
God is Sovereign over Jonah. (Jonah 1:1-2)
God is Sovereign over Jonah’s Rebellion. (Jonah 1:3)
God Demonstrates His Sovereignty Over the Weather and Lot Casting in His Pursuit of Jonah. (Jonah 1:4-16)
God is Sovereign over Sea Creatures. (Jonah 1:17)
MIM Outline      
Provisional Title: See Jonah Run.
MIM:  Rebelliousness against God’s Sovereignty is pointless, despite Jonah’s best efforts to the contrary, God still delivered Jonah to Nineveh. 
Sometimes God Requires things of us that are uncomfortable, unpleasant, or that make us Angry; but he is Sovereign and knows what is best for the world and us.
Running away solves nothing when you serve THE Sovereign God that transcends time and space; Our God is Huge and his will cannot be escaped.
In his Sovereignty God Pursues His disobedient Children sometimes in unexpected ways.
God in his Sovereignty wanted to use Jonah as a picture of the Christ to come, this required Jonah’s disobedience, the picture starts here.  
Practical Applications:
              I.              God has a plan for your life and it is better to be used by God willingly.
              II.             God’s in His Sovereignty is not surprised when we act in rebellion; he accomplishes 
                              his purposes any way.
              III.            God will still pursue us to the ends of the earth, out of love for us, because of his 
                              Names Sake, and to restore our relationship with Him (Gospel Presentation).
IV.            Dr Akin says, "don't ask God should I go to the missions’ field; ask God, why should I 
                 stay here in America." (Call to Missional living)

Provisional Title: Jonah’s Prayer acknowledging his helplessness, and repentance. 
Jonah 2

God uses Jonah’s Rebellion to continue painting a portrait of Christ; Jonah was cast out from God’s presence, he wore a crown of weeds, and was delivered up from “Sheol” or the Pit, to reach mankind (Nineveh). 
Jonah Represents Christ praying in the Garden, committing to a course with a high personal cost out of fear and obedience to the Lord because Salvation is of the Lord.
God in His Sovereignty provides Jonah with Deliverance, as He did for Christ.

                    Presentation)

Provisional Title:  God addresses Jonah’s Anger
Jonah 4
MIT:  Jonah learns of God’s compassion for rebellious people.  
Jonah in his arrogance and misplaced priorities thought he knew better than God, felt betrayed by God, may have felt he and God had betrayed Israel, and likely felt as though God had made him a false prophet; a crime punishable by death. (Jonah 4:1-4) 
Jonah wants to watch and see what God decides. (Jonah 4:5)

Provisional Title: See Jonah’s Rage.
MIM: Misplaced priorities, arrogance, and misunderstanding leads to anger, we must always keep Christ’s Kingdom Priorities in mind in all we do or say so that we might see God save his lost sheep.
God created, and is sovereign over our emotions.
We do not always get to see what God is doing, but when God allows us to see – always watch. 
A lost soul is more important to God than our displeasure, we must make God’s priorities our priorities.  
Practical Applications:
We are often angry over things we ought not be, God can handle our emotions!  
 God has a (metaphorical) secret inner workings room, and every now and then he allows us a glimpse inside; this is never an accident – God is trying to teach you something so do not miss it. 
 Perhaps today’s Ninevites are the Muslims in our communities, what are you doing personally to engage these and other unlovely people?
 God loves us enough that he would send a storm, and a fish that swallowed a man, and spit him up so that we could know God; but he didn’t stop there, he sent his one and only Son (the new and better Jonah) to die for our misdeeds (Gospel presentation).
