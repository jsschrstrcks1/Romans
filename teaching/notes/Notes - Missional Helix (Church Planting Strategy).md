## The Missional Helix

**Most important part of a church planting strategy?**
Having a church plant is more than just knowing our ideals.

The Missional Helix is built on four quadrants that continually inform one another:

### 1. Theological Reflection (TR)
The anchor. Biblical truth that shapes everything else. The other three quadrants exist to apply this, not replace it.

### 2. Cultural Analysis (CA)
Have to keep our finger on the pulse of culture.
- Raises awareness and understanding of broader cultural issues
- Helps us understand what people are thinking and *why*
- So that we can apply the TR and Biblical truths

### 3. Historical Perspective (HP)
Helps show us how things got where they are.
- What happened in the past that informs current actions?
- *Why do churches meet for about an hour on Sunday morning?*
- What have others done in the past that we can learn from — both good and bad?
- What have they done, how did they do it, and why?

### 4. Strategic Formation (SF)
- Leads to the practice of ministry based upon the previous three
- SF informs our TR, forming a helix — a continual pattern of reflection
- All four influence each other
- Ministry results from each of the four quadrants of the helix

## 10 Cultural Trends

1. **Connections are more digital, less personal** — Facebook, etc. Cell use has increased by 200 million people.

2. **Place** — 10% of people changed their address. The average American drove less in 2010 than in 2000. More urban society, more public transport. Gas prices may be a factor.

3. **Cities** — Rural to urban transition over the last 100–200 years. Gentrification. Live/work/play.

4. **Demographics** — White Americans are only a bare majority (63.7% per Wikipedia).

5. **Polarity** — More connection has allowed for tighter subcultures. The right moved further right; the left further left.

6. **The self-shot** — More obsessed with seeing ourselves in places other than the mirror. Easier with modern technology. Increased effort related to image grooming: tanning, tattooing, etc.

7. **Pornography / Informality** — Porn more pervasive but still underground. Informality rising: untucked shirts, billionaires in jeans, the most powerful CEO is called "Steve." No more journalistic codes. Cohabitation embraced.

8. **Liquidity** — Wealth disconnected from real assets. Hedge funds make billions trading shares of bets on the future price of shares.

9. **Complexity** — So many divisions of power, no one can amass too much.

10. **Oral Learners** — The most interesting pressure point: not literate. Most any international mission will need to tackle educational literacy alongside gospel literacy.

## Barna Mega Themes

1. **Theological illiteracy in the church** — The Holy Spirit treated as a symbol, not a living being. Most believe Easter is a religious holiday but don't associate it with Christ.

2. **Church becoming more ingrown, less outreach-oriented.**

3. **The world is less interested in spiritual things.**

4. **Community action escalating, especially by young people** — But without a spiritual basis, their involvement will wane.

5. **Tolerance is winning over the church** — The church is avoiding making discerning choices for fear of being labeled judgmental. Knowing where to draw the line is perplexing, but there can be no tolerance for tolerance.

6. **Christian influence is largely invisible** — No longer salt and light. Is this because of the media? Or because we're neglecting fundamentals in discipleship and evangelism altogether?

---

## The Rise of the Nones

More people than ever claim no religious affiliation — even those who once would have identified with a tradition.

---

## Theological McCarthyism

Recoiling — guilt by association.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

Evaluated as preparation for any future preaching or teaching use:

1. **The Theological Reflection quadrant is empty.** The framework names TR as the anchor and says it is "Biblical truth that shapes everything else" — but nowhere in the notes is any biblical truth specified. The anchor has been named but not cast. Any sermon or teaching built from these notes would need to supply the actual theological content that is entirely absent here.

What does "Cultural Analysis" of digital connectivity tell the church planter to do differently? The notes stop before the point where they become useful.

### 11. Blind Spots

The notes assume that the four quadrants of the helix are self-evidently gospel-shaped — that "Theological Reflection" will naturally be Christ-centered and that the other three quadrants will take their cue from it. But the TR quadrant as described could be filled with any theological content, including content that is not gospel-centered. The framework is a container; it does not specify what goes in it. A church planting strategy built on Theological Reflection without specifying that TR is grounded in the gospel of Christ crucified is a framework that can be filled with anything.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The framework language itself may create an illusion of theological seriousness. "Theological Reflection is the anchor" sounds robust. But naming TR as the anchor is not the same as doing TR. The notes give the framework its fullest treatment and the actual theological content no treatment at all. The structure looks theologically rigorous; the substance is theologically empty at this stage.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The Barna observation about theological illiteracy — specifically that "the Holy Spirit is treated as a symbol, not a living being" and that "most believe Easter is a religious holiday but don't associate it with Christ" — is a devastating diagnosis of the congregation these notes presumably aim to reach. ### 14. Surgical Fixes

These fixes are addressed to any preacher or teacher who might use this material in a ministry context:

1. **Fill the TR quadrant before building anything else.** Before the helix can function, the theological content must be specified. Write one paragraph that answers: What is the gospel? What is the church? What is mission? Until those are answered, the helix has no anchor.

2. **Add a fifth element or treat the gospel as the center of the helix, not just one quadrant.** The Missional Helix as described could be applied to any community organization. The church's Theological Reflection must be explicitly identified as the gospel of Jesus Christ crucified, risen, and reigning — or the framework will produce churches that are culturally sensitive and historically aware but not gospelly distinct.

3. **Connect the Barna themes to Scripture.** The Barna observations about theological illiteracy and tolerance are not just survey data — they are descriptions of people who need the gospel. Each Barna theme could be given a biblical text that addresses it: theological illiteracy → Hosea 4:6; church inwardness → Acts 1:8; tolerance as capitulation → Romans 12:2. This connection would give the notes homiletical traction.

4. **Develop "Theological McCarthyism."** The phrase is evocative but undefined. If it means "guilt by association" in theological discourse (recoiling from any idea that sounds like it came from a suspect source), it describes a real and damaging ecclesial pathology. It deserves treatment, not just labeling.

5. **Date and source the cultural trend data.** Several observations reference specific statistics (200 million cell users, 10% address change, 63.7% white Americans). These appear to be from roughly 2010–2012. If this material is used in any ministry context, the data needs to be updated or explicitly marked as historical baseline rather than current trend.
