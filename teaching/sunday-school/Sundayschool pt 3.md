### Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **No governing text.** This is the foundational structural problem. Romans 8:29–30 is cited but not opened. The lesson teaches about the Bible's teaching rather than opening the Bible's teaching. Every truth in this lesson is true — but the congregation cannot learn to read Scripture by watching a teacher cite Scripture. Expository teaching trains the congregation to trust the text by demonstrating how the text generates its own meaning. That is absent here.

2. **The conscience is never pressed.** There is not a single moment in this lesson where a particular person in the room is cornered by the truth. The "lost people" reference is third-person and descriptive. The final application ("actively taking steps to follow God") is directed at no one in particular. But there is no moment in this lesson where the Savior himself is held up — where his life, death, and resurrection are proclaimed as the center of all things. The lesson teaches the structure of salvation without ever preaching the Savior.

### Blind Spots

The teacher likely does not see that the lesson's greatest strength — doctrinal precision — is also its limitation. Precision without proclamation produces theological students, not worshipers. The lesson knows the right answers but does not yet know how to make those right answers produce love and trembling before God. There is also likely an unexamined assumption that teaching the ordo salutis is equivalent to teaching the gospel. It is not. The ordo is a map of how God saves; the gospel is the news that God has saved. Maps matter — but you cannot eat a map.

### Illusion of Strength

The Horton citation gives the lesson an air of theological weight that slightly exceeds the actual depth of the surrounding content. The citation is accurate and well-chosen, but it does the exegetical work that the lesson itself should do. It reads as a shortcut to credibility rather than a window into Scripture. Similarly, listing the ordo salutis creates the impression of systematic completeness without the passage (Romans 8:29–30) itself being opened or applied.

---

### Missed Opportunity

Romans 8:29–30 is a text of staggering pastoral consolation. Paul's golden chain — foreknown, predestined, called, justified, glorified — is written in the past tense, including glorification. Every person in that chain is already glorified in God's purpose. Paul uses this not to produce theological taxonomy but to answer the question of Romans 8:28: "How do we know all things work together for good?" The answer is: because you are already glorified in God's eternal counsel. That assurance — the comfort of the ordo as pastoral anchor rather than doctrinal checklist — was entirely missed. The lesson used Romans 8:29–30 as a skeleton but bypassed its heart.

### Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Anchor the entire lesson to a governing passage.** Romans 8:28–30 is rich enough to carry everything this lesson attempts. Open it. Walk through Paul's argument. Let the ordo emerge from the text rather than being imposed on it. The lesson's content does not need to change — but it needs a textual home.

2. **Add a direct address moment after the effectual calling section.** Something like: "Some of you in this room are not sure whether the mud has been washed from your eyes. You've heard the gospel before but it hasn't seized you. I want to speak to you directly..." This is the moment the lesson needs to break from third-person teaching into second-person address.

3. **Preach Christ at the center of the ordo, not just within it.** Atonement is item #2 on a list. It deserves a paragraph. What did it cost? What did it accomplish? Why is it the only basis on which the rest of the chain holds? The cross needs to be the sun around which the planets of the ordo orbit — not one planet among nine.

4. **End with a specific call, not a general encouragement.** Replace "we should be actively taking steps to follow God" with concrete obedience: specific spiritual disciplines, a named sin to repent of, an act of witness, a step of trust. The conclusion as written tells people to be more Christian without telling them how or pressing them to do it now.

5. **Use the Puritan 'uses' pattern to close.** After the doctrinal teaching, run through at least three uses: (1) conviction — "if you are trusting your works for salvation, you are on sinking sand"; (2) consolation — "if you are in Christ, your glorification is already secured in the mind of God"; (3) direction — "therefore, pursue sanctification not to earn God's favor but because you already have it." This structure turns teaching into preaching.
