Sovereignty Without Pride, Zeal Without Extremes

A Reformed Baptist Study for Men Walking in Truth and Grace

Chapter 17 – The Danger of Pride: When Doctrine Inflates Instead of Humbling

Summary:

As Belcher's journey unfolds, a subtle but serious danger emerges — one that strikes closest to the men most committed to truth: pride disguised as orthodoxy.

The doctrines of grace were given to crush boasting. Yet in sinful hands, they can become fuel for superiority. Some men move from "I am saved by grace alone" to "I understand grace better than others." That is not Reformed theology. That is old-fashioned flesh wearing theological clothing.

Paul anticipated this. In a letter soaked in the doctrines of grace, he asked a question that should silence every theological ego:

"What do you have that you did not receive? If then you received it, why do you boast as if you did not receive it?" (1 Corinthians 4:7)

If total depravity is true, then we were no better.
If election is true, then we were not smarter.
If effectual calling is true, then we did not awaken ourselves.

Right doctrine should leave a man stunned, not smug.

True Calvinism produces:

* Humility — because we contributed nothing
* Gratitude — because we deserved nothing
* Compassion — because we were no different
* Evangelistic urgency — because God uses means

False or "over-balanced" Calvinism produces:

* Coldness toward the lost
* Intellectual arrogance over the uninformed
* Fatalism that excuses disobedience
* Withdrawal from gospel witness

The difference is not in the doctrine itself — it is in the heart that receives it.

This danger is not hypothetical. It lives and breathes in the academy. When the doctrines of grace first click for men in seminary — when election, effectual calling, and particular redemption suddenly come into focus — entirely too many of them grow arrogant. Rudely condescending. They walk out of a classroom on the doctrine of God's sovereignty and immediately lord it over everyone who hasn't caught up yet. They seem to think they have the whole Bible figured out, and everyone else is dumb, or backwards, or beneath serious conversation.

That is not the fruit of the Spirit. That is the flesh wearing a Geneva gown.

A man who truly understands total depravity should be the most humble man in the room — because he knows what he was. A man who truly understands unconditional election should be the most grateful man in the room — because he knows he did nothing to deserve it. And a man who truly understands effectual calling should be the most patient man in the room — because he remembers that God had to drag him to the light.

If your theology makes you look down on other believers, your theology hasn't gone deep enough. It has lodged in your intellect but hasn't reached your knees.

And Paul warned the Corinthians precisely about this: "This 'knowledge' puffs up, but love builds up" (1 Corinthians 8:1). A man can be right about every point of doctrine and still be useless to the kingdom if his knowledge has made him proud rather than pastoral.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Paul systematically dismantled human pride across thirteen chapters of Romans. Romans 1–3 proved that every mouth is stopped — Jew and Gentile alike stand guilty. Romans 3:27 asks the question that should echo in every man's conscience: "Then what becomes of our boasting? It is excluded." Romans 9 pressed further: salvation "depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy" (Romans 9:16). Romans 11:18 warned the Gentile believers: "do not be arrogant toward the branches." Romans 11:20 drove it home: "So do not become proud, but fear." And Romans 12:3 brought it into daily life: "not to think of himself more highly than he ought to think, but to think with sober judgment, each according to the measure of faith that God has assigned."

Our sermon series has traced this theme from midnight to morning — and at every stage, pride is what keeps men asleep in the dark. In Romans 14, we saw how pride wears church clothes: the strong despise the weak, the weak judge the strong, and both assume a role God never assigned them. The doctrines of grace don't create pride; they expose and destroy it — unless we twist them into trophies instead of receiving them as gifts.

Key Scriptures:

* 1 Corinthians 4:7 — "What do you have that you did not receive? If then you received it, why do you boast as if you did not receive it?"
* Romans 3:27 — "Then what becomes of our boasting? It is excluded."
* James 4:6 — "But he gives more grace. Therefore it says, 'God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble.'"
* 1 Corinthians 8:1 — "This 'knowledge' puffs up, but love builds up."

Reflection Questions

1. Where has theology ever tempted you toward pride instead of humility — in conversations, in your home, in the way you view other believers?
2. Why are men particularly vulnerable to intellectual arrogance — and how does the doctrine of total depravity expose that vulnerability?
3. Romans 3:27 says boasting is "excluded." What forms of spiritual boasting do you still carry?
4. How does pride disguise itself as discernment, conviction, or theological confidence?
5. What evidence shows that grace has truly softened your heart — not just sharpened your mind?

Group Discussion

* Why can good doctrine still produce bad attitudes? Discuss the difference between conviction and condescension.
* Read 1 Corinthians 4:7 aloud. If everything we have is received — including our understanding of grace — what ground does any man have for boasting?
* Spurgeon wrote: "I am never ashamed to avow myself a Calvinist; I do not hesitate to take the name of Baptist... but if I am asked what is my creed, I reply: It is Jesus Christ." How does this guard against making a system our identity rather than Christ Himself?
* What habits help cultivate humility in men who love theology? How can we guard against becoming theological critics instead of shepherds?

Prayer Focus

"Lord, search me and expose the pride I cannot see. Guard my heart from doctrine that inflates instead of humbles. Let me boast in nothing but the cross of Christ, where my pride was crucified and Your grace was revealed. Make me gentle, bold, and full of grace."

Chapter 18 – Unconditional Election: The Justice and Goodness of God

Summary:

Here Belcher presses into one of the most difficult and glorious doctrines — and the question every honest man eventually asks: "How is it fair for God to choose some and not others?"

Scripture reframes the question entirely: How would it be just for God to choose any?

Justice would condemn all. Mercy rescues some. And that mercy flows not from any foreseen goodness in man, but from God's own purpose, wisdom, love, and glory.

Election is not arbitrary — as if God flipped a coin. Nor is it reluctant — as if God wished He could save all but something prevented Him. Election is the free, purposeful, and loving act of a sovereign God who set His heart on a people before the foundation of the world. It flows from the depths of who God is, not from anything found in us.

R.C. Sproul wrote:

"If there is one single molecule in this universe running around loose, totally free of God's sovereignty, then we have no guarantee that a single promise of God will ever be fulfilled."

Election does not shrink God's goodness. It magnifies it. Because it means God's saving work depends entirely on God's saving character. And that is the only foundation that will never crack.

The real objection to election is not intellectual — it is moral. Men resist it because it strips away the last vestige of human contribution. And nothing offends pride like being told you had nothing to do with your own rescue.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Romans 9 was some of the most challenging ground we covered in our sermon series. Paul's answer to the fairness objection was immediate and absolute: "Is there injustice on God's part? By no means!" (Romans 9:14). He did not soften the doctrine or apologize for it. He appealed to God's own words to Moses: "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" (Romans 9:15).

The potter-and-clay argument of Romans 9:19-21 was not designed to silence questions with raw power, but to humble us before a wisdom we cannot fully comprehend. Paul's anguish for Israel (Romans 9:1-3) proved that belief in election does not kill compassion — it deepens it. A man who weeps for the lost while trusting God's sovereignty has found the biblical center.

And the doxology of Romans 11:33-36 — "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways!" — was not the conclusion of a man who had explained everything, but of a man overwhelmed by a God too great to explain. That is where doctrine is meant to land: not in a neat system, but on your knees.

Key Scriptures:

* Romans 9:14-16 — "Is there injustice on God's part? By no means! For he says to Moses, 'I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion.' So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy."
* Ephesians 1:4-5 — "He chose us in him before the foundation of the world, that we should be holy and blameless before him. In love he predestined us for adoption to himself as sons through Jesus Christ."
* Deuteronomy 29:29 — "The secret things belong to the LORD our God, but the things that are revealed belong to us and to our children forever, that we may do all the words of this law."
* Romans 11:33 — "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways!"

Reflection Questions

1. When you first encountered the doctrine of election, what was your honest reaction? Has it changed?
2. Why does unconditional election offend human pride — and why is that offense itself revealing?
3. How does Romans 9:14-16 answer the charge that election is unjust?
4. What is the difference between God being fair and God being good — and which does Scripture emphasize?
5. How does election deepen your worship and strengthen your assurance in seasons of doubt?

Group Discussion

* Read Romans 9:19-23 together. Why is the potter-and-clay analogy uncomfortable — and what does that discomfort reveal about our hearts?
* Discuss: If God saved only by strict justice, what would every man receive? How does that reality reframe the "fairness" objection?
* Sproul wrote: "If there is one single molecule in this universe running around loose, totally free of God's sovereignty, then we have no guarantee that a single promise of God will ever be fulfilled." How does total sovereignty strengthen rather than threaten your confidence in God's goodness?
* How can we discuss election with unbelieving friends or struggling brothers in a way that is honest, humble, and hopeful — without softening Scripture or losing compassion?

Prayer Focus

"Sovereign Lord, I bow before mysteries I cannot solve. Thank You for setting Your love on me before I could ever seek You. Silence the pride that demands explanations, and give me the humility to worship where I cannot understand. Let Your election produce in me not coldness, but wonder."

Chapter 19 – The Danger of Extremes: Hyper-Calvinism and Man-Centered Theology

Summary:

Belcher now exposes two opposite errors that threaten every man who takes theology seriously — and both are alive in our churches today.

Extreme 1 — Hyper-Calvinism (the "Over-Balanced" Error):

"If God wants to save the lost, He can do so on His own; I don't have to be involved."

That is not a caricature. It is a sentence that has been spoken from real pulpits, by real pastors, to real congregations. And it has done real damage — not just to the lost who never heard the gospel, but to the doctrine of sovereignty itself. Because when men twist God's sovereignty into an excuse for laziness, they hand the world a reason to reject what the Bible actually teaches.

This error has become, for many people, synonymous with Calvinism. Not because it is true of those who faithfully believe what Scripture teaches about election and grace — but because a few men across the centuries twisted what the Bible said to support their own passivity. And the reputation has stuck.

The hyper-Calvinist has so over-balanced his theology toward sovereignty that he has lost all urgency for the lost. He reasons: "If God has already chosen His elect, why preach? Why plead? Why weep?" He takes the doctrines of grace and builds a fortress — not for the glory of God, but for the comfort of his own system.

History gives us the clearest example of this error — and its cure.

In the late 1700s, before the modern missionary movement began, many Particular Baptists in England believed strongly in God's sovereignty — but some had drifted into exactly this kind of passivity. A young cobbler-turned-preacher named William Carey began studying Scripture and geography, and he became convinced that the Great Commission still applied — that Christians were obligated to take the gospel to the nations.

At a ministers' meeting in 1786, Carey raised the question of overseas missions. According to later missionary accounts, an older minister — often identified as John Ryland Sr. — reportedly rebuked him, saying something like: "Young man, sit down. When God pleases to convert the heathen, He will do it without your aid or mine."

Historians debate the exact wording — we don't have a transcript of the meeting. But whether those precise words were spoken or not, that sentiment absolutely reflected the attitude present in parts of the church at the time.

Carey did not reject God's sovereignty. He embraced it.

But he also saw something others had neglected: the same sovereign God who ordains the salvation of His elect also ordains the preaching of the gospel as the means.

So Carey wrote his famous pamphlet: An Enquiry into the Obligations of Christians to Use Means for the Conversion of the Heathens. The key phrase there is "use means." God uses means. He uses preaching. He uses prayer. He uses sending. He uses sacrifice.

In 1792, Carey helped found the Baptist Missionary Society. In 1793, he sailed to India. And from that small beginning, the modern missionary movement was born.

Carey did not believe missions were unnecessary because God is sovereign. He believed missions were unstoppable because God is sovereign.

That is the difference between hyper-Calvinism and historic Reformed theology.

Hyper-Calvinism says: "If God wants to save them, He will do it without me."
Biblical Calvinism says: "Because God has His people, I will go — and I will preach with confidence."

And history proved which vision bore fruit.

Extreme 2 — Man-Centered Arminianism (Dr. Bloom's Error):

"God has done His part; now it depends on man." This leads to pressure-driven evangelism, fragile assurance, and subtle pride in human decision. Dr. Bloom means well — he wants to honor human dignity and moral agency. But in doing so, he has made the Creator subordinate to the creature and salvation contingent on the very will that Scripture says is enslaved to sin.

Both errors distort biblical balance. Both fail the text. Both fail the lost.

Historic Reformed theology affirms: God ordains the ends — and the means. He ordains salvation. He ordains preaching. He ordains prayer. He ordains missions. The Great Commission was not optional. It was commanded by a sovereign King who told Paul in Corinth: "Do not be afraid, but go on speaking and do not be silent, for I am with you... for I have many in this city who are my people" (Acts 18:9-10) — and then sent Paul to find them through the means of preaching.

Spurgeon captured this balance with characteristic clarity in his Defense of Calvinism:

"That God predestines, and yet that man is responsible, are two facts that few can see clearly. They are believed to be inconsistent and contradictory to each other. If, then, I find taught in one part of the Bible that everything is fore-ordained, that is true; and if I find, in another part of the Bible, that man is responsible for all his actions, that is true; and it is only my folly that leads me to imagine that these two truths can ever contradict each other."

Biblical Calvinism produces bold evangelism, deep prayer, patient discipleship, and confidence in God's power. It never produces passivity. A man who truly believes God has His elect will preach more urgently, not less — because he knows the Word will not return void. And Paul himself endured everything — shipwreck, prison, beatings — "for the sake of the elect, that they also may obtain the salvation that is in Christ Jesus with eternal glory" (2 Timothy 2:10). Election did not make Paul passive. It made him unstoppable.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Paul himself modeled this balance throughout Romans. In Romans 9, he taught unconditional election with unflinching clarity — the potter and clay, the sovereign choice of Jacob over Esau. Then in Romans 10:1, without pausing to reconcile the tension, he wrote: "Brothers, my heart's desire and prayer to God for them is that they may be saved." The same apostle who wrote "it depends not on human will or exertion" also asked: "How then will they call on him in whom they have not believed? And how are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard? And how are they to hear without someone preaching?" (Romans 10:14).

Paul never resolved the tension philosophically — he lived it pastorally. Romans 11 ends not with a systematic chart but with a doxology: a man on his knees before a God whose ways are "inscrutable." That is where theology is meant to land — not in a system that explains everything, but in worship before a God who is greater than our systems.

Key Scriptures:

* Romans 10:14-15 — "How then will they call on him in whom they have not believed? And how are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard? And how are they to preach unless they are sent?"
* Acts 18:9-10 — "Do not be afraid, but go on speaking and do not be silent, for I am with you... for I have many in this city who are my people."
* 2 Timothy 2:10 — "Therefore I endure everything for the sake of the elect, that they also may obtain the salvation that is in Christ Jesus with eternal glory."
* Isaiah 55:8-9 — "For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways, declares the LORD."

Reflection Questions

1. Have you ever been tempted toward hyper-Calvinism — using sovereignty as an excuse for passivity in evangelism or prayer?
2. What is appealing about the Arminian position, and where does it fall short of Scripture?
3. How does Paul hold together election (Romans 9) and evangelistic urgency (Romans 10) without contradiction?
4. Why is it dangerous to build a theological system that is neater than Scripture itself?
5. What specific unbeliever are you praying for right now — and how does sovereignty strengthen rather than weaken that prayer?

Group Discussion

* How does hyper-Calvinism subtly contradict the Great Commission? What does evangelistic apathy look like practically in a man's life — in his silence at work, his avoidance of hard conversations, his comfort in the pew?
* Spurgeon believed as firmly in election as any man who ever lived — yet preached to thousands and wept for the lost. How does Acts 18:9-10 show that God's sovereignty is meant to fuel courage, not silence?
* Why does man-centered theology ultimately create pressure and insecurity rather than peace? How does Dr. Bloom's position unintentionally make salvation rest on the weakest link — the human will?
* How can our group model both doctrinal depth and evangelistic warmth? Discuss practical ways to share the gospel confidently without manipulation.

⸻

Prayer Focus

"Lord Jesus, keep me from extremes. Guard me from a cold theology that knows everything but loves no one, and from a shallow theology that loves everyone but knows nothing. Let me trust Your sovereignty fully and obey Your command courageously. Make me a man who prays boldly, speaks faithfully, and rests confidently in Your saving power."

Closing Reflections — Strong Theology, Soft Hearts

Summary Truths:

1. Pride — The sin that turns doctrine into a trophy instead of a gift. Sovereignty should humble us, not harden us.
2. Election — The doctrine that proves God's justice and magnifies His mercy. It should fuel evangelism, not freeze it.
3. Balance — The wisdom to hold sovereignty and responsibility together without collapsing either. Doctrine should produce worship, not pride.

Spurgeon, in his Defense of Calvinism, reminds us:

"The old truth that Calvin preached, that Augustine preached, that Paul preached, is the truth that I must preach to-day, or else be false to my conscience and my God."

Our theology must always serve our worship, not replace it. Our convictions must produce compassion, not contempt. And our confidence must rest in Christ, not in our system's ability to answer every question.

The balanced Reformed man is:

* Humble under grace
* Bold in witness
* Deep in prayer
* Tender in heart
* Steadfast in truth

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Doctrinally Warm, Textually Thin — A Faithful Study Guide That Falls Short of a Sermon**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A well-intentioned study of pride, election, and theological balance that handles Reformed doctrine with warmth and pastoral care, while functioning more as a thematic commentary and discussion guide than a text-governed proclamation of any specific passage.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**RESULT: CONDITIONAL PASS — with significant qualification**

**Scopus Test:** This document does not preach a single text. It spans 1 Corinthians 4:7, Romans 9, Romans 11:33, and multiple supporting passages across three chapters. The "main point" of each chapter is thematic — pride corrupts doctrine, election is just, theological extremes must be avoided — rather than arising from the sustained argument of any one passage. There is no single governing text from which a thesis demonstrably arises.

**Authority Test:** The preacher clearly submits to Scripture's claims, including the uncomfortable ones (unconditional election, the rejection of Arminianism). There is no editing of hard texts.

**Ruling:** The gate does not fully close because the material is doctrinally faithful and Scripturally grounded. But the material does not function as expository preaching — it functions as a thematic men's study guide. The rubric bends here, but cannot fully pass the Scopus Test since no single text governs the whole. Evaluation proceeds with this caveat prominently noted in the Rubric Fit Note.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — TRIGGERED (partial):** Christ is present but not prominently proclaimed as the crucified and risen Savior. The material discusses grace, election, and humility, but Christ's finished work — His atonement, His resurrection, His mediatorial role — is never explicitly proclaimed. He appears in quoted prayers ("boast in nothing but the cross of Christ") and in passing references, but is not the center of the exposition. A devout Jew who believed in God's sovereign election could track nearly all of this material without stumbling over the necessity of Christ's blood.

**Flag 4 — TRIGGERED:** Application is heavily generic. "Be humble." "Evangelize." "Hold tension." These are correct but not differentiated. The men in the room — the proud seminary type, the discouraged doubter, the passive brother — are addressed only in the broadest strokes. No one is cornered specifically.

**Flag 5 — PARTIAL:** Exhortation is present but soft. The reflection questions and group discussion prompts do function as calls to response, but the direct imperative voice — "You must now do this" — is largely absent from the expository content itself. The sermon tells what to think more than it commands what to do.

He believed missions were unstoppable because God is sovereign." This is memorable, historically grounded, and captures the entire theological argument of Ch. 19 in a single sentence. The historical hedging ("Historians debate the exact wording") is admirable and demonstrates careful-not-clever integrity.

**Strength 3 — Ch. 17 Penetration on Theological Pride**
"If your theology makes you look down on other believers, your theology hasn't gone deep enough. It has lodged in your intellect but hasn't reached your knees." This line has genuine preaching force. It diagnoses the precise failure mode of many Reformed men — intellectual reception of grace without experiential reception — and does so memorably.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

**Weakness 1 — Christ Is Decorative, Not Central**
The document mentions Christ in prayers and in passing, but He is not the gravitational center of any chapter. The Synoptic Test is nearly triggered: a devout Jew who believed in sovereign election and was convicted of intellectual pride could follow nearly the entire argument without stumbling over the blood of Christ. Election in Ephesians 1:4 is "in him" — in the beloved Son — but that Christological anchor is never developed. The doctrine of grace without the cross of Christ is incomplete.

**Weakness 2 — Application Stays at the Category Level**
Every application lands in the same place: "be humble," "evangelize," "hold the tension." These are correct principles. They are not conscience-searching applications. Where is the specific man — the one who silenced a new believer with a Greek word last Sunday, the one who hasn't spoken of Christ to his neighbor in three years, the one who uses "God is sovereign" to avoid the awkward conversation? The document knows the categories of failure but does not corner anyone who lives inside them.

**Weakness 3 — The Genre Problem: This Is a Study Guide, Not a Sermon**
The bullet lists, reflection questions, group discussion sections, and prayer prompts are excellent study guide features and poor sermon features. The material is pedagogically structured rather than homiletically structured. The result is that the document trains the mind well but misses the moment of direct apostolic address — the "Thus says the Lord" that lands with force and demands a response.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The author is likely unaware that the document presents the hyper-Calvinist error far more vividly and with more concrete illustration (Carey, the passive pastor) than the Arminian error. Dr. Bloom is a placeholder figure — he "means well" and his error is described in abstract terms. This asymmetry means that Reformed men reading this study are not equally confronted with the specific failure modes of their own tradition versus those of the other.

Additionally, the document does not address the women, the doubters, or the suffering in the congregation. It is explicitly a "Reformed Baptist Study for Men Walking in Truth and Grace" — which is a legitimate scope — but even within that scope, there is no address of the man who is theologically correct but spiritually dry, or the man whose election provides him with assurance rather than urgency.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Spurgeon quotations feel like the document's strongest moments. They are strategically placed and well-chosen. But they carry a risk: Spurgeon's authority is doing theological work that the author's own exposition has not done. When Spurgeon says "That God predestines, and yet that man is responsible, are two facts that few can see clearly," it feels like the tension has been resolved — but it has only been endorsed by a trusted name. The theological argument for how sovereignty and responsibility cohere remains undeveloped in the document's own voice.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Romans 11:33-36 is quoted and pointed to as the place where "doctrine is meant to land — not in a neat system, but on your knees." This is the most powerful move in the document. But it passes in two sentences. The doxology of Romans 11 — the natural culmination of the entire doctrinal argument of Romans 9–11 — deserved to be the climactic close of all three chapters, the moment where the preacher kneels the congregation before the mystery of God's sovereign grace. Instead the document ends with a summary chart and a final prayer. The doxological potential of the text was named but not entered.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (4)

1. **Anchor each chapter to a single governing text.** Ch. 17 should be driven by 1 Corinthians 4:7 as its thesis. Ch. 18 should be driven by Romans 9:14-16 as its governing argument. Ch. 19 should be driven by Romans 10:14-15 as its commissioning text. Currently, multiple texts are listed as supports; none governs. Restructure so that every point arises from and returns to the one text.

2. **Insert a gospel proclamation section in each chapter.** At minimum, Ch. 18 (on election) must explicitly state that election is "in Christ" (Eph. 1:4), that the ground of our standing before God is Christ's righteousness imputed, and that election produces assurance precisely because it rests on Christ's finished work and not our own. Without this, the doctrine of election can produce assurance that is grounded in divine decree rather than in union with Christ's person — which is a subtle but dangerous drift.

3. **Corner a specific sinner in Ch. 17.** After the list of true vs. false Calvinism, insert a direct address: "Some of you in this room have used the doctrines of grace as a reason to despise your church. You have left a congregation because they weren't Reformed enough, and you felt nothing but relief. That is not the fruit of sovereign grace — that is the flesh in a theological suit. And the doctrines you claim as your trophies condemn you." This kind of specific, searching direct address is currently absent.

4. **End Ch. 19 (and the whole document) with the Romans 11 doxology as proclamation, not as illustration.** Preach the text. Let the congregation stand inside "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God!" — not as a quotation used to close an argument, but as the place the whole study has been driving toward. The document should land the congregation on their knees, not in their notebooks.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**TRIGGERED — PARTIAL**

The document is not dead. Ch. 17's treatment of theological pride has genuine heat, and the Carey narrative in Ch. 19 has life. But the overall pattern triggers the dead orthodoxy diagnostic: theology scores high (14/18) while gospel centrality scores low (7/15). The material knows its doctrine and presents it without pastoral fire at the level the rubric requires. The correct theology is present; the cross-centered flame that should ignite it into proclamation is largely absent.

> "This material is doctrinally sound but insufficiently Christ-centered at its core. The doctrines of grace are well-defended and the dangers well-identified, but the cross of Christ — the only ground on which grace stands — is assumed rather than proclaimed. A study that explains why pride is wrong without repeatedly driving men to the blood that covers it has not yet become preaching."

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement

The document carries genuine doctrinal weight and occasional pastoral warmth, but it has not yet broken through to the eternal gravity that makes a man want to kneel — it informs and corrects where it should also wound and heal.

**Weight of Glory Bonus: +2/5**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This rubric is designed for expository sermon manuscripts. This document is a thematic men's study guide — a hybrid of commentary, discussion curriculum, and devotional material spanning three related chapters. The rubric strains at several points:

- The Berean Gate cannot be cleanly applied because there is no single governing text.
- The Sermonic Force category penalizes study-guide formatting that is entirely appropriate for this genre.
- The Cognitive Load Modifier is calibrated for a single sermon; a multi-chapter study guide is by design denser.

**The rubric is applied with these adjustments:** Genre allowances are made for structure, format, and cognitive load. The theological, gospel, and conscience categories are applied without adjustment, as they measure faithfulness regardless of genre.

**Bottom line:** This is a strong study guide. It is a weak sermon. If the intent is a Sunday proclamation, significant reconstruction is needed. If the intent is a men's group curriculum, the score should be read accordingly — and the primary concern remains: Christ must be proclaimed, not assumed, in any genre of Christian teaching.
