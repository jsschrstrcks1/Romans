1 CORINTHIANS 2 — THE POWER IS NOT IN THE RHETORIC

Read 1 Corinthians 2:1-5:

"And I, when I came to you, brothers, did not come proclaiming to you the testimony of God with lofty speech or wisdom. For I decided to know nothing among you except Jesus Christ and him crucified. And I was with you in weakness and in fear and in much trembling, and my speech and my message were not in plausible words of wisdom, but in demonstration of the Spirit and of power, so that your faith might not rest in the wisdom of men but in the power of God." (1 Corinthians 2:1-5, ESV)

Paul came to Corinth — a Greek city that prized rhetoric — and refused to play that game.

He did not come with lofty speech. He did not come with clever wisdom. He came with one thing: Jesus Christ and Him crucified. He came in weakness and fear and trembling. His words were not the words of a polished orator. They were a demonstration of the Spirit and of power.

Why did he refuse the rhetoric? So that the faith of the Corinthians would rest where it belonged. If he had won them with eloquence, their faith would have rested on his eloquence. When eloquence failed, their faith would fail. Paul wanted their faith resting on something else — on the power of God, demonstrated through the simple proclamation of Christ crucified.

A preacher who relies on his charm has built his hearers' faith on his charm. When the charm wears thin, the faith goes with it. A preacher who relies on his theological vocabulary has built his hearers' faith on his vocabulary. When the vocabulary stops impressing them, the faith stops too.

The only safe foundation is Christ crucified, plainly preached. That is what Paul refused to compromise. We should refuse the same.

1 TIMOTHY 4 — LIFE AND DOCTRINE TOGETHER

Read 1 Timothy 4:13 and 16:

"Until I come, devote yourself to the public reading of Scripture, to exhortation, to teaching... Keep a close watch on yourself and on the teaching. Persist in this, for by so doing you will save both yourself and your hearers." (1 Timothy 4:13, 16, ESV)

Notice the order in verse 13. Public reading of Scripture. Exhortation. Teaching. The Scripture is read. Then the hearers are exhorted from it. Then they are taught its content. That is the rhythm of faithful ministry.

Verse 16 is the warning against a temptation every man in ministry knows. "Keep a close watch on yourself and on the teaching." Both. Not one or the other. A man can keep a sharp eye on his teaching and let his own life become unwatched, and the result is hypocrisy — eventually exposed, often catastrophically. A man can keep a sharp eye on his life and let his teaching wander, and the result is sincere men leading their hearers into error. Both eyes have to be open.

The faithful preacher watches both. The same is true for any man who teaches the Bible to anyone — including the man who only teaches his own children. Watch your life. Watch your teaching. Persist in this.

TITUS 1:9 — HOLDING FIRM AND REBUKING ERROR

Read Titus 1:9:

"He must hold firm to the trustworthy word as taught, so that he may be able to give instruction in sound doctrine and also to rebuke those who contradict it." (Titus 1:9, ESV)

Two halves of one calling. Hold firm to the word. Then use it for two purposes: to instruct in sound doctrine, and to rebuke those who contradict it.

A preacher who instructs but cannot rebuke has not done the second half. He gives positive teaching and lets error walk freely through the back of the room. A preacher who rebukes but cannot instruct has not done the first half. He spends his time exposing error and never builds up sound understanding.

Both. Together. Tied to the trustworthy word as taught.

ACTS 20 — THE WHOLE COUNSEL OF GOD

Read Acts 20:26-27:

"Therefore I testify to you this day that I am innocent of the blood of all, for I did not shrink from declaring to you the whole counsel of God." (Acts 20:26-27, ESV)

This is Paul's farewell to the Ephesian elders. He says he is innocent of the blood of all of them — the standard from Ezekiel 3 and 33, where the watchman who fails to warn the people bears their blood on his head. Paul says he is innocent of that blood. Why? Because he did not shrink from declaring the whole counsel of God.

The whole counsel. Not the favorite parts. Not the parts that will fill the seats. Not the parts that will keep the offerings up. The whole counsel of God.

A preacher who only preaches the doctrines that flatter his Calvinism is not preaching the whole counsel. A preacher who only preaches comfort and never warning is not preaching the whole counsel. A preacher who never preaches sin clearly enough that men feel it is not preaching the whole counsel.

Paul did not shrink. The shrinking is the problem.

THE MARKS OF FAITHFUL PREACHING

Pull the seven passages together and the marks are plain.

Exposition of the text — the book is read, and the sense is given (Nehemiah 8:8).

The whole counsel of God — not the comfortable parts only (Acts 20:27).

Christ crucified at the center — not the rhetoric or the cleverness of the speaker (1 Corinthians 2:2).

Sound doctrine paired with the willingness to rebuke contradiction (Titus 1:9).

The work of a workman — exegesis is labor, and there is no shortcut (2 Timothy 2:15).

Public reading of Scripture before the exhortation, before the teaching (1 Timothy 4:13).

A life and a doctrine watched together (1 Timothy 4:16).

A man preaches faithfully when the Bible carries the sermon, when Christ is at the center, when sin is named, when the gospel is offered, when the whole counsel is in view, and when his life backs up his doctrine. Anything else dressed up as preaching is not the same thing — even if the man is a Calvinist, even if the church loves him, even if the pews are full.

CONSCIENCE APPLICATION — PART 2

Four traps press on the man who teaches the Bible — whether from a pulpit, in a small group, or at his kitchen table.

The Entertainer Trap. If the laughter carries the room and the text carries nothing, that is a problem. Humor is not forbidden. But a sermon is not a comedy set with a verse on the end. Read Nehemiah 8:8 again. Was the book read? Was the sense given? If the answer is no, the time was wasted regardless of how warm the room felt.

The Quote-Collector Trap. Puritans, reformers, and pastors of the past are witnesses, not pulpits. They lived close to the text and they help us live close to the text. But a sermon that consists of an hour of quotations has not preached. It has admired the men who preached. Read 2 Timothy 4:2 again. Preach the word. Then quote whoever helps the people understand it.

The Lecture Trap. A theological dissertation is not a sermon. The men in the room do not need to be impressed by your terminology. They need to hear the word read clearly, the sense given, sin named, Christ proclaimed, and the gospel offered. Read 1 Corinthians 2:1-2 again. Paul refused the lofty speech and chose the simple proclamation. We should refuse the same.

The Fatalism Trap — preaching aimed at no one in particular, because "God will do what God will do." This is the Reformed man's specific temptation. He stops urging the lost to come because he believes God draws His own. He stops calling the saints to obedience because he believes God preserves His own. The result is a preacher who declares true things flatly into the air and watches nothing happen. Read Acts 20:26-27 again. Paul did not shrink. The whole counsel includes the urgency. The same God who decrees the end has decreed the urgency that is the means.
