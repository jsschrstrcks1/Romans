Men's Bible Study — Quickie Study

Psalm 23 — Why the Minutia Matters

A Reformed Baptist Study for Men Walking in Truth and Grace

---

WHY THIS STUDY

Some of you love the doctrines we've been studying. Some of you are still grumbling. "Why does it matter whether the atonement was an offer or an accomplishment? Why do we need to know the difference between fore-love and foresight? Why split hairs over election and free will when we could just read the Bible and love Jesus?"

Fair enough. Tonight we're going to read the Bible. We're going to read the most beloved passage in the entire Old Testament — Psalm 23 — and I'm going to show you something. Every single line of comfort in this psalm that you love, that you quote at funerals, that you whisper in hospital rooms, that you cling to at 3 a.m. when the walls close in — every one of those lines is built on the very doctrines you've been calling minutia.

Pull the theology out, and the psalm doesn't just get weaker. It collapses.

So tonight the question isn't "Why does this doctrine matter?" The question is: "Do you want Psalm 23 to be true — or do you want it to be a nice poem?"

---

Read Psalm 23 aloud together (ESV):

> The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want.
> He makes me lie down in green pastures.
> He leads me beside still waters.
> He restores my soul.

---

LINE BY LINE — WHAT EACH COMFORT REQUIRES

---

"The LORD is MY shepherd"

That possessive pronoun — "my" — is the most theologically loaded word in the psalm. It is the word that makes everything else personal. Not "the LORD is a shepherd." Not "the LORD is the shepherd of the world in general." MY shepherd.

Where does that "my" come from?

It comes from election. It comes from the doctrine some of you find uncomfortable — that God set His love on particular people before the foundation of the world. Ephesians 1:4 — "He chose us in Him before the foundation of the world." John 10:14 — "I know my own and my own know me." The sheep are His because He chose them. Not because they wandered into the right pasture. Not because they were smarter sheep. Because the Shepherd claimed them.

If election is not true — if God did not choose you specifically — then you cannot say "my." You can only say "a." And "The LORD is a shepherd" is a theological observation. "The LORD is MY shepherd" is salvation.

That's Journey in Grace chapter 6. That's Romans 9:16 — "It depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy." That's the doctrine you said was minutia. But you need it to say the first five words of the psalm.

**Without election, you lose verse 1.**

---

"I shall not want"

This is not a promise that life will be comfortable. It is a promise that nothing essential will be missing — because the Shepherd provides for His own.

But who is this promise for? Everyone? If the atonement was a general offer — if Christ died for all people in the same way, merely making salvation available — then this promise has no teeth. It is an offer of possible provision. "You might not want, if you hold up your end."

But if Christ's death actually secured something — if the atonement accomplished redemption for His people — then "I shall not want" is a verdict, not a hope. It is the Shepherd saying, "I have already provided everything you need, because I already paid for it."

That's Journey in Grace chapter 21. That's Hebrews 9:12 — "securing an eternal redemption." That's the difference between an atonement that offers and one that accomplishes. You said that distinction was hair-splitting. But you need it for "I shall not want" to mean anything more than wishful thinking.

**Without definite atonement, "I shall not want" becomes "I might not want."**

---

"He makes me lie down in green pastures. He leads me beside still waters."

Count the pronouns. He makes. He leads. Not "He offers green pastures and hopes you find them." Not "He points to still waters and waits for you to walk there." He makes. He leads.

This is irresistible grace. This is the effectual call. The Shepherd does not suggest. He moves the sheep. He brings them where they need to be. John 6:37 — "All that the Father gives me will come to me." Not might come. Will come. The Father gives, and the Son receives. And the sheep are led — not because they chose the right pasture, but because the Shepherd's hand is on them.

That's Journey in Grace chapter 8 — "The Voice That Raises the Dead." John Murray said, "The call of God is not an invitation but a divine summons." The green pastures are not a buffet line. They are where the Shepherd carries His sheep.

If grace is not effectual — if it merely invites and then waits — then verse 2 should read: "He showed me where the green pastures were and I decided to go there." That's not what David wrote. David wrote a Shepherd who acts. Who makes. Who leads. Because the Shepherd is sovereign, and the sheep are His.

**Without effectual grace, the Shepherd becomes a tour guide.**

---

"He restores my soul"

Not "He helps me restore my soul." Not "He gives me tools for self-restoration." HE restores.

This is regeneration. This is the new birth. This is Ezekiel 36:26 — "I will give you a new heart and a new spirit I will put within you." The man whose soul needs restoring cannot restore it himself. That was the whole point of Journey in Grace chapter 11 — and the whole point of Romans 7. The wretched man cannot unwretch himself. He cries out, "Who will deliver me?" — and the answer is not "you will, with better effort." The answer is Christ.

If regeneration is the sinner's work — if the new birth is something you produce by deciding harder — then "He restores my soul" is a lie. You restored your own soul. God just watched.

But David didn't write that. David wrote a Shepherd who does what the sheep cannot do for themselves.

**Without regeneration, the sheep must restore themselves. And dead sheep can't do that.**

---

"He leads me in paths of righteousness for his name's sake"

Look at the last three words. FOR HIS NAME'S SAKE. Not for your sake. Not because you earned the right path. Not because your obedience was impressive enough to qualify. For His name's sake.

This is the glory of God in salvation. This is the truth that salvation — from first to last — is about God's honor, not yours. Romans 9:17 — "For this very purpose I have raised you up, that I might show my power in you, and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth." Romans 11:36 — "For from him and through him and to him are all things. To him be glory forever."

The paths of righteousness are not a reward for good sheep. They are God's decision to glorify His own name through the lives of people who deserved the ditch. That is not a footnote. That is the engine of the entire psalm. The Shepherd leads — and He leads for HIS name's sake — because if it depended on the sheep's worthiness, every sheep would be lost.

Some of you think the doctrines of grace make God sound cold or distant. But "for his name's sake" is actually the warmest thing in the psalm. Because it means the path doesn't depend on you. If it were "for your sake," you'd have reason to worry. You know your sake. You know how unreliable your sake is. But His name's sake? His name never fails.

**Without God's sovereign purpose, the path depends on the sheep. And sheep are terrible navigators.**

---

"Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me"

This is the line people quote when the doctor gives bad news. When the phone rings at 2 a.m. When a man is sitting in a dark room and doesn't know if he can get through another day.

"I will fear no evil."

On what basis? On what possible ground can a sinful man walk through the worst darkness imaginable and say, "I will not be afraid"?

Because "you are with me." The Shepherd is present. The Shepherd does not leave.

But does He leave? Can He leave? If your salvation depends on your continued performance — if you could lose your standing with God by sinning badly enough — then you SHOULD fear the valley. Because in the valley is where you are weakest. In the valley is where temptation is fiercest and faith is thinnest. If God's presence with you depends on your grip on Him, then the valley is exactly where you'd expect Him to let go.

But perseverance — the doctrine that God preserves His people — says He doesn't let go. John 10:28-29 — "I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand. My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all, and no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand." Romans 8:38-39 — nothing can separate us.

That's Journey in Grace chapter 9. That's the doctrine Joel Beeke summarized: "Perseverance is not the power of the Christian to hold onto Christ, but Christ holding fast to the Christian."

The man in the valley who believes in perseverance says, "I will fear no evil." The man who doesn't believe in perseverance has to say, "I hope I don't fear evil — but I'm not sure the Shepherd will still be here if I stumble."

**Without perseverance, you should be afraid in the valley. Because you're on your own in there.**

---

"You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies"

Not after the enemies are gone. Not once the battle is over. In their presence. While they watch. The Shepherd feeds His sheep in the middle of the war.

This is the confidence that comes from justification. Romans 8:33-34 — "Who shall bring any charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is to condemn? Christ Jesus is the one who died — more than that, who was raised."

The enemies are real. Sin, death, the accuser — they are present. But the table is prepared because the verdict has already been rendered. The debt is paid. The case is closed. "It is finished" — Journey in Grace chapter 21. The table is not set because the enemies are weak. The table is set because the atonement is complete.

If justification is not a finished verdict — if your standing before God is still being negotiated — then you don't get a table. You get a foxhole. You eat with one eye on the door, wondering if the enemies will take back what was never fully secured.

**Without justification, there is no table. Only a trench.**

---

"Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life"

That word — "surely." Not hopefully. Not probably. Not "if I keep it together." Surely.

And "all the days of my life." Not most days. Not the good days. Not the days when your devotional life is strong and your conscience is clean. All.

What kind of theology can produce "surely" and "all the days"? Only a theology where God's commitment to you is unconditional. Only a theology where His love is not a response to your performance but a decision rooted in His own character. Hesed — steadfast, covenant love. The love of Romans 8:35 — "Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? Shall tribulation, or distress, or persecution?" The love that Journey in Grace chapter 6 calls unconditional election.

If God's goodness toward you is conditioned on your goodness toward Him, then David is lying. You don't get "surely." You get "maybe." You don't get "all the days." You get "the days when you deserve it."

But David wrote "surely." Because David knew the Shepherd, and he knew the Shepherd's love was not earned and could not be lost.

**Without unconditional love, "surely" becomes "hopefully." And "all the days" becomes "some of them."**

---

"And I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever"

The psalm ends with forever. Not "for now." Not "until further review." Forever.

This is glorification. This is Romans 8:30 — "Those whom He justified He also glorified." Past tense — because from God's side, it's already decided. This is Journey in Grace chapter 16 — the certain end of grace. What God began in election, He will complete in glory.

If the end is uncertain — if "forever" depends on whether you finish strong enough — then the psalm ends with anxiety, not rest. "I shall dwell in the house of the LORD... I hope. If I make it."

But David didn't write that. He wrote "forever." Because the Shepherd who chose the sheep, called the sheep, justified the sheep, and preserved the sheep through the valley will bring the sheep home. The work is His from start to finish. And what He starts, He finishes.

**Without glorification, "forever" is a question mark. And you cannot rest in a question mark.**

---

SO — WHY DOES THE MINUTIA MATTER?

Because Psalm 23 is not a poem. It is a theology. And every line of comfort depends on a doctrine:

| Line | Comfort | Doctrine It Requires |
|------|---------|---------------------|
| "The LORD is MY shepherd" | Personal belonging | Election |
| "I shall not want" | Certain provision | Definite atonement |
| "He makes me... He leads me" | Sovereign care | Effectual grace |
| "He restores my soul" | Inner renewal | Regeneration |
| "For his name's sake" | God-centered security | God's sovereign purpose |
| "I will fear no evil" | Fearlessness in darkness | Perseverance of the saints |
| "You prepare a table before me" | Peace in the presence of enemies | Justification |
| "Surely... all the days" | Unwavering confidence | Unconditional love |
| "I shall dwell... forever" | Eternal home | Glorification |

Pull any one of those doctrines out, and the line it supports goes with it.

The man who says "I don't care about election — I just love Psalm 23" doesn't realize he's saying "I love the building but I don't care about the foundation." The building stands because the foundation holds. Psalm 23 comforts because the doctrines of grace are true.

That's why the minutia matters. Not because we want to win arguments. Because we want Psalm 23 to hold when the valley comes.

---

Reflection & Discovery Questions

These questions are designed to let the men arrive at the connection themselves. Don't explain the answer. Ask the question. Let the room get quiet. Let them think. The "aha" has to be theirs — not yours.

1. Read verse 1 out loud: "The LORD is MY shepherd." Now — what makes that word "my" true? Why can David say "my" instead of "a"? What had to happen before David was born for that word to be there?

2. Read verse 2: "He makes me lie down... He leads me." Who is doing the action in those sentences — the sheep or the shepherd? Now think about your own conversion. Who was doing the action — you or God? When did you first realize that?

3. Read verse 4: "I will fear no evil, for you are with me." David is in the valley of the shadow of death. That's the worst place a man can be. Why isn't he afraid? What would have to be true about the Shepherd for a man to say those words in a hospital room? What would have to be true for him NOT to be able to say them?

4. Read verse 5: "You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies." The enemies are still there. They haven't been defeated yet. So why is David eating instead of running? What has to have already happened for a man to have peace while his enemies are watching?

5. Read verse 6: "Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life." David doesn't say "hopefully." He says "surely." He doesn't say "some days." He says "all the days." What kind of love produces the word "surely"? What kind of love could only produce "hopefully"?

6. Think about the hardest season of your life. Did you lean on Psalm 23 during that time? If so — what exactly were you leaning on? What had to be true about God for the psalm to hold your weight?

---

Group Discussion — Drawing It Out

Leader's note: The goal of this discussion is NOT to lecture. It is to ask a question and then be quiet. Let the men work it out. When a man connects a line of the psalm to a doctrine — that's the moment. Don't rush past it. Let it land.

* **Exercise:** Go around the room. Each man picks one line from Psalm 23 — the line that means the most to him. Then ask him: "What has to be true about God for that line to work?" Don't supply the doctrinal term. Let him describe it in his own words. If he describes election without using the word, he owns it now.

* **Scenario:** A man is sitting with his dying wife. He opens to Psalm 23 and reads, "I will fear no evil, for you are with me." She looks at him and asks, "How do you know He's with us?" What do you tell her? Walk through what you'd actually say. — Now ask: which of the things you just said came from what we've been studying?

* **The rewrite test:** Take verse 6 and rewrite it as if God's love were conditional — as if His goodness toward you depended on your performance. What does the verse become? Read your rewrite out loud. Does it comfort you? Would you quote it at a funeral? Why not?

* **The uncomfortable question:** Have you ever loved the comfort of Psalm 23 while being annoyed by the theology behind it? What does it mean that the thing you lean on in the dark is built on the thing you argued against in the classroom?

* **Closing question:** After everything we've studied in Romans and Journey in Grace — is there a line in Psalm 23 that reads differently to you now than it did six months ago? Which one? What changed?

We have wanted the building without the foundation. Open our eyes tonight. Show us that every 'surely' in Scripture is held up by Your sovereign grace. Every 'forever' rests on Your finished work. Every 'my' depends on Your election. We need these truths — not for the classroom, but for the valley. When the shadow comes, let us stand on a psalm that holds. And let that psalm hold because the doctrines of grace are not minutia — they are the bedrock of every promise You have ever made to Your people. In Christ's name. Amen."
