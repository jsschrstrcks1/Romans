REFLECTION QUESTIONS

1. Read Daniel 9:1-3 again. Daniel knew the prophecy was certain — and he prayed for chapters anyway. Where in your life have you stopped asking God for something because you decided it was already settled?

2. Christ Himself prayed for Peter (Luke 22:32) rather than relying on a bare decree. What does that tell you about the relationship between divine certainty and your own prayers for the people you love?

3. The early church in Acts 4 confessed the most predestined event in history (the cross) and then asked for boldness. Where in the past month has your doctrine quieted rather than deepened your prayer life? Be specific. The question is not theoretical.

4. Read Ezekiel 36:36-37. God promises restoration, then tells His people to ask Him for it. How does this passage change the way you think about the relationship between God's sovereignty and your prayers?

5. Read Nehemiah 8:8. Now think about the last sermon you heard. Was the book read? Was the sense given? Did you understand the reading? If not, what was the sermon actually doing?

6. Acts 20:26-27 says Paul did not shrink from the whole counsel of God. What part of the whole counsel are you most tempted to leave out — at home, at work, in your church?

GROUP DISCUSSION

* Read Acts 4:23-31 aloud together. Walk through verse 28 first — the early church's confession that everything done to Christ was predestined. Then read what they pray for next. What does this say to the man who has used "God's sovereignty" as a reason to pray less? What does it say to the man who has never thought sovereignty and prayer belonged together at all?

* Read Romans 10:1. Paul wrote Romans 9 and then this sentence. How do you hold them together in your own prayers for the lost in your life? Where does the chain break for you?

* Read Ezekiel 36:36-37 together. God promises, then commands asking. How does this change the way you understand the relationship between God's plan and your prayer? Does it settle the question for you, or does tension remain?

* Read Nehemiah 8:8 together. Now ask the harder question. Of the preaching you have sat under in the past year, how much would pass this test? How much would not? What should we do with that, as men who love our churches but want to be honest about what we are hearing?

* Read 1 Timothy 4:16. "Keep a close watch on yourself and on the teaching." Discuss honestly: which of those two have you watched more carefully in the past year, and which have you let drift? What would change if you watched them both with equal care?

* For the man who has been theologically confident for years and has not seen new conversions in his circle in a long time — is it possible that your doctrine has become a reason to not engage rather than a reason to engage? How would you tell the difference?

* For the man who has never been sure whether he himself has been called by God — what did the gospel sound like in this study? What is keeping you from coming to Christ tonight? Talk to one of the brothers before you leave.

CLOSING REFLECTION — WHAT THIS CHAPTER TEACHES

The doctrines of grace are not tested in theological disputes. They are tested in two specific places.

The first is on the knees. A man who believes in sovereignty and does not pray has not understood sovereignty. The Bible's most sovereignty-minded men are the Bible's most prayerful men. Daniel prayed after reading the prophecy. Christ prayed for the disciple whose faith was already secured. The early church prayed after confessing predestination. Paul prayed for the salvation of those he had just argued were not saved by their own will. James commanded prayer that has great power as it is working. Ezekiel gave us the principle: God promises, then commands asking. The pattern is one pattern. There is no other Reformed pattern in the Bible.

The second test is in the pulpit. A Calvinist preacher who does not labor at exposition, who substitutes humor for the text or quotations for the sermon, who lectures instead of preaches, has not been faithful to the doctrine he claims. The doctrine demands a workman. The Bible says so plainly. The same God who decreed the end has decreed that the means is faithful preaching by faithful men.

A man who stands the two tests is a Calvinist worth listening to. A man who stands neither has the doctrine without the practice, which is no Christianity at all.

PRAYER FOCUS

"Father, the doctrine has to walk on two legs. Forgive me for the times I have used Your sovereignty as a reason to pray less, and forgive me for the times I have used my theology as a reason to labor less. Make me a praying man. Make me an honest hearer of the word. Make me a careful handler of Scripture in every place You have given me to teach. Search me, O God, and know my heart."

FINAL GROUP PRAYER

"Sovereign Lord, who made the heaven and the earth and the sea and everything in them —
You have decreed our salvation, and You have decreed the means.
You have appointed prayer. You have appointed preaching. You have appointed faithful men.
We have wanted the doctrine without the labor, and the assurance without the asking.
Forgive us for both.
Make us men who pray the way Daniel prayed and the way Paul prayed and the way the early church prayed —
who confess Your sovereignty and ask boldly anyway,
who pray for the lost by name as if it mattered, because it does.
Make us men who handle Your word with care —
reading the book, giving the sense, refusing every shortcut.
Make us preachers and teachers and fathers and friends
who are watched both in life and in doctrine,
and found faithful in both.
And for any man among us who has not yet bowed to Christ —
draw him tonight, by the same gospel that drew us all.
We ask in Jesus' name. Amen."
