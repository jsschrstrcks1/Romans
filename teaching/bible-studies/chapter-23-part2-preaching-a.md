PART 2 — IS CALVINISM THE CURE OF ALL THE ILLS OF THE CHURCH?

The Saturday conference put a different question on the table.

Two preachers in the morning failed. Two preachers in the afternoon were faithful. All four were Calvinists. The doctrine was the same in all four pulpits. The difference was not in the theology. The difference was in the obedience to what Scripture requires of a man who handles the word of God.

This is the corrective the men in the room need just as badly as Part 1's corrective. Theology is a foundation. It is not a method. It is not a personality. It is not a substitute for the work the Bible demands. A Calvinist who does not labor at exegesis, organization, and humble delivery will be as ineffective as anyone else who does not labor at those things — and possibly more harmful, because his hearers will assume his theology is a guarantee of his faithfulness when it is not.

Let the Bible itself set the marks. Then we will know what we are looking for in our pulpits and what we are looking for in our own preparation, whether we preach formally or simply teach the children at our table.

NEHEMIAH 8 — THE CENTRAL DIAGNOSTIC TEXT

Read Nehemiah 8:1-3, 8 carefully.

"And all the people gathered as one man into the square before the Water Gate. And they told Ezra the scribe to bring the Book of the Law of Moses that the LORD had commanded Israel. So Ezra the priest brought the Law before the assembly, both men and women and all who could understand what they heard, on the first day of the seventh month. And he read from it facing the square before the Water Gate from early morning until midday, in the presence of the men and the women and those who could understand. And the ears of all the people were attentive to the Book of the Law... They read from the book, from the Law of God, clearly, and they gave the sense, so that the people understood the reading." (Nehemiah 8:1-3, 8, ESV)

Verse 8 is the job description.

"They read from the book." That is the first thing. Not a meditation about the book. Not a story near the book. Not a reflection inspired by the book. The book itself, read aloud.

"From the Law of God." That is the second thing. Not from the latest periodical. Not from a celebrity quote. The Law of God. The Bible.

"Clearly." That is the third thing. Plainly enough that the people can hear it. The voice carries. The words land.

"And they gave the sense." That is the fourth thing. Not a cute observation. The sense. What the passage actually means in the sentence it is in. Exposition. The translation of the text into the understanding of the hearers.

"So that the people understood the reading." That is the goal. Not entertainment. Not admiration of the speaker. Understanding.

Test every sermon you sit under by Nehemiah 8:8. Was the book read? Was the sense given? Did the people understand the reading? If yes, it was preaching. If no, it was something else — however well dressed.

2 TIMOTHY 4 — THE CHARGE BEFORE PAUL'S DEPARTURE

Paul writes 2 Timothy from a Roman cell, knowing his time is short. The letter is his final charge to a young pastor. Read 2 Timothy 4:1-5:

"I charge you in the presence of God and of Christ Jesus, who is to judge the living and the departed, and by his appearing and his kingdom: preach the word; be ready in season and out of season; reprove, rebuke, and exhort, with complete patience and teaching. For the time is coming when people will not endure sound teaching, but having itching ears they will accumulate for themselves teachers to suit their own passions, and will turn away from listening to the truth and wander off into myths. As for you, always be sober-minded, endure suffering, do the work of an evangelist, fulfill your ministry." (2 Timothy 4:1-5, ESV)

The verb is preach. The object is the word. Not entertain. Not amuse. Not impress. Preach the word.

Notice verse 3. Paul predicts a time when the people will not endure sound teaching. They will accumulate teachers who tell them what they want to hear. They will turn from truth to myths. The remedy is not to give them what they want. The remedy is for the preacher to keep doing the work — sober-minded, enduring suffering, doing the work of an evangelist.

A preacher who substitutes humor for exposition has begun to scratch the itching ears Paul warned about. A preacher who fills an hour with quotations rather than handling the text has done the same in a different key. The fix is not to add more humor or better quotations. The fix is to preach the word.

2 TIMOTHY 2:15 — THE WORKMAN WHO HANDLES THE TEXT

Read 2 Timothy 2:15:

"Do your best to present yourself to God as one approved, a worker who has no need to be ashamed, rightly handling the word of truth." (2 Timothy 2:15, ESV)

The image is a workman. The image is labor.

A workman shows up at the worksite and does the job. He does not improvise. He does not hope his charm will carry the day. He has tools and he uses them. He has skills and he sharpens them. He has a foreman, and his work will be inspected.

"Rightly handling" the word of truth means not bending it, not twisting it, not flattening it, not extending it past what it actually says. It means cutting straight. The Greek word carries that idea — like a man laying out a road, like a stonemason cutting a block square.

Exegesis is work. There is no shortcut. A preacher who refuses the work has not been faithful, regardless of what theology he professes. A man who teaches the Bible at his own kitchen table — to his children, to his wife, to a friend over coffee — owes the same workmanship. Not the same training. Not the same length. The same care.
