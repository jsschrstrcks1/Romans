# Beautiful But Not Safe
## Genesis 1–2 (Focus: 2:5–15)

*[Part of the through-the-Bible series. Precedes "Sin Starts in the Heart" (Gen 2:15–17) and the Gen 3 sermon. Introduces the anthropic principle, the grandmother's painting illustration, and the "beautiful but not safe" framework — all of which are referenced as prior teaching in subsequent messages.]*

---

We talked last week about how the first few passages of the Bible are some that have been twisted quite a bit. Tonight we hit another big one — and I've been hearing it quite a bit lately. There is a popular preacher out there who says God created the earth once in Genesis 1, destroyed it, and then started over in Genesis 2. I hope you've been following along at home, because if you have, you'll notice that absolutely nowhere in Genesis 1 or 2 does it say "and God said oops, let's start over." In fact the opposite is true. He says of his creation — it is *very good.*

Now this part of Scripture ought to be taken literally. But there is also an element of poetry to it. In American poetry we lean heavily on meter and rhyme. Hebrew poetry works differently. One of its primary forms is chiasm — and it is woven all through Genesis 1. When you get home tonight, compare verses 1 and 4, then 2 and 5, then 3 and 6. You'll see it. The first three days were days of dividing things. The final three were days of filling those same things. God divided the light from the darkness — then filled the day and night with the sun and moon. He divided the sky from the waters — then filled them with birds and fish. He divided the dry land from the sea — then filled it with animals and man.

This tells us we serve an orderly and organized God.

There is something else at work here too — something you find in virtually every oral tradition society. They use repetition as a mnemonic. A way to help people remember. *And God said... and it was so... and God saw that it was good... and there was evening and there was morning.* That rhythm is not an accident. God created things in an orderly, step-by-step way that could be pressed into a form easy to repeat and share through oodles of generations that would follow.

That is further evidence of God's inspiration of Scripture. He knew how people worked. He knew how we would best be able to remember things in an oral tradition society, and so he told the story — and inspired the writing down of the story — in such a way that it would be easily remembered, easily passed down the generations, and thus limiting, and indeed eradicating, corruption from his holy Word. God wanted us to know things. He wrote us this love letter. He defended it. And he personally worked with the authors to ensure his meaning would come through properly and be preserved.

## Genesis 2:5–15

Up to this point everything has been created perfect. We talked last week about the anthropic principle — about how God built such intricate balance into creation that life could not only exist but thrive. For thousands and thousands of years — probably five to ten thousand years — creation ran exactly as designed.

Not only was everything created perfect, but its perfect nature reflected the image of God. Especially in man — but in nature to some extent as well.

My grandmother was an excellent painter. She will probably never be famous, but we certainly love and appreciate her work. I had already put my name on the back of a painting I liked — I was happy with that as my inheritance. But she had these other paintings of Indian children. They were her favorites. They had been fixtures in her kitchen for as long as I could remember, and I never thought of them as something to inherit — they were just part of the house.

I was talking with her one day and she was a little saddened, a little disappointed, that no one had expressed interest in the Indian kids. I wasn't aware she'd painted them. Anyway, she got a little teary-eyed and said, "I want those to go to someone special who is going to appreciate them as much as I do. I put so much of myself into those paintings, and I just want someone who will appreciate that to have them." Then she gave them to me. I was pretty floored, as you can imagine.

But I think that fits here so well. She poured so much of herself into her work. It is in a very real way a reflection of her — her gifts, her talents, her abilities, and how she viewed the world. There is so much one can learn about God from his creation. And if we had been around in Adam and Eve's day, we would have seen so much more than we see now, because creation had not yet been cursed. It was a perfect and pure reflection. Where my grandmother's reflection in her work was limited by her skill with a brush and palette, and by the medium she painted on — God had no such limitations. His reflection was perfect.

We were working on the sign for the church this week — if you'd like to see it, ask Dad, he has it on his phone. We would love to have your input. But the photo we're using was taken on a day with absolutely no wind and no waves. The entirety of Moon Lake was completely still and flat, and it reflected in a beautiful way the setting sun, the boat dock, and even the trees on the far shore.

That is how God created things to be. That was the gift he gave us, and made us stewards of.

## The Sabbath

After all of that, on the seventh day God rested. Now he wasn't tired. He didn't need to recharge his power after all that work. He rested because he wanted to set an example for us to follow.

It is easy to push the Sabbath aside and just keep going with life — fit in that extra appointment, cross one more thing off the list. But rest is important for man. Jesus himself said the Sabbath was made for man, and not man for the Sabbath.

Some people get really worked up about the Sabbath. Is it Saturday? Is it Sunday? We better make sure we have church that night. But the point is that it is *for man* — for our sake. In Middle Eastern countries, churches meet on Friday night while the mosques are having their main service. In America people usually meet on Sundays, and great — I'm glad they're meeting. But the important thing is not the name of the day. The important thing is that we take time to rest, to focus on the Lord, and to be with our families each week.

I'll admit I chuckled a little thinking through this part, because some of these denominations get so exercised about meeting or not meeting on Saturday or Sunday because it's the day of rest. But whatever day it is, it is not restful for your pastor — not for your children's workers, not for the people with responsibilities in the church. It is just not a restful day for them. So yes — take time to rest. Take time with your family. And most importantly, spend time with God every morning. It just starts the day out right. It's cleansing, washing, refreshing. You wouldn't go to work without taking a bath, would you? Then don't neglect the Scriptures — they help wash the soul. Don't routinely neglect the Sabbath either.

## Beautiful But Not Safe

I was reading about some tropical activity heading our way while I was thinking through this sermon. And it occurred to me that we can see something of God in the different things he created.

Take mountains. You can see a reflection of his majesty in them. But sometimes mountains move — and when they do, they are not very safe. Or the Grand Canyon. I have nothing to support this scientifically, but I believe it was probably formed during the flood. It is a breathtaking thing to look at. I have never been, but I would love to go someday and see it and take pictures of it for myself. Until then I have to settle for the work of other photographers. But the scope of it, the intricacy of design — there is nothing else quite like it. God created it for us to see and appreciate, to love and to know him better. But even today people literally get sick trying to explore it. People get lost. People get eaten by wildlife. Beautiful — but not exactly safe.

And the sea. The gulf we live so close to is a beautiful thing. Vast and powerful. Sometimes peaceful and still, and at other times it swallows massive ships beneath its surface. Also not safe. When a tropical storm comes, you all know exactly what I'm talking about — downed trees, downed power lines, flooding. Remember how it flooded back behind Jasmine, and nearly flooded the parsonage all those times?

God's creation is beautiful, but it is not always safe. Pretty much anywhere you live there is that tension. In Michigan or Ohio you have heavy snows that can trap you indoors. In the West there is Death Valley — a place that was not named for its safety. Mudslides, wildfires that burn down entire towns. Not to mention earthquakes, volcanoes, and tsunamis.

That brought to mind something C.S. Lewis put in *The Chronicles of Narnia*. In those stories there is a lion — Aslan — who represents Christ. He dies for the sins of mankind. At one point, one of the children asks whether Aslan is safe. Mr. Beaver answers her: *"Safe? 'Course he isn't safe. But he's good. He's the King, I tell you."*

I believe Lewis had it right. God is good. But God is not necessarily safe.

I can see the objections already. What about "my God shall supply all my needs according to his riches in glory"? What about the passage where Jesus says the birds don't know where their next meal is coming from, but your Father feeds them — and how much more will he care for you? All of that is true. Every word of it.

But listen — because I have two more points and then we're done.

## Following God Isn't Always Safe

I've told this story before, so forgive me for telling it again. But I had an aunt named Judy. She had schizophrenia. She wanted with all her heart to serve the Lord, and she committed to following his paths for her. Those paths led her down some dark roads. She was raped. Her husband left her. Her children were taken away. And her mother — my grandmother — had to have her institutionalized, which in those days meant time in a jail facility to get the mental health help she needed. Can you imagine having to do that to your own child?

It ultimately led to a diagnosis and medication that helped Judy be as normal as possible. But not so normal that she could live on her own. She had to live in a halfway house for the mentally ill. What she was allowed to do and where she was allowed to go was monitored. She lost a lot of her freedom, and a fair amount of her dignity. But she had told God — *wherever you want me to go, Lord, I will go.* Turned out he needed her to be a missionary to a mental health facility.

When Dad was cleaning out her apartment, he found bags upon bags upon bags of letters and correspondence. Because of her illness she never threw anything away. In those bags he found letter after letter from people thanking her for sharing the gospel with them. Thanking her for discipling them. For helping meet needs. For being Christ to a group of people who maybe would not have heard the gospel otherwise.

What she went through was sad from our perspective. When she stowed away on a ship bound for Europe she was not being safe. When she tried to walk across Turkey penniless and ill, she was not exactly doing something safe. But as many strange turns as her story takes, I feel certain she was in the Lord's will. When the best doctors failed her, God sent her to Europe, where she accidentally ran into a man who knew a researcher in New York on the cutting edge of schizophrenia treatment. That man gave her medicine and put her on a boat bound for New York. From knowing her, I know beyond a shadow of a doubt that she understood God was using her right where she was. I brought up the idea of her moving to Florida a few times, or living on her own — she was so close to being able to manage it. But each time she said, "No — God needs me here a while yet." She was his missionary, and on some level she knew it.

Following Christ is sometimes hard. Sometimes God allows us to go through things to shape and mold us — to better equip us for his purposes. The hows and whys may not make sense to us. They make sense to God. And while following him is not necessarily safe in this life, it guarantees our eternity. Ultimately, following God is the safest thing a person can do. In the scope of eternity, the hundred years of your life are like a breath of air — here for a moment and then gone. In the face of eternity, a trillion years is just getting warmed up.

Someone once said we are no fools when we give what we cannot keep to gain what we cannot lose. Maybe God asks you to do something hard. Maybe he asks you to stand up in front of people and speak about his Son — to your community, to your family. Maybe someone here will be called to the foreign mission field. If they take your life — if they take something you could not keep anyway — and you get to go be with your Savior, what better thing could you possibly say to him than: *I obeyed you even when it cost me dearly.* Because he did the same thing for his Father. Christ obeyed God unto death.

Praise God, he does not ask everyone to be a martyr. But maybe he will ask you to share the gospel with a Muslim, or a Jehovah's Witness, or a Catholic family member.

## Gospel Invitation

That's another way God is not safe — in sending sin to its deserved end, he is just. Maybe some of you within the sound of my voice have never come to a point in your spiritual life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins. If that's you, I want you to come talk to me when the praise team comes up.

Because even the best things we try to do apart from Christ — Scripture says they are like filthy rags. Romans 3:23 says all have sinned and fallen short of the glory of God. Not most. All. That sin separates us from God and makes us subject to his wrath.

And our God is a big God. The heavens — all of that vast, incomprehensible space — Scripture calls his footstool. We serve a big, powerful God. It is a fearful thing to fall into the hands of an angry God.

But God loves you too much to leave you there. Romans 5:8–9 says that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. We deserved death, and he chose to take our punishment. Unlike every other religion, our Savior defeated sin, death, hell, and Satan — and is fully alive, seated at the right hand of the Father. Buddha is dead. Muhammad is dead. Jesus is the only one whom death could not hold.

So if you have never asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins, come talk to me during the song.

Maybe you want to rededicate — and we had a rededication just this week. If that's you, come talk to me. Maybe you're ready to join the church and have a voice in its direction. Come talk to me.

AJ has asked to be baptized, and we are going to set up a time and place — probably at my parents' house. We will announce the time next week. If anyone else would like to be baptized and is a believer in Jesus Christ, come talk to me.

If you have anything you would like prayer for, come talk to me. And if you just want to come forward and talk to God, the altar is open.

**FLAG — C.S. Lewis attribution (CORRECTED IN MANUSCRIPT):**
The raw notes attribute the "not safe but he's good" line to Lucy. This is wrong. In *The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe* (Chapter 8), **Lucy asks the question** ("Then he isn't safe?") and **Mr. Beaver answers**: *"Safe? ... Who said anything about safe? 'Course he isn't safe. But he's good. He's the King, I tell you."* The manuscript has been corrected to attribute it to Mr. Beaver. The pastor should note this before preaching so he doesn't revert to the original in delivery — anyone who knows the book will catch it.

**FLAG — "No fool" quote (ATTRIBUTED LOOSELY IN MANUSCRIPT):**
The raw notes have: "We are no fools who give up what we cannot keep to gain what we cannot earn." The actual Jim Elliot quote (journal, October 28, 1949) is: *"He is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain that which he cannot lose."* Two differences: (1) the ending — Elliot's is "cannot lose," the raw notes have "cannot earn"; (2) Elliot's is third-person singular. Additionally, nearly identical wording appears to originate with Philip Henry (1631–1696), father of the Bible commentator Matthew Henry. The manuscript uses "Someone once said..." to avoid a misquote under attribution. If the pastor wants to attribute it directly, use the exact Elliot wording.

**FLAG — Pulsar / astronomical claim (NOT USED IN MANUSCRIPT):**
The raw notes reference "a pulsar that takes 89 million light years to travel around just once." This mixes units — light years measure distance, not time or orbital period. The claim as stated is not scientifically coherent and could not be verified. It has been removed from the manuscript and replaced with a general reference to the vastness of space ("Scripture calls his footstool"). If the pastor has a specific source for the astronomical figure, it can be reinserted with correct units and attribution.

**FLAG — Grand Canyon / flood claim:**
The pastor flags this himself in the raw notes: "I have nothing to support this." The young-earth creationist argument that the Grand Canyon was formed during the Genesis flood is a minority scientific position. The self-aware disclaimer is appropriate for a congregational setting and has been preserved in the manuscript.

The anthropic principle discussion, the grandmother's painting illustration, and the Moon Lake reflection are all cross-referenced in those later messages.
