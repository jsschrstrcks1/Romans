# The Wicket Gate
## *A Pilgrim's Progress Easter Message*

**Text:** John 10:7–11

## People Group: The Khmer of Cambodia

We start each week by lifting up a people group that needs our prayers.

Today: the Khmer people of Cambodia. You've probably heard of the Khmer Rouge. Maybe you've seen *The Killing Fields*. Cambodia was the site of one of the worst genocides of the twentieth century — between 1975 and 1979, Pol Pot's communist regime killed between one and a half and two million of their own people. A nation that has walked through that kind of darkness.

There are nearly fifteen million Khmer people. Nearly all of them are Buddhist. There are precious few workers in this part of the world sharing the gospel. Pray for the Lord of the harvest to send out workers. Pray for the workers already there — that they would be encouraged, invigorated, and sustained.

## Two Rules for Pilgrims

While you're finding it — I want to ask you something. There were two things that every pilgrim in the story had to do, or they were going to get into trouble. Who remembers what they were?

That's right. Stay on the straight path. And start at the wicket gate.

One of those is easier to explain than the other. Who can tell me what it means to stay on the King's Road? Maybe one of the kids has an answer?

Good — that's exactly right. It means to follow Jesus.

Was there ever a moment in the story when it would have been a good idea for Christian to *not* follow the King's Road? No. What happened every time someone wandered off it? Trouble. Every single time.

It's like that in our lives. As followers of Jesus Christ, when we pursue anything other than what Christ wants for us, we are opening a can of worms. We are turning down side roads that lead nowhere good.

---

## The King's Road — and What Happens When You Leave It

We are easily distracted by shiny things. We're told we have a God-given right to pursue life, liberty, and happiness. I'm not sure where they find that in Scripture. What God actually wants from us is obedience. He gives us a map and clear instructions — and we are so quick to turn right or left. To turn from Christ toward whatever catches our eye.

Christian did it twice that I want to point to.

**First** — he followed the advice of Worldly Wiseman and turned aside toward Legality Hill. He thought he could handle his burden of sin by navigating the maze of legalism. Did that help? No. He ended up more battered and bruised than before. Rocks hit him. He fell. He had to run and duck and came out in worse shape than if he had simply followed his map from the beginning.

**Second** — the King's Road had some rough patches, and along one stretch there was a low wall, and on the other side of it was a beautiful green meadow. Soft grass. No rocks. No problems, right? So they hopped the wall — and walked straight into the hands of Giant Despair and into the dungeons of Doubting Castle. Would they have ended up there if they had stayed on the King's Road? Not a chance. The giants did everything they could to grind the pilgrims down, to fill them with despair, to make them weary of living. None of that was happening on the King's Road.

Leaving it led to trouble. Every single time it was tried.

## The Green Meadow We Keep Choosing

We do this in our own lives, don't we.

Look at people who get caught up in drugs or alcohol. They are either not feeling what they want to feel, or feeling too much — and they want to control it. They want the high or the low to drown out whatever they can't face. They look at that bottle, that pill, that high — and they see a lush green meadow. What they find instead is Doubting Castle. Imprisoned by their own choices.

Some of you know exactly what that meadow looks like. You know what it cost.

We do it with other things too. With food. With sex. With the truth. People twist things in their own minds until whatever their heart desires seems better than what God has designed.

The sexual revolution of the 1960s and '70s is a clear example. As a culture we decided God's design for sex was too limiting. We wanted to write our own rules — more partners, different partners, no commitment required. Our own pleasure became the compass heading. And we have been paying for it ever since.

It has shipwrecked marriages. It has broken families. And it is our children who pay the highest price.

Roughly one in four children in America today grows up without a father in the home. According to U.S. Census data, 23 percent of all children live with one parent. And the data on what that does to kids is not kind.

Children from fatherless homes are twice as likely to drop out of high school. Seventy-five percent of adolescent patients in chemical abuse treatment centers come from fatherless homes. And the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services is plain about the darkest end of that road: 63 percent of all youth suicides — 63 percent — come from fatherless homes. Children without fathers are twice as likely to die by their own hand. Twice.

We wander off the path and tell ourselves it will be fine. Sometimes it is fine for a while. But it runs dry. It always runs dry. And it does not run dry quietly — it runs dry in our kids. The bill for the sexual revolution is in. Our children are paying it.

If you knew that something you chose to do today would cost your child their life tomorrow, would you choose it? Of course not. Yet we are discovering that it is often our children who pay the greatest price for our rebellion against God's design.

## Vanity Fair

And then there was Vanity Fair — a city with every worldly pleasure available. Food and drink, entertainment, luxury, anything you could want. The residents were furious at the idea that anything desirable might exist that their city couldn't provide.

Here's what struck me about that city: every person who lived in Vanity Fair started as a pilgrim. Every one of them knew exactly what God wanted. And they let their comforts — their security, their pleasures, their own sense of what was good — override what they knew.

Have you ever been caught doing something you knew was wrong? That's exactly what the people of Vanity Fair were doing, with full knowledge.

And time and again in the story, that led to consequences. As a parent, when you catch your child doing something they know is wrong — you correct them, don't you? Because you love them and you want them to grow. God does the same. Sometimes through life circumstances. Sometimes more directly.

*[Play clip: 1:31:00 – 1:35:35]*

When indeed, Christian. When will we learn to hold to the King's path? His rules are not arbitrary. Violating them is not without consequence. His map is straight and true, and it leads directly to his presence — which is the only place you will find true and lasting peace, and hope, and joy.

## The Wicket Gate

Now — let's talk about the wicket gate.

Would someone read John 10:7–11 for us?

> "So Jesus again said to them, 'Truly, truly, I say to you, I am the door of the sheep. All who came before me are thieves and robbers, but the sheep did not listen to them. I am the door. If anyone enters by me, he will be saved and will go in and out and find pasture. The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy. I came that they may have life and have it abundantly. I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep.'"

Any guesses what — or who — the wicket gate is?

In the film, it was a great statue of an angel on the side of a mountain, where all true pilgrims had to begin their journey. In Bunyan's book it was a narrow wicket gate. In truth — it is Christ himself.

I want you to notice something. God through the Holy Spirit had been working on Christian long before he ever reached that gate. He was working on him from the moment Christian felt the weight of his burden. He was working through the Evangelist along the road. All the way to the cross, where the burden finally fell off Christian's back and he was free. That road begins with meeting Christ. There is not a single moment in a person's life more important than the moment they come to faith in Jesus Christ.

Charles Spurgeon — I quote him most weeks for those of you who've been here a while; for those of you who haven't, Spurgeon was likely the greatest preacher of the nineteenth century, a man God used to shape the thinking of an entire generation — Spurgeon wrote this in his devotional *Morning and Evening*:

> "If Christ be anything, He must be everything. O rest not till love and faith in Jesus be the master passions of your soul."

We can do no better than to be in the center of God's will. That doesn't mean we won't encounter trouble. We all encounter trouble. David's elevator is making frightening sounds, and the elevator company has not been responsive. Imagine waking up tomorrow to find your independence threatened — your freedom at risk. That is essentially where David is. But he has peace, because he knows who is in control. He has comfort, because he knows God will provide a way. He may have some nerves along the way — that's normal. Christian was afraid of the lions, too. But he pressed on.

All of it begins with a relationship with Jesus Christ.

Going to church is a good thing. It is not enough on its own. Being a good person is a good thing. It is not enough. I am grateful for every dollar you give — genuinely grateful. But when you stand before God, he will not ask about your checkbook. He will ask about your relationship with his Son.

If you have that relationship, you have nothing to fear. If you don't — you have no hope. Scripture is clear that no amount of good deeds can overcome even a single sin. Safe to say we have all committed more than one.

## The Gospel

Let's look at the text.

Romans 3:23:

> "For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."

Children know how to sin without being taught. Nobody shows a child how to lie or how to sneak an extra cookie. We are born with hearts that love what we shouldn't — bent toward sin from the beginning. It's like being covered in mud from the Slough of Despond at the start of the film.

Romans 6:23:

> "For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord."

Because our hearts are tainted by sin, and because that overflows into everything we think and do, what we have earned is death. Not just a physical death — but an eternity separated from God, paying for our crimes. Sin separates us from God. Even one sin is enough. And we have all committed more than one.

That's the bad news. Here is the good news.

Two thousand years ago, God sent his Son to die in our place. We deserved death and hell. He took our place. As a result, what was coming to us has been absorbed by him, and what he earned is offered to us as a free gift.

John 3:16:

> "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."

This is the heart of Easter. Christ died. Thousands watched. The soldiers who carried out crucifixions forfeited their own lives if they failed — they had every reason to finish what they started. He was buried. More than five hundred people saw him alive afterward. Five hundred witnesses. Who else in human history has done that? Who else has come back from the other side to tell us the way? No one.

And we cannot earn what he offers. Ephesians 2:8–9:

> "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast."

None of us earned it. None of us are capable of earning it. It is entirely a gift.

Romans 10:9:

> "Because, if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."

If you have never come to that point — come find me before you leave today.

## Editorial Notes
*(Production reference — not for delivery)*

**Khmer death toll**: Oral delivery said "over one million." Historical consensus is 1.5–2 million. Corrected to reflect scholarly range.

**Suicide and fatherlessness statistics**: The oral delivery included two figures that could not be used as stated: (1) "half of all children from broken homes commit suicide" — mathematically impossible given prevalence rates; (2) "90% of those who commit suicide come from broken homes" — not supported by published research. Both were replaced with verified figures from the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services and Census Bureau research: 63% of youth suicides involve fatherless homes; children from fatherless homes are twice as likely to die by suicide. Source: [America First Policy Institute — Fatherlessness Brief](https://www.americafirstpolicy.com/issues/issue-brief-fatherlessness-and-its-effects-on-american-society); [Columbia University School of Social Work](https://socialwork.columbia.edu/news/adult-children-divorce-show-greater-risk-suicide-study).

**Divorce prevalence**: Oral delivery cited "1 in 2 kids from broken homes." U.S. Census data (2019, Pew Research) places the figure at 23% of children living with a single parent. The higher figure may reflect the cumulative divorce rate rather than current household composition. Language adjusted to use Census-sourced figures.

**Specific divorce outcome percentages** (35% health outcomes, 48% drug/alcohol): These figures circulate in secondary sources but vary significantly by primary study. Replaced with verified figures: 75% of adolescent chemical abuse patients from fatherless homes (National Fatherhood Initiative / Columbia University research); twice as likely to drop out of high school (widely replicated).

**Scripture corrections from oral delivery**:
- Romans 3:23: "fallen short" → ESV "fall short" (present tense)
- John 3:16: "his one and only son" (NIV) / "whosoever shall believe" (KJV) → ESV "his only Son" / "whoever believes"

**Spurgeon quote confirmed**: *Morning and Evening*, Evening June 28 (Exodus 7:12). Also in sermon "The Power of Aaron's Rod" (spurgeon.org).
