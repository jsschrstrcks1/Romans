Acts 13:1-3
Intro – were switching things up a little bit this week. We had been in Joshua, and took a break from Joshua to talk about Persecution in the 2/3s world, and this week were going to continue that break and look at acts 13. This is an interesting chapter, and fitting I think, because it focuses on the very beginning of Pauls ministry. Right at the beginning of the chapter – read that later if you like – Paul and Barnabus are commissioned by the church at Antioch to be missionaries sent out to the rest of the known world. We talked about Antioch a few months back, and you may remember that these same people that are commissioning Paul, had been terrorized and persecuted by Paul not all that long ago. So it's a huge deal for this church. It might be like Obama coming to our church, having been led to the Lord and discipled by Billy Graham. If Obama had hunted you and tried to kill you. The fact they even let him into the church is a wonder, really, because of the depths and lengths that Paul had gone through to try and persecute the church in Jerusalem. You'll remember we covered the stoning of Stephen last year, and we talked about how Paul stood in a position of honor while they unceremoniously murdered him. Stoning's were a big deal, you know my little cousin Augie likes to pick up rocks and toss them into the canal behind my parents' house. He always wants to find the biggest rocks he can and make the biggest splash he can. AJ a few weeks ago tossed a concrete bag shaped piece of concrete in to make the biggest splash possible, and Augie just adored it. His face was beaming. He was so happy. I kind of think he wanted his dad to go get the rock out of the water and throw it in again. But like Augie, these people when they would stone someone wouldn't pick up little tiny rocks, they would go for the biggest rock they could carry, and smash it down with murderous intentions. They weren't throwing pea gravel. I bring this up to point out that at this time and in this place dangers abounded. Not only were the Jews hunting Christians, at various points Romans were hunting Christians. You'll remember that Nero Persecuted Christians – even impaling some upon pointed sticks to light the streets of Rome. Others were worse than Nero – after Nero, the empire was kind of unstable for a while. They had four different emperors in a single year. Then Vespasian built the colosseum, but was followed by Titus who destroyed the temple in Jerusalem, and then by the infamous Domitian who was most remembered for his persecution of Christians.

Point is, at various points during Paul's life going outside as a believer in Jesus Christ was Dangerous. So in being commissioned as a missionary he was writing a blank check with his life. You know what I mean by that, a check with your account information and signed by you, with the dollar amount left blank. Even if it cost Paul his life he was willing to go, and do, and say whatever God wanted him to go and do and say, wherever God called him to be.
Lets read acts 13:1-3
I love how it describes the process. They were worshipping praying and fasting together and the holy spirit said. I mean maybe they were singing power in the blood, and God said "set apart paul and barnabus and send them out". Pauls life kind of reminds me of reminds me of William Borden – you guys might have heard this before, forgive me if so. But Borden was wealthy beyond most of our wildest imaginations slightly over 100 years ago. Borden you might remember is a dairy brand. His family owned the brand, thousands of heads of cattle, and tens of thousands of acres that they worked to make todays equivalent of billions of dollars. So any way, for Bordens 16th Birthday his parents sent him on a trip around the world by boat. He traveled all over asia, the middle east, Russia, Europe. He was sent to every capital city of note and expected to live in opulence absorbing all of the culture each place had to offer for as long as he wanted to stay. However rather than opulent luxury he was Struck by the poverty – heart broken he wrote in his diary. He came back to the states humbled by how he lived in comparison to the rest of the world. Many of whom would go to bed without a meal. Any way, shortly there after the Lord got ahold of William Borden. He gave his life to the Lord, went to Yale – which at the time was fairly well into its decline from the sterling seminary it had been. But the Lord used him even there, he planted a church amongst his peers that within three years had swelled to over 300 students. By graduation there wasn't a student at the school that hadn't been touched by Bordens ministry. The calling of the Lord was quite evident in the life of William Borden. That call cemented in him as a sophomore in college. He felt the Lord calling him back to China, to a muslim people group. Once he was certain of the Lords calling his iron will set its sights on china no matter the cost. He wrote that day in the margin of his bible "No retreats". When his wealthy father heard of his sons decision, he threatened to cut off young borden from his family treasure. Borden told his father to do what he must, but that "as for me, I will serve the Lord". That day in his Bible young Borden – only 24 years old wrote no reserves. The day after graduation he hopped a ship sailing for Egypt where he planned to learn Arabic, and then board another ship bound for China. However within days of arrival he contracted spinal meningitis, and within the month he had died. His effects were shipped back to the states, and underneath the words no retreats and no regrets in his bible, he had written a third phrase – no regrets and dated it may 19th 2013 – which was the day he was told he would not recover from his illness.

Borden had written a blank check with his life. The people we watched last week, the believers living in those extreme situations have written blank checks with their lives, and Paul here, has written a blank check with his life. One of my favorite preachers – David Platt who has several young children including one he and his wife have adopted from over seas, said "you don't fully trust God until you can trust him with your kids". Let that sink in a minute, he says you don't fully trust God until you can trust him with your kids. The first lecture of my first class in the missions program at Trinity the professor said you need to learn to count the costs. He pointed out that most of the evangelism left to do in the world was in Muslim countries, and that being a Christian in certain areas in some of these countries was a good way to be killed. He told us that some day the cost of following God may be my life. I had never thought of it in those terms… I went home from school really contemplative that day. But I came to the decision that I would follow God even if it cost me my life. A few years back at a missions training event I was attending they made us run through these simulations, these scenarios. They were trying to make us fail as a team. In one of the scenarios one of the team members was kidnapped, and they would deliver us a bloody looking finger every few minutes until we declared before the world that we weren't really missionaries that we were cia spies or else we must give them 400,000$ and we would get our friend back.

Until that point it had never occurred to me that following Jesus could cost me a friend, a spouse should I have one one day, or the life of my child. This was yet another sobering day for me. I could follow the Lord if it cost me my life sure, but what about the lives of my family? My friends? Ultimately God gave me a peace on the issue. But it was something I wrestled with for the rest of the event. If God calls you to do something, he will give you the strength you need to get through it. Besides that, if my loved one dies, and I know they are Christians, I know where they are going to go when they die. I know I will get to see them again one day. I know that death isn't good bye, they just got to run on ahead a little bit.

Flip over to acts 20:21 with me for a moment. I want to drive home this idea that paul saw his life as a blank check. 21 testifying both to Jews and to Greeks of repentance toward God and of faith in our Lord Jesus Christ. 22 And now, behold, I am going to Jerusalem, constrained by the Spirit, not knowing what will happen to me there, 23 except that the Holy Spirit testifies to me in every city that imprisonment and afflictions await me. 24 But I do not account my life of any value nor as precious to myself, if only I may finish my course and the ministry that I received from the Lord Jesus, to testify to the gospel of the grace of God.
Vs 24 I think hits the nail on the head – he doesn't value his own life, he doesn't consider it precious to himself. He's come to the conclusion that if God wants him to go to Jerusalem, even though he knows they are looking for him there, even though he's been told by the disciples not to, even though a prophet named Agabus tells him in ch 21 that he will be imprisoned, Paul still feels led of God to go to Jerusalem. He definitely went to prison, but while in prison he wrote a good chunk of the new testament.
So then the first point is that as Christians the example of scripture, and the example of those Christians who've gone before us is to live life as a blank check for God. There have been people called into missions by God at every stage of their life. Young people, and the elderly. I met a man in Haiti who was 98 years old, and still serving the Lord as an evangelist in that country. Sometimes what we are called to do might not be easy, and sometimes isn't fun. The cost may be high, but the rewards are eternal. Besides that, being in the center of the Lords will for your life is the safest place you could possibly be.

I read a book recently that talked about a man and his family who were missionaries to Mogadishu. He spent 13 years there, and after 13 years his son had an asthma attack and died. The missionary and his family left the country and returned to the united states feeling defeated. They felt like they had met the somali's physical needs, they had been feeding tens of thousands of Somalis every day. But had never adequately met their spiritual needs. In fact when this man and his family left the country, they were the last known believers in the country, and when they left, there were none left that anyone knew about.
But after their sons funeral, and after they returned to the US, they got letters, and phone calls. They heard from people that had been deeply impacted by their son for the Gospel. They had maybe failed to tell everyone they encountered about Christ, but their son had not. He'd been quite the little evangelist. So many seeds were planted amongst the children of Somalia that their parents began writing letters to this man en masse demanding to know why they hadn't been told more about this Jesus!
This family had written a blank check to God. It cost them the life of their son. But isn't that a familiar story? We know that God sent his son to us, to die on the cross for us. Crucifixion is one of the most barbaric and bloody ways humans have ever invented to end the life of another human. And yet it wasn't just the crucifixion that Christ endured. He was scourged with the cat of 9 tales, he was mocked, beaten, humiliated, and then he was crucified. And with one of his final breaths what does he cry out but "father forgive them for they know not what they've done.
Paul said in Philipians 3, and bearing in mind this is one of the prison epistles, this was something he wrote shortly after being arrested and taken to jail for following the Lords will. Paul said "7But whatever gain I had, I counted as loss for the sake of Christ. 8Indeed, I count everything as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord. For his sake I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as rubbish, in order that I may gain Christ" and down a few verses he continues "that I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his sufferings, becoming like him in his death, 11that by any means possible I may attain the resurrection from the dead." Paul was literally praising God not because his damp prison cell was comfortable, but because God is good. Because he was able to share in the sufferings of our savior. Because the only thing he still had was the only thing that matters and that was assurance of his salvation through Christ. Whatever Paul went through, whatever trials he endoured he never went through anything more difficult than God had asked Christ to endure. Praise God that God doesn't ask us to walk down any roads he wasn't willing to walk down himself.
Maybe you're here tonight and you don't have that hope, and joy that comes from having a relationship with Christ.
