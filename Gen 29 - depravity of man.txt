Genesis 29:3–15 — The Depravity of Man


We've all heard this story before. But my thinking kept circling back to a subject that might raise a few eyebrows. No psychological profiling here, no glass-half-full tricks, and no need to raise your hand — but how many of you would say the Bible teaches that man is basically good? We're going to talk about that deeply tonight. First, let's read the story of Laban and Jacob.

While you're turning there, let me catch you up on the last couple of chapters. When Esau heard his father favored women from Laban's line, he decided to marry a half-sister rather than a Canaanite — not his first or second wife. Maybe he was starting a collection. We don't know, just that he had many. Jacob dreams about a staircase to heaven, and then in verse 3 he encounters some of Laban's shepherds around a well. The well was artesian — a constant flow of water out of the ground — and so they would cap it with a massive stone to hold the water in. When the stone was moved, water flowed out into troughs dug into the ground around it, and the livestock could drink. A stone like a millstone: the kind you've seen on TV if not in person — a flat round stone the size of a dinner table, several feet thick, solid. An enormous weight. Two or more men would ordinarily be needed to move it. Now let's read.

Genesis 29:3–15

To finish the story: Laban and Jacob agree that Jacob will work seven years in exchange for Rachel's hand in marriage. Her bride price was his labor. Jacob worked faithfully for seven years, and at the end of it, using a garment and darkness, Laban married Jacob off to Leah instead. Shortly after the wedding Jacob figured out what had happened. Scripture describes Leah as having weak eyes — some say she had trouble seeing, some say it's a play on words, that she may have been cross-eyed or had some condition that was considered unattractive in that culture. Leah would have been aware of the deception, and in my view she is as much a victim as Jacob. He didn't love her, didn't want her, and woke up disappointed next to her. Jacob was still in love with Rachel, so he went back to Laban and worked out another agreement — seven more years for Rachel's hand. At the end of those seven years Laban honored his promise, Jacob married Rachel, and they got out of town with the sheep Laban had given them.


I mentioned the depravity of man. Let's look at Laban first.

When Jacob's grandfather Abraham sent his servant to find a wife for Isaac, Laban was transfixed by the wealth he saw — hundreds of millions of dollars in treasure, silks, silver, gold. He was so taken with it that he was willing to sell his daughter off before she had ever been consulted. This time, when Jacob arrives, Laban is taken with something different: Jacob's strength. He moved that stone alone — a stone ordinarily requiring two or more men. When Laban sees what Jacob can do, his first thought is not "Does God want me to allow my daughter to marry my grandson?" His first thought is how to exploit his grandson most efficiently. He had money. What he apparently lacked was strong labor. So when he hears what Jacob has done at the well, and sees Jacob for himself, he agrees to the plan immediately.

In the culture of the day, a daughter you couldn't marry off was a liability. When she married, you received bride price. Laban received a fortune for Rebekah. Now he has two more daughters to settle — one of them with a condition that had apparently kept the suitors away. No line of men at his door trying to make a match. So Laban deceives Jacob the same way Jacob deceived his own father: he used darkness — Isaac's eyes were dim — and thick garments to disguise one person as another.

It struck me hard how true the words of Galatians 6:7 are: do not be deceived, God will not be mocked — you reap what you sow. Jacob sowed deceit. He was tricky with his brother, lied to his father, and manipulated things with his mother's help so that he inherited the bulk of his father's estate and the blessing meant for Esau. Now, years later, the harvest comes in. He sowed deceit, and in the fullness of time it ripened. Laban lied and cheated him not only out of the woman he loved but out of fourteen years of his life — and that's before we get into how Laban manipulated the sheep agreement.

Theologians call this innate condition depravity. Is man basically good, or basically evil? Laban was an evil man. He put his own comfort, desires, and wealth above the lives of the people in his own family — including the daughter he received money for and committed to travel with a stranger she had never met, and apparently as an afterthought consulted about it afterward.


But this passage isn't the only one that exposes the true nature of the human heart. John Piper writes: "There is no doubt that man could perform more evil acts toward his fellow man than he does. But if he is restrained from performing more evil acts by motives that are not owing to his glad submission to God, then even his 'virtue' is evil in the sight of God."

Let's turn there. Romans 14:23: "Whatever does not proceed from faith is sin."

This is a radical indictment of all natural virtue that does not flow from a heart humbly relying on God's grace.

Bill Gates and his wife Melinda have the laudable goal of giving away all of their money before they die. I imagine their kids have opinions about that. But here is the thing — no matter how much good their money does, if they are not believers in Jesus Christ, it is sin. The ten most generous people in the world by dollar amount have together given something like twenty billion dollars in a single year. They helped people in Haiti, built schools in Africa, dug wells, tried to help the environment, gave to causes they believed in. But apart from Christ they may as well have set it on fire. Whatever does not proceed from faith is sin.

That means if Mother Teresa was not a believer in Jesus Christ, all of her sacrifice was for nothing — not in terms of its effect on others, but before God it was sin. Maybe the motivations were mixed. Maybe there were other things only God knows. But what we can take to the bank is this: anything we do apart from faith in Christ is sin. Say it with me. "Whatever does not proceed from faith is sin." "Whatever does not proceed from faith is sin." "Whatever does not proceed from faith is sin." Romans 14:23.


There are four things I want you to remember about the depravity of man.


First: our rebellion against God is total.

Apart from God's direct action in our lives, apart from his grace, there is no delight in God, no willing submission to his sovereign authority. Piper says: "Totally depraved men can be very religious and very philanthropic. They can pray and give alms and fast, as Jesus said (Matthew 6:1-18). But their very religion is rebellion against the rights of their Creator, if it does not come from a childlike heart of trust in the free grace of God. Religion is one of the chief ways that man conceals his unwillingness to forsake self-reliance and bank all his hopes on the unmerited mercy of God (Luke 18:9-14; Colossians 2:20-23)."

The totality of our rebellion is seen in Romans 3:9-10 and 18: "I have already charged that all men, both Jews and Greeks, are under the power of sin, as it is written: None is righteous, no not one; no one seeks for God.... There is no fear of God before their eyes."

While man does seek to fill the void in his heart, he is not by nature seeking God. John 3:20-21: "Everyone who does evil hates the light, and does not come to the light, lest his deeds should be exposed." Only those already in the light can seek the light — and that light is salvation in Christ.


Second: in our total rebellion, everything we do is sin.

Paul says in Romans 7:18 that apart from Christ, all a man can think and feel and do is bound to sin. Outward conformity to the created order is not "goodness" when measured against God. It is good that a man does not murder his neighbor — but refraining from murder is not the same as choosing to follow the Lord. That restraint is more a tacit obedience to the nature God designed for humans to have, a nature that was corrupted in Adam's fall. It is not righteousness.


Third: our inability to submit to God is total and complete.

Romans 8:7-8: "For the mind that is set on the flesh is hostile to God; it does not submit to God's law, indeed it cannot; and those who are in the flesh cannot please God." A mind set on the flesh is a mind not yet redeemed by Christ. Apart from Christ our minds are completely enslaved to sin. Man cannot reform himself.

Ephesians 2:1 says we are dead in our trespasses and sins. When you are dead you are incapable of life. A corpse cannot reanimate itself. Apart from Christ we are like stone — hard and closed off in our natural inclinations toward God. That is why Ephesians 2:8-9 says, "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast." Ezekiel says our hearts are blind and incapable of seeing God. Grace is not the assist — grace is the whole thing.


Fourth: our rebellion is deserving of eternal punishment.

Ephesians 2:3 — we are by nature children of wrath. God created everything perfect. We sinned and shattered it. We became warped in a way that reproduces in every generation. As a result we have earned and deserve the wrath of God. Romans 6:23 — we deserve death. We deserve eternal separation from God. The corruption of our hearts has made us as good as dead to him.

Piper writes: "The reality of hell is God's clear indictment of the infiniteness of our guilt. If our corruption were not deserving of an eternal punishment, God would be unjust to threaten us with a punishment so severe as eternal torment. But the Scriptures teach that God is just in condemning unbelievers to eternal hell (2 Thessalonians 1:6-9; Matthew 5:29f; 10:28; 13:49f; 18:8f; 25:46; Revelation 14:9-11; 20:10). Therefore, to the extent that hell is a total sentence of condemnation, to that extent must we think of ourselves as totally blameworthy apart from the saving grace of God."


In summary: our rebellion against God is total. Everything we do in that rebellion is sin. Our inability to submit to God or reform ourselves is total. And we are therefore totally deserving of eternal punishment.

If you have never come to a point in your life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sin, then everything we've talked about tonight has been about you. Perfectly, purely, totally corrupt — incapable of doing any good in God's eyes — and as a result bound for an eternity separated from God. That is the bad news. The good news is that God planned around our sin. He knew we would miss it, and so he made a way for us to spend an eternity with him. That way is Christ.
