# Worry Is Lack of Faith
## Philippians 4:4–7 / Matthew 6:25–34

---

Good morning, welcome to Lakeview Fellowship. I hope you've brought your Bibles this morning. Open with us to Philippians chapter 4, verses 6 and 7.

I want to talk about worry. And I want to make a distinction up front, because these two things get lumped together and they're not the same thing.

Stephen Davey defines worry as lack of faith in God. That's an internal thing. It's up here, in your head, and down here, in your heart. Anxiety is the body's physical expression of the worry in your mind. When your heart says *God can't take care of this* — that's worry. And that worry produces anxiety in your body. They're related. They're not identical.

Now. I'll be honest with you. I'm a worrier.

I try not to let it out too much in the middle of things. You have to lead with confidence. But inside my head it's a little like the locker room after a football game. *Evans, you needed to be a half-step faster. Brady, if you'd looked a little east you'd have seen Gronk open — that was a touchdown.* I go through things play by play. Good football teams — even when they win — get corrected by the coach. So I worry. I revisit. I try to prevent the same problems from coming back around.

I've heard people say worry is necessary. If we don't worry, we repeat our failures. We create the same problems over and over. No worry, doom yourself before you start.

But here's where that argument falls apart.

Worry is lack of faith in God.

The moment we make a case for the *necessity* of worry, we're really arguing that there are situations where it's reasonable to stop trusting God. And I know you people. I know some of you do exactly that. Men and women both. Moms especially.

I'm going to tell a story on my mom real quick. I don't think she'll mind — it's typical of mothers, and it's typical of how we fail in this area.

My sister Ellisha called her one afternoon on the way home from work. Mom asked what she had planned. Ellisha started to answer — but the phone cut out. What Ellisha actually said was: *oh, you know, the normal things — grocery shopping, pick up laundry soap, then laundry, and then I'll come by with some hors d'oeuvres before dinner, I'll see you around seven.* Mom didn't hear all that. She just knew Ellisha got off work at five, and if everything was fine with no stops, she could be home by six.

By six-fifteen, mom had convinced herself that Ellisha was dead on the side of the road. Not just dead — burned to death in a massive fireball of a car wreck, with the vultures picking her bones clean. Ten calls later. Twenty. Fifty. And by six-thirty mom was in the car, driving over to Ellisha's house to look for her.

Ellisha was at the grocery store. Completely oblivious.

We worry about the things and people we love. Of course we do. But in that moment, that worry says something to God. It says: *you are not capable of taking care of this person.* That's where it becomes sin. That's the line.

I could see on my dad's face he was thinking about the Scripture that says the battle is the Lord's — but prepare your horses anyway. Yes. That's right. When you're facing something that could make you worry, act. Prepare. Do what you can do. But there is a wall past which none of us can go. And past that wall, the old pastoral saying holds: *until you have prayed, you can do no more.* To do less than give that worry to God — to let it consume you instead — is to say: *I know better. I can handle this better than you can.* Or more simply: *I don't trust you. Not with this, God.*

---

Now. We all know we're broken people.

Is there anyone here who has it all together? Raise your hand if you do.

Nobody.

If we're honest, we can barely manage the small radius of things right around us. We're probably better at being the Monday-morning quarterback in our own lives — after the game is over, saying *coulda, shoulda, woulda, maybe we'd have won* — than we are in the moment.

We had a situation the other night. You guys know we have sheep and we have dogs. Prey animals and predators. Dogs can eventually be trained to do about anything. But puppies don't come with the full set of instructions. It takes them a while to understand you don't want them eating your other animals.

So Wednesday night I'm at dinner with some of the guys. I get a text message. Voice to text — you know how well that works. What I received said: *all the dogs are loose and gone, all the sheep are in the canal, I'm leaving now, Greg's in a tent.* I hop up, grab my bill, grab my leftovers, and take off. I get partway home, finally reach one of the kids on the phone — and they tell me everything is fine. Only two sheep got out. One of them is already on the kayak. One dog was loose but she's tied up now. No worries.

Under those terms, based on my understanding of the situation, I turned around and went back to dinner.

Come to find out — two of three dogs were out, one got out twice, half the sheep were in the canal at two different points, and they really needed me. But I was finite. I was operating on imperfect information from a limited source. Like a ten-year-old child giving you a situation report.

That's every single one of us, compared to God.

Let's read.

> Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice. Let your reasonableness be known to everyone. The Lord is at hand; do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus.
> — Philippians 4:4–7 (ESV)

---

I love that it starts with a command to rejoice.

In Greek — as in many languages — repeating a word adds weight. Kids do it in English today. If your child says *I'm really really really really hungry*, you know they see this as urgent. They need to eat right now. Paul is doing that. He's not making a request. In the Greek it's a command. He commands it twice in a row to press the full weight of it.

Rejoice. But not in earthly things. Earthly joys are fleeting and easily ruined. Your new truck can break down. Your puppy can get run over. Your husband or wife can let you down. These things happen — we've all seen them happen. Joy anchored here is fragile. It's a dim picture of the joy to come. So when anxiety starts closing in, the command is to refocus — not on the circumstances, but on the Lord. The eternal source of joy who cannot break down, cannot disappoint, cannot be taken from you.

I've had to remind myself of this again recently: guard your heart with thanksgiving. It leads to gladness. This isn't a suggestion.

Paul knows exactly what he's commanding. Elsewhere in his letters he goes through the list — shipwrecked, snake-bitten, stoned and left for dead, escaped over a city wall at night in a basket. He wrote this letter from a prison cell in Rome. He knows what it costs to rejoice when your health is failing, when every circumstance conspires against you. He's done it. When he commands it, he's not commanding it from somewhere safe.

---

What gets us into trouble is when we turn inward.

Someone called it navel gazing — that moment when everything narrows to *what I want, what I think, especially what I feel.* I had this conversation with a cousin this week. This cousin had said, in effect: you think you've made things hard for me, but just you wait — God is going to make it right, and then you'll see.

Here's the thing. There are times when God allows an injustice to go on for an entire lifetime. Think about slavery. Many people were born slaves, lived as slaves, and died as slaves. The injustice was never resolved in their lifetime. God was doing something in them, and in the people around them, that they couldn't see. Tertullian said the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church. I fully believe the tears of the Christian help water that seed. Even when we cannot understand what God is doing, we know he is doing something. And we can trust him.

We just have to lift our eyes up, off ourselves, and put them back on Christ.

I think about the songs sung by enslaved people as they worked. Unspeakable conditions. And they could still sing with real joy about the day they would leave this earth and be with Christ. *Swing Low, Sweet Chariot* is all about that. This life is not all there is. This life is not what we live for. This life is a boot camp for eternity. And God commands us to rejoice, and not to be anxious about anything — and then he tells us what to do instead. Refocus on the Lord. Take it to God.

---

What do we do after that? Do we sit in our own heads and fret and fuss?

There is a lot to be concerned about right now. The coronavirus. The vaccine. The direction of our nation away from God. Financial concerns. Health concerns. Concerns about family and loved ones.

So when you're afraid because of COVID — what should you do? Be anxious about nothing. Take your requests to God through prayer and supplication with thanksgiving. Verse 6.

When you're afraid of the vaccine — what should you do? Be anxious about nothing. Take your requests to God through prayer and supplication with thanksgiving. Verse 6.

When you're worried about your child who is struggling and hurting — Be anxious about nothing. Take your requests to God through prayer and supplication with thanksgiving. Verse 6.

When you get the cancer diagnosis — that is a horrible diagnosis. But what should you do? Be anxious about nothing. Take your requests to God through prayer and supplication with thanksgiving. Verse 6.

When it seems like the world is winning in the government, and immoral things are becoming law — Be anxious about nothing. Verse 6.

When your finances are in trouble and you don't know where the money is coming from — Be anxious about nothing. Verse 6.

When you're around someone who won't wear a mask — Be anxious about nothing. Verse 6.

When you actually catch COVID — Be anxious about nothing. Verse 6.

When there's sabre-rattling from Iran or somewhere in the Middle East keeping you up at night — what should you do? Be anxious about nothing. Take your requests to God through prayer and supplication with thanksgiving. Verse 6.

Nowhere in Scripture does it say: *be anxious about nothing, except, you know, when you might actually get sick.* There is no footnote. There is no exception. Scripture says so.

---

Someone read verse 7 for me. Nice and loud for Facebook.

God will give you peace. He will guard your heart. You will be set free from the bondage of worry through the gospel. Not a slave to fear and anxiety — free in Christ. Because for you and me, if we die?

So what.

It only gets better from here.

For the Christian, this world is the only hell we will ever experience. This is the worst it gets. Even if you've had a relatively good life, this is the floor. Eternity is better. And it lasts incomparably longer.

Paul says it in Philippians 1:21 — *to live is Christ, and to die is gain.* To be absent from the body is to be present with the Lord. When we take that last breath, the very next instant we are in the presence of our Savior. I think that moment will be so glorious — seeing Christ in glory, seated at the right hand of the Father — that we won't need eleven billion years for the sadnesses and losses and disappointments of this life to lose their sting. I think in that first instant they will be washed away. Like dirt being hosed off.

We have to live with eternity in view. We have to live our lives focused on our Savior. Faith, not fear.

Turn with me to Matthew chapter 6, verses 25 through 34.

> Therefore I tell you, do not be anxious about your life, what you will eat or what you will drink, nor about your body, what you will put on. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothing? Look at the birds of the air: they neither sow nor reap nor gather into barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not of more value than they? And which of you by being anxious can add a single hour to his span of life? And why are you anxious about clothing? Consider the lilies of the field, how they grow: they neither toil nor spin, yet I tell you, even Solomon in all his glory was not arrayed like one of these. But if God so clothes the grass of the field, which today is alive and tomorrow is thrown into the oven, will he not much more clothe you, O you of little faith? Therefore do not be anxious, saying, 'What shall we eat?' or 'What shall we drink?' or 'What shall we wear?' For the Gentiles seek after all these things, and your heavenly Father knows that you need them all. But seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you. Therefore do not be anxious about tomorrow, for tomorrow will be anxious for itself. Sufficient for the day is its own trouble.
> — Matthew 6:25–34 (ESV)

This is Jesus speaking. He's addressing anxiety directly. He talks about health — about adding days to your life. The pandemic is in view here too, I think. But notice also how he talks about the birds, the lilies. God promises to take care of them. He will take care of the things and the people you have been white-knuckling. All we have to do is follow Christ. He will take care of the rest.

---

I want to leave you with one more picture.

The other night I brought home two hoof treats from the farm store — one for Joy, one for Cooper. When I put them down, Cooper ignored his and went straight for Joy's. Joy was turning inside out with anxiety. She wanted her treat. It was only fair.

I told her to wait until Cooper goes to bed. I told her I'd give her his when he did.

She didn't fully understand. She's still a dog. But at the end of the night, she got the better treat by waiting.

That's what's in store for us.

That, however, starts with the Gospel.

---

*[Gospel presentation and prayer.]*

**Scripture verified (ESV):**

**Opening text:** The pastor announces Philippians 4:6–7 but reads 4:4–7. The exposition begins at verse 4 (the doubled "Rejoice" command). The manuscript reads from 4:4 throughout. If preached again, consider announcing the passage as 4:4–7 from the opening.

**"To be absent from the body is to be present with the Lord":** Traditional KJV paraphrase of 2 Corinthians 5:8. The ESV reads: "we would rather be away from the body and at home with the Lord." The pastor uses the traditional form; the theological meaning is identical. The ESV reference for any follow-up is 2 Corinthians 5:8.

**Stephen Davey attribution:** The definition of worry as "lack of faith in God" is attributed to Stephen Davey (now Colonial Church, Cary, NC). Consistent with Davey's teaching on Philippians 4. Exact source not confirmed — note the attribution when preaching, verify before publishing.

**"Until you have prayed, you can do no more" — source UNVERIFIED:** The raw notes attribute this saying to John Owen. It has been removed from the body text because the source cannot be confirmed — the manuscript presents it as an unnamed pastoral principle. If you locate the Owen source before preaching, add his name back. If the source turns out to be someone else, attribute accordingly. Do not use Owen's name from the pulpit until verified.

**Paul's sufferings — source clarification:** The list (shipwrecked, snake-bitten, stoned, basket over wall) is drawn from 2 Corinthians 11:24–27 and Acts. The raw notes say "just a little bit earlier goes on down the list" — but this list does not appear in Philippians; it is in 2 Corinthians. Corrected to "Elsewhere in his letters" in the manuscript. The snake bite is from Acts 28:3–5 (Malta), which postdates 2 Corinthians; it is a real event but not in Paul's own suffering-lists. Included here as the pastor's own rhetorical list, not as a citation of a specific passage.

**"The battle is the Lord's — but prepare your horses anyway":** The pastor's father invokes this principle in the sermon. The background texts are Proverbs 21:31 — "The horse is made ready for the day of battle, but the victory belongs to the LORD" (ESV) — and 2 Chronicles 20:15–17, where God tells Jehoshaphat the battle belongs to him. The dad's paraphrase holds both passages together: act and prepare, but the outcome is God's. The pastor affirms this. No misuse of Scripture here; note the references for any follow-up discussion.

**Tertullian:** "The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church." Standard attribution to Tertullian, *Apologeticus*, 50.13. This is a paraphrase of the Latin original (*sanguis Christianorum semen est*). The paraphrase form is widely accepted in scholarly and pastoral contexts. Attribution is correct.

**"Swing Low, Sweet Chariot":** Traditional African American spiritual from the antebellum era. No single credited author. The association with longing for death and heaven (rather than Underground Railroad imagery, which came later) is the appropriate reading for the theological point being made here.

**COVID-era context:** The references to COVID, the vaccine, masks, and Iran place this sermon in the 2020–2021 window. These applications are preserved as historically authentic to the moment. If this sermon is adapted for another season, the specific examples would need updating; the homiletical structure — the same answer to every fear — is repeatable in any season.
