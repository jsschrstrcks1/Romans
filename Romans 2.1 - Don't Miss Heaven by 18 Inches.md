## The "Therefore" — Romans 2:1

Therefore — anytime you see *therefore*, you need to look back a chapter and figure out what it's there for.

Looking back to last week's study, Paul talked mostly about three things: first, Christ; second, that God was and is reflected in creation; and third, he contrasted that with a description of the outward acts of man's sin nature, which culminated in verse 32 — declaring that those who practice such things deserve to die.

Therefore Paul says: *therefore*, "O Man" — you Jews who judge these Gentiles are without excuse. You're doing the exact same things.

---

## The Greek Vocative — "O Man"

I want to draw your attention to where Paul says "O Man."

In checking the Greek, I noticed a familiar word with an unusual case. You know how we have past, present, and future tense — or can add a letter to make a word plural, or a prefix to make a word negative? In Greek they have a similar setup, but it's more versatile and extensive. There are well over a hundred such endings. I'm certain that if I had completed the full four years of Greek offered at Southeastern I probably would have recognized it immediately. But this word was unusual because of the ending.

So I looked it up in my Greek-to-English dictionary and found a discussion of the word's ending. Apparently it is rare to use it, and rarer still to use it in the way Paul did here. I won't bore you with all the details, but the conclusion was that Paul was feeling a little hot under the collar when he wrote "O Man." The use of the vocative case generally adds weight — and then the specific way he uses it here, it's almost like he's having a strong emotional outburst.

If one can yell using words, Paul is doing so here. It's like what kids do on Facebook when they start typing in all caps — basically yelling at people over the internet, which is generally considered rude. But Paul has some pretty good justification for his outburst.

*[Note: Romans 2:1 uses "ὦ ἄνθρωπε" — "O man." The particle "ὦ" combined with the vocative case does carry heightened emotional force in Greek. The pastor's description is linguistically accurate: the construction is emphatic and relatively rare in Paul's writing.]*

## The Charge: You Do the Same Things

It would seem the corruption in the temple was so blatant it was well known even among the Gentiles — look at verse 24: *"The name of God is blasphemed among the Gentiles because of you."* (Romans 2:24 ✓)

The people speaking out against stealing were thieves themselves. Those preaching against adultery were engaging in extramarital affairs. Those who claimed to worship God were robbing temples. While they claimed the Law was going to save them, while they claimed to live up to the letter of the Law in public — in private they were just as rotten, where it matters, as any pagan in any city.

Can you imagine a world where the priests are the perpetrators of the very sins they preached against?

- I think about all the Catholic altar boys violated by their spiritual leaders.
- I think of Michael Jackson, who at one time professed to be a Christian.
- I think about the Subway spokesman — you remember, the one that lost all the weight eating at Subway — who pleaded guilty to charges of pedophilia.
- I think about churches ordaining homosexuality and speaking out in favor of abortion.
- I think of church leaders who fleeced their flock — embezzling money given to them for ministry.
- I think of a couple of pastors I've known who preached the gospel on Sunday, but on Monday were sleeping with some other woman.
- I think of John Q. Churchgoer who isn't much better. Attending church on Sunday, getting drunk with the boys on Friday — or doing drugs, or having an affair, or any of the other things we've talked about.

## The "Britney Spears Effect"

While you and I know it's not our deeds that save us, they do give us a certain reputation.

Let me ask you this — if you were to ask your friends who knew you before Christ, would they be able to tell a difference between who you are now and who you were then? In the same way you can tell when a person has smashed their face onto a red-hot stove burner, you can tell if someone is really a Christian. You can't approach the true and living God and not be changed. If you have a relationship with him, you will be different.

Consider Moses — after he came down the mountain he glowed to the point where he wore a veil for the rest of his life to conceal it. (Exodus 34:29–35 ✓) Could you imagine what it would be like if the Lord got ahold of you to the point where you reflected enough of His glory that you glowed in the dark?

If God could change the world with twelve people sold out for him, imagine what He could do with this church.

---

Back to the passage. I read something a number of years ago that said, basically, that Christians are largely their own worst enemy when it comes to the Gospel. It said damaged Christians acting in ways that reflect said damage is the most often cited reason for non-believers rejecting the Gospel — especially among Muslims.

This is something I've written about. I've called it the **Britney Spears effect.**

*[Note: The "Britney Spears effect" is the pastor's own coined concept. The name is illustrative — the same point applies equally to any celebrity (Bieber, Jay-Z, Prince, etc.) who gives public lip service to God while living in open contradiction to it. The specific concern: when a celebrity identifies publicly as a Christian and then lives in visible contradiction of it, the watching world — particularly the Muslim world — takes their behavior as representative of Christianity itself. The result is that the watching world concludes Christianity is a sham, its God is dead or indifferent, and its people are hypocrites.]*

When she first came on the scene she was talking about the Lord, saying she wanted to honor him, giving glory to him on the public stage. She toured the world. And then she started letting things go to her head — drugs, conforming to the world, dressing and acting immodestly. But to the Muslim world, she was still symbolic of Christianity.

They heard her say she was a Christian. And then they saw her. And they said: *Their God must be dead. Must not care. Must not enforce his word. Their God is useless.*

Boy, won't they be surprised when every knee bows and every tongue confesses that Jesus Christ is Lord, as the Scriptures say. (Romans 14:11; Philippians 2:10–11 ✓)

---

But how often do we do the same thing? How often do we conform outwardly on Sunday, but then ignore our Bibles all week? How often do we treat Christ the way Abraham treated his wife — when things got tense, he'd tell everyone she was his sister and effectively hand her off to whoever was looking at her. How often do we, in word or deed or by lack thereof, deny Christ to fit in?

I've seen it all before — people hanging out with their friends from the world (which is fine — if you don't have any friends who don't know Jesus, go make some!) — and they're all cursing together. Did Jesus curse? Did Jesus get drunk? Do drugs?

We are Christians — which means to be Christlike. Did Christ ever set aside the words of his Father because he was too busy?

That last one hits me hard sometimes. It's so easy to put priority on everything else but your relationship with God. Even at seminary, it's easy to get so busy *learning about* God that you forget to *spend time with* him. Building that relationship — the one that saves you from eternal torment — is the most important relationship in your life. Christ was obedient unto death (Philippians 2:8 ✓). A few weeks ago I said we are obedient unto discomfort. Tonight I'm telling you that often we are only obedient until *busy*.

## Inward Before Outward

The Law in question here is the Mosaic Law. The purpose of the Law is to show us how incapable we are of meeting God's standard.

Martin Luther — you know who he was, right? He nailed his 95 Theses to the wall of the Wittenberg church in Germany — one of the foundational events of the Protestant Reformation. Luther said: *"The Law proves to us that we cannot stand before God on our own righteousness, and that drives us to the Gospel."* *(paraphrase)*

*[Note: The concept is Luther's — the Law/Gospel dialectic is central to his theology, developed most fully in his Lectures on Galatians and throughout his sermons. The specific phrasing here is a paraphrase of that teaching, not a traceable verbatim quote.]*

Paul makes the point that even the religious leaders of the day were falling very short of God's standard — all while self-righteously declaring their holiness before the Law.

The priority isn't outward conformity — though that will come. The priority is inward conformity, which overflows into outward conformity. Get your heart right. Study the love letter from our Creator God. Let his will become your will. His wants, your wants. Then the outward changes come. Then the world fades away. Racism fades away. Addictions fade away. Then we really do begin to reflect Christ.

People will begin to see the outline of the stove on our face.

So to summarize: don't be a hypocrite — but also don't be so wrapped up in outward appearance that you neglect your relationship with God. Focus on the relationship, and *He* will begin helping you change the things in your life that you hate.

Even Paul had them — *"that which I hate, that I do; those things I want to do, those I do not do."* (Romans 7:15 ✓) If God can use a murdering Pharisee to write half the New Testament — if God can use a donkey — he can use you and me.

## Don't Miss Heaven by 18 Inches

Our last point tonight: **don't miss heaven by 18 inches.**

I was listening to a preacher in chapel at Trinity one time. He was preaching to the choir, but he was preaching the gospel hard. I love to hear the gospel preached — like the hymn says, *"I love to tell the story, for those who know it best seem hungering and thirsting to hear it like the rest."*

*[Note: "I Love to Tell the Story" — hymn by A. Katherine Hankey, 1866. ✓]*

So I didn't think anything of it. But a guy I was sitting next to was passing me notes complaining about it. We're in seminary. He'd been in church his whole life. Greek homework to do. Why should he have to sit through this. I kept shushing him.

A couple minutes later the preacher began to wrap up, and he said: *"Maybe some of you out there have grown up in church, have been a Christian from a young age, and now you're in seminary — and you're thinking this is pointless, that you're wasting your time, that there are better things you could be doing than sitting here listening to the Gospel preached to the choir."*

I was kind of floored. But he continued.

*"This is for you. There are people in this room that may know a lot about Christ, but don't know Christ."*

He pleaded — *please, please, don't miss heaven by 18 inches.*

18 inches, of course, is the distance between your brain and your heart.

*Don't spend all your time learning about God and neglect your relationship with God.*

A friend of mine walked out.

*[Note: Name withheld — real person, friend of the pastor from his seminary years. Anonymized out of pastoral care and respect for privacy.]*

Within just a few weeks he came out as gay, decided he was going to live that way as fully as he could, moved back home, and is living with another man. I believe that preacher may have been sent there specifically for him.

One day we're all going to have to stand before God. For me, my Lord and Savior Jesus Christ is going to say, *"I've got this one, Father — I've paid his debt."*

For my friend — I'm not so sure. There is a verse that talks about people who die thinking they are saved when they aren't. They will say: *"But Lord, I did this for you, and that for you"* — and Jesus is going to say —

> *"I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of lawlessness."*
> — Matthew 7:23 ✓

The Pharisees are clearly in view in that verse. Modern Pharisees are those who attend church, help set up and tear down, bring food to the potluck, serve on a committee, have attended church their whole lives — look like a Christian, smell like a Christian, maybe even fool those around them — but the most important part, the most necessary part, the part most readily available — **salvation** — is not something they yet have.

One day, Romans 2:16 says, God will judge the secrets of mankind by Christ Jesus. Without Christ we could never measure up. With Christ, there are no worries, and hope for the future. Ultimately that judgment is in Christ's hands.

**Do you know him today?**

If you aren't like my dog — so excited about Christ that you can hardly contain it — then maybe something's wrong.
