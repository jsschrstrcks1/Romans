### The Baptist Faith and Message

This week we are continuing our journey through the Baptist Faith and Message and will be talking about the Holy Spirit. The Baptist Faith and Message is the document Baptists have agreed upon as a group will be the basis of our cooperation with each other.

There's a saying — often attributed to Augustine, though it actually comes from a Lutheran theologian named Rupertus Meldenius during the Thirty Years' War — "In the essentials unity, in the non-essentials liberty, and in all things charity." For Baptists, these are the things from Scripture that we agree are the essentials. More could be added, you could take some away. The Bible and what it says is more important. But this is kind of like bumpers when you're bowling. They help keep us all on track together.

I digress. The Baptist Faith and Message says:

> **Article C. God the Holy Spirit**
>
> The Holy Spirit is the Spirit of God, fully divine. He inspired holy men of old to write the Scriptures. Through illumination He enables men to understand truth. He exalts Christ. He convicts men of sin, of righteousness, and of judgment. He calls men to the Saviour, and effects regeneration. At the moment of regeneration He baptizes every believer into the Body of Christ. He cultivates Christian character, comforts believers, and bestows the spiritual gifts by which they serve God through His church. He seals the believer unto the day of final redemption. His presence in the Christian is the guarantee that God will bring the believer into the fullness of the stature of Christ. He enlightens and empowers the believer and the church in worship, evangelism, and service.

There is so much we could talk about. There are controversies and all sorts of things. We will dip our toe into some of that — only because there is so much bad teaching out there about the Holy Spirit.

### A Youth Trip to Washington, D.C.

Let me start with a story. I went on a youth trip to Washington, D.C. back in the late '90s. It was several days of worshiping God, of sitting under great Bible preachers and teachers, and then at the end of it we marched on the Capitol building and worshiped God some more on the National Mall. Hundreds of thousands of kids and tens of thousands of youth workers.

Every day they had breakout sessions, and the different teachers would give a snippet of what they planned to talk about. This one guy spoke really passionately about prayer and improving your prayer life, and I figured who couldn't do better when it comes to prayer, so I went. The room we met in was probably 20 times the size of our little church, and there was a coil of teenagers in a line all the way around the room. At the end of the line there was this teacher, and he was praying over each person that came through individually. Wasn't what I was expecting, but still cool. I was in line with a friend. We talked and the time passed quickly.

We finally get up to the front and the guy asked me if I had ever been "baptized in the Holy Spirit." I told him I was a believer in Jesus Christ and had been baptized in water before, and that I'm a Baptist, so we don't really talk about such things.

Have you ever heard people talk about being "baptized in the Spirit" before? Have you ever heard the phrase "slain in the Spirit," "laughing in the Spirit"? Or any of a number of other things the Holy Spirit allegedly does? Needless to say, as this was before Bible college, I didn't know what to say to the man, and he prayed for me, and I went on about my day.

Other people talk about speaking in tongues and that it's a sign you've been saved and so forth. If you were ever approached by someone that said you have to speak in tongues to be saved, what would you tell them?

I think if we just cover these things — the ministry of, the gifts of, and proof of the Holy Spirit in our lives — we will be doing well.

### What Happened at Pentecost

So let's start with our Scripture — Acts 2:1-9, 36-40. Our first stop is going to be verses 3-4:

> And divided tongues as of fire appeared to them and rested on each one of them. And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit gave them utterance.
> — Acts 2:3-4 (ESV)

This is an important point for the church as a whole. This moment signified the start of the church — the new covenant completed in Christ and sealed with the Holy Spirit. There were basically three periods, to lump everything into big un-nuanced buckets. There was the Old Testament way that God dealt with people. Then Christ comes, and it's this sort of transitional time where even Christ was following the law. And then after Christ we have this new covenant, this new system where we no longer have to offer sacrifices. This is sort of the first day of the church, and Peter is the first recorded preacher. It's definitely a momentous occasion. God brought in this new covenant, this new dispensation, with fanfare.

But what precisely happened there has been a subject of debate for the last 125 years or so.

### Tongues: What Languages?

You see in your Bible where it says "tongue"? That is a figure of speech. We have a lot of figures of speech in English, don't we — sometimes called colloquialisms. We might say to someone sitting on a sofa that "you're on the right path." We aren't talking about their literal physical direction, but generally speaking in terms of the direction of their thoughts or life. It depends upon some cultural knowledge and conversational context to explain the meaning precisely. The same is true of the word "tongues." That is a literal translation of the word. As you know, they aren't talking about the organ in your mouth — they are talking about languages. But what languages?

This is where the debate comes in. Some people tie that word to another passage that talks about speaking in "tongues of angels" and argue that speaking in tongues is always an angelic prayer language. Other people argue that it's other terrestrial languages. Honestly, the Greek word for tongues is flexible enough to allow for either. So what do we have to look at? We can look at the other words in the passage and at the passage's context to try to gain some insight.

First, let's look at the word directly before it. *Other.* "Other" is an interesting word. In English it's pretty broad — Mr. Webster says "other" refers to a thing that is different or distinct from something already mentioned or known, or that it means additional. My grandmother used to love additional things. You'd go over and visit, and spend a couple hours, and as you were leaving she would say, "Oh, one more thing!" She had something *other* to talk to us about.

If you look at the Greek word, you see something similar. Except *heteros* (ἕτερος) is in numerous places translated as "neighbor," as "stranger," as "man," as "the latest in a series" according to Strong's number 2087. If Luke had wanted to say the apostles were speaking an angelic prayer language, he had lots of words he could have used to do so. This word for "others" is a clue that Luke is speaking about earthly languages.

Let's see what else we can find. The next thing worth noting is that Luke is speaking in plain language. He's not telling a parable. He's relaying a historical account. He's not telling a story to emphasize a point — he's being very literal. When the Bible is speaking literally, we should take it literally.

Third, let's read verses 5-6:

> Now there were dwelling in Jerusalem Jews, devout men from every nation under heaven. And at this sound the multitude came together, and they were bewildered, because each one was hearing them speak in his own language.
> — Acts 2:5-6 (ESV)

Very clearly, here, Luke is talking about different languages. If a Russian had been present, the Russian would have heard the disciples speaking in Russian. If you had been there, you would have heard the disciples speaking in your heart language. For most of us that would be English. Dalia would probably have heard Arabic. Someone from France would have heard French, and so forth.

They aren't speaking gobbledygook. They are speaking in real, terrestrial, earth languages. This isn't something they took a class in. It is something that only happens to those that are real believers in Jesus Christ. You caught the sequence, right? The tongues of fire settled over the believers, then the Holy Spirit filled them. They weren't going around praying for God to fill them. They were waiting for the promised Helper that Jesus said he would send, and then — bam — God acted on the believers to fill them with the Holy Spirit. Right after which they start speaking other earthly languages.

There is a missional and evangelistic need in the early church. Jerusalem is strategically at the crossroads of the known world. Traders came from as far away as England and Asia to trade. People from scores of peoples, tribes, and language groups would have been present that day, and so it makes sense for God to move in this way in this place.

There is another passage — 1 Corinthians 13:1 — that says, "If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am a noisy gong or a clanging cymbal." I wish we had the time to focus on that this morning, but needless to say that passage is speaking in hyperbole — it's using "tongues of angels" as a way to drive home a point. Luke is speaking literally and descriptively, whereas Paul is giving a sermon illustration. Make sense? There is more there. But we're getting off track.

### The Ministry of the Holy Spirit

It strikes me that mankind fails at every task God sets before us without his help, don't we? Told us not to eat from the tree, and we ate from the tree. Told us to follow the law, and sacrifice when we couldn't, and we couldn't even do that. So he had to send his Son to die for us. We can't even be interested in the things of the Lord on our own — Scripture rightly says the things of the Lord are foolishness to those that don't believe. We couldn't secure salvation on our own. We can't maintain salvation on our own. The best we can do is filthy rags.

So God — after paying our sin price, after securing our salvation, after defeating sin and death and hell and Satan — had to send us his Holy Spirit to indwell in us, to do a work in us, because we would be unable to grow towards Christ without his help also.

I heard a pastor once say God has already done the hard part. Boy, has he! He even has to do a work in us before salvation or we would reject the good news outright and call it foolishness. It says so in Scripture too.

I wasn't going to do this, but let's quickly turn over to Romans 8 and read with me verses 5-11:

> For those who live according to the flesh set their minds on the things of the flesh, but those who live according to the Spirit set their minds on the things of the Spirit. For to set the mind on the flesh is death, but to set the mind on the Spirit is life and peace. For the mind that is set on the flesh is hostile to God, for it does not submit to God's law; indeed, it cannot. Those who are in the flesh cannot please God. You, however, are not in the flesh but in the Spirit, if in fact the Spirit of God dwells in you. Anyone who does not have the Spirit of Christ does not belong to him. But if Christ is in you, although the body is dead because of sin, the Spirit is life because of righteousness. If the Spirit of him who raised Jesus from the dead dwells in you, he who raised Christ Jesus from the dead will also give life to your mortal bodies through his Spirit who dwells in you.
> — Romans 8:5-11 (ESV)

So who lives according to the flesh? Lost people, right? So lost people have their minds on things of the flesh. Galatians 5:19-21 fleshes this out. Paul writes:

> Now the works of the flesh are evident: sexual immorality, impurity, sensuality, idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, divisions, envy, drunkenness, orgies, and things like these. I warn you, as I warned you before, that those who do such things will not inherit the kingdom of God.
> — Galatians 5:19-21 (ESV)

So if we're in the flesh, we're bound for hell, right? No other option. Because when we are in the flesh, we are hostile to God. We are wittingly or unwittingly working directly against God. The Greek word for hostile could also be translated as hatred. If our natural posture towards God is hatred, that has to be fixed before we can approach the throne of grace. God in the richness of his mercy does just that for us. It would be a challenge — wouldn't it, men — to go to someone you hate and ask them for a favor. Much less ask them to give up the life of their son for you. Scripture says the things of the Lord are foolishness to those that don't believe.

At least until the moment of salvation — when the Holy Spirit shows up and seals everyone that's a believer. The moment you become a believer in Jesus Christ, God sends his Holy Spirit to dwell inside of you. In that moment, someone who's truly saved gets all of the Holy Spirit they will ever get. It's not like some cosmic gas tank where you can run out. We are all granted the same amount of the Holy Spirit that Elijah and Elisha had. But unlike those two, it never leaves us once we are saved. Remember Elijah and the prophets of Baal at Mt. Carmel (1 Kings 18)?

### Proof of the Holy Spirit in Your Life

So we've scratched the surface of the ministry of the Holy Spirit. Let's talk more about proof of the Holy Spirit in your life.

Spiritual maturity is a big mark of this. People that believe in God, that have the Holy Spirit in their life, grow in Christ. It's natural, normal, and expected.

It's easy to say you follow Christ, and maybe some can fake it for a few weeks or a year, but you can't fake it across the arc of your life. We knew a guy that came to a church we used to go to for a time that was one way at church and lived the rest of his life as though he didn't know Jesus. In church on Sunday, partaking of the vices, cursing like a sailor, bragging about his sin, and encouraging others to sin. Sunday morning he had that nice shiny face, clean language — he dressed and talked the part on Sunday. The expression that comes to mind is lipstick on a pig. He may have worn the uniform on Sunday, but he fought for the enemy Monday through Saturday.

Maybe you can fool me — I'm not perfect. It wouldn't be that hard. But you won't fool God on that point.

So you have to look at your own heart and examine it:

- Are you closer to God now than you were a year ago? Five years ago? Ten?
- Are you reading God's Word more than you were a year ago? Five years ago? Ten?
- Are you praying more than you were a year ago? Five years ago? Ten?

I know a guy that used to be a policeman. Retired. God got ahold of him and he became a pastor. I ran into a deputy one time that knew him before Christ, and over the course of the conversation I mentioned he's a pastor now, and the deputy was shocked. That guy? Wow. Maybe I need to go meet this Jesus.

Would people speak of you in that manner?

How about this — are you more humble now than you were? That's a tricky one. Paul says people are puffed up by knowledge, but that love builds up. Reading and memorizing Scripture is so important — but do you allow yourself to look down on those that haven't memorized as much? That don't read as much?

How about in your approach to others? Jesus was described as being full of truth and grace. Are you all grace with people, letting things slide that you shouldn't? Or are you all truth with no grace? Either isn't good. Both are needed. Jesus was hard when he needed to be — he chased the money changers out of the temple — and he offered grace to the woman at the well. Navigating those situations well comes with a depth of relationship with God and with the presence of the Holy Spirit. There is more that could be said here, but we're already long at this point.

### The Todd Bentley Warning

Let me ask you this. If someone approached you and asked if you had ever been "baptized" in the Holy Spirit — what would you tell them? And "laughing in the Holy Spirit"? Did we see any of that in the Bible today? What about over the last year as we have completed reading through the entire Bible together? Hmm.

I won't say God can't do anything. Because he can. He can make people speak in languages that they don't understand. I don't think it's normal for it to happen today and given out as a gift like Peter had. But God is capable of doing that if he chooses. God can heal anyone he wants. God can reveal information to anyone he wants. I won't put God in a box like that. But if God thought it was important for people to know about all of these extra things, wouldn't God have told us about them?

I'm reminded of a "revival" — big air quotes — that happened not too far from here not so many years ago. This man from a denomination that practices such things — the "slain in the Spirit" and "laughing in the Spirit" type things — was "preaching." I say that loosely, because what I heard of what he had to say on YouTube sounded more like an hour of him bragging about what he had done and how God had told him to do ridiculous things. If the stories were true, some of them were sinful. If they weren't true, saying they were was sinful.

Anyway, this man Todd Bentley told a story about one night at the "revival" — big air quotes — where this little old lady in a wheelchair was pushed up to the edge of the stage, and she was praying and worshiping God, and Todd said "the Holy Spirit came upon me and told me to kick her in the face with my biker boots."

First of all — I don't even know where to start with that. I think he means to say the Holy Spirit started talking to him, but instead he's saying things from the Old Testament, where one didn't have the Holy Spirit, and then one did for a time. But we don't ever have more or less of the Holy Spirit once we are saved!

Secondly — does that sound like something the Holy Spirit would ever command someone to do? Certainly not. I wish I could say that this was a unique occurrence. But every night that this man preached, he had another story like this. Grabbing a crippled woman's legs and using them like baseball bats. Kicking a man with polio in the face. Punching women. And this is supposedly of the Holy Spirit? Does that sound like the Holy Spirit we've discussed today?

We have to have discernment. We have to know what our Bible says so that when we encounter these charlatans, we can know. Todd Bentley was pretty obvious to us. But there were hundreds of thousands upon hundreds of thousands that went and were entertained and believed this was a thing of God — to the point where he made national headlines back in 2008. Around a half million people attended this show. Since 2008, tens of millions of people have seen this and believed in Todd.

I'm reminded of a quote often attributed to Spurgeon — though it likely comes from his student Archibald Brown in an 1889 lecture called *The Devil's Mission of Amusement*:

> "A time will come when instead of shepherds feeding the sheep, the church will have clowns entertaining the goats."

The sad thing about Bentley — I think he is deceived himself. He's all about feelings and emotions. He's not about the Word of God. Be wary of anyone like that. Anything that conflicts with the Word of God is NOT of God.

Bentley says he's here to — and I quote — blow the "bum hole" out of the church and put back a whole new thing. Bentley says all of this started not when he encountered Jesus for the first time, but when he bodily encountered an angel. Not even an angel would whisper in the ear of Todd Bentley the things that have come out of his mouth. If it's not God or an angel that he's listening to — who does that leave?
