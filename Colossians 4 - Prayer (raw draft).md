# Prayer
## Colossians 4:2–4

---

We're going to talk about prayer today.

I heard a story the other day about a pilot flying his small homebuilt airplane. He had been enjoying the beauty of God's creation — great weather, a good plane, open sky. But he lost track of time. Without realizing it, he flew so far that he didn't have enough gas to get back to his home airport. So he calls air traffic control and asks what he should do. They find a nearby airport that can handle his aircraft and reroute other traffic. They figure if he's careful and the wind stays down, he can make it with a couple of gallons to spare.

But the calculations — or the wind — were off. A few miles short of a safe landing, his plane starts singing the klaxon. Out of gas.

He calls air traffic control one more time. "What do I do?"

The controller said: "Repeat after me — *Our Father, which art in heaven.*"

I think a major misconception is that prayer is only for emergencies. But think about that in the context of your relationship with your spouse. Christ is the bridegroom of the church. We are his bride. Husbands — wives — what if your partner only spoke to you when they had made such a mess of things that they couldn't fix it on their own?

How would that make you feel? Neglected? Ignored? Used?

How do you think it makes God feel? God wants us to have a relationship with him. To enjoy him. When we neglect him, it harms us — and it is offensive to God.

How long would you stay married to someone who absolutely ignored you unless they needed the checkbook? *"Hey honey, I messed up again, I need you to come bail me out."* Which is exactly what we do to God, isn't it?

Prayer is a marvelous gift given to us by our Creator. What other king — much less King of Kings — gives his subjects the ability to communicate with him from anywhere, at any time, about anything? And yet so many people save it for when trouble comes. We should absolutely pray when trouble comes. But it shouldn't be the *only* time we pray.

I had a friend in high school. I won't name her. I saw her as a friend, and I would give her rides and take her places out of friendship — mostly rides to hang out with my sister. Most of the time I was heading past her parents' house anyway and I didn't mind. We were friends. But after a year or so it started to feel one-sided. Like I was her servant. Like rides were expected and obligated, and if I wasn't available I was the bad guy. I also started to only hear from her when she wanted a ride. So one day I told her: if she really was my friend she needed to communicate with me more than just when she wanted something. If she couldn't do that, she shouldn't continue to ask me for rides.

I was offended. I felt used.

It's been approaching two decades since I heard from her.

It cut me to the core one day in Bible college when I realized we do the exact same thing to God.

Prayer is a tremendous gift. Each of us in this room knows someone who has been miraculously healed by God in response to prayer. We have seen God answer prayers for the lost — working in their heart and mind to draw them toward himself. We have seen the miracle of lost people becoming found, those dead according to Ephesians 2 being brought back to new life in Christ.

So often we neglect this tool, this resource, this treasure we have access to through Christ.

You can be a believer in Jesus Christ and not pray. You can be a husband who never talks to his wife. But what a shallow thing that would be. What depths of joy, comfort, contentment, peace, and love would you miss out on if you never spoke to your wife? What depths of joy, comfort, contentment, peace, and love do we miss out on when we never talk to our Bridegroom?

F.B. Meyer wrote in *The Secret of Guidance* that the great tragedy of life is not unanswered prayer but unoffered prayer.

⚠️ *[Verification flag: This quote is widely attributed to F.B. Meyer's The Secret of Guidance. Verify exact wording against published text before citing in print.]*

What does Scripture say about prayer? Let me read Colossians 4:2–4.

> 2 Continue steadfastly in prayer, being watchful in it with thanksgiving. 3 At the same time, pray also for us, that God may open to us a door for the word, to declare the mystery of Christ, on account of which I am in prison— 4 that I may make it clear, which is how I ought to speak.
> — Colossians 4:2–4 (ESV)

It starts with *continue steadfastly.* Calvin Wittman, preaching on this passage, says the Greek word for "steadfastly" can be translated: *persist in*, *adhere firmly to*, *remain devoted to*, or *give unremitting care to*.

⚠️ *[Verification flag: Calvin Wittman's sermon on Colossians 4 is cited here. The lexical analysis is sound — προσκαρτερέω does carry these meanings — but the specific sermon source has not been verified.]*

Every time that Greek word appears throughout the New Testament it carries a sense of devotion — of starting and never stopping. It is an imperative command. Prayer isn't something we can choose to pick up and put back down. Prayer is something we are commanded to do by our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ.

Paul continues: we must pray for doors to be open so the gospel can be heard. Pray steadfastly, remain devoted to praying, give unremitting care to praying for the lost — that doors would open for the word.

Don't raise your hands, but how many of you have been praying daily for the lost in our community? That doors would be open?

All throughout the New Testament this is the command of Jesus through Scripture. In Luke 11 we are told to ask, seek, and knock. In the Greek the idea is to start asking and *keep asking*, to start seeking and *keep seeking*, to start knocking and *keep knocking*. We are to be persistent in prayer. That's different from long prayers. Some people pray because of how it looks to others. Others don't pray because they're worried what people will think. But that isn't what God cares about. He wants sincere, open communication.

Some people say prayer is hard — or boring. Well, you aren't alone in thinking that. There was a famous missionary, a man who started orphanages and fed hundreds of thousands of hungry people and orphans. George Müller. He struggled with that too. He wrote:

> "It is a common temptation of Satan to make us give up the reading of the Word and prayer when our enjoyment is gone; as if it were of no use to read the Scriptures when we do not enjoy them, and as if it were no use to pray when we have no spirit of prayer. The truth is that, in order to enjoy the Word, we ought to continue to read it, and the way to obtain a spirit of prayer is to continue praying. The less we read the Word of God, the less we desire to read it, and the less we pray, the less we desire to pray."
> — George Müller

⚠️ *[Verification flag: This quote is widely attributed to Müller's journals / A Narrative of Some of the Lord's Dealings with George Müller. Verify exact wording against published source before citing in print.]*

These were lessons learned the hard way. I've read his journals, and he spent more time battling depression and illness than anyone I'm aware of. And what's his response? In order to enjoy the Word, we must read it. In order to obtain a spirit of prayer, we must pray. It is when we neglect these things that we begin to lose our satisfaction, contentment, and joy in the Lord.

So what I want to do is spend the rest of our time in prayer. We're not worried about the clock. We're not worried about lunch. We're worried about healthy communication with our Savior. We'll go around the room, and as God puts things on your heart, pray for them — one person at a time. I'll open, and when everyone has been quiet for a moment I'll close.

There are so many needs — in our community, our church family, our extended families. In our county, our state, our country, our military, our government. There are so many things to be thankful for. If you woke up with air in your lungs and sight in your eyes, that's something to thank God for. Let's not go to him only in a crisis. Let's go to him in the good times too — with thanksgiving, with praise.

Let's pray.
