### Opening — The Number

We're going to be in John 14 this morning. If you've brought your Bible — and I hope you have — go ahead and find that.

I read something this week that I haven't been able to shake. It's a statistic about the state of the world, and I want to share it with you before we get into the text because I think it frames everything we're going to talk about this morning.

Of the roughly 8.3 billion people on the earth right now, approximately 3.4 billion of them are unreached. I want to make sure we understand what that word means. Unreached does not mean they have heard the gospel and rejected it. It does not mean they've sat in a pew somewhere and decided they weren't interested. Unreached means they have never had access to the gospel at all. There is no missionary in their community. There is no Bible in their language. Not only do they not know Jesus — they will likely never even hear his name.

We pray for a people group every week here, usually from this category — people with no access to the gospel at all. And I want you to hold that number in mind as we work through this today.

3.4 billion.

When our nation was attacked on September 11, 2001, and nearly 3,000 people lost their lives, the country was rightly shaken out of its apathy. We pulled together. We went to war. We spent the blood of our sons and daughters to make sure that the people responsible couldn't do it again. We said: this cannot stand.

3,000 people.

3.4 billion is not 3,000. It is — I can't even say a fraction of a percent of a percent — it is a number so much larger that the comparison barely makes sense. 3,000 was enough to put us all in the streets. 3.4 billion should be keeping us up at night. Yet most of us sleep just fine.

I think the reasons for that have a few key elements that need to be addressed this morning, and the text is going to help us get at them.

---

### Part 1 — The Question of "Which Way"

The first problem is this: a lot of people — including a lot of people who call themselves Christians — believe there are multiple ways to God.

They don't all say it the same way. Some say that whatever you sincerely believe is fine. Some say that as long as you're a good person, God will take that into account. Some say that every religion is pointing at the same mountain even if the paths look different. Buddhist, Mormon, animist, Muslim — just be devoted, be sincere, do more good than bad, and you'll be fine.

I knew a pastor who had a phrase I've never forgotten. He would say: *"It doesn't matter how many old ladies you help across the street, it will never be enough."* And he was right. It doesn't matter whether you've done nothing worse in your life than tell a small lie, or whether you are the person who flew the plane into the tower — the weight of your sin, the cost of your sin, exceeds your ability to balance the scale by an infinite amount. There is no positive thinking, no amount of good deeds, no devotion to any religion on earth that will get you to heaven.

John Wood, writing about the exclusivity of Christ, puts it this way:

> "The storyline of Scripture points to Jesus as the only way for salvation. From the early moments following the Fall, God makes a plan to save the people He created in His image. Eve, who rebelled against God's command, would one day, through her offspring, bear a child who would crush the head of the serpent, and whose heel would be bruised in the process (Gen. 3:15). This promise of a serpent-crushing Messiah signaled the first indication of the power of God for salvation that would be fulfilled in Jesus Christ. Throughout the Bible, there is an array of verses that demonstrate that the early promise in Genesis of salvation through a Messiah was fulfilled exclusively in Jesus Christ. For instance, the idols and gods of the nations failed to provide a way of salvation compared to the spiritual power and deliverance found in the God of Israel (Ps. 96:5; 97:7). Jesus' claim for Himself is that He alone is the means to salvation: 'I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me' (John 14:6). In his bold sermon delivered before the council in Jerusalem, Peter claimed, 'There is salvation in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given among men by which we must be saved' (Acts 4:12). And finally, the Apostle Paul made clear that Jesus Christ is the exclusive fulfillment of the law, making salvation available in Him alone (Rom. 10:1–4)."

That's the argument of the whole Bible in one paragraph. Let's read the passage.

Now look at verse 6 with me again.

*I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me.*

Jesus says what? He says *I am the way.* Does he include anyone else in that list? Does he say, "I am the way, but also, Mohamed is an equally valid way"? Does he say, "I am the way, or if you prefer, Joseph Smith is also an option"? Does he say, "I am the way, but also — if you do more good than bad, if you're sincere enough, if you attend enough — that will get the job done just as well"?

He says: *No one comes to the Father except through me.*

Not "few people." Not "the exceptional cases." No one. None. That is as exclusive a statement as you can make.

Acts 4:12 says it again from another angle. Peter is standing in front of the council — the same council that handed Jesus over to be crucified — and he says: *"And there is salvation in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given among men by which we must be saved."*

No other name. Not the name of any prophet, any teacher, any institution, any religious tradition, any self-proclaimed messiah.

Which brings me to a man you may or may not have heard of. His name was Jose Luis de Jesus Miranda.

Miranda operated out of Doral, Florida — the Miami area — and had a significant following. He claimed to be Jesus Christ. Not inspired by Christ. Not a prophet of Christ. Jesus Christ himself. He had "666" tattooed on his arm — and he encouraged his followers to do the same — claiming it was a symbol of wisdom. He taught that sin does not exist, that the devil does not exist, and that hell does not exist. He also claimed to be the Antichrist — but he redefined the term to mean someone who had moved beyond the Jesus of the Gospels.

His followers were devoted. One of them, when asked what would happen if he turned out to be wrong, basically said: so what if I end up wrong? I'm in a wonderful place right now.

Think about that for a moment. That is a person who has concluded that *this world is as good as it gets.* That this — this broken, painful, death-haunted world — is the ceiling. I can't imagine choosing to believe that. People have to be working very hard to keep their eyes shut.

Jose Luis de Jesus Miranda died in 2013 of cirrhosis of the liver. And we know what the scripture says. We all take our last breath, and we stand face to face with God. That goes for Jose Luis de Jesus Miranda. It goes for every false prophet who has ever claimed to be the way when there is only one Way.

It goes for the 3.4 billion people who have never heard his name.

### Part 2 — The Unregenerate Heart

The second problem is this: if we're waiting for people to find God on their own, we are going to wait forever.

Romans 3:10-11 says it plainly:

> *"None is righteous, no, not one; no one understands; no one seeks for God."*

Not some. Not most. None. No one is seeking for God on their own. Left to ourselves, we don't even know what we're missing — let alone seek it. And here is what that means practically: if you are someone who has not yet trusted Christ, but you've been coming to church, or watching online, or asking questions — there is a very good chance that what you're feeling is not your own initiative. God is drawing you toward himself. He has plans for you. He is doing something in you right now.

John Wood writes this: *"The image of the unregenerate heart is not one of a well-intentioned wanderer seeking truth, but of an idol factory producing false worship."*

That's the picture. Not a person who is lost but sincerely trying to find their way. An idol factory. Calvin in the Institutes described the human mind as a perpetual forge of idols. Not searching. Producing. The unregenerate heart doesn't wander toward God — it manufactures substitutes. And it is very productive at that work.

Let me give you an illustration that I think captures something of the problem.

Beethoven lost his hearing progressively over the last thirty years of his life. By the time he was in his late forties, he was effectively deaf — communicating through notebooks because he couldn't hear conversation. And yet some of his greatest music was written after his hearing was gone. The Ninth Symphony. The late string quartets. Work that required him to imagine — in total silence — what those sounds would produce, and to render that imagining into notation that other people could perform.

Now. Let me ask you something. Could a person who was born deaf — who had never heard music, never studied an instrument, never learned notation — could that person sit down and produce the Ninth Symphony? No. The deaf person from birth who has never encountered music would have no framework for what they are even trying to do. They might pick up something and make sounds with it. But it would not be the Ninth Symphony. It would be noise.

That is what it looks like for a person without Christ to try to find their way to God. They are working with no framework for what they are even trying to do. Romans 3 says they don't seek God. They don't understand what they are missing. They produce noise and call it music.

The only person who changes that — who gives the deaf man ears to hear — is Christ.

### Part 3 — The Problem of the Wrong Gospel

The third problem is that even when we know there is only one way, and we know people need the gospel, we can easily lose track of what the gospel actually *is.*

John MacArthur has written and preached extensively on this problem — one theme he has returned to across decades of ministry is that the church has grown shallower at the very moment it has the most technological capacity to spread the gospel to the ends of the earth. He identifies two compounding causes: pastors who lack the theological training to define things biblically with any depth, and a cultural pressure not to offend — which produces a synthetic gospel without enough truth in it to save anyone. *(The specific sermon or text where this is expressed most directly needs to be confirmed before quoting verbatim from the pulpit — cf. Ashamed of the Gospel, 1993.)*

He also says in that same vein that part of the problem is a de-emphasis on the Reformation. And you hear this — "the Reformation isn't relevant anymore." But what the Reformation did was precisely this: it *defined* the gospel. It drew a line in the sand and said — this is the gospel. These other things are not. We have to keep the main things the main things, or we lose the plot entirely.

Here's a concrete example of how this happens. Around the turn of the twentieth century, something called the "social gospel" began to gain traction in a lot of churches. And it started from a real and legitimate concern — prisons were barbaric places, and Christians banded together to improve conditions, to give inmates access to education and books so that when they got out they might have a fighting chance. A good thing. Baptists were involved. A good goal.

But when social improvement starts to *replace* the gospel rather than serve it, it's gone too far. And it did go that far. The social gospel became, in many churches, a substitute for the actual gospel. Social improvement as salvation.

Today in Baptist life — and more broadly in American Christianity — something similar is happening with politics. Ask yourself honestly: how much time did you spend this week talking about or consuming political content? Now ask yourself honestly: how much time did you spend sharing the gospel, or praying for the unreached, or thinking about the 3.4 billion people who have no access to the gospel at all?

I'm not throwing stones. I live in the same world you do. But if the political conversation got more of your mental energy this week than the gospel — that's an issue worth naming. Politics has become a kind of idol in our generation. And just like obesity is different from alcohol addiction — you can't solve it by simply quitting, because you have to stay engaged with civic life — but our engagement with politics has to be *secondary* to our engagement with the gospel. Not primary.

3.4 billion people, with no access. And we are arguing about tax rates.

Neither tax policy nor any other political outcome has ever raised someone from the dead. Only the gospel does that.

### Close — What Then

So here we are.

If you are a Christian this morning, the gospel is the most important thing you possess. Not because a pastor told you to care about it. Because the people around you — your kids, your friends, your coworkers, your neighbors — are among the people who are lost. Some of them may be among the 3.4 billion who have genuinely never heard. Most of them are closer than that. But lost is lost. And someone who is lost and going to face the wrath of God for their sins matters. 3,000 people mattered enough that a nation went to war. What does 3.4 billion demand of us?

If you are *not* a Christian — if you've been coming around, watching, asking questions — I want to say something to you directly. Romans 3:10-11 says no one seeks for God on their own. If you are seeking, something is happening. God is drawing you. He does not do that without purpose.

Romans 10:9 says this: *"because, if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*

That's the door. It is the only door. There is no door number two, no alternative entrance, no path around the back. But it is a real door, and it is open, and anyone who walks through it is saved.

There is one more passage I want us to turn to together. Let's go ahead and open to Romans 3:23.

## Fact-Check Flags Before Print or Repeat

**1. World population — CORRECTED**
The notes say "7.3 billion," which was accurate around 2015. Current world population (2026) is approximately **8.3 billion**. Manuscript uses the current figure.

**2. Unreached peoples — UPDATED**
The notes say "nearly 3 billion." Current Joshua Project data (2025) puts the figure at approximately **3.4 billion** in unreached people groups. "Nearly 3 billion" understates current data. Manuscript uses 3.4 billion.

**3. Jon Wood quotes (both) — REMOVED FROM MANUSCRIPT**
Two quotes attributed to "Jon Wood" appeared in the original draft. Neither quote could be found in any indexed source. Per careful-not-clever standards, unverified attributed quotes cannot be preached as direct quotations. The theological content from both passages has been incorporated as the preacher's own synthesis, retaining all Bible references (Gen. 3:15, Ps. 96:5/97:7, John 14:6, Acts 4:12, Rom. 10:1-4) and the "idol factory" concept (reattributed to Calvin's Institutes I.11.8 — "the human mind is, so to speak, a perpetual forge of idols" — a well-documented source). If a full citation for "Jon Wood" can be provided (author, title, publication, date), the attributed quote may be restored.

**4. MacArthur quote — SOURCE NOT CONFIRMED; CONVERTED TO PARAPHRASE**
The block quote attributed to John MacArthur ("Not only do we not know what the gospel is...") could not be traced to a specific sermon, book, or recorded talk. Themes are consistent with MacArthur's documented ministry concerns (cf. *Ashamed of the Gospel*, 1993). The manuscript now presents these as a summary of his documented themes, not a direct quote. Before any verbatim version is used from the pulpit, the specific source (sermon title, date, conference, or book) must be confirmed.

**5. Genesis 3:15 — ESV text note**
The ESV reads *"he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel."* The word "crush" reflects the NIV and other translations, not the ESV. The manuscript uses "bruise" throughout, consistent with the ESV. This is not a theological error — the Hebrew root (שׁוּף) admits both renderings — but ESV speakers should use "bruise."

**6. Miranda "death angels" threat — NOT IN MANUSCRIPT**
The claim that Miranda threatened his wife and children with "death angels" appeared in the original raw notes but was not incorporated into this manuscript. It remains unverified from indexed sources. Do not add this detail without a specific traceable source (documentary, news report, or court document).

**7. 9/11 death toll — CONFIRMED**
The accepted official death toll is 2,977 victims. "Nearly 3,000" is accurate.

**8. Romans 3:10-11 paraphrase — CORRECTED**
The notes rendered this with additions ("really" and "on their own") not in the ESV text. Manuscript uses the ESV text directly.

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**PASS — with qualification.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — "Christ is the only way, no one seeks God on their own, and the church must not lose the gospel to substitutes" — is derivable from John 14:6 and its surrounding argument. The main point of the sermon does arise from the text, at least at the topical level.

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text's plain claims and does not soften v.6. The exclusive "no one" is pressed rather than hedged. However, the sermon treats John 14:1-16 primarily as a proof-text for three thematic points rather than as a passage with its own internal movement (the disciples' distress, Jesus' comfort, Thomas's question, Philip's misunderstanding, the Spirit's promise). The passage is opened but not inhabited.

**Gate passes. Score proceeds.**

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 — Partial: Main point does not fully equal text's point.** John 14:1-16 is set in the upper room discourse; it is first and foremost a word of comfort to troubled disciples about Jesus' departure, the Father's house, and the coming Spirit. The sermon takes v.6 as its center but nearly ignores vv.1-5 and vv.7-16. The passage's own movement — from troubled hearts to the promise of the Helper — is never traced. The sermon uses the passage; it does not preach it. This is a partial flag (does not reach full trigger) but constrains the exposition score significantly.

**Flag 3 — Present: Christ is not quite absent, but the cross is.** The sermon names Christ as "the only way" repeatedly but never explains *why* or *how* — the atonement, justification, the specific work of the cross, penal substitution — none of these are articulated. An unbeliever sitting through this sermon hears that Jesus is the only door, but never hears what He did to open it. The door is pointed to; no one explains that it was opened by blood. This is a live fatal flaw flag.

**Flag 5 — Present: No actual exhortation.** The close offers Romans 10:9 and names the "door," but the sermon never summons anyone to walk through it. There is no moment where the preacher looks at the unconverted and says, "Repent. Believe. Today." The closest is "God is drawing you" — which is observation, not exhortation. The congregation was informed and moved toward urgency, but never summoned.

**Score is capped at 69 due to Flags 3 and 5.**

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Partially Christ. The sermon correctly orients toward Christ as the only way, but the bulk of the conscience-searching concerns the listener's own complacency (missions, politics, evangelism). The congregation is pointed toward responsibility more than toward the Savior.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas about Him, primarily. The opening statistics create emotional weight. But God himself — his holiness, his wrath against sin, his love in sending Christ, his mercy at the cross — does not appear with presence or weight. The sermon is *about* God without being *saturated* with Him.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Convicted on missions complacency — yes, and genuinely. Comforted — not meaningfully; the comfort of the gospel (that Christ's blood is sufficient, that God is for us) is not developed. Informed — significantly. The balance tips toward "informed" over "convicted and comforted."

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The 3.4 billion / 3,000 juxtaposition is a genuinely powerful opening move.** The comparison is emotionally calibrated, non-manipulative, and stays in service of the text's urgency rather than becoming spectacle. The return to this number in the close creates structural integrity that is often absent in topical-evangelical preaching.

2. **The politics/gospel application is admirably direct and self-implicating.** "I'm not throwing stones — I live in the same world you do" is honest pastoral voice. The preacher names a real idol in a real congregation without caricature or softness. This is conscience work.

3. **The Beethoven illustration for the unregenerate heart is conceptually precise.** It goes beyond the standard "lost sheep" analogy to capture the *noetic* effect of sin — the inability to even have a category for what is being sought. This earns its place in the sermon and is native to the text's argument.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The cross is absent.** The sermon preaches Christ as the only door without explaining what He did to open it. John 14:6 claims Jesus is the way — the sermon never asks why, and never answers. Penal substitution, the wrath of God, the imputed righteousness of Christ, justification by faith alone — none of these appear. The 3.4 billion need not just the address of the door but the mechanism by which it opens. This is the deepest failure in the manuscript.

2. **John 14:1-16 is a proof-text, not a preached passage.** The sermon derives one verse (v.6) and leaves fifteen others untouched. The disciples' troubled hearts (v.1), Jesus as the one who has *seen* the Father (vv.9-11), the promise of the Spirit as Comforter (vv.16), the "greater works" promise (v.12) — all of this is ignored. The passage's emotional register (comfort in sorrow, presence in departure) is never inhabited. The congregation learned John 14:6; they did not inhabit John 14.

3. **The exhortation is structurally absent.** The close lists Romans 10:9 and names the "door" as real and open — but no one is summoned. The non-Christian is told that God may be drawing them (observation) and that the door is open (description). They are not called to repent, believe, confess, turn. The Christian is told the gospel matters — but there is no "therefore go," no specific next step, no practical summons. The sermon ends before it reaches its imperative.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears to be unaware that the sermon's structural center of gravity is *not* John 14 but the missiological and apologetic concerns the preacher brought to the text. John 14:1-16 is a comfort text to grieving disciples — but the sermon mines it for its exclusivity apologetic and moves on. The preacher may not see that the text's most powerful claim for this sermon's purpose is in the *context* the disciples were in: the hour of the cross is approaching, Jesus is departing, and the disciples are terrified. The "way" Jesus is about to open is the way of his death. That's the sermon's missed engine.

Additionally, the preacher is likely unaware how much of the application lands on evangelical complacency (the congregation's failure to care about missions) rather than on the grace that motivates mission. The sermon convicts toward missions without fueling that conviction with gospel love. "Christ died for sinners like you; go tell sinners like them" is the engine — and it is not present.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The **three-point structure** creates an impression of expository thoroughness that the actual text work does not support. The structure is logically coherent and memorable — but the three points are thematic overlays, not the passage's own movement. A listener might leave feeling they received a solid biblical sermon because the outline was clean and the text was cited repeatedly. What they received was a thematically organized evangelistic talk using John 14:6 as its anchor. This is not the same thing, and the difference matters for preaching formation.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**John 14:1 is the sermon's most neglected verse, and it is the sermon's most powerful:** "Let not your hearts be troubled. Believe in God; believe also in me." This is Jesus, on the night of his betrayal, hours from the cross, commanding his disciples not to be troubled — and doing so by pointing to himself as the object of saving trust. The disciples had every reason for their hearts to be shaken. Jesus was the only reason not to be. That is not only the exclusivity argument — it is the comfort argument, the hope argument, the "I am going to prepare a place for you" argument. The sermon could have moved from those troubled disciples to every troubled conscience in the room — the person who is afraid of death, afraid of judgment, afraid of loss — and said: there is only one word that speaks to that fear, and it is the name of Jesus. That sermon would have been more faithful to John 14 than the sermon preached, and would have carried the cross at its center rather than leaving it off the page.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3-5)

1. **Preach the cross before you preach the call.** Before Romans 10:9 is read, the sermon must explain *why* Jesus is the only door: because sin incurs wrath, because the law cannot save, because Christ alone bore the penalty and rose in vindication, because faith in him — and not in any work, religion, or sincerity — is the only basis of justification. One paragraph, clearly articulated, before the close. Without it, the invitation is a door pointing into darkness.

2. **Inhabit John 14:1-5 in the opening.** The disciples are troubled. The hour is the cross. Jesus says: believe in me. The sermon currently opens with statistics; it could open with the disciples' fear and let the statistics amplify the text's own stakes. Anchor Part 1 in the emotional situation of the passage before moving to the exclusivity argument.

3. **Add a direct exhortation to the unconverted in the close.** After Romans 10:9, say plainly: "If you are here today and you have not trusted Christ — I am asking you, right now, to repent of your sin and trust him. Not to think about it. Not to come back when you're ready. Today is the day of salvation. Believe in your heart. Confess with your mouth. He will not turn you away." This is the difference between information and preaching.

4. **Cut or compress the social gospel history.** The section on prison reform and the social gospel adds a third conceptual layer (historical case study) to a sermon that has already landed the politics application with more punch. It dilutes the momentum in Part 3. Either cut it entirely and let the politics application breathe, or reduce it to one sentence bridging to the political point.

5. **Address the believer who has grown cold to the gospel's urgency.** The sermon touches complacency broadly but does not corner the person who once cared about missions and no longer does — the backslider in evangelical affection. A sentence like: "Some of you used to pray for the unreached. You used to give to missions. You used to lose sleep over the lost. What happened? The gospel didn't change. You drifted. Come back." This is the Perkins' backslider category and it would add significant pastoral penetration.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon has evangelical heat and genuine urgency. It is not dead. However, the heat is directed primarily at missions complacency rather than at the grace of Christ — which means the fire is pointed outward (toward the 3.4 billion) without first burning inward (at the cross). This is evangelical activism rather than dead orthodoxy, but it is a first cousin.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement

The sermon carries genuine evangelical weight at the missiological level — the 3.4 billion framing is morally serious and the comparison with 9/11 has the gravity of eternal consequence — but the absence of the cross means the sermon's gravity is humanitarian rather than doxological, and the Weight of Glory bonus cannot be awarded where the glory of Christ's atoning work has not been proclaimed.

**Bonus: +2/5** (for the legitimate and serious weight of the evangelistic burden, not for gospel gravity)

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

John 14:1-16 is a Farewell Discourse passage — a complex literary form that blends comfort, Christological disclosure, promissory theology, and eschatology. The rubric's categories apply well here, but the passage's primary genre is *pastoral comfort*, not *apologetic argument*. The sermon pulled the passage toward apologetic argument, which is legitimate at v.6 but strains the rest of the text. The rubric correctly identifies this as an exposition failure. The FCF for this passage is not merely "people believe in false ways" but "disciples are troubled and afraid because their Lord is departing" — and the gospel comfort available here (he prepares a place; he will come again; the Spirit will be with you) is at least as rich as the exclusivity apologetic. The rubric fit is sound; the passage had more to give than the sermon asked of it.
