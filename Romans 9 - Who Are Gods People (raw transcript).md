## Who Are God's People?

There are two groups of people that can be considered God's people.

As we all know from having read the Old Testament, God had chosen for himself a people. Though they rejected him, he still helped, protected, and led them out of every silly thing they stumbled into. Several times they rejected the Lord and did vile things in "worship" of other gods.

*[Note: The pastor references Roman temples having treatment centers for sexually transmitted diseases as a consequence of ritual prostitution. This is historically attested — sacred prostitution was practiced at certain temples in the ancient world, including some dedicated to goddesses such as Aphrodite/Venus. The claim is a reasonable historical generalization, though practices varied considerably by location and period.]*

Even so, God would raise up leaders to help them find their way back. He sometimes allowed them to go into captivity as a way of purifying his people. He led them personally for a time, but they rejected that and demanded a king. The kings may have started out good, but went bad quickly, and before long they were worshipping idols in the temple again. Chance after chance after chance was given. Time and again they would return to the Lord, only to have their children and grandchildren reject him in favor of some lump of wood or metal or rock. Yet God was patient with them.

Eventually they came to worship the system God had established to illuminate their deficiencies. Compliance with the law replaced their faith in God. Around that time God sent his son Jesus to die on the cross. Compliance with the law was superseded by faith in Christ.

## Three Views on Who Are God's People Today

**View 1 — America is God's people.** I heard someone argue this recently. There is a grain of truth in that, surrounded by some ridiculous twisting of facts.

**View 2 — The Traditional Reformed View.** At the moment of Christ's death, Israel ceased to be God's people. From that moment forward — governed by the new covenant — only those who had faith in Christ were God's people.

**View 3 — The Dispensational View.** Israel continued to be God's people, and anyone who accepts Christ is grafted into Israel.

For my part I've cherry-picked what I believe are the best parts of the last two. Scripture does support the idea that the Gentile is grafted into Israel — I believe that is true. But based upon Hosea 2, and passages in Isaiah, and based upon Romans 9:25–33, I would argue that God's people are the remnant of Israel that believed in Christ Jesus, AND any Gentile who believes in Christ Jesus is grafted into God's people.

*— Romans 9:25 quotes **Hosea 2:23** (not Hosea 1)*
*— Romans 9:26 quotes **Hosea 1:10** (not Hosea 2)*
*— Romans 9:27–28 quotes Isaiah 10:22–23 ✓*
*— Romans 9:29 quotes Isaiah 1:9 ✓]*

**What about the rest of the Jews?** I would argue they still hold a special place in God's heart — like the child that has run away with the circus. He is no less your child, but by his own decision he does not benefit from the advantages of living in your house.

One of the often-cited signs of the Lord's return is that many Jews will start becoming believers in Jesus Christ. We know based on that, that God's plan for them — and their role in the present and future — is not yet finished. God is working things for their good. But at this point they are rejecting God. Scripturally, I have a hard time with the idea that anyone who refused to believe in Jesus during their life will be with him in heaven. But I also have a hard time with the reformers' conclusion that the Jews have completely ceased being his chosen people.

Did he reject them when they worshipped Baal? Did he reject them when they violated their bodies in sexual worship of other gods? Did he reject them during any of the thousands of times they complained as God and Moses led them to safety? Certainly not. So the truth is somewhere in the middle.

We as Gentiles are grafted in, AND those Israelites who are actively rejecting God are not his people as long as they remain in that state.

With that understanding, let's re-read:

> *"For not all who are descended from Israel belong to Israel, and not all are children of Abraham because they are his offspring."*
> — Romans 9:6–7

## Is God Good?

I have a dear friend who struggles with this question. As a college student she was introduced to hyper-Calvinism, became convinced it was true, and then concluded on her own that if God chooses some and leaves others to choose him, he must not be good.

Most people hear Calvinism and get a bad taste in their mouth. Much has been said in the name of Calvinism, and modern Calvinism isn't exactly what John Calvin taught. Calvinism isn't all bad. I don't know that I agree with every last bit, but there is definite good in knowing both sides of an argument. On this particular issue and in a number of other places, I think the Calvinists are closer to the scripture than some of the alternatives.

## Calvinism vs. Arminianism

Like we talked about last week and like we read tonight — according to scripture, God chooses you.

The Arminian would say no. They use some clever language: "God is a gentleman and would never work to make someone do something against their will."

*[Note: This is an accurate representation of popular Arminian apologetics, though the phrase "God is a gentleman" is modern and informal, not from Arminius himself. The pastor correctly notes that Arminius, Luther, and the other reformers would not have held the position that God is categorically unable or unwilling to influence the human will. Arminius affirmed prevenient grace (a form of divine influence on the will) and would not have used such language to restrict God's sovereignty.]*

**Pharaoh as the test case.**

> *"But the Lord hardened Pharaoh's heart and he would not listen to Moses and Aaron, just as the Lord had said to Moses."*
> — Exodus 9:12

Also Exodus 10:20, Exodus 10:27, and Exodus 11:10.

*[All four references confirmed: Exodus 9:12, 10:20, 10:27, and 11:10 all record either God hardening Pharaoh's heart or Pharaoh hardening his own heart — often alternating. ✓]*

What we see is time after time God does a miracle, and time after time either Pharaoh hardens his own heart or God hardens it for Pharaoh. The Arminian who says God chooses not to affect the will of man clearly hasn't read this story carefully.

Is there free will? Yes, absolutely. Can God override it? Yes, absolutely.

That point is the fundamental difference between the two camps. Calvinism says: "My God is big, he is sovereign, and he has the ability to influence my actions." The Arminian says: "I am big, and God cannot or will not influence my actions." These are oversimplifications of both camps, and neither is completely wrong or completely right.

**A personal note.** I graduated from Trinity College.

*[Note: Trinity College of Florida (originally Florida Bible Institute, founded 1932) was established by William T. Watson, a pastor of a Christian and Missionary Alliance church in St. Petersburg, Florida. The pastor's description of it being "founded by a church planter from the Christian and Missionary Alliance denomination" is substantially accurate. Trinity later became nondenominational in character while retaining its evangelical foundation. Billy Graham is among its most noted alumni.]*

Trinity was founded by a church planter from the Christian and Missionary Alliance denomination — passionate about the gospel, missions, and church planting. Christian and Missionary Alliance is an Arminian denomination. So needless to say I knew a lot of Arminians. As adamant as each of them were that God couldn't affect the will or the heart of a man, without fail, anytime one of them prayed they would ask God to prepare so-and-so's heart. They would claim so-and-so for the Lord, they would beg God to intercede in so-and-so's life and draw so-and-so to him. Basically they believed in their hearts and prayers that God was sovereign over the will of man enough to ask him to change people's hearts — but when it came to their theology they got it twisted.

Once an Arminian sees God as being bigger than oneself, once they realize that God CAN overcome free will, many Arminians become Calvinists. That one shift makes a big difference across many areas of doctrine.

The Arminian believes he is stronger than God, because he believes he can give his salvation back. We know scripture says we are in God's hand, and unless we are stronger than God, once saved we can't get away. The Arminian believes that his own will determines his salvation. But scripture says:

> *"For it is by grace you have been saved… and this not of yourselves, it is the gift of God."*
> — Ephesians 2:8

*[Ephesians 2:8–9 ✓]*

Some Arminians fall into legalism — the legalist tends to seek to do it all in his own power; the Calvinist has a tendency toward antinomianism, saying "I don't have to do anything in my own power because it's God who does it anyway." I could go on.

Whether Calvinism or Arminianism is right is not the most important part here. The majority of folks who have studied scripture enough to pick a side have probably done so out of a genuine desire to believe rightly what scripture teaches. Being a Calvinist or Arminian alone is not enough to save or enough to separate you from God. There are many good, sincere, and strong Christians on both sides — and our denomination was, is, and will likely continue to be one that embraces both.

## Back to Paul's Point: Is God Just?

You'll remember we talked about Jacob and Esau — we read Romans 9:13: *"Jacob I loved and Esau I hated."*

Is God just in not choosing everyone? Is it okay that God would pick Jacob but not Esau? Paul weighs in:

> *"You will say to me then, 'Why does he still find fault? For who can resist his will?' But who are you, O man, to answer back to God? Will what is molded say to its molder, 'Why have you made me like this?' Has the potter no right over the clay, to make out of the same lump one vessel for honorable use and another for dishonorable use? What if God, desiring to show his wrath and to make known his power, has endured with much patience vessels of wrath prepared for destruction, in order to make known the riches of his glory for vessels of mercy, which he has prepared beforehand for glory."*
> — Romans 9:19–23

Paul is saying, in short, something of an echo of Job — you remember when God asks Job: "Oh, and where were you when I hung the stars in the sky? When I told the water where it could go? Where the snow is stored in summer?" We and our best understanding don't measure up. We can't understand all the complexities of God and his creation.

## Two Illustrations

### 1. The Two-Dimensional People

*[Note: This illustration is drawn from or inspired by Edwin Abbott Abbott's "Flatland: A Romance of Many Dimensions" (1884), a classic thought experiment about beings limited to two dimensions encountering objects from a higher dimension. The pastor does not attribute it by name, which is common in oral preaching.]*

The two-dimensional people were going about their way when suddenly a small circle pushed some of them aside. The circle slowly grew bigger and bigger. All they knew was that they were displaced. They couldn't sense the roundness of it, or its shape, beyond the fact that they were being displaced. The richness and scope and shape of the ball were almost entirely beyond the comprehension of the two-dimensional people, who could barely discern the presence of the ball. Eventually the size of the circle began to shrink, and before long everything was as it had been. All they had known was that something had displaced them.

In some ways we experience God in a similar way. We can know some of him, but as the scriptures say, his ways are not our ways. His thoughts are not our thoughts. His wisdom is above our wisdom. All we can know are the things he allows us to know. There are things we may never fully understand this side of heaven. What we do know is that God is good, God is sovereign, and we can trust God — even when something is difficult.

### 2. The Watering Can

*[Note: This parable, in the pastor's own original telling, involves two clay pitchers — one perfect, one with a thumb hole through the side. The processed versions in Pt 2 and Pt 3 adapted this into a "cracked pot" parable and modified several details. The following is the raw pastoral version.]*

Have any of you ever worked with clay? The master potter went out to his wheel one day and decided to make two pots.

The first one came out perfect — beautiful in its design and details. Had he entered it in an art show it would have won first place. Tiffany's would have wanted to put it in their catalog. It was quite possibly the perfect pitcher.

So the potter sits back down and decides to make a second. The second was going quite well, sure to rival the first in every way. But as the potter picked it up, his thumb plunged through the side of it.

Both pots were kilned. Both were set by the back door for use the next day in collecting water. The second pot sat there looking at the first — thinking about how beautiful the first was, how elegant, how refined, how perfect. It thought about its great hole and began running headlong into self-doubt. Surely the potter wouldn't keep a vessel that was flawed. Surely he'd throw it away. Surely he'd dash it on the rocks.

But morning came and the potter collected both pitchers and went to the well. He filled both and began walking back to the house. The first held water perfectly. The second tried furiously to keep from sloshing, to stay still so at least some water would make it back — but the end result was an epic failure. He was completely empty.

Day after day the same drama played out. Each day the second little pot grew more depressed, more dejected, more desperate to try to hold the water in. Days turned to weeks, weeks to months, months to a complete year — and still this little pitcher couldn't hold the water in.

Finally, in an act of desperation, it shouted out to the potter: *"Why would you make me this way? I'm so useless with your thumb hole in me. I thought you'd fix me, or at least smash me and make another one, but you didn't. You insist on using me every day even though I have completely failed in your purpose for me. I am useless and I don't deserve to continue existing."*

The potter looked at the pitcher and said: *"Silly child. You are not a pitcher at all — you're a watering can. That hole in your side was there by design. You are indeed perfect in every way. You are serving your purpose perfectly. Look — look at the path we walk every day. Every morning I carry you back to the house in my left hand, and every afternoon in my right. Look at all the flowers that have grown up where you've been watering, where you've been serving your master's purpose. I spent so much more time on you than I did on the other pot. It was simply meant to carry water — but you were meant to give life. Look around the path. See all the flowers? That is your purpose, little one."*

## Answering the Question: Is God Good?

Was the potter evil for making two vessels for different purposes — one for bringing water to the house, and one for watering the garden path? Certainly not.

God is not obligated to choose everyone. He chooses some of us out of an overflow of his love and grace and mercy. If those he doesn't choose don't choose him, is it God's fault — or the fault of the one rejecting God? It certainly doesn't make God evil. In fact I think it proves his goodness that he saves any of us at all. We all deserve to burn in hell. But instead he calls some of us to him — he throws us a life raft. He wishes that all would choose him, that none would perish. But if no one perished, would there really be free will? If everyone perished, he would be just. But he chose to save just the right number of people so that his will could be accomplished in the best possible way.

## Closing Applications

Often times we are like the watering can. God is using us in ways we don't understand, sometimes in spite of our best and misguided efforts to thwart him. Remember Jonah? He fought too — and what did that get him? Swallowed by a fish, spat up on land, and sent to the very people he hated, so that he too could pour out a message of life. We are in good company, because Christ poured out his blood on the cross, and by doing so allowed us to experience life, and life abundant.
