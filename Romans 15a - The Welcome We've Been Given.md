# The Welcome We've Been Given
## A Sermon on Romans 15:1-13

**Series**: The Night Is Far Gone
**Part V**: Walking in Daylight
**Previous**: Romans 14:13-23 (Love That Surrenders Its Rights)

---

## Opening

We've spent the last several weeks in Romans 14 — learning what it means to live together as people who disagree on secondary matters.

We talked about the weak and the strong.
We talked about disputable matters and non-negotiables.
We talked about not passing judgment on one another.

Most commentators agree that the chapter break between Romans 14 and 15 is in the wrong place. Paul's argument that began in 14:1 doesn't really end until 15:13. The chapter division — added centuries after Paul wrote — interrupts what was meant to be read as one continuous thought.

So don't think of Romans 15 as a new topic. Think of it as the climax of what Paul has been building toward.

And if you left Romans 14 thinking, "This is about tolerance" — you missed it.

Romans 14 is not about tolerance. Tolerance is cold. Tolerance keeps its distance. Tolerance says, "I'll put up with you."

Paul has something far warmer in mind.

Today we come to Romans 15, and Paul gives us the *why* behind everything he's been saying. It's not about tolerance.

It's about welcome.

And not just any welcome — the welcome Christ gave us.

We ask you to send that same welcome to Myanmar — through workers, through Scripture, through dreams, through whatever means you choose. Open doors. Open hearts. ## I. The Obligation of the Strong (vv. 1-3)

Paul begins with a command — and it's not optional.

*"We who are strong have an obligation to bear with the failings of the weak, and not to please ourselves."* (v. 1)

Notice: this is not advice. It's not a suggestion. It's an *obligation*.

The Greek word is *opheilomen* — we owe this. It's a debt. The strong owe something to the weak.

And what do we owe?

Not correction. Not condescension. Not rolling our eyes and tolerating them until they grow up.

We owe them *weight-bearing*.

The word "bear" here is *bastazein* — to carry, to lift up, to take on a burden. It's the same word used in Galatians 6:2: "Bear one another's burdens, and so fulfill the law of Christ."

The strong don't get to stand at a distance and critique. The strong get under the burden and lift.

### Why?

*"For Christ did not please himself, but as it is written, 'The reproaches of those who reproached you fell on me.'"* (v. 3)

Paul quotes Psalm 69:9 — a psalm of suffering, of unjust persecution, of bearing the weight of others' sin.

Christ didn't please himself.

Think about that. The Son of God — the one who deserved every pleasure, every honor, every comfort — did not come to be served. He came to serve. He came to bear.

And what did he bear?

*The reproaches that should have fallen on you fell on him.*

Your shame. Your guilt. Your failure. Your weakness.

He didn't stand at a distance and tolerate you. He got under the burden and carried it to the cross.

That's the model. That's why the strong have an obligation.

If Christ bore your weight when you had nothing to offer — how can you refuse to bear the weight of a brother or sister who is weaker than you?

### Why Does Paul Quote the Old Testament?

Paul pauses in verse 4 to explain why he just quoted Psalm 69:

*"For whatever was written in former days was written for our instruction, that through endurance and through the encouragement of the Scriptures we might have hope."* (v. 4)

This is crucial. Paul is saying: the Old Testament wasn't just written for ancient Israel. It was written *for us*. For our instruction. For our endurance. For our hope.

The Scriptures produce two things in us: *endurance* and *encouragement*. And both of those lead to *hope*.

When you're tempted to give up on a difficult brother — the Scriptures give you endurance.
When you're weary of bearing burdens that aren't yours — the Scriptures give you encouragement.
When you wonder if any of this will ever matter — the Scriptures point you to hope.

This is why we read the Word. This is why we preach the Word. Not because it's a religious habit, but because without it we have no fuel for the endurance and no ground for the hope.

## II. The Goal: One Voice (vv. 5-6)

Now Paul prays. And his prayer reveals the goal of all this weight-bearing:

*"May the God of endurance and encouragement grant you to live in such harmony with one another, in accord with Christ Jesus, that together you may with one voice glorify the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ."* (vv. 5-6)

Did you catch that?

The goal is not that we all agree on everything.
The goal is not that the weak become strong.
The goal is not that the strong win every argument.

The goal is *one voice*.

A church that glorifies God together — despite differences, despite disagreements, despite the thousand ways we could divide.

This is what the world cannot produce.

The world can produce uniformity — everyone thinking the same, speaking the same, conforming to the same.

But the world cannot produce unity in diversity. The world cannot produce a room full of different people — different backgrounds, different convictions, different weaknesses — lifting their voices together to glorify the same God.

Only Christ can do that.

And notice where it comes from: *"the God of endurance and encouragement."*

This unity isn't manufactured. It's granted. It's a gift from the God who endures with us and encourages us through his Word.

### The Night Is Far Gone Connection

We are walking in daylight now. Part V of our journey through Romans.

And people who walk in daylight walk *together*. They don't stumble over each other in the dark. They don't crash into walls they can't see. They move as one — because they can see the same sun rising.

The dawn is coming. And the dawn will bring a choir — every nation, every tongue, every tribe — singing with one voice.

We practice that now.

Every Sunday when we gather, we rehearse eternity.

## III. The Command: Welcome (v. 7)

Here's the hinge of the passage:

*"Therefore welcome one another as Christ has welcomed you, for the glory of God."* (v. 7)

This is the summary. This is the application. This is everything Paul has been building toward since Romans 14:1.

**Welcome one another.**

Not tolerate. Not endure. Not put up with.

*Welcome.*

The Greek is *proslambanesthe* — to receive into one's home, to take to oneself, to embrace fully.

And notice the grammar: this is a present middle imperative. That means Paul isn't giving a one-time command — he's calling for ongoing, continuous action. *Keep welcoming one another. Never stop receiving one another.*

This is the word used in Acts 28:2 when the natives of Malta welcomed Paul and his shipwrecked crew: they showed them "unusual kindness."

This is the word used in Philemon 17 when Paul asks Philemon to receive the runaway slave Onesimus "as you would receive me."

This is intimate. This is costly. This is real.

And where does this welcome come from? It doesn't come from you. Piper puts it this way: welcoming one another is "from him and through him and to him." It's not human hospitality dressed up in religious language. It's participation in the very welcome of Christ — receiving others the way you were received, for the same purpose: the glory of God.

### As Christ Welcomed You

And here's the standard: *as Christ has welcomed you.*

Think about how Christ welcomed you.

Did he wait until you cleaned up your act?
Did he wait until you agreed with him on every point?
Did he wait until you were strong enough to be useful?

No.

He welcomed you when you were dead in your trespasses.
He welcomed you when you were his enemy.
He welcomed you when you had nothing to offer but your sin.

*"While we were still sinners, Christ died for us."* (Romans 5:8)

That's the welcome you received.

Now give it.

## IV. The Scope: All Nations (vv. 8-12)

Now Paul does something remarkable. He stacks up Old Testament quotations — one after another — to prove that this welcome was always the plan.

*"For I tell you that Christ became a servant to the circumcised to show God's truthfulness, in order to confirm the promises given to the patriarchs, and in order that the Gentiles might glorify God for his mercy."* (vv. 8-9a)

Why does Paul suddenly bring up Jews and Gentiles?

Because behind all the talk of "weak" and "strong" lies a deeper division. The church at Rome had Jewish believers and Gentile believers sitting in the same room, eating at the same table, worshiping the same Lord — and sometimes clashing over the very things that once separated them.

John Murray notes that Paul's reference to Jews and Gentiles "suggests that the exhortation to mutual acceptance had in view the need to overcome all racial prejudice and discrimination in the communion of the saints at Rome."

This isn't abstract theology. This is table fellowship. This is who you eat with. This is who you invite into your home.

Christ came for the Jews — to confirm the promises.
And Christ came for the Gentiles — to extend the mercy.

The welcome wasn't an afterthought. It was always the design. The promises God made to the patriarchs — to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — were never meant to stay within one ethnic boundary. God told Abraham: *"in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed"* (Genesis 12:3). The promise to the patriarchs was always global in scope.

And Paul proves it with four Old Testament passages:

1. **2 Samuel 22:50 / Psalm 18:49**: "Therefore I will praise you among the Gentiles, and sing to your name."

2. **Deuteronomy 32:43**: "Rejoice, O Gentiles, with his people."

3. **Psalm 117:1**: "Praise the Lord, all you Gentiles, and let all the peoples extol him."

4. **Isaiah 11:10**: "The root of Jesse will come, even he who arises to rule the Gentiles; in him will the Gentiles hope."

Four quotations. Four different sources — the Law, the Prophets, and two from the Psalms. All making the same point: the inclusion of the Gentiles in the praise of God has always been part of God's purposes.

Look at that first quotation. It's from a psalm of David — Psalm 18 — where David praises God for victory over the nations. But Thomas Schreiner notes that Paul may be reading this typologically: he hears the risen Christ speaking these words. David, as the anointed king, pointed forward to the greater Anointed One. So when David says *"I will praise you among the Gentiles,"* Paul hears the Messiah claiming the nations as his own.

This changes everything. It's not just that God predicted Gentile inclusion. It's that the Messiah himself — in the songs of Israel — was already singing about it.

Do you see what Paul is doing?

He's saying: this isn't new. This was always God's plan. The Law, the Prophets, the Psalms — they all pointed to a day when the nations would be welcomed.

The thief on the cross — welcomed.
The Roman centurion — welcomed.
The Samaritan woman — welcomed.
You — welcomed.

### The Dawn of All Nations

Isaiah's vision in chapter 11 is the vision of the dawn.

*"The root of Jesse will come, even he who arises to rule the Gentiles; in him will the Gentiles hope."*

This is where history is going. Not to tribal division. Not to national isolation. Not to ethnic fragmentation.

History is going to Revelation 7:9-10:

*"After this I looked, and behold, a great multitude that no one could number, from every nation, from all tribes and peoples and languages, standing before the throne and before the Lamb, clothed in white robes, with palm branches in their hands, and crying out with a loud voice, 'Salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!'"*

That's the dawn. That's where we're walking.

And the way we welcome one another now — across differences, across weaknesses, across the thousand things that could divide us — is practice for that day.

## V. The Benediction: Abounding in Hope (v. 13)

Paul closes with a benediction — a prayer-wish that summarizes everything:

*"May the God of hope fill you with all joy and peace in believing, so that by the power of the Holy Spirit you may abound in hope."* (v. 13)

Three things to notice:

**1. The Source: The God of Hope**

Not a distant God. Not a God who tolerates you. A God of hope — a God who is himself the ground of your hope.

**2. The Content: Joy and Peace in Believing**

Not joy and peace in achieving.
Not joy and peace in being strong.
Joy and peace *in believing* — in trusting, in resting, in receiving.

**3. The Result: Abounding in Hope**

Not surviving in hope. Not scraping by in hope.

*Abounding.*

Overflowing. Spilling over. More than enough.

And how? *By the power of the Holy Spirit.*

This is not something you manufacture. This is something the Spirit produces in you as you trust the God of hope.

## What This Means for You

**For the one who feels weak:**

You are not a burden. You are not a project. You are not merely tolerated.

You are welcomed — the same way Christ welcomed every sinner who ever came to him. The strong in this church owe you weight-bearing, not condescension. And if anyone makes you feel like a second-class citizen of the kingdom, they have forgotten how they got in.

**For the one who feels strong:**

Your strength is not for yourself. It's for service.

You owe a debt to the weak — not because they earned it, but because Christ bore your weakness first. Every time you're tempted to roll your eyes, to dismiss, to "tolerate" — remember how you were welcomed.

**For the one whose marriage is struggling:**

Can you welcome your spouse the way Christ welcomed you?

Not waiting for them to change first. Not keeping score. Not tolerating them until they meet your expectations.

What if you got under the burden instead of standing at a distance critiquing?

**For the one estranged from family:**

Welcome doesn't mean pretending the hurt didn't happen. But it does mean leaving the door open.

Christ left the door open for you — while you were still a sinner.

**For the one fighting addiction:**

You are still welcome.

You didn't get clean first. You didn't earn it. Christ welcomed you in your mess — and the church should do the same.

The struggle doesn't disqualify you. The struggle is exactly where grace meets you.

---

## Closing

The night is far gone. The day is at hand.

And people who walk in daylight walk together. They bear one another's burdens. They welcome one another. They lift their voices as one.

We are practicing for the dawn — for that great day when every nation, every tribe, every tongue will stand before the throne and sing.

Until then, we welcome.

Not because the weak have earned it.
Not because the strong are generous.
Because Christ welcomed us.

And that welcome — unearned, undeserved, utterly free — is the foundation of everything.

*"Therefore welcome one another as Christ has welcomed you, for the glory of God."*

May the God of hope fill you with all joy and peace in believing, so that by the power of the Holy Spirit you may abound in hope.

---

## Benediction

May the God who bore your weakness give you strength to bear another's.
May the Christ who welcomed you when you were far off draw you near to one another.
And may the Spirit who fills you with hope make that hope overflow — until the dawn.

Amen.
