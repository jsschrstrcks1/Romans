# Psalm 145 — Good to All

**Text: Psalm 145** (full)

---

Imagine with me for a minute someone you love. A child or grandchild. Your spouse. Someone close. Imagine that this person has been falsely accused. They were a good person — loving, kind, the kind of person who went out of their way to help people. But along the way they threatened a powerful group. A shadowy organization — organized crime, a mafia if you will.

The mafia arranges a corrupt charge, enforced by a corrupt judge. Your loved one is arrested.

I remember a time when someone I knew was accused of something. In those situations you usually have one of two reactions. My first thought in that case was, *"Oh. Well. That actually makes some sense."* But other times I've thought — no. That just isn't right. That person did not, would not, could not do what they're being accused of. You know what I mean?

Back to our story. Your loved one is in that second category. Even people who only knew them on the edges argued with the accusers — "That couldn't possibly be true." But the mafia has rigged the court. Rigged the jury. Rigged the prosecution and made sure the defense was worthless. Open and shut. And sadly, it finds your loved one guilty.

The sentence? Death.

The executioner they selected never makes a mistake — which is both horrible and merciful, because the method of execution is awful. For your loved one to linger longer than necessary would be even more awful. When the executioner doesn't get it exactly right, the men with the baseball bats come and break the legs, and then confirm death by driving a spear through the heart. Naturally, if it has to happen, quicker is kinder.

But it gets worse.

They plan to fillet your loved one. They will disfigure this person's body until their insides are on the outside, and then march them through the streets of Hudson — an evil parade, in front of everyone.

Later, after everything is done, one of the men on the periphery of the conspiracy — the one who drove the spear — comes to you and asks your forgiveness for his part in the murder of your innocent child.

What would you say?

---

I know a man whose friend went through something real and very close to this.

My friend was a missionary in Chad — which isn't exactly known for its fair and just legal system. His friend, we'll call him Lou, had a daughter who was kidnapped, raped, and murdered. Her body was left in a river.

Over time they found a man who confessed to being part of it. In his confession he admitted that something like sixty men had raped her. They had kept her unconscious with drugs. They raped her until she died, and then they threw her body in the river. They very nearly got away with it. One of the men involved agreed to tell the story in exchange for a lighter sentence.

A few years later, God brought my friend and Lou to the very prison that housed one of the men who had killed Lou's daughter. They didn't know it. The man didn't know it. My friend preached the gospel. An altar call was given. At the end, Lou was assigned to talk to one particular table — the table that held the man who had killed his little girl.

Could you imagine? Could you extend God's grace to the man who killed your child in such a way?

Lou wept. And he hugged the man. And he shared the gospel with him.

---

With that as our backdrop, let's read our passage.

---

There is so much in this psalm. But I want to zero in on one verse, because I think it's the key to everything else here.

Verse 9:

> The LORD is good to all,
>     and his mercy is over all that he has made.

*All.*

That word includes a man named Longinus.

We don't know for certain that was his real name — tradition gives him that name, and several ancient sources broadly agree on it, though the historical record is thin. What those sources tend to agree on is this: he was a Roman centurion present at the crucifixion. He may have been the man who held Jesus's wrists while another drove the nails through them. He may have held the feet of Christ as he was nailed to the cross. Maybe he was the man who swung the hammer. Maybe he was the man who swung the Roman flagrum — the multi-thonged scourge with weighted metal tips — that shredded Christ's flesh and muscle and left him nearly unrecognizable. The instrument that caused his insides to be on the outside.

One thing the sources widely agree on: he was the man who drove a spear into Christ's side to confirm he was dead.

And we know from Matthew 27:54 what happened to at least one of the centurions that day —

> When the centurion and those who were with him, keeping watch over Jesus, saw the earthquake and what took place, they were filled with awe and said, "Truly this was the Son of God!"

Tradition holds that Longinus became a Christian. The Catholic Church counts him as a saint.

The important thing isn't the tradition. The important thing is the forgiveness — even for the men who swung the whip, who swung the hammer, who drove the spear into the heart of the Son of God.

Could you forgive a person like that? I'm not sure anything but God can produce the kind of forgiveness Lou showed. Or the kind of forgiveness God showed toward those who wielded the instruments of Christ's death.

---

But here's the thing. In a real sense, we are all just as guilty as Longinus.

You may never have swung a hammer and driven nails through someone's hands. But you and I are both guilty of committing sins that required the death of Christ. As the hymn puts it — *"it was my sin that held him there, until it was accomplished."* We didn't swing the hammer, but our actions and inactions created the need for Christ to go to that cross. Even if we only ever committed one sin. One little white lie is enough.

This is the backdrop for this whole psalm. It's in that context that verse 9 lands: *"The LORD is good to all, and his mercy is over all that he has made."*

That's us. You and I are no better and no worse than those who swung the hammer and drove the nails. No worse than those who held him there. No worse than any other descendant of Adam. No better, either. We have an equal share in the shame and guilt of putting to death the Son of God.

And yet —

Verse 10:

> All your works shall give thanks to you, O LORD,
>     and all your saints shall bless you!

We are being given a pardon we don't deserve and can never earn. We are offered freedom and life when we deserve death and hell. For that, we should praise him. We should speak of his glory, his power, his kingdom — verse 11. We should tell everyone we encounter how they too can receive this kind of love. This kind of mercy.

There is no sin so ugly that God's grace and mercy can't forgive it.

---

Charles Spurgeon wrote about verse 2 — *"Every day I will bless you"* — this way:

> "He uses the word 'bless' not merely for variation of sound, but also for the deepening and sweetening of the sense. To bless God is to praise him with a personal affection for him, and a wishing well to him: this is a growingly easy exercise as we advance in experience and grow in grace."

And in a sermon on these opening verses, Spurgeon observed:

> "Observe that David is firmly resolved to praise God. My text has four 'I wills' in it. Frequently it is foolish for us poor mortals to say 'I will,' because our will is so feeble and fickle; but when we resolve upon the praise of God, we may say, 'I will,' and 'I will,' and 'I will,' and 'I will,' till we make a solid square of determinations."

David heaps praise after praise. Maybe because he understood the weight of his own sin debt. David was a murderer. He had been a terrible father. He had been disobedient to God — and on and on. We all know who David was. David knew who he was too, and so did God. But God didn't reject David. And so David praises him — so earnestly that commentator David Guzik writes: "We get the feeling that David felt it would be dishonorable to withhold his praise to God or to give Him half-hearted praise."

I think David understood having a debt he could never fully repay. The best he could do — the best we can do — is praise God. Not because we have to, or because it's required. We are free. There are no strings. But out of gratitude. Out of thanksgiving. He would have been right to include strings, to attach conditions. But he didn't. Want your debt paid? Here. Free.

---

Now — this psalm employs a form of Hebrew poetry called chiasm, or reverse parallelism. It's a literary device that helps people remember: a series of supporting points builds toward a central thesis, and then those supporting points are restated. Some readers identify two chiasms in this psalm.

The central axis of the first — the thesis everything builds toward — is in verses 8 and 9:

> The LORD is gracious and merciful,
>     slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love.
> The LORD is good to all,
>     and his mercy is over all that he has made.

The central axis of the second is in verses 17 and 18:

> The LORD is righteous in all his ways
>     and kind in all his works.
> The LORD is near to all who call on him,
>     to all who call on him in truth.

Everything else in the psalm builds toward both of these statements. The grace of God. The compassion of God. The steadfast love of God. The righteousness of God. The kindness of God. The nearness of God. His quickness to help — not just those the world considers deserving. Because in truth, none of us deserve it. None can. And yet he helps all who call on him.

---

Let me walk through some of what this psalm says about God.

**Verse 20** — He watches over all who love him.

**Verse 19** — He fulfills the desire of those who fear him. He hears their cry and saves them.

**Verse 16** — He satisfies the desire of every living thing.

**Verse 15** — He meets our needs. The eyes of all look to him, and he gives food in due season.

**Verse 14** — The LORD upholds all who are falling and raises up all who are bowed down. He holds us up when we can't stand. He lifts us when we've collapsed.

**Verse 13** — His kingdom is an everlasting kingdom. It will never end. He is sovereign over all things — past, present, future. His kingdom is glorious and splendid.

**Verse 7** — God is abundantly good and righteous.

**Verses 5 and 6** — Not only is everything God does awesome — literally something that inspires awe — but who he *is* is glorious and splendid. His majesty. His wondrous works. Awesome deeds.

And because of all of this —

**Verses 1 through 3** — We should exalt God. Praise his name. Extol it. Exalt means to hold in high regard. Extol means to praise vigorously. Not once — every day, verse 2. Not for a season — forever and ever.

Our feeble minds can't even grasp all the reasons we have to praise God. We can't conceive of it in our most expansive imaginations. So verse 4 — we should tell our children, our friends, everyone. We should commend God to the next generation. We should speak of the wonderful things he has done for us. We should meditate on those good things.

---

I think it's tempting — let me back up.

I was at dinner this week with someone. All through the meal, everything was negative. My life is awful. Things are so hard. Woe is me. Feel bad for me. And yet this same person has just been given — basically given — thousands, maybe tens of thousands of dollars' worth of help. Their response wasn't gratitude. It was complaints and sadness.

Have you ever given someone a gift, only to get that kind of response? We knew a kid like that. Loved her to death. But when you gave her a present — no matter how much thought and work and expense went into it — her response was: *"Why would you give me that? Do you even know me?"*

It's easy to fall into that trap. And I understand it — this world is hard. Jesus said so himself. But he also said, *"Take heart; I have overcome the world"* (John 16:33). Don't fall into the habit of focusing on all the bad things that can and do happen. Keep your focus on God — the founder and perfecter of our faith (Hebrews 12:2). Keep your focus on what he has done. We have so much to be grateful for, every single day. Keep God and his work in the foreground, and let the rest of the junk fade where it belongs.

---

Now — if you don't yet know Christ, not all of these promises are for you. Some of them, yes. But the big ones — salvation chief among them — are not yet yours.

That starts with the gospel.

We've already covered much of it this morning. We deserved far worse than we get. We have all sinned. We are all guilty, in the death of Christ. We swung the hammer even if we never touched the nail. We drove the spear even if we were miles away. Every sin we have ever committed put him on that cross.

But the LORD is good to all, and his mercy is over all that he has made.

Even you.

## Source Notes

*Items flagged for verification before pulpit or print use.*

**Matthew 27:54 — corrected in manuscript.** Draft used NIV wording ("were terrified, and exclaimed, 'Surely he was the Son of God!'"). Updated to ESV: "they were filled with awe and said, 'Truly this was the Son of God!'"

**Psalm 145 verse citations — corrected in manuscript.** Draft cited several verses in NIV wording. Key corrections applied:
- v.8: "rich in love" (NIV) → "abounding in steadfast love" (ESV)
- v.14: "holds us up / lifts up those who are bowed down" (paraphrase) → ESV: "upholds all who are falling and raises up all who are bowed down"
- v.17: "faithful in all he does" (NIV) → "kind in all his works" (ESV)
- v.19: "fulfills their desires" → ESV: "fulfills the desire of those who fear him; he also hears their cry and saves them"

**Psalm 145 as acrostic psalm.** Psalm 145 is also an acrostic psalm — each verse begins with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet (aleph through taw; one letter, nun, is missing in the Masoretic text but present in the Septuagint and Dead Sea Scrolls). The chiastic reading is a valid interpretive framework, but the acrostic structure is the primary literary feature scholars identify. Attribution for the two-chiasm analysis should be noted if it comes from a specific commentary.

**Longinus — tradition vs. history.** Longinus is named in early Christian documents including the Acts of Pilate (part of the Gospel of Nicodemus, 4th–5th century). He does not appear in canonical Scripture or in extant Roman military records. The traditions about his later conversion and martyrdom are matters of church tradition and hagiography, not documented secular history. The Catholic Church's feast day for Longinus is March 15.

**"Cornelius, the man whose daughter was healed earlier in the book of Matthew" — removed from manuscript.** The draft sentence conflated three distinct figures: (1) the unnamed centurion of Matthew 8:5-13, whose servant/son was healed; (2) Jairus (Matthew 9:18-26), a synagogue ruler — not a centurion — whose daughter was raised; and (3) Cornelius of Acts 10, a centurion who became the first Gentile Christian convert, with no daughter healing associated. Some early traditions do identify the Matthew 8 centurion with Longinus, and others with Cornelius, but these are separate traditions. The sentence was removed to avoid propagating the confusion.

**Roman flagrum / "cat o' nine tails" — corrected in manuscript.** The Roman instrument of scourging was a flagrum or flagellum — a multi-thonged whip with attached pieces of bone or metal. The "cat o' nine tails" is a specific naval punishment instrument from a later period. The manuscript uses "Roman flagrum" instead.

**"It was my sin that held him there, until it was accomplished"** — This lyric is from *"How Deep the Father's Love for Us"* by Stuart Townend (1995). It is a modern worship song widely used as a congregational hymn. The manuscript retains "hymn" as common congregational usage; if cited in print, attribution to Townend (1995) is appropriate.

**Spurgeon quotes — both from Treasury of David on Psalm 145.** Both quotes are plausible Spurgeon; verify exact wording and location in Treasury of David before citing in print. The first quote (on blessing God with personal affection) and the second (on the four "I wills") are attributed to his Psalm 145 commentary.

**"David considered it dishonorable to withhold his praise or give God half-hearted praise"** — Presented as something "some have suggested." Source not identified in the draft; needs attribution before use in print.

**"Take heart; I have overcome the world"** — John 16:33 ESV: "But take heart; I have overcome the world." Matches ESV. Correct as cited.

**Hebrews 12:2 — "founder and perfecter"** — ESV (2011 revision) reads "the founder and perfecter of our faith." The pre-2011 ESV used "author and perfecter." Manuscript updated to current ESV ("founder and perfecter"). Either rendering is defensible depending on which ESV edition is in use; note for consistency.

**Lou's story / Chad missionary account.** Presented as a real account from the pastor's personal connection to a missionary friend. No identifying names used in the manuscript. If used in any print or broader distribution context, verify that the missionary friend and Lou are comfortable with the account being shared publicly.
