## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Heart, Preaching Skeleton: A Sermon That Wants to Be but Hasn't Fully Arrived**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm but structurally underdeveloped opening of John 12 that captures genuine gospel instincts and pastoral heart while lacking exegetical discipline, theological precision, and the applicatory depth the text demands.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with significant qualification.**

The Scopus Test: The sermon's thesis is approximately "How you respond to Jesus determines everything — like Judas (rejection) or like Mary (surrender)." This thesis does arise from the passage, which contrasts Judas' greed with Mary's extravagant worship. The main point loosely tracks the text's own contrast.

The Authority Test: The preacher does not stand above the text as editor — he engages it sincerely and with evident belief. He does not soften or omit the hard material on Judas. He passes, but barely: the sermon spends considerable energy on speculative background material about Judas that the text itself does not support, and the passage's third major movement (the crowds, Palm Sunday approach, the Pharisees' despair in vv. 12–19) is entirely absent.

**Gate Result: Pass (qualified). The main point arises from the text, but the sermon treats only a fragment of the passage.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No score cap triggered**, but two flags are near-threshold and must be named:

- **Flag 4 (Application is entirely generic) — NEAR MISS**: The "Are you like Judas or like Mary?" binary is present and has real applicatory force, but it operates as a broad typology rather than pressing specific sins in specific hearers. The application does not differentiate within the congregation with Perkins-level precision.
- **Flag 5 (No actual exhortation) — NEAR MISS**: The closing paragraphs approach exhortation, but the call to response is soft ("ask yourself, are you sure?") rather than direct. The unbeliever is nudged rather than summoned.

No fatal flaw flags are formally triggered. Score is not capped.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Mixed. The Judas section presses self-examination well. The Mary section points toward Christ, but the application ("be like Mary") pulls back toward self-focus.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Mostly ideas. The warmth of the pastoral concern is present, but the sermon does not produce an encounter with the living God. The song lyric does more emotional lifting than the proclamation.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Lightly convicted (the "Are you like Judas?" section has real bite) and somewhat informed, but not deeply comforted. The gospel of grace is stated but not proclaimed with the force that produces comfort.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Genuine Pastoral Directness in the Judas Application**: "Are you here for some other angle? Do you have knowledge about Jesus without knowing him?" — This is a real question, asked with real care for the congregation. It has Puritan instinct even without Puritan development. The preacher knows that religious self-deception is the most dangerous condition in the room.

2. **Appropriate Epistemic Humility About Non-Canonical Material**: The immediate dismissal of the apocryphal Judas texts ("We don't know the truth, scripture is the only source of absolute truth and it is quiet on the matter") is exactly right. The preacher does not sensationalize, does not over-speculate, and correctly returns to Scripture as the only authoritative source. This is a good instinct that should be extended to the entire sermon's method.

3. **Vivid Incarnational Imagery**: "We were like Jesus feet. Dirty, cracked, stinky old sinners" — this is earthy, honest, and genuinely memorable. It connects the physical detail of the passage to the spiritual reality of the congregation's need. This is the kind of imaginative bridge that good preaching builds.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The Sermon Does Not Open John 12:7**: Jesus himself interprets Mary's act — "It was intended that she should save this perfume for the day of my burial." This is the exegetical and theological center of the entire episode. The anointing is a preparation for the cross. Mary is not merely worshipping a wonderful teacher — she is anointing the Lamb before slaughter. The sermon bypasses this entirely and reads the anointing as a generic response to wonder (at Lazarus' resurrection), losing the Passion gravity that makes this text what it is.

2. **The Structure Is Incomplete**: The title announces "Three people and one dirty dog." The sermon delivers two people (Judas and Mary) and the dog. The third character — likely meant to be a figure from vv. 12–19, perhaps the crowd, or the Pharisees, or Lazarus himself — is entirely absent. This is either a draft that was not finished or a structural promise that was abandoned without explanation. Either way, the congregation is left with an asymmetrical structure that undermines the sermon's coherence.

3. **The Gospel Is Stated But Not Proclaimed**: "Confessing the Lord Jesus Christ with your mouth, and believing that God raised him from the dead in your heart makes you saved" is doctrinally correct but functionally an announcement rather than a proclamation. What did Christ accomplish? For whom? At what cost? Why should the cost matter to me? The cross is gestured toward ("He gave his life") but never explained as penal substitution, never connected to the wrath that makes grace astonishing, never opened so that the congregation understands why the gift requires a response rather than just inspires one.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

- **The text's Passion frame is invisible to the preacher.** John 12 is not primarily a contrast between Judas and Mary — it is the opening of the Passion sequence. Jesus is already moving toward the cross. The anointing is burial preparation. The preacher sees the characters but not the shadow of Golgotha that falls over all of them. This creates a sermon that feels inspirational where the text is urgent.

- **The "prayer of salvation" framing at the close** ("Forgiveness is just a prayer away") risks reducing conversion to a transaction. The preacher's theology is better than this phrase suggests, but the language may leave hearers thinking a prayer accomplishes what only the Spirit and the blood accomplish.

- **Philip is mentioned in the closing exhortation** ("Be more like Philip") but Philip has not appeared in the sermon. This is likely a remnant of a more developed outline and is confusing to anyone tracking the sermon's argument.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Third Day song and the "Jesus Paid It All" hymn quote **feel like gospel proclamation** but are functioning as **emotional substitutes for proclamation**. The congregation hears the theology through a familiar song's voice rather than through the preacher's own heralding. This creates the impression that the gospel has been proclaimed when it has been referenced. A congregation that has heard this sermon has been pointed toward the gospel but has not been driven through it by the preacher's own voice. The song does what the sermon should have done.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**John 12:7 — "It was intended that she should save this perfume for the day of my burial."**

This is the most important verse in the passage for this sermon's own argument, and it goes unused. If the sermon is asking "How should we respond to Jesus?" — Jesus himself answers: with the full weight of what he is about to do. Mary's extravagance is not merely grateful worship of a miracle-worker. It is an anointing. It is funerary preparation. It anticipates the cross. If the preacher had opened this verse, the sermon would have had a cross-centered gravity that currently only the hymn quote provides. The contrast with Judas would also have sharpened: Judas is concerned with money; Mary is giving everything she has to prepare the one who is giving everything he has. The cross would have been the hinge on which the entire sermon swings.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild around John 12:7 as the sermon's center.** Jesus interprets the anointing as burial preparation. Make this the pivot: Mary doesn't fully understand what she's doing, but she is doing the right thing at the right time. The cross is already present in this room. Structure the sermon around what this means for Judas (who betrays the one being anointed for burial), Mary (who anoints him), and — if the third character is retained — the crowd or the Pharisees (who respond to the same reality in different ways).

2. **Replace the Third Day song with your own proclamation.** The theological content of the song lyric is strong. But the preacher should say it in his own words: "What do you have to give that is worth what he gave? Nothing. And yet he does not ask you for what you cannot give — he asks you to receive what you cannot earn." Let the preacher's own voice carry the gospel rather than outsourcing it to a recording artist.

3. **Sharpen the exhortation from invitation to summons.** "Ask yourself, are you sure?" is a good question. But add a direct command: "If you are not sure, do not leave this room in that condition. You have been warned. You have heard the gospel. Now respond. Repent and believe." The congregation should feel the weight of the moment, not just its warmth.

4. **Either deliver the third character or drop the title.** "Three people and one dirty dog" promises three people. Deliver the third — perhaps the crowd of vv. 12–19 who see the resurrection but do not truly understand, making them a middle category between Judas' rejection and Mary's surrender. Or change the title to reflect what the sermon actually is. An incomplete structural promise undermines credibility.

5. **Apply Perkins' differentiation to the closing.** The current binary (Judas-type / Mary-type) addresses the unconverted and the growing believer. Add one more: the backslider who once responded like Mary but has returned to the vomit of the world. "Some of you were like Mary once. You gave everything. And then life happened, and you pulled back, and you are still here but you are not here with everything. What would it look like to anoint his feet again today?"
