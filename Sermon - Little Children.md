# Little Children
## 1 John 2:28–3:10

---

## Little Children

The phrase "little children" is a term of endearment in the Greek. In English it can come across as condescending — something you'd hear on a schoolyard as an insult. But in the Greek it carries the warmth of fatherly love. It's the kind of thing a beloved grandfather would say.

And I think that's a helpful picture for understanding John when he wrote this letter. While Paul was teaching the faith in the hall of Tyrannus, I can picture John — a beloved grandfather sitting around a fire, having a chat with the churches he had come to love. This was written near the end of John's life. He's not lecturing. He's talking to people he deeply cares about.

1 John 2:28:

> And now, little children, abide in him, so that when he appears we may have confidence and not shrink from him in shame at his coming.
> — 1 John 2:28 (ESV)

---

## Remain in Him

Abide in him. Remain in him. What does that look like practically?

Reading your Bible daily. Prayer. Fellowship with other believers. Worship. Evangelism. These are not electives — they're co-requisites. You don't pick and choose. They work together.

We grow when we communicate with God — through prayer, through reading Scripture, through worship. And we grow when we communicate to each other what God has done for us — through evangelism and fellowship. These are not optional extras for the mature Christian. They are the baseline.

People who ignore these are like those kids that walk around with earphones in their ears all day. You can't talk to them. They can't hear you. They've shut out the world — and they've shut out the people who are trying to reach them.

Some of you are in the middle of major transitions right now. New kids. Family in from out of town. People moving. Life is loud. But the psalmist says:

> "Be still, and know that I am God. I will be exalted among the nations, I will be exalted in the earth!"
> — Psalm 46:10 (ESV)

This is necessary to remain in him. You can't abide if you won't be still.

Jesus said it himself in John 15:4:

> "Abide in me, and I in you. As the branch cannot bear fruit by itself, unless it abides in the vine, neither can you, unless you abide in me."
> — John 15:4 (ESV)

The whole vine-and-branches passage in John 15:1–11 drives this home. Cut off from the vine, the branch is dead wood. Connected to the vine, it bears fruit. There is no third option.

## Children of God

Now look at 1 John 3:1:

> See what kind of love the Father has given to us, that we should be called children of God; and so we are. The reason why the world does not know us is that it did not know him.
> — 1 John 3:1 (ESV)

A man named after the apostle Mark once said something interesting on his deathbed. Marco Polo was talking to someone a day or so before he passed, and he was asked what the most interesting thing he'd seen while crossing China was. Marco responded that the man should read his book about his travels. But that he had left out the most interesting things — because they were so much more fantastic that no one would have ever allowed them to be printed. They would seem false.

"I have not told half of what I saw," he said.

I think that's a picture of what John is getting at here. We are princes and princesses — sons and daughters of the Most High God. He's the King of kings and Lord of lords. We are his heirs. And we are not known because he is not known.

It's interesting — and slightly amusing — to see the hubbub about the royal family visiting America. Met with celebrities, the president, and other noteworthy people in our country. When they come they don't do so in any sort of official capacity. They don't come to offer us anything. But we throw them dinners and other niceties appropriate to their position as the royal family of England.

Now — compare that with Christ. Born in a manger. In a stall. In what probably amounted to a feed trough. He was greeted by pagans, by paupers, and threatened by a king. Yet he offered us *everything*. In the words of the old hymn — we owed a debt we could not pay; he paid a debt he did not owe.

Yet no paparazzi. No state dinners. No pageantry — just animals. A stable. The smell of feces.

## The Practice of Sin

Pay special attention to the phrase "makes a practice of sinning." It comes up a few times in this passage and is helpful for understanding what John is saying. Look at 1 John 3:4–6:

> Everyone who makes a practice of sinning also practices lawlessness; sin is lawlessness. You know that he appeared in order to take away sins, and in him there is no sin. No one who abides in him keeps on sinning; no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him.
> — 1 John 3:4–6 (ESV)

This is not about sinning once and falling. This is about sinning without ceasing. The Greek carries the idea of ongoing, habitual, unrepentant practice. John is not setting an expectation of earthly perfection. Our sins are covered by the blood of the Lamb. Our lives are, as Paul says:

> hidden with Christ in God.
> — Colossians 3:3 (ESV)

But there is a difference between a Christian who stumbles and grieves over their sin, and a person who wallows in it as a way of life.

## The Righteous Do What Is Right

Notice with me verse 7:

> Whoever practices righteousness is righteous, as he is righteous.
> — 1 John 3:7 (ESV)

Now hear me — doing right does not *make* us become righteous. Doing what is right is *becoming* to the righteous. Stephen Davey put it well with that distinction. Rephrased, you could read it this way: "Everyone who is righteous does what is right." The doing flows from the being — not the other way around.

Then verse 8:

> Whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil, for the devil has been sinning from the beginning. The reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the works of the devil.
> — 1 John 3:8 (ESV)

The one who practices sin — literally in the Greek, who makes it their ongoing habit — is of the devil. Doctors don't know it all, otherwise they wouldn't call it a "practice." But the devil has been at it a long time. And those apart from Christ have one thing they are exceedingly good at — sin.

The wages of sin is death. And bad motives mar even the most humanitarian people in the world. Seeking celebrity. Applause. Potential financial gain. Trying to buy their way into heaven or atone for past mistakes. All are improper motives for serving God.

## The Dog and His Vomit

He is righteous. He takes away our sin. And returning to sin is like a dog returning to its vomit:

> Like a dog that returns to his vomit is a fool who repeats his folly.
> — Proverbs 26:11 (ESV)

It's gross. But I think that's how God sees it. He is infinitely holy and just. Sin is infinitely wicked and wrong. How can the two mix?

So often people take for granted the rules we live by. How many of you have sped this week? Ran a red light? A stop sign? We treat traffic laws — laws that exist to protect us and others — like suggestions. Are you so in love with God that you can't help talking about the Lord with other people? Or have you forgotten the love that should be first in your life?

---

## Known by Their Fruit

You have heard it said — you will know them by their fruit. What kind of fruit do you have in your own life? What kind of fruit do you see in the lives of those you pour your life into?

Some of you know exactly what I'm talking about. You've been pouring into someone and the fruit is growing. Others of you — and you know who you are — you've been coasting. No one is growing because you haven't planted anything. You haven't invested in anyone. You're consuming but not producing.

This is multiplication discipleship. It's not just about what *you* bear — it's about what grows from the people you invest in. So go. Find someone. Pour into them. That is the call.

## Closing

J. I. Packer, in *Knowing God*, wrote:

> In the New Testament, grace means God's love in action toward people who merited the opposite of love. Grace means God moving heaven and earth to save sinners who could not lift a finger to save themselves. Grace means God sending his only Son to the cross to descend into hell so that we guilty ones might be reconciled to God and received into heaven.
> — J. I. Packer, *Knowing God*

A Puritan prayer from *The Valley of Vision*:

> May the matter of my prayer be always wise, humble, submissive, obedient, scriptural, and Christ-like. Give me unwavering faith that supplications are never in vain — that if I seem not to obtain my petitions, I shall have larger, richer answers, surpassing all that I ask or think.
> — *The Valley of Vision*, "The Prayer of Love"

We are at our spiritual best when we are shipwrecked on the island of God's sovereignty. Amen.
