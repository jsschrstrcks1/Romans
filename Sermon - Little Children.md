# Little Children
## 1 John 2:28–3:10

---

## Little Children

The phrase "little children" is a term of endearment in the Greek. In English it can come across as condescending — something you'd hear on a schoolyard as an insult. But in the Greek it carries the warmth of fatherly love. It's the kind of thing a beloved grandfather would say.

And I think that's a helpful picture for understanding John when he wrote this letter. While Paul was teaching the faith in the hall of Tyrannus, I can picture John — a beloved grandfather sitting around a fire, having a chat with the churches he had come to love. This was written near the end of John's life. He's not lecturing. He's talking to people he deeply cares about.

1 John 2:28:

> And now, little children, abide in him, so that when he appears we may have confidence and not shrink from him in shame at his coming.
> — 1 John 2:28 (ESV)

---

## Remain in Him

Abide in him. Remain in him. What does that look like practically?

Reading your Bible daily. Prayer. Fellowship with other believers. Worship. Evangelism. These are not electives — they're co-requisites. You don't pick and choose. They work together.

We grow when we communicate with God — through prayer, through reading Scripture, through worship. And we grow when we communicate to each other what God has done for us — through evangelism and fellowship. These are not optional extras for the mature Christian. They are the baseline.

People who ignore these are like those kids that walk around with earphones in their ears all day. You can't talk to them. They can't hear you. They've shut out the world — and they've shut out the people who are trying to reach them.

Some of you are in the middle of major transitions right now. New kids. Family in from out of town. People moving. Life is loud. But the psalmist says:

> "Be still, and know that I am God. I will be exalted among the nations, I will be exalted in the earth!"
> — Psalm 46:10 (ESV)

This is necessary to remain in him. You can't abide if you won't be still.

Jesus said it himself in John 15:4:

> "Abide in me, and I in you. As the branch cannot bear fruit by itself, unless it abides in the vine, neither can you, unless you abide in me."
> — John 15:4 (ESV)

The whole vine-and-branches passage in John 15:1–11 drives this home. Cut off from the vine, the branch is dead wood. Connected to the vine, it bears fruit. There is no third option.

## Children of God

Now look at 1 John 3:1:

> See what kind of love the Father has given to us, that we should be called children of God; and so we are. The reason why the world does not know us is that it did not know him.
> — 1 John 3:1 (ESV)

A man named after the apostle Mark once said something interesting on his deathbed. Marco Polo was talking to someone a day or so before he passed, and he was asked what the most interesting thing he'd seen while crossing China was. Marco responded that the man should read his book about his travels. But that he had left out the most interesting things — because they were so much more fantastic that no one would have ever allowed them to be printed. They would seem false.

"I have not told half of what I saw," he said.

I think that's a picture of what John is getting at here. We are princes and princesses — sons and daughters of the Most High God. He's the King of kings and Lord of lords. We are his heirs. And we are not known because he is not known.

It's interesting — and slightly amusing — to see the hubbub about the royal family visiting America. Met with celebrities, the president, and other noteworthy people in our country. When they come they don't do so in any sort of official capacity. They don't come to offer us anything. But we throw them dinners and other niceties appropriate to their position as the royal family of England.

Now — compare that with Christ. Born in a manger. In a stall. In what probably amounted to a feed trough. He was greeted by pagans, by paupers, and threatened by a king. Yet he offered us *everything*. In the words of the old hymn — we owed a debt we could not pay; he paid a debt he did not owe.

Yet no paparazzi. No state dinners. No pageantry — just animals. A stable. The smell of feces.

---

## The Practice of Sin

Pay special attention to the phrase "makes a practice of sinning." It comes up a few times in this passage and is helpful for understanding what John is saying. Look at 1 John 3:4–6:

> Everyone who makes a practice of sinning also practices lawlessness; sin is lawlessness. You know that he appeared in order to take away sins, and in him there is no sin. No one who abides in him keeps on sinning; no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him.
> — 1 John 3:4–6 (ESV)

This is not about sinning once and falling. This is about sinning without ceasing. The Greek carries the idea of ongoing, habitual, unrepentant practice. John is not setting an expectation of earthly perfection. Our sins are covered by the blood of the Lamb. Our lives are, as Paul says:

> hidden with Christ in God.
> — Colossians 3:3 (ESV)

But there is a difference between a Christian who stumbles and grieves over their sin, and a person who wallows in it as a way of life.

---

## The Righteous Do What Is Right

Notice with me verse 7:

> Whoever practices righteousness is righteous, as he is righteous.
> — 1 John 3:7 (ESV)

Now hear me — doing right does not *make* us become righteous. Doing what is right is *becoming* to the righteous. Stephen Davey put it well with that distinction. Rephrased, you could read it this way: "Everyone who is righteous does what is right." The doing flows from the being — not the other way around.

Then verse 8:

> Whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil, for the devil has been sinning from the beginning. The reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the works of the devil.
> — 1 John 3:8 (ESV)

The one who practices sin — literally in the Greek, who makes it their ongoing habit — is of the devil. Doctors don't know it all, otherwise they wouldn't call it a "practice." But the devil has been at it a long time. And those apart from Christ have one thing they are exceedingly good at — sin.

The wages of sin is death. And bad motives mar even the most humanitarian people in the world. Seeking celebrity. Applause. Potential financial gain. Trying to buy their way into heaven or atone for past mistakes. All are improper motives for serving God.

---

## The Dog and His Vomit

He is righteous. He takes away our sin. And returning to sin is like a dog returning to its vomit:

> Like a dog that returns to his vomit is a fool who repeats his folly.
> — Proverbs 26:11 (ESV)

It's gross. But I think that's how God sees it. He is infinitely holy and just. Sin is infinitely wicked and wrong. How can the two mix?

So often people take for granted the rules we live by. How many of you have sped this week? Ran a red light? A stop sign? We treat traffic laws — laws that exist to protect us and others — like suggestions. Are you so in love with God that you can't help talking about the Lord with other people? Or have you forgotten the love that should be first in your life?

---

## Known by Their Fruit

You have heard it said — you will know them by their fruit. What kind of fruit do you have in your own life? What kind of fruit do you see in the lives of those you pour your life into?

Some of you know exactly what I'm talking about. You've been pouring into someone and the fruit is growing. Others of you — and you know who you are — you've been coasting. No one is growing because you haven't planted anything. You haven't invested in anyone. You're consuming but not producing.

This is multiplication discipleship. It's not just about what *you* bear — it's about what grows from the people you invest in. So go. Find someone. Pour into them. That is the call.

---

## Closing

J. I. Packer, in *Knowing God*, wrote:

> In the New Testament, grace means God's love in action toward people who merited the opposite of love. Grace means God moving heaven and earth to save sinners who could not lift a finger to save themselves. Grace means God sending his only Son to the cross to descend into hell so that we guilty ones might be reconciled to God and received into heaven.
> — J. I. Packer, *Knowing God*

## Benediction

A Puritan prayer from *The Valley of Vision*:

> May the matter of my prayer be always wise, humble, submissive, obedient, scriptural, and Christ-like. Give me unwavering faith that supplications are never in vain — that if I seem not to obtain my petitions, I shall have larger, richer answers, surpassing all that I ask or think.
> — *The Valley of Vision*, "The Prayer of Love"

We are at our spiritual best when we are shipwrecked on the island of God's sovereignty. Amen.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Warm Pastoral Tone with an Underdeveloped Gospel Core and a Drifting Applicatory Frame**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A tender opening of 1 John 2:28–3:10 that honors the pastoral warmth of the letter and correctly handles the "practice of sinning" exegesis, while failing to press the conscience with specificity, leaving the gospel as atmosphere rather than proclamation, and concluding without a clear exhortation to response.

---

### 3. Berean Gate
**PASS — with a flag.**

*Scopus Test:* The main point of the sermon — abide in Christ, for children of God bear the mark of their Father — is genuinely present in the text. The movement from 2:28 through 3:10 does track the text's flow. Pass.

*Authority Test:* The sermon does not edit the hard parts out; it quotes 3:8 ("of the devil") without flinching. But it moves away from the text's structural logic before it fully lands. The hardest claim in the passage — that the one who habitually sins has "neither seen him nor known him" (3:6) — is noted but never pressed. The preacher stands under the text without running from it, but does not fully submit to its searching force. Qualified pass.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Two flags triggered. Score capped at 69.**

**Flag 3 — Christ is present but not essential.** The Packer quote at the close is the strongest gospel moment in the sermon, but it is a quotation, not proclamation. The sermon could survive largely intact without the finished work of Christ at its center. The cross appears as background scenery (the debt illustration, the manger reference) but is never preached as the ground of the children-of-God identity that is the passage's own claim.

**Flag 4 — Application is substantially generic.** The calls to "read your Bible," "pray," "fellowship," "find someone and pour into them" are the kinds of imperatives that could be transplanted into any sermon on any passage on any Sunday. The congregation's actual condition is not addressed. There is no differentiated conscience-work. The traffic-light illustration and the "coasting" charge gesture toward specificity but do not arrive there.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 16/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (7/10):* The sermon follows the text's sequence reasonably — 2:28, 3:1, 3:4–6, 3:7, 3:8 — and the "practice of sinning" exegesis (ongoing, habitual, unrepentant) is correct and important. But significant portions of the passage receive no treatment: 3:2–3 (the hope of seeing him as he is, the purifying hope), 3:9–10 (born of God, unable to keep sinning as a settled pattern). These are not incidental verses — 3:9 is the passage's most exegetically contested and pastorally vital claim. Skipping it leaves the sermon incomplete.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5):* The Johannine context (beloved elder, end of life, warmth) is set up well. But the epistle's own argument — the tests of genuine faith — and the immediate antecedent (the antichrist warning of 2:18–27) are not connected.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5):* The Greek note on "little children" (term of endearment) is helpful. The ongoing-action note on "practice of sinning" is the best exegetical moment. But the crucial 3:9 ("cannot keep on sinning, for he has been born of God") is entirely absent — the passage's hardest exegetical claim, left unaddressed.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5):* The sermon does not import ideas the text doesn't have, but it does fail to follow the text's own logic to its conclusion. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is vaguely "we sin and drift," but the text's specific concern — the false teacher who claims to know God while practicing lawlessness — is not identified.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7):* Christ appears throughout, but functionally as example (he appeared, he is righteous, he destroys the devil's works) rather than as the ground of the children's status. The incarnation illustration (manger, stable, animals) is evocative but treats the birth rather than the atonement.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* The passage's placement in the arc of redemption — children of God now, but what we shall be not yet revealed (3:2, unaddressed) — is not developed.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4):* The distinction between imputed righteousness and behavioral righteousness (the Davey quote: "doing what is right is becoming to the righteous") is the sermon's best doctrinal moment. Clear and precise.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4):* No confessional errors. The indicative-before-imperative structure is present, if not consistently sustained. The regeneration-holiness link (3:9) is missed.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel (2/5):* The gospel is present in fragments — the hymn reference ("we owed a debt we could not pay"), the Packer quote — but never preached as proclamation. Justification by faith is not articulated. The mechanism of how sinners become children of God is assumed.
- *Necessity of Christ (2/5):* The sermon says Christ appeared to destroy the devil's works and take away sins — both textually present and accurate. But Christ is not shown to be *necessary to the hearer today* — the sermon does not press "you need him" with urgency.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5):* The sermon moves toward the cross in the closing illustration, but this comes after the exhortatory material. The cross should be the gravity well around which the application orbits; here it appears as a landing note.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15**
- *Heart Penetration (3/5):* The "coasting" charge and the "you know who you are" address do land — briefly. The traffic-law illustration makes a fair point about treating God's law as optional. But these are touches, not sustained pressure.
- *Specificity (2/5):* "Find someone and pour into them" is not specific. "Read your Bible daily, pray, fellowship, worship, evangelism" is the standard discipleship checklist. No moment where a particular sinner in a particular condition is cornered by the text.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon addresses "believers who are drifting" and gestures at the unregenerate. Perkins' seven categories are largely unaddressed. The humbled-but-not-yet-believing person receives nothing. The backslider is gestured at with the "coasting" line but not specifically addressed. The self-righteous person who has confused behavioral morality with children-of-God identity — the passage's actual opponent — is invisible.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**
- *Call to Response (2/4):* "Find someone. Pour into them. That is the call." is the sermon's most direct exhortation, but it arrives at the very end, without grounding in the preceding exposition, and addresses only one aspect of the text's concern. What must the listener *believe*, *repent of*, or *trust* specifically from this passage? Not answered.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The discipleship multiplication point is useful. The means-of-grace list is present. But "go find someone" as the sermon's concluding commission does not carry the weight that the passage's own ending does — children of God manifest love; children of the devil do not (3:10, unaddressed).

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**
- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* The section headings are clear and followable. A listener could reconstruct the outline. The progression from abiding to identity to sin-pattern to fruit is a reasonable path through the text.
- *Progression (2/3):* The sermon builds toward the discipleship call, but the Packer quote feels like a new thought after the conclusion rather than part of a unified movement. The benediction prayer, beautiful as it is, does not connect to the passage's argument.
- *Unity (1/2):* The sermon has one main idea loosely — "abide, because you are children of God, and children bear the Father's marks" — but it is not stated as a single governing sentence. The traffic-law illustration and the Marco Polo illustration pull in slightly different directions.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**
- *Relevance (1/2):* Marco Polo is culturally distant. The royal family illustration is topical but generic. The earphones illustration lands — it speaks to the congregation's world.
- *Clarity (1/2):* The stable/manger contrast with the royal family is the strongest illustration — contrast between Christ's welcome and his worth. But it develops the incarnation, not the atonement, and the passage's concern is different.
- *Memorability (1/1):* "We owed a debt we could not pay; he paid a debt he did not owe" will be remembered. The dog-and-vomit is vivid by design (from Proverbs). Credit for using what the text gives.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**
- *Reads as Preaching (2/2):* This reads like oral material — breath-length sentences, direct address, conversational register. The grandfather-by-the-fire framing is sustained.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The sermon does not build to a roar. The most convicting material (3:6 — "no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him") is quoted and explained but not pressed with force.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1):* Sentence variation is present but the sermon does not compress toward a climax. The benediction is the warmest moment, but warmth is not the same as force.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Mostly of myself and my practices. Christ appears but does not dominate.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas, warmly presented. God is not weighty here; he is approachable.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Informed, with touches of gentle challenge. Not convicted.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The "practice of sinning" exegesis** — The distinction between habitual, unrepentant practice and the believer who stumbles and grieves is correct, textually grounded, and the best exegetical work in the sermon. This is what the passage actually requires, and the preacher delivers it without flinching.

2. **The indicative-before-imperative doctrinal structure** — "Doing right does not *make* us become righteous. Doing what is right is *becoming* to the righteous." This is the 1 John 3:7 interpretation done correctly, and it resists the moralistic drift that plagues this passage.

3. **Pastoral register and accessibility** — The sermon sounds like a man who loves the people he is preaching to. The grandfather framing is not sentimental; it is exegetically defensible and sets the tone appropriately for John's letter.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **1 John 3:9–10 is entirely absent.** These are the passage's most demanding and most clarifying verses — "No one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him; and he cannot keep on sinning, because he has been born of God. By this it is evident who are the children of God, and who are the children of the devil." The sermon stops at verse 8. This is not a small omission — it is the passage's own conclusion and the ground of its entire argument. The FCF (why does this text exist?) lives here.

2. **The gospel is never proclaimed — only quoted.** The Packer quote is excellent. But quoting Packer about the gospel is not the same as preaching the gospel from the text. The passage itself supplies the proclamation: "the Son of God appeared to destroy the works of the devil" (3:8), and the children of God identity rests on regeneration (3:9). Neither the mechanism of adoption nor the finished work of Christ as the ground of the hearer's standing before God is preached as good news for sinners in the room today.

3. **No differentiated conscience-work.** The sermon addresses the congregation as a relatively uniform body of believers who need to be reminded to abide and disciple. The text demands that the preacher distinguish: the person who genuinely abides from the person who practices sin and is "of the devil" — a sobering division that must be pressed. Who in the room is actually in the second category and doesn't know it? That question is raised by the passage and ignored by the sermon.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how thoroughly the passage is a *diagnostic* text — John is not writing to help comfortable believers grow; he is writing to help believers test whether their faith is genuine, given the presence of false teachers. The sermon treats it as a growth text (how to abide and bear fruit) rather than an assurance-and-examination text (are you actually born of God?). This shift in genre-identification changes everything: what applications are appropriate, who is the primary addressee, and what the gospel moment must accomplish.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The pastoral warmth and the conversational directness feel like pastoral intimacy. The "you know who you are" lines and the "coasting" charge feel like conscience-work. But they are not. Pastoral warmth is not the same as pastoral searching. Naming a general category ("coasters") is not the same as cornering a conscience. The sermon *feels* pastorally engaged because of its register, not because of its precision. The warmth masks the absence of specificity.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**1 John 3:2–3** — "Beloved, we are God's children now, and what we will be has not yet appeared; but we know that when he appears we shall be like him, because we shall see him as he is. And everyone who thus hopes in him purifies himself as he is pure." This is the passage's most magnificent verse and the key to the entire applicatory movement. The children-of-God identity creates a *purifying hope* — the eschatological vision of Christ transforms the believer's present practice. The sermon touches the identity (3:1) and the practice (3:4–8) but entirely skips the *link* between them (3:2–3). The hope of seeing him as he is is what powers the holiness the sermon calls for. Without 3:2–3, the application has no engine.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach 1 John 3:2–3 as the sermon's hinge.** The movement from identity (3:1) to purifying hope (3:2–3) to examination (3:4–10) is the text's own structure. The sermon currently jumps from identity to sin-examination without the hope that makes holiness possible. Add a section after "Children of God" that expounds 3:2–3: "We shall be like him — and everyone who holds that hope purifies himself." This is the sermon's missing engine.

2. **Preach 3:9–10 as the closing examination.** "By this it is evident who are the children of God, and who are the children of the devil." The text ends with a diagnostic verdict. The sermon should too. Ask the congregation directly: By this standard — the practice of righteousness, the practice of love — what do your patterns reveal?

3. **Proclaim the gospel from within the passage.** 1 John 3:8b — "The reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the works of the devil." This is a gospel sentence. Preach it as one. Who in the room is still bound by the devil's works? Christ came to destroy them. His appearing is good news for the practicing sinner and the struggling saint alike. Do not outsource this to Packer.

4. **Differentiate the congregation at the conclusion.** Address three groups explicitly: (a) the believer who stumbles and grieves — here is your assurance; (b) the person who suspects their faith may be nominal — here is your examination; (c) the person who makes no pretense of faith — here is your gospel call. The text demands all three.

5. **Remove or relocate the benediction prayer.** The Valley of Vision prayer is beautiful but entirely disconnected from 1 John 3. The prayer is about supplication; the passage is about identity, examination, and the destruction of the devil's works. End the sermon on the text's own note — the evidence of the children of God.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The warmth is genuine and the affection for the congregation is evident, but the sermon does not carry eternal gravity — it does not make God weighty, does not press the hearer toward the "what we shall be" wonder of 3:2, and does not leave anyone trembling or astonished.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
The rubric fits this passage well. 1 John 2:28–3:10 is an epistolary text with strong applicatory, doctrinal, and gospel dimensions — exactly the genre this framework is designed for. The conscience-differentiation categories (Perkins) are especially well-suited here, since John himself distinguishes hearers: the genuine child of God, the one who makes a practice of sin, the one who is "of the devil." No rubric strain. The framework bends nowhere; the passage invites all of it.
