# The List I Burned
## A Sermon on 1 Thessalonians 3:12-4:8

**Series**: The Night Is Far Gone

---

## Opening

One thing I've always liked about this church, one thing I've always tried to be a part of, is how quickly people are to demonstrate their love for each other in acts of service. This one isn't feeling well, so that one steps up and is helping out. I got to be part of a chain like that yesterday. Someone was going to help another one — they needed food, but didn't have a ride — so I offered to give a ride to the grocery store, not knowing that I was helping one person help a second person. As a church, people are quick to offer to cook meals, or do what is needed to help each other. To help paint. Or whatever it is. One of you wants to know when we're going to paint, not so that they can help paint, because they can't climb ladders, but because they want to bring food over for us while we paint.

Things like that. Most of you remember Larry — of Larry and Judy. Judy of course recently passed away. But Larry, when he would get back home, would always be bragging on us, telling them: "I was packing up to go, and I needed help, and people from the church came and helped me. They helped me tie down tarps, and get everything prepared as though for a hurricane, because we were going out of town." Or when they were here for the first tropical storm, someone called them and offered to help them prepare for it. Larry said a few times churches up north just aren't like that. When we tell people we love them, we are willing to follow through with acts of service as our resources allow. It's a beautiful thing.

Three men in the church once upon a time had committed to each other to help each other rebuild if anything happened to one of the others' homes. All three were capable builders. All three canceled their insurance. Two have passed on, and the third now lives in Texas. But had anything ever happened they would have been right there for each other, and right there for us too, to the extent that they could be.

We love each other. We do what we can to help each other — it's a beautiful thing.

I think this is part of the picture of the kind of love that is in view in our passage today.

## Love Is Patient

Verse 12 starts with "may the Lord make your love increase and overflow for each other."

What does that kind of love look like in practice? Of course my mind goes straight to the 1 Corinthians 13 passage. The Corinthians had a problem with love. Paul addresses that, and explains to them what love is. He says:

> Love is patient and kind; love does not envy or boast; it is not arrogant or rude. It does not insist on its own way; it is not irritable or resentful; it does not rejoice at wrongdoing, but rejoices with the truth. Love bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things. Love never ends.
> — 1 Corinthians 13:4-8 (ESV)

Which is a high standard to bear, right? Just think about "love is patient." What does patient mean? It's a kind of long-suffering, right? Putting up with something you don't necessarily have to, for an extended period. It's talking about difficult people, in difficult situations.

A pastor named Colin Smith wrote:

> "Love expands your capacity to put up with difficult people and desperate situations. Living in this world, you have to develop the capacity to handle the injuries, disappointments, frustrations, let-downs, conflicts, and offenses that you will experience throughout the course of your life. How do you develop that capacity? How do you live in a world like this, when people will wrong you and disappoint you in so many ways? Answer: Love will give you the capacity to suffer long. Growing in love will give you the resilience you need to live in this fallen world."
> — Colin Smith, "Seven Distinguishing Marks of Genuine Love"

It's about putting other people first. You first, me second.

---

## Love Is Kind and Does Not Envy

Secondly, love is kind and does not envy — I'm going to run these together a little bit. If being patient is about putting other people first, so is being kind, and not envying.

Kindness carries with it an element of generosity. Generosity puts others first. Envy says "I'm discontent because of what you have." What's that start with? "I." Not others. "I."

If you run into someone from school, and it's obvious they are doing better than you, envy allows that to make you upset. Kindness rejoices to God with them for their success.

Love, then, is the antidote for envy.

---

## Love Does Not Boast

Next was boasting, pride, and self-seeking — or putting yourself first.

Jonathan Edwards wrote:

> "A person of selfish spirit is ready to make much of the afflictions that he himself is under, as if his privations or sufferings were greater than those of anybody else."
> — Jonathan Edwards, *Charity and Its Fruits*, "The Spirit of Love the Opposite of a Selfish Spirit"

We all have met people like that, right? Some people aren't happy unless they have something to complain about. Someway they are being wronged. Someway they are suffering. I had a friend whose daughter was dying of cancer. Precious little girl. Maybe three years old. He told me a story about how one of his friends came to the hospital, and spent four and a half hours with my friend. Ostensibly he was there to comfort my friend. But he managed to make it all about himself instead. He talked at length about his own personal recent medical diagnosis. How he had this terrible foot fungus. For four and a half hours. While my friend was sitting there reeling from the news that his daughter likely would never live long enough to graduate kindergarten, much less to homecoming and prom, and he'd never get to walk her down the aisle. His heart is breaking, while his selfish friend's foot was stinking that place up.

Some people boast in their troubles. If you're going to boast, boast in the Lord.

Not in your own self, or pridefully of your own accomplishments, or speak self-centeredly about your own suffering. My friend needed a friend. His friend was just trying to distract him, and didn't know what to say. But at the end of the day, what he said wasn't very loving.

Love — putting others first — in that situation might have meant not talking at all, and giving your friend a chance to talk. Just being there to listen.

One of the most loving things I remember from when my own father was in the hospital after his stroke was someone brought a whole huge bag full of coffee and donuts for us. I'm not a huge donut person — I like them, but I try to avoid that much sugar — but those donuts kept us from having to leave the room and maybe miss whatever the doctor would say if he came out while we were gone. Those were the kindest and best donuts we had ever eaten. We were and are grateful. It freed us to be where we needed to be, without worry. It was wonderful.

Real love has a genuine interest in other people. It puts yourself in their shoes, and doesn't boast in your struggles or victories. It meets them where they are at, with what they need.

Maybe it's donuts. Maybe it's a ride to the store. Maybe it's a bag of groceries. Maybe it means climbing on a roof and pulling down some branches. Maybe it's just sitting there quiet, and listening well.

---

## Love Is Not Rude

Next — love isn't rude. I think that's a hard one. When people let you see them argue, rudeness is a go-to move for most in our culture. Saying things to each other intended to cause pain is a go-to.

I was walking in a parking lot, and a guy wearing a "U.S. ARMY Vietnam VETERAN" hat almost ran me over. He finished loading his groceries in the car, popped it in reverse, and wasn't paying attention to the fact people were walking through the crosswalk behind him. He wasn't paying attention, but I was, and he came closer than I'd like to hitting me. I scrambled out of the way, and he had his window down. So I said "careful," and he hopped out of that car with every bit of spit and vinegar he possessed and started screaming and yelling at me to "go back to my country." I gently pointed out that part of my family came over on the Mayflower. I guess if I have to go back, so does everyone. He insulted my intelligence, called me the N-word, a snowflake, and just kept going like that. Here in Hudson, at the Hudson Winn-Dixie.

I told the story to one of my friends, who was an Army veteran, and he was looking pretty ashamed, so I stopped telling him the story, and asked what was up, and he said: "If that guy's going to be a jerk, he should take off his Army veteran hat first."

Christians, we do that too, don't we? We go around with our Jesus bumper stickers, blasting Christian radio, and then cut someone off in traffic, or speed, or shoot the bird at someone. Is that the kind of thing that God likes to see his children do? Do you think that makes God proud of us? Is that behaving with the kind of others-centric love that Paul is prescribing for the Corinthians, or describing in the Thessalonians? Certainly not.

Another area we do this in is tipping. As I understand it, the young men and women that serve our food are fairly dependent upon tips. That $100 meal you ate — they are taxed based on the receipts — so that hundred dollar meal, the government says "hey, you sold $100, you had to have been paid $15." Of that $15, they could owe — depending on how they do their taxes — as much as 30% of that. So when you sit somewhere and have a $100 meal, and don't leave a tip, in practice they just paid as much as $5 to serve your ungrateful, unloving self. Did you know waitresses — and I have a number of them in my family, including my grandmother. Grandma Baker was a waitress — just one of her three jobs that she worked to raise her kids. Waitresses hate working Sundays the most, because people who go to church will leave them tracts, and not tips. Here they are trying to feed their family, depending on you to pay for their time, and you end up taking $5 out of their food budget for their kids. If you can't afford to leave a good tip, you really can't afford to go out. If you don't leave a tip, don't leave a tract. If you charge them $5 for the privilege of serving you for an hour, what are they going to think about that fake $20 bill tract that you left on the table? What will that make them think of your Jesus?

Grow in love by being the opposite of rude. Be courteous and considerate. Use polite words. Being courteous carries with it the idea of discretion. Just because you know something about someone doesn't mean you should always say it.

Always say it if it will protect a child. Always say it if it will save someone's life, or eternity.

But if it is being said to make you feel relevant to the conversation, or elevate yourself somehow, or for a whole host of other bad reasons, you're better off just keeping your mouth shut. Love is courteous, and courtesy is discreet when it needs to be.

## Love Is Not Easily Angered / Keeps No Record of Wrong

Next — love is not easily angered, and it keeps no record of wrong. One of the commentators said it this way: "love practices restraint."

Colin Smith wrote:

> "Love is not easily provoked, not irritable, not touchy, and not easily upset or offended."
> — Colin Smith, "Seven Distinguishing Marks of Genuine Love"

Ever known someone you had to walk on eggshells around, so that you didn't provoke them, or make them irritable, or upset? I think we all have. People you can't speak freely around for fear of upsetting them.

Jonathan Edwards writes:

> "If a person allows himself long to hold anger towards another, he will quickly come to hate him. And so we find that it actually is among those that retain a grudge in their hearts against others for week after week, and month after month and year after year. They do in the end truly hate the person against whom they thus lay up anger, whether they own it or not."
> — Jonathan Edwards, *Charity and Its Fruits*

He continues to write specifically to those who are easily angered: Ask yourself, "What good has been obtained by your anger, and what have you aimed at in it?" Because maybe all your distempered spirit has done is cause another to stumble in anger. To make them hate. To make them sin. Your sin might be causing your brother or sister to stumble.

Keeping no record of wrong is a hard one, and a form of restraint also. I have neighbors that I've told you about, that are frequently doing things to cause me damage. I don't want to recount the stories of how I've been wronged while saying to keep no record of wrong. If you've been around a while you'll have heard those stories as they happened. One thing Pastor Bob was quick to suggest in a crisis was to keep good records. If you think it could go to court, especially, keep detailed records. Spoke to so-and-so, on this date, at this time. I said XY and Z, and he said ABC. So when things started to happen, I decided based on that advice to make a log of the things that had happened, that I could verify, that they had done.

I ended up with a list of over $2,500 in damages. I kept up with it for a few weeks, but the list just made me angrier and angrier every time I looked at it. Every time I added to the list, my love for my neighbors decreased.

So eventually I burned it. I don't want to be an angry person. Rehearsing the harms that I had experienced only made things worse for me. It wasn't loving, it wasn't fun. It wasn't a joyful experience. But it was a record of wrong. So I got rid of it, locked up the most important things, and decided not to worry about the rest.

## A Call to Purity

Love was the bookends in our chapter today. The middle part, which we will touch on briefly before we close, talked mostly about avoiding sexual immorality of all kinds. It also had a general call to purity. Sexual immorality is sex before or outside of marriage, and because of the world we live in today, sex with someone of the same gender is also, based on the biblical standard, sexual immorality.

In today's world, most people have engaged in sexual immorality in the past. But there is forgiveness in God! And he blesses obedience.

But a fresh start begins with the gospel.

## Theology Mapping

| Doctrine | Expression in This Sermon | Connection |
|---|---|---|
| **Sanctification (Progressive)** | "May the Lord make you increase and abound in love" — love is something that grows; Paul urges them to do "more and more"; each attribute of love is a growth area | Sanctification is not instantaneous perfection but progressive increase — the Thessalonians already love well, and Paul says: do it more |
| **Love as Others-Centrism** | "You first, me second" as the throughline; patience = tolerating what you don't have to; kindness = generosity; envy starts with "I"; boasting elevates self; rudeness disregards others | Biblical love is structurally opposed to self-centeredness — every attribute of 1 Corinthians 13 can be mapped as "others first" |
| **Sin's Compounding Effect** | Edwards: unchecked anger becomes hatred; rehearsing wrongs increases anger; the damage list made love for neighbors decrease | Sin is not static — it metastasizes; the record of wrong didn't document harm, it cultivated hatred |
| **Christian Witness** | Bumper stickers + bad driving; tracts without tips; the Army hat analogy — representing something you're not living up to | The credibility of the gospel is inseparable from the conduct of those who bear its name — wearing the label without the love is worse than wearing nothing at all |
| **Sexual Ethics** | 1 Thess 4:3-8 — God's will is sanctification; abstain from sexual immorality; sex outside marriage and same-sex activity are sin by biblical standard; forgiveness is available | Sexual purity is framed as part of love — "that no one transgress and wrong his brother in this matter" — sexual sin is not private but relational |
| **Forgiveness and Fresh Starts** | "There is forgiveness in God! And he blesses obedience"; burned the damage list to stop the cycle of anger | Forgiveness operates in two directions — receiving it from God for past sexual sin, and extending it to neighbors by destroying the record of wrong |
