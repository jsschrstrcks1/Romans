- **"Prevenient grace" — terminological note**: The preacher uses the phrase "prevenient grace" to describe a work of God that "inevitably leads to salvation." In Arminian/Wesleyan theology, "prevenient grace" refers to a preceding grace that enables a person to respond to the gospel but does not compel them — salvation is not inevitable. The description here ("inevitably leads to salvation") is the Reformed/Calvinist position, more accurately described as "effectual calling" or "irresistible grace." The term and the theology are from different traditions. Given the Reformed framing evident throughout this sermon series, consider replacing "prevenient grace" with "effectual calling" or simply "the work of God in a soul."

In preparing this week for Sunday school we've had a number of things pop up. We had bugs and vehicle troubles. Basically between Ellisha, Mom and Dad, and me, all of our vehicles have had something break or some kind of work done on them, and the red truck broke twice.

The blue truck had a transmission go out. Ellisha's car blew a head gasket. The Hyundai's brakes went out. My truck had the air worked on — it leaks, but we just can't figure out where. The red truck had a fuel pump go bad, and then maybe the battery, and the solenoid came apart in Dad's hand. His shot wore off mid shelf-installation. And they have bugs — this will be the third time they have had to have the exterminator out.

So much drama, and so much noise. Headaches, and work, and stress. Literal headaches too — with the storms and seasons changing the barometric pressure changes, and sometimes that gives me a headache. Had a pretty bad one on Monday, to the point where I was nauseous.

Every time we turned around, every time we went to do something, something had broken.

To top it all off this week I've been moving my library from one end of my house to the other. Ever moved a library? The best part was my house is so small that in order to move things around, most of the other things had to go outside. It's been a physically tiring week as well. Thousands of books.

In the midst of that, it's hard to slow down and pursue Christ, isn't it.

He talks about a shallowness in the church *(Tozer, paraphrase)*, and the need for simplicity. Sometimes I think we become so busy and caught up in the drama of the week and daily struggles that we do our best to passively resist Christ without trying to. We let the car troubles, and bug troubles, and work, and everything else drown out the voice of God in our life.

I think instead of focusing on ourselves — I'm upset because my air doesn't work and it's going to be hot, or I'm upset because I'm down to one car, or my brakes, or that boss of mine, or this or that circumstance — we need to be doing two things.

Firstly, we need to slow down. Let's read:

We serve a God that can make wars cease, that can drop desolations on the earth, that can cast mountains into the sea. Think about what that would be like — a great mountain there one moment, the next being tossed into the sea. The pinnacle of our machines of war can be laid to waste, shattered (v. 9). What do we really have to worry about with such a God watching out for us? He promises to be our refuge and strength.

If you're like me, your brain is already running in the background saying things like, "Refuge and strength… as long as he isn't using these circumstances to grow me. He's my protector, unless I need a lesson taught by being unprotected. He's my strength, unless I need a lesson in humility and weakness."

I think that's exactly the thinking Tozer was talking about when he said the church is all too often wooden *(paraphrase)*.

My favorite verse in there was "Be still, and know that I am God."

Sometimes we have to stop and just take a minute, and re-orient our hearts and minds towards Christ. We need to shift our focus from ourselves and our circumstances onto our Savior who can do all these things and more. We need to be looking for the hand of God at work in the midst of the circumstances.

*[Discussion prompt — invite congregant to share:] Or about the time Shannon spent with her dying grandma?*

God is always at work. We just need to be still, and wait, and watch — keep our eyes off ourselves and on Christ, so that we can join in.

I'm convinced selfishness is the pet sin of our generation. We are so consumed with what we want and how we feel and what seems right to our own twisted hearts that we can't or don't do those things.

So sometimes God has to help us. I talked to Dottie about her knee one time. He brought her low, and stripped everything away. You guys lost your business, your retirement, her health, and it seemed like at one point you might even lose her. But on the other side of that, she's closer to the Lord than ever. She may have her doubts some days. But doubts are proof of the pudding, I think. Because of God's effectual work in a soul — without God having done a work that leads to salvation, you don't have any interest in things of the Lord — a Christian who has spent years in the church and sometimes wonders is evidence of salvation. Someone walks in off the street and wonders — that's a whole different story. Proof that God is at work and that they need to have the gospel shared with them right away.
