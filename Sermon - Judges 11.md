The Book of Judges covers the period from the death of Joshua to the dawn of the monarchy. Political and religious turmoil accompanied Israel's attempts to occupy the land that had been conquered and divided by lot under the leadership of Joshua. Apart from the struggle against the Canaanites at the time of Deborah, Israel's adversaries came from outside the land. Most of these, such as Moab, Midian, and Ammon, periodically plundered the land. The Philistines, however, who at this time entered Palestine in greater numbers, contested with Israel for permanent possession of the land.

Tragically, the Israelites even fought among themselves. Ephraim was ravaged by Manasseh (ch. 12), and Benjamin was almost annihilated by the other tribes (chs. 20–21). Between the days of Joshua and Samuel, Israel plummeted to moral and spiritual disaster. Over and over the pattern of sin followed by oppression was repeated. Occasionally God raised up a Deborah or a Gideon to turn the people back to himself, but the intervals of revival were all too brief.

Judges falls directly within the heroic age, a time when divinely provided leaders were called on to save the day by personal exploits. It was an unstable period characterized by tribal migrations and lack of central authority. People led dangerous yet simple lives in which warlike pursuits alternated with ordinary peacetime activities.

Some of the judgeships most likely overlapped. Samson and Jephthah (about whom we read tonight), for example, may have ruled simultaneously—one in the west, in Canaan, the other in the east.

## 11:1–6

First thing I want to point out, is that the bible in this passage brings up Jephthahs parentage. His father Gilead is only mentioned as having one wife, and yet he has Jep (for short) outside of his marriage vows. Further, the woman Jep's father gilead had the affair with was a prostitute. Scripture condemns prostitution as displeasing to God (Lv 21:9); the law decreed that offspring of an illegitimate relationship were to be barred from entering the assembly of the Lord to the tenth generation (Dt 23:2). This and likely issues related to inheritance lead to Jep being "driven out" some translations say by his brothers. Nevertheless Jephthah, born illegitimately of a prostitute, was asked by the elders – some of whom may have been his brothers - of Gilead to be their leader. Three considerations apply here.

First, the irregular circumstances of one's birth, or having been driven from one's home, did not render a person unable to serve the Lord (Jdg 11:11) or to be guided by God in accomplishing great things (v. 29). The same is true for you and I. It doesn't matter what you're parents have done, or what you had done before you were saved. Praise God He (Eph 3:20) is able to do far more abundantly than all that we ask or think, according to the power at work within us. In otherwords anything good that is accomplished by our hands was not accomplished directly by our hands. Its like when you're little and learning to fish. Your dad buys the Pole, the reel, the hooks and tackle. He buys the boat, the gas, and casts for you. He has his hand on yours while you real, and when you get back to shore, he lets you tell everyone that you cought the fish. In reality, he had done everything, and even helped you real. Had you not been there, that fish would still have been caught, just not by you. However because we are loved, because of Christ's obedience unto death, and because of the Holy Spirits indwelling, we are able to sometimes accomplish God's will. When we fail, its not a reflection of God. I'm reminded of the song Jesus take the Wheel. There have been many parodies of this song – making light of the literal side of that statement. However the main thrust of the song, that Jesus ought to be in control of our lives remains true. I was reflecting one time with a friend while I was in Bible college that it seemed like people (my self-included) were anxious to try to let God take the reins of their life. Until things go differently than we plan, and then we try to jump back in and snatch the controls back. This betrays a lack of patience, but also of faith in God. If you believe God loves you and has a plan for your life, then what are you doing jumping back in and grabbing the reins? Far better to trust and depend on the one who is able to do far more abundantly than all we can ask or imagine. We may not like his timing, or his method, or direction, but he has a higher view. In battle, in the mountains, General Robert E Lee liked to keep the high ground in a fight. You have all sorts of tactical advantages, from being able to pick your targets easier, to being able to hide behind rocks. PLUS they have to try and find the path. You already know the path because you're half way up the mountain to start with. Lee liked the mountains though, because he had a fuller view of the battlefield. His soldiers may not have enjoyed the same view that Lee had, but Lee could see the field, and his soldiers knew that because he had the better vantage point they were better off obeying their leader because he knew things they did not yet know. The same is true for us and Christ. We are in a spiritual war – and only God can see the whole battlefield. We can only see the part that's right in front of us, going on right now. God can see the past, the present, and the future (and all possible variations thereof). He knows what the enemy will do before he has the chance to do it. He knows what impact situations will have on us both for the better and the worse. So obeying his lead – even, or especially when it doesn't make sense is critical. Then when you disobey God, you are in a sense telling God that you don't trust him to do or to know what is best for your life. You are saying "I know better than you God". As we've discussed before the word I is at the heart of every sin. Sin is putting your own, needs, wants, or lusts above the needs of others, or the commands of God. Every sin then has the letter I at its core. Taking the reins of our lives back from God then is not only unwise, but sinful. We are to allow God to direct our paths. My grandmothers favorite verse was Proverbs 3:5-6. Trust in the LORD with all your heart, and do not lean on your own understanding. In all your ways acknowledge him, and he will make straight your paths. Additionally I want you to note that as was the case with Jep, even when you don't understand, acknowledge, and follow Him, that he can and will sometimes choose to use you any way.
