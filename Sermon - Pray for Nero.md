# Pray for Nero
## A Sermon on 1 Timothy 2:1-7

---

## People Group of the Week: The Dhanuk of India

If you've brought your Bibles — and I hope you have — please open to 1 Timothy chapter 2 with me. While you're turning there I want to talk about the Dhanuk people of India.

The Dhanuk build their houses in clusters in the river basins and on the edges of forests. They are occupied with fishing and animal husbandry, and many earn their livelihood by working as agriculture laborers and household servants. Dhanuks believe in magic, witchcraft and ghosts. They are divided into two groups in terms of religion: those who worship Kali are called Kaliyaha and others are called Maharkhiya.

The Dhanuk community speaks nearly 30 languages. Evangelistic and church planting projects need to accommodate these language subdivisions.

Being of a low caste, the standard of living is low, and they are often unable to meet their own basic needs. Pray for their salvation, and that their physical needs would be met.

Who will pray?

## Opening

I imagine by now most of you have read the news about our President having COVID? I was disheartened this week to see people that call themselves Christians cheering because he's sick. These are actual comments that I read on ABC News' Facebook page when it was announced that the leader of the free world, the man who God has chosen to be our president for our blessing or punishment, Joe Biden had COVID.

I'm going to censor the language a little bit. It's both sides that need to be addressed on this topic. However it's mostly the Republicans that have been coming across my feed, so you're going to hear more about them. Also I went back to copy them word for word, and the most offensive ones had already been taken down. However.

I saw someone write "thank goodness, maybe now he will die." Saw so much profanity. People saying the Eff Joe Biden thing, followed by "in Jesus' name." So much hate. Someone wrote "I sure hope COVID succeeds where Jesus failed, I hope Biden dies, and Trump becomes president."

What part of Romans 13 does that fit under?

> Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God. Therefore whoever resists the authorities resists what God has appointed, and those who resist will incur judgment.
> — Romans 13:1-2 (ESV)

There is no authority except from God. You know who Paul was talking about? Nero.

## Pray, Don't Mock

Prayer. We should be in prayer. We shouldn't be mocking. We shouldn't be making fun of. We should be praying for all of those in public office whether we agree with them or not. How do I know this?

Paul says so. Let me read again verses 1-2. You know what leader he's talking about there? Emperor Nero. He was awful, and I don't just mean his singing. He would at the tip of a spear force people to attend his recitals. Women would be trapped and give birth in the auditoriums. Men would fake death just to be carried out on gurneys. He was bad. But that wasn't the worst of it. You see Nero had a problem. His problem wasn't so much with women like he thought it was, his problem was with sin. He loved sin. He hated holiness. So when he was bored of his wife, and wanting to marry the girl he'd been cheating with, his mother objected. This was so reprehensible to Nero that he staged a series of elaborate plots to kill his own mother. He tried to poison her and failed. He tried to stage elaborate accidents around her and failed. He even had a collapsible boat built that would sink beneath the sea while she was traveling, and kill her. But Nero's mother escaped that too. Turns out she could swim. So he ended up taking her out the old fashioned way, and had her clubbed to death. Nero had his own mother clubbed to death because she drew a moral line in the sand. Something she should have done much earlier in her life. To be frank.

Anyway, so she dies, and to celebrate, Nero hosts these elaborate orgies where immorality is glorified. Two years after his mother was killed, he decided the time had come to put aside his faithful wife, and take up this other woman. So rather than just divorcing his wife Octavia, he decides to put her to death, and then when he decides his home is too small, he is believed to have set fire to the heart of the city of Rome, and burned much of it down. Then built a house big enough to have an indoor lake, and a massive statue of himself constructed. Burning down much of the city of Rome seemed to be to most a line crossed that never should have been. So Nero started looking for a scapegoat and found it in the fledgling Christian church. He blamed them, and started having them arrested, and tossed to the wild animals in the Colosseum. One of the more horrific things he did was dip live Christians in oil, impale them on a stake and set them on fire. Nancy Pelosi might be a lot of things, but she's not impaling folks and burning them alive yet, is she?

Yet what does Paul command Timothy's posture to be towards Nero? He demands we offer "supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings … for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions."

When was the last time you prayed in earnest for your enemies in Washington? I don't care if your enemy is Joe Biden, Nancy Pelosi, Mitt Romney or the Donald. What does God want us to do? Supplicate, pray, intercede, and give thanksgiving.

I think that is a benchmark the church at large falls short of. Christians fall short of. It's easier to post nasty memes and make fun, than it is to stop, and spend a few minutes in prayer.

## Rebelling Against God's Authority

Said another way, everyone who mocks, and maligns, and in any way seeks to subvert the authority of ANY of the leaders God has established is guilty of rebelling against God. In mocking God's choice of a leader, you mock God. If the church had mocked Nero, they would be in sin, just as much as we are when we mock the leaders God has put over us.

That doesn't mean we can't disagree. We can have discussion, and debate. But don't descend to the point where we're calling names, and being disrespectful. That dishonors God, and isn't behaving in a manner worthy of the price that was paid for you.

---

## Four Words for Prayer

You'll notice we aren't just commanded to pray the once. Paul adds weight to the command to be in prayer for them by repeating different words for the same task over and over. Four times he gives us a word for pray. He says we are to give our "supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings … for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions."

So the liberal Supreme Court justices — what should we be doing for them? What about the conservative justices? What about the town mayor? What about the state senators? What about foreign leaders? The supreme leader of North Korea? What about the Russians? What about your pastor? Pastors of neighboring churches? Pastors of churches you disagree with? Youth pastors? Televangelists?

Get the idea? Pray for them. Not just simple platitudes — it's so easy to say "and Lord we pray for the president, and his cabinet," and move on to other things. Spend some time actually digging deep. Give supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions. Pray for them.

That means when you have a president you love, you should pray for him. When you have a president you disagree with, you should pray for him. When you have a president you're lukewarm towards, what should we do? Pray.

Quick poll: is there anyone in leadership that we shouldn't be praying for? Certainly not.

## God Desires Their Salvation

Read verses 3-4 for me someone. What's it say? Think about the context there, what did he just say in the context of Nero as emperor. Hey church, I know Nero is persecuting you, he's trying to kill you all because he wanted a bigger house, but pray for Nero because God's desire is for Nero's salvation.

What's that mean for us? God desires Nancy Pelosi to follow him. God desires Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez to come to a knowledge of the truth. God desires Donald Trump, if he doesn't already, to follow Christ. God desires even Joe Biden to come to a saving knowledge of his Son. God desires everyone to follow His Son. To come to a more full and true understanding of Christ, and what Christ did. To know God and who he is! God wants these things for us. In truth these things are out of our reach apart from God's help anyway, so be praying that God gets a hold of the heart of the political leader you dislike.

God wants us to pray towards these ends. We should be praying these things not just for our president, and the House, and the Senate, and the Supreme Court, but for all the military leaders, for all the generals, and captains, and lieutenants, and majors. For police captains, and sergeants, and sheriffs. For doctors — who teach doctors. For professors, and principals. When was the last time you prayed for your kids' or grandkids' principals and teachers? They have your kids and grandkids 8 hours a day every day. They have a huge role in your kids' lives. They should be people we hold up in prayer daily. For their salvation, that God would be making these teachers more like himself. That they would be discipled. You want to see our country change? Start praying hard for the salvation of all of our teachers and leaders.

Not for our comfort, but for God's glory!

## One Mediator

But maybe you're here today, and don't yet know Jesus as your Savior. This next part is for you. Paul wrote in verse 5, "For there is one God, and there is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus, who gave himself as a ransom for all."

Let's start with one mediator. What is a mediator? If there is a dispute, you might call in a mediator to settle it.

If you default on a credit card, you might end up in court, or sometimes mediation. If you go to mediation, one side airs their grievances, and the other side works out what they can reasonably be expected to do to make things right. The mediator hears both and works out a middle ground. Makes a path between the two. With God we owe him a debt we can't ever hope to repay! So Christ came down and paid it all. So he is the only one we have to satisfy and please. He didn't appoint pastors or priests as mediators — he says he is the mediator. I can point you to Christ, and I can point you to Scripture, but I can't absolve you of sin. That's up to Christ to do. You have to talk to Christ about that. That's what the Bible says.

Not only is he the mediator, but he's the sacrifice, and ransom for our sins. We owed a debt because of sin that we can't ever hope to pay, and so Christ paid that with his life.

God is perfect holiness, and perfect justice, and perfect love, and perfect grace and mercy. So in order to make a way for us to be with him in paradise, he had to do something radical. Something that kept him from compromising any of his attributes. So he made a punishment that was worthy of the price of our sin — our death. He then sent his Son to drink the cup of the totality of God's wrath. He who knew no sin, became sin for us. Then he died on the cross, and rose again three days later.

You don't have to go to a priest — Jesus wants you to go to him. It's that easy.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastorally Urgent, Exegetically Thin — A Prophetic Application Riding a Shallow Text**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A timely and courageous application of 1 Timothy 2:1-7's call to pray for rulers that successfully confronts political hatred in the congregation but moves too quickly from the text to application, leaving the passage's gospel center underexplored and its exhortation incomplete.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with concern.**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's main point — "pray for your political enemies because God commands it and desires their salvation" — does arise from the passage. 1 Timothy 2:1-7 explicitly commands prayer for rulers and grounds it in God's universal saving desire and Christ's singular mediation. The connection is legitimate.

*Authority Test*: The preacher submits to the text's commands, including the uncomfortable one (pray for those you despise politically). He does not soften the imperative. The gate is passed.

*Concern*: The sermon uses the text more as a launching platform than a sustained dwelling place. The passage's explicit gospel argument (vv. 5-7 — one God, one mediator, ransom, apostolic testimony) receives only late-sermon attention and is not integrated into the main argument. The sermon is text-referencing more than text-governed.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No fatal flaws triggered. Score cap not applied.**

- Gospel is present in vv. 5-7 section — Christ is named, penal substitution is gestured toward
- Main point aligns with text's imperative
- Application is specific (named political figures, concrete situations)
- Exhortation is present, though weak
- Accessible to ordinary hearers — clear, conversational, approachable
- No inaccessibility flag

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity (6/10)*: The sermon derives its basic application from the text correctly, but does not work through the passage's argument systematically. Verses 3-4 are read aloud but not opened — the congregation is asked to read them ("Read verses 3-4 for me someone") without the preacher teaching through them. Verses 6-7 (Paul's apostolic testimony and its grounding) receive no treatment at all.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5)*: The Nero background is effectively deployed. However, the broader Pastoral Epistle context — Timothy's charge, the household of God ordering function of 1 Timothy, the connection to the "quiet and peaceable life" as a missional strategy — is absent.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5)*: The four Greek-rooted words for prayer (supplications, prayers, intercessions, thanksgivings) are noted but not opened. The preacher flags them and moves on. This is the sermon's best exegetical opportunity and it passes without examination.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5)*: No eisegesis. The sermon stays close to the text's intention but does not mine its depth. The FCF (fallen condition: the human tendency to make political loyalty into moral identity, which corrupts both prayer and witness) is implicit but never named with precision.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7)*: Christ appears in the final section and is presented as the one mediator and ransom. The connection to the sermon's main argument is made ("pray for Nero because God desires his salvation, and Christ is the only path to that salvation"), but it is not organically developed throughout. Christ feels appended rather than woven in.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3)*: The Nero parallel is historically grounded. The placement of Paul's command within the context of a persecuted church adds genuine redemptive-historical texture. Limited but present.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4)*: The atonement language is mostly sound — "he who knew no sin became sin for us," "ransom," "cup of God's wrath" — but the phrase "it's that easy" at the conclusion is imprecise and leans toward decisionism. Justification by faith is not explicitly stated; the mechanism of reception is underdeveloped.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4)*: No contradictions of confessional theology. The "desires all people to be saved" language in v. 4 is not addressed with any precision regarding its scope — the sermon treats it as straightforwardly universal without noting any theological complexity. For a Reformed Baptist context, this is a missed clarifying moment, though not a contradiction.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 8 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (3/5)*: The gospel appears but is compressed into a late-sermon paragraph aimed at unbelievers. Penal substitution is present in embryonic form. Justification by faith is not named. The cross is mentioned; the resurrection is mentioned. The mechanism of how one receives this — faith, repentance — is absent until "Jesus wants you to go to him."
- *Necessity of Christ (3/5)*: Christ is necessary to the argument in the mediator section, but for the first two-thirds of the sermon, the exhortation to pray for leaders could theoretically be preached from a Jewish synagogue — the necessity of Christ to the command is only introduced late.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5)*: The sermon does not move toward the cross throughout — it applies a biblical command, then appends the gospel. The cross is present but not gravitational. The Synagogue Test is partially triggered for most of the sermon's body: a faithful Jew who prays for Nero from obedience to God's command could sit through the first two-thirds without offense.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration (4/5)*: The opening is genuinely searching. The specific, named examples ("I hope COVID succeeds where Jesus failed") are confrontational and concrete. The preacher names a real sin in real congregants and confronts it directly. This is the sermon's strongest section.
- *Specificity (4/5)*: Named political figures, specific platforms (Facebook), specific behaviors (posting memes). The application is grounded in identifiable contemporary life, not abstraction.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5)*: The sermon addresses the politically hateful, but only them. The believer who is already praying for leaders is not addressed. The doubter who struggles to believe God could want Nero's salvation is touched briefly but not developed. The hardened partisan who will leave unchanged is not directly confronted. Perkins' taxonomy: primarily addresses categories 3 (knowledgeable but unhumbled) and partially 5 (believers), but leaves the others largely untouched.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4 / 8**

- *Call to Response (2/4)*: There is a repeated imperative ("pray!"), but the call to response is general rather than structured. The preacher tells the congregation what to do but does not lead them to a specific, summoned moment of doing it. The rhetorical energy is in the diagnosis, not the summons.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4)*: The argument that praying for leaders' salvation serves the mission of God is briefly made ("you want to see our country change? Start praying for the salvation of all of our teachers and leaders"). This is genuinely missional and equipping — but it is underdeveloped and passes quickly.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6 / 9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4)*: The sermon moves in a discernible arc: confrontation of sin → biblical command → historical grounding → scope of prayer → gospel ground → evangelistic close. The sections are somewhat titled, which helps. However, the movement from "pray for leaders" to "one mediator" feels like a gear change rather than a logical progression.
- *Progression (2/3)*: The first half builds well. The second half (the four words for prayer, the scope expansion, the one-mediator section) feels like three separate movements loosely joined.
- *Unity (1/2)*: There are effectively two sermons here — a prophetic confrontation of political hatred, and a gospel presentation for unbelievers. Both are valid; they are not fully unified.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4 / 5**

- *Relevance (2/2)*: The Facebook comments, the named politicians, the COVID news cycle — these are from the congregation's world and land with genuine force.
- *Clarity (1/2)*: The Nero historical narrative is vivid and memorable, but it goes long. The comedic register (his singing, the swimmers, Nancy Pelosi not impaling people yet) works in places but risks undercutting the gravity of the command. The credit card mediation illustration is serviceable but generic.
- *Memorability (1/1)*: The Nero detail will be remembered. The Facebook comments will be remembered.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3 / 5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2)*: This manuscript has clear oral energy. Direct address is present ("When was the last time you prayed in earnest for your enemies in Washington?"). Breath-length paragraphs. Conversational cadence.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2)*: The opening section carries conviction. The middle section loses heat as the scope of prayer expands into lists (Supreme Court justices, military leaders, principals). The force dissipates into catalogue.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1)*: The sermon does not build to a climax. The final gospel paragraph arrives suddenly and quietly rather than as the culmination of accumulated force. The sentence variation is present but the compression and climactic structure are absent.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?*
Mostly of the political situation and my own failure to pray. Christ enters late and briefly. The sermon does not lift the eyes to Christ as its gravitational center. **Partial "yes" — but only in the final section.**

2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?*
The confrontation of political hatred carries genuine prophetic force — there is a moment of encountering God's authority. But the encounter is mediated primarily through contemporary application rather than the text itself. **Partial — the prophetic confrontation is real, but the theocentric encounter is thin.**

3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?*
Convicted — the opening is genuinely convicting. But the conviction is not driven deep to the gospel remedy before the sermon moves on. ### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Prophetic courage in the opening.** The preacher names a specific, contemporary, embarrassing sin in the Christian community and confronts it directly without hedging. The quoted Facebook comments ("I hope COVID succeeds where Jesus failed") are piercing. This is the most authentically pastoral moment in the sermon — the shepherd confronting the flock over a real and named transgression.

2. **The Nero deployment.** Using Paul's actual historical context — a church under active persecution praying for the man who is dipping them in oil and setting them on fire — is exegetically honest and pastorally powerful. The Nero narrative goes long, but the conclusion it grounds ("what Paul commands toward Nero, you must do toward Biden") is genuinely text-governed and rhetorically effective.

3. **Concrete scope expansion.** The movement from "pray for presidents" outward to Supreme Court justices, military leaders, principals, and teachers is pastorally equipping. The application that the congregation's children's teachers deserve sustained prayer because they have them eight hours a day is specific, grounded, and practically useful.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is appended, not integrated.** The sermon's governing argument — pray for your enemies because God desires their salvation and Christ is the one mediator — is present in the text. But the Christ-content of vv. 5-7 does not shape the sermon until the final section, where it functions as an evangelistic addendum rather than the grounding premise. The entire body of the sermon could be preached by someone who did not believe in Christ's unique mediation. The gospel is the text's own argument for why we pray — it should be the sermon's spine, not its postscript.

2. **The four words for prayer are named and abandoned.** The preacher correctly identifies that Paul uses four distinct Greek words (supplications, prayers, intercessions, thanksgivings) and notes this is significant — then does nothing with them. This is the exegetical heart of the passage and the most practically useful section for teaching the congregation how to pray. Simply distinguishing "supplication" (specific asking), "intercession" (pleading on another's behalf), and "thanksgiving" (for leaders, even difficult ones) would transform the application from "pray more" into concrete pastoral instruction.

3. **No summoned response.** The sermon tells the congregation to pray but never actually leads them to do it. A sermon on the command to pray that ends without a model prayer, a directed moment of intercession, or a structured call to commit to specific prayer practices has not completed its own logic. The imperative is repeated but never crystallized into an actual "therefore, today, this week, you will..."

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see the degree to which the sermon's energy is driven by the contemporary political moment rather than by the text's own weight. The opening effectively diagnoses a congregational failure, but the text itself does more than correct misbehavior — it grounds Christian political prayer in eschatology, soteriology, and Christology. The sermon treats 1 Timothy 2:1-7 primarily as a corrective to social media behavior rather than as a theology of intercession rooted in Christ's unique mediatorial office. The passage is richer than the sermon allows it to be.

There is also a mild moralistic drift risk: the dominant message is "stop being politically hateful and start praying" — which is correct — but without the gospel as the enabling power for this obedience, the sermon risks reducing Christian prayer to ethical improvement. The indicative (Christ is the one mediator who ransomed us) must precede and power the imperative (therefore pray for all people), not follow it.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Nero historical narrative feels like strong exposition — it is vivid, detailed, and lands with force. But it functions primarily as rhetorical illustration rather than exegetical opening of the text. The preacher spends considerably more time describing Nero's atrocities than opening Paul's argument. The result is that the congregation knows more about Nero's mother than about why Paul connects prayer for rulers to Christ's mediation for all people. The narrative energy signals exegetical depth that is not actually present.

Similarly, the opening Facebook comments create an impression of prophetic boldness, but the actual exposure of the heart behind political hatred — idolatry of political outcomes, misplaced eschatological hope, love of tribe over love of enemy — is not named. The behavior is confronted without the idol beneath it being exposed.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The phrase "that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way" (v. 2b) receives no treatment. This is a theologically rich phrase — Paul grounds the prayer for rulers not merely in God's saving will but in the church's capacity for ordered, missional life. The "quiet and peaceable life" is not comfort-seeking but a condition for the church's witness and mission. Unpacking this would connect the command to pray directly to the church's calling in the world and would provide the theological bridge between "pray for leaders" and "God's desire for all people to be saved" — which is the argument's backbone.

Additionally, the meditation on "one God, one mediator" in v. 5 is the most anti-pluralist statement in the New Testament's political theology. Paul tells a persecuted minority church that the emperor is not lord, that there is one God and one mediator — not Caesar. This is explosive in its original context and completely ignored in the sermon. The passage is not just commanding prayer; it is grounding that prayer in the exclusive lordship of Christ against the imperial cult. This would have transformed the sermon's force entirely.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3-5)

1. **Rebuild the sermon's spine around vv. 5-7, not v. 1.** The reason Paul commands prayer for all people, including the emperor, is that there is one God who desires all people to be saved, and one mediator — not Caesar — between God and humanity. Make this the governing premise of the sermon, not the conclusion. The imperative ("pray") flows from the indicative ("Christ is the one mediator who ransomed all"). Restructure: ground the command in Christology, then apply it to Nero/Biden.

2. **Open the four words for prayer.** At minimum, distinguish supplication (specific petitions), intercession (standing in the gap), and thanksgiving (gratitude for government's providential function even under bad leaders) with one concrete application each. Transform "pray for them" from a repeated imperative into a pastoral how-to that equips the congregation to actually do it. Ten minutes of instruction here replaces the Nero narrative and serves the text better.

3. **Name the idol beneath the behavior.** Political hatred is a symptom. The root is the misplacement of eschatological hope — the belief that the right political leader will produce the good life, so the wrong one is an existential threat. Naming this idol (misplaced political messialism) and exposing it through the gospel (Christ alone mediates between God and humanity; no president functions as lord) would press the conscience far deeper than confronting social media comments.

4. **Lead the congregation in actual intercession.** End the sermon by modeling the prayer it commanded. A two-minute structured pastoral prayer for named political leaders — supplications for their specific needs, intercessions for their salvation, thanksgiving for the providential function of government — completes the sermon's own logic and sends the congregation out equipped, not just instructed.

5. **Sharpen the evangelistic close.** "It's that easy" is imprecise and leans toward decisionism. Replace with: "There is one mediator. He gave himself as a ransom. The door is open. Come to him in repentance and faith — not because it is easy, but because he is enough." The passage's own language ("ransom for all") is powerful enough to carry the close without softening it.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon has pastoral heat, prophetic edge, and genuine applicatory force — particularly in the opening confrontation. The weakness is not bloodless orthodoxy but rather a thin expositional foundation beneath a warm pastoral application.

The sermon carries genuine prophetic urgency in confronting political idolatry but does not yet reach the weight that comes from standing under the full gravity of Christ's exclusive mediation and the church's eschatological mission — the eternal stakes of prayer are named but not felt. **+2 / 5**
