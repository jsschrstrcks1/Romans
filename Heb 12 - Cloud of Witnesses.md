## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Earnest Pastoral Effort Buried Under Athletic Illustrations and Missing the Text's Gravity**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A sincere but underdeveloped opening of Hebrews 12:1–2 that made energetic use of sports illustrations while failing to exposit the passage's argument, press the conscience from the text itself, or proclaim the gospel with the force the author of Hebrews intended.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**Conditional Pass — with significant drift.**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's thesis — "throw off sin and train spiritually" — loosely corresponds to Hebrews 12:1–2, but the passage's main point is the *sufficiency of Jesus as the pioneer and perfecter of faith, who endured the cross for the joy set before him, so that we do not grow weary and lose heart*. The sermon lands on the training metaphor and never fully arrives at the christological anchor. The sermon passes the gate barely — the text is the occasion — but drifts significantly from the text's own climax.

*Authority Test*: The preacher does not edit out the text's claims, but he does not fully submit to its weight either. Verses 3–11 (the discipline of suffering as sonship) are untouched. The passage's "do not lose heart" urgency (v. 3, v. 5) is absent. The sermon passes, but only on a technicality.

**Result: Pass (narrow) — with noted drift**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score cap triggered at 69.**

- **Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary**: The sermon mentions Christ's death and the cross, but the christological core of 12:1–2 — Jesus as *archegos kai teleiotes*, the originator and perfecter of faith — is never exposited or proclaimed. The mention of Christ's suffering appears as one illustration among many, not as the passage's governing reality. A listener could receive this sermon's main thrust (train spiritually, cut sin, read your Bible, pray) without Christ being essential to the argument.

- **Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: "Read your Bible, pray, share the gospel, worship, fellowship" — these are the standard five spiritual disciplines applied to virtually any text. There is no application specific to what Hebrews 12 says to do, how the cloud of witnesses functions as a motive, or what the author of Hebrews means by *fixing our eyes on Jesus*. The application is disconnected from the exegesis.

- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon ends with "Are you Jane?" and a prayer. There is no clear call: *What must I now believe, repent of, obey, or pursue in light of Hebrews 12:1–2?* The congregation is asked to self-identify with a character type but is never summoned to specific response grounded in the text.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily of myself — the call is to my devotional discipline, my decision to not be Jane, my training regimen. Christ is present but instrumental.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas — and ideas about sports, and about Jane. The sermon's emotional center is a personal anecdote, not the weight of God's Word.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** There is some conviction in the Jane section. But it is guilt-motivation (you should pray more, you should read your Bible), not text-grounded conviction arising from the passage's own argument. Mostly informed and challenged generically.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Genuine pastoral self-implication**: "I know I can be Jane" — the preacher includes himself in the rebuke. This is the Puritan practice of *autopathy* (preaching first to yourself) and it gives the Jane section its only real pastoral warmth and credibility.

2. **Accessible oral register**: The sermon is clearly written to be spoken. The conversational tone, the named congregation member, the local illustrations — this is not an essay. The preacher knows his people.

3. **"Don't be Jane to God"**: This is a genuinely memorable pastoral image. The fair-weather friend who only calls when she needs something is a human universal, and the application — "Don't be Jane to God" — is concrete and sticky. A congregation will carry this image.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text is abandoned almost immediately.** Hebrews 12:1–2 is quoted but never exegeted. The "cloud of witnesses" is not connected to the faith-hall of Hebrews 11. "Pioneer and perfecter of faith" (*archegos kai teleiotes*) — the passage's governing christological title — receives no treatment whatsoever. The sermon floats free of the text after the first paragraph and never fully returns. The longest stretch without engaging the passage runs through the entire Jane section and into the closing.

2. **Christ is a motivational example, not the ground of endurance.** The sermon's implicit argument is: "Christ suffered and endured — so should you." This is moral exemplarism. The passage's actual argument is: "Fix your eyes on Jesus, the one who *authored* and *perfects* your faith, who *for the joy set before him* endured the cross — therefore run." The difference is between Christ as role model and Christ as the source and sustainer of faith. The sermon collapses this into the former.

3. **The application is divorced from the exegesis.** The five spiritual disciplines (Bible, prayer, gospel, worship, fellowship) are not derived from Hebrews 12. They are appended. The passage's own application is specific: *fix your eyes on Jesus* (v.2), *consider him who endured hostility* (v.3), *do not lose heart* (v.3). These applications — especially for a congregation facing their own weariness — go undelivered.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that **Hebrews 12 is a suffering passage, not a discipline passage**. The original audience was not undisciplined — they were weary and on the verge of apostasy under pressure and persecution. The author's call is not primarily "train harder" but "look at Jesus when you want to quit." The sermon entirely misses the pastoral occasion of the text: exhausted, suffering saints on the edge of giving up. Every application in the sermon is directed at comfortable people who need to do more. The passage was written for broken people who need to hold on.

Additionally, the preacher likely does not see how the Jane illustration has displaced the text as the sermon's emotional center. Three paragraphs on Jane versus three sentences on "fix your eyes on Jesus."

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sports illustrations feel like strength — they are vivid, personal, and engaging. But they actually weaken the sermon by functioning as the sermon's primary content rather than as windows into the text. The congregation remembers the Royce Gracie story and the Jane story; they do not remember Hebrews 12:2. The illustrations have swallowed the exposition. What reads as pastoral warmth and accessibility is, in structural terms, an avoidance of the hard work of exegeting and applying the text itself.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 2 is the passage's summit: *"For the joy set before him, he endured the cross, scorning its shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God."* This is one of the most compressed and glorious statements in the New Testament about Christ's motivation and its eschatological resolution. The sermon mentions it briefly ("He was to be reunited with his father") but misses its full weight: the cross was not merely something Christ endured through discipline — it was something He willingly chose because of the joy beyond it. This is both christological bedrock and the template for Christian endurance under suffering. The sermon had the verse, named the joy, and then moved on to James and Paul's trials without stopping to let the congregation behold Christ in it.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the sermon's spine around Hebrews 12:2.** The text's argument is: *because of who Jesus is and what he did, fix your eyes on him and run.* Let that be the controlling idea. Every illustration and application should feed into it, not replace it. The question the sermon must answer is not "Are you training hard enough?" but "Are you looking at Jesus?"

2. **Exegete "pioneer and perfecter of faith."** This is the passage's christological engine. *Archegos* means originator, trailblazer, the one who went first and opened the way. *Teleiotes* means the one who brings to completion, who finishes what he started in us. This is not Christ as example — this is Christ as the author of our faith from first to last. Spend three minutes here and the rest of the sermon has a foundation.

3. **Address the suffering believer explicitly.** The passage was written for people who are weary and tempted to quit. Add a direct address: "Some of you in this room are not undisciplined — you are exhausted. You have been faithful and it has cost you. The author of Hebrews didn't write this passage for people who need to try harder. He wrote it for people who have tried hard and are on the verge of giving up. And his answer is not 'try harder' — it is 'look at Jesus.'"

4. **Relocate the Jane illustration.** Compress it to one paragraph and use it specifically to illustrate the "fix your eyes on Jesus" call — the contrast between using Christ as a vending machine and actually beholding Him as Savior and Lord. Give the text the three paragraphs currently devoted to Jane.

5. **Add a clear exhortation grounded in the text.** Close with: "The author of Hebrews tells you what to do right now. Fix your eyes on Jesus. Not on your performance, not on your sin, not on your progress — on Jesus. Stop looking at yourself long enough to look at Him. He endured the cross for the joy of bringing you to the Father. He is seated at the right hand of God right now, your great High Priest, your pioneer and perfecter. Look at Him. Run toward Him. That is the race."

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — the problem here is not cold orthodoxy but insufficient orthodoxy. The sermon has pastoral warmth and genuine earnestness. The failure is exegetical and christological, not temperamental.

The sermon has a candle of genuine pastoral affection for the congregation — the self-inclusion in "I know I can be Jane" and the naming of real people shows a preacher who loves his people — but the eternal weight of Hebrews 12 (the great cloud of witnesses, the pioneer and perfecter of faith, the cross endured for joy, the throne of God) is largely absent, and the congregation is left with athletic motivation rather than eschatological vision.
