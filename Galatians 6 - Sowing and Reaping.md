# Galatians 6:7–10 — Sowing and Reaping

**Date preached**: August 23, 2020
**Primary Text**: Galatians 6:7–10 (ESV)
**People Group**: Sindhi Samas of Pakistan

---

I read a story this week about a man named Major Arthur Peuchen. He was a first-class passenger on the Titanic. As the ship was sinking and the lifeboats were being launched, Peuchen had a decision to make. Back in his stateroom he had a tin box with three hundred thousand dollars in bonds and stocks. A fortune. But he left it all behind. Instead, he grabbed a good-luck pin and three oranges.

Three oranges.

What had been treasure an hour before was now worthless, and what would have been an afterthought at breakfast was now the most valuable thing he owned. When the ship is going down, your priorities change in a hurry.

It strikes me that this world is a lot like the Titanic. People last year were talking about what a sorry year 2019 was. Hurricanes and protests. Racial tensions. People flying drones over airports. As a church we had a lot happen too — sickness and trauma and death. 2020 has been difficult in other ways. There is always an amount of death and trauma and sickness. But with this COVID-19 thing, there are few people who can still say in August of 2020 that they don't know anyone who has been affected by the virus. I personally know several people who have been sick, and at least two who have lost loved ones. Financially, 2020 has been a train wreck for many. It's a weary time to be alive.

Yet God has put us here for a reason. Even if we can't see it. Even if we don't understand it, God has a plan for us to be here at this point in human history. God wanted you right here, right now, for a reason. What sorts of things will you choose to do with your time? Will you pursue the diamonds of the world? Or will you go after the oranges of heaven?

Our scripture today will be in Galatians chapter 6, and while we're turning there I'd like to talk about our people group of the week.

The Sindhi Samas of Pakistan. They are a people group with a storied past. Their ancestors used to be in charge — they descend from the Samma Dynasty that ruled the Indus Valley from the fourteenth to the early sixteenth century. Their religion is largely centered around veneration of Sufi saints and dead ancestors, fused with Islam. Many rural Sindhi Samas have no clear understanding of their Muslim faith and still believe in folk spirits. They live in a difficult place to reach, making them a difficult people to reach. They have a great need for medical care to help heal their bodies, and the gospel to heal their souls. Pray that God would raise up people capable of meeting both needs.

Who will pray for them?

Let's read our scripture. Galatians 6:7–10:

> Do not be deceived: God is not mocked, for whatever one sows, that will he also reap. For the one who sows to his own flesh will from the flesh reap corruption, but the one who sows to the Spirit will from the Spirit reap eternal life. And let us not grow weary of doing good, for in due season we will reap, if we do not give up. So then, as we have opportunity, let us do good to everyone, and especially to those who are of the household of faith.
> — Galatians 6:7–10 (ESV)

I love this passage, but it's strong medicine sometimes. Who hasn't grown weary once in a while? Weary in dealing with difficult people, weary in dealing with difficult circumstances. We have been dealing with a contractor for months about this shed, and for the last two or three months he hasn't even returned our calls. It's a difficult situation. We have another guy who's never been to our church, who says he's an employee here, and at the same time, I'm told, he posts near-pornographic things on his Facebook page.

Scripture says you reap what you sow. What are you sowing? What are you planting in your life?

"For they sow the wind, and they shall reap the whirlwind," says Hosea 8:7. Is your time being spent on the oranges of God, or the diamonds of the world? What are you sowing in your life?

As I was preparing for the sermon this week, I heard a heartbreaking story about a missionary couple that went to Africa. Their names were David and Svea Flood. They were young and excited. David's focus wasn't as much on the Lord as Svea's, it seems, and so they sowed different things with their lives. They both knew that God had called them to Africa, and felt as though God had called them to give the rest of their lives bringing the gospel to this one little tribe in Africa composed of one village. Maybe 650 or 700 people in the whole tribe, all in one little village.

So they packed up their life and got on a ship and traveled to Africa. They went to meet the tribe, and the tribal chief was instantly suspicious of them, so he didn't allow much contact with the tribe. In fact, he limited it to just one little boy who was allowed to sell them eggs and chicken for food.

Things went on this way for about six months, and then Svea realized she was pregnant. Back in the 1920s it was four months of travel to get back out of Africa and back to Sweden where they were from. So they prayed and asked God to provide regardless of the circumstances. They would have their baby in Africa.

The chief meanwhile was still unmoving. Only that one boy, twice a week, no other contact.

Svea decided that even if they were never able to reach another African, she was going to do her best to reach that little boy. About a month before she was due to give birth, at their weekly meeting time, the little boy — overcome by the grace of God — got down on his knees, prayed, and was made a new creation. The first and only Christian in that small village. The sum total of their work in Africa to this point was one little boy.

As the pregnancy came to its completion, the chief, in a moment of warmth and humanity, allowed the village's midwife to attend to Svea at the child's birth. After almost a week of labor, the baby was born alive, and the mother — incredibly weak — died a few hours later.

David, overcome by grief and struggling with the practicalities of raising a little girl alone in the African bush, went out and dug a crude grave for his twenty-seven-year-old wife. He made a little cross out of wood, painted it white, and wrote on it simply: "Svea Flood."

The next morning he began his hike down the mountain to the mission station. When he got there he loudly declared, "God has ruined my life. I can't raise this baby alone. Take her, if you want." He stomped out of the mission and slammed the door. He headed back to Sweden.

His focus was set on things below. But God provides anyway.

A missionary couple — the Ericksons — were the ones who were given the little baby. They called her Aggie. Within about eight months the Ericksons died, and Aggie ended up with an American couple. They raised her in America and told her all they knew about her parents. She had a happy childhood with Christians that loved her dearly, grew up, married a man named Dewey Hurst, and built a life.

Years later — decades later — a magazine arrived for her in Swedish, a language she couldn't even speak. But she opened it anyway, and in the pictures she saw the little white cross marked "Svea Flood." She knew her mother's name, and so she found someone who could translate it for her.

The article was about her parents.

That little boy that her mother had won to the Lord went back to his village full of excitement and love for the Lord, and he told his parents everything that Svea had told him. Overcome by God's grace, they too became believers in Jesus Christ. Full of excitement and love for the Lord, they began telling their friends and neighbors, and within a year even the chief had become a believer in Jesus Christ. All pointing back to Svea's love for that little boy who sold her the eggs and the chicken twice a week.

This whole revelation started Aggie on a mission to find her dad. Several months later she had tracked down her half-siblings. Her father had remarried. She met with them, and she was pleased to find out they were believers in Jesus Christ, but they warned Aggie — when you meet Dad, never mention God. It's a rule we grew up with. We weren't allowed to mention God because Dad says if God is real, God isn't good.

So she went to see her father the next day. He had remarried, but had wasted much of the rest of his life in grief and sorrow. He was quite ill and weak.

Her father David, right out of the gate, said, "You know I never wanted to give you up. I just didn't know how I could make it all work."

Aggie responded, "Dad, it's ok, because God is so good. He put me in the Erickson family, and later with another family, and I was raised to love the Lord. And Dad — guess what God did with that little boy in Africa."

She told him the whole story. How six hundred people in that village had become believers, all because of the faithfulness of Svea Flood. That God was good, and despite God's plan involving taking her mother home, that the whole village they had committed to giving their lives to reach had been reached.

With tears in his eyes they hugged, and David rededicated his life to the Lord, and died within a few weeks, surrounded by his family.

Aggie went back to the US, and the Bible college she was planning to attend sent her family to Africa to meet the people her mother had impacted so heavily. By this point air travel was fairly common and readily available, so they were able to fly there, and then they spent a couple weeks traveling into the interior, back up the mountain to where her parents' home had been. Still several miles away, she encountered a man who introduced himself and began telling her about Jesus. She smiled and laughed and told the man she was there to see her mother's grave, and to meet the man who as a boy had sold her parents chickens and eggs twice a week.

The man said he was that boy, and he hugged her, and thanked her for her mother's sacrifice. That little boy, now a man, was in charge of the national church — a church of 110,000 people. All tracing back to the sacrifice of a twenty-seven-year-old woman named Svea Flood.

The man took Aggie to where her mother was buried, and when they arrived, Aggie got down on her knees and stuck her fingers in the soil and thanked God for using her mother in such a powerful way.

The way I see it, there are at least two kinds of people when things get hard.

There was David. He focused on the circumstances of life. He focused on the fact that his wife died, and that life was hard. That lack of dependence on God made everything more difficult. Even as a missionary, the focus of his life was not on the Lord, and so when things grew hard, he grew weary of doing good. He was trying to do it all in his own power, and when you do that, you will inevitably burn out. Maybe not today, maybe not tomorrow. But as with David, you will eventually hit a circumstance you can no longer bear up under. At that point, you can get bitter and turn away from God as David did, or you can turn to the Lord and depend on His strength like Aggie and Svea did.

Svea, by contrast, lived a life centered around the Lord. So when months of discouragement came down the pipe, she said, "It's ok, Lord, whatever you want from me. I know you have me here for a reason, and if this little boy is the reason, then praise God." So she shared the gospel with that little boy twice a week for all of the time the Lord gave her.

In the end she died. But so what? For someone whose life is centered around Christ, death is a promotion. When we die, we go on to glory and live with our Lord and Savior, the one our lives have been centered around. "For to me to live is Christ, and to die is gain," Paul says in Philippians 1:21. David forgot that. David allowed the difficult things he was going through to shape his thinking. Lord willing, we won't fail so spectacularly and give our children away. But when our lives aren't built and centered around Christ, we will eventually fail.

How do you center your life around Christ? It starts with God doing a work in your heart. Apart from God doing a work in your heart, you'll have no interest. I came across a whole bunch of great quotes this week about free will.

One good one was from a guy named Toplady — poor kid with that name. Anyway, he wrote: "A man's free will cannot cure him even of the toothache, or a sore finger; and yet he madly thinks it is in its power to cure his soul." For our purposes, it madly thinks it is in its power to persevere alone.

Spurgeon wrote: "Free will carried many a soul to hell, but never a soul to heaven." God has to do a work in our heart, or else we have no interest in the things of God. Our hearts are bent and warped by sin towards sin.

Martin Luther put it this way: "We are compelled to serve in the kingdom of Satan, until we be liberated by a Divine Power." There are only two masters, and unless you've been freed by God, you serve the other one by default. However good you think you are, whatever good you've done has been for the glory of our enemy until God frees you and you have a relationship with Him. You have no choice, no ability, no agency to do anything other than what your master desires. Even the good things we do at that point are done with wrong motives.

But the good news is that God liberates us from that. We don't have to stay chained. God can and will and does liberate us.

Steven Lawson says, "You give up dirt for diamonds." We've taken the things that don't matter in the scope of eternity and traded them in for the things that do matter.

What are you sowing? The diamonds of the world will burn. The oranges of heaven — the fruit of the Spirit, the work of the gospel, the love poured out in the name of Christ — that lasts. Svea Flood sowed faithfulness into one little boy twice a week, and 110,000 people stand in the harvest.

Don't grow weary in doing good. In due season, you will reap, if you do not give up. If you've never come to a point in your life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sin — today is the day. Come talk to me. Come up to the altar and talk to God. And Christian, if you've grown weary, if the world has beaten you down, if you're sowing in tears and can't see the harvest — don't give up. The same God who turned one little boy into a church of 110,000 is the God who sees your faithfulness right now.

Won't you come?
