Thanksgiving is one of the better holidays I think, right up there with Christmas and easter. In listening to people preach this week it struck me that one of the purposes of mankind was to give thanksgiving to God. We ought to be thankful for our very existence. 

There have been a lot of comments on facebook this week talking about the church being thankful on thanksgiving and worshipping the false gospel of consumerism on black Friday. But then black Friday came and went, and the stores were disappointed. Less people turned out for the ”Sales” than normal. While I’m not opposed to a good sale, and have participated in black Friday my self from time to time, I think its vital to remember a few things. 

lets read our passage. Psalms 107:1-3

God didn’t have to create us. Had he not decided to, we would never have had the opportunity to see a pretty sunrise or set. We’d never have the opportunity to worship God, to choose. We never would have even come into being. Further, we should be thankful for his continued patience with us. When I was a kid my mom would on occasion say “I brought you into this world, I can take you out, and make another one that looks just like you”. While that was not in the scope of my mothers power, not really, it was within the scope of God’s power. He created us perfect, in a perfect world, and as soon as he asked us not to do something, we ran right over and messed things up just like any kid does when told not to take a cookie from the jar. At that moment, he could have, and would have been just in doing so, he could have obliterated mankind, and the earth and started fresh. Whats a weeks work to a God of infinite time? He could have made us differently so we didn’t screw up so badly so quickly, but he chose to continue working with us even though we had taken a sledgehammer to his perfect creation shattering his reflection that he had so painstakingly imbued everything with. 

You ever spend a bunch of time working on something? Trying to make something nice and special, only to have a kid come along and trash it before you had a chance to gift it, or put it on display? That’s basically what we did to God. 

He would have been within his rights to destroy us; instead he died for us. For this we should be thankful. 

We should be thankful for every nail hammered into the hands of Christ, thankful for each of the 39 lashes Christ took from the cat of 9 tails, thankful for christis substitutionary atonement for our sins. 

He has done so much for us, we owe him so much gratitude, and thanks. Even then he didn’t stop. 

Did you ever stop to think that earth took 7 days to complete. Earth, our solar system, our universe, our galaxy, and there are millions of them out there. Every scenic vista, every sunset, every mountain, all implicitly or explicitly created that first week. 

God has been working on heaven as far as we know ever since. Earth is pretty amazing. Just imagine what our God could create with a year. Or ten years. Now ten thousand years. What a place heaven will be. What a wonderful God we serve. 

When Paul commands believers to stop sinning, he also commands believers to put thanksgiving in its place. Paul writes: “Let there be no filthiness nor foolish talk nor crude joking, which are out of place, but instead let there be thanksgiving” (Ephesians 5:4).
Paul knows that we are always acting. We either act with sinful, thankless hearts which leads to foolish talk or crude jokes, or we act with thankful hearts and thereby please God with our speech. John Piper explains,
The key to unlocking a heart of gratitude and overcoming bitterness and ugliness and disrespect and violence is a strong belief in God, the Creator and Sustainer and Provider and Hope-giver. If we do not believe we are deeply indebted to God for all we have or hope to have, then the very spring of gratitude has gone dry. (“Violence, Ugliness, and Thanksgiving”)
We have so much to be thankful for. 

If nothing else, we weathered a severe storms this year, and were all still here. The important things remain. 

I want to do two things with the balance of our time. Firstly I want to go around the room and name things we are thankful for. Second Lets spend a few minutes in prayer. We all have had successes and trials this year. Lets spend some time thanking God for these.

### 1. Verdict Title
**Warm Gratitude Without a Text: A Devotional Reflection That Has Not Yet Become a Sermon**

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A well-intentioned Thanksgiving reflection that gestures toward Psalm 107 and the cross but never opens the passage, never develops a text-governed argument, and substitutes warm sentiment for exposition and exhortation.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**FAIL — Scopus Test and Authority Test both fail.**

The sermon announces Psalm 107:1-3 as its passage and then never returns to it. Not a single verse from Psalm 107 is explained, developed, or applied. The preacher stands above the text as curator, selecting a sentiment the text echoes without submitting to what the text actually says.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Substitutionary atonement is named.** The brief sentence — "thankful for Christ's substitutionary atonement for our sins" — is theologically correct and more precise than many evangelical Thanksgiving sermons bother to be. It plants a flag worth expanding.
2. **The illustrations are congregation-accessible.** The mom anecdote and the kid-destroying-the-project image are both drawn from ordinary life, land without confusion, and require no cultural translation. This is a natural instinct for illustration that serves the congregation well.
3. **The oral register is genuine.** This reads as spoken word, not a written paper. The preacher is not performing — he is talking to people he knows. That relational authenticity is a foundation to build on, not a weakness.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **Psalm 107 is never preached.** The passage is read and then completely abandoned. The psalm has an extraordinary structure — four vignettes of people in desperate need (wanderers, prisoners, the sick, sailors) who cry to the Lord and are delivered — a refrain that repeats four times: "Then they cried to the Lord in their trouble, and he delivered them." This is the most thanksgiving-saturated passage in the entire Psalter, and none of it appears in the sermon. The preacher brought his ideas to the text rather than receiving ideas from it.
2. **There is no gospel summons.** The cross is mentioned in a single sentence. No one in the congregation is told: you are a sinner, Christ died in your place, repent and believe. The Synagogue Test is triggered — a devout Jew could sit through this sermon without discomfort. That is not a compliment.
3. **The closing "exhortation" dissolves into an activity.** "Let's go around the room" is not a response to the Word of God; it is a small-group exercise. A proper closing exhortation would name a specific response the text demands — perhaps: "Cry to the Lord in your trouble; He has promised to deliver you. Do that today." Instead, the sermon hands the mic to the congregation before it has finished preaching.

---

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely does not see that the passage he chose — Psalm 107 — is one of the richest Thanksgiving texts in Scripture, with a built-in homiletical structure that the sermon never uses. The psalm's own refrain ("Oh give thanks to the Lord, for he is good, for his steadfast love endures forever") is the answer to every section, and the four vignettes (wanderers, prisoners, the sick, sailors) provide four categories of hearers that map directly onto a Thanksgiving congregation. The preacher has the right text and the wrong sermon.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The mention of "substitutionary atonement" and the phrase "thankful for every nail hammered into the hands of Christ" sounds like strong gospel content, and by the standards of a generic Thanksgiving devotional, it is. But in context, these sentences function as gospel decoration rather than gospel proclamation. They are named without development, connection to the text, or applicatory weight. The sermon feels more gospel-centered than it is because the vocabulary is correct.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Psalm 107's four rescue vignettes are the missed opportunity of this sermon. Each group — the wilderness wanderer, the prisoner in darkness, the sick man near death, the storm-tossed sailor — represents a human condition that is likely present in the Thanksgiving congregation. The sick member. The person who felt lost this year. The one who felt imprisoned by addiction, grief, or despair. The one who felt the storm rage in their marriage or family. The psalm invites the preacher to say: "Which of these are you? All of them cried out. All of them were delivered. That is the ground of your thanksgiving today." This sermon instead gives a generalized "be thankful" that reaches no one specifically.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific, Actionable)
1. **Preach the psalm.** Walk through Psalm 107:1-9 (at minimum) and trace the argument: the Lord's steadfast love (v.1) is demonstrated by His gathering the redeemed (v.2-3) and rescuing them from the wilderness (v.4-9). The sermon's main point should be: "God's steadfast love endures forever — and it has proven itself by gathering and rescuing people just like you." Everything currently in the sermon can remain as illustration if it is anchored to this argument.
2. **Add a gospel presentation built on the psalm's own logic.** Verse 2: "Let the redeemed of the Lord say so — those he has redeemed from trouble." Ask: Are you among the redeemed? Psalm 107 assumes an answer. The New Testament answer is Christ — the one who went into the wilderness for us (Matt. 4), the prisoner who freed us (Isa. 61), the one who bore our sickness (Isa. 53:4), the one who stilled the storm (Mark 4:39). This produces organic Christ-centered preaching from the text's own trajectory.
3. **Replace the "go around the room" close with a direct exhortation.** The participatory close is not wrong for a Thanksgiving gathering — but it should follow a proper exhortation, not replace it. End with one clear call: "Cry to the Lord in your trouble. He has promised to deliver you. He has proven it at the cross. Do not leave this room without telling him your trouble and thanking him for his promise."
4. **Develop the Ephesians 5:4 application.** This is the most specific applicatory moment in the sermon and it is dropped immediately. Press it: What does a thankless heart look like in your specific life? What crude jokes, what cynical speech, what bitterness replaces thanksgiving? Name it. Then show how thanksgiving is not just the absence of that — but what flows from seeing yourself as "redeemed from trouble" by a God whose steadfast love endures forever.
5. **Cut or compress the Black Friday / storms / heaven speculation material.** These are diversions that consume space without advancing the sermon's argument. The heaven speculation ("imagine what God could build in ten thousand years") is imaginative but untethered to the text. Brevity and textual density will serve this sermon far better than expansiveness.
