# The World's Oldest Family Feud
## Genesis 16:1-16

---

### Catching Up: Genesis 14-15

Before we get started on chapter 16, let me catch you up on what's happened since we last left Abram.

In chapter 14, Abram made a sacrifice through Melchizedek — who some scholars believe may have been Shem, one of Noah's sons. God was pleased, and he appeared to Abram once more in chapter 15. The first thing Abram asked about was his kids. "God, what are you going to give me since I obviously haven't had any children."

Think about that for a second. You're having a conversation with God where he is answering you back, and Abram could have said or asked for anything. But the only thing on his mind right then are his children. He's essentially saying — maybe you can't heal Sarah's womb, maybe it's just too hard, but I still believe you can do things. Since you couldn't follow through on your promise, what will you give me?

That whole line of thinking is kind of impudent, if you ask me. God made all these promises — huge promises. But when it doesn't happen in a time, and in a way that made Abram happy, he decides to put the thumb screws to God.

He's like I was a few months back at a restaurant. Three of us went out and ordered three prime ribs and a shrimp appetizer. The hors d'oeuvres came and the inside was still frozen. We sent it back, and they microwaved it, and it came back out — acceptable, but nowhere near the high expectations we had for a relatively nice restaurant. Then they finally brought out our steaks. We'd asked them to sear the outside on the grill. They said fine. They brought us steaks that were cold clear through. I don't think that steak saw the inside of an oven, much less a grill.

So we sent those back. They put some water on the plate and microwaved them. At home, fine, whatever. But when you are taking people out for their anniversary, at a nice restaurant, and you pay $25 for the best steak in their house — and you don't get what you want, you get it slowly, and it's not what you want, how you want it, when you want it — you get a little cantankerous. The waitress, who was fantastic by the way, kept our drinks full and was constantly apologizing for the back of the house. She explained everything to the manager. He came over and offered to take $2 off the bill for the trouble we had. I took it. But I haven't been back, and I haven't recommended Kally K's to anyone since.

That's more or less what Abram is doing here. Look, I came to your restaurant. You promised good food. What I received was less than that. How are we going to make this right?

God told Abram in chapter 15 that he was going to have an offspring of his own blood — that though there would be points of turmoil, and times where things looked bleak for his people, ultimately they would be as numerous as the stars in the sky.

That brings us to chapter 16.

### The Plan

God has just told Abram he's going to have kids of his own. The implication is a child with Sarai. But what ends up happening is that Sarai decides she's going to have to help God along.

Read with me. Genesis 16:1-16.

---

So Sarai hatches this plan. Maybe, like Abram, she thought God was having a hard time fixing her womb — that her damage was so severe he couldn't heal it. But in truth God was just trying to teach them, and us, a lesson in faith. As we've talked about before, God almost never does things in the time we want. He does them in his time, and often for the same reason. I've told you about Haiti before, and how the money and the passport came a lot later than I had wanted — but arrived at the precise time God wanted them to.

Maybe due to impatience, maybe due to lack of faith, Sarai appeals to local custom to try and help God out. While this seems strange to us today, in Abram's day a marriage was often less about companionship and more about producing children. Barrenness would have struck at the very heart of Sarai's identity. Because of the nature of marital arrangements at that point in the Old Testament, it was customary in that part of the ancient world for a wife to offer her slave to her husband if she could not produce children herself. In fact, ancient records suggest there were preferred arrangements for this kind of thing, and it's likely Hagar came from that world — though we know for certain only that she was Egyptian. Because of the nature of the arrangement, and the fact that Hagar was a slave, any child she bore was by rights Sarai's. Normally she would have raised the child as her own.

So while this seems strange to us, back then this was standard operating procedure.

I want you to notice something about Sarai: she didn't pray before she hatched this plan. She didn't pray before she executed it. And Abram didn't pray before he obeyed his wife and went along with it.

Hagar, I think, gets the worst end of this deal. Because Sarai commanded it, she had no real choice. She was probably in her teens — if she'd been older, she likely would have been married off already. What was done to her was, by any fair reading, very close to rape. Not only that, but she would not have been allowed to keep the child once he had finished nursing, at around two years of age.

Then Sarai started mistreating her. And Hagar ran.

### God Saw Her

Next time you wonder if you've done things so bad that you can never be saved, I want you to remember this story. God used this man and this woman to be the father and mother of nations. Not only the Israelites, but most of the countries that are now Muslim will trace their ancestry back to Ishmael — Hagar's son — and thus back to Abraham. All the trouble in the Middle East is the world's oldest family feud, and it all comes down to a little girl who was mistreated.

From Hagar's perspective it was terrible. From Sarai and Abram's perspective, this was something that happened all the time. Some of their friends and acquaintances had done the same. So this horrible thing they did to this poor teenage girl — they were numb to it. Their consciences were unpricked by what they were doing. The same was true of Abram. We don't see him objecting.

But God saw her circumstances. Being moved to compassion, being righteous and just, full of mercy and love — God appeared to her. Look at verses 8-12.

---

One of the things I love about the Bible is that what it said thousands of years ago is still descriptive of the world today. Think about the Muslim community worldwide — many are descended from Hagar. Scripture says Ishmael's hand would be against everyone, and everyone's hand against him. With all the terror attacks, with all the unrest, the riots, and all the tension — scripture said it would happen within a couple of centuries of Noah's flood. Four thousand years ago. God said it would happen, it was recorded in scripture, and it has proved true to this very day.

God is amazing like that. And we shouldn't expect anything less from a God capable of speaking the universe into existence.

I've said it before — there are stars so enormous that our minds can barely fit the scale. Stars that dwarf our sun. Stars larger than earth's entire orbit. The heavens, scripture says, are his footstool. People, we serve a big God. When our big God wants to do something, he can and he will. If he wants to make a woman in her nineties have a baby, he absolutely can. And in Sarai's case, as we'll see in the next chapter, he absolutely did.

---

### Sarai

Let's look at Sarai for a minute, and then a little more at Abram, and then we'll close.

Sarai was a strong woman. I think she had to be. In this passage, Abram dropped the mantle of leadership and Sarai picked it up. We don't know for certain, but I suspect that happened fairly frequently in their marriage. After the situation in Egypt — where Abram told everyone she was his sister and she ended up being given to Pharaoh — I think she probably took a more active role in family decisions from that point on.

I also don't think she had the same level of faith that Abram had. When Abram had doubts in the previous chapter, he asked God directly and received a direct answer. When Sarai had doubts about God's promise, she turned not to God but to the worldly wisdom of the day. And in doing so, she had a little girl put in a position that was, from our perspective, not far from rape.

Because of her exposure to this custom, she was numb to what she was doing. Her conscience wasn't sounding any alarms. The same was true of Abram. Every time we see Abram talking in this chapter, he's essentially saying, "Yes dear." Verse 2 says he listened to the voice of his wife instead of the voice of God. And later in the chapter, when Sarai starts mistreating Hagar, Abram tells her to do whatever she pleases.

In this chapter, Sarai has the backbone. Sarai is leading. And Sarai gets mad at Abram for it.

---

### Sin-Tainted Logic

Consider this: whose plan was it? Abram followed her plan. But who did she blame when she didn't like the outcome of her own plan? Did she point the finger at herself? Certainly not. She blamed Abram.

We're going to call this sin-tainted logic.

How often do we do the exact same thing? We screw up and we blame God. We screw up and we point the finger at our spouse, or at anyone else. I heard someone say it was their drug dealer's fault they were addicted. But unless the dealer held a gun to his head and made him do the drugs, that's not the drug dealer's fault. It's his own fault. It's his sin nature. It's his own innate corruption that causes him to do things God hates.

Everyone knows the old saying — you can't get help until you admit you have a problem. That's absolutely true. When we stop pointing the finger at everyone and everything else, then we can find help. There we can find hope, and forgiveness. Scripture says when we confess our sins, God is faithful and just to forgive our sins and cleanse us from all unrighteousness. Salvation is precipitated by realizing that you and your sinful actions are exactly why you need saving in the first place.

---

### Abram

Fortunately — and I'm saying this again because it bears repeating — there is nothing you can do that is so bad that God can't forgive you. Abram is fortunate in this regard as well. He lets his wife be given to another man. He constantly forgets God's promises. He questions God's ability to fulfill them. When he has a chance to stand for what's right, he doesn't. He bows to the will of his wife and the weight of local culture.

If Abram had stood up in this moment, the world would look different.

This is a heavy mantle we carry, men. Scripture says we are to be the leaders of our households. We need to be leading our families in love, according to scripture. We are held accountable for our own failings, and to a point, for the failings of our families. This shouldn't make us controlling — we are to lead in love.

Scripture says the church is the bride of Christ. How did Christ show us he loved us? Greater love has no man than this — he who lays down his life for a friend. That is exactly what Christ did for us, and more. The method of his death is arguably one of the worst ever conceived by mankind.
