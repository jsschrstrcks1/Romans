# The Party and the Excuses
## Luke 14:15–24 — The Parable of the Great Banquet

---

## The Two Invitations

In the culture of Jesus's day, two invitations were sent for a banquet. The first went out well in advance — a save-the-date, if you will. The second was sent on the day of the event to let guests know that everything was ready. So everyone had already RSVP'd. Everyone had already said yes.

Let's read it. Luke 14:15–24:

> When one of those who reclined at table with him heard these things, he said to him, "Blessed is everyone who will eat bread in the kingdom of God!" But he said to him, "A man once gave a great banquet and invited many. And at the time for the banquet he sent his servant to say to those who had been invited, 'Come, for everything is now ready.' But they all alike began to make excuses. The first said to him, 'I have bought a field, and I must go out and see it. Please have me excused.' And another said, 'I have bought five yoke of oxen, and I go to examine them. Please have me excused.' And another said, 'I have married a wife, and therefore I cannot come.' So the servant came and reported these things to his master. Then the master of the house became angry and said to his servant, 'Go out quickly to the streets and lanes of the city, and bring in the poor and crippled and blind and lame.' And the servant said, 'Sir, what you commanded has been done, and still there is room.' And the master said to the servant, 'Go out to the highways and hedges and compel people to come in, that my house may be filled. For I tell you, none of those men who were invited shall taste my banquet.'"
> — Luke 14:15–24 (ESV)

No one initially rejected the invitation. But all of the original guests reject it on the day of the party. What's worse — they are very transparently liars in their excuses.

---

## Flimsy Excuses

Pretty much everyone in this room has bought something expensive at some point, right? The first excuse is that the man had just bought a field and needed to go check it out. Think about that for a moment. If you just dropped a hundred grand or more on a piece of property — wouldn't you have checked it out in advance? Wouldn't you be familiar with every rock and tree? If you were buying a car, wouldn't you have already taken it for a test drive and had a mechanic look at it first? The car is a much smaller purchase. Yet the fellow who purchased the team of oxen — the financial equivalent of buying a new car — seems to have neglected to do his due diligence.

And the newlyweds. In our culture that might fly as an excuse. But back then it wasn't even close. Newly married couples in that culture spent much of their first year attending exactly these types of social events. Being newly married pretty much obligated your friends and family to have you over all the time. It helped the newlyweds financially. But more than that, it was a learning experience. A friend of mine once said that you don't really know anyone until you've vacationed with them. The more time you spend with people, the more likely you are to see their flaws, and with married people you're more likely to see the flaws in their marriage. I think this would have had a couple of effects on the newlyweds — it would show them that no one's happy marriage is perfect. It would show them how people who've been married a long time deal with disagreements. Lots of wisdom — and other things — for better or worse were passed on this way. So this newly married couple would have been pretty much obligated to come to the banquet.

All three of these excuses are flimsy lies. The host would have been deeply offended.

## The Xbox Illustration

We went over this passage with the kids, and I related it to video games — specifically the Xbox. The first Xbox that came out was simply called Xbox. Then the Xbox 360, then the Xbox One, and then the Xbox One S. The Xbox One S was the latest and greatest — the Xbox that everyone who wanted an Xbox wanted. I told them to imagine they were getting ready for their birthday party and they invite everyone they cared about. All their friends and loved ones. But each one had a different lame excuse.

The first person said, "Hey, I'm sorry I can't come. I just dug out this old Xbox 360, and I'm going to play this one game that I've beaten a hundred times and I'm bored with."

As the host — as the birthday boy or girl — that would make you feel bad, wouldn't it?

## Two Kinds of People

So who are the invited guests in our story tonight? I think we need to know who they are before we can understand the rest of it.

In broad terms, there seem to be two types of guests, right? Those who go, and those who don't. Other Scriptures break it down the same way. Matthew 7:13–14 says:

> "Enter by the narrow gate. For the gate is wide and the way is easy that leads to destruction, and those who enter by it are many. For the gate is narrow and the way is hard that leads to life, and those who find it are few."
> — Matthew 7:13–14 (ESV)

So there are those that find the narrow gate, and those that don't. Those that go to the party, and those that don't. Other parables mention the wheat and the tares. Or sheep and goats. Or wheat and chaff. This duality of two kinds of people in the world is a common theme in Scripture.

I think to better understand the nature of the people who go — and those who don't — we need to look at and understand the party.

---

## The Party

So if God is the host of the party, the party has to be heaven. And heaven is a pretty amazing place. What do we know about it?

Jesus tells us that it has many rooms. John 14:2:

> "In my Father's house are many rooms. If it were not so, would I have told you that I go to prepare a place for you?"
> — John 14:2 (ESV)

The angels can see the face of God from heaven. Matthew 18:10:

> "See that you do not despise one of these little ones. For I tell you that in heaven their angels always see the face of my Father who is in heaven."
> — Matthew 18:10 (ESV)

There is worship in heaven that we can scarcely imagine. Revelation 5:9–10:

> And they sang a new song, saying, "Worthy are you to take the scroll and to open its seals, for you were slain, and by your blood you ransomed people for God from every tribe and language and people and nation, and you have made them a kingdom and priests to our God, and they shall reign on the earth."
> — Revelation 5:9–10 (ESV)

And it is beyond anything we can conceive. 1 Corinthians 2:9:

> "What no eye has seen, nor ear heard, nor the heart of man imagined, what God has prepared for those who love him."
> — 1 Corinthians 2:9 (ESV)

God will wipe away every tear. Revelation 21:4:

> "He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away."
> — Revelation 21:4 (ESV)

You'll notice that there are both no tears and God wiping away tears — which leads me to believe they are tears of happiness. The whole experience of heaven will be so glorious that we shed happy tears. Tears of being reunited with loved ones. Of being free from infirmity. No more wheelchairs, or kidney stones, or cancer, or Alzheimer's. Or shingles.

## Uncle Rich

Speaking of cancer — we got a great report last night at church. Remember we've been praying for my uncle with cancer? Rich? Some of you guys may have met him. He had this really rare, aggressive form of cancer that had metastasized. They gave him a terrible prognosis and said there wasn't really anything they could do. He found a doctor willing to try a type of chemo, and he's had several rounds. They did a PET-CT scan, and when he showed back up to his appointment to have it read, his normal doctor was out — and the replacement doctor couldn't read the scan. So the appointment and all the travel was a waste.

So yesterday his doc was back, and Rich went to see him. Initially they'd found a bunch of tumors, big and small, in his abdomen. There were spots of concern related to his gallbladder, pancreas, kidneys, bladder, and bones. There were two spots of cancer on his liver and four on his lungs.

The results of the most recent scan show all of it gone — except two small spots on his lung. Praise the Lord. Prayer works. One day when we get to heaven there will be no more cancer. Amen?

## Those Who Reject the Invitation

So if there are two groups of people, and the party is heaven — what are those two groups?

Let's start with the first group — those that rejected the invitation. In the other examples they would be the goats, the tares, and the chaff. They would be the ones that found the wide gate that leads to destruction. This first group is any individual that rejects God's Son Jesus Christ and his payment for their sin on the cross.

You see, according to Scripture — and I'm summarizing here — things were originally created to be perfect. Scripture says lions lay down with lambs. There was no sin, no cancer, no sickness. No wheelchairs were necessary. No emphysema or COPD or Alzheimer's. It was created to be perfect.

There was one command we were given, and that was not to eat from the tree at the center of the garden. Like any kids — what did Adam and Eve do? They promptly ate from the prohibited tree.

That act of sin broke the mold, so to speak. The ten-dollar theological phrase is "imputation by corporate headship." But it just means that when Adam and Eve sinned, it shattered the created order in a way that is unrepairable by us. Our best efforts to repair it fall far, far short. Further — as a result of this shattering — we are all born with the innate ability and capacity for sin. You could even go so far as to say a love for sin. We all pursue sin with our lives, trying to find wholeness. Trying to repair this shattering of creation by doing things that temporarily make you feel whole again, but that ultimately leave you feeling emptier than you were to start with.

Scripture talks about this. Romans 3:23:

> for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God,
> — Romans 3:23 (ESV)

And a couple chapters later, Romans 6:23:

> For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.
> — Romans 6:23 (ESV)

The wages — what we deserve for our lives pursuing sin instead of God — is death. Eternal separation from God is all that we deserve.

Some of you have been making excuses. Maybe not about a field or a team of oxen. But you've been putting God off. Something always comes up. Something always takes priority. Hear me — the excuses in this parable aren't just bad. They're insulting. And the host noticed.

## The Cost to the Father

Now — God invited all of us to the party in heaven. A thing that ultimately cost his Son his life. Think about that for a second. If your child gave his or her life for something, and your loved ones completely rejected not only what your son had purchased with his life, but also your son. As a parent — how would that make you feel?

This is just a microcosm of how God feels. He is a perfect and holy and just and infinite God who experienced the death of his Son in deeper ways than we can ever imagine. In a more full and complete way than we can ever imagine. That's not to make light of anyone who's lost a child. It is a devastating thing. But as a human being, we can only experience a small amount of the things God can experience, right? God is infinite. We're not. God knows all and sees all. I grew up thinking my parents were both of those things, but in truth only God knows all, only God sees all.

Our best, most intense love is a flickering candle compared to the blazing inferno of the love of God. And so because we are limited, while it is devastating for us, we can't experience things in the same way God does.

I think the hymn has understated it where it says, "How great the pain of searing loss; the Father turns His face away." God experienced the loss of Jesus in dozens of ways beyond our ability to comprehend. So when we reject his Son and choose the wide gate — when we reject his Son and his Son's invitation to heaven — God is absolutely just to respond with wrath.

## Those Who Accept the Invitation

The second group are those that go to the party. They are the sheep, the wheat, and those that find the narrow gate. These are the group of people that at some point before they die, cease rejecting Jesus Christ and his sacrifice and his invitation to spend eternity in heaven with him.

Scripture describes this second group. Look with me at the second half of verse 21:

> "Go out quickly to the streets and lanes of the city, and bring in the poor and crippled and blind and lame." And the servant said, "Sir, what you commanded has been done, and still there is room." And the master said to the servant, "Go out to the highways and hedges and compel people to come in, that my house may be filled."
> — Luke 14:21–23 (ESV)

It reminds me of a song by a man named Lecrae. He sang:

## The Broken

I don't think it's possible to go through this life without being wounded, damaged, and broken. Everyone carries with them scars and wounds and trauma. Maybe something you did when you were younger. Or maybe something that happened to you.

I know someone that went through something incredibly traumatic. Her alcoholic father assaulted her as she was walking into her home and raped her. Someone that was supposed to love her and protect her. Someone who was supposed to be her hero. Someone who was supposed to love her unconditionally — beat her up and violated her in one of the most evil ways possible. Her father was the opposite of everything a father is supposed to be.

In the aftermath, her mind broke. Back before people knew what schizophrenia was, she developed it. The only thing she could really depend on was God. And she clung to him with everything she had, and ended up — years and years later — being a missionary to the mentally disabled.

I'm sure we all have stories of loss, of pain, of trauma, of abuse. We are all broken in different ways, from different things. We all have baggage from that. Don't we.

God didn't necessarily send for the popular kids or the people who had it all together. God brought in the weary travelers, the broken, the hurting, the damaged, messed-up people. He sent for people like me. Like you.

## The Gospel

So I mentioned before that we have all sinned, all fallen short, and the payment we deserve is to be eternally separated from God. We — and God's image placed upon us at creation — were shattered by Adam's sin. And Ephesians 2:8–9 says:

> For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast.
> — Ephesians 2:8–9 (ESV)

We're broken, and we are incapable of fixing ourselves. It's what we deserve for our pursuit of sin.

But Romans 5:8:

> but God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.
> — Romans 5:8 (ESV)

That is to say — there was nothing special or particularly redeeming about us. We were a messed-up, broken bunch of people.

> "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."
> — John 3:16 (ESV)

God made a cosmic trade. His Son — for the wretched, busted shambles of people. The only requirement was that they have a relationship with his Son Jesus. That relationship is the difference between the two groups. Sheep and goats. Wide gate, narrow gate. Wheat and chaff. Wheat and tares. If you have a relationship with Jesus Christ, you are invited to the party. If you don't have that relationship — it's not too late to change sides. Romans 10:9:

> because, if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved.
> — Romans 10:9 (ESV)

---

## Closing

It's that easy. So as the praise team comes up to sing this final song — take an honest look at yourself and think about which group you are in.

The one that rejects Jesus Christ and ends up encountering the wrath of God? Or the one that accepts the invitation, has a relationship with Jesus Christ, and goes to the party?

Don't make excuses. Not tonight.
