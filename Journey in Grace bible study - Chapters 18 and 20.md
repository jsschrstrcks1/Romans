Chapter 18 – Is It Fair? The Justice of God in Unconditional Election

Summary:

On the following Friday evening, Terry and Ira return to the hardest objection against unconditional election: if God chose the elect on the basis of His sovereign will alone, without any consideration of anything in man, is God unjust?

They reach their answer through two lines of argument.

**First, the order of the decrees.** If God's decree of election followed the decree of the Fall (this view is called infralapsarianism), then at the moment of election all men were already seen as fallen and undeserving. None had any basis to claim God's grace. All were deserving of eternal hell. In that context, how could anyone accuse God of being unjust for choosing to save some from a mass that was already condemned? Grace, by definition, is undeserved. You cannot call it unfair for someone to receive what no one earned.

**Second, the dictionary test.** Even if one placed the decree of election before the decree of the Fall (supralapsarianism), the charge of injustice still fails. Ira and Terry consulted a dictionary and examined the exact words men use to accuse God: *arbitrary*, *despot*, *tyrant*, *dictator*, *autocrat*. Every one of these words describes the relationship between equals — a man usurping power over other men. But God and man are not equals. God is the eternal sovereign; man is the limited creature. To press upon God an equality with man and judge Him by human standards of justice is to commit a fundamental category error. Furthermore, all these words describe one who *usurps* power — which cannot be said of the One who alone possesses His power by right of His own person.

**Their conclusion:** Unconditional election is not only biblical — it is defensible. They ended the evening in awe, not argument. And they reached one more insight: you can ask many questions about grace — what is it? what does it produce? — but *why* grace falls on a particular sinner is not a question grace will answer. The moment you demand a reason for grace, you have made it something earned. And then it is no longer grace.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Paul anticipated this exact objection. Romans 9:14 asks it directly: *"What shall we say then? Is there injustice on God's part?"* His answer is blunt: *"By no means!"* He does not soften it. He does not apologize. He appeals to God's own declaration: *"I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion"* (Romans 9:15, ESV). The potter-and-clay argument in Romans 9:20-21 makes the same move Ira and Terry made with the dictionary — it exposes the category error of judging the Creator by the standards we apply to fellow creatures. And Romans 11:33 is where both Paul and Ira's evening end: in doxology rather than a system.

Key Scriptures:

* Romans 9:14–16 — "What shall we say then? Is there injustice on God's part? By no means! For he says to Moses, 'I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion.' So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy."
* Romans 9:20–21 — "But who are you, O man, to answer back to God? Will what is molded say to its molder, 'Why have you made me like this?' Has the potter no right over the clay, to make out of the same lump one vessel for honorable use and another for dishonorable use?"
* Romans 11:33 — "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways!"
* Deuteronomy 29:29 — "The secret things belong to the LORD our God, but the things that are revealed belong to us and to our children forever, that we may do all the words of this law."

2. Under infralapsarianism (election follows the Fall), why does the "fairness" charge collapse entirely?
3. Ira and Terry concluded that you cannot ask *why* about grace, because the moment you demand a reason, it ceases to be grace. How does that insight change the way you think about your own salvation?

Chapter 20 – For Whom Did Christ Die?

Summary:

After the painful meeting at First Baptist (chapter 19), Ira returns home certain the door is closed. But in the days that follow, his heart grows burdened — not for Lime Creek, but for the fifty people sitting in a thousand-seat sanctuary. It was "the picture of people dying in a desert place." He begins wrestling with how a man knows God's will for his life.

The next Friday evening, Terry and Ira take up the doctrine of limited (or particular) atonement — the hardest of the five points of Calvinism, and the one that generates the most objections. Ira reviews the doctrine in three statements:

1. **Limited atonement states that the death of Christ had a particular design and purpose toward the elect**, rather than just a general design and purpose toward all men.
2. **Because God's particular design is for the elect, the salvation of the elect is guaranteed.** Any other purpose or design would only make salvation *possible* for all men, but would *guarantee* the salvation of no man.
3. **Limited atonement is the logical result of the first two points of Calvinism.** If man is totally depraved and cannot come to God except on the basis of election, then the atonement must be for the elect — or they could not be saved. And if Christ died for all men in the same manner, including the non-elect, then there would be men in hell for whom Christ had died and made atonement.

They then lay two sets of Scripture passages side by side.

**The "particular" passages** — those that speak of Christ dying for *specific* people:

> "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep." — John 10:11 (ESV)

> "…Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her, that he might sanctify her, having cleansed her by the washing of water with the word, so that he might present the church to himself in splendor, without spot or wrinkle or any such thing, that she might be holy and without blemish." — Ephesians 5:25-27 (ESV)

> "Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers, to care for the church of God, which he obtained with his own blood." — Acts 20:28 (ESV)

> "Therefore he had to be made like his brothers in every respect, so that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in the service of God, to make propitiation for the sins of the people." — Hebrews 2:17 (ESV)

> "…even as the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." — Matthew 20:28 (ESV)

**The "universal" passages** — those that appear to speak of Christ dying for the whole world:

> "For to this end we toil and strive, because we have our hope set on the living God, who is the Savior of all people, especially of those who believe." — 1 Timothy 4:10 (ESV)

> "And we have seen and testify that the Father has sent his Son to be the Savior of the world." — 1 John 4:14 (ESV)

> "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life." — John 3:16 (ESV)

> "The next day he saw Jesus coming toward him, and said, 'Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!'" — John 1:29 (ESV)

> "…that is, in Christ God was reconciling the world to himself, not counting their trespasses against them…" — 2 Corinthians 5:19 (ESV)

> "This is good, and it is pleasing in the sight of God our Savior, who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth. For there is one God, and there is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus, who gave himself as a ransom for all…" — 1 Timothy 2:3-6 (ESV)

> "…but we see him who for a little while was made lower than the angels, namely Jesus, crowned with glory and honor because of the suffering of death, so that by the grace of God he might taste death for everyone." — Hebrews 2:9 (ESV)

> "He is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world." — 1 John 2:2 (ESV)

**Three possibilities emerge to resolve the apparent contradiction:**

**Possibility 1: There is a contradiction in the Bible.** Christ died for the elect *and* for every lost person — and these two statements simply contradict each other. *Ruled out.* Scripture does not contradict itself. Both sets of statements are true and must be reconciled.

**Possibility 2: The Bible teaches universalism — all men will be saved.** If Christ is "Savior of the world" and "Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world," then all humanity will be saved. *Ruled out.* The same Scripture that speaks of Christ dying for "the world" also speaks clearly of those who do not believe perishing and remaining under God's wrath (John 3:16b, 18, 36; 5:24; Matthew 10:28). The Bible does not teach universalism.

**Possibility 3: Christ made a general atonement for the sins of all men, which included the sins of the elect.** Thus Christ died for the elect and non-elect in the same manner; the difference is that salvation is applied only to those who accept it, and those who do not remain under God's wrath. *This view looks plausible.* It's well past midnight. They agree to think on it and pick it up next week.

The chapter closes with Ira driving back to school — and coming around a dangerous curve to find flashing emergency lights, a stretcher, and Todd climbing into an ambulance. The girl Todd had been with was killed in the wreck.

THE BIBLICAL CASE FOR PARTICULAR REDEMPTION

What follows is an expanded set of passages and arguments for use in tonight's discussion. Limited atonement is the most debated of the five points — and the most misunderstood. These passages provide the strongest biblical foundation.

**1. Christ defines the scope of His own sacrifice.**

> "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep… I lay down my life for the sheep… My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand." — John 10:11, 15, 27-28 (ESV)

The sheep are a specific group — those given to Him by the Father (v. 29). He does not lay His life down for those who are not His sheep. The safety of the sheep is total and certain precisely because the sacrifice was for *them*.

**2. Christ explicitly excludes the world in His High Priestly prayer.**

> "I am praying for them. I am not praying for the world but for those whom you have given me, for they are yours." — John 17:9 (ESV)

The night before the cross, in His longest recorded prayer, Jesus explicitly says He is *not* praying for the world — but for the ones the Father gave Him. If His intercession is particular, it is reasonable to conclude that His atoning sacrifice — the basis of that intercession — is equally particular.

**3. The ransom is "for many," not "for all."**

> "…even as the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." — Matthew 20:28 (ESV)
>
> "…the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." — Mark 10:45 (ESV)

Jesus chose the word "many," not "all." This echoes Isaiah 53:11-12:

> "Out of the anguish of his soul he shall see and be satisfied; by his knowledge shall the righteous one, my servant, make many to be accounted righteous, and he shall bear their iniquities… yet he bore the sin of many." — Isaiah 53:11-12 (ESV)

**4. The golden chain guarantees every link.**

> "For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son… And those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified." — Romans 8:29-30 (ESV)

No one falls out of the chain. Everyone predestined is called. Everyone called is justified. Everyone justified is glorified. If the atonement were for all men without distinction, then either all men would be justified — which is universalism — or the atonement fails for some, which undermines its power entirely.

**5. The "for us" of Romans 8 is the language of particular redemption.**

> "He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things? Who shall bring any charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is to condemn? Christ Jesus is the one who died — more than that, who was raised — who is at the right hand of God, who indeed is interceding for us." — Romans 8:32-34 (ESV)

The "us" here is defined by context: those who are called according to his purpose (v. 28), those predestined, called, justified, glorified (vv. 29-30), "God's elect" (v. 33). The death of Christ, His resurrection, and His ongoing intercession are all on behalf of this specific people.

**6. The atonement is inseparable from the intercession.**

Christ's priestly work is one: He both dies for His people and intercedes for His people. He is "always living to make intercession for them" (Hebrews 7:25, ESV). Does Christ intercede for every person without exception? No — He said plainly, "I am not praying for the world" (John 17:9). Since His sacrifice and His intercession are one priestly act, the same specificity applies to both.

**7. The cross accomplished what it set out to accomplish.**

> "…just as the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." — Matthew 20:28 (ESV)

Ransom language implies *actual payment for actual captives*. A ransom paid for all people would require all people to be freed. But Scripture is clear that many remain under condemnation. Therefore, either the ransom failed — or it was paid for a particular people, and every one of them *will* be freed.

**8. Revelation pictures the redeemed from every nation.**

> "…and you were slain, and by your blood you ransomed people for God from every tribe and language and people and nation…" — Revelation 5:9 (ESV)

The scope of the atonement is *globally broad* — from every nation, every people. But it is still particular: Christ ransomed *people* from among those nations, not every person within every nation. This guards against the error that particular redemption is a small, parochial salvation. It is gloriously vast — but it is a ransoming of specific people, not a general provision.

HANDLING THE HARD PASSAGES

The "universal" passages need not be avoided. Here is how to handle them carefully and honestly.

**"The world" (κόσμος) in Scripture does not always mean "every individual person."**

In John's writings alone, "world" carries multiple meanings:
* The created physical universe (John 1:10a)
* The system of sin and rebellion opposed to God (1 John 2:15-16: "Do not love the world or the things in the world…")
* Humanity broadly — Gentile nations, not just Israel (John 11:51-52: Christ would "gather into one the children of God who are scattered abroad")
* All kinds of people from all nations

When Jesus is called "Savior of the world" (John 4:42; 1 John 4:14), the first readers — Jewish — would have understood this as a stunning statement: *He is not just the Messiah for Israel.* He is Savior for Gentiles too. The universality is about *scope across nations*, not *guarantee for every individual*.

**1 Timothy 4:10: "Savior of all people, especially of those who believe."**

The word "especially" (ESV) signals two distinct senses. Christ is Savior of all men in a general, providential way — He sustains life, restrains judgment, extends common grace to all. He is Savior of those who believe in a particular, saving way. The passage teaches two different kinds of saving benefit, not universal redemption.

**1 Timothy 2:3-6: "desires all people to be saved" and "a ransom for all."**

Read in context: Paul is calling for prayer "for all people… for kings and all who are in high positions" (vv. 1-2). The concern is that Christians not assume God's saving purpose is limited to one social class or people group. "All people" means *all kinds* of people — including Gentiles, including emperors, including enemies. This passage does not teach that every individual without exception will be saved. It teaches that the gospel is for *all kinds* of people.

**1 John 2:2: "not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world."**

John is writing to Jewish believers. "Not for our sins only" — not just for us Jews — "but also for the whole world" — for Gentiles too. This is the same move Paul makes in Romans 11 when he speaks of God's mercy extending to both Jew and Gentile. The contrast is ethnic and corporate, not a statement that every individual is atoned for.

**The key question every man needs to ask:**

Does the cross of Christ *secure* salvation, or does it merely *make salvation possible*?

If it merely makes salvation possible, then the cross accomplished nothing decisive. Christ bled and died, and it is entirely up to man whether that death accomplishes anything. The outcome of Calvary rests with the sinner.

If the cross *secures* salvation for the elect, then the cross is a triumph — not a possibility. "It is finished" (John 19:30) means what it says. The debt was paid. The ransom was made. The people of God will come.

As John Owen argued: the Father imposed His wrath due upon, and the Son underwent punishment for, either (1) all the sins of all men, (2) all the sins of some men, or (3) some sins of all men. Option 3 saves no one fully. Option 1 requires universal salvation. Option 2 is particular redemption — Christ bore all the sins of His elect people, securing their salvation entirely.

1. Ira summarizes limited atonement in three statements. Which of the three is most clarifying to you — and which is hardest to hold?
2. The logical argument runs like this: if man cannot come to God except by election, and election is particular, then the atonement must be particular or it accomplishes nothing for the elect. Does that logic hold up to you? Where does it feel unstable?
3. Belcher lays out three possibilities to resolve the "particular" and "universal" passages. Why do possibilities 1 (contradiction) and 2 (universalism) fail? What does their failure tell us about how to approach possibility 3?
4. The "universal" passages use words like "world" and "all." Does it change anything to know that "world" in John's writings often means "Gentile nations" rather than "every individual person"? How do you test that interpretation?
5. John 17:9 is striking — Christ says explicitly, "I am not praying for the world but for those whom you have given me." What does His intercession being particular tell us about His atonement being particular?
6. The question at the bottom of this: does the cross *secure* salvation or merely *make it possible*? What hangs on the answer?

Group Discussion – Chapter 20

* Read John 10:11-15 and 27-30 aloud together. Christ says He lays down His life "for the sheep" and that His sheep "will never perish." How do these verses frame the question of the atonement's scope?

* John Owen's trilemma: Christ died for (1) all sins of all men, (2) all sins of some men, or (3) some sins of all men. Walk through each option. Which is the only one that makes sense of both the particularity passages and the universality passages together?
* Limited atonement is the doctrine men most often leave behind when they accept the other four points. Why is it the hardest one? What does it cost a man emotionally to believe it — and what does he gain?
* Does knowing Christ died *specifically* for you — not as one of seven billion lottery tickets, but intentionally, personally, to secure your salvation — change anything in your worship, your assurance, or your boldness?

Closing Reflection — What These Two Chapters Teach Together

Chapter 18 established this: you cannot accuse God of injustice for electing some from a mass of humanity that deserves nothing but wrath. Grace, by definition, cannot be demanded.

Chapter 20 presses the same logic deeper: if election is particular, the atonement must be particular — or it secures nothing. Christ did not die in vague hope that someone might accept it. He died to ransom a people. He laid down His life for the sheep. He prayed for the ones given to Him. He bore the sin of many. He obtained the church with His own blood.

The atonement is not a small thing made available to everyone. It is a great thing, accomplished for a great people, gathered from every nation — purchased fully, not partially, at the cross of Christ.

That is the doctrine. And it should land a man in the same place chapter 18 did: not in smug satisfaction, but in worship.

> "Worthy are you to take the scroll and to open its seals, for you were slain, and by your blood you ransomed people for God from every tribe and language and people and nation." — Revelation 5:9 (ESV)
