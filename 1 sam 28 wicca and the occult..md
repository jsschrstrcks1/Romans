## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Passionate Pastoral Warning With Scattered Aim and an Underdeveloped Gospel Center

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A Spirit-motivated sermon on 1 Samuel 28 that rightly warns against occult involvement and models genuine pastoral concern while suffering from structural wandering, an underpowered gospel articulation, and a primary application (reaching others) that overshadows personal confrontation of the congregation itself.

### 3. Berean Gate
**Result: Conditional Pass — with concern.**

The Scopus Test passes loosely. The sermon's controlling idea — that turning to mediums/occult powers is sinful, spiritually dangerous, and opposed by God — does arise from 1 Samuel 28 and the surrounding Levitical law. The passage is referenced and its narrative logic (Saul's desperation → disobedience → death) is tracked.

The Authority Test passes. The preacher does not soften the text's severity. He states plainly that God's face turns against those who seek mediums (Lev. 20:6) and connects this to eternal consequence.

Concern: The sermon's stated purpose — "reaching those embroiled in Wicca" — is an evangelistic/missiological purpose, not the text's primary purpose. The text is more fundamentally about the consequences of persistent disobedience and the horror of God's silence. The sermon uses the text as a launching pad for outreach instruction rather than deriving a proclamatory thesis from the passage's own argument. It passes the gate but presses against it.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Flag triggered: #4 — Application is largely generic and outward-facing.**
The primary application throughout is directed at reaching *others* in the occult — how to witness, how to pray beforehand, how to use Scripture. The congregation is not pressed on whether *they* have dabbled in these things, trusted in functional substitutes for God's word, or have loved ones being reached right now. The text corners Saul; the sermon largely does not corner the hearer.

**Flag triggered: #5 — No actual exhortation to a clear response.**
The sermon ends on "Creation, The fall, The cross, Redemption" — four words — with no summoning call. The congregation is informed and instructed but never looked in the eye and told, "Now do this. Today. Go."

**Score cap applied: 69 maximum.**

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Partially of Christ, but mostly of a task (outreach to occult practitioners). The sermon's center of gravity is missiological activity, not Christ himself.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas, anecdotes, and instruction. God is present in the illustrations but does not arrive as a weight in the sermon's proclamation.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Informed, and usefully so. But the conscience is not searched, and the heart is not cornered.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Genuine pastoral urgency and local specificity.** The opening — sparked by a real advertisement from a false prophetess — grounds the sermon in genuine righteous anger and local pastoral concern. The Pasco County search data and the reference to Wiccan covens adopting local roads are exactly the kind of specificity that makes a congregation feel their preacher knows their world.
2. **The Denete workplace story** is the strongest moment in the sermon. A practicing witch casting spells on the preacher daily, with no effect, followed by her own question — "Why don't my spells work on you?" — and the preacher's answer pointing to Christ, is a genuine, memorable, text-illustrating account. This is the sermon's most powerful illustration and comes closest to showing Christ as necessary, not merely helpful.
3. **The theology of spiritual warfare is handled with pastoral realism.** The preacher does not sensationalize or minimize demonic activity. The framework — demons are real, God is greater, a child of God is protected — is biblically grounded and practically useful. Ephesians 6:12 is well-deployed.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The text is announced but never opened.** The sermon says "turn with me to 1 sam 28 starting in vs 6-20" but the manuscript gives no indication those verses were read, walked through, or proclaimed. The narrative around the passage is present; the passage itself is absent from the preaching. This is a structural failure — the sermon's primary text functions as a backdrop, not a source.
2. **The Vatican digression is the sermon's most damaging detour.** Nearly 400 words are spent on papal authority, Pope Francis on atheists, and a quote attributed to Pope Boniface about child abuse. Regardless of the theological point's validity, this material (a) cannot be verified in the manuscript for accuracy, (b) shifts the congregation's attention to institutional critique rather than personal confrontation, and (c) occupies real estate that belongs to the text. It feels like a separate sermon fragment inserted here.
3. **There is no moment where the congregation is personally confronted.** Saul is confronted. The woman who advertised false gifts is confronted. People in Wicca are described. But the person sitting in the pew — who may have consulted a psychic, who may have played with a Ouija board as a teenager, whose child may be in a Wiccan coven, who relies on horoscopes or superstition as functional substitutes for God's Word — is never cornered. The sermon preaches around the congregation rather than into it.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's stance is almost entirely outward-facing — "here's how to reach *them*" — rather than inward-pressing — "here's what *you* must reckon with." This is a common trap in topical-evangelistic sermons: the congregation becomes a mission training class rather than hearers under the Word. The preacher's pastoral heart is genuine, but it is directed at the people outside the room more than the people in it.

Additionally, the CARM founder story and the Denete story are both conversion-adjacent testimonies told to validate that the occult is real and God is greater. Both are effective. But the sermon does not ask why someone would turn to these things in the first place — what hunger, what despair, what God-shaped vacuum — and therefore misses the deeper pastoral diagnosis that would make the application land on the unconverted or drifting hearer sitting there.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The sermon *feels* evangelistically grounded because it gives practical witnessing tips and contains real testimony. But the practical witnessing section ("keep taking them back to scripture," "have someone praying during") is instruction about evangelism, not evangelism itself, and not pastoral address to the congregation's own hearts. The preacher may feel the sermon has done its evangelistic work because it discussed how to evangelize — but the congregation was never itself evangelized or pressed toward repentance in this sermon.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
First Samuel 28:6 is the hinge of the entire narrative — and the most pastorally powerful verse in the passage: "And when Saul inquired of the Lord, the Lord did not answer him, neither by dreams, nor by Urim, nor by prophets." The silence of God. This is the moment that drives Saul to the witch. The sermon could have pressed deep here: What does it feel like when God goes silent? What drives a person to seek out counterfeit voices when the true voice goes quiet? Is that not *our* temptation — to fill God's silence with something that answers us? That verse was the doorway into genuine pastoral heart-work and was walked past without comment.

Likewise, Samuel's word in verse 19 — "tomorrow you and your sons will be with me" — is theologically extraordinary. Samuel, who died in covenant faithfulness, is where he is. Saul, who disobeyed persistently, is going there tomorrow. The sermon notes it briefly but does not pause to ask what this means for the congregation: Where will *you* be tomorrow? This is the text's own evangelistic pressure point, and it was passed over.

### 14. Surgical Fixes
1. **Read the passage aloud to the congregation and let it speak.** 1 Samuel 28:6-20 should be read dramatically and with care — especially the silence of God in verse 6 and the terror of Samuel's appearance in verses 12-15. The congregation needs to hear the text, not just hear about it.
2. **Cut the Vatican digression entirely.** It does not arise from the text, introduces an unverifiable quote about child abuse that could cause harm if the attribution is inaccurate, and shifts the sermon's energy toward institutional polemic. Redeploy those words toward pressing the congregation's own hearts.
3. **Add a direct address section targeting those who have personally dabbled.** After the Leviticus quotation, address the room: "Some of you have been there. Maybe it was a Ouija board at a slumber party when you were fourteen. Maybe it was a psychic reading when your marriage was falling apart. Maybe it's horoscopes every morning before you open your Bible — if you open your Bible at all. The Word of God today is the same Word it has always been: God's face is against this. Not because He's cruel — but because He is jealous for you."
4. **Build toward an actual exhortation at the close.** The ending ("Creation, The fall, The cross, Redemption") is a skeleton without flesh. Preach those four words. Tell the congregation what the cross means. Tell those who don't know Christ what they must do. Tell those who do know Christ what they must do with that knowledge — specifically, today.
5. **Mine 1 Samuel 28:6 — the silence of God.** This verse is the pastoral and theological core of the entire passage. Make it the hinge of the sermon. The occult is attractive because it *promises to speak when God seems silent.* Name that temptation, diagnose its root (unbelief, despair, the hunger for a word), and then preach Christ as the Word of God who has spoken definitively and does not go silent (Heb. 1:1-2).
6. **Sharpen the gospel articulation.** "Salvation in Jesus Christ" must be unpacked: What did Christ do? Why was it necessary? How does one receive it? The Ephesians 2:8-9 quote is already in the manuscript — use it not as anti-papal ammunition but as the gospel proclamation it is.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** This sermon does not suffer from cold correctness. It suffers from the opposite problem: pastoral warmth and personal testimony that have not yet been organized under the discipline of the text's own argument. The fire is present; the structure needs rebuilding around it.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
There are genuine sparks of eternal weight here — particularly in the Denete story and the CARM testimony — but the sermon does not sustain a sense of divine gravity across its full length; the Vatican digression and anecdotal wandering dissipate what the better moments build.

No significant genre strain. The rubric fits this text and sermon type without distortion.
