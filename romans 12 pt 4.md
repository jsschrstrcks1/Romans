**FULL SERMON MANUSCRIPT — WEEK 4**
**Romans 12:14–21 — Overcome Evil with Good**
*(45–55 minutes, single-spaced)*

---

**INTRODUCTION — THE CAMERA TURNS OUTWARD**

Open your Bible with me to Romans 12.

We have spent four weeks in this chapter. And if you have been with us, you know where we have been.

Week 1, Paul called us to the altar — present your bodies as living sacrifices. Everything downstream from Romans 1–11 starts there. We don't obey to earn grace. We obey *because of* grace. The mercies of God are the only thing strong enough to produce that kind of surrender.

Week 2, he showed us what those surrendered lives look like inside the body — many members, one body, gifts given by grace for the good of the whole.

Last week we stayed in verses 9–13, and Paul showed us the internal culture of that body. What does it actually look like when genuine love is at work inside a church? Not polite distance. Not managed coexistence. Not religious performance. Real love — with a moral backbone, with family affection, with hands that open and homes that welcome.

This week, Paul turns the camera outward.

He is no longer describing how we love the person in the seat next to us. He is describing how we love the person who wants us gone. How we love the one who slanders us, the one who persecutes us, the one who has done us genuine, deep, personal harm.

This is harder. This is where the gospel either proves itself or gets exposed as decoration.

Let's read the text. Romans 12, beginning at verse 14:

*"Bless those who persecute you; bless and do not curse them. Rejoice with those who rejoice, weep with those who weep. Live in harmony with one another. Do not be haughty, but associate with the lowly. Never be wise in your own sight. Repay no one evil for evil, but give thought to do what is honorable in the sight of all. If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all. Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God, for it is written, 'Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord.' To the contrary, 'if your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him something to drink; for by so doing you will heap burning coals on his head.' Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good."*

Let's pray.

Father, we have read Your Word. Now help us feel the weight of it, and by Your Spirit, help us to want what it asks. We can hear this passage and nod. That is not enough. We need You to do something in us that we cannot do ourselves. So do it — for Your glory, and for our good.
In Christ's name, amen.

**POINT 1 — BLESS, DO NOT CURSE (v. 14)**

*"Bless those who persecute you; bless and do not curse them."*

When Paul wrote this, he was not writing theory.

The church in Rome knew what persecution looked like. Not the inconvenience of a changed cultural atmosphere. Not the frustration of being mocked online. Paul was writing — probably in the mid-50s AD, just a few years before Nero's reign turned dark — to people who had already watched brothers and sisters suffer for the name of Christ. In a few short years, Christians in Rome would be dipped in oil and lit as human torches to line the streets of the city. Fed to lions in the arena. Crucified. The families of those men and women were in the pews when this letter was read aloud.

Paul says: bless the people doing this to you. Bless them. Do not curse them.

That is not a reasonable command. That is a supernatural one.

Notice what he does not say. He doesn't say *tolerate* those who persecute you. He doesn't say *endure* them. He says *bless* them. That is an active word. It means to speak well of them, to pray for them, to desire their good. The opposite of cursing is not silence. It is blessing.

Some of you are thinking of someone right now. Someone who did something you didn't deserve. Someone who took something from you — your reputation, your marriage, a relationship, trust you extended that got thrown back in your face. We will get there. Hold that person in mind as we go.

Now — before I go further I want to say something about where much of the pressure on the western church actually lives these days.

It does not live in the arena. Praise God, it does not. There is real, blood-red persecution happening in other parts of the world — Nigeria, North Korea, parts of the Middle East — where Christians are being killed for their faith, churches burned, pastors executed. That is happening right now, today. The church in those places knows what Romans 12:14 costs in ways most of us, frankly, do not. We should pray for them. We should give. We should not pretend our experience is equal to theirs.

But here in the West, much of what we call persecution lives somewhere else entirely. It lives in the comment section. In the break room. In the family that rolls their eyes at your faith. And — I want to say this carefully, because I think the church needs to hear it — it lives in our politics.

Politics has become the functional religion of our age. People find their identity in it, their enemies through it, their hope in whoever wins the next election. The church has not been immune — and I will include myself in that. When we curse the politician the other half of the country voted for, we are not just being rude. We are revealing what we actually worship.

The test is simple: can you genuinely pray for the leader you despise most? Not mock him. Not curse her. Genuinely pray — that God would lead them, that the Lord would draw them close, that their decisions would reflect His will. Romans 12:14 says bless those who persecute you. First Timothy 2:1–2 says offer prayers and intercessions for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions. Not the ones you voted for. All of them. The ones you find it hardest to pray for are, in most cases, the ones who need it most.

I'll say this too. I believe that the longer someone is truly discipled into the Word of God, the looser their grip gets on any political identity. Not because politics is unimportant — it isn't. But scripture has a way of putting things in their proper place. The gospel is not a platform. Christ is not a candidate. And when we spend more energy cursing whoever sits in the White House than we do sharing the gospel with our neighbors, we have made an idol out of something that will not survive the age to come.

So let me ask you plainly — when is the last time you genuinely prayed for the leader you can't stand? If you can't bring yourself to bless the politician you disagree with most, it is worth asking honestly before God whether politics has become more of a savior to you than Jesus has.

**POINT 2 — ENTER THEIR WORLD (v. 15)**

*"Rejoice with those who rejoice, weep with those who weep."*

This verse often gets passed over on the way to the weightier commands. Don't pass over it.

This is harder than it looks.

Rejoicing with those who rejoice is not difficult when you love the person. When your best friend gets the promotion, when the diagnosis comes back clean, when the baby is born healthy — of course you rejoice. That is easy. But what about when the person who is rejoicing is someone who has hurt you? What about when the person weeping is your enemy? Can you enter their grief honestly? Can you sit with them without a hidden part of you feeling like they earned it?

Human nature wants to reverse this. We rejoice when rivals fail. We feel something almost like grief when enemies succeed. We dress it up — we call it justice, we call it what they deserve — but underneath it is something much older and much uglier.

Paul's command here is not to manage these feelings from the outside. It is to have them transformed from the inside. The man who is genuinely weeping with someone who wronged him has been changed. Not coached. Changed. That is the work of the Spirit, not the work of effort.

So what do you do when you cannot feel it? When you are sitting across from someone in their grief and you feel nothing — or worse, when some part of you is glad? You bring it to God. You confess it for what it is. You ask him to give you what you do not have. And then you show up anyway. Because love, Paul told us last week, is genuine. And genuine love does not wait until it feels right. It does the loving thing first, and trusts God to meet it there.

---

**POINT 3 — KILL YOUR PRIDE (v. 16)**

*"Live in harmony with one another. Do not be haughty, but associate with the lowly. Never be wise in your own sight."*

If you want to know the engine underneath most of what breaks down in human relationships — here it is. Right here. Pride.

The refusal to live in harmony almost always comes from the conviction that my way is right and your way is wrong. The refusal to associate with the lowly almost always comes from fear that being seen with them will cost me something. Being wise in your own sight — that is the root of all of it. I know better. I see clearly. The problem is always out there, never in here.

Paul cuts it off. Do not be haughty. That word in the Greek — *ta hypsēla phronountes* — means thinking high thoughts about yourself. Don't do it. And notice the antidote is not "think lowly of yourself." It is *associate with the lowly*. Go where the humble people are. Go where the overlooked people are. Let them have access to you. Let their lives push back on your comfortable assumptions.

Jesus did this consistently. He ate with tax collectors. He talked to Samaritans. He touched lepers. He did not manage his associations for the sake of his reputation. He moved toward the margins — not as a program, not as an outreach strategy — because the margins were full of people who knew they needed help.

That kind of posture is not possible if you are invested in being wise in your own sight.

*Never be wise in your own sight.* That is a hard command to sustain, because the self is very good at convincing us that this time, right now, we are the reasonable one. We come by it honestly. But it is still pride. And pride is still the thing underneath most broken relationships.

Kill it. That is what Paul is saying. Kill it before it kills something else.

---

**POINT 4 — LEAVE IT TO GOD (vv. 17–19)**

*"Repay no one evil for evil, but give thought to do what is honorable in the sight of all. If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all. Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God, for it is written, 'Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord.'"*

I want to spend some time here, because this is where the passage gets most personal and most costly.

*Repay no one evil for evil.* Not even a little. Not even proportionally. Not even when they started it.

*If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all.* Notice the honest limits Paul builds in. He does not say peace will always be achieved. He says: go as far as you possibly can. Make sure, so far as it is within your power, that the lack of peace is not coming from your side. We said this last week as well — genuine love does not mean passively enabling evil. There are situations where peace does not fully depend on you, and Paul knows that. But he demands we exhaust our options first. He demands we look in the mirror before we look at them.

And then — *never avenge yourselves.*

Never.

That word in the Greek — *mē heautous ekdikountes* — literally means: do not do the justice yourself. Don't take it into your own hands.

I want to tell you a story.

I know a man — someone I went to school with, who went on to become someone of note in the world. I am going to leave his name out for now, for reasons that will make sense one day. He had a daughter. She was kidnapped. The men who took her were brutal beyond what I will describe from this pulpit, and when they were done, they killed her and dumped her body. When the police called him in to identify her, she was barely recognizable. He identified her by a piece of jewelry in her pocket with her name engraved on it.

He was furious. I mean that word. Homicidal. He said himself he wanted those men dead. I cannot sit here and tell you I would have felt differently. Every father in this room understands exactly what he felt.

But then something happened. The Lord got ahold of him. He became a Christian. And a few years later, he found himself involved in prison ministry — and without knowing it, he ended up in the same facility holding the man who had confessed to killing his daughter.

He wrestled with God the entire service. The whole sermon. But when, at the end of the message, his daughter's killer raised his hand saying he had come to faith in Christ — my friend ran across the room to him. He spent the next two hours talking and praying with him. He has gone back to that prison, week after week, for fifteen years, discipling the man who murdered his child. And that man is now sharing the gospel and making disciples inside those walls.

That is Romans 12:19. That is what it looks like when someone actually believes God when He says, *"Vengeance is mine. I will repay."*

Now — here is the theological weight underneath that command. Paul is not asking us to let evil go unpunished. He is asking us to trust God to punish it better than we can.

Think about what God's wrath actually means. The hundred years of a human life is a drop against eternity. If you truly believe in the God of scripture — whose wrath is described in Romans 1, whose justice is not diminished but fully satisfied in the cross of Christ — then God's wrath is not a comforting metaphor. It is a terrifying, permanent, complete reality for everyone who dies outside of Christ.

So when we take vengeance into our own hands, we are not just disobeying a command. We are, in a real sense, saying to God: *I don't trust you to deal with this fairly. I need to handle it myself.* And that is a revealing thing to admit. Because if we cannot trust him with justice for someone who has wronged us, we have to ask — are we genuinely trusting him with anything? Are we trusting him with our salvation? You cannot fully compartmentalize faith. Either the God who saved you is also the God who is just — or none of it holds together.

Vengeance is his. He will repay.

Leave it there.

---

**POINT 5 — OVERCOME EVIL WITH GOOD (vv. 20–21)**

*"To the contrary, 'if your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him something to drink; for by so doing you will heap burning coals on his head.' Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good."*

Paul is quoting Proverbs 25:21–22 here. And the phrase *heap burning coals on his head* has confused people for a long time. Let me clear it up, because it matters.

He is not saying: be kind to your enemies so that God will punish them worse. That would collapse into a clever form of revenge, and it would contradict everything Paul has just said. In the ancient world, the burning coals imagery was associated with shame and moral exposure — the burning sensation of being convicted by kindness. When you show genuine grace to someone who has wronged you, you make the ugliness of what they did visible by contrast. Not with words. Not with confrontation. With grace. You do not shame them. The grace shames them. And sometimes — not always, but sometimes — that is what breaks a person open.

Not the argument. Not the confrontation. Not getting what they deserve.

The unexpected kindness of someone they wronged.

That is what happened in the prison. That is what my friend did when he ran across the room to his daughter's killer. The man didn't deserve that. He knew it. And that knowing — that burning — is what the gospel works with.

Paul ends with the summary: *Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good.*

This is not passive. This is the most aggressive command in the passage. Paul is telling us to fight. He is telling us that evil does not get the last word. He is telling us that the way we wage war against it is not with matching fire, but with something that burns hotter and lasts longer than anything evil can produce.

Do not be overcome by evil. That is a real warning. It is possible to be overcome by it. It is possible to let what someone did to you define you, harden you, make you into a smaller and meaner version of yourself. It is possible to win an argument, exact a revenge, come out on top — and still be overcome. Because being overcome by evil is not about losing. It is about being changed by it into something you were not made to be.

Overcome evil with good.

Not because evil doesn't deserve a response. Not because what happened was fine. Not because your pain doesn't matter. But because you are not the final justice. God is. And because the gospel you believe — if it is real — is the most powerful force in this room. More powerful than whatever was done to you. More powerful than whatever you have done. More powerful than any political force, any cultural current, any personal enemy.

---

**CLOSE — THE GOSPEL UNDERNEATH ALL OF IT**

We are not capable of this naturally. I want to be honest about that.

What Paul describes in this passage — loving enemies, blessing persecutors, trusting God with vengeance, overcoming evil with active, costly goodness — this is not the fruit of trying harder. It is the fruit of being transformed. And transformation, Paul told us back in verse 2, comes from the renewal of the mind. And that renewal comes from actually believing — really believing, in your bones, not just in your head — that the gospel is true.

If you know that every sin you have ever committed was laid on Christ. If you know that God looked at the full account of your life — every lie, every cruelty, every hidden ugliness — and chose, in the death of His Son, to absorb the justice that should have fallen on you — then you know what grace at cost looks like. And you know that the person who wronged you, the one you want to curse — they are not more guilty before God than you were before Christ. God allowed his only Son to die for people like us. We are the ones responsible for Christ's death. And God forgave us. If he can do that — if he can invite us into his heaven knowing what we are — then there is no human offense that is beyond our mandate to love. There is no enemy so formidable that God's wrath is not sufficient to handle them. There is no wound so deep that the gospel cannot reach underneath it.

So we bless. We pray. We enter each other's grief. We lay down our pride. We refuse revenge. And we fight — hard, actively, with everything we have — to overcome evil with good.

Not because we are impressive. Because He is.

*Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good.*

Let's pray.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The unbeliever is not directly addressed**: The sermon's final section proclaims the gospel clearly but directs it entirely toward the believer ("if you know... then you know..."). The unconverted hearer — who may be sitting in the room precisely because they were brought by a believing friend, or who is present and self-righteous and undisturbed — is not specifically addressed. The sermon explains what the gospel does in a believer but does not invite the unbeliever to believe it. A single paragraph addressed directly to the person who cannot do these things because they have not received the grace that funds them would close this gap.

2. **Verse 15 receives minimal development**: "Rejoice with those who rejoice, weep with those who weep" (Point 2) is treated with reasonable insight (the diagnosis of schadenfreude is good) but the structural weight of what Paul is doing here — calling the church into genuine empathic solidarity as a counter-cultural community — is not developed. The congregation is told that "love shows up anyway" without being given the theological grounding for why gospel-recipients specifically can weep with enemies. The indicative/imperative dynamic that funds every other command in the passage is briefly missing here.

3. **Spiritual differentiation is limited to the person of injury**: The sermon's conscience work consistently addresses one primary category: the person who has been wronged and wants revenge. This is the passage's primary concern, and the sermon is right to stay there. But the person who has never suffered significant injury — the prosperous, relationally comfortable, socially insulated churchgoer — is not given a corresponding point of entry. What does "overcome evil with good" mean for the person whose life contains no dramatic enemies? The hardened, the comfortable, and the person who cannot identify with the passage's scenarios are left without traction.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher's imagination for this passage is shaped primarily by dramatic suffering — persecution, murder, political opposition. The illustrations and applications are drawn from extreme situations (prison, martyrdom, Nero). These are textually appropriate, but they may cause the congregation member whose "enemy" is a passive-aggressive coworker, a distant parent, or a neighbor who has never spoken to them to feel that the passage does not quite apply to their situation. The sermon does not help the congregation locate enemy-love in the ordinary and mundane — which is, for most hearers, where the command will actually be required.

Additionally, the sermon's political application speaks almost entirely to one political posture: the person who curses leaders they oppose. The inverse failure — using political loyalty to justify ignoring the poor or dehumanizing those on the other side — is equally common and equally a failure of Romans 12:14, but it goes unaddressed.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The prison illustration is so powerful that it may create the impression that the sermon's application work is complete after Point 4. In fact, the sermon's differentiation work largely ends there. Points 5 and the close are proclamation and summary — beautifully done — but they do not add new categories of hearer or new forms of conscience engagement. A reader who experiences the emotional weight of the prison story may feel fully addressed when in fact only one dimension of the passage has been pressed into their life. The illustration is not the weakness — it is genuinely excellent. The weakness is that nothing comparable is offered for the person who is not a victim of grave injury.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The passage's closing command — "Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good" — contains a warning that the sermon identifies but does not fully press: "It is possible to be overcome by evil. It is possible to let what someone did to you define you, harden you, make you into a smaller and meaner version of yourself." This diagnostic is precisely right and the sermon states it well. But it does not spend time with it. There is a congregation of people who have been subtly overcome — not by open sin but by bitterness, by coldness, by a narrowing of the heart — who do not recognize themselves in that description because it has happened slowly and quietly over years. The sermon names the condition and moves past it. A brief pastoral stop here — describing what being slowly overcome looks like, naming its symptoms (the hardened elder, the cynical deacon, the person who stopped giving because someone once misused the money) — would have made the warning land differently.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Add a direct address to the unconverted hearer in the close.** After the gospel proclamation in the conclusion, add 3–4 sentences specifically addressed to the person who knows they cannot do these things: "If you are sitting here this morning and you know — honestly know — that you do not have this love and cannot produce it, I want you to hear this: the gospel is not just the fuel for this kind of living. It is the door into it. Christ did not die to make you a better person. He died to make you a new one. And if you have never received that — if you have been moral but not born again — today is a day you can be."

2. **Develop the empathy command (v. 15) with its gospel grounding.** In Point 2, add the indicative underneath the imperative: the reason we can genuinely weep with enemies is that we ourselves were God's enemies when Christ wept over Jerusalem and went to the cross for us. The gospel funds the empathy Paul commands. Without naming the indicative, the imperative stays at the level of moral aspiration.

3. **Locate enemy-love in the ordinary and mundane.** Add one concrete, non-dramatic example of what "overcome evil with good" looks like in an everyday situation — the neighbor who complained about your fence, the coworker who took credit for your work, the family member who excluded you from a decision. The prison illustration covers the dramatic. The congregation also needs to know what the command means in their Tuesday afternoon.

4. **Tighten the political application to a single decisive challenge.** The Point 1 political section is the right content but runs slightly long. The key question ("When is the last time you genuinely prayed for the leader you can't stand?") is the sermon's sharpest conscience probe. Land there faster and move on. The theological reflection on the gospel not being a platform is valuable but could be trimmed by half without losing the point.

5. **Press the "being overcome slowly" warning in Point 5.** Name the symptoms of gradual hardening — the cold elder, the person who has stopped hoping for the person who wronged them, the one who still prays for enemies but no longer means it. Give the quietly-overcome person a mirror before calling them to fight.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon has genuine pastoral heat, conscience-level application, and a close that earns its gospel proclamation. It does not read as correct but bloodless. The prison illustration alone disqualifies dead orthodoxy — that is not the work of a man going through theological motions.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The sermon carries real eternal gravity — particularly in the treatment of divine wrath as "terrifying, permanent, complete," in the prison illustration's demonstration of what the gospel actually costs and actually does, and in the conclusion's move from the specific commands to the specific cross. A man who has been with God could preach this with trembling, and a congregant who has been with God could receive it with tears. **+4/5**

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Romans 12:14–21 is a paraenetic passage with a strong ethical imperative cluster culminating in a programmatic summary statement. The rubric applies well. The passage is more directly applicable than most of Paul's ethical material, which means the rubric's conscience and applicatory categories are under greater pressure here — the text itself nearly does the application work, so the question is whether the preacher presses it into the congregation with differentiation and specificity, or simply restates it with illustration. This sermon presses it with genuine force in some directions and leaves others untouched, which the rubric captures accurately. No genre strain.
