## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastoral Heart Without a Preaching Spine: Promising Narrative, Missing Proclamation**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A conversational opening of 1 Kings 3 that offers genuine warmth and interesting detail while failing to establish a governing thesis, press the conscience with the text's own weight, or ground the gospel in the passage's actual argument.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: FAIL**
The sermon does not produce a single thesis sentence that arises demonstrably from the passage's own argument. The preacher begins with Solomon as a character study, pivots to a sin-tolerance illustration (the noose), detours through elaborate speculation about logistics (how long did it take to sacrifice 1,000 cattle?), offers a Hebrew word study, and closes with a brief gospel paragraph. What is the main point? Is it: "Humble yourself before God"? "Sin has consequences"? "God rewards sincere effort"? "Wisdom is the best gift"? The sermon does not decide, and therefore neither can the listener. The main point of the text — God's gracious covenant response to a young king who comes in humility and asks for others rather than himself — is never clearly articulated as the sermon's thesis.

**Authority Test: PASS (partial)**
The preacher does not stand above the text as an editor in the sense of softening uncomfortable material. However, the sermon does quietly import ideas the text does not supply (the Pikes Peak illustration as a measure of sincerity, "I think God is responding to the lengths Solomon is willing to go," the cremation math as a substitute for exegesis). These are not hostile to the text, but they displace it.

**Berean Gate Verdict: FAIL on Scopus. The sermon does not have a governing thesis that arises from the passage's main point. Evaluation continues with score cap engaged — no score above 69.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation: TRIGGERED**
The gospel appears in the final paragraph only, briefly, almost as an afterthought: "We are lost and mired in sin apart from Christ, but with Christ, we have hope and a future." This is not a gospel proclamation. There is no mention of Christ's death, resurrection, or atonement. There is no call to repentance or faith. Justification is entirely absent. A listener could not understand how to be saved from this sermon.

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic: TRIGGERED**
The application amounts to: avoid small sins, honor God like Solomon tried to, pursue Christ. There is no differentiated address. The self-righteous are not cornered. The doubter is not found. The backslider is not named. No specific conscience-searching call is made.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation: TRIGGERED**
The sermon ends without a clear "therefore." There is no moment where the preacher looks the congregation in the eye and says what they must now believe, repent of, obey, or pursue in response to this text. The final paragraph drifts into description of Holy Spirit ministry and an analogy about mud — warm and not unhelpful — but it is not exhortation.

**Score cap: 69 maximum. Three fatal flaw flags triggered.**

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the text's most powerful applicatory edge is the question he never asks: **What would you ask for if God said, "Ask what I shall give you"?** Solomon's answer is the diagnostic. He did not ask for long life, wealth, or victory over enemies (v. 11) — he asked for a heart to serve others. That is the text's search of the congregation's soul, and it goes entirely unused.

The preacher also likely does not see that the "God responds to effort" interpretation edges toward merit theology — a significant doctrinal drift that needs correcting precisely because it sounds so natural.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The cremation math section **feels** like depth — it signals preparation, effort, curiosity, and pastoral humor. The congregation will likely enjoy it. But it is the sermon's most significant misdirection. It fills the space where textual exposition should be, produces a conclusion the text does not authorize, and trains the listener to look at Solomon's effort rather than at God's grace. The preacher's research energy went into calculating sacrifice duration rather than into the text's own argument.

The Hebrew word study similarly **feels** substantive — and the instinct is right — but the translation observations are not connected to any claim the sermon makes. They sit in the sermon as exegetical color without structural function.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Verse 9 is the sermon's heart, and it goes untreated.** "Give your servant therefore an understanding mind to govern your people, that I may discern between good and evil, for who is able to govern this your great people?" Solomon asks for the capacity to serve others well. He does not ask for himself. And God's delight is explicit: "It pleased the Lord that Solomon had asked this" (v. 10). The text is a window into what God finds pleasing — not the quantity of sacrifice, not the geography of worship, but the posture of a heart that forgets itself in concern for others. This is also the Christological depth of the passage: Solomon foreshadows the greater Son of David who came not to be served but to serve. The sermon walks past this entirely.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Establish a thesis in the introduction.** Before anything else, state the sermon's one claim: e.g., "God delights in the heart that forgets itself to serve others — and this text shows us why, and drives us to the only One who ever did that perfectly." Everything in the sermon should prove or develop that claim.

2. **Relocate the pet-sins section.** The noose illustration and sin list are genuinely strong, but they belong *after* the text has established the diagnostic — not before. Use the text's own question ("What would you ask for?") to create the conviction, then bring the concrete sins in as evidence.

3. **Cut or compress the cremation math.** One sentence is enough: "A thousand animals — this was months of sustained sacrifice, not a spontaneous gesture." Then move immediately to the text's own explanation of why God responded: because of what Solomon asked, not how much he sacrificed.

4. **Add a gospel proclamation paragraph grounded in the text.** The Christological connection is available: Solomon the wise king, pointing to Christ the Wisdom of God (1 Cor. 1:24), who asked not for his own life but for ours. The cross is where God's wisdom and God's love meet. Name the cross. Name justification. Give the convicted hearer somewhere to go.

5. **Add a direct exhortation before the close.** After the application, add a "therefore" moment: "So what must you do? Bring your self-seeking heart — your wish-list for God — and lay it down at the foot of a cross that already secured everything you need. Ask for wisdom to serve the people around you. And trust the One who already asked the Father on your behalf."

6. **Apply Perkins' differentiation.** Address at least three hearer types distinctly: the self-seeker (what are you really asking God for?), the believer who has drifted into compromise like Solomon (the Pharaoh's daughter pattern), and the person not yet in Christ who needs more than better priorities — they need new birth.
