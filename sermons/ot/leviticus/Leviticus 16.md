So we finally got to Leviticus. How has your reading been in Leviticus? Is it kind of challenging?

When I was in bible college we had a couple of classes where we had to read through the entire bible, after numbers, Leviticus was the book of the bible that people complained about the most. I understand it. This book was written by moses, and so much of moses law seems like it doesn't apply to us today. Right? I mean we don't have to follow the dietary law any more thanks to God's revelation to Peter. We don't pattern our government directly after the way God gave moses. We don't seem to have any connection to so much of this.

OR do we.

I heard a pastor in NC preach on the subject of the law. He told a story about a married man that came up to him and said "Pastor, I was walking through the mall the other day, and I saw this really beautiful woman in front of me in very skimpy clothing. And I knew what I was supposed to think. I was supposed to recognize that this woman, while beautiful, is not some object for my sexual pleasure. I was supposed to remember that honoring her, my wife, and the God who saved me is far more satisfying to me than indulgence in lust would be.
"But, Pastor, that's not what I felt in the moment. And isn't forcing myself to do what's right—turning the other direction and going home to my wife—relying on the Law? Well, I didn't want to be a Galatian heretic, so I acted on my heart's desires and got the girl's number, then went home and believed the gospel to get God's forgiveness."
Do you think I'd say to that man, "Great work! That's exactly how I was hoping you'd understand and apply Galatians"?
Of course not. The LAW does have a place in our life, and part of that is to teach us more deeply about Christ.

Its easy to say that Christ lived a perfect and sinless life. In fact we say that so easily, and gloss over the details so readily that Leviticus is necessary to see just how amazing that task was. Think about your kids for a minute. We had VBS last week, and I know many of your kids. They are great kids. But have they ever done something wrong? Certainly! All of them have been disobedient at some point in their life, all of them have done something to dishonor their parents, all of them have taken that cookie out of the cookie jar after they were told to keep out. All of them have done these things. ALL of our kids can't even keep the ten commandments, how can we expect them to keep all of the 613 laws of the old testament? Yet Jesus, even when he was a child, never did anything wrong. Never lied, never stole, never cheated. Never disobeyed, never dishonored his parents, never broke a single one of the ten commandments, much less any of the other 613 laws. Not even as a child.

What would that be like parents? To raise a kid that never did anything wrong?

So then do those laws not tell us something about our savior? Many of them are about how to think or act – Ex 20:3 says we are not even to entertain the thought that there might be other god's besides God. That we are to emulate God's ways. To reprove sinners, Leviticus 19. To not take revenge, to not bear grudges, to honor those who know and teach God's word. Not to mention the command for men to not shave their beards. All of that (and more) was just from leviticus 19. What does that tell us about Christ? That in ALL of his ways, he emulated God's ways. What a task he accomplished.

Some of them make me ask why – you know what I mean? Like how California prohibits walking your elephant off leash on Sundays. It seems like there must have been a story there. Like with moses prohibition on eating worms. Lev 11.

Some are straightforward. In a world where refrigeration doesn't exist – don't eat fat left outside overnight. Pretty straightforward and clearly a bad idea. Bugs, maggots, bacteria, probably a good way to die of food poisoning. Pretty surprised God had to spell out that maggots were off the menu also.

There are basically three types of laws – Civil, Ceremonial and Moral.

Civil laws were set in place to help the nation as a whole. Without their civil laws they would have had chaos. Jesus kept the civil laws that he would have brushed up against perfectly. He fulfilled the civil law, and started a new Israel. This NEW Spiritual Israel was not governed by the same civil law that moses had established. So these laws have passed away. There is A TON of wisdom in them, they are good guidelines. These laws were sort of like our own societal politics. How should we take care of public health? The poor? Etc. A preacher I like In North Carolina that may be the next president of our denomination rightly points out that ALL the civil laws were fulfilled in Christ.

Likewise Christ perfectly kept, and fulfilled the Ceremonial laws. The purpose of the ceremonial law was to demonstrate just how Holy GOD is, and remind us of just how fall short of that we fall. PLUS and perhaps more importantly it points to Christ. Fall short in this way or that way, and a sacrifice must be made, something flawless and innocent takes the punishment you deserve. This was in part to drive home the COST of their sin. Some of us would have had pretty costly childhoods. Or imagine when you were a teenager and rebelling, how much that would have cost in sheep? Yet Christ lived a perfect and sinless life. He would never have needed to make an offering for sin, and so he was the only one ever able to be an offering for sin. In so doing he fulfilled the law, satisfied God's wrath for sin, and instituted a new system for handing sin going forward.

The third kind of law was the Moral law. Unlike the other two types of laws that were linked to the politics of the day, or the particular covenant God had made with the people at that time, the Moral law remains the same. Most of the moral laws were not only repeated but intensified. Christ kept these perfectly as well, as we've already discussed. The moral law was adjusted in another way though. Were not just meant to obey. Though we should obey. Christians keep the moral commands, not because "it's the law," but because they love God and want to be like him.

We have talked about all of this to get to our scripture this morning. Read Leviticus 16:30-31 with me. This is one of many pictures of many elements of Christs ministry that we can find in Leviticus. This day of attonement is also the premise behind the idea of a scape goat. As part of their ceremony – lambs would be put to death, their blood taken into the holy of holies, and burned on an alter. Afterwhich they would release an unblemished goat to run into the wilderness. The "scape goat".

There is just so much symbolism for Christ here. Christ is the lamb right – the lamb that takes away the sin of the world. The lamb that was crucified for us on the cross at Calvary. The lamb that was beaten, and mocked, and nailed to the tree for you and I. Likewise in the days before Christ, if you sinned, you would have to take a perfect little lamb to the temple, and the high priest would kill the lamb and the lambs blood would cover your sins. Likewise when the high priest and the jews had Christ put to death, and his blood flower out of him. That blood he shed so freely for you and I did more than cover our sins. Because of his perfect life, his blood atones for our sins. His blood payed for our sins. Because he had lived a perfect life, and was able to fulfill all three types of law, he was able to make this new covenant with us.

Paul says ""Where, O death, is your victory? Where, O death, is your sting?" 56 The sting of death is sin, and the power of sin is the law. 57 But thanks be to God! He gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ. 1 Corinthians 15:55-57

Sin then has lost its power, because the Civil and ceremonial laws have been fulfilled and changed. The Moral law which Christ affirms still apply to us today. But in Christ we have victory!

So we talked about a couple of purposes of the Law, to show us how far short we fall, and to point to Christ. There is one more purpose that were going to talk about and then we'll close.

Turn with me to Galatians 3:19-26

"Why then was the law given? It was added for the sake of transgressions until the Seed to whom the promise was made would come …. Is the law therefore contrary to God's promises? Absolutely not! …. the Scripture imprisoned everything under sin's power, so that the promise might be given on the basis of faith in Jesus Christ to those who believe …. The law, then, was our guardian until Christ, so that we could be justified by faith. But since that faith has come, we are no longer under a guardian, for through faith you are all sons of God in Christ Jesus." (Galatians 3:19-26 CSB)

That's a hard thing to wrap our minds around sometimes I think. That we are really free.

The Moral law is a guardian. It helps us identify our sinful desires so we can curb them. A curb helps keep you pointed in the right direction. Helps keep you from going too far in a parking lot, helps keep you on the road and pointed in the right direction. So it guards us by giving us a standard or by being a curb.
It guards us by showing us our reflection. It shows us the good, the bad and the ugly. I think that's a large part of why people don't like to read or preach Leviticus. Who really wants to see the depths of their depravity?

The law is sort of like a thermometer. It shows the temperature of our heart, but doesn't change it.

Someone said it like this: "When you are an unbeliever, the Law points to your need for grace. When you are a believer, grace points to your need for the Law." So the Law is sort of like a compass informing our conscience.

JD Greear says its like this: when you find your self desiring something that's sinful, you have to take a minute, to change your focus, and pray to God something like this: "God, I am going to choose to do the right thing, but I really want to do the wrong thing. Please, because of Jesus, fix my broken 'wanter."

I think that's a good place to start. There is a biblical principal called progressive sanctification. That's the idea that over time, our wants, our will and our desires will slowly come more into line with God's wants, will, and desires for us. The more time we spend on our relationship with the Lord, the more this takes place. The more – because of Christ – our "wanter" is restored.
All of this starts with a relationship with Jesus though.
Gospel.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Well-Intentioned Survey That Stays on the Surface — The Day of Atonement Deserves More Than a Flyover

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A friendly and accessible introduction to the purposes of the Law that gestures toward Christ and the Day of Atonement but fails to open Leviticus 16 exegetically, apply it specifically, or issue an actual summons to the congregation.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: MARGINAL PASS (borderline)**
The sermon's stated interest is in the purposes of the Law and how Leviticus points to Christ — and Leviticus 16 is referenced in service of that theme. However, the main point of the sermon (the Law has three purposes: to show us how far we fall, to point to Christ, and to serve as guardian) is not the main point of Leviticus 16 itself. The Day of Atonement text (Lev 16:30–31) is read aloud and then used as a springboard for Law/Gospel theology rather than as a text to be opened. The sermon is about the Law in general; it is not a sermon on Leviticus 16.

**Authority Test: PASS (partial)**
The preacher submits to the text's broad claims and does not noticeably soften anything. There is no evident editorializing against the text. However, the sermon quietly sidesteps the hard specificity of Leviticus 16 — the blood rites, the two goats, the high priestly entry into the Holy of Holies, the annual nature of corporate atonement — in favor of general categories.

**Ruling:** The sermon passes the gate narrowly, but the evaluator notes that this is closer to a topical sermon illustrated from Leviticus 16 than a genuine expository treatment of the passage. The gate condition is met, but barely.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic: TRIGGERED**
The application throughout is vague. "All of this starts with a relationship with Jesus" is not a specific call. The congregation is told to let the law be a curb, a thermometer, a compass — but no specific sin is named, no particular hearer is addressed, no concrete action is demanded. The sermon ends at the level of principle rather than penetration.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation: TRIGGERED**
The sermon explains and illustrates but never summons. There is no "therefore" moment. The congregation is not called to believe, repent of a specific sin, pursue a particular obedience, or do anything concrete. The word "Gospel." appears at the close as a heading, not as a declaration or a call.

**Score is capped at 69.**

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Mostly Christ, in abstract typological categories. But the Christ on offer here is more a doctrinal concept than a living Lord who demands and satisfies. The married man at the opening made the listener think of himself — which is the right move — but the text was not then brought to bear on that self with any specificity.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas. The taxonomic structure, the metaphor clusters, and the survey format keep God at arm's length. There is no moment of arrest, no moment where God speaks with weight through the preacher's voice.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Informed. The sermon explains categories competently. It does not convict — the conscience is touched but not cornered. It does not comfort — the gospel's power is stated more than experienced.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The married man illustration** (paragraph 2–3) — This is genuinely strong. It is specific, local, morally acute, and introduces the Law/Gospel tension with pastoral honesty. This is the kind of opening that earns the right to preach.

2. **The typological core is sound** — The connections between the Day of Atonement's ceremonial elements and Christ's atoning work are legitimate and covenantally grounded, not artificially imposed. The scapegoat → Christ typology is real and worth developing at much greater length.

3. **Accessible register** — The sermon is preachable, warm, and comprehensible. It would not lose a first-time visitor, a teenager, or a new believer. This is a genuine pastoral instinct and should not be lost in revision.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text is never opened** — Leviticus 16:30–31 is read, named, and then used as a peg for general typological observations. What does "you shall be clean from all your sins" actually mean? What does "a Sabbath of solemn rest" communicate about God? What was happening in the Holy of Holies? What does it mean that this had to happen *every year*? None of these questions are asked, let alone answered. The passage deserved a sermon, not a mention.

2. **No exhortation — the sermon ends with a heading, not a command** — "Gospel." appears as the final word but is not a gospel declaration, a gospel call, or a gospel command. The congregation is left with progressive sanctification as a concept but not summoned toward anything. What must they now believe? Repent of? Do? These questions go unanswered.

3. **Conscience engagement collapses after the opening** — The married man illustration promises a sermon that will press into real life and real sin. That promise is not kept. The moral law is described as a "curb" and a "thermometer" but is never actually applied to any specific sin in the congregation's life. The sermon speaks about conviction without producing it.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

- **The preacher likely does not see how much the sermon is a survey and not a proclamation.** It reads as if prepared to cover the ground of Leviticus before arriving at a text — but covering ground is not the same as preaching a text. The instinct to contextualize is good; the execution leaves the text buried.
- **The gospel is present as background music, not melody.** The preacher likely believes the gospel has been clearly preached. But "his blood paid for our sins" surrounded by taxonomy and metaphor is background music. The congregation may not feel they have heard good news — only good theology.
- **The closing "Gospel." may feel like a landing to the preacher but reads as an abrupt stop to the congregation.** What gospel? For whom? How do they receive it? The word is offered as a destination without a path.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

**The three-part law taxonomy creates the impression of theological depth.** Civil, ceremonial, moral — the categories are handled correctly and with some confidence. But correct categories are not the same as preached truth. The taxonomy is the scaffolding; the sermon never builds the house. The congregation leaves with the categories rather than with Christ.

**The typological survey feels like exposition.** Naming connections between the Day of Atonement and Christ's work creates the feel of an expository sermon. But naming a typological connection is not the same as opening it. The preacher lists what the type points to without unfolding why it is designed that way, what it demands of the hearer, or how it changes them.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Leviticus 16 is one of the most Christ-saturated, theologically dense passages in the entire Torah. The Epistle to the Hebrews devotes four chapters (Heb 4–10) to unpacking it. This sermon had access to the entire scaffolding of Hebrews and chose to build a taxonomy instead.

Specifically: **the annual repetition of the Day of Atonement is the great missed opportunity.** Hebrews 10:1–4 makes the argument that the very fact it had to be repeated every year proves it could not finally cleanse. The conscience of the worshiper was never fully settled. One sacrifice, once, forever — that is what Yom Kippur pointed toward and could never achieve. This is the theological heart of the passage, and it goes entirely unpreached. A congregation left with this truth would feel both the weight of their guilt (no annual sacrifice can truly cleanse) and the explosive relief of Christ's once-for-all offering.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild the sermon's architecture around Leviticus 16 itself, not around a law taxonomy.** Move the civil/ceremonial/moral discussion to a brief contextual frame (2–3 minutes maximum). Then spend the sermon's body in the text: the two goats, the blood in the Holy of Holies, the annual repetition, and the Hebrews 10 commentary on what all of it was pointing toward.

2. **Preach the inadequacy of the repeated sacrifice before you preach its fulfillment.** Let Leviticus 16 function as the hammer (the law) before Christ functions as the balm. The congregation needs to feel the weight of "this had to happen every year and it still wasn't enough" before they feel the relief of "once for all."

3. **Add a direct exhortation at the close.** The JD Greear prayer is a good resource — but it is offered as an example, not a command. Say it directly: "Some of you are carrying guilt that no annual ritual could have cleansed. You have been coming to God, confessing, believing, and the guilt returns. Bring it to Christ. His blood does not cover your sin — it removes it. Come to him now. Repent. Believe. And do not leave carrying what he has already paid to take from you."

4. **Press the married man illustration to its conclusion.** You opened with a man who misused the gospel as a license for sin. What does Leviticus 16 say to that man? The scapegoat bearing away sins was not a blank check for more sin. The Day of Atonement was accompanied by the command: "you shall afflict yourselves." Name what affliction looks like today. Turn the illustration into a specific use.

5. **Replace the metaphor cluster (curb/thermometer/compass) with one strong image from Leviticus 16 itself.** The text provides far more powerful imagery than borrowed metaphors can. The image of the high priest — once a year, alone, trembling, with blood, in the presence of God on behalf of the entire nation — is already a sermon. Use it.

6. **The conclusion must not be a word; it must be a summons.** "Gospel." is a topic heading. "Christ has done what Yom Kippur could never do. His blood has entered the true Most Holy Place. Your name has been carried before God by the Great High Priest. Believe him. Rest there." — that is a conclusion.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This passage — Leviticus 16 — is a ceremonial/legal text embedded in the Mosaic covenant, and its genre does stress the rubric's categories in specific ways. Exposition in a ceremonial law passage legitimately involves typological and redemptive-historical work rather than verse-by-verse propositional extraction. The rubric accommodates this: the Christ-centeredness subcategory explicitly rewards "organic covenantal movement." The conscience and exhortation categories, however, do not bend for genre — Hebrews 10 proves that Leviticus 16 can produce profound conscience application and urgent exhortation. Genre does not excuse the sermon's weakness here; it only reframes what good exposition looks like.
