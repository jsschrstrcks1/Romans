### Opening — Mark Dever and the Book of Numbers

I was listening to Mark Dever preach this week. He was preaching through the entire book of Numbers in a single sitting. Don't worry. We are not doing that tonight.

But we are going to spend some time in Numbers. And Dever had some insights that got me thinking, and you'll hear the gleanings of that tonight.

His point was this: as you read through Numbers again and again, you see the Israelites locked in a cycle they can't break out of. Over and over and over. They start out lacking contentment. That lack of contentment leads them to sin. Leads them to grumbling. Leads them to idolatry. And it even leads Moses — Moses — to sin.

At one point Moses says to God: *these people you've saddled me with make me wish I were dead.*

Think about that for a moment. Dealing with discontentment — other people's discontentment, his own — to the point where death seems like the better option. Some of you have been there. If you're there now, or someone you love is there, please get help. I know a counselor. She's excellent. Don't try to carry that alone.

But that's where Moses lands in tonight's passage. He is struggling under the weight of his people's whining. And before we judge him for it — let's be honest about what that whining looks like.

### Illustration — The Stuffed Animal

I love my nieces and nephews. I do. And I just got back from a wonderful week with them — thank you for letting me go. We spent the week by the pool, reconnecting, catching up on some much-needed rest. It was a great week.

But there was one moment.

One of the boys wanted a little stuffed animal from a gift shop. A five-dollar item. You can get it at Rural King for three. It was honestly a little on the girly side, and this boy just had to have it at all costs. He said the thing every kid says when they've locked in on something: *I'll never ask for anything again. I just want this stuffed toy. Please get me the toy.*

I didn't have my wallet with me. If I had, I probably would have bought it for him just to make the whining stop. Five dollars is sometimes a small price to pay for peace. His parents were there, though, and they made the right call — they told him to wait and see what else he might come across that he'd like better. It took a half hour, but it was the better decision.

Because here's the thing about that toy. He would have played with it for five minutes and never touched it again. No lasting value. No lasting joy. It would have satisfied for a moment and then sat in the bottom of a toy box until someone threw it away.

It's wonderful being young, because the thing your heart most desperately wants only costs five dollars.

As adults, what we covet tends to be far more costly. Houses. Status. Relationships. Authority. Power. What's yours? We all have something.

Pastor Zitt shared last week that for him, for a season, it was illegal drugs. It doesn't matter what the object is. The lesson is the same. Discontentment, left unchecked, leads to sin.

### Part 1 — What Discontentment Really Is

I want to walk through several things discontentment actually is. This isn't an exhaustive list. But each one is worth sitting with.

**Discontentment is a failure of trust.**

Contentment is trusting God. Completely. Discontentment is the opposite of that — which means it's the opposite of faith. When you're discontent, what you're actually saying is: *God, I know what I need better than you do.*

Say that out loud and it sounds absurd. We would never say that to God's face. But when we nurse discontentment in our hearts, when we let it grow, that's exactly what we're doing. We're declaring ourselves the better judge of our own situation.

**Discontentment is a complaint against God's plan.**

Paul Tripp puts it sharply: *I love me and have a wonderful plan for my life.* There's a lot of truth in that. When we're discontent, we're not just unhappy about our circumstances. We're insisting that our plan is better than His. That we should be the sovereign one.

**Discontentment covets what God has chosen not to give.**

Romans 8:32 asks: *He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things?*

If He isn't giving it to you right now, He has a reason. And that reason is grounded in a God who already gave His Son for you. Whatever He's withholding, He is not withholding out of indifference. He knows what you need. He proved it on the cross.

**Discontentment tells God He's made a mistake.**

My circumstances are wrong. They should be otherwise. I will not be at peace until they change to match what I want.

That's what discontentment says. And notice what it assumes: that your desires are trustworthy. But your desires, apart from God, are shaped by a sin nature. By selfishness. Why would you trust that over the wisdom of God?

**Discontentment denies the wisdom of God.**

Raise your hand if you think you know more than God. Anyone? If you think you're smarter? More wise?

Nobody. Of course nobody.

And yet — isn't this exactly what Eve did in the garden? She didn't raise her hand either. She just acted on it. *Has God really said?* That question is at the heart of every sin ever committed. And it is at the heart of discontentment. We may not say it, but we're asking it. *Has God really said He knows best? Has God really said this is good for me? Has God really said I can trust Him here?*

### Illustration — The Lottery Winner

Let me tell you a story I heard recently.

A woman up in the northeast won one of those multi-state jackpots. One of the enormous ones. She'd been buying the same ticket every week for years. This particular week she bought her ticket, went home, and didn't check it for several days. When she finally looked, she had won something in the range of three hundred million dollars.

She called her boss and quit before she ever cashed the ticket. Bought a new car. A boat. An RV. Then she cashed in, made the rounds on the news programs, and spent like she had an endless well. She started buying houses for family members. Paid off her best friend's mortgage. Paid off her best friend's son's student loans. Before she knew it she had burned through six months of income.

So she called one of those companies that buys out lottery winnings — you get all the cash now, minus their cut, and they collect your payments for the next thirty years. She took the deal.

Within a year, she had spent every dollar. She had never finished paying off the things she bought. She had nothing left. No job. No retirement. No savings. And people were still calling her asking for more.

She ended her own life. What she believed was the best thing that had ever happened to her destroyed her.

The things we think will finally satisfy — the thing we're convinced we need — are not always what we think. Sometimes what we're most desperate to have would wreck us. We don't always know what's good for us. God does.

### Part 2 — How God Feels About Discontentment

I've heard people say over the years, *Wouldn't it be great if God spoke as clearly today as He did with Israel?* No. That would not be great.

When God needed to communicate with particular force, people's houses caught fire. The ground opened up and swallowed people whole. Plagues came and killed tens of thousands. God's anger is a terrible thing, and we should have a very healthy fear of it.

Look at verse 1 again: *His anger was kindled, and the fire of the LORD burned among them and consumed some outlying parts of the camp.* God got so angry at Israel's complaining that the perimeter of the camp caught fire. Over three million people — some estimates run even higher — and fire started breaking out around the edges of their camp because of their whining.

That's how God feels about discontentment.

I've read about a plant called Stapelia. It's a large flower — I saw a picture of one that dwarfed the man standing next to it. Its color has been described as "the color of three days of rotted meat." A scientist who tried to study it in a greenhouse described the smell as *worse than an entire herd of rotting elephants* — and reported that the stench was so severe his team couldn't enter the building.

In Leviticus we read about sacrifices and offerings that were a *pleasing aroma* to the Lord. There are things that please God. And discontentment is not one of them. If a pleasing aroma is what faithfulness looks like before God, then discontentment is the Stapelia — rotten and repulsive.

Thank God for the cross. Because Christ satisfied the wrath of God — He took the full weight of it — so that you and I are not washed away by it.

### Part 3 — What to Do About It

The Puritan Jeremiah Burroughs wrote extensively about discontentment and its connection to sin. He wrote:

> *Have good thoughts of God and make good interpretations of his dealings toward you. It is very hard to live comfortably and cheerfully among friends when one makes harsh interpretations of the words and actions of another. The only way to keep sweet contentment and comfort in Christian societies is to make the best interpretations of things we can. Likewise, a primary way to help keep comfort and contentment in our hearts is to make good interpretations of God's dealings with us.*

*(Contentment, Prosperity, and God's Glory)*

We know God is good. We know He has good plans for His children. We know He is working all things for our good. We can take that to the bank. But when we grow discontent, we're challenging all of that. We're assuming He's doing something wrong. That we know better.

Burroughs says this is making bad interpretations of what God is doing.

I took a counseling course some years ago, and the professor described what he called the four horsemen of the marital apocalypse — four patterns that, when they take root, tend to end marriages. One of them is always assuming your partner has evil motives. If you say to your wife, *You look nice today,* and she responds in complete seriousness, *What — you think I look bad every other day?* — you might be living with that horseman.

What he meant was a compliment. She received it as a criticism. When you consistently interpret your partner's motives with suspicion and doubt, you have a serious problem.

The same thing applies to your relationship with God. If you're discontent with what He's allowing — if you're questioning His motives, His methods, His wisdom — then you have constructed a version of God who is working against you. And that version is not real.

Philip Holmes wrote: *If we truly believed what the Bible says about how God sees us, it would transform the way we interpret all His actions as mercies. I know that in the midst of my battles with discontentment and besetting sins, it's hard to view what is happening in my life as anything but a condemnation and punishment.*

Most of us know this from the inside. In the middle of hard seasons, it is difficult to see what is happening as anything but punishment. The mind slides there naturally. But that is a bad interpretation of a good God. Burroughs' prescription applies directly: make good interpretations of His dealings. The God who did not spare His own Son is not withholding good from you out of indifference or cruelty.

After pride, discontentment may be the thing we struggle with most on this side of heaven.

### Tell Yourself the Truth

One of my nieces, when she was smaller, had a habit of turning her hopes into certainties. *I hope it rains today* would slowly, over the course of an afternoon, become *It's going to rain today.* It wasn't necessarily true. But she hoped it would be, and the hope and the fact got twisted together until she couldn't tell the difference.

We had to regularly remind her: tell yourself the truth.

That's the first move when discontentment sets in. Tell yourself the truth about who God is and what He has done.

The work of the cross is infinitely more than we ever deserved. If God had only done that — died for rebellious sinners who contributed nothing to their own salvation — He would already have been incomprehensibly merciful. And yet He does far more than that. You have stories. Every one of you has a story of how He has worked in your life for your good. Sometimes toward your comfort. Sometimes toward your joy. Always for your good. Hold onto that when things are hard.

### Keep Your Eyes on the Prize

Paul writes:

> *Indeed, I count everything as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord. For his sake I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as rubbish, in order that I may gain Christ.* (Philippians 3:8)

The best possible things this world can offer — the best — are rubbish measured against knowing Christ. That stuffed animal the boy wanted? Rubbish. The new house? Rubbish. The relationship you've been chasing? Rubbish. The shiny thing, whatever it is — rubbish. None of it compares to the surpassing worth of knowing Jesus.

Now remember who wrote that. Here's what preceded it:

> *…with far greater labors, far more imprisonments, with countless beatings, and often near death. Five times I received at the hands of the Jews the forty lashes less one. Three times I was beaten with rods. Once I was stoned. Three times I was shipwrecked; a night and a day I was adrift at sea; on frequent journeys, in danger from rivers, danger from robbers, danger from my own people, danger from Gentiles, danger in the city, danger in the wilderness, danger at sea, danger from false brothers; in toil and hardship, through many a sleepless night, in hunger and thirst, often without food, in cold and exposure. And apart from other things, there is the daily pressure on me of my anxiety for all the churches.* (2 Corinthians 11:23-28)

That man — with those marks on his body, with that list behind him — says the best things this world has to offer are garbage in comparison to knowing Jesus.

James says it the same way: *Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds.* (James 1:2)

In Romans he says: *For I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us.* (Romans 8:18)

And Jesus himself said it this way:

> *The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field, which a man found and covered up. Then in his joy he goes and sells all that he has and buys that field.* (Matthew 13:44)

We may never know, on this side of heaven, the why behind the things that make us discontent. But we can hold onto the Who — the one who is the source of every lasting joy.

When discontentment rises, tell yourself the truth. Then fix your eyes on the treasure.

He is worth more than anything you are grieving. He is worth more than anything you have wanted and not received. He is worth more than whatever you have lost.

---

### Gospel

Maybe you don't know Him today. Maybe you've been polite about Jesus for a long time — nodding at the right moments, keeping a respectful distance. Maybe you've been chasing the thing that was going to finally satisfy, and you keep finding out it doesn't.

There is only one source of lasting joy. And He is not a thing to be acquired. He is a person who gave everything to make a way for you.

You are not here by accident. The God who provided bread from the sky for three million people in a desert has not lost track of you. And He is not your enemy. Whatever He is withholding, whatever He is allowing, whatever road He has you on — He is not your enemy.

He is the treasure in the field.

Do you know Him today?

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and accessible opening of Numbers 11:1-15 that faithfully names discontentment as sin and grounds it in the character of God, while falling short of a full gospel proclamation and leaving several categories of hearers unaddressed.

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS**
The sermon's thesis — discontentment is a complex of sins against God's wisdom, sovereignty, and goodness, and the cure is found in right thinking about God and in the surpassing worth of Christ — arises demonstrably from the passage. The text's own argument (Israel's complaining, God's wrath, Moses' collapse) is the organizing spine.

**Authority Test: PASS**
The preacher does not soften the text. God's wrath is stated plainly. The Stapelia illustration is striking rather than comfortable. The preacher stands under the Word.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — Partial: Christ present but not essential.** The gospel section at the end ("He is the treasure in the field") gestures toward Christ as the cure, and Romans 8:32 is cited as Christ-grounding. But a careful listener could receive the sermon's core argument — discontentment is sin, make better interpretations of God's dealings, count your blessings — without the cross being strictly necessary. The gospel functions as a closing invitation rather than the structural load-bearing answer to the problem the sermon has just diagnosed.

**Flag 5 — Partial: Exhortation present but thin.** "Tell yourself the truth. Fix your eyes on the treasure." This is directional but not imperative. The sermon closes with an invitation to the unconverted but does not issue a clear call to the convicted believer: *repent of this specific sin, this week, in this way.* The language remains encouragement rather than summons.

**Score cap: Not fully triggered — the flags are partial.** But they register as significant weaknesses.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 18/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (7/10):* The sermon stays close to Numbers 11:1-15. The five-part anatomy of discontentment (failure of trust, complaint against God's plan, coveting, telling God He's mistaken, denying His wisdom) is drawn from the text's dynamics rather than imposed. However, significant verses receive little treatment: the "rabble" distinction (v.4), the manna description (vv.7-9), and particularly Moses' own collapse — which is the text's most theologically loaded moment — are underdeveloped. Moses' sin is named but not exegeted. The passage's own climax at vv.11-15 (Moses' breakdown before God) receives less weight than the opening complaint.
- *Contextual Awareness (4/5):* The Mark Dever framing helpfully situates Numbers 11 within the book's cycle. The pattern of Israel's complaint and God's response is acknowledged. No treatment of how this fits into the larger wilderness narrative or the Pentateuchal arc.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5):* The meaning is clear, but the preacher does not take the congregation into the text's own mechanics — why the fire, what "the rabble" signals, what manna's significance is. The passage is paraphrased and applied rather than opened.
- *Logical Faithfulness (4/5):* No eisegesis detected. The move from discontentment-as-sin to the cure via right thinking is text-governed. The Philippians and Romans cross-references are appropriately deployed, not proof-texted.

**FCF (Fallen Condition Focus):** Identified and named well: the human heart's refusal to trust God with its circumstances. This is concrete and specific.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7):* Christ appears — Romans 8:32 is the strongest Christ-move in the sermon, grounding God's current withholding in His past giving of the Son. The Philippians 3:8 "surpassing worth" section gives Christ real weight. But the connection is not fully developed as a covenantal or redemptive-historical logic — it is more motivational than theological. The closing gospel call is brief.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (1/3):* The manna is not identified as a type pointing forward to the Bread of Life. Israel's wilderness grumbling is not set within the Exodus-to-Christ arc. This is a significant missed connection given the passage's richness.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4/4):* No theological sloppiness detected. The statements about God's wrath, the cross satisfying wrath, and contentment as trust are accurate.
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse (4/4):* No confessional contradiction present. The sermon does not engage explicit soteriological or covenant categories, so the positive test is largely not in play — but nothing contradicts Reformed theology.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (2/5):* "He took the full weight of it" (God's wrath) appears in one sentence in Part 2. The closing gospel section speaks of Christ as treasure and source of lasting joy but does not articulate justification by faith, atonement, or the terms of the gospel. An unbeliever listening closely would not know what to do with what they've heard.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5):* Christ is genuinely necessary to the sermon's argument — Romans 8:32 and the Philippians 3:8 section establish that the cure for discontentment is Christ-valued-above-all. The argument would be weaker without Him.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5):* The cross appears ("Thank God for the cross") but as an aside rather than a landing point. The sermon's gravitational center is Burroughs' prescription — make good interpretations of God's dealings — not the cross.

*Synagogue Test:* A devout non-Christian could attend this sermon and leave largely untroubled by the necessity of Christ's blood. The moral and theological content — trust God, He knows best, don't complain — does not require the cross as its foundation. This is a flag.

*Law/Gospel Dialectic:* The law (discontentment is sin and provokes God's wrath) is well-swung as a hammer. But the gospel balm is too light and too late to fully relieve what the hammer has struck.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10/15**

- *Heart Penetration (4/5):* The five-part anatomy of discontentment is genuinely conscience-pressing. "Raise your hand if you think you know more than God" is the kind of moment that reaches across the aisle of the mind to the heart. The Eve connection is strong.
- *Specificity (4/5):* The examples are grounded — stuffed animal, lottery winner, drug addiction, marriage "horsemen." These land in real life. The ask is appropriately concrete: "What's yours? We all have something."
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon addresses primarily the believer who is struggling with discontentment. The self-righteous person (who has already mastered contentment, or thinks they have) is not addressed. The backslider and the hardened-but-religious are not distinguished. The closing gospel section reaches for the unconverted, which is good, but the spectrum of Perkins' hearers is mostly collapsed into one.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Call to Response (2/4):* "Tell yourself the truth. Fix your eyes on the treasure." This is directional but not imperative. There is no moment where the preacher looks the congregation in the eye and says: *You must repent of this specific thing. This week. Stop telling God He's made a mistake. Come to the table.* The language remains the encouraging register rather than the commanding one.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The Burroughs prescription is genuinely useful equipping. But no outward dimension — witness, corporate life, how the church bears one another's discontentment — is addressed.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 7/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* Three clear parts (What discontentment is / How God feels about it / What to do) are well-labeled. The structure is traceable.
- *Progression (3/3):* The sermon builds: diagnosis → divine response → cure. This is sound movement.
- *Unity (1/2):* The "Tell Yourself the Truth" and "Keep Your Eyes on the Prize" sections feel like two separate closing movements rather than a unified conclusion. The sermon ends three times (Burroughs application, Philippians section, gospel invitation).

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

- *Relevance (2/2):* The stuffed animal, the lottery winner, and the marriage horsemen are native to ordinary life. The Stapelia flower is a surprising find that earns its place.
- *Clarity (2/2):* All illustrations clarify rather than compete. The lottery winner is the strongest — it does the theological work of showing that what we crave can destroy us.
- *Memorability (0/1):* The stuffed animal and the lottery winner will be remembered. But no single image rises to the level of the sermon's dominant metaphor that a listener will carry home as the sermon's fingerprint.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2):* The manuscript is oral. Sentence rhythms are preacher-length. Direct address is present ("Some of you," "What's yours?"). It breathes.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The conviction is real but inconsistent. The Stapelia illustration and the Eve connection land hard. The closing sections drift toward encouragement and lose the edge.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1):* The sermon's climax does not compress into shorter, sharper sentences. The Philippians section is good content but does not read as a sermon's apex moment.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?*
Mostly of myself and my discontentment, with a Christward gesture at the end. The balance tips anthropological.

2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?*
Ideas about Him, with moments of genuine weight (the wrath section, the Romans 8:32 application). Not fully encounter.

3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?*
Convicted — the anatomy of discontentment as sin genuinely presses. But conviction is not fully relieved by gospel, leaving the hearer with diagnosis and encouragement rather than full pastoral medicine.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The five-part anatomy of discontentment** (Part 1) is theologically precise and pastorally searching. Each element ("Discontentment tells God He's made a mistake") is an accurate diagnosis that lands in the conscience without being merely moralistic.

2. **Romans 8:32 as the grounding move** — "He who did not spare his own Son... if He isn't giving it to you right now, He has a reason" — is the sermon's most powerful theological moment. It grounds God's withholding in God's proven generosity, not vague optimism.

3. **Illustration quality** is consistently high. The lottery winner is the sermon's best: it does theology, not just decoration. It shows — concretely and memorably — that getting what you want is not always good for you. The Stapelia illustration is bold and effective.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel section is too brief and too late.** "Thank God for the cross. Because Christ satisfied the wrath of God — He took the full weight of it" is one sentence embedded in Part 2 and never developed. The closing gospel invitation (the final section) does not articulate *how* Christ is the treasure, what He has done, or what response is required. An unbeliever would not know how to be saved. A doubter would not be freshly grounded. The law has time to land; the gospel does not.

2. **Moses' collapse is underdeveloped.** The sermon's title is "The Sin We Don't Call Sin," and Moses — who is presented at the opening as a sympathetic parallel — commits that sin at the text's climax (vv.11-15). But his lament before God is not exegeted. What does Moses' own discontentment reveal about this sin's reach? What is God's response to it? The sermon's most theologically charged material is treated as setup for illustrations rather than as the passage's own answer.

3. **Exhortation lacks the imperative edge.** "Tell yourself the truth. Fix your eyes on the treasure" is counsel, not command. The sermon never summons the congregation: *Repent of your distrust. Confess the specific thing you are coveting. Come back under the sovereignty you have been fleeing.* The movement from application to exhortation is the sermon's structural gap.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how anthropologically oriented the sermon is. The structure — five things discontentment is, how God feels about it, what to do — keeps the listener's focus primarily on their own sin and their own need to fix their thinking. God and Christ appear as solutions to a human problem rather than as the sermon's center of gravity. A listener could emerge with a sharper self-diagnostic and a somewhat clearer sense of Christ's worth — but not with an enlarged vision of God Himself.

Additionally, the Burroughs prescription ("make good interpretations of God's dealings") is a good cure but carries a cognitive risk: it can sound like a self-help exercise that happens to have a theological name. Burroughs himself roots this in the character of God revealed in Christ. That grounding is present here but faint.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The illustration density creates a sense of pastoral warmth and accessibility that may mask the gospel thinness. The sermon feels full because it is full of good material — Burroughs, Tripp, Paul, James, Jesus' parable, the lottery winner. But a full sermon is not necessarily a gospel-saturated sermon. The richness of illustration covers for the thinness of atonement. A listener who processes the sermon's emotional texture may not notice that justification was never clearly stated.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The manna is the passage's most underdeveloped treasure. Israel had daily bread from heaven — literally — and was still discontent. This is the sermon's deepest irony and its most powerful typological opening: the bread that fell from the sky was insufficient to satisfy Israel, pointing forward to the Bread of Life who alone satisfies permanently (John 6:48-51). Jesus himself makes this connection. The sermon has Numbers 11 in hand and never opens the door that leads to John 6. That is not a forced Christ-jump — it is the passage's own trajectory.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Develop the gospel section into a full movement.** After the Philippians 3:8 passage, the sermon needs a paragraph that names what Christ has done — substitutionary atonement, justification, union with Christ — and makes clear that the cure for discontentment is not primarily better thinking but a Person who has bought you. "He is the treasure in the field" is the right image; it needs the cross underneath it.

2. **Exegete Moses' breakdown (vv.11-15) as the sermon's pivot.** Moses commits the sin the sermon is diagnosing. Rather than moving past it quickly, stay there. What does it mean that even Moses — the man of God — was not immune? How did God respond to him? (Graciously — He gave him help.) That is the text's own pastoral answer to the problem.

3. **Add the manna typology.** One paragraph connecting manna to the Bread of Life (John 6) gives the sermon its organic Christological center and avoids the "Jesus jump" problem — because Jesus made this connection himself.

4. **Replace the final encouragement with an imperative exhortation.** "Tell yourself the truth" should become: "Repent of what you have told God about Himself. Name the thing you are coveting. Lay it down. And trust the God who gave His Son." The sermon needs a commanding edge before its gospel invitation.

5. **Address the self-righteous hearer.** Add one paragraph for the person who does not struggle with obvious discontentment but whose contentment is grounded in their circumstances going well — this is a different form of the same sin, and it is the most dangerous. "Some of you are content right now — and it's because things are going your way. Test that. What happens to your trust when they don't?"

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** Exposition scores 18 and Conscience scores 10 — the ratio does not meet trigger criteria. The sermon is not bloodless; it has genuine pastoral heat. The gospel thinness is a weakness, not a marker of orthodoxy without fire.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries real pastoral gravity — the wrath section and the lottery winner illustration feel weighty — but it does not fully arrive at the "eternal significance" the bonus requires; the closing sections drift toward encouragement and lose the eternal register the opening established. **+2/5**

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This is a narrative passage from the Torah, and the rubric fits it well. Narrative preaching can be evaluated on textual fidelity, gospel centrality, and conscience force without strain. The one category that bends slightly is Redemptive-Historical Awareness: narrative passages in the Pentateuch often carry richer typological freight than the rubric's standard questions surface, and this sermon's failure to engage the manna typology is exactly the kind of gap that a rubric oriented toward didactic texts might underweight. The flag is appropriately raised here: the typological opportunity is not a bonus — it is the passage's own Christ-connection, and its absence is a genuine exposition gap.
