## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Warm-Hearted Devotional That Misses the Sermon

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A friendly opening of 1 Samuel 16 that captured the congregation's affection and surfaced several true observations about God's electing grace while drifting from the passage's own argument, leaving the gospel largely unpreached and the conscience unsearched.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: MARGINAL PASS (barely)**
The passage's main point is God's sovereign, grace-driven election of David — the Lord looks on the heart, not the outward appearance. The sermon does arrive at this in verse 7, so the gate technically opens. However, the sermon's actual main idea drifts: it becomes "God can use anyone" (an application) rather than "God sees the heart and chooses according to his own sovereign grace" (the text's own claim). The sermon uses the passage as a springboard for a four-point life-purpose framework borrowed from Andy Stanley, which is not native to the text at all.

**Authority Test: SOFT FAIL**
The preacher does not stand above the text as an editor, but he does quietly leave the text behind. The final third of the sermon is built on Andy Stanley's three-point framework (with a fourth added), none of which arise from 1 Samuel 16. The text's own climax — the anointing of David and the coming of the Spirit — is not the sermon's climax. The preacher submits to what the text says in passing, but the sermon is governed more by a life-application grid than by the passage's argument.

**Verdict: The gate opens narrowly. The sermon is text-adjacent, not text-driven. Proceed to scoring with this noted.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation: TRIGGERED**
The gospel is listed as a placeholder at the end ("Gospel") with no content. What is given prior to that is an implied gospel: "If you've never met Jesus, then hearing and responding to the gospel is your first step." But the atonement is never explained. Christ's substitutionary work is not proclaimed. Justification by faith is absent. A placeholder is not a gospel presentation. The congregation heard about what Christ was descended from; they did not hear why Christ came, what he accomplished, or how a sinner is reconciled to God.

**Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary: TRIGGERED**
The references to Christ are genealogical and peripheral. Christ appears as a notable descendant of David and Bethlehem's greatest citizen, but not as the crucified and risen Savior whose finished work is the ground of salvation. The sermon could be preached in a synagogue — the Christ-connections given (Bethlehem, lineage, David's line) are ones devout Jews would affirm. Nothing in the sermon requires the cross to be true.

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic: TRIGGERED**
The applications are: know God's word, keep God's commands, do evangelism, worship God in spirit and truth. These are not wrong, but they arise from Andy Stanley's framework, not from 1 Samuel 16. They are equally applicable to any text in Scripture. No one was addressed specifically. No conscience was pressed on a particular sin. The grieving widow, the comfortable elder, the young person living in quiet rebellion, the new believer trembling under doubt — none received a word shaped for them by this text.

**Score capped at 69.**

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Holy Spirit observation (verses 13 and Samson comparison)**: The insight that David's anointing was permanent where the Judges' Spirit-anointing was intermittent is a genuinely good exegetical observation that most hearers would not find on their own. This is the sermon's strongest expository moment.

2. **The "smelly hillbilly shepherd" characterization of David**: This is vivid, pastoral, and honest — it makes the radical nature of God's choice concrete and lands with warmth. The congregation will remember it.

3. **The personal illustration about the father and the bar**: A grounded, specific, and moving story that illustrates the Spirit's use of apparent failure. It is the right kind of illustration — personal, local, and theologically pointed toward God's sovereignty.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a placeholder, not a proclamation.** The word "Gospel" appears at the end of the manuscript as a heading with no content beneath it. The atonement is not preached. No one hearing this sermon knows why Christ had to die, what that death accomplished, or what faith in him requires. This is the sermon's mortal wound. The invitation at the close — "if you've never met Christ, today is the day" — is a door with no room behind it.

2. **The Andy Stanley framework colonizes the second half of the sermon.** From "Stanley's 3 purposes" onward, the sermon is no longer preaching 1 Samuel 16. The four-point life-purpose outline (know the Word, keep commands, evangelize, worship) is generically true but arises from no verse in the passage. The text is abandoned. The congregation came to hear 1 Samuel 16; they received a brief devotional on David followed by a topical framework from another preacher.

3. **Verse 7 — the text's theological heart — receives one paragraph and then is left behind.** "The Lord looks on the heart" is the passage's most searching and dangerous claim. It should have been turned on the congregation: What does God see when he looks on your heart? Is there love for him there, or is there the same outward-appearance religion Samuel brought to Jesse's house? This question is never asked.

### 11. Blind Spots

- The preacher likely does not see that the Andy Stanley detour is a structural abandonment of the text. It feels like application and extension; it functions as a departure.
- The preacher likely does not realize the gospel was not actually preached. The warm invitation at the end creates the feeling of gospel proclamation without the substance of it.
- The genealogical connections are interesting and not wrong, but the claim that Bethlehem was "so named because it was prophesied that one day Living Bread would come from heaven" is eisegesis. The name is ancient and descriptive; it was not given in anticipation of John 6. This could mislead a careful hearer.
- The sermon addresses everyone with the same warmth and leaves everyone in the same posture — encouraged and slightly motivated. No one was disturbed. No one who came in self-satisfied should have left that way.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal illustrations (Iron Man, the father at the bar, Dottie's leg) are warm and memorable — they create the feeling of pastoral depth and real connection. But illustrations are the sermon's strongest element, which means the sermon's energy flows toward the most decorative category and away from its load-bearing ones. A congregation that leaves saying "that was encouraging" and "I loved that story about his dad" has been entertained pastorally. They may not have been changed.

The invitation at the end ("if you've never come to a point where you've met Christ, today is the day") creates the feeling of gospel proclamation. But the feeling and the thing are different. No one was told what Christ did, what his death accomplished, what faith means, or what they are being saved from. The invitation is sincere; the gospel it implies was never delivered.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Verse 7 is a two-edged sword the sermon never unsheathed.** "The Lord looks on the heart" is not only an encouragement about who God chooses — it is a terrifying diagnostic about what God sees. Samuel looked at Eliab and thought: surely this is God's anointed. He was wrong. How many people in the congregation sit under the same delusion — outwardly religious, inwardly distant, confident in their standing because of family, church attendance, or moral respectability? The text offers a direct confrontation with self-righteous religion. The sermon walks past it entirely.

Additionally, the permanent nature of David's Spirit-anointing (verse 13) is introduced and then dropped. The preacher notes the contrast with Samson but never draws the line forward to the New Covenant promise of the Spirit (Ezekiel 36:27, John 14:16–17, Pentecost). The opportunity to show how David's anointing was a shadow of what every believer now possesses — the permanent indwelling Spirit — was present and unused.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach the gospel explicitly.** Before the invitation, the sermon needs a clear, content-laden gospel presentation: humanity's sin and its deserved consequence, Christ's substitutionary death bearing that consequence, his resurrection as the Father's vindication, and the call to repentant faith. This does not need to be long. It needs to be present.

2. **Remove the Andy Stanley framework entirely.** It is not native to this text. Replace it with three applicatory questions arising directly from 1 Samuel 16: (a) Are you judging yourself or others by outward appearance? (b) What does God see when he looks on your heart — is there love for him, or performance? (c) Have you been discounted by others, or discounted yourself, in ways that have kept you from serving God? These arise from the passage. Stanley's framework does not.

3. **Turn verse 7 on the congregation as a diagnostic, not merely a comfort.** The text says God looks on the heart. Ask: What does he find there? Distinguish: the self-confident religionist (he needs to be alarmed), the sincere doubter (he needs to be encouraged), the new believer (he needs to be assured). The text already contains the material for this differentiation.

4. **Tighten the introduction.** The genealogical section (Rahab, Ruth, Boaz, Moabites, Lot's daughters) is interesting but long relative to the textual work that follows. Cut it by half and spend that time on the passage's structure.

5. **Give the Spirit-anointing theme a New Covenant payoff.** The contrast between Samson and David is good. Extend it: David's permanent anointing points forward to what every believer now has by right of the New Covenant. This is a gospel application native to the text.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

**1 Samuel 16 is narrative, and the rubric bends slightly here.** The passage is not an apostolic letter or prophetic oracle; it is a historical narrative in which meaning is conveyed through plot, characterization, and divine commentary (verse 7). The rubric's expectation of verse-by-verse exegetical progression applies differently here — the preacher is right to draw theological themes from the narrative rather than parsing clauses. However, this does not excuse the departure from the passage's own argument. Narrative sermons still have a scopus — a main point — and the text's main point (the Lord's sovereign, heart-examining election) must govern the whole. The rubric bends; it does not break.
