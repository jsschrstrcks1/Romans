# Judges 6:36–40
## *Lord, Just Once More*

**Text:** Judges 6:36–40

> "Then Gideon said to God, 'If you will save Israel by my hand, as you have said, behold, I am laying a fleece of wool on the threshing floor. If there is dew on the fleece alone, and it is dry on all the ground, then I shall know that you will save Israel by my hand, as you have said.' And it was so. When he rose early next morning and squeezed the fleece, he wrung enough dew from the fleece to fill a bowl with water. Then Gideon said to God, 'Let not your anger burn against me; let me speak just once more. Please let me test just once more with the fleece. Please let it be dry on the fleece only, and on all the ground let there be dew.' And God did so that night; and it was dry on the fleece only, and on all the ground there was dew."

---

## Introduction

We're in Judges 6 tonight, starting at verse 36. If you brought your Bible, I hope it's already open — if not, follow along.

Let me catch you up from last week. Gideon was standing in the middle of an angry mob. He had torn down the altar to Ba'al in his father's field — a massive structure — knocked down the Asherah pole beside it, and burned two bulls on the rubble. The people of the town and the surrounding area came looking for his head.

His father Joash stepped out and cut the crowd off cold. *If Ba'al is a god, let Ba'al defend himself.*

The crowd dispersed.

But war is coming. A confederation of kingdoms — Midianites, Amalekites, and the peoples of the East — has crossed the Jordan and is camped in the valley of Jezreel. They are Ba'al worshipers, all of them, and they are furious about what happened to that altar.

And God has already told Gideon: *Go in this your strength. I will be with you.*

Not suggested. Not implied. Directly commissioned. By an angel who appeared to this man personally and sat with him at his threshing floor.

And Gideon puts out a fleece.

---

## The Anatomy of a Fleece

First thing I want you to see: a fleece demonstrates belief. You don't lay wool on a threshing floor and ask God to act on it if you don't believe God exists. There's faith underneath this — even if it's not strong faith. Most of us have been here. I've been here. You believe enough to ask. You just don't quite believe enough to move without a sign.

God can honor these. He does, situationally. He honored this one.

But notice what's actually happening. Gideon has already received his orders. God didn't come to him with a question — he came with a command. And Gideon is saying, essentially: *I believe you could do this. But before I'm willing to obey you, you're going to have to prove it to me.*

That is not faith. That is a negotiation.

And yet — God stoops to it. Twice.

First night: wet fleece, dry ground. He wrung the wool out in the morning and filled a bowl with water. Everything around it was bone dry.

Second night: Gideon goes back. *Please don't be angry with me. Just once more.* Dry fleece, soaked ground. God did it again.

God didn't rebuke him. He didn't lecture him. He just honored the request, both times. I find that extraordinary. It tells you something about the God we serve. He is not fragile. He is not offended by the weakness of a man who believes *in* him but doesn't quite believe *him* yet. He stooped to a wet blanket on a threshing floor — twice — because the man he called was still becoming the man he called him to be.

---

## Why Gideon Asked

Two motivations. Both are in the text.

**Fear.** Remember — Gideon tore down those altars at night. Not in the afternoon. Not in front of witnesses. At night, when no one could see him, because he was afraid of his father and afraid of the townspeople. And that was a small act compared to what he's being asked to do now.

The force assembling against Israel was enormous. We learn from Judges 8:10, after the battle, that 120,000 men had fallen and 15,000 were still running — meaning Gideon was going up against roughly 135,000 men. This wasn't one kingdom. It was a coalition. Midianites, Amalekites, peoples of the East — all of them together, pointed at Israel. Judges 7 tells us their camels were without number, like sand on the seashore. They filled the valley like locusts.

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery cited 85,000 cavalry in addition to the 135,000 infantry. This figure does not appear in the biblical text of Judges 6–8. The Bible describes the enemy's camels as beyond counting but gives no cavalry count. Please verify the source before preaching it, or drop it. The 135,000 total is confirmed from Judges 8:10.*]

It is a reasonable thing to be afraid of. Even a brave man measures what he's walking into.

**Peace.** Gideon had named his altar *Jehovah-shalom* — "The Lord sends peace." That name tells you who this man was before the battle and who he still wanted to be. He wasn't looking for a fight. He knew what war cost — to the enemy, to his own men, to the families those men wouldn't come home to.

Charles Spurgeon observed that Gideon had lived largely in peace and would have preferred to continue that way. [*Editorial note: Attributed to Spurgeon in the oral delivery. Please verify the specific source — sermon title or volume in the Metropolitan Tabernacle Pulpit series — before preaching it as a Spurgeon quote. If it can't be confirmed, the observation stands without the attribution.*]

God called him a mighty man of valor. But the man himself wanted quiet fields and a quiet family. Those two things don't contradict each other. Sometimes the men who hate war most are the most careful with the lives of the men they lead into it.

---

## God Uses Broken People

Here's what all of this tells us, and it's not a small thing.

God didn't call a man who had it together. He called a man who was afraid. Who demanded proof before he'd obey. Who asked God twice to act before he was willing to move.

The old saying holds: God doesn't call the qualified. He qualifies the called.

David — a man after God's own heart — had a man killed to cover what he had done with that man's wife. [*Editorial note: The oral delivery uses the word "rape" to describe David's relationship with Bathsheba. This is a serious interpretive claim. The Hebrew text of 2 Samuel 11 describes David sending for Bathsheba and taking her; Nathan's parable frames it as the rich man seizing the poor man's lamb by force. Given the extreme power differential — a king summoning a subject — many scholars, including conservative ones, argue the encounter cannot have been genuinely consensual. This is a defensible reading, but it is interpretive. If you preach it this way, do so intentionally and be ready to explain it. If not, "had a man killed to cover what he had done with the man's wife" carries the moral weight without the contested claim.*]

Paul, who wrote roughly half the New Testament [*Editorial note: The oral delivery says "two-thirds." Paul authored 13 letters, 14 if Hebrews is included. The New Testament contains 27 books. 13 of 27 is approximately 48 percent. "Roughly half" is accurate; "two-thirds" is not.*] — stood in a place of honor while men and women were stoned to death for their faith in Jesus Christ. He approved. He considered it good work.

God used him to write the letters the church has been reading for two thousand years.

If he can use people like that, he can use you. That is not a comfort — it is a declaration. Apart from Christ, we are incapable of doing good. But God looks at the mess and says: *I can use that one.* What a mercy — to be allowed to participate in service to the living God.

---

## The Weight of Command

What Gideon couldn't escape was the weight of the lives that were going to depend on his decision.

I read a book about George W. Bush a while back — his account of the days after September 11th. He was being moved from place to place, everyone competing for his attention. People wanted a statement, a response, some sign that someone was in charge. He was slow to give one, and he took criticism for it.

What the book said was that he had spent that time in prayer. In his flesh he wanted to go to war — that was his immediate instinct. But he also knew that men were going to die for whatever decision he made. He wasn't willing to make it without first bringing it to God. He prayed for guidance. He prayed that if they went to war, those men's blood wouldn't be spent wastefully. He prayed for a quick conclusion and for the possibility of peace on the other side.

Like him or hate him — that was about the most useful thing he could have been doing in those hours.

John Bunyan put it this way: *"You can do more than pray after you have prayed, but you cannot do more than pray until you have prayed. Pray often, for prayer is a shield to the soul, a sacrifice to God, and a scourge to Satan."*

Do nothing until you've prayed. After you've prayed, you have options.

Both Gideon and Bush felt the weight of the lives that would be spent on their behalf. Neither entered it casually.

---

## A Shadow of Gethsemane

As I've been sitting in this passage this week, I keep seeing another garden.

Turn with me to Luke 22:41.

> "And he withdrew from them about a stone's throw, and knelt down and prayed, saying, 'Father, if you are willing, remove this cup from me. Nevertheless, not my will, but yours, be done.' And there appeared to him an angel from heaven, strengthening him. And being in agony he prayed more earnestly; and his sweat became like great drops of blood falling down to the ground."

Gideon is on the eve of a battle for his people's freedom from an enemy that has crushed them. Christ is on the eve of a battle for ours — against sin, death, hell, and Satan. Both knew what was coming. Gideon knew the cost of war. Christ knew what the cross was going to be.

He would be scourged. He would carry his own cross through the city, mocked by the people he had come to save. Soldiers would gamble for his clothing at the foot of the cross. And then they would nail him.

We say *hand* and picture the palm. But the biblical use of that word, in that era, included the wrist and the lower forearm. The nails went there — through the bones of the wrist — because the forearm could bear the weight of a man. Through the palm alone, the hand tears. [*Editorial note: This is a commonly cited claim in evangelical teaching on crucifixion, associated with the research of Dr. Pierre Barbet and others. It is widely taught but not universally settled by archaeologists and anatomists. Consider qualifying it: "medical researchers who have studied Roman crucifixion believe the nails went through the wrist rather than the palm."*]

He went through all of it willingly. He asked the Father if there was another way. When the answer was no — he went.

Now watch where the shadow of Gideon holds, and where it breaks.

Gideon named his altar *Jehovah-shalom*. The Lord sends peace. He named it before the battle — before anything had been decided, before a soldier had moved. As if the peace was already on its way. Jesus is the peace that altar was pointing toward. He himself is our peace — Ephesians 2 — having broken down the dividing wall through the blood of his cross.

Gideon was threshing wheat when the angel found him. Christ will separate wheat from chaff finally and completely at the last judgment.

Gideon's ministry began in secret — hidden, at night, with only a handful of servants watching — and became very public before it was over. Christ's ministry had the same movement: thirty years of silence in Nazareth, then water into wine at Cana, and then not quietly at all.

But here is where the shadow breaks.

Gideon was protected. Jesus was handed over. Gideon's three hundred went home. Jesus went to the grave. Gideon was betrayed by no one. Jesus was betrayed by a friend.

And both of them won.

Gideon delivered his people from the Midianites. Jesus delivered his from sin and death. Gideon saved them for a generation. Jesus saved his for eternity.

---

## The Gospel

That's why he went through it. Not as an example. Not as a teacher. As a substitute — taking what we had coming so that we could receive what he had earned.

If you've never trusted him — tonight is the night. He went through all of it because he wanted a relationship with you. His Father wanted one with you.

*(Present the gospel.)*

---

## A Word to Believers

If he was willing to die for you — is it too much to ask to open your Bible once in a while?

I'm noticing fewer of you bringing your Bibles to church. If you have one, bring it next week. This Word cost something. Treat it like it did.

---

*Keep Nicole in your prayers this week. The funeral went well. She's grateful for every meal.*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastorally Warm Narrative Sermon That Reaches Christ Organically but Leaves the Congregation Uninstructed and Unexhorted**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A faithful opening of Judges 6:36–40 that moved with genuine pastoral warmth and reached Christ through credible typological movement, while stopping short of searching the conscience, differentiating hearers, or summoning any specific response.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — that God stoops to use weak, fearful, doubting people — arises demonstrably from the passage's own argument. Gideon's repeated request for confirmation, and God's patient double-honoring of it, grounds the main claim.

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text rather than editing it. He does not soften the embarrassing aspects of Gideon's behavior — calling it a negotiation, not faith — and allows the OT narrative to carry full weight without domesticating it.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 5 is triggered: No actual exhortation.**

The sermon concludes with two gestures: a placeholder gospel presentation ("*Present the gospel.*" — never written out) and a brief rebuke about Bible-bringing. Neither constitutes actual exhortation. The congregation is informed, warmed, and told a truth. They are never summoned. There is no "therefore" moment. No one is told what to now believe, repent of, obey, or pursue in specific terms.

**Score is capped at 69.**

Additionally, Flag 1 is borderline triggered. The gospel section consists of: two sentences of kerygmatic assertion and a stage direction in italics. There is no clear articulation of justification by faith, no named call to repentance, no explanation of what trusting Christ means or requires. An unbeliever in the room would not know how to respond. The gospel was gestured toward, not proclaimed.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 18/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (7/10):* The sermon remains close to the text. The fear/peace duality is drawn directly from the narrative details (midnight altar-tearing, Jehovah-shalom). Good. However, a long stretch in the middle — the Bush illustration, the Bunyan quote — passes without any return to the passage. The sermon launches and occasionally doesn't come back.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5):* The sermon situates the passage within the Gideon narrative and gestures toward the larger Judges pattern. The fallen condition — doubt masquerading as prudence, faith that believes *in* God but not *him* — is named clearly. What's missing is deeper awareness of how Israel's covenantal failure (they have been given over because of idolatry) contextualizes Gideon's weakness. The FCF is implicit but not named with full precision.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5):* The passage's mechanics are handled competently and accessibly. The "wet fleece/dry ground, then dry fleece/wet ground" structure is explained clearly. No Greek or Hebrew deployed (not a failure — this is narrative, and the preacher doesn't force it).
- *Logical Faithfulness (4/5):* No eisegesis detected. The comparisons to David and Paul are warranted — they are not proof-texted but used illustratively in service of the main claim.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (5/7):* The typological movement to Christ is one of the sermon's genuine strengths. The Gethsemane parallel is not a "Jesus jump" — it is earned through specific structural comparisons (secret-to-public ministry, pre-battle prayer, the moment of submission). The shadow/break language is theologically careful: Gideon was protected, Jesus was handed over. This is credible covenantal typology.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* The sermon places the text within the redemptive arc without forcing it. The organic connection to Ephesians 2 ("He himself is our peace") through the Jehovah-shalom altar name is genuinely good exposition.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4):* The substitution language ("taking what we had coming so we could receive what he had earned") is sound. However, justification is named by concept but not explained with precision. "Apart from Christ, we are incapable of doing good" is a correct assertion but appears in a secondary position and is not developed.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4):* No contradictions of confessional theology detected. The sermon does not engage soteriological themes deeply enough to test for precision at points of dispute, but it doesn't contradict them either.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7/15**

This is where the sermon most clearly fails.

- *Clarity of the Gospel (1/5):* The gospel section is two sentences and a placeholder. Justification by faith is not articulated. What it means to "trust him" is not explained. An unbeliever is not equipped to respond.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5):* The typological movement earns this. Christ is not decorative — the entire shadow/break structure makes him necessary to the sermon's argument.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5):* The cross is described (scourging, nails, mocking) with some vividness but without theological weight. The crucifixion details land more as emotional narrative than as atonement theology. The sermon gestures at penal substitution but doesn't press it.

*Synagogue Test:* A devout Jew or Muslim could sit through this sermon and find most of it agreeable. The typological section would be interesting to them. The explicit gospel section is so compressed as to present no real offense. This is a meaningful failure.

*Law/Gospel Dialectic:* The law is never used as hammer. There is conviction of a general kind (we are fearful, doubtful, broken) but no specific exposure of sin, no stripping of self-trust. The gospel is offered before the law has done its work.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15**

- *Heart Penetration (3/5):* The sermon has genuine pastoral warmth and touches real human fear — the dread of leading others, the weight of command. The Gideon/Bush comparison lands emotionally. But "touching" fear is not the same as searching the conscience. The sermon moves the heart without cornering it.
- *Specificity (2/5):* The Bush illustration is specific. Everything else is general. "God can use you" applied to the full congregation is the broadest possible application of a truth that should have differentiated. Who in this room is a Gideon? Who is delaying obedience because they want a sign? Who has already received their commission but is still asking for a fleece?
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon addresses one category from Perkins' taxonomy — the believer who is weak and needs encouragement. It does not speak to the unbeliever (except through the unfinished gospel section), the backslider, the knowledgeable-but-unmoved, or the hardened. The congregation is treated as a homogenous group of discouraged but sincere saints.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 2/8**

- *Call to Response (1/4):* The Bible-bringing rebuke is the closest thing to a call to response. It is minor, slightly prosecutorial in tone, and unconnected to the sermon's main argument. The unbeliever is invited to trust Christ ("tonight is the night") — but without any indication of what that means or requires.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (1/4):* The sermon does not equip. It encourages. There is a difference. A listener leaves knowing that God uses broken people — a true comfort — but not knowing what to now do with that comfort.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 7/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* The progression from anatomy of a fleece → why Gideon asked → God uses broken people → the shadow of Gethsemane → gospel is traceable and largely clear.
- *Progression (3/3):* The sermon does build. The typological move to Gethsemane is the climax, and the sermon rightly places it near the end. There is genuine cumulative momentum.
- *Unity (1/2):* The Bush illustration, while emotionally connecting, competes with the dominant idea. The sermon has one good idea (God stoops to use doubting, fearful people) and one interesting parallel (Gideon as shadow of Christ) — but these are developed in a somewhat additive rather than integrated way. The Word to Believers at the end ("bring your Bible next week") is a non sequitur and fractures the sermon's ending.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

- *Relevance (2/2):* The Bush illustration is drawn from civic and political life — accessible, concrete, non-academic. The Gethsemane parallel is itself a kind of extended illustration that illuminates by contrast.
- *Clarity (1/2):* The Bush illustration is effective but extended. It nearly overshadows the Gideon material it's meant to illuminate. The Bunyan quote is good but lands in isolation — not organically connected to the surrounding argument.
- *Memorability (1/1):* "Wet fleece, dry ground. Dry fleece, wet ground. He stooped to it twice." That image will be carried home.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2):* The manuscript is clearly oral. Direct address is present. Sentence lengths vary. The casual connective tissue ("Like him or hate him —") is authentic and preacherly.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The conviction is present in tone but not in density of Scripture-grounded assertion. Long stretches go without returning to the text. The editorial notes (accurate and useful) actually interrupt the sermonic force when encountered as a reader — though they'd be invisible in delivery.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1):* The sermon does not have a climactic compression point. It has a strong section (the shadow/break typology) but does not build sentence-length and force to that moment. It arrives there conversationally rather than with increasing heat.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Both. The typological section directs toward Christ effectively. The "God uses broken people" theme, applied without differentiation, risks leaving the hearer thinking primarily about their own potential. The balance is imperfect.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas about Him, largely. The Gethsemane section comes closest to an encounter — the detail about Jesus asking if there was another way and then going when the answer was no carries genuine weight. But the sermon as a whole is explanatory rather than worshipful.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Comforted and informed. Not convicted. The law does not land before the comfort arrives. The self-righteous, the hardened, and the backslider are not addressed. The sermon consoles the discouraged without first exposing what made them discouraged.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The typological movement to Gethsemane is textually earned and theologically sound.** The shadow/break structure — naming specifically where the parallel holds and where it breaks — avoids the "Jesus jump" failure and produces genuine redemptive-historical illumination. The detail that Gideon named his altar before the battle ("as if the peace was already on its way") and connecting it to Ephesians 2 is a moment of real expository creativity.

2. **The portrait of Gideon's dual motivation (fear and peace) is pastorally astute.** Drawing both motivations directly from textual detail (midnight altar-tearing, the name Jehovah-shalom) demonstrates exegetical care and pastoral empathy. This is not eisegesis — it is listening to what the narrative is showing.

3. **The editorial notes embedded in the manuscript are a model of preacherly integrity.** The flags on unverified statistics, the Spurgeon attribution, and the crucifixion nail claim reflect the ethos of careful-not-clever. A preacher who catches his own errors in print is a preacher who can be trusted in the pulpit.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel section is a stage direction, not a proclamation.** The line "*(Present the gospel.)*" is the most damaging element in the manuscript. It reveals that the preacher has not yet decided what the gospel looks like in this specific sermon. Justification by faith is mentioned by concept but not articulated. An unbeliever who stayed for that section would have been moved but not equipped. The Synagogue Test is not passed.

2. **No hearer is ever cornered.** The application of "God uses broken people" floats at maximum altitude — true for everyone, pressing on no one. There is no moment where the person delaying obedience because they are waiting for a sign is directly addressed. There is no word for the person who has already received their commission and is negotiating with God rather than obeying. Perkins' full taxonomy is ignored. The sermon preaches to "the congregation" as a mass, not to the distinct consciences within it.

3. **The conclusion is structurally broken.** The sermon's natural climax is the shadow/break typology followed by the gospel. Instead, the conclusion fractures into: two-sentence gospel gesture → stage direction → Bible-bringing rebuke → prayer request for Nicole. The Nicole pastoral note is appropriate for a bulletin or announcement — not a sermon ending. The congregation's last impression is a minor rebuke about Bible logistics rather than the gravity of what Christ did on the eve of his own battle.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that comfort without conviction is not the full gospel ministry. The warmth of the sermon — genuinely present, genuinely pastoral — may function as a substitute for the searching work the text actually demands. Gideon's behavior is labeled "not faith — that is a negotiation." That is a strong diagnostic claim about Gideon. But the preacher does not then turn it on the congregation: *Who here is also in a negotiation with God right now?* The text has a harder edge than the sermon allows it to reach.

There is also a possible blind spot around the Christological move. The typological section is the preacher's evident intellectual strength — and it draws sermonic attention toward that strength at the expense of applicatory weight. The sermon is most alive when it is tracing the shadow. It is least alive when it should be pressing the conscience. These are likely related.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The typological section reads as the sermon's crowning achievement — and in many respects it is. But it also functions as a form of evasion. The preacher has invested his most careful work in connecting Gideon to Christ rather than in connecting the text to the hearer. The intellectual labor of the shadow/break structure creates the feeling of depth and completeness. It does not replace the applicatory work that was never done. A listener could walk away thinking the sermon was profound — and leave with no specific obligation, no named sin, no commanded response.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The text's own pivot point is underpreached. When Gideon says "let not your anger burn against me; let me speak just once more" — that moment of fear before an already-patient God is a compressed portrait of the entire problem of unbelief. A man who has received direct divine commission, watched God honor his first test, and still cannot trust — that is a window into every believer's hesitation at the edge of obedience. The sermon names it ("a negotiation") but moves past it quickly. That verse deserved a full section on the anatomy of delayed obedience — and how Christ's "nevertheless, not my will" is the answer to every Gideon-moment in the congregation's life.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific)

1. **Write the gospel section.** Remove the stage direction entirely. In its place, write 150–200 words that articulate clearly: (a) what sin is and why it requires a substitute, (b) what Christ did specifically — penal substitution, not just sacrifice — and (c) what it means to trust him and what response that requires tonight. The Gethsemane section has already done the emotional preparation; the gospel section must now land the theological payload.

2. **Add differentiated direct address after "God Uses Broken People."** After the general assertion that God uses broken people, insert a paragraph that addresses at least three distinct hearers: the person delaying obedience while waiting for another sign ("You've already heard from God. The fleece has been answered. What are you still waiting for?"); the person who has given up because of past failure ("God used Paul, who held the coats"); and the unbeliever who came tonight not sure why ("He went through all of it because he wanted you — and he doesn't require you to have yourself together first").

3. **Press "that is a negotiation" onto the congregation.** The sermon correctly identifies Gideon's fleece as a negotiation rather than faith. This diagnosis must be turned on the hearers with a direct question: *What fleece are you laying out for God right now? What are you asking him to prove before you'll obey what he's already told you?* Name plausible examples: the marriage decision, the financial step, the confrontation that needs to happen, the repentance that has been postponed.

4. **Cut or relocate the Nicole note.** The pastoral care note at the end undermines the sermon's conclusion. Move it to before the sermon begins, or to a bulletin insert. Let the sermon end on the weight of what Christ did — or on a clear exhortation arising from it.

5. **Tighten the Bush illustration.** It is effective but too long. Two paragraphs on a president dilute the Gideon focus at a critical juncture. Reduce to three sentences: the decision, the prayer, the point. Then return immediately to Gideon.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon is not cold or bloodless — it carries genuine pastoral warmth. The failure is applicatory thinness, not doctrinal iciness. The fire is present. It needs direction.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The typological section — where Gideon's shadow breaks against the cross — carries genuine eternal gravity, but the sermon does not sustain that weight through to its conclusion, leaving the congregation moved but not standing under what the text and its fulfillment actually demand of them.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This rubric is applied to an Old Testament narrative passage with a sermon that operates primarily in narrative-typological mode. The rubric bends slightly here: narrative passages do not have propositional structures that yield neat theses, and the "FCF" (Fallen Condition Focus) must be inferred from character action rather than explicit didactic address. This evaluation treats that appropriately. The typological evaluation criteria (Christ-centeredness, redemptive-historical awareness) are well-suited to this material and were the most productive sections of the analysis. The exhortation and conscience categories apply with full force — narrative sermons still demand application — and the failures identified there are genuine, not artifacts of rubric misfit.
