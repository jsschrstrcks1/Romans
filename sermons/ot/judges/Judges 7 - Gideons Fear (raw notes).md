## Gideon's Fear

We talked last week about how Gideon was fearful even in light of the fact God sent an angel to deal with him Directly. He tossed out the fleeces, he was assured time and again that God would be with him, and yet he's still a little afraid. But he sends his people out into the lands around him, and gathers up all those who are willing to fight for their freedom. All in all 32000 show up. They are outnumbered 5:1. I can see it now, Gideon gets the final tallies in for all the troops, totals them all, and starts to think, man were doomed. That's when God appeared to Gideon and says hey Gideon? You have too many troops. Go get rid of some – send home any who are afraid. This was part of the Mosaic Law, it is believed that fear is contagious, and so they sent hom all who were afraid, and Gideon was left with 10,000 troops. I can just picture the color draining out of Gideons face. I was outnumbered 5:1, and now its 15:1. When God appeared to him again and said hey Gideon? 10000 troops is way too many. I need you to get rid of some more troops. So when they go to drink, separate those who drink from their hand, with those who drink on all fours like a dog. Send those home who drink on all fours, and retain only those who drink lap from their hand. So Gideon did so and was left with 300 men. A lot of people speculate as to why God had him separate the hand drinkers from the dog lappers. Some said that it was a matter of battle field awareness. That those who drank from their hands were doing so because they wanted to keep an eye on their enemy. But frankly I don't know if I buy that. From what we read in scripture tonight we know that they win with only 300 soldiers who aren't even called upon to wield a sword. The midianites killed the midianites while Gideon's soldiers stand by and watch. So why would battlefield awareness have played a role?

I think that in the end it came down to submission to God, to dependence upon God. To faith in God to deliver them. If your enemy is close enough to watch – the only place you'll find comfort enough to be able to turn your eyes from the enemy is in God. He's the one that gives you peace in the midst of your enemy. He's the one who can let you rest at the doorstep of your enemy. In him is the only place we today can find true and lasting peace as well. Facing turmoil? Take your eyes of the storm, and look at Jesus. Troubles at home? Look to Jesus. Troubles at work? Look to Jesus. Health troubles? Look to Jesus.

Helen Keller once said the world is moved along, not only by the mighty shoves of its heroes, but also by the aggregate of tiny pushes of each honest worker.

Its almost like God said Gideon, all I need are 300 little pushes and we will move the midianite world. I'm sure Gideon was glad when God didn't send any of the 300 home, and on the eve of battle, I can imagine the speech Gideon would have made trying to rally the troops. You might remember the general that said "I know we are surrounded, that's good, don't let any of them get away". Its kind of like that for Gideon and his forces at this point, and Gideon faithful and obedient man though he was still experiencing a little fear.

Something important to note here is that Gideon, inspite of being afraid didn't let fear stop him. He was in a hard place, but he did what God wanted him to do regardless of the odds or difficulty. In the end he was outnumbered 450:1. 300 guys vs 135000 soldier's, and that isn't counting cavalry. The cavalry may have had horses, or camels or more likely a combination of regiments of both. He's a great example of how to handle the difficult things in life that God asks you to do. When God tells him to take 300 men and march them around a camp of potentially 20000 people, Gideon does it. He was a little afraid, he wanted to make sure God was with him, but in the end and against all odds he was obedient to God. Even when it didn't make sense. Even when it seemed impossible. Even when it seemed too hard Gideon kept putting one foot in front of the other. A pastor we all know and love likes to say inch by inch lifes a cinch, yard by yard life is hard. Sometimes that means being obedient moment by moment. I'm sure Gideon tried to project confidence, but that on the inside he was still a bit nervous. Who goes to battle without a sword after all? But God was good, God honors his promises, and God came through in the end.

## Gideon's Foes

Still a little afraid, God offers Gideon one more proof that God is with him. God says if you're still afraid run down into the midianite camp with your body guard and there you will hear more proof.

SO Gideon does, and the midianites were talking about a dream that they had. (13-14). The dream was that a cake of barley came down and struck the tent of the midianites and not only was the tent struck but it was knocked upside down completely upset. That barley was used is I think a reference to Gideon. Gideon said all along that he was the least of his people, the weakest of them all, the least likely to win. Now he's commanding an army of only 300, and god uses barley, which according to spurgeon at the time was the least of all the grains. Yeah it fills the void, yeah it sorta tastes like real crab, but it's the least of all the crab meats. Its as though God is nodding at Gideon's statement earlier that he's the weakest. But God can even use the weakest to accomplish great things.

## God's Faithfulness

So we looked at Gideons fears, now lets look at Gideons Foe, and Gods faithfulness.
At this point Gideon divides his forces into three groups of 100. Each group gathers around the enemy and prepares for the fight. In one hand they had a torch covered in a clay jar, in the other a trumpet. You'll notice Gideons forces went to war without a weapon. Despite Gideons fears, I think that is itself an act of great faith. Would you go to the Daytona 500 to race, and leave behind your Racecar? Certainly not. Would you go to milk your cow without something to put the milk in? certainly not. Unless God tells you to. God has a penchant for using musical instruments in war fare. Remember Jericho? Marching and marching and marching, and then shouting and playing their instrument, the entire city fell except for Rahabs house. Here God makes use of instruments again. These 300 men gathered up around the midianites, and smashed their jars, and waved their torches. The smashing of the jars, would have startled the sleeping camels and war animals. Considering they were all in tents, its likely that the Camals did most of the work of flattening and killing many soldiers. Camels aren't particularly bright, but they are big, and fast. A camel can run about 40 mph. Vs 12 says the camals were without number, like the sand on the seashore. We know there are at least 135,000 men, it's a war camp. There would have been women serving to make food, to maintain their clothing, there would have been armor smiths, vast herds of meat animal, people to take care of the herds. There would have been salesman, and basically a war camp that size would have been a portable city. In addition to the hundred thousand soldiers, there would have probably been twice that number in support industries, artisans, and so forth. Some may have had spearwives, and so forth. 135,000 soldiers was probably more like 400,000 people in total. It wouldn't be un-reasonable to expect a camal per person. Camals were a dual purpose animal. They provided milk, which would be both drank, and used in cooking. They provided meat as well, and were used for pack animals, and to ride. Wealthy families would have had at least two camals per person for travel, and another camal per person for luggage. Additionally they would have likely had a herd of meat camels as well. In a camp of 400,000 people it wouldn't have been outrageous to have a half million camels. One thing I've learned this week, is that war camels are kind of tightly wound. They tend to choose the most alert, and most athletic camels. In horse terms they would have chosen "hot" or "warm blooded" horses. Likewise they wanted camals with those same qualities. Horse people I want you to think about something. Picture a half million high strung sleeping horses. Then startle them all awake with shouting, horse eating trumpet music and fire. In short there would have been a half million stampeding 2000 lb camels tearing through the camp in the dark. Imagine being woken up at 3am by a dozen camels tearing through your tent. You probably wouldn't get out of that alive. Certainly not unharmed. This was exactly what the soldiers did. They shattered the clay jars, which would have startled the camals, which would have trampled their soldiers, and stampeded their other food animals. J. Vernon McGee said the torches would have provided just enough light for the midianites to attack themselves. Those that got out of their tents un-harmed were so startled they fought each other and the end result was the utter defeat of the enemy army. God caused the midianites complete defeat.

God is just as faithful to us today. You guys have watched Kristina and Jeremy's transformation. We've rejoiced along side them as they have fought for sobriety for going on two years now. 21 months. God has delivered many of us as well. We all have stories and a past. Those of you – like me – who have been Christians since you were children have just as powerful of a testimony because Praise God that he kept us from becoming addicted to alcohol, or from getting messed up in drugs, or gangs, or a hundred other things that we could have gotten twisted up in. God's power to preserve is just as strong as his ability to rescues us from our own poor decisions. Which is just as strong as his ability to save us from the eternity in Hell that we so richly deserve.

I heard a preacher this week say "You can never be to small for God to use, just too big". Gideon knew he was the barley of soldiers. The artificial crab meat of fighters. But God chose to use him any way, because Gideon was willing to submit to God in the teeth of his fears.

What that looks like today starts in Romans 3:23. (gospel)

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Warm Pastoral Heart, Thin Exegetical Spine — A Story Well Told but Insufficiently Preached**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A genuinely affectionate and accessible retelling of Judges 7 that communicates God's faithfulness through Gideon's fear, but remains primarily a narrative survey with an underdeveloped gospel center, absent exhortation structure, and no serious conscience work.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS (barely)**
The sermon's animating thesis — that God is faithful and uses the weak who submit to Him despite fear — does arise from the passage's own argument. Judges 7 is about divine sovereignty displayed through radical reduction, and the sermon orbits that. The main point is not foreign to the text.

**Authority Test: PASS (with concerns)**
The preacher does not obviously edit or soften the text. He works through it with genuine enthusiasm. However, he moves quickly past the theological weight of the reduction (vv. 2–3, "lest Israel boast") without dwelling on it — which is the text's own interpretive key. The passage insists God stripped the army *specifically so Israel could not claim the victory*. That point is gestured at but not driven home.

**Berean Gate: PASS — with noted drift from the text's own emphasis.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG 1 — APPLICATION IS ENTIRELY GENERIC (Fatal Flaw #4)**
The application sequence — "Facing turmoil? Look to Jesus. Troubles at home? Look to Jesus. Troubles at work? Look to Jesus. Health troubles? Look to Jesus." — is the definition of generic application. No conscience is cornered, no specific sin or struggle is named, no hearer is addressed distinctly.

**FLAG 2 — GOSPEL PRESENTATION IS A PLACEHOLDER (Fatal Flaw #1)**
The sermon concludes with: "What that looks like today starts in Romans 3:23. (gospel)." This is a note to self, not a gospel proclamation. The finished work of Christ is not presented — it is announced as something that will happen. This is a raw notes document, so some charity is appropriate, but as submitted there is no actual gospel presentation, only a pointer to where one should go.

**Score is capped at 69.**

Large sections (the reduction sequence, the dream interpretation) are narrated but not exegeted.
- *Contextual Awareness (5 pts): 3/5* — There is awareness of the Gideon series and the larger Mosaic law background on fear-exempt soldiers (Deut. 20:8). But the book of Judges' larger theological argument (the cycle of apostasy, divine discipline, crying out, and deliverance) is absent. Where does this text sit in the Judges spiral?

- *Logical Faithfulness (5 pts): 2/5* — The Helen Keller quote and the "inch by inch" pastor quote are imported and feel decorative. ### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Genuine pastoral knowledge of the congregation.** The Kristina/Jeremy sobriety praise, the prayer requests, the specific people named — this preacher knows his sheep. The application about God's power to *preserve* (not just rescue from addiction) is a specific, pastorally astute observation that will resonate with the room.

2. **Honest handling of exegetical uncertainty.** "Frankly I don't know if I buy that" regarding the battlefield-awareness theory of the hand-drinkers is admirable. A preacher who admits what he doesn't know builds more trust than one who papers over gaps with confident speculation.

3. **Native, well-targeted illustration.** The artificial crab meat analogy and the horse-people/camel analogy are congregation-specific, genuinely amusing, and serve the text's point about God using the least and weakest. These are good instincts.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a note to self, not a proclamation.** "What that looks like today starts in Romans 3:23. (gospel)." This is a bracket indicating where the gospel should go. In the final sermon, there must be an actual gospel presentation — penal substitution, justification by faith, the necessity of repentance and trust in Christ's finished work. As submitted, an unbeliever would leave with no idea how to be saved.

2. **The text's own theological engine is ignored.** Judges 7:2 is the interpretive key to the entire passage: God reduces the army *"lest Israel boast over me."* God's motive is His own glory, and He will not share it. This is not mentioned in any developed way. The sermon communicates "God uses the weak" but does not push to "God *requires* our smallness because He will not tolerate glory-theft." That harder, sharper truth is the text's own point — and it has massive applicatory force.

3. **The application is a generic list.** "Facing turmoil? Look to Jesus. Troubles at home? Look to Jesus." This is not application — it is a bumper sticker. Application at this level presses no one and corners no one. The sermon needed to name specific sins (self-reliance, refusing to trust God's method because it seems foolish, fear that masquerades as prudence) and address specific hearers (the person who refuses to give to the food pantry because they're afraid of their own finances, the person who won't share their faith because the odds seem too long).

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that his enthusiasm for the military and logistical details (camel counts, stampede physics, war camp population estimates) is serving his own interests more than the congregation's understanding. The five sentences of camel biomechanics are interesting — and they crowd out the two sentences that would have opened the text's theological heart.

He also likely does not see how much the interruptions (praise report mid-sermon, prayer requests embedded in the body) communicate to the congregation that the text is one item among equals rather than the authoritative word around which everything else orbits.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The camel section *feels* like strong preaching — vivid, researched, compelling, congregation-specific (the horse people). But it is primarily an impressive piece of historical reconstruction that serves as an *explanation* of how God won rather than an *exposition* of *why* God won. The sermon is most energized in a section that does not carry the text's theological weight. Confidence in the material masked the theological gap.

Similarly, the three-part outline feels like strong structure — and it is the best-organized element of the sermon. But the structure follows the narrative surface of the text rather than the text's argument. A narrative outline and an expository outline are different things.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Judges 7:2 is the sermon.** "The Lord said to Gideon, 'The people with you are too many for me to give the Midianites into their hand, *lest Israel boast over me, saying, "My own hand has saved me."'"** This is one of the most explicit statements of divine glory-jealousy in all the Old Testament. God is not stripping the army to make a point about faith — He is stripping the army because *He will not share His glory with man*. That is the FCF. That is the theological heart. That is the gospel bridge (because the one place where man's "my own hand has saved me" is permanently destroyed is the cross, where God sends His Son to do what no human arm could accomplish). The sermon touched this ("God can use the weakest") but never pushed to the terrifying and glorious reality that God *insists* on this — and that insistence is both law (you are not the hero of your story) and gospel (the true hero has already won).

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Open with Judges 7:2, not with the army count.** Let the text's own stated reason be the sermon's thesis from the first minute: "God will not share His glory with you. And that is not a threat — it is the best news you will hear today."

2. **Cut the camel population estimates by 60%.** Keep the stampede image (it's vivid and theologically useful — chaos as God's instrument). Remove the half-million camel calculation and the weight/speed analysis. One sentence: "Imagine a war camp of hundreds of thousands of soldiers stampeded in the dark by their own animals — that's what 300 unarmed men with torches and trumpets accomplished. God didn't need a sword."

3. **Replace the "look to Jesus" bullet list with differentiated address.** Address at least three hearers: (a) the person who won't take the "foolish" step of obedience because the odds don't make sense — this text is for you; (b) the person whose army is too big, who trusts their preparation and resources — God may be about to reduce you; (c) the unbeliever who doesn't yet know the one who went to the cross alone, unarmed, outnumbered, and won.

4. **Write out the gospel, don't bracket it.** Romans 3:23 is a starting address, not a complete gospel presentation. Finish the sentence: "We have all sinned and fall short of the glory of God — and the God who will not share His glory sent His Son to bear the punishment for our glory-theft, so that through faith in Christ alone we could be clothed in His righteousness." That is the Judges 7 gospel connection.

5. **Move the praise report to before or after the sermon.** Mid-sermon interruptions train the congregation to treat the text as an intermission between church activities. Protect the sermonic moment.

6. **Crystallize the main idea into one sentence and repeat it.** Suggestion: *"God strips away every human resource so that when He saves, only He gets the glory — and Christ is the final proof that He means it."*

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

**Narrative OT passage — rubric bends modestly here.** Judges 7 is historical narrative, and the rubric's exposition categories were designed with discourse texts partly in mind. The FCF and Christological movement categories apply well and are not strained. The narrative genre rewards vivid retelling (which the preacher does well) while demanding that the preacher explain *why the narrator recorded these events* — which is where the sermon falls short. The rubric's gospel centrality expectations are fully appropriate: narrative texts in the OT carry gospel freight through typology and covenantal pattern, and the preacher should be expected to draw that out organically. No categories need to be waived for this genre.
