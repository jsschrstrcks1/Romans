# Judges 6 — Called in the Wine Press
## *The Whine Press and the Idol*

*[Raw sermon notes — Pt. 1 of the Gideon series. The polished follow-up sermon is "Judges 6 - Lord Just Once More.md," which covers vss 36–40 and opens with a recap of this message.]*

---

Were going to look at Gideon tonight. So if you've brought your bible and I hope you have, turn with me to Judges 6:11. While you're turning there I want to catch you up on what has happened in the first half of this chapter, in fact there are two key verses. Like with all the other judges, the chapter starts with "and Israel did what was evil in the eyes of the Lord" and then the Lord gives them over to the Midianites. The midianites would pass through, steal their food, and leave. They did this for seven years.

It kind of reminds me of that kids movie "bugs life". Do you remember that movie? Most of you who were kids, or had kids in the 90s probably will. It's a story about an ant colony that has been subjugated by gang of grasshoppers. They fly in, beat people up, threaten to eat the princess if they aren't given a certain amount of food and so forth. Bad brutes that are running a mafia like protection scam. Feed us, and we'll keep you from getting beaten up by the other guys. Don't feed us, and we'll beat you all up worse than the other guys. Any way the bulk of the movie is about this one little misfit ant that's always getting in the way and messing things up. He gets sent out to find warrior bugs to help defend the ants and along the way finds himself, finds a group of actors that think he wants them to pretend to be warriors, and then they fight off the grasshopper horde using brains, community, and an appeal to a higher power.

That is very similar to what we are going to see with Gideon. He seems to be following God but growing disillusioned. Look at vs 11–13.

Israel is in trouble, and an Angel appears to the man, and what is his response? He Boldly Whines and whines and whines some more (Well he was standing in a whine press – yuk yuk).

It reminds me of a story I heard about a man and a woman who were driving down the road in their big ol pick up truck. The wife in the passenger seat, and the husband behind the wheel. And the wife complains you know, before we were married we used to sit so much closer together. Now you're way over there, and I'm way over here! The husband replied "who moved?".

Gideon is complaining that they are being oppressed by their enemy but in truth and as is the case for us today, God is always there. It's us that move away from Him. The Israelites had begun to worship Baal and his consort Asherah. I won't go too deeply into detail, but the long and short of it is baal worship is disgusting, centering around sex believing that sleeping with prostitutes would make baal and Ashera sleep together, which would cause the rain. Sometimes children were sacrificed on the cornerstone of buildings if the building was important enough. Its vile sick and twisted. Later in our passage tonight it talks about the Ashera your father has "erected". This is a play on words. Please don't make me spell it out for you. The context of the last few minutes should explain just what is meant by "erect". That word isn't in every translation, but it is in a number of the older manuscripts. Any way

This sort of vile idolatry makes God angry. He has very specific rules for Sex and Marriage, things everyone would have known as part of the mosaic law. That had been forsaken. This entire people that had turned their back on God and his commands were allowed to be brought under subjugation by the midianites.

I know I beat this drum a lot. But it's a problem that is a lot more pervasive than people in America realize. Remember when you sent us to Peru a few years back? We were going into communities where every house was 6' x 10'. Made entirely of corrugated tin. Maybe a family of 6 or 8 lived there. They didn't know any different and were happy with and proud of their homes. Some even had pictures of their children hanging in their home. They felt wealthy indeed. In Haiti I saw a family of 8 living under a few pieces of paneling probably taken off a derelict car – leaned against a tree. No pillow. No Egyptian cotton sheets, no microwave, no tv, no Netflix, no internet, no facebook. And they were happy. They praised God for their abundant bounty. In Dominican republic on one side of Santo Domingo the city looked like times square NY. On the other it looked like people had been dropping bombs for sport. Houses half torn down, falling down, roads with craters large enough to trap a tank. No electricity except on Saturday nights for four hours (if it lasted that long). No indoor running water, no flushing toilets. They did have a convenient ditch beside the road for dumping raw sewage into. Haiti was the same in many places. In fact in Haiti I met and worked with a man named Willow. He was 89 years old, and out working in the 110f heat working as a stone mason to help build this school. He was incredibly amazing. He took a quick look at a rock and knew exactly where to hit it, to make it split into the exact shape he wanted it. He made a 4' wall perfectly flat and level north to south, and east to west, top to bottom it was perfect. We checked it with a level because we were blown away by his craftsmanship and it was indeed perfect. Any way, after we had constructed the shape of the school to four feet our time was almost up, and Willow – pastor willow – invited us to his home for dinner. We found out he made about 30$ a month. His home was larger than most. It had two rooms one was about 6' by 7' and the other was maybe 8 x 8. So maybe 85 square feet in total. I think I was the tallest person he'd ever had in his house. I had to duck until I was offered a place to sit. Any way, through the course of the night my missionary friend explained that the meal tonight had cost Willow a small fortune. Up until that night they didn't have a dining room table, or chairs. He had bought them specifically because he wanted to have us over and feed us in the manner we were accustomed to. How much did the table cost? 80$ — 10$ for each of the 12 chairs. 200$ so he could let us sit in relative comfort. He had purchased and butchered at almost 90 years old a goat. Goats cost 3$ us/lb on the hoof, or still alive, down there at the time. A young 20lb goat then was almost 60$. Then because he wanted to make the national dish of Haiti for us – a rare treat that they save for year around. And because Pumpkins were out of season he traveled to Dominican republic to one of the markets that imported them.

All together one meal one night cost the man a little better than 300$. Nearly a years wages. Because he wanted to bless us while we were there.

When was the last time you felt inclined to spend 10 months of your salary to bless someone? To further Christs kingdom? To care for the needy and downtrodden? We Americans have a tendency to worship security, which we think we can buy with money. We worship entertainment, which we can definitely buy with money. The average American spends 500$ a year on their booze bill, almost 10 grand on insurance – and an additional 4k per extra person. We spend on average 3900$ going to see movies, on cable, internet and so forth. The average American man spent almost 500$ on porn last year. What does he have to show for that? Broken marriages, broken families and hurting kids.

How much was given to charity? Its hard to put a finger on the exact total. The survey I was reading lumped charitable giving, savings, and a few other things under "other expenditures". In 2010 the average American put about 8% of their income into this "other category" – savings, charitable giving, clothing, etc. In 2011, it was only 5% of their total income. By 2015 despite the "average" income being a lot higher, the amount people gave had continued to drop and so these average Americans were only giving around 3 pct of their total income. Let me ask you. If you saw a checkbook where an individual spent 6% of their income on entertainment but only 3% on charitable giving, what would that tell you their priority is? On themselves? Or on God?

In 2010 the average American spent more on dog food in a year than they spent on charitable giving. I'm a firm believer in feeding your animals, don't get me wrong. But now we as a people are 3x less giving. I argue that it comes back to the fact that we as a people worship money first and foremost. Secondly we worship comfort. In 2015 we spent 3900$ on average for making our homes a comfortable temperature. 4000$ on household furnishings (tv, couches, etc). 1000$ on scents, and soaps, and shampoos, and conditioners and frufru stuff for the bath tubs.

I'm not saying its wrong to treat yourself. I'm saying we as a people put too high of a priority on our own comfort, convenience and safety. And I see a lot of this in our passage tonight.

---

Lets read again: 14–17. It strikes me that at the heart of Gideon's objections is a question: does God really care about my people and I? He does of course. We know scripture says God disciplines those he loves. God was in the middle of a time of discipline for the nation of Israel when this whole scene went down (Judges 6:1–6). One of the commentators says God does not allow his children to sin successfully for long.

- Secondly God rebuked them – Judges 6:7–10.
- Thirdly God had blessed them as a nation generously in the past (vs 13).

Another question I see Gideon asking in this passage is "will God take care of me?" Were going to get into that more next week. But we can see a hint of it here and now. Read with me vs 25–30.

God gives him a command. Go pull down the idols of the false Gods. Make an alter and prepare a bull on the alter. Two things I want you to notice right away. Gideon took 10 servants, two bulls, his father's herd, his father's alter with a big enough hunk of wood to produce a fire that would consume a pair of bulls. Gideon was no poor man. He was living in luxury. I think he was at least in part afraid of losing the comfort and security that comes from his fathers money and that's why he obeyed God in the middle of the night. If he had done all this in the day, people would have tried to stop him, his father may have made threats. These Israelites all believed that Baal was a real God and a real threat if they didn't worship him properly. Are you seeing the parallels? His attempts at subterfuge didn't really hide his actions. The next morning it didn't take long and they were knocking on Gideon's door. Gideon's father's response was very bold. He saw that his son had torn down the idol, and lived. This kindled or rekindled his father's faith, and so when people came to punish his son for destroying the big dead piece of wood they were worshiping, in that moment he knew Baal was dead, and he said "if Baal is truly a God, let Baal defend himself" and he issued a warning that the real God the King of Kings and Lord of Lords was going to move against the supporters of Baal, and that there would be none left living.

This worship of security, of lets face it, this worship of money, of materialism — is an idol in America today. Thousands of years later and we're still worshiping a chunk of a dead tree thinly sliced and dyed green. We use it to buy the illusion of security, but let me ask you, where does real security come from? John Piper said "Prosperity cannot be a proof of God's favor. Since it is what the Devil promises to those who worship him." We use our money, our national prosperity to buy short term comfort, but think about the eternal things we may sacrifice on the alter of this dead piece of wood? We may be entertained here on earth. But we won't be laughing if because of our desire for entertainment we spend an eternity separated from God in Hell.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Energy Squandered in the Middle Distance — Text, Heart, and Gospel Never Converge**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A sermon that opens on Gideon's called cowardice and carries genuine warmth and illustration but loses the text entirely in a long spending-statistics detour, marks its gospel presentation as a bracketed placeholder, and never summons the congregation to specific repentance or faith.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with a significant qualification.**

The *Scopus Test* passes narrowly: the preacher's main concern — God's people abandoning God for false security, and God calling a reluctant man from that same false security to obedience — does arise from Judges 6. God's discipline of a comfortable, idolatrous people, and the call of Gideon from the winepress, is genuinely the passage's argument.

The *Authority Test* is more troubled. The sermon does not edit the text's hard declarations, but it also barely stays near enough to the text to be accountable to it. Large portions of the sermon — especially the spending statistics and Haiti narrative — are thematically adjacent to the passage but not textually derived from it. The preacher stands as a concerned pastor making reasonable inferences, but he is not standing under the text as a herald of its specific argument. The sermon passes the gate because the main idea is legitimate; it does not pass cleanly.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score capped at 69. Two fatal flaws triggered.**

**Flag 2 — Main point drifts from the passage's own argument:** The sermon begins in the text (vss 11–13, 14–17, 25–30) but spends the majority of its runtime on American consumerism statistics and a lengthy missions travelogue. These themes are inferred from the text's idolatry motif, but the passage's own argument — God's sovereign discipline, Gideon's cowardice, the divine call and commission, the destruction of the Baal altar as first act of obedience — is never developed. The sermon uses the text as a launching pad rather than dwelling beneath it.

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation:** The gospel is marked in the manuscript as a bracketed note — *(Gospel presentation)* — which is not a sermon; it is a reminder to give one. The text as delivered contains no articulation of Christ's finished work, no proclamation of justification, no explanation of how the gospel answers idolatry. The communion text at the close is not an integrated sermon element — it is a liturgical add-on. The gospel is absent from the sermon's argument, not woven into it.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 11/25**
- *Textual Fidelity* — 5/10: The sermon begins with the passage and touches the right themes (Israel's idolatry, God's discipline, Gideon's fear, the altar-destruction command). But the passage disappears for extended stretches. Verses 11–13, 14–17, and 25–30 are referenced but not opened. The text is used as a frame, not as the governing authority. The Fallen Condition Focus is partially present — idolatry and false security — but identified thematically rather than exegetically derived.
- *Contextual Awareness* — 2/5: The pattern of Judges (apostasy → discipline → cry → deliverer) is gestured at in the opening but never named or developed. The book's theological argument about Israel's spiral is not engaged.
- *Exegetical Clarity* — 2/5: No exegetical work is present. The play on "erected/Asherah" is noted with appropriate pastoral caution, and the naming of Baal worship is helpful, but no word study, no close reading, no unpacking of the text's own structure occurs.
- *Logical Faithfulness* — 2/5: The move from "Gideon feared losing comfort" to "Americans worship money" is an inference, not an exegetical argument. It isn't wrong — but it isn't demonstrated from the text. The result is topical preaching with a narrative frame.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 10/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness* — 3/7: The sermon does not present Christ. The communion text stands apart. There is no movement from Gideon's deliverance as type, no connection to the Angel of the Lord in verse 11 (a significant missed redemptive-historical moment), no gospel logic.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* — 1/3: The Judges cycle is briefly named but not placed in the redemptive arc. The Angel of the Lord — who is likely a Christophany and is a major theological moment in the text — is passed over without comment.
- *Doctrinal Precision* — 4/4: The theology of sin as idolatry is handled well. God's discipline of those He loves (Hebrews 12:6 cited) is correct. No theological errors detected.
- *Confessional Alignment* — 2/4: No confessional errors. But no positive confessional contribution. The sermon does not engage grace, covenant, or the doctrines of grace where the text invites it.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 3/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel* — 0/5: The gospel is a placeholder. A non-Christian listening to this sermon would hear a call to generosity, a rebuke of materialism, and a warning about Hell — but would not hear how to be saved.
- *Necessity of Christ* — 1/5: Christ is decorative, present only in the communion liturgy and a closing implication. He is not necessary to the sermon's argument.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity* — 2/5: The mention of "spending an eternity separated from God in Hell" gestures toward the weight of sin's consequences. The Piper quote about prosperity and the Devil's promises is pointed. But there is no movement toward the cross as the answer to what is diagnosed.

*Synagogue Test*: A devout Jewish or Muslim hearer could sit through this sermon and be largely untroubled by Christ. The gospel is not scandalous here — it is absent.

*Law/Gospel Dialectic*: The law lands (idolatry, materialism, spending habits). The gospel does not relieve it. This is a problem. The congregation is convicted — and then left there.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15**
- *Heart Penetration* — 3/5: The personal missions illustrations (Haiti, Willow, Peru, Dominican Republic) are vivid and emotionally engaging. They press the comfort question. The Willow story in particular is powerful. The comparison of spending habits does land as conscience-pressing.
- *Specificity* — 4/5: This is the sermon's genuine strength. - *Spiritual Differentiation* — 1/5: The sermon addresses the congregation primarily as comfortable Americans in need of conviction about money. **5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8**

- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness* — 2/4: The missions illustrations implicitly model radical generosity. The Gideon altar-destruction story models costly obedience. But neither is connected to the congregation's specific obedience. The "what now" remains unanswered.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**
- *Clarity of Outline* — 2/4: The sermon moves in discernible sections — context and text, whining/complaint, missions travelogue and statistics, return to Gideon, closing warning — but these sections are not clearly connected. A listener could not sketch the outline.
- *Progression* — 2/3: The sermon does build in emotional weight through the Haiti story, and the Gideon altar section does advance. But the long middle detour interrupts momentum significantly.
- *Unity* — 1/2: There are at least two competing main ideas — Gideon's called obedience despite fear, and American materialism as modern idolatry. These are related but not unified under one crisp thesis.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

- *Clarity* — 1/2: The Willow story is long. It works emotionally but loses its point under its own weight. The Bug's Life analogy is entertaining but the connection to the sermon requires the listener to make several leaps.

### 9. Strengths

1. **The Willow illustration is exceptional.** A 89-year-old stone mason on $30/month spending $300 to host missionaries is not a point about poverty — it is a demonstration of the theology of worship the sermon is trying to build. The detail is precise, personal, and earned. It is the sermon's one moment that will be remembered.

2. **The preacher's voice is genuinely oral.** This reads as a man talking to people he knows and loves, not performing for an audience. The humor (the winepress pun, the truck joke), the self-aware asides ("please don't make me spell it out"), and the direct address throughout create pastoral presence on the page. That is not nothing — many preachers lose this in the editing room.

3. **The connection between Gideon's fear and comfort and the congregation's own idolatry of security is exegetically legitimate and practically incisive.** The observation that Gideon obeyed at night because he feared losing his father's wealth and social standing is a real textual insight. If the sermon had stayed here and developed it, the application to American materialism would have had exegetical legs.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses

1. **The gospel is a placeholder, not a proclamation.** The italicized note *(Gospel presentation)* at the sermon's close is an acknowledgment that the sermon knows the gospel should be there — and a confession that it isn't. A sermon that diagnoses idolatry and warns of Hell without presenting the only remedy has used the law without the gospel. The congregation is convicted. They are not released into grace. This is not a minor gap — it is the structural center that is missing.

2. **The statistics detour collapses the sermon's textual accountability.** The section running from "When was the last time you felt inclined to spend 10 months of your salary..." through the American spending statistics runs for several paragraphs without a textual anchor. The preacher has left the passage and is giving a personal finance sermon. The application is not wrong, but it is not preaching — it is commentary on U.S. consumer data. The application must arise from the text, not replace the text.

3. **Gideon's call and commission are never actually preached.** The passage includes the Angel of the Lord's appearance (possibly a Christophany), the divine address "The LORD is with you, O mighty man of valor," Gideon's theological objection, God's direct commission, the sign of the fire-consumed offering, and the altar-destruction as first act of costly obedience. These are not side notes — they are the passage's argument. The sermon mentions them briefly but does not open any of them. The most theologically rich elements of the text are sacrificed to make room for spending statistics.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears not to see that his strongest material is in the text itself. The Gideon narrative contains everything the sermon is reaching for — comfort, fear, idolatry, divine call, costly first obedience — and it does so dramatically. The detour into American consumer statistics is unnecessary if the text is preached fully. The preacher seems to trust his illustrations more than the text, when in fact the text is the most compelling illustration available.

There is also a blind spot around differentiated address. The sermon speaks to the congregation as a single type — comfortable American Christians who spend too much on themselves. It does not see the widow on a fixed income, the young believer wrestling with giving, the unbeliever who doesn't know why Christian generosity would even be rational, or the man who is already giving generously and needs comfort, not further conviction.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The statistics section *feels* sharp and confrontational — numbers and percentages create an impression of precision and research. But this section is the sermon's most vulnerable point. The end notes themselves flag that the sources are unverified. Statistics without sources are not a scalpel; they are a blunt instrument. If a member fact-checks them and finds errors, the sermon's credibility collapses. More fundamentally, the statistical conviction does not arise from the text — it arises from demographic data. That is not a sermon; it is an op-ed with a Bible verse attached.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The Angel of the LORD in verse 11 is almost certainly a Christophany — a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ. This is a significant redemptive-historical moment. The divine address "The LORD is with you, O mighty man of valor" spoken over a man hiding in fear is a grace-word — God names Gideon by what He will make him, not by what he presently is. This is the gospel in miniature: God declares what He will accomplish before the recipient has done anything to deserve it. The sermon walked past this entirely. This moment, properly opened, would have organically connected Gideon's call to Christ's call of sinners and provided the gospel center the sermon lacks.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Write the gospel in, not down.** Remove the placeholder *(Gospel presentation)* and replace it with a genuine 3–5 minute proclamation: the idolatry of Israel and Gideon's own comfort-worship diagnoses the same condition in every hearer. The law cannot save them from it. Only Christ's blood — shed for idolaters — can. Make the indicative precede the imperative.

2. **Open the Angel of the LORD passage (vss 11–12) as the sermon's theological center.** The divine address over Gideon is not just encouragement — it is the pattern of saving grace. God calls what is not as though it were (Romans 4:17). The preacher who sees this will preach it with authority; the congregation will feel the weight of being named by God rather than by their failure.

3. **Cut the spending statistics to their minimum.** The Willow illustration does all the work these paragraphs attempt. One powerful concrete story outweighs a page of unverified statistics. Verify the remaining data or remove it. The end notes' own warning must be heeded before this reaches the pulpit.

4. **Issue a concrete exhortation at the close.** Name the idol specifically. Then summon a specific response: "This week, tear down one thing. Cut one expense. Give it away. Not to earn God's favor — you already have it in Christ — but because idols must be pulled down, and the altar of the Lord must be rebuilt." The congregation needs a "therefore" moment, not just a warning.

5. **Address at least two or three distinct hearer types.** After the main application, pause and speak directly to: the one who genuinely cannot give more (comfort, not condemnation), the new believer who doesn't yet understand Christian giving (instruction), and the comfortable, well-resourced person who knows they should change and is choosing not to (warning with gospel urgency). This takes three minutes and transforms the sermon from a general address into pastoral preaching.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries real pastoral warmth and one genuinely luminous illustration in the Willow story, but it does not yet carry eternal gravity — the congregation is stirred but not undone, convicted but not released, and the weight that a man named by God and called from the winepress to tear down false altars should produce is present as a theme but not yet as a weight that rests on the listener's chest.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Judges 6 is OT narrative, which creates natural tension with a rubric calibrated for expository sermon manuscripts. The rubric bends slightly here: narrative sermons legitimately spend time inside the story before driving to application, and the expectations around "logical faithfulness" must account for typological and analogical movement from text to congregation. That adjustment was applied. Even accounting for genre, however, the fundamental gaps — missing gospel proclamation, underdeveloped text work, absent exhortation — are not genre issues. They are preaching issues that the narrative genre does not excuse.
