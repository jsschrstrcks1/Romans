Its interesting to note that the Bible, unlike most other books, depicts its subjects as real people. Even when the things they do are disgraceful. This is I think a sign of the authenticity of the Bible. If it tried to sugar coat things; that is to say that it depicts men in an idyllic fashion – then I think what it has to say would be a little more suspect. But because it depicts people in good times and bad, it makes me feel like it can be trusted. On top of knowing in our hearts that it's the Word of God, and seeing the difference it makes in our lives over time, we can trust it because its brutally honest. First though, lets look at the authorship and historical setting of the book of Judges.

Firstly we don't really know who the author was. One of my professors at seminary says that his professor in Israel at seminary claims that there is a single jewish tradition that holds to accumulation of texts by Nehemiah, and then re-combining the bits and pieces back into a contiguous whole by Ezra. The Book of Judges covers the period from the death of Joshua to the dawn of the monarchy. Political and religious turmoil accompanied Israel's attempts to occupy the land that had been conquered and divided by lot under the leadership of Joshua. Apart from the struggle against the Canaanites at the time of Deborah, Israel's adversaries came from outside the land. Most of these, such as Moab, Midian, and Ammon, periodically plundered the land. The Philistines, however, who at this time entered Palestine in greater numbers, contested with Israel for permanent possession of the land.

Tragically, the Israelites even fought among themselves. Ephraim was ravaged by Manasseh (ch. 12), and Benjamin was almost annihilated by the other tribes (chs. 20–21). Between the days of Joshua and Samuel, Israel plummeted to moral and spiritual disaster. Over and over the pattern of sin followed by oppression was repeated. Occasionally God raised up a Deborah or a Gideon to turn the people back to himself, but the intervals of revival were all too brief.

Judges falls directly within the heroic age, a time when divinely provided leaders were called on to save the day by personal exploits. It was an unstable period characterized by tribal migrations and lack of central authority. People led dangerous yet simple lives in which warlike pursuits alternated with ordinary peacetime activities.

Some of the judgeships most likely overlapped. Samson and Jephthah (about whom we read tonight), for example, may have ruled simultaneously—one in the west, in Canaan, the other in the east.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Pastoral Heart Wrestling With a Hard Text — But the Sermon Never Arrives

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A faithful attempt to draw encouragement from Jephthah's irregular origins that accumulates helpful observations but never drives them into a governing thesis, gospel center, or conscience-level exhortation.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**Partial Pass — with concern.**

The Scopus Test: The sermon does not produce a single traceable thesis arising from the passage's own argument. The bullet-point summary at the end lists five separate ideas ("don't make foolish vows," "don't think your past spoils your future," "God can use anyone," "sin starts in your heart," "trust God") — these are not one unified point; they are five competing points. The passage itself (Judges 11) carries a central movement: God sovereignly uses a rejected, illegitimate man to deliver his people, and this same man's rash vow produces catastrophic consequences — a unified narrative about the danger of self-reliance before a holy God. The sermon does not land there.

The Authority Test: The preacher does not edit or soften the text. He handles the uncomfortable material honestly (the vow, the daughter, the implication of child sacrifice). The authority test passes.

**Gate decision**: The sermon passes on authority but is structurally diffuse. The main point of the sermon does not equal the main controlling point of the passage. Evaluation proceeds but with this noted as a significant structural weakness.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Two flags triggered. Score capped at 69.**

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic.** The applications offered ("trust God," "don't make foolish vows," "your past doesn't define you") are true statements but could be preached from a dozen unrelated passages. Nothing in the application is specific to the dynamics of this congregation, this particular text, or the fallen condition Judges 11 addresses. There is no differentiated address — the believer, the doubter, the backslider, and the unbeliever are all given the same general encouragements.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The sermon explains and illustrates but never summons a response. The closing bullets are observations, not imperatives. At no point does the preacher look the congregation in the eye and say: "Therefore, this week, stop doing X — repent of Y — pursue Z." The congregation was informed but not summoned.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 13/25**
- Textual Fidelity: 5/10. The preacher covers vv. 1–6 and the vow/daughter section, but large portions of the chapter (the negotiation with Ammon, vv. 12–28; the Spirit's descent in v. 29; the specifics of the vow in vv. 30–31; the daughter's response in vv. 36–40) receive no treatment. The sermon is built on selected details, not the passage's own flow.
- Contextual Awareness: 3/5. The historical background section is competent and the placement in the Judges cycle is noted. The Deuteronomic sin-cycle, which is the interpretive key to all of Judges, is gestured at but not developed as the lens for reading the chapter.
- Exegetical Clarity: 3/5. The parallel with Joseph is suggestive. The illegitimacy laws from Deuteronomy 23:2 are cited correctly. The exegetical moves are present but not disciplined.
- Logical Faithfulness: 2/5. The extended "Jesus Take the Wheel" discussion, the Robert E. Lee military illustration, the grandmother's favorite verse (Proverbs 3:5–6), and the farm-arrival illustration are loosely connected to the text. They do not arise from the passage's argument — they are imposed from outside.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18**
- Christ-Centeredness: 4/7. The messianic genealogy connection (Perez, Rahab, Solomon) is genuine and textually grounded. The comparison of Jephthah's grief to the Father giving the Son is emotionally evocative but theologically imprecise — the analogy limps significantly and is not developed carefully enough to bear the weight placed on it.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3. Judges is placed in its historical period competently. The Christological connection is made but not through covenantal logic — it feels more like a thematic leap than a textual landing.
- Doctrinal Precision: 4/4. The discussion of original sin, imputation from Adam, and the nature of the fall is accurate and handled with more precision than the rest of the sermon. This is a genuine strength.
- Confessional Alignment: 2/4. No contradictions of confessional theology. The treatment of imputed sin is sound. However, the gospel content is vague and motivational rather than confessionally precise.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 6/15**
- Clarity of the Gospel: 2/5. "Trust God (gospel)" appears in the closing bullet, but the gospel is never actually proclaimed. The finished work of Christ, justification by faith, and the necessity of the cross are absent as distinct declarations. The name of Jesus appears incidentally in illustrations but not as the proclamation of good news.
- Necessity of Christ: 2/5. Christ is mentioned but not essential to the sermon's argument. The points about trusting God, not making foolish vows, and not letting your past define you would survive without any explicit Christology.
- Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5. The Jephthah/Father-sacrificing-Son analogy gestures toward the cross but is not grounded in the text and is not developed to a clear gospel proclamation. The sermon does not move toward the cross — it moves toward practical encouragement.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 6/15**
- Heart Penetration: 2/5. The farm illustration is warm and personal. The fishing illustration conveys a genuine pastoral instinct. But the sermon does not press on the conscience — it encourages without first convicting.
- Specificity: 2/5. "Trust God" and "your past doesn't define you" are true but generic. There is no concrete description of what trusting God looks like for this congregation this week, or what specific sin the text is confronting.
- Spiritual Differentiation: 2/5. There is no differentiated address. The sermon speaks to one undifferentiated audience. The backslider, the self-reliant, the mourning parent, and the person in active sin are never addressed distinctly.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 2/8**
- Call to Response: 1/4. The closing bullets are declarative, not imperative. No clear call is given.
- Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 1/4. The sermon ends without sending the congregation anywhere specific.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**
- Clarity of Outline: 2/4. The three-point structure around vv. 1–6 is clear. But the sermon then jumps to the vow/daughter without signaling the transition, and the closing bullet list feels like a separate document appended to the sermon.
- Progression: 2/3. The early sections build reasonably, but the sermon does not climax — it simply stops.
- Unity: 1/2. Five closing bullets on five different themes is not unity.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**
- Relevance: 1/2. The farm-arrival scene is vivid and contextually rooted. The Robert E. Lee / high ground illustration is functional but generic.
- Clarity: 1/2. The fishing illustration and "Jesus Take the Wheel" reference are accessible but do not arise uniquely from this text.
- Memorability: 1/1. The farm scene (horses, sheep, geese, roosters, guineas) is genuinely memorable and shows a preacher who sees the world.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**
- Reads as Preaching: 1/2. There is warmth and pastoral voice throughout. But the long paragraphs, absence of paragraph breaks at emotional climaxes, and loose syntactic structure read more like a Bible study transcript than a preached sermon.
- Conviction on the Page: 1/2. The doctrinal section on original sin has force. The rest meanders.
- Cadence and Breath: 1/1. The preacher's voice is present and genuine. Conversational cadence comes through.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily myself and my potential. Christ appears incidentally rather than centrally.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Mostly ideas — and not always tightly connected to the text.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Mildly informed and encouraged. Neither convicted nor deeply comforted by a gospel proclamation.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Honest engagement with the hard material.** The preacher does not flee the vow and the daughter. He names the wickedness directly, calls the vow sinful, and sits with the moral complexity. This is more faithful than many preachers who hurry past Jephthah's daughter or spiritualize her away. The section beginning "Who of you would be able to sacrifice your child to fulfill a vow to the Lord?" shows genuine pastoral courage.

2. **The doctrinal section on original sin and imputation is precise and accurate.** The discussion of Adam as representative, sin imputed to all, and the rebuttal of the "literal fruit" distraction demonstrates doctrinal competence and is handled better than most of the sermon's other theological moments. This section could anchor a stronger sermon.

3. **The farm-arrival illustration is specific, local, and genuinely illuminating.** The detail of the horses, sheep, geese, roosters, and guineas all greeting the returning traveler makes the stakes of Jephthah's vow viscerally real to a congregation that likely shares that agricultural world. This is the rubric's ideal: an illustration native to the text and native to the congregation.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **No governing thesis.** The sermon does not answer the question: "What is the single thing this passage is saying, and why does it matter eternally?" The five closing bullets are symptoms of this problem. A sermon that ends with five unrelated takeaways never had one controlling idea. The preacher must go back to Judges 11 and ask: what is the authorially intended main point of this narrative unit? (Likely: God sovereignly uses the rejected and outcast to accomplish his purposes — and the same Jephthah who trusts God in battle destroys his own house through a rash vow made in self-reliance. The contrast between Spirit-empowered dependence and rash self-provision is the text's own argument.)

2. **Gospel absent as proclamation.** "Trust God (gospel)" in a bullet point is not a gospel proclamation. The sermon never explains what the good news is, what Christ has done, what the hearer must trust Christ *for*, or how justification works. An unbeliever sitting through this sermon would not know how to be saved. A believer would not be freshly amazed at grace. The cross appears only in the imprecise Jephthah/Father analogy, which, while moving, is not grounded carefully enough to constitute a gospel declaration.

3. **Extended digressions disconnect the sermon from its text.** The "Jesus Take the Wheel" discussion, the Robert E. Lee section, the Adam and Eve digression on the "literal fruit," and the grandmother's favorite verse all interrupt the flow without returning to the passage. The longest stretch in the sermon — several hundred words — does not reference Judges 11 at all. The sermon loses its orbit around the text and never fully recovers.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely does not see that the "encouragement" frame he has placed over the passage is doing interpretive work that the text resists. Judges 11 is not primarily a story about not letting your past define you — it is a tragedy as much as a triumph. Jephthah is simultaneously a Spirit-filled deliverer and a man whose rashness destroys what he loves most. The text holds both of those truths together without resolving the tension. A sermon that only extracts encouragement from Jephthah's story flattens the text's tragic dimension and misses the Deuteronomic warning about vows (Deuteronomy 23:21–23) that the chapter embodies. The preacher also appears unaware of how much of the chapter his sermon leaves unread.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The Robert E. Lee / high-ground illustration feels like a strong moment because it is vivid and military (and likely resonates with the congregation). But it is illustrating "trust God's judgment because he sees more than you" — a general truth that does not arise from this text. Lee is not in Judges. The illustration works emotionally but masks the fact that the sermon's application at that point is disconnected from the passage's actual argument. The apparent strength of the illustration conceals the absence of textual grounding beneath it.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
The negotiation passage (Judges 11:12–28) — Jephthah's extraordinary legal and theological argument to the king of Ammon, demonstrating unexpected knowledge of Israel's history and the sovereignty of Yahweh — is entirely omitted. This is arguably the most theologically rich section of the chapter. Here is an outcast, a man "born of a prostitute," making a more sophisticated covenantal argument than most of Israel's trained priests. This is where the sermon's theme of God using the rejected could have been textually anchored with maximum force. The preacher found an illustration in the farm yard that the text already contained in verses 12–28.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Write one thesis sentence before writing anything else.** It must arise from the passage and contain the text's own tension: "Judges 11 shows us a man sovereignly chosen by God despite his disgrace — who then ruins his house through a rash vow, teaching us that the same self-reliance that gets us into trouble cannot be baptized into trust in God." Then cut everything that does not serve that sentence.

2. **Proclaim the gospel explicitly in its proper place.** After the law has landed (Jephthah's vow, our own rash self-reliance before God, the consequences of living as if we know better than the Lord), the sermon must pivot to the gospel: Christ's perfect obedience where we have been rash; his death as the vow-keeper who paid the price our broken promises deserve; his resurrection as the guarantee that God's purposes are not thwarted by our failures. Make it plain, concrete, and unavoidable.

3. **Add differentiated direct address.** After the gospel, address specific categories of hearers: "Some of you are Jephthah before the battle — you've made promises to God you know you can't keep. Some of you are Jephthah after — your rash words have already cost you something you cannot get back. Some of you have never trusted God with the outcome at all." Name them. Corner them before Christ.

4. **Cut the Robert E. Lee illustration and the 'Jesus Take the Wheel' material entirely.** Both are imported. Replace them with the negotiation scene in vv. 12–28, which contains the sermon's theme already embedded in the text. Let the text illustrate itself.

5. **End with a single, imperative exhortation, not five bullets.** "This week, identify the one place where you have grabbed the reins back from God — and lay it down. Not as a vow (Jephthah warned you about those) — but as an act of faith in the God who chose you before you were worthy and will finish what he has started." One clear "therefore" moment. Send them somewhere specific.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The problem in this sermon is not orthodox theology without pastoral warmth — the preacher has warmth and pastoral instinct throughout. The problem is an underdeveloped theological framework and absent gospel proclamation, not bloodless correctness.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
There are glimmers of eternal weight — the farm scene, the honest reckoning with the daughter's death, the genuine doctrinal care over original sin — but the sermon does not yet carry the gravity of eternity because it does not yet carry the gospel; a sermon that ends at "trust God" without naming why trust is possible through the blood of Christ cannot bear the full weight of glory.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Judges 11 is Old Testament narrative — a genre that stresses the rubric's categories most heavily around Redemptive-Historical Awareness and Exposition. Narrative sermons are not obligated to produce a three-point doctrinal outline, and the rubric does not require one. However, even narrative preaching must identify the passage's own theological and narrative argument, show where it sits in the covenantal story, and press the hearer through that specific window toward Christ and response. The Rubric Fit is appropriate for this passage: narrative does not reduce the obligation for gospel centrality or exhortation — it changes the form in which they must appear, not whether they must appear.
