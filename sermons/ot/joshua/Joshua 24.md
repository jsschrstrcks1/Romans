## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
"Patriotic Devotion with a Biblical Backdrop — A Moral Appeal That Doesn't Reach the Gospel"

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A sermon that opens Joshua 24 as a springboard for American gratitude and a basic idol-warning, but never exposits the text's actual argument, never clearly presents the gospel, and replaces the passage's covenantal confrontation with a comparatively gentle lifestyle challenge.

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: FAIL**
The sermon's thesis is approximately: "America is blessed like Israel was; we should therefore choose God over our idols (TV, etc.)." The text's thesis — Joshua's covenantal ultimatum demanding exclusive, informed covenant loyalty from a people who have now received the fulfilled promises of God and must make a once-for-all confessional decision — is not the sermon's thesis. The sermon uses the passage as an occasion; it does not arise from it.

**Authority Test: FAIL**
The preacher stands above the text rather than under it. Joshua 24 contains one of the most theologically loaded speeches in the Old Testament: YHWH's first-person rehearsal of his acts from Abraham through the conquest, Joshua's startling warning that Israel is incapable of serving the LORD and that their professed loyalty will turn to judgment against them (vv. 19-20), the covenant renewal ceremony, and the setting up of a witness stone. None of these structurally central elements are handled. The sermon reads the chapter selectively and replaces its covenantal logic with a gratitude-comparison-application frame.

> This sermon is not text-driven. The main point of the sermon does not arise from the main point of the passage. The Berean Gate is not passed.

Because the Berean Gate is not passed, the full scoring framework is applied as a diagnostic only — scores reflect what the sermon does, not what it should be assigned as a "passing" grade. Score is capped by Fatal Flaw Flags regardless.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation:** The sermon ends with the single word "Gospel" as a placeholder. There is no actual gospel content in the manuscript. Christ's death, resurrection, substitutionary atonement, repentance, and faith are entirely absent. The "gospel" referred to at the close is not present. **TRIGGERED.**

**Flag 2 — Main point does not equal text's point:** Addressed above under the Berean Gate. The sermon imports a patriotic-gratitude framework onto a covenantal renewal passage. **TRIGGERED.**

**Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary:** The sermon could be delivered word-for-word in a synagogue or a mosque with only minor adjustments. Jesus appears in a single closing line paraphrasing John 14:6, but he is not preached — he is gestured at. The sermon's entire argument holds without him. **TRIGGERED.**

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic:** "Choose God over your idols," "watch less TV," "read your Bible more." These could close any moralizing talk on any text. No differentiated address. No hearer is cornered. **TRIGGERED.**

**Flag 6 — Inaccessibility not triggered** — the sermon is accessible in terms of language and register.

**Score is capped at 69.** Multiple fatal flaws are present. This is a structural failure, not a matter of polish.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Pastoral familiarity.** The sermon names specific congregation members (Bob, Irma, Rob, Julie) and references real events in their lives. This is not technique — it is genuine pastoral knowledge. The congregation feels known.
2. **The TV-show illustration.** The move from "raise your hand if you watch a TV show" to "don't raise it for this" is honest, quietly disarming, and is the sermon's single most effective conscience moment.
3. **Oral accessibility.** The register is natural, the pacing is conversational, and the sermon is free of jargon. These are real homiletical virtues that many more technically skilled preachers lack.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a placeholder.** The sermon closes with the single word "Gospel" as a note-to-self. This is the most fundamental failure: the sermon's entire edifice rests on an invitation to respond to a gospel that was never preached. What is a person responding to? What has Christ done? Why does his blood matter here? Nothing in the manuscript answers these questions.
2. **The text is not preached — it is illustrated against.** Joshua 24 is treated as a historical parallel to America's situation rather than as the Word of God making a claim on the hearers. The passage's own most powerful moments — Joshua's warning in vv. 19-20 that the congregation cannot serve this God, the corporate confession in vv. 16-18, the covenant ceremony in vv. 25-27, the witness stone — are entirely absent from the sermon.
3. **The introduction consumes the sermon.** The first half of the sermon is extended material about America's wealth, food waste, disability income, and gas prices in Haiti. This material is not biblically derived and is not tethered to the text's argument. It displaces the passage and establishes a patriotic-gratitude frame that the text itself never establishes.

### 11. Blind Spots

- The preacher likely does not see that the America/Israel parallel is theologically loaded in ways that require careful qualification. America is not Israel. The covenant promises given to national Israel under the Mosaic economy are not simply transferable to a modern nation-state. The sermon assumes this parallel without examining it, and the assumption shapes everything.
- The passage's most jarring verse — "You cannot serve the LORD, for he is a holy God; he is a jealous God; he will not forgive your transgressions or your sins" (v. 19) — is invisible in this sermon. Joshua says the people cannot do what they just promised to do. This is the theological heart of the passage and the deepest pastoral hook in the chapter. It points directly to the need for a mediator, for grace, for Christ. It was not seen, or was consciously avoided.
- The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is assumed to be "we're ungrateful and have idols," but the text's specific diagnosis is more penetrating: a people who have been given everything, who genuinely mean their covenant vow, who are nonetheless constitutionally incapable of keeping it without divine grace. That is a different sermon — and a far more powerful one.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The extended opening feels confident and well-researched. The statistics about comparative global wealth are compelling and the congregation will find them interesting. This creates an impression of a well-prepared preacher with a strong opening. But this is the sermon's greatest structural liability: the introduction establishes a sociological frame (comparative blessing) that the text itself does not establish, and then the rest of the sermon is forced to operate within that frame rather than within the text's covenantal frame. The research displaced the exposition.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Joshua 24:19-20 is one of the most remarkable passages in the Old Testament. Joshua gathers the nation, rehearses God's acts, invites a choice — and when they choose, he tells them they are incapable of it. "You cannot serve the LORD." This is not discouragement; it is diagnostic. It sets up the entire need for the New Covenant, for the Spirit who writes the law on the heart, for Christ who fulfills what Israel could not. This verse is the passage's theological engine, and it points directly to the cross. It was not mentioned. A sermon built on v. 19 — "You say you'll serve him. Joshua says you can't. But there is One who served in your place" — would have been both more faithful to the text and more gospel-saturated.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild from the text's own argument.** Identify the main point of Joshua 24 (God's rehearsal of covenant acts, Joshua's covenantal ultimatum, the people's inadequate self-confident response, and the covenant renewal). Let that be the sermon's backbone, not the America parallel.
2. **Preach vv. 19-20.** "You cannot serve the LORD" is the theological hinge of the chapter. It should be the pastoral hinge of the sermon. Use it to expose the congregational tendency toward confident, shallow covenant vows that depend on self-strength rather than grace.
3. **Write out the gospel.** Do not leave "Gospel" as a placeholder. The sermon's closing call is an invitation to respond to something that was never proclaimed. Write out what Christ has done, why his blood is necessary, what repentance and faith mean — and let that be the answer to the "you cannot serve the LORD" diagnosis.
4. **Cut the introduction by two-thirds.** One or two comparative wealth illustrations will establish the "we are blessed" point. Seven minutes of statistics displaces the text and fatigues the listener before the passage is opened.
5. **Address more than one Perkins category.** The sermon currently speaks to the consistent churchgoer. Add a direct word to the person who has never trusted Christ (different from a closing invitation), the person who once was fervent and has drifted, and the person who is making confident spiritual vows they are not keeping.
6. **Replace the TV-show illustration's conclusion.** Currently it ends with "what does that say about your priorities?" This is a good conscience question, but it needs a gospel answer, not just a behavioral correction. What it says about our priorities is that we are exactly what Joshua 24:19 diagnoses — people who say "we will serve the LORD" and quietly do not.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered in the classic sense — the issue here is not correct-but-bloodless theology. The sermon is warm and pastorally genuine. The problem is pre-orthodoxy: the theological content has not yet been filled in. This is more accurately described as **moralistic accessibility**: the congregation is given a behavioral challenge (prioritize God over TV) with a genuine heart behind it, but without the doctrinal and gospel substance that would make that challenge possible to obey or understand.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon has pastoral warmth and genuine congregational love, but the eternal gravity that comes from standing under the Word of God and proclaiming a finished work is absent — because that work was not preached.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Joshua 24 is narrative and legal/covenantal in genre — a covenant renewal ceremony combining historical prologue, stipulations, and covenant ratification. The rubric applies well to this genre. The particular strength of this passage for this rubric is its natural gospel trajectory (vv. 19-20 pointing to the need for a better covenant and a better mediator), which makes the Gospel Centrality and Exposition categories especially weighty. The passage does not strain the rubric; it rewards it — but only if the passage is actually preached. The sermon's failure is not a genre problem; it is an engagement problem.
