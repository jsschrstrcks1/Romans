### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The sermon has not yet preached the text.** After reading Exodus 14:1–10, the preacher has not returned to the passage to open it. The text was read; it has not been expounded. The entire manuscript so far is pre-sermon material — context-setting that should occupy two to three minutes, not the bulk of what is written.
2. **The archaeological material risks becoming the sermon’s main point.** The extended treatment of Nuweiba, the obelisks, the Gulf of Aqaba’s depths, the chariot bones — presented as a cumulative apologetic for the historicity of the crossing — runs the risk of teaching the congregation to trust the Bible because archaeology confirms it. This is a significant theological error in the making: biblical authority does not rest on archaeological corroboration. The text is true because God said it, not because Solomon erected a pillar and scientists found chariot wheels.
3. **The Christmas detour is a category error.** The aside about Christmas, pagan holidays, and the Catholic coopting of the birth of Christ is unrelated to Exodus 14 and does not serve the sermon’s argument. It introduces a topic (liturgical calendar controversies) that would require its own careful treatment and simply dissipates sermonic energy at the point where the preacher should be building toward the text’s main claims.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher appears unaware of the typological freight that Exodus 14 carries in the canon. This passage is not simply a remarkable historical event to be defended archaeologically — it is the Old Testament’s paradigmatic image of salvation. Paul reads it as a type of baptism (1 Cor 10:1–2). Isaiah returns to it as the template for the new exodus (Isaiah 43:16–21; 51:9–11). The Gospel of Mark structures Jesus’ ministry as a new exodus. The entire redemptive-historical argument for why Exodus 14 belongs in a Christian pulpit is invisible in the fragment as written. The preacher has the passion of an apologist where the text demands the voice of a herald.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The archaeological detail creates an impression of sermon depth. A congregation hearing about navigation charts, GPS coordinates, obelisks with defaced inscriptions, and coral-encrusted chariot bones may feel that they are receiving something richly researched and authoritative. But research is not exposition. The weight of the material is historical, not theological. The strength is real in the domain of apologetics; it is an illusion in the domain of preaching. The preacher who has done this research has done good work — but that work belongs in a Bible study handout as supporting material, not as the primary content of the sermon.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Exodus 14:13–14 — not yet reached in the sermon — contains one of the most extraordinary pastoral imperatives in the Torah: "Fear not, stand firm, and see the salvation of the Lord, which he will work for you today. For the Egyptians whom you see today, you shall never see again. The Lord will fight for you, and you have only to be silent." This is a text that was made for preaching. The command to stand still while God fights is the Old Testament’s version of "by grace you have been saved, through faith, and this not of yourselves." The congregation stands between the Egyptian army and the sea, paralyzed, and God says: *be still and watch me*. This is the sermon. The fragment has not arrived here yet — but the preacher should know that this is where the text is taking him, and every introduction decision should be made in light of this destination.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)
1. **Cut the archaeological section to three to four sentences maximum.** The research is genuine and the corrected visual of the sea crossing is worth keeping. But reduce it to: "There is strong evidence that this crossing happened at Nuweiba in the Gulf of Aqaba — walls of water 100 feet deep, a ten-mile crossing. They have found chariot bones there, encrusted in coral. This wasn’t a Sunday-school picture. This was a terrifying miracle." Then return immediately to the text.
2. **Cut the Christmas detour entirely.** It serves nothing in this sermon. Remove it.
3. **Establish the sermon’s thesis before reading the text.** Open with a question the text answers: "What does it look like when God saves someone? What does the person who is being saved have to contribute?" Then read the passage, and let the answer emerge from the text itself.
4. **Develop Exodus 14:13–14 as the sermon’s center.** "Stand still and see the salvation of the Lord" is the thesis. Moses is not telling the people to try harder — he is commanding them to stop and watch God work. Build the whole sermon around this imperative and its implications: God is the author, executor, and completer of salvation. His people contribute their helplessness.
5. **Connect to Christ explicitly before closing.** 1 Corinthians 10:1–2 gives the preacher New Testament warrant. The sea crossing is a type of baptism — death to the old life, passage through judgment, arrival at freedom. Jesus is the greater Moses who does not merely lead through the sea but *becomes* the judgment his people pass through. This connection is not forced — Paul makes it himself. Use it.
