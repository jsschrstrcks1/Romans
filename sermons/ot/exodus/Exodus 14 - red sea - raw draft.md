We talked a couple weeks ago about how different contexts are important. Todays passage in exodus 14 – if you’ve brought your bibles and I hope you have, if not open to page_____ in your pew bibles – gains new depths when you consider the geographic historic and archeological contexts. 

As you may already know ex 14 is the story of the crossing of the red sea. This is after the the plagues in Egypt. You remember that story, I think everyone has seen charleton hestons moses. Hopefully you all read this story here recently too! Did you all make it through your weekly reading? I hope so. If not there is always next week. Its hard to establish new habbits. 

Any way, so where we are in our story today is right after the tenth plague. Every egyptian was grieving, and mourning the loss of their first born. In their grief they send the Israelites off with lavish gifts. Basically it was worth any cost to get these people whose God was angry with them out of their city. So they loaded the Israelites up with everything they could carry, and sent them off.

But as we will read something happens. Scripture says God hardens the hearts of Egypt. When we read our passage, please Make note of that in your bible. There are entire denominations that are founded around the idea that mans will is sovereign over God. Yet time after time after time we see in this story God hardening the heart of a man or men. If God has the power to harden pharaohs heart, does he not have the power to soften peoples hearts? To draw people to himself? Absolutely and Praise God for it, no one is ever too far Gone for God to reach, and woo them too himself. 

So when you hear people say God cannot change the heart of man, or God always respects mans free will – you can say “yeah that’s true, but that doesn’t keep him from interfering so that God can accomplish his will – look with me at exodus 14!”. 

Alright, lets read our scripture, exodus 14:1-10. 

First thing I want you to notice is they emphasize the location. Vs one they mention several land marks, and even what direction they were supposed to be facing. That caught my eye, and you know me, that triggered some research. I found some navigational charts for the red sea, and found where others before me had figured out where the Israelites would have crossed. Turns out there is a place called Dalia forgive me for butchering Arabic, but its called Nuweiba. If you look on maps from more than a hundred years or so ago, you find that it was called “Nuweiba al Muzayyinah” or roughly waters moses crossed. Or waters of moses crossing. There are a lot of things whose real location has been changed over the years, not only geographical markers, but dates – look at Christmas, Jesus was probably born in the spring as far as we can tell. But over the years the catholics coopted Christ’s birth and used it to obscure a pagan holiday. I don’t think Christ minds. Its more important that we celebrate Christ, than that we get the day right. But this has happened with historic locations a few times as well. So this alone wasn’t enough to convince me. I did some more digging, and I found out that there is an obelisk on the Egyptian side that has been defaced. It had some writing on it, but little is still there. Across the sea there is another pillar – that still has writing. It commemorates the crossing of moses, and was placed by Solomon to Honor God, and the miracle he did at the crossing of the sea, and the destruction of the armys of pharaoh. Little wonder the one on the opposite shore was defaced! Next I turned to navigation charts of the area – the gulf of aqaba – just a little bit north, and a little bit south the sea drops off quite steeply. Where the Israelites likely crossed is about 90-100ft deep at its deepest. To the north and south of this place, the sea drops down to a little under 2000 meters, or just short of 6000 feet deep. 

I don’t know about you guys, but whenever I pictured this, I had the bible story book picture of men walking between walls of water that would have came to their shoulders. It would have been an inconvenience but not anything too dangerous. But no, they would have been surrounded by walls of water up to a hundred feet deep, and not only that the crossing is around ten miles at that point by my best ability to estimate using google maps. Not only that but they have found bunches and bunches and bunches of busted up chariots. They found the bones of men and horses. All of which has been preserved, and encrusted in corral. By contrast, they really haven’t found many other places where anything remotely close to this could have happened. Precious few places are so shallow, none of them are littered with archeological evidence. None of them are called the place where Moses crossed, and none of them were recognized by the ancient Israelites as the place where it actually happened. So its pretty clear that this is THE spot where it happened. Isn’t that cool? Sciences proving events found in scripture. See the difference the geographical context can make? You go from 2 million people in chest deep water crossing a short river, to 2 million people crossing through a supernaturally made channel with walls of water towering 100ft on either side. Its significant the impact it makes here isn’t it? If we hadn’t looked at history and archaeology how would we have known that the area had a name like “where moses crossed” or the pillars on both sides! See how it adds a new level of depth to what youre reading? A lot of the time when you reach something that’s difficult or seams dry, that’s just because you have to go deeper. Make sense? 

The next thing I noticed was in vs 6 – ALL his Army, and 600 choice chariots. So what we know of Egyptian military design is that there would have been at least two types of chariots. The normal cheap, mass produced kind – each wheel had four spokes. And then there was the flashy gold veneered type with wheels with six spokes. The six spokes represented an advance in wheel construction. It let them go over worst terrain with less concern over damage.  It also gave them more surface area to cover in gold inlays. 

So they had two types of chariots. Historians think there were about 20000 normal chariots, and 600 of the choice chariots. Likely the choice chariots would have been commanders. There were likely mercenaries there as well. The Egyptians of this period were known to hire greek, phonecians, and so forth. This would have been towards the end of the prominence of the greek peoples. Just past their prime in fact. Rememeber they said of helen of troy that she had a face that could launch a thousand ships? The greeks may well have had that many in their fleet. They were a powerful fighting force. The Spartans for instance said come home with your shield or on it. Their whole way of life was militaristic in Sparta. Everything celebrated those who fought. More than likely they would have been a terrifying force on foot, while the Egyptians were on chariots and perhaps horse back. Indeed at this point in time the Egyptians were the equivalent of the worlds super power. Could you imagine being unarmed, heavily laden, and then suddenly had the full might of the US army deployed against you? It would be a terrifying thing. According to The Reformation study bible, there may have been as many as three Egyptians per chariot. A driver with a sword, an archer, and a runner with a sword. So twenty thousand chariots is sixty thousand soldiers. 

Even with 2 mil Israelites the Egyptians and their mercenaries would have slaughtered them. The casualties would have been in the hundreds of thousands, if not over a million. These people were strong from a life of hard work, but they were not soldiers, they were not armed, they were carrying their children, and belongings. Apart from the direct intervention of God, they would have been massacred. 

The Israelites knew how vulnerable they were alone. They knew that Short of God’s divine intervention all was lost. 

I think that was the point God had to bring them to, in order to deepen their faith. Look with me at vs 11-13. They are basically asking God through moses, why. Have you ever been to a point in your life where you asked God that question? Everything seemed to be going in the wrong direction. Perhaps youre enemies are encamped around you. Perhaps you have an unstoppable force on one side, and an uncross able sea on the other. 

I forget who it was, but someone once said, sometimes you’re the hammer, sometimes youre the anvil, and sometimes you’re the Iron. The raw lump of metal being tossed into the crucible, heated and scraped, hammered, and shaped. Being forged from a useless lump into a useful tool. 

Like any of us, the Israelites were a stubborn and forgetful people. They had to learn this lesson over and over and over. I like to think I’d do better, but usually for almost all of us that wouldn’t be true. Do we not have to learn the same lessons over and over sometimes too? Especially the lesson of trust in God, of faith in God, dependence upon God. We – especially we americans – are an independent lot. We have this idea that WE can pull OURSELVES up by OUR bootstraps. That we are sufficient for anything we set our minds to. We think We know better, work harder, lead better, come up with better solutions, and so forth than anyone else. The problem is we are idiots compared to God. We cant see much beyond the end of our face, and yet God sees everything past present and future. His Higher perspective means he KNOWs whats coming where we THINK we know. The Israelites had to have this lesson beat home, and sometimes we do too. 

When your problems surround you. When everything looks hopeless, frequently that’s when God chooses to move. All the Israelites could see was death behind them, and death in front. 

But look what God was doing – vs 15-20

Geography yet again, plays a role in the place God sent them. 

IF you look at the place God sent them on a map, you will see there is only one way onto the beach God sent them to, and that is through a narrow canyon. Its wide enough for a two lane road, but definitely a pinch point. It was basically the perfect spot for them to be. when God created the place, he knew what would come centuries later, and made it easily defensible, and made a spot that could more easily be crossed. With the canyon being the only way in, it was a simple thing for God to make a single pillar of fire and smoke in one spot that blinded and intimidated the Egyptians into not entering the canyon. 

Could you imagine what it would have been like to be an Egyptian here? Right before this was all the plagues, frogs, and boils, and bugs, and a river of blood. All ten plagues culminating in the death of every first born that wasn’t covered by the blood of the Passover lamb. Then right after that, you start chasing this large group of civilians to slaughter them, and all of a sudden a pillar of fire appears blocking your path. I think their hair would have been standing on end. They may have had to have had a fair amount of convincing to follow their pharaoh into battle. Yet we don’t hear of any staying behind. What we read is 21-31. What we read is total and complete victory over the mighty army of the Egyptians. 

I think vs 31 is key here. God allowed all this to transpire so that they would believe in him more deeply. So they would trust him more fully. So they would rely upon him – rather than themselves – when things get hard. 

God brought his people into the crucible, and made them eye their certain doom. Made them stare in the face the worst thing they could possibly imagine, and brought them through it so that He and They could have a better relationship. 

Sometimes people have to truly reach bottom before they are willing to put their entire will in God’s hands. 

I once read a story of a lady. She was born into a troubled family. Her father was kind of absentee. She developed an eating disorder thinking she just wasn’t pretty enough, wasn’t thin enough for her father to love her. She started trying to find love any where she could. In the arms of men, anywhere. Finally at 18 for her birthday present she asked that her mom send her to a place to help her with her eating disorder. She got counselling, and like so many young people that need counseling, thought she should be a counselor. Went to college, and then grad school, struggling on and off again with her eating disorder. Finally graduated, got a decent job at a hospital counesling people, and had her own practice besides. Two years later, shes at work, and the polie show up at her door, and tell her that one of her patients had committed suicide. She was rocked. A week after the funeral, she received a notice that she was being sued. The family was blaming her for her patients death. The police came and collected all of her records. It took four years for her to get through the trial, and at the end of it all they decided she was negligent, that it was her fault this man had killed himself. She was devastated. She embraced her eating disorder with vigor. She was punishing her self. She became increasingly depressed. She lost her job, she lost her practice, she lost her liscense. She found the bottom of the bottle though almost every night. Then her husband left her. 

Finally it all came to a head, she bought a package of rat poison. She decided to kill her self and had forgone her drinking this night to this point. If she were going to take her life, she wanted to do so sober. That night, out of the blue one of her friends dropped by. They had sort of a short conversation, and at the end of it, her friend gave her a devotional with a gospel presentation in it. She figured reading couldn’t hurt, she opened it up and read a clear gospel presentation. She ended up weeping on her knees on the floor praying for Christ to save her. The next day she joined alcoholics anonymous, and has been sober for something like twenty years now. She said through this whole process her eating disorder sort of melted away. She developed relationships that help hold her accountable in all the areas shes weak in. But don’t miss this, it wasn’t until she entered the crucible, and was stuck in the fires of the forge that she reached a point where she was willing to have a relationship with God. 

As believers sometimes we still have to go in the crucible, and into the fire. But its because God is refining us. He’s growing us. He’s helping us to have a greater capacity to know him, to trust him, to depend upon him. 

But just like with moses, and the Israelites. God has the plan before he ever allowed them to enter the situation. He had planned out the battle field, shaped the canyon, the sea bottom, he had orchestrated their release from Egypt, and everything else we read tonight all to woo them back to him. 

It sort of reminds me of a rollercoaster. I read a thing one time by a gal who said the reason she loves rollercoasters is because of the thrill she gets – feeling like things are out of control, and yet being really safe. It strikes me that while we are in the crucible, we may feel like things are out of control. They are out of OUR control, but we are safe, because all things are in God’s control. 

Lets close in prayer.
