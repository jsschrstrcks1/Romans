## The Reliability of Scripture

The power of God is related to another subject too that I'd like to touch on tonight. I was talking to someone on Facebook this week, and they told me they believe in the God of the bible, but not the bible itself. He was sincere in this! I don't know how one can come to an understanding of who the God of the bible is, and why he's worth following without the bible itself, or without a direct encounter by the person that says these things with God.

If it's the latter, its also true that God is working and moving in their life. Any way, so I wanted to take a minute to make sure you guys are equipped to answer a person who says something like that.

If they truly do believe in God, and believe he has power, and if he did send a message about himself to earth so that we could know him, wouldn't he preserve his word so that ALL could know him?

Lets look at the facts. There are 10,000 complete manuscripts from the first through 14th centuries just in Greek. If you add in other languages there are about 25,000. If you add in partial copies, and individual pages, and so forth there are much much more than that. Something like 25,000 just in Greek, and I don't know how many if you added in the other languages of antiquity. So for easy math lets call it 50,000 ancient manuscripts and fragments.

Know how many discrepancies there are?

If the bible was fallible, and made by man, edited by monks, or anything like that, wouldn't we be able to see it in such a huge body of work? I hear all the time people say that third century monks re-wrote scripture to reflect what they wanted. But if that were true, then the second century, and first century works would be different. If that were true the fragments that go as far back as 7 years after the originals were written would be different than what came after the third century. But that's not what we have.

What we have, is 50,000 plus manuscripts and fragments, and papyri and scrolls that have a mere handful of differences across time. Most of which are spelling — an extra letter written or omitted in someone's name. Some of them are the same line written a second time. Very very few have a single line skipped. But none of them have any errors across all 50,000 documents that have anything to do with doctrine. They sing all the same notes like a perfect choir. Its like 50,000 emperor penguins marching in a line. All the same, all moving together. All singing the same song. They are the same.

But isn't that exactly what we would expect from the God the bible describes? A God that is powerful enough to direct a fish to swallow a man whole, and deliver him marked for life, but unharmed upon the shore of Nineveh? Isn't that what we would expect from the God that closed the mouths of the lions protecting Daniel? The God that directed at least two of every animal onto Noah's ark. That protected Noah and his family, inside the ark from a storm that destroyed the rest of the world? The God capable of feeding millions of people in the desert for decades. The God who hung the stars in the sky, the God who placed the earth in exactly the right spot for life to succeed on earth. Any closer to the sun and we'd die, and further and we'd die. If the moon were bigger, we'd die. If the moon were smaller, we'd die. If the moon were closer, we'd die, and further and we'd die. God designed life here on earth to work despite all the possible variables that could have stopped it.

## Creation, the Fall, and Restoration

God wanted a relationship with us and wanted us to choose to glorify him — and that was why he created us in the first place. He stamped Adam and Eve with his image, and set them loose in the world to do so. He made a world where lions laid down with lambs. Where sheep and predators could literally lay down together and not worry because God had designed everything perfect and the predator didn't need to eat the sheep to live. A world with no mosquitos. With no sandspurs, with no decay and death. No Alzheimer's or cancer. No Zika virus. God designed everything to be perfect and good, and to reflect himself.

As you know God only gave Adam and Eve a couple rules. Name stuff, multiply, and that tree over there, don't eat it.

Like any kid, they went straight to that tree, and disobeyed.

You've heard the phrase "shot heard round the world" right? Talking about the American Revolution, this group of British soldiers had basically just massacred 8 Americans that were not part of a militia for being part of a militia. Not too many weeks later, they were crossing a bridge, and the militia there decided they had had enough. Its not known who fired first, the British or Americans, but someone did and that moment has been recorded in history as the shot heard round the world. It — however — is quiet and sedate compared to the effects of the "bite" heard round the world. When Eve, and then Adam ate from the forbidden tree, God's image stamped across all of creation was shattered. Broken like a mirror hit with a baseball bat.

Part of our responsibility now as believers is to restore that. We help restore that in others through evangelism. In ourselves through prayer to, and worship of God. By reading the word he meticulously preserved and passed down to us. By fellowshipping with other believers. So in commanding us to do these things, he is commanding us to do the exact things that are restoring us.

Ultimately it won't be fully finished before we reach heaven. But think about this. God made things perfect. We broke it. God sent his son to die for us, and gave us the tools we needed to restore it, commanded we use them and yet still we neglect them, and sometimes reject some of them, so when those of us who've accepted Christ get to heaven — he still has to finish the repairs. God is so good, even when we don't deserve it.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastoral Heart Without a Textual Home — Genuine Gospel, Scattered Structure**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm, multi-topic pastoral message with a sincere gospel invitation and genuine love for the congregation, but one that never opens Genesis 21, moves through four unrelated subjects with no unifying textual argument, and cannot be evaluated as a sermon on its named text.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**FAIL — Evaluation terminates the scoring pathway.**

**Scopus Test**: The sermon is titled "Genesis 21 — My God Is So Great," but Genesis 21 is never quoted, referenced, read, or opened. The text does not appear. The sermon's content — Sarah's barrenness and healing (Genesis 21 is the occasion, not the text), manuscript reliability, the Fall, the gospel invitation, and a testimony about occult deliverance — has no traceable origin in Genesis 21's argument. The main point of Genesis 21 (the miraculous birth of Isaac, the fulfillment of God's covenant promise to Abraham, the expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael, and the covenant with Abimelech) is absent from the sermon.

**Authority Test**: The preacher does not stand under Genesis 21 as its herald because he never opens it. He preaches about the events it contains (Sarah's barrenness) only tangentially and does not derive his argument from the passage.

> "This sermon is not text-driven. The main point of the sermon does not arise from the main point of the passage. No score is assigned. The sermon must be rebuilt from the text, not decorated with it."

However, in the interest of full diagnostic value to the preacher, a partial evaluation of the sermon's actual content follows below — not as a score, but as pastoral feedback on what is present.

### 5. Score Breakdown

*No score assigned — Berean Gate failed. Narrative diagnostic follows.*

**Exposition & Hermeneutics**: Cannot be scored. Genesis 21 is not opened. The Romans road citations in the gospel section are used appropriately but are proclamatory pivots, not expository treatment of their respective texts.

**Theology & Doctrinal Integrity**: The theology present is largely sound. The affirmation of Scripture's preservation, the Fall as image-shattering, and the gospel invitation using substitutionary language ("Christ said daddy not this one" — colloquial but capturing penal substitution's logic) are all directionally faithful. No confessional contradictions. The restoration language ("Part of our responsibility now as believers is to restore that") risks semi-Pelagian framing — the believer restores the imago Dei through their own spiritual disciplines — and would benefit from clearer grounding in the Spirit's sanctifying work.

**Gospel Centrality & Force**: The gospel section is genuinely strong for its context. Romans 3:23, 6:23, 5:8, and 10:9–10 are deployed in their classic evangelistic sequence. The personal testimony of the friend rescued from the occult is vivid and compelling. The gospel is present, clear, and personally applied.

**Conscience & Applicatory Force**: The sin list ("Ever told a lie? Even a white one? Stolen? Said the Lord's name in vain? Sex outside of your marriage?") is specific and effective for an evangelistic address. It presses the conscience of the unbeliever. The section on believers restoring the imago Dei is too brief to be practically useful.

**Exhortation & Response**: Genuinely present. "Today is the day" is a real exhortation. The call is clear.

**Structure & Logical Flow**: The sermon's most significant weakness outside the textual failure. The structure is: opening observation about Sarah's barrenness → Facebook conversation about Bible reliability → manuscript evidence → Creation and the Fall → gospel invitation → occult testimony → gospel invitation again. These sections do not flow from one another. Each stands alone. The manuscript evidence section appears to belong to a different sermon (cf. Gen 1 above). There is no organizing idea that unifies the material.

**Illustration & Imagination**: The occult testimony is the sermon's most powerful illustration — specific, memorable, escalating, and gospel-centered. The "bite heard round the world" mirror analogy is vivid. The emperor penguin choir image for manuscript unity is charming and effective. The preacher has genuine illustrative instinct.

**Sermonic Force**: The oral voice is present and warm throughout. The tone is that of a pastor who loves his people and wants them to be saved. "Maybe that's you tonight" and "God is so much better than we deserve" are genuine pastoral moments.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is clear, specific, and personally applied.** The friend's testimony from occult bondage to gospel freedom is the kind of illustration that functions as a sermon within a sermon. It is vivid, escalating, personally witnessed, and lands exactly on the substitutionary work of Christ. The grandmother's wisdom and the deployment of the Romans Road is a genuine moment of pastoral evangelism that will be remembered.

2. **The sin list presses the conscience without abstraction.** "Ever told a lie? Even a white one? Stolen? Said the Lord's name in vain? Sex outside of your marriage?" addresses specific sins that a congregation member can immediately locate in their own life. This is Puritan specificity in vernacular form. It does not hide behind "all have sinned" without showing the congregation what that means in their actual week.

3. **Genuine pastoral warmth and accessibility.** The sermon has a voice that communicates love for the congregation. The opening observation about male infertility in biblical narrative is a small moment of pastoral humor that builds trust. The direct address throughout ("Maybe that's you tonight") is intimate and real. This is not performance — it is pastoral care.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Genesis 21 is not preached.** The sermon title claims Genesis 21, but the chapter is never opened. Isaac's birth — the covenant fulfillment that Genesis 21 exists to announce — is the culmination of Abraham's faith journey, the first visible installment of the promise "in your offspring shall all the nations be blessed," and the immediate typological precursor of the greater Son. None of this is present. The congregation received no word from Genesis 21.

2. **No organizing principle holds the sermon together.** The movement from barrenness → manuscript reliability → Creation/Fall → gospel invitation → occult testimony → gospel invitation is not a development — it is a collection. Each section is self-contained. A listener cannot summarize the sermon in one sentence because there is no one sentence to find. This is not a minor structural issue; it means the sermon's theology cannot accumulate or build to its conclusion.

3. **The restoration section risks misframing sanctification.** "Part of our responsibility now as believers is to restore that" — meaning the shattered imago Dei — implies that believers are the agents of their own restoration through spiritual disciplines. This is not false (means of grace are real and commanded), but it needs immediate grounding in the Spirit's agency. Without it, the passage reads as self-improvement with God's tools rather than the Spirit's transforming work through appointed means. The sentence "he still has to finish the repairs" in the next paragraph partially corrects this, but the framing is imprecise enough to mislead.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that the sermon's individual components — while each containing genuine value — are actually fragments of several different sermons. The manuscript reliability material belongs to a Sola Scriptura sermon. The Creation/Fall/Restoration material belongs to a gospel-foundations or systematic theology series. The occult testimony belongs to a sermon on spiritual warfare or the sufficiency of Christ. The evangelistic invitation is genuine evangelism. None of these are Genesis 21.

The preacher may also be unaware of the pattern across this corpus: both this sermon and Gen 1 (Two Ways to Know Truth) end with the identical closing gesture — "All of that starts with a relationship with God/Jesus Christ." The gospel invitation appears to function as a closing ritual rather than the culmination of the sermon's own argument. This is worth examining. The gospel should not be a closing formula — it should be what the sermon has been building toward all along.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal testimony of the friend rescued from the occult is compelling enough that it may create the impression the sermon has achieved something it hasn't. The story is emotionally powerful, the gospel application is sound, and the grandmother's witness is genuinely moving. A congregation member who was affected by this story might leave feeling they heard a great sermon. They heard a great testimony embedded in a sermon that never opened its text. The illustration's power masked the absence of a textual foundation.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Genesis 21 is one of the most theologically rich texts in the Abraham cycle. The birth of Isaac is the visible fulfillment of the promise made in Genesis 12, confirmed in Genesis 15, sealed in Genesis 17, and now realized against all human probability in the bodies of a 100-year-old man and a barren 90-year-old woman. The text carries its own "My God is so great" argument — the impossibility is the point. Paul uses this exact event in Romans 4:17–21 ("He is the father of us all... in the presence of the God in whom he believed, who gives life to the dead and calls into existence the things that do not exist") and in Galatians 4:22–31 (the typological contrast of Hagar and Sarah as the two covenants). The sermon's theme — God's power over impossibility — is exactly right. The text supports it. The preacher simply did not open the text to let it speak.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific)

1. **Open Genesis 21 and let the text supply the sermon's main point.** Read verses 1–7. The text's main point is the fulfillment of God's word against all human impossibility: "The LORD visited Sarah as he had said, and the LORD did to Sarah as he had promised." Preach this. Sarah's laughter (v.6) is the sermon's emotional center — the laugh of disbelief that became the laugh of joy. Build everything from here.

2. **Unify the existing material under the text's argument.** The manuscript reliability section can serve the sermon if framed as: "God kept his word to Sarah; he keeps his word in his written Word too." The Fall and restoration section fits under Isaac as the beginning of the covenant line that will produce the One who restores what Adam broke. The occult testimony fits under: "Our God has power over every pretender — he proved it to Abraham, he proved it to my friend." The material is usable; it needs a textual spine.

3. **Clarify the sanctification framing.** Replace "Part of our responsibility now as believers is to restore that" with language that roots the means of grace in the Spirit's work: "God has given us the tools of restoration — his Word, prayer, fellowship, worship — and his Spirit works through them to conform us to the image of his Son." This preserves the practical call while grounding it theologically.

4. **Move the gospel invitation from a closing formula to a textual culmination.** If the sermon is rebuilt around Isaac's birth, the gospel invitation becomes: "God kept his word to Abraham despite impossibility — he keeps his word to you. He promised that all who call on the name of the Lord will be saved. That promise is as certain as Isaac's birth." This makes the gospel the arrival point of the sermon's argument rather than its final housekeeping item.

5. **Develop the Isaac typology for believers.** Galatians 4:28 — "Now you, brothers, like Isaac, are children of promise." Every believer in the room is an Isaac — born not by human effort but by divine promise and power. This single move connects the sermon's theme (God's power over impossibility) to the congregation's identity in Christ, and provides the specific, differentiated application that the sermon currently lacks.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement

The sermon carries genuine pastoral love and a real urgency for the lost — the occult testimony and the grandmother's witness have authentic weight — but the Weight of Glory cannot settle on a sermon without a text, because it is through the proclaimed Word that God himself speaks; when the text is absent, the sermon must bear its own weight, and no preacher's heart, however warm, can substitute for the living and active Word that judges the thoughts and intentions of the heart.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This rubric is fully applicable to Genesis 21 — it is narrative, but narrative with explicit theological statement and covenantal significance. The rubric does not strain here. The problem is not that the rubric is poorly fitted to the genre; the problem is that the sermon did not engage the text the rubric was meant to evaluate. A rebuilt sermon on Genesis 21 would be well-served by this framework, with particular attention to the covenant fulfillment themes in Exposition, the Adam/Christ typology in Theology, and the Isaac-as-child-of-promise application in Conscience & Applicatory Force.
