# Habakkuk 2 - Look Among the Nations

**Habakkuk Series: Faith in the Dark - Week 2 of 6**
**April 26, 2026**
**Text:** Habakkuk 1:5-11
**Status:** Draft

---

Last week we heard Habakkuk's cry. Four verses - the rawest prayer in the Old Testament. *How long, O Lord? Why do you make me see this? The law is paralyzed. Justice goes forth perverted.* A prophet on his knees, watching the people of God act as though God does not exist, and demanding that God do something about it.

We said something important last week. We said that lament is not a failure of faith - it *is* faith. Faith that refuses to be satisfied with silence. Faith that keeps knocking. And we left Habakkuk on his knees, in the dark, waiting.

This week, God answers.

And I want to warn you before we read it: God's answer is more terrifying than His silence. Some of you spent this past week wishing God would speak into your situation - wishing He would break through, do something, move. Be careful what you ask for. Habakkuk asked God to act, and God told him what He was about to do, and it was worse than anything Habakkuk had imagined. Sometimes the silence is easier than the answer. Sometimes you pray for God to move and then God tells you how He's going to move and you wish you could take the prayer back.

That's where we are this morning. God opens His mouth. And everything gets harder.

---

Open your Bibles to Habakkuk chapter 1. We're picking up at verse 5. Follow along as I read verses 5 through 11.

*"Look among the nations, and see; wonder and be astounded. For I am doing a work in your days that you would not believe if told. For behold, I am raising up the Chaldeans, that bitter and hasty nation, who march through the breadth of the earth, to seize dwellings not their own. They are dreaded and fearsome; their justice and dignity go forth from themselves. Their horses are swifter than leopards, more fierce than the evening wolves; their horsemen press proudly on. Their horsemen come from afar; they fly like an eagle swift to devour. They all come for violence, all their faces forward. They gather captives like sand. At kings they scoff, and at rulers they laugh. They laugh at every fortress, for they pile up earth and take it. Then they sweep by like the wind and go on, guilty men, whose own might is their god."*

This is God's answer to Habakkuk's prayer.

Let that sink in. Habakkuk prayed: *Why don't you do something about the evil in Judah?* God said: *I am. I'm sending the Babylonians.*

---

Verse 5.

*"Look among the nations, and see; wonder and be astounded. For I am doing a work in your days that you would not believe if told."*

Four commands. *Look. See. Wonder. Be astounded.* God is telling Habakkuk to lift his eyes. Habakkuk has been looking *inward* - at Judah, at the violence, at the perverted courts, at the paralyzed law. God says: look *outward*. Look at the nations. Because what I am doing is bigger than what you are seeing. The answer to your prayer is not coming from inside Judah. It's coming from outside. From a direction you are not watching.

That's worth pausing on. Habakkuk expected God to fix the problem from the inside - to send a revival, to raise up a righteous king, to restore the courts, to put teeth back in the law. That would have been the comfortable answer. God could have done it that way. He'd done it before - Josiah's reforms, a generation earlier, were exactly that kind of inside job. But God doesn't repeat Himself on demand. He is not obligated to answer your prayer the way you prayed it. He is obligated to answer it according to His own wisdom, and His wisdom is not always yours.

*"I am doing a work in your days."* Present tense. Not *I will do* - *I am doing.* The work is already underway. Habakkuk hasn't seen it yet, but God has already set it in motion. The Babylonian army is already drilling. Nebuchadnezzar is already consolidating power. The siege engines are already being built. By the time God tells Habakkuk about it, the work is not a plan - it's a fact on the ground that just hasn't arrived yet.

*"That you would not believe if told."*

This is one of the most misused verses in the Bible. You've probably seen it on a t-shirt or heard it at a missions conference. *"Look among the nations - be amazed - God is doing something incredible!"* And it gets applied to church growth, or a new building campaign, or a revival breaking out in some country you've never been to. I've heard it quoted that way. You probably have too.

But read the context. The thing God is about to do is not a blessing. It is a judgment. God is about to raise up the most violent, ruthless, godless empire on the planet and aim it at His own people. That's the work Habakkuk would not believe. It's not good news. It is the most terrifying answer a prophet could receive.

When God says *"you would not believe it if told"* - He means it. The answer is so far outside what Habakkuk expected, so contrary to what seemed right, so apparently contradictory to God's own character, that even hearing it described would not be enough to make him believe it. And yet it's true. God is doing it. Right now. In your days.

Some of you are living in the middle of a *"you would not believe if told"* season. Something has happened in your life that you could not have predicted and still can't explain. The diagnosis. The betrayal. The phone call. The collapse of something you built with your own hands and thought God was blessing. And if someone had told you five years ago this is where you'd be, you would not have believed it. You'd have said: *Not me. Not this. God wouldn't do this to me.* But here you are.

And the hardest part is not the thing itself. The hardest part is that God is not surprised by it. He's not scrambling. He's not adjusting. He told Habakkuk: *I am doing a work.* Not: *A work is happening to you.* *I am doing it.* God is the subject of the verb. That changes everything - and we'll come back to it.

---

Verse 6.

*"For behold, I am raising up the Chaldeans, that bitter and hasty nation, who march through the breadth of the earth, to seize dwellings not their own."*

*"I am raising up."* Not: *the Chaldeans are rising.* Not: *History is taking its course.* *I am raising up.* God is the one who lifts empires and sets them down. Daniel 2:21 says it plainly: *"He changes times and seasons; he removes kings and sets up kings."* Every throne in the history of the world sits where God put it. Every empire that has ever risen, rose because God raised it. And every empire that has ever fallen, fell because God was done with it.

That includes the Chaldeans. The Chaldeans - or Babylonians, the terms overlap in the Old Testament - were a Semitic people from the lowlands of southern Mesopotamia. By the time Habakkuk is writing, roughly 610 to 605 BC, they are the emerging superpower. Nineveh fell in 612. Assyria is collapsing. Egypt is weakened. And into the vacuum steps Babylon under Nebuchadnezzar - young, aggressive, and ruthless.

God calls them *bitter and hasty.* Those two words are doing more work than they look like. *Bitter* means fierce, sharp, vicious - a people with no softness in them, no mercy, no restraint. *Hasty* means impetuous, headlong, rushing - they don't deliberate, they devour. They move fast and they move hard. You don't negotiate with the Babylonians. You don't appeal to their better nature. They don't have one.

*"Who march through the breadth of the earth, to seize dwellings not their own."* They take what doesn't belong to them. That's their operating principle. They cross borders, they seize cities, they occupy homes other people built, and they call it theirs. They are empire in its rawest form - power that exists to acquire, and acquires because it can.

And God says: *I am raising them up.*

That sentence should stop you cold. This is not God observing from a distance. This is God actively deploying a pagan, violent, idol-worshiping nation as His instrument. He is using the worst people on earth to accomplish His purposes among the best people on earth - or at least the people who were supposed to be the best. Judah had the covenant, the temple, the law, the prophets. Babylon had siege engines and cruelty. And God chose Babylon as His tool.

---

Verses 7 through 10. Let me walk through this portrait, because God paints it in detail.

*"They are dreaded and fearsome; their justice and dignity go forth from themselves."*

That last phrase is devastating. *Their justice and dignity go forth from themselves.* They make their own rules. They answer to no law but their own. They don't recognize the authority of any code, any covenant, any standard outside themselves. They are a law unto themselves. The Babylonian system of governance had no interest in the legal systems of conquered peoples. They imposed their own order on everything they touched. Their justice is self-derived. Their dignity is self-assigned.

Do you see what God is doing here? He is describing Babylon with words that echo what Habakkuk just said about Judah. Back in verse 4, Habakkuk's complaint was that *"justice goes forth perverted"* - the law was being twisted by wicked people in Judah. Now God says: you think perverted justice is bad? Wait until you meet a nation whose justice proceeds entirely from itself. You have corrupt judges. They have no judges at all - only power. You complained about twisted law. They don't even pretend to have one.

*"Their horses are swifter than leopards, more fierce than the evening wolves."*

An evening wolf is a wolf that hasn't eaten all day. It hunts at dusk, desperate and vicious. That's the image. These are not soldiers marching in formation. These are predators.

*"Their horsemen press proudly on. Their horsemen come from afar; they fly like an eagle swift to devour."*

The eagle here - the Hebrew is *nesher* - is most likely not the noble bird you're picturing. The *nesher* in Palestine was a griffon vulture. A scavenger. A bird that circles over the dying and descends to feed. That's the picture God is painting. The Babylonian cavalry is not a noble army on a righteous mission. It is a vulture descending on a carcass.

*"They all come for violence, all their faces forward."*

Every single one of them. There is no dissent in the ranks. No mercy. No reluctance. Every face is set forward. Every soldier is moving in one direction. They come for violence the way a river comes for the sea - it's what they do. It's the only thing they do.

*"They gather captives like sand."*

Like sand. Not like prisoners of war with names and families and futures. Like sand. Uncountable. Indistinguishable. The Babylonians practiced mass deportation - uprooting entire populations, relocating them hundreds of miles from home, stripping them of language and culture and identity. They gathered *captives like sand* because they did not see captives as people. They saw them as material.

*"At kings they scoff, and at rulers they laugh. They laugh at every fortress, for they pile up earth and take it."*

They are not impressed by your defenses. Your walls, your armies, your alliances, your treaties - they laugh at all of it. *They pile up earth and take it* - that's a siege ramp. They look at your fortress and they start shoveling dirt until they can walk over the top of your wall. It's not clever. It's not elegant. It's brute force applied until the obstacle is gone. And it works. Every time.

---

Verse 11.

*"Then they sweep by like the wind and go on, guilty men, whose own might is their god."*

Two things in this verse, and both of them matter.

First: *they sweep by like the wind and go on.* The Babylonians don't stay. They conquer and they move. They are a storm, not a settlement. They pass through. They leave wreckage. And they keep going to the next nation, the next city, the next wall to laugh at. They are not building a civilization. They are consuming one after another.

Second - and this is the theological hinge of the whole passage: *guilty men, whose own might is their god.*

God calls them guilty. Right here. In the same breath that He says *"I am raising them up,"* He says *"guilty men."* He is not confused about who they are. He is not endorsing their character. He is not saying they are righteous, or justified, or on the right side of history. They are guilty. Their might is their god - they worship their own power, their own strength, their own military machine. They are idolaters of the most basic kind. And God is using them anyway.

Hold those two truths together, because the whole book depends on it:

*God is raising up the Chaldeans.* And *the Chaldeans are guilty.*

God is sovereign over them. And they are responsible for what they do. Both. At the same time. Not one canceling the other. God is not guilty because He uses them. They are not innocent because God sent them. The sovereignty of God and the guilt of man exist simultaneously, and neither one diminishes the other.

This is the doctrine that our confession states plainly. The 1689 London Baptist Confession, chapter 5, paragraph 4:

*"The almighty power, unsearchable wisdom, and infinite goodness of God so far manifest themselves in His providence that His determinate counsel extends itself even to the first fall, and all other sinful actions both of angels and men; and that not by a bare permission, but such as has joined with it a most wise and powerful bounding, and otherwise ordering and governing of them, in a manifold dispensation to His most holy ends; yet so, as the sinfulness of their acts proceeds only from the creatures, and not from God, who being most holy and righteous, neither is nor can be the author or approver of sin."*

Listen to that carefully. God's counsel *extends itself* to sinful actions - not by bare permission, not by stepping aside and letting it happen, but by *ordering and governing them to His most holy ends.* And yet the sinfulness *proceeds only from the creatures, and not from God.* God is sovereign over the sin. The sinner is responsible for the sin. God is not the author of evil. The creature is. Both statements are true. Both must be held.

That is what Habakkuk 1:5-11 is teaching. God raises up the Chaldeans - that's sovereignty. The Chaldeans are guilty men whose own might is their god - that's human responsibility. And God is not apologizing for either half of the equation.

---

This pattern is all over the Bible. Let me show you.

Go back to Genesis 50. Joseph, standing in front of the brothers who sold him into slavery, who threw him in a pit, who lied to their father and said he was dead. Twenty years of separation. Slavery in Egypt. A false accusation that put him in prison for years. And Joseph says to the men who did it: *"As for you, you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to bring it about that many people should be kept alive, as they are today"* (Genesis 50:20).

Two subjects. Two intentions. One event. *You meant evil.* Real evil. Real guilt. Joseph doesn't minimize what they did. He doesn't say it wasn't that bad, or they were just confused, or it all worked out so who cares. He says: you meant evil. And then: *God meant it for good.* God was working through the very act of evil to accomplish the salvation of a nation. Not in spite of their sin. *Through* it. God's purpose ran through the sin like a river through a canyon - the canyon didn't create the river, but the river used the canyon.

That's what's happening in Habakkuk. God is using the Babylonians the way He used Joseph's brothers. They are guilty. And God is using their guilt to accomplish His purposes.

Or go to Isaiah 10. God calls Assyria *"the rod of my anger"* - He sends Assyria against Israel as an instrument of judgment. But then God turns around and says: *"Shall the axe boast over him who hews with it, or the saw magnify itself against him who wields it?"* (Isaiah 10:15). God used Assyria. But Assyria had its own wicked motives, and God held them accountable for those motives.

Or go further - to Isaiah 44 and 45. God names Cyrus, the Persian king, by name - a century before Cyrus is born - and calls him *"my shepherd"* and *"my anointed."* A pagan king. A man who did not know the God of Israel. And God says: *I will use him to rebuild my city and set my exiles free.* God uses pagan kings like tools in a carpenter's hand. The tool doesn't know the carpenter's plan. The tool doesn't have to be righteous to be useful. It just has to be where the carpenter puts it.

And that leads us to the place where this doctrine reaches its fullest and most staggering expression.

---

The cross.

Acts 2:23. Peter, preaching at Pentecost, says this about the death of Jesus: *"This Jesus, delivered up according to the definite plan and foreknowledge of God, you crucified and killed by the hands of lawless men."*

Read that sentence again. Two clauses. *Delivered up according to the definite plan and foreknowledge of God.* That's sovereignty. God planned it. Before the foundation of the world, the Lamb was slain. Before Judas was born, the betrayal was written. Before Pilate took office, the verdict was sealed. The cross was not an accident. It was not a backup plan. It was not God reacting to human wickedness. It was the *definite plan* of God.

And: *You crucified and killed by the hands of lawless men.* That's human responsibility. Real guilt. Real sin. Lawless men drove the nails. Lawless men mocked Him. Lawless men gambled for His clothing while He bled. Peter does not say: *Well, it was God's plan, so nobody's really at fault.* He says: *You did this. You are guilty. And God planned it.*

Acts 4:27-28 makes it even more explicit. The early church prays: *"For truly in this city there were gathered together against your holy servant Jesus, whom you anointed, both Herod and Pontius Pilate, along with the Gentiles and the peoples of Israel, to do whatever your hand and your plan had predestined to take place."*

Herod. Pilate. The Gentile soldiers. The Jewish crowd. Four groups. All guilty. All acting out of their own wicked motives - jealousy, cowardice, cruelty, mob fury. And all of them doing *whatever God's hand and God's plan had predestined to take place.*

The cross of Jesus Christ is the supreme instance of what Habakkuk 1:5-11 is teaching. God used the most wicked act in the history of the world - the murder of His own Son - to accomplish the most glorious purpose in the history of the world - the salvation of sinners. The hands that crucified Christ were guilty. The plan that sent Christ to the cross was God's. And the result of that convergence is the gospel.

If God can take the murder of His Son and turn it into the salvation of the world, then God can take the Babylonians and use them to discipline His people. And if God can take the Babylonians and use them to discipline His people, then God can take the worst thing that has ever happened to you and bend it toward a purpose you cannot yet see.

Romans 8:28 - *"And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose."* All things. Not some things. Not the pleasant things. Not the things that make sense. All things. Including the Babylonian invasion. Including the cross. Including the thing in your life that you would not have believed if told.

---

But I want to be careful here, because this doctrine can be mishandled, and when it is, it does damage.

This is not a doctrine you throw at someone who is bleeding. You don't walk up to a person in the wreckage of their life and say *"God is sovereign, cheer up."* That's not pastoral care. That's cruelty with a Bible verse attached. David Platt said something I've been thinking about all week: *"Short-term circumstances provide a poor measurement of the long-term character of God."* That's right. But you have to let people live in the short-term before you hand them the long-term. Habakkuk is going to get to *"Yet I will rejoice in the LORD"* by the end of this book. But he's not there yet. He's in chapter 1. And so are some of you.

The doctrine of God's sovereignty over evil is the ground you stand on. It is not a club you swing at the hurting. It's not a slogan. It's not a bumper sticker. It is a reality that holds you up when the floor drops out - but only if you learned to stand on it before the floor dropped. That's why we preach it now. Not because it solves your problem this week. Because it will hold you when the problem comes that you can't solve.

And let me say one more thing about how God uses hard providences, because the Bible draws a line here that matters.

Hebrews 12:5-6: *"My son, do not regard lightly the discipline of the Lord, nor be weary when reproved by him. For the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and chastises every son whom he receives."*

There is a difference between discipline and wrath. Discipline is what a father does to a child he loves. Wrath is what a judge does to a criminal he condemns. They look similar from the outside. Both hurt. Both involve suffering. But they come from different places and they go to different ends. Discipline is corrective - it aims at restoration. Wrath is punitive - it aims at justice. Discipline says: *I love you too much to leave you where you are.* Wrath says: *You have earned what is coming.*

In Habakkuk, Judah receives discipline. The Babylonians bring it - but God sends it, and He sends it because He loves His people and will not let them stay in their sin. The Babylonians themselves will receive wrath. God will hold Babylon accountable for every cruelty, every atrocity, every captive gathered like sand. That's what the five woes in chapter 2 are about - we'll get there in a few weeks. But the point for this morning is: if you belong to God, the hard thing in your life is not wrath. It is discipline. And discipline, however painful, is proof that you are His child, not evidence that He has abandoned you.

Hebrews 12:11: *"For the moment all discipline seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained by it."*

*Later.* Not now. Later. Right now it hurts. Right now it doesn't make sense. Right now it feels like God is using the worst possible instrument to do the worst possible thing to you. Welcome to Habakkuk 1:5-11. That's exactly what the prophet is hearing. And the prophet is about to argue back - that's next week. But the argument doesn't change the answer. The Babylonians are coming. And God is the one sending them.

---

So what do you do with this? What do you do when God's answer to your prayer is harder than His silence?

Let me speak to three groups of people in this room.

First - some of you are in the middle of something that looks like Babylon. Not a literal army, but something that has marched into your life uninvited, that is bitter and hasty, that seizes things that are not its own. A crisis. A loss. A season that feels like occupation. And you have been praying for God to remove it. You have been asking: *How long?* And the answer that is forming - the answer you're beginning to suspect - is that God is not going to remove it. He sent it. Not because He's cruel. Because He's working. And the work He is doing is bigger than the work you asked for.

I am not telling you to like it. Habakkuk didn't like it. He's about to file a second complaint in the next seven verses - a complaint even more intense than the first. But I am telling you: the thing that has invaded your life has not escaped God's notice. It has not escaped God's control. And it has not escaped God's purpose. *"I am doing a work in your days."* If you belong to Him, the Babylonians in your life are not wrath. They are discipline. They hurt. But they are going somewhere. And the somewhere is restoration, not destruction.

Second - some of you are watching someone else go through it. You're watching a friend, a family member, a brother or sister in this church walk through something terrible, and you don't know what to say. Good. Don't say too much. Job's friends were at their best when they sat in silence for seven days. They became useless the moment they opened their mouths. Sometimes the most pastoral thing you can do is sit with someone in the rubble and not explain it. Don't quote Romans 8:28 at a person who is weeping. Sit with them. Weep with them. Romans 12:15 - *"Weep with those who weep."* That verse comes before *"Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good."* The weeping comes first. The theology comes later. And often the theology comes not from your mouth but from theirs - when they are ready, when the Spirit brings it, when the ground has settled enough to build on.

Third - some of you are the Babylonians.

I don't mean that the way it sounds. I mean: some of you are being used by God in someone else's story, and you don't know it. You're the boss who had to deliver the hard news. You're the doctor who had to read the results. You're the friend who had to say the thing nobody wanted to hear. You're the parent who had to set the boundary that your child hates you for. And you feel guilty about it - or at least conflicted. You didn't want to be the instrument of someone's pain. But sometimes God puts you in that position. The question is not whether you can avoid being used. The question is whether you will be faithful in how you're used - and whether you will trust that God's purposes are bigger than your discomfort.

The Babylonians didn't know they were serving God's purposes. You do. That's the difference. You have the privilege of being a conscious instrument in the hand of a sovereign God. Handle it with fear. Handle it with humility. Handle it with kindness. But handle it - because the alternative is to refuse the assignment, and that is a worse kind of unfaithfulness.

---

If you are here this morning and you do not know Christ, I want to be direct with you.

Everything I have said this morning assumes a relationship with God that you may not have. I've talked about discipline, and discipline is for children. I've talked about God working all things together for good, and that promise is for *"those who love God, who are called according to his purpose."* If you don't love God - if you've never turned from your sin and trusted in Christ - then the hard things in your life are not discipline. They are a preview. They are a taste of what it looks like to live in a universe ruled by a holy God while standing outside His mercy.

That is not where you want to be. Habakkuk 1:11 says the Chaldeans are *"guilty men, whose own might is their god."* That is a description of every person who has ever lived without Christ. Your own might is your god. Your intelligence. Your effort. Your career. Your reputation. Your ability to hold it together. And it is not enough. It will never be enough. The Babylonians looked unstoppable - the greatest military machine on earth - and within a century they were gone. Conquered by the Persians. Reduced to rubble. Their own might failed them. Yours will too.

But here is the good news - and it's the same good news every week, because there is only one gospel. The God who sent the Babylonians also sent His Son. The God who used the wicked hands of Herod and Pilate and the Roman soldiers to nail Christ to a cross - that same God was accomplishing your salvation through the very act that looked like the worst defeat in history. The cross looked like Rome winning. It was God winning. The cross looked like death. It was the death of death. The cross looked like the end of the story. It was the beginning.

Christ bore the penalty for sin. Not His own sin - He had none. Yours. Mine. The full weight of every act of violence Habakkuk ever saw, every perversion of justice, every *chamas* - every act of cruelty the strong have ever inflicted on the weak - all of it was laid on Him. And He absorbed it. And He died under it. And three days later He walked out of the tomb, because death could not hold the one man who had never deserved it.

That is the gospel. And it is available to you this morning. Not because you've earned it. Not because you've cleaned up your life. Not because you've made yourself presentable. But because God, in His sovereign purpose, has brought you into this room on this Sunday to hear this word. *"I am doing a work in your days that you would not believe if told."* What if the work God is doing in your days - right now, this morning - is saving your soul?

Romans 10:9 - *"If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*

Come talk to me after the service. The door is open.

---

We're two weeks into this series. Habakkuk has cried out, and God has answered, and the answer is terrifying. Next week, Habakkuk fires back. He has a second complaint - and it's sharper than the first. He says, in effect: *God, I know You're holy. I know You're sovereign. But how can You use people who are worse than us to judge us? How can Your solution to injustice be more injustice?*

That's chapter 1, verses 12 through chapter 2, verse 1. The sermon is called *I Will Take My Stand.* If you want to read ahead, brace yourself again. Habakkuk is not done arguing. And God is not done answering.

For this week, take two things with you.

One: God hears. He heard Habakkuk. He answered Habakkuk. The answer was not what Habakkuk wanted, but it was an answer. God did not leave the prophet in silence. If you have been praying and hearing nothing - keep praying. The silence does not mean God is absent. It may mean God's answer is still forming. Or it may mean His answer has already arrived and you haven't recognized it yet, because it came in a shape you didn't expect.

Two: God is working. Even when His work looks like judgment. Even when His instruments are bitter and hasty. Even when the cavalry on the horizon looks like the enemy. God is the subject of the verb. *"I am doing a work."* He is doing it. Not watching it happen. Doing it. And the God who did His greatest work through the worst act in human history - the murder of His own Son - is more than capable of doing something redemptive through the worst season of yours.

You may not believe it if told. Believe it anyway. That's faith. That's Habakkuk. That's the life we're called to - trusting a God whose ways we cannot trace, whose instruments we would not choose, and whose purposes we cannot yet see.

*Pray.*
