# Habakkuk 4 - The Righteous Shall Live by Faith

**Habakkuk Series: Faith in the Dark - Week 4 of 6**
**May 10, 2026**
**Text:** Habakkuk 2:2-5
**Status:** Draft

---

Last week, Habakkuk took his stand. He climbed the watchtower, planted his feet, and said: *I will look out to see what he will say to me.* He had no guarantee that God would answer. He had no promise that the silence would break. He turned anyway - not because the confusion had cleared, but because the God underneath him had not moved.

This week, God speaks.

And what He says is not the explanation Habakkuk wanted. It is not a defense of His methods. It is not a point-by-point answer to the complaint about Babylon. God does not explain. God declares. And the declaration He makes from that watchtower is the most quoted sentence in the Old Testament - seven words in Hebrew that will echo through Paul, through Hebrews, through a monk on a staircase in Rome, through the Reformation, and into this room this morning.

Open your Bibles to Habakkuk chapter 2. We are reading verses 2 through 5.

*"And the LORD answered me: 'Write the vision; make it plain on tablets, so he may run who reads it. For still the vision awaits its appointed time; it hastens to the end — it will not lie. If it seems slow, wait for it; it will surely come; it will not delay. Behold, his soul is puffed up; it is not upright within him, but the righteous shall live by his faith. Moreover, wine is a traitor, an arrogant man who is never at rest. His greed is as wide as Sheol; like death he has never enough. He gathers for himself all nations and collects as plunder all peoples.'"*

---

God does not answer Habakkuk with an explanation of His ways. He answers with a command for how to live: *the righteous shall live by faith.*

That is the spine of this sermon. That is the spine of this book. And that sentence - born in a watchtower, carried by Paul into three epistles, read by Martin Luther on a staircase - that sentence broke open the Reformation and reshaped the Christian world.

But before we hear it the way Paul heard it, we need to hear it the way Habakkuk heard it. In context. On the ground. In the dark.

---

## The Vision Written Plain (2:2-3)

Verse 2.

*"Write the vision; make it plain on tablets, so he may run who reads it."*

God tells Habakkuk to write. Not to ponder. Not to meditate privately. Write it down. Make it plain. Make it so clear that a man running past the tablet can read it without stopping.

Spurgeon caught the force of this: *"It is not for us to bury the gospel under a mass of fine words, but to set it forth in the simplest and clearest possible language; for it is not the power of human words that God blesses, but the truth itself as it is applied to the heart by his Spirit."*

The vision is not hidden. It is not coded. It is not reserved for scholars or prophets or theologians. God writes it plain - on tablets, in public, where the ordinary person can see it. That is how God has always communicated His decisive word. He does not whisper it to the elite. He writes it where the runner can read it.

And you are the runner. This vision was not written for Habakkuk alone. It was written so that you would read it, believe it, and carry it with you when you leave this room.

And what is the vision? In Habakkuk's immediate context, it is God's declaration about what is coming: Babylon will rise, Babylon will devour - and Babylon will fall. The five woes of chapter 2 are coming next week. Babylon's judgment is certain. But before God announces the woes, He announces the principle that will outlast every empire that ever rises and falls: *the righteous shall live by faith.* That is the vision. Not a geopolitical forecast. A way of life.

---

Verse 3.

*"For still the vision awaits its appointed time; it hastens to the end — it will not lie. If it seems slow, wait for it; it will surely come; it will not delay."*

The vision has a schedule. And the schedule is not ours.

Spurgeon addressed the apparent contradiction in this verse: *"Is that a contradiction, 'Though it tarry... it will not tarry'? No: to us, it appears to tarry; but, in God's way of reckoning, it does not really tarry. To our impatient spirits, it seems long in coming; but God knows that it will not be a moment beyond the appointed time."*

This is the hardest thing about faith. Not believing that God exists. Not believing that God is good. Believing that God's timing is right when everything in you screams that He is late. The vision is not late. You are early. Your clock and God's clock are not synchronized - and His is the one that keeps perfect time.

Peter will say it centuries later: *"The Lord is not slow to fulfill his promise as some count slowness, but is patient toward you, not wishing that any should perish, but that all should reach repentance"* (2 Peter 3:9). What feels like delay to you is patience toward someone else. What feels like silence is sovereignty working on a schedule you cannot see.

Some of you have been waiting. For the marriage to heal. For the diagnosis to change. For the prodigal to come home. For the prayer to be answered. And the silence has been so long that you have started to wonder whether God heard you at all.

He heard you. The vision awaits its appointed time. It will not lie. It will surely come. And it will not be one moment late.

The psalmist knew this waiting: *"Wait for the LORD; be strong, and let your heart take courage; wait for the LORD!"* (Psalm 27:14). Two commands to wait. One command to take courage. That is the ratio. Waiting is the posture. Courage is what God gives you while you hold it.

Waiting is not passive. Habakkuk did not sit down in a corner after God told him to wait. He wrote the vision. He made it plain. He proclaimed it. Waiting, in the biblical vocabulary, is not the absence of action - it is action aimed at the right thing. You wait for God by staying in the Word. You wait for God by gathering with this body on Sunday. You wait for God by praying when the ceiling feels like iron and the answer has not come. You wait for God by refusing to manufacture your own resolution when His has not arrived.

The person who stops waiting is the person who starts building. Building their own solution. Building their own timeline. Building their own version of the vision that doesn't require faith. And that is exactly what the next verse describes.

God does not answer Habakkuk with an explanation of His ways. He answers with a command for how to live: *the righteous shall live by faith.*

---

## The Righteous Shall Live by Faith (2:4)

Now we arrive at the center.

Verse 4.

*"Behold, his soul is puffed up; it is not upright within him, but the righteous shall live by his faith."*

Before God tells you how the righteous live, He tells you how the proud die.

---

*"His soul is puffed up; it is not upright within him."*

The proud soul. This is the contrast. This is Babylon's portrait - and it is the default portrait of every human heart that has not been broken by grace.

The proud soul trusts itself. Its power. Its analysis. Its record. Its net - and we talked about the net last week. The Nelson Study Bible puts the contrast plainly: *"A proud person relies on self, power, position, and accomplishment; a righteous person relies on the Lord."*

The proud soul is puffed up - the Hebrew carries the image of something swollen, bloated, distorted. Not solid. Not stable. Inflated. The proud soul looks large but is hollow. It looks strong but is fragile. It looks self-sufficient but is desperate. And it is not upright - it is crooked at the core. The foundation is wrong, and everything built on it leans.

That is the diagnosis. Not of Babylon alone. Of every life that runs on self-reliance. Every career built on performance. Every identity anchored in achievement. Every prayer life that died because you decided you could handle it yourself. The proud soul is puffed up. It is not upright. And it will not stand when the weight comes down.

That's the problem. Now here's the answer.

Some of you are the most competent people in every room you walk into. You fix things. You manage things. You hold things together - at work, at home, in this church. And you have built your entire identity on that competence. You do not need help. You do not ask for help. You have not asked God for help in so long that prayer feels like a formality. You show up on Sunday because it is the right thing to do, not because you are desperate for the Word. That is the proud soul. It does not look arrogant from the outside. It looks responsible. It looks strong. But the foundation is wrong - and God is about to name the alternative.

---

So what is this faith that separates the righteous from the proud?

*"The righteous shall live by his faith."*

The Hebrew word is *emunah*. And it does not mean what most people think it means.

*Emunah* does not mean intellectual agreement. It does not mean doctrinal correctness. It does not mean a feeling of confidence. The root of this word - *aman* - is used in the Old Testament to describe the supporting posts of a doorframe. The weight-bearing structure that holds the door in place. That is the image underneath the word. Faith is not a mood. Faith is a doorpost. It is the thing you lean on when your legs give out and the doorframe is the only thing between you and the floor.

Don't miss this. This is where everything turns.

The righteous do not live by explanations. They do not live by sight. They do not live by their own strength. They live by *emunah* - by leaning their full weight on the character and promises of God the way a man leans on a doorframe when he cannot stand on his own. That is what faith is. Weight-bearing trust. The kind that holds you up when nothing else will.

Habakkuk has waded as far as reason will take him. He asked *How long?* He heard the terrifying answer. He asked *How can You?* He got no explanation. Reason has run out. And now God says: the righteous shall live by faith. Not by answers. Not by understanding. Not by having it figured out. By faith. Lean on Me. I will hold.

Habakkuk does not yet explain how God can declare sinners righteous - but he establishes the ground Paul will later stand on: the righteous do not live by what they are in themselves, but by trusting what God provides. If God's eyes are too pure to look on evil - and Habakkuk said exactly that in chapter 1, verse 13 - and yet He saves people by faith, then the righteousness that gives us standing before this holy God cannot be our own. It must come from somewhere else. It must be given. And that is exactly what Paul will unfold in Romans. But the seed is here. In Habakkuk. In the seventh century before Christ.

Some of you know this. You could explain emunah to your small group. You could draw the line from Habakkuk to Romans on a whiteboard. And you have never leaned. You have studied faith without exercising it. You have admired the doorpost without throwing your weight on it. This verse is not a concept for you to master. It is a command for you to obey: *live by faith.* Not think about faith. Live by it.

That's what faith is. Now watch what Scripture does with it.

---

And this one sentence does not stay in Habakkuk. It echoes across the entire New Testament.

Paul quotes Habakkuk 2:4 three times. Three different letters. Three different angles on the same diamond. And together, they tell the full story of what faith does.

**Romans 1:17** - *"For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith for faith, as it is written, 'The righteous shall live by faith.'"* Here the emphasis falls on *life*. Faith gives you life. Faith rescues you from wrath. Faith is how you survive the judgment of God - not by your own righteousness but by the righteousness God reveals and gives.

**Galatians 3:11** - *"Now it is evident that no one is justified before God by the law, for 'The righteous shall live by faith.'"* Here the emphasis falls on *justification*. The law cannot do it. Your obedience cannot do it. Your record cannot do it. Sola fide. Faith alone.

**Hebrews 10:38** - *"But my righteous one shall live by faith, and if he shrinks back, my soul has no pleasure in him."* Here the emphasis falls on *perseverance*. Faith is not a one-time decision. It is a way of life. The righteous shall live by faith - today, tomorrow, next year, in the dark, in the silence, in the waiting. And if he shrinks back - if he abandons the watchtower, if he stops leaning on the doorpost - God has no pleasure in him.

Three uses. One message. You don't live by what you do. You live by trusting what God gives.

Life. Justification. Perseverance. Faith starts you. Faith declares you righteous. Faith keeps you standing when the night is long and the answer has not come. From faith to faith - as Paul says in Romans 1:17. Faith is not a one-time event. It is the mode of the righteous life. It is the air you breathe from the moment you first trust Christ until the moment you see Him face to face.

Spurgeon saw the thread and named it: *"This grand text was quoted by Paul when he wrote his Epistles to the Romans, the Galatians, and the Hebrews. It proves that Old Testament saints understood New Testament life. David and Abraham lived by faith, even as Paul and Peter and the other apostles did."*

Same gospel. Same faith. Two testaments. No gap.

---

## Luther and the Staircase

Here's the thing. This sentence did not stay on a tablet in ancient Judah. It did not stay in Paul's letters. Fifteen centuries after Paul, it set fire to the Western world.

Tradition holds that around 1510, a young Augustinian monk named Martin Luther traveled to Rome. He was tormented by the holiness of God and the weight of his own sin. He climbed the Scala Sancta - the so-called Holy Stairs - on his knees, reciting a prayer on every step, trying to earn merit, trying to reduce his time in purgatory. Step by step. Prayer by prayer. Earning, earning, earning.

And somewhere on those stairs, a sentence he had read a thousand times in Paul's letter to the Romans struck his conscience with a force he had never felt before: *"The just shall live by faith."*

Not by climbing. Not by earning. Not by the blistered knees of a monk on a staircase in Rome. By faith.

He stopped. He stood up. He walked back down the stairs.

Think about what that means. He stopped climbing. He stopped earning. He stopped trying to add one more prayer to the pile and hoping it would be enough. He stood up - not in defiance but in surrender. He surrendered the project of making himself righteous. He received what had already been given.

He went home to Wittenberg. Within a few years, the ninety-five theses were nailed to the church door. Within a decade, the Reformation had split the Western church and reshaped the Christian world. And the verse that started it was not Romans 1:17 - it was Habakkuk 2:4, the verse Paul was quoting.

Luther's conversion on those stairs was not from paganism to Christianity. It was from religion to faith. From climbing to receiving. From performance to promise. And that is the same conversion Habakkuk 2:4 demands of every person in this room - including the ones who have been Christians for thirty years. You can climb stairs your whole life. Or you can stand up and receive what was already given.

Habakkuk heard it in a watchtower. Luther heard it on a staircase. And both of them realized the same thing: God is not asking for your effort. He is calling for your faith.

John Bunyan - the tinker from Bedford, the man who wrote *Pilgrim's Progress* from a prison cell - described what happens when this sentence lands on a soul:

*"One day, as I was passing into a field, this sentence fell upon my soul: 'Your righteousness is in heaven.' And I thought with all I saw, with the eyes of my soul, Jesus Christ at God's right hand. 'There,' I say, 'was my righteousness.' So that wherever I was, or whatever I was doing, God could not say of me, 'He lacks righteousness,' for that was just before Him."*

Your righteousness is in heaven. Not in your performance. Not in your prayer life. Not in your church attendance. Not in how well you managed the crisis or held the family together or kept up the appearance of faith when you felt nothing inside. Your righteousness is in heaven - seated at the right hand of the Father. And wherever you are, whatever you are doing, God cannot say of you, *"He lacks righteousness"* - because it is right there before Him. In Christ.

Our confession - the 1689 London Baptist Confession, chapter 11, paragraph 1 - says it with precision:

*"Those whom God effectually calleth, he also freely justifieth, not by infusing righteousness into them, but by pardoning their sins, and by accounting and accepting their persons as righteous; not for anything wrought in them, or done by them, but for Christ's sake alone."*

Not infused. Imputed. Not wrought in us. Credited to us. For Christ's sake alone. That is what Habakkuk 2:4 means. That is what Luther grasped on the staircase. That is what Bunyan saw with the eyes of his soul in a field outside Bedford. And that is what you are being offered this morning.

Some of you can teach this doctrine. You know the word *imputation*. You could explain it on a whiteboard. And you do not believe it about yourself. You believe it for others - for the new convert, for the broken addict, for the prodigal who just came home. But not for you. Because you know what you've done. You know what you're still doing. You know the gap between what you profess on Sunday and what you live on Tuesday, and the gap feels too wide for even imputed righteousness to cover.

Hear Bunyan again: *"Wherever I was, or whatever I was doing, God could not say of me, 'He lacks righteousness,' for that was just before Him."* Wherever you are. Whatever you are doing. God cannot say you lack righteousness - because your righteousness is not in your hands. It is in heaven. At the right hand of the Father. In Christ. And Christ is not going anywhere.

---

## The Proud Soul's Appetite (2:5)

And then God draws the contrast one more time - in case you missed it.

Verse 5.

*"Moreover, wine is a traitor, an arrogant man who is never at rest. His greed is as wide as Sheol; like death he has never enough. He gathers for himself all nations and collects as plunder all peoples."*

God names wine for a reason. Wine intoxicates. It distorts the drinker's self-assessment - the drunk man thinks he is steady when he is falling. That is the proud soul's condition. He thinks he is upright. He thinks he is strong. He thinks he is in control. And he is swaying. Self-reliance is an intoxicant. It makes you feel large while it makes you hollow.

The proud soul is never satisfied. His appetite is as wide as the grave - and like the grave, he never says *enough*. He devours nations. He collects peoples like plunder. He drinks and is never full. He conquers and is never at peace.

This is Babylon. But it is also every life built on anything other than faith. The career that was supposed to be enough - but you needed the next promotion. The house that was supposed to be enough - but you needed the renovation. The approval that was supposed to be enough - but you needed it again this morning. The proud soul's appetite has no floor. It is Sheol-wide. It swallows everything and digests nothing.

The righteous live by faith. The proud live by appetite. And the appetite never, ever stops.

And it is not only the appetite for money or power. Some of you have an appetite for approval. For validation. For someone - anyone - to say *you are enough.* You have swallowed compliment after compliment and they pass through you like water. You are never full. Because the approval of people is Sheol-wide. It cannot satisfy. Only the declaration of God can fill that hole - and His declaration is this: *the righteous shall live by faith.* Not by approval. Not by performance. Not by being needed. By faith. That is the only meal that satisfies.

Next week, God pronounces five woes on that appetite. Five judgments on the empire that swallowed nations and worshiped its own net. But this week, the question is not about Babylon. It is about you. Which life are you living? The inflated life that is never satisfied - or the faith-leaning life that rests on the doorpost of God's promise?

There are only two options. There is no third.

---

If the righteous must live by faith - and none of us does perfectly - then either this sentence condemns us or it drives us to the One who fulfilled it.

The writer of Hebrews takes Habakkuk 2:3 - *"the vision awaits its appointed time; it will surely come"* - and reads it this way: *"Yet a little while, and the coming one will come and will not delay"* (Hebrews 10:37). The vision becomes a Person. The *coming one* - *ho erchomenos* - is Christ. The vision Habakkuk awaited on the watchtower, the vision God told him to write on tablets, the vision that would surely come at the appointed time - it arrived in a manger in Bethlehem.

And the righteous one who lived by perfect faithfulness? That is also Christ. He leaned His full weight on the Father through Gethsemane, through Golgotha, through the silence of the tomb. His faith never cracked. His *emunah* never gave way. And His righteousness - His doorpost-sturdy, never-failing faithfulness - is the righteousness credited to everyone who trusts Him.

Paul says it in a single sentence: *"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God"* (2 Corinthians 5:21).

He became what we are so we could become what He is. That is the exchange. That is the gospel. That is what Habakkuk 2:4 has been pointing to since the seventh century before Christ.

The righteous live by faith - but only because Christ lived in perfect faithfulness. His *emunah* held. On the night He was betrayed, when every human reason to abandon the mission was screaming in His ears - His faith held. On the cross, when the Father turned His face away and the weight of every sin that would ever be committed pressed down on His body - His faith held. In the tomb, when the silence was absolute - His faithfulness did not break. And on the third day, the Father vindicated that faithfulness by raising Him from the dead.

That is the righteousness that is credited to you when you believe. Not your wavering trust. His unbreakable faithfulness. Not your doorpost, which creaks and bends. His, which has never moved.

---

This is the fourth time in this series I have read you this verse. That is not repetition. That is mercy.

Romans 10:9 - *"If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*

*Believe in your heart.* That is *emunah*. That is Habakkuk 2:4. It is leaning your full weight on the doorpost of God's promise and trusting it to hold. You cannot climb your way there. You cannot perform your way there. You cannot manage or earn or achieve your way into the righteousness of God. You receive it - or you keep climbing stairs.

Some of you have been climbing a long time. You are exhausted. Your knees hurt. Your conscience is heavy. And the voice in your head says: *One more step. One more effort. One more try, and maybe God will accept you.*

Stop climbing. Stand up. Look at Christ at the right hand of the Father. Your righteousness is there. Not on the staircase. Not in your effort. Not in the next fix, the next achievement, the next attempt to prove you are worth something. There. In Him. Finished. Credited. Yours by faith.

David Platt put it this way: *"Just as you trust in God for your salvation, trust in God amidst your suffering. Same. It's faith."* The same weight-bearing reliance that saves you is the reliance that carries you through every dark night in this series. It is not a different faith for salvation and a different faith for suffering. It is one faith. One doorpost. One Christ. And He holds.

Come talk to me after the service. Come talk to one of our elders. The door is open.

---

We are four weeks into this series. Habakkuk cried out. God answered with Babylon. Habakkuk argued back. He took his stand on the watchtower. And now God has spoken the sentence that holds the whole Bible together: *the righteous shall live by faith.*

Next week, God turns from the righteous to the wicked. Five woes. Five judgments on the empire that devoured the world and worshiped its own strength. Chapter 2, verses 6 through 20. Babylon's bill comes due.

But this week - this week is yours.

God does not answer Habakkuk with an explanation of His ways. He answers with a command for how to live. And the command is not *try harder.* The command is not *figure it out.* The command is not *climb another step.*

The command is: trust Me.

Seven words. One life. The righteous shall live by faith - so stop trying to stand on anything else.

*Pray.*
