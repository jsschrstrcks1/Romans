# Habakkuk 3 - I Will Take My Stand

**Habakkuk Series: Faith in the Dark - Week 3 of 6**
**May 3, 2026**
**Text:** Habakkuk 1:12-2:1
**Status:** Draft

---

Two weeks ago, Habakkuk cried out: *How long, O Lord?* Last week, God answered. And the answer was worse than the silence. God said: *I am raising up the Chaldeans.* The most violent, most idolatrous empire on earth - and God is using them as His instrument against His own people.

Now put yourself in Habakkuk's place. You cried out because of injustice in Judah. God's response is to send a nation that is more unjust. You complained about sin among the people of God. God's answer involves more sin - from people who don't even pretend to know God. You asked Him to fix the problem. He is fixing it with something worse than the problem.

This is not what Habakkuk wanted to hear. And he does not pretend it was.

We spent two weeks in the dark. Today, Habakkuk shows us how to stand in it.

---

Open your Bibles to Habakkuk chapter 1. We're picking up at verse 12, and we're going through chapter 2, verse 1. Follow along.

*"Are you not from everlasting, O LORD my God, my Holy One? We shall not die. O LORD, you have ordained them as a judgment, and you, O Rock, have established them for reproof. You who are of purer eyes than to see evil and cannot look at wrong, why do you idly look at traitors and remain silent when the wicked swallows up the man more righteous than he? You make mankind like the fish of the sea, like crawling things that have no ruler. He brings all of them up with a hook; he drags them out with his net; he gathers them in his dragnet; so he rejoices and is glad. Therefore he sacrifices to his net and makes offerings to his dragnet; for by them he lives in luxury, and his food is rich. Is he then to keep on emptying his net and mercilessly killing nations forever?"*

Chapter 2, verse 1:

*"I will take my stand at my watchpost and station myself on the tower, and I will look out to see what he will say to me, and what I shall answer when I am reproved."*

---

Notice where Habakkuk starts. He does not start with his problem. He starts with his God.

Verse 12.

*"Are you not from everlasting, O LORD my God, my Holy One? We shall not die. O LORD, you have ordained them as a judgment, and you, O Rock, have established them for reproof."*

This is one of the most theologically dense verses in the Minor Prophets, and it's a prayer. Habakkuk stacks five truths about God in two sentences - five things he knows to be true before he says one word about what confuses him. John MacArthur, preaching through this text, put it this way: *"Habakkuk does not stand in the middle of the problem trying to solve it. Instead, he backs away from the problem to stand on what he knows about God."*

That is the first lesson of this text, and it may be the most important one in the entire series. When you cannot understand what God is doing, go back to what you know about who God is. Don't start with the problem. Start with the Person.

Five truths. Walk through them with me.

First: *"from everlasting."* God is eternal. He predates the problem. He predates Babylon. He will outlast both. Whatever is happening in the sixth century before Christ - and whatever is happening in your life this week - is a moment in the life of a God who has no moments. He inhabits eternity. The crisis that feels like it will never end is already behind Him.

Second: *"O LORD"* - Yahweh. The covenant name. The name God gave to Moses at the burning bush. Habakkuk is not appealing to some generic deity. He is appealing to the God who said *"I AM WHO I AM"* - the God who made promises to Abraham, who rescued Israel from Egypt, who established David's throne. This is covenant language. Habakkuk is holding God to His own word.

Third: *"my God."* The possessive pronoun matters. This is not a detached theologian constructing a syllogism. This is a man in anguish speaking to the God he belongs to. *My* God. That word carries the weight of a relationship. Habakkuk has the right to bring this complaint because he belongs to the One he's complaining to.

Fourth: *"my Holy One."* And here is the attribute that creates the entire problem. If God were not holy, there would be no complaint. An unholy God using an unholy nation would raise no questions. It's precisely because Habakkuk believes God is holy - of purer eyes than to see evil - that the use of Babylon is intolerable. The complaint only works because the theology is right. Habakkuk's faith is the engine of his protest.

Fifth: *"O Rock."* Deuteronomy 32:4 - *"The Rock, his work is perfect, for all his ways are justice. A God of faithfulness and without iniquity, just and upright is he."* Habakkuk is standing on Deuteronomy 32. He is reaching back to Moses and pulling up the oldest creed Israel has - the song of the Rock - and planting his feet on it. God is immovable. God is stable. God does not shift.

Five truths. Eternal. Covenant-keeping. Personal. Holy. Immovable. And Habakkuk stacks all five of them before he asks one question. That is not the prayer of a man who has lost his faith. That is the prayer of a man whose faith is precisely the reason he's arguing.

And then, embedded inside the complaint, a confession: *"We shall not die."*

That sentence is not optimism. It is covenant logic. Habakkuk reasons backward from God's character to a conclusion: if God is faithful, if God is the Rock, if God made promises to Abraham and David - then He will not let His people be utterly destroyed. Babylon may come. The invasion may be devastating. But extinction is off the table. God's covenant holds.

This is the covenant logic that runs through the whole Bible. God bound Himself to Abraham by His own name. He promised David a house that would stand. He sealed a new covenant in the blood of His own Son and promised that none whom the Father gave Him would be lost. When God enters covenant, He does not break it. That is bedrock - and it is what Habakkuk is standing on when he says *"We shall not die."*

Jeremiah is going to say the same thing from inside the rubble of Jerusalem that Habakkuk says here before the rubble comes. Lamentations 3:22-23: *"The steadfast love of the LORD never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning; great is your faithfulness."* One prophet writes it in anticipation. The other writes it in the ashes. Both stand on the same ground: God's faithfulness outlasts the worst thing that happens to His people.

---

Verse 13.

*"You who are of purer eyes than to see evil and cannot look at wrong, why do you idly look at traitors and remain silent when the wicked swallows up the man more righteous than he?"*

This is the sharpest question in the book. Sharper than *"How long?"* in verse 2. Sharper than anything in chapter 1. Habakkuk uses God's own holiness as the basis for his complaint against God. You are too pure to look at evil - so why are You looking at this? You cannot tolerate wrong - so why are You tolerating the Babylonians swallowing up people who are, at least comparatively, more righteous than they are?

The Nelson Study Bible puts it plainly: *"Habakkuk's point seems to be that God's holiness should have prohibited Him from using a 'dirty' instrument like Babylon to accomplish His purposes in judging and reproving His own people."*

Feel the weight of that. Habakkuk is not asking a casual question. He is pressing God's character against God's actions and saying: these two things do not fit. You are holy. They are not. And You are using them against us. Explain Yourself.

Some of you have been here. Not with Babylon - but with the instrument God chose to bring pain into your life. The betrayal that came from inside the church, not outside it. The person who hurt you who should have known better - who did know better. The circumstance that looks like the opposite of what a holy God should allow. And your complaint is not *"God, are You there?"* That was two weeks ago. Your complaint now is sharper: *"God, I know who You are. And what You are doing does not match."*

Habakkuk felt that. He said it out loud. And God did not strike him down for it.

---

Now the complaint turns. Verses 14 through 17. Habakkuk has established who God is. Now he describes what the Babylonians are doing - and the image he uses is devastating.

Verse 14.

*"You make mankind like the fish of the sea, like crawling things that have no ruler."*

Fish have no shepherd. No king. No protector. In the ancient world, fish were the lowest metaphor for helplessness - creatures that exist to be caught. Habakkuk is saying: God, You have reduced Your image-bearers to the status of catch. Nations that were made in Your image, nations that carry the *imago Dei* - and Babylon scoops them up the way a fisherman scoops up perch. No dignity. No distinction. No names.

And notice the accusation: *"You make mankind like the fish."* Not: *Babylon makes mankind like fish.* *You* do. Habakkuk is laying this at God's feet. God is the one who has permitted His creatures to be treated as catch - and Habakkuk will not let Him off the hook for it.

Verses 15 and 16.

*"He brings all of them up with a hook; he drags them out with his net; he gathers them in his dragnet; so he rejoices and is glad. Therefore he sacrifices to his net and makes offerings to his dragnet; for by them he lives in luxury, and his food is rich."*

The image turns grotesque. The fisherman hauls in nation after nation - and then he worships his net. *He sacrifices to his net and makes offerings to his dragnet.* The instrument of destruction becomes the object of worship. Babylon does not credit God for its victories. It credits its own military power. Its own siege engines. Its own cavalry. The net caught the fish, and the fisherman bows down to the net.

This is not just an ancient problem. Every empire worships its net. The net is military strength, economic leverage, technological dominance, cultural power. Whenever a nation begins to say *"We built this - our strength, our resolve, our ingenuity"* - it is sacrificing to its net. Moses warned Israel about exactly this in Deuteronomy 8:17-18: *"Beware lest you say in your heart, 'My power and the might of my hand have gotten me this wealth.' You shall remember the LORD your God, for it is he who gives you power to get wealth."*

The fisherman who worships his net has forgotten who filled the sea.

And it happens in individual lives too. You build a career and worship the career. You build a family and worship the family. You build a reputation and worship the reputation. You build a ministry and worship the ministry. The net is whatever you made with your own hands that you now cannot imagine living without. The moment you sacrifice to it - the moment it becomes the thing you cannot lose, the thing you protect at all costs, the thing that defines you - you have made offerings to your dragnet. And you have forgotten the God who gave you the sea.

Some of you know exactly what your net is. You felt something tighten in your chest when I named it - the job, the child who won't speak to you, the reputation that took the hit, the ministry that didn't grow the way you wanted it to. That tightening is the sound of the altar. You have been burning incense to the net without knowing it. And the God who filled the sea is waiting to take back His place on the throne.

---

Verse 17.

*"Is he then to keep on emptying his net and mercilessly killing nations forever?"*

This is how the complaint ends. Not with an answer. With a question. Habakkuk does not resolve the tension. He does not construct a theodicy. He does not write a philosophical treatise on the problem of evil. He asks the question - and then he does something else entirely.

That matters. Some questions are not ours to answer. Some tensions are not ours to resolve. Some mysteries are meant to be carried, not solved. The person who demands an explanation before they will trust God has put their understanding above God's character. The person who says *"I will not believe until I can explain"* has made their own intellect the judge of the Almighty. Habakkuk does not do that. He asks the question. He feels the full weight of it. And then - with no answer on the horizon, no guarantee that one is coming, no way to know whether God will speak at all - he turns.

Adrian Rogers said it well: *"Faith doesn't live by explanations, but by promises."*

Habakkuk has no explanation for what God is doing. He has five attributes of God that he stacked in verse 12. And those five attributes are going to hold him - not because they answer his question, but because they are true whether the question gets answered or not.

Here's the thing: the question in verse 17 is going to get answered. God is going to speak in chapter 2. But Habakkuk doesn't know that yet. He has no guarantee that an answer is coming. And he turns anyway. That is what faith looks like when it has nothing left but God.

---

Chapter 2, verse 1.

*"I will take my stand at my watchpost and station myself on the tower, and I will look out to see what he will say to me, and what I shall answer when I am reproved."*

After six verses of anguished complaint - after stacking God's attributes, after the fishing metaphor, after the unanswered question - Habakkuk uses five verbs of resolve in one sentence: *I will take my stand. I will station myself. I will look out. I will see. I will answer.* The grammar shifts. The tone shifts. This is not resignation. This is resolution.

Three things about this verse. And this is where the sermon lives.

---

First: *"I will take my stand."*

This is a decision. Not a feeling.

Habakkuk does not say *"I feel peaceful about this."* He does not say *"I understand now."* He does not say *"I've worked through my questions and I'm in a good place."* He says: *I will take my stand.* That is volition. That is will. That is a man planting his feet on a spot and refusing to leave it - not because the confusion has cleared, but because the God underneath him has not moved.

Faith is not the absence of confusion. Faith is the decision to hold your ground when confusion is all you have.

Some of you need to hear that. You have been waiting for the feeling to come back before you start trusting again. You are waiting for the fog to lift, for the prayer to be answered, for the circumstances to change - before you'll open the Word, before you'll pray with any conviction, before you'll come back to this gathered body with your whole heart. You have told yourself: *When I feel it again, I'll get back to it.*

Habakkuk says: do not wait for the feeling. Take your stand. The feeling may or may not come. The ground under your feet is not a feeling. It is the character of God - eternal, covenant-keeping, holy, immovable. And that ground does not shift because your emotions do.

---

Second: *"I will look out to see what he will say to me."*

This is expectation. Not passivity.

A watchman in ancient Israel had a specific job. He stood on the wall or the tower, and he looked outward - not inward. He scanned the horizon for what was coming. He did not leave his post when the night got dark. He did not abandon the wall when the hours dragged. He stayed. He watched. He waited for the dawn - not because he could see it, but because he knew it was coming.

Habakkuk is not sitting in a corner with his arms crossed. He is on the watchtower, leaning forward, scanning the horizon. He expects God to speak. He does not know when. He does not know what God will say. But he believes God will say something. And that expectation is the difference between waiting on God and giving up on God. The posture looks similar from the outside. The heart is entirely different.

Psalm 130:5-6: *"I wait for the LORD, my soul waits, and in his word I hope; my soul waits for the Lord more than watchmen for the morning, more than watchmen for the morning."*

The psalmist repeats it. *More than watchmen for the morning.* The watchman knows the morning is coming. He has seen it every day of his life. He does not know exactly when it will break the horizon tonight. But he does not doubt that it will come. That is the posture.

Isaiah 40:31: *"But they who wait for the LORD shall renew their strength; they shall mount up with wings like eagles; they shall run and not be weary; they shall walk and not faint."*

Waiting is not weakness. In the biblical vocabulary, waiting is the posture of strength under pressure. It is the runner who holds his form in the last mile when everything in him wants to quit. It is the soldier who holds the line when he cannot see the reinforcements. It is the saint who prays when the ceiling feels like iron and the answer has not come and the morning has not broken.

The watchtower is not a recliner. It is a military post. And Habakkuk is not resting. He is ready.

---

Third: *"And what I shall answer when I am reproved."*

This is humility. Not entitlement.

This clause gets overlooked. Habakkuk expects that when God speaks, God may correct him. He is not standing on the watchtower as a plaintiff who is confident in his case. He is standing there as a servant who knows he may be wrong. He holds his complaint in one hand and his humility in the other, and he does not let go of either one.

That is the mark of mature faith. Immature faith either asks no questions - out of fear that God might be offended - or demands answers as though God owes them. Mature faith asks the hardest questions it has and simultaneously says: *I may be the one who needs correcting.* Habakkuk is willing to be reproved. He brings his sharpest argument to the watchtower and holds it loosely enough that God can take it from his hands.

Some of you are very sure you are right about your situation. You have analyzed it. You have prayed about it. You have decided what God should do, and you are frustrated that He hasn't done it. Habakkuk would say: bring it all to the tower. Every analysis, every conviction, every complaint. Bring it. But hold it open. Because the God who is about to speak may not say what you expect. And if He corrects you, the correction is mercy - not punishment.

Spurgeon read Habakkuk's watchtower exactly right: *"I shall look to God, and I shall also look to myself. There shall be an expectation as I gaze upward to my Lord, and there shall also be an examination as I look within at my empty, guilty, good-for-nothing self."*

Expectation upward. Examination inward. That is the watchtower. Not one direction - both. The man who only looks up is sentimental. The man who only looks in is morbid. The man who does both - who expects God to speak and expects that he himself may need correcting - is the one who is actually ready to hear.

---

Now - where does this leave us? What is the watchtower for us?

Habakkuk stationed himself where God had promised to speak. For Habakkuk, that was the prophet's post. For us, the watchtower has an address. Our confession - the 1689 London Baptist Confession, chapter 1, paragraph 1 - says this:

*"The Holy Scripture is the only sufficient, certain, and infallible rule of all saving knowledge, faith, and obedience."*

The watchtower is the Word. It is the Scriptures, opened on your kitchen table on Tuesday morning when you don't feel like reading. It is the gathered worship of the church on Sunday, when your heart is cold and the last thing you want to do is sing. It is the Lord's Table, when you take the bread and the cup and remember that the body was broken for you. It is the preaching of the Word, when a man stands in this pulpit and says *"Thus says the Lord"* and the Spirit takes the text and applies it to your conscience.

Taking your stand at the watchtower is not mystical. It is concrete. It means: do not abandon the means of grace when God is silent. Stay in the Word. Stay in the church. Stay at the Table. Stay under the preaching. Stay at your post. Because that is where God has promised to meet His people - and He keeps His promises.

Some of you used to be at the post. You were here every Sunday. You opened the Word in the morning. You prayed with your family. Something happened, and you stopped - not dramatically, but quietly. The drift was so slow you barely noticed. 2:1 is a call back. The tower is still standing. Come back to your post.

Paul told Timothy the same thing: *"All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work"* (2 Timothy 3:16-17). Teaching. Reproof. Correction. Training. That is what happens on the watchtower. The Word teaches us what is true. The Word reproves us where we are wrong. The Word corrects our course. The Word trains us for the road ahead. And none of that happens if we leave the tower.

---

Now hear me.

There was One who stood where Habakkuk stood - but with infinitely more at stake.

In the garden of Gethsemane, on the night before He died, Jesus looked at what was coming and said: *"My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as you will"* (Matthew 26:39).

Hear that. Jesus brought the complaint. *If it be possible, let this cup pass.* He felt the weight of what was coming - the wrath of God poured out on sin, absorbed in His own body - and He asked the Father to take it away. That is Habakkuk's prayer in Gethsemane. That is honest, anguished, raw petition aimed at the throne.

And then He took His stand. *Not as I will, but as you will.* He did not come down from the cross when they mocked Him. He did not summon twelve legions of angels when the nails went in. He stayed at His post. He held the line. He endured the silence of heaven - *"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"* - and He did not abandon the mission.

And then it was finished.

On the cross, the very thing Habakkuk could not understand was answered. How can a holy God use unholy instruments? Look at Calvary. Wicked men drove the nails. Sovereign God made the plan. God used the most unjust act in history to accomplish the most just purpose in eternity.

And the cross was not only sovereignty over evil. It was substitution. He took the wrath we had earned. Our sin was laid on Him. His righteousness was credited to us. This is the heart of the gospel - not that God overlooked our rebellion but that He punished it in His Son so He could pardon us.

Peter says it in his first letter: *"When he was reviled, he did not revile in return; when he suffered, he did not threaten, but continued entrusting himself to him who judges justly. He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, that we might die to sin and live to righteousness. By his wounds you have been healed"* (1 Peter 2:23-24).

*He continued entrusting himself to him who judges justly.* That is the watchtower. That is the stand. Jesus entrusted Himself to the Father - the Father who sent Him to the cross, the Father whose plan included the nails and the thorns and the darkness at noon. He did not understand the plan. He trusted the Person behind it. And the Person behind it raised Him from the dead on the third day.

---

If you are here this morning and you have never taken your stand with Christ - you have never trusted Him with the full weight of your sin, your confusion, your life - I am asking you to do it today.

Habakkuk took his stand on five attributes of God. I am asking you to take your stand on one fact: Christ died for sinners, and God raised Him from the dead.

You may look at this world - the violence, the injustice, the cruelty - and say, *"This is why I don't believe in God."* Habakkuk would agree with your diagnosis. He saw the same world you see. He would reject your conclusion. The violence and the injustice are not evidence that God doesn't exist. They are evidence that the world needs the God who does. And that God has done something about it - not by explaining it from a distance, but by entering it in the person of His Son and absorbing the full weight of it in His own body on a cross.

Faith doesn't live by explanations. Faith lives by promises. And here is the promise:

Romans 10:9 - *"If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*

You will be saved. Not you might be. Not you could be, if you try hard enough. *You will be saved.* That is a promise from the God who is from everlasting, who is holy, who is the Rock. And the Rock does not make promises He cannot keep.

Come talk to me after the service. Come talk to one of our elders. The door is open.

---

We are three weeks into this series. Habakkuk has cried out, God has answered, and Habakkuk has argued back. And then he took his stand. Next week, God speaks again - and this time, what He says will change the world. Chapter 2, verses 2 through 5. The sermon is called *The Righteous Shall Live by Faith.* That sentence - written by a seventh-century prophet in a watchtower, quoted by Paul, quoted by the writer of Hebrews, read by Martin Luther on a staircase in Rome - that sentence broke open the Reformation and reshaped the Christian world. You are about to hear it at home, in the context it was born in.

For this week, take one thing with you.

Take your stand. That is not a metaphor. It is a Monday-morning instruction. Open the Word tomorrow - not because you feel like it, but because the watchtower is where God speaks. Gather with this body next week - not because your heart is on fire, but because the means of grace are not optional when the night is long. Pray - not because the ceiling has opened, but because the God on the other side of it is from everlasting. Station yourself. Look out. Expect Him to speak.

Habakkuk took his stand in the dark, with no guarantee that the light was coming. You have something Habakkuk didn't have. You have a cross behind you and an empty tomb. You have a resurrected Christ seated at the right hand of the Father. You have the completed Scriptures in your hands. You have more light than Habakkuk ever dreamed of - and if he could hold the line in the dark, you can hold it in the dawn.

Take your stand. He will speak.

*Pray.*
