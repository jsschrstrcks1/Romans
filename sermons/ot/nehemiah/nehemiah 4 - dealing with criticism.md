Dealing with Criticism

Is it constructive?
Some criticism is valid and legitimate, and some is meant to pull you down. While we can never fully know another person's motives, think their criticism through and decide if it's helpful in the situation or not. If you put all this time into a project and someone comes along and says your project is terrible, and you've done everything wrong. Then check the blueprint, or check the syllabus, or check the Master's plan, and compare, and if you're following directions well, then it's not constructive criticism is it? If you show up late, and unprepared, and throw it together at the last minute, and someone says "hey your project is terrible, you did it all wrong, then you need to re-evaluate your approach to the project. Right?

If it is constructive and you legitimately are in the wrong, then – and this is hard – accept that you're not perfect.

I've struggled with this, and I see Wyley struggling with it now. When he was learning to talk. He wouldn't say things in front of us really until he had mastered the thing he wanted to say. So he went from saying almost nothing to speaking in full sentences because it's important to him to do everything he does not only well but rightly.

In my own life, I was placed as a first semester college student into 5 classes. 5 is a lot. 4 is considered full time, the expectation is that if you are taking 12 class hours that you should be doing about 36 hours of homework each week for those same classes. Which means between homework and lectures you're putting in 48 hours for school. Most people have to have a job, which means another 20-30 hours of work for a total of 70-80hrs for most. I was put into 5 classes, 15 class hours, 45hrs outside of class, and was working two part time jobs for a total of 100hrs a week. On top of that at Trinity we were expected to put in an additional 10 hours a week volunteering at church. I was at church Wednesday night from 5-9pm, Sunday from 9-9 most weeks, and sometimes we would have additional things through the week. At the end, looking back across my time, I put in on average (not counting missions trips etc) about 18hrs a week at church. So there are only 24hrs in a day right? 7 days in a week. That meant between church, work and school I was putting in about 118hrs out of the 168hrs there are in a week. I averaged about 5 hrs of sleep a night that first semester. So any way, my first semester in addition to putting me into 5 classes, one of the classes was a Greek class. Junior level Greek class.

That semester almost killed me. I ended up getting an A, a B, a C, and a pair of F's (I ended up studying for one class in another class, and the prof took it personally). If I had understood the work load, I would have done things differently. If the school had understood the amount of work hours I was putting in they would not have put me into so many classes, much less a Greek class. Regardless, after I got my midterm grade back in the Greek class, I felt like I had been given some pretty serious but fair criticism. It rocked me for a few days. I was offered and accepted the opportunity to bring my midterm grade up to a D in Greek. What I didn't know, was the opportunity was offered so that I could withdraw while passing. Foolish me still thought I could pull out a passing grade in all the classes. It stung. It was the first time I had failed at anything that I could remember. But it was fair and constructive criticism to say I said yes to too many things, and that forced me to say no to homework sometimes. I realized how dangerous it was at one point when I fell asleep at a traffic light on my way into school. Anyway. Some criticism while harsh is valuable, and should be taken to heart. Everybody fails sometimes. It's just part of life. Don't take it personally. Learn from it and move on. Some criticism is ridiculous and should be dismissed without taking it to heart.

One way to help you decide if criticism is constructive or not is to evaluate someone's motives. Does the person have your best interests at heart? If a teacher fails you, it isn't because they don't like you and want to make you feel bad, or think you're stupid or a bad writer. They have a vested interest in helping you to succeed. Parents – good parents – do too. Close friends usually don't want to make you feel bad. In fact scripture says better to have wounds from your friend, than kisses from your enemy. Constructive criticism from your friends, while potentially painful, is far better than the alternative.

If the person has your best interests at heart, and the criticism is constructive then thank the person and subsequently make a game plan to address it.

Conversely if someone offers criticism without your best interests at heart, or if the things they say are not constructive, then we should deposit such criticism in the circular filing cabinet where that sort of thing belongs.

Let's take a look at the criticism Nehemiah received. Starting about half way through verse 2 the critics start to say "What are these feeble Jews doing? Will they restore it for themselves? Will they sacrifice? Will they finish up in a day? Will they revive the stones out of the heaps of rubbish, and burned ones at that?" 3 Tobiah the Ammonite was beside him, and he said, "Yes, what they are building—if a fox goes up on it he will break down their stone wall!" The insults question the ability of the Jews to build the wall – he calls them feeble. Are you people really strong enough to build the wall? Won't you get tired and quit? Second their motives are questioned. Are they doing it for themselves? That one is two fold. Not only are motives questioned but he implies they aren't sufficient on their own. Third he questions their resolve. Will they quit after a single day? Do they have what it takes to stick it out? Fourth he questions their materials. Are the things they are using going to be able to stand up against the attack of enemies? Finally they argue that it won't even stand up to a fox. A fox is a relatively small creature. It's interesting to note that the word that was translated into fox here, is usually translated as Jackal. They are a similar size. I don't know why they translated it as fox here, but as far as I can tell, from what I've learned in Hebrew it would be better translated as Jackal. A female jackal can weigh as little as 13 pounds, while a large male once tipped the scales at 30 pounds. Likewise the red fox weighs as little as 5lbs and large specimens have been reported as big as 30 pounds. In any event, whatever kind of little dog it is, the point is any small animal that touches the wall could make it crumble. He may as well have said if a sparrow lands on it, it's going to tip over and fall. They wanted to demoralize the workers.

The point is obviously not constructive criticism, these people are trying to sow discord, and discontent. They want to crush morale. They want to make the Israelites go back to living and acting like a defeated discouraged people.

So in Nehemiah's case, he rightly evaluated the source of the criticism. At this point in time Jerusalem was at the crossroads of the world. They had Persia on one side, Egypt on another, the Philistines in a third direction, and Samaritans in a fourth direction. When this city was rebuilt many scholars think the surrounding nations especially the Samaritans were concerned that they would have less trade, and thus less money. If half the trade you were getting is now going to Jerusalem it takes a big bite out of your tax revenue. So these people that mocked Nehemiah and those rebuilding the city. He planned this out for months, he showed up early, looked things over personally, and was doing what he believed God wanted him to do. So he was able to dismiss them. Because he recognized their end goal, he was able to address them directly.

We've talked about imprecatory prayers before. Here is one of Nehemiah's. He prays in verse 4 that their taunts be turned back against them. That they be taken as slaves by a neighboring kingdom, and finally, and I think the most severe of all the prayer requests, Nehemiah asks that God not forget the sin of these people. Basically he's asking that His enemies, who were also God's unrepentant enemies, be sent to hell. Have you ever played Monopoly? You know that one card? Go to jail, go directly to jail, do not pass go, do not collect $200? That's basically what Nehemiah prays here. That as a result of their continued demonstrated unrepentance, and their status as the enemies of God, that not only would the duration of their earthly life be miserable, but that they would spend an eternity as an enemy of God. An eternity in Hell. Some people feel funny about imprecatory prayers, but I think they are absolutely okay to pray. With some conditions. For one, your focus is on the repentance of the individual. David always prayed this way: "Lord bring them back to yourself, or else – punishment goes here." For two, don't pray imprecatory prayers on people or things that are causing you to sin. I went to school with a fellow that struggled with pornography that prayed imprecatory prayers against the porn stores down in Tampa on his way to school every day. Should they close? Certainly. But if you're struggling with that, and God closes the porn stores, won't you just turn to the internet? Or gas station garbage magazines? Or a hundred other alternatives. God would rather you learn to say no to your temptations. So keep the person or thing's repentance at the forefront, and don't pray imprecatory prayers to get your own behind out of trouble. A good example of a way you could pray this today is for those who are being persecuted around the world. You could pray: "God please intercede on behalf of those being persecuted for their faith. May their captors be so struck by the testimony of those they persecute that they would turn or return to you Lord, and if not, may any who persecute true believers be miserable until they turn back to you, and if they don't Lord remember their sin into eternity and allow them to reap the full consequences that they deserve. In Jesus' name we pray."

So we talked about types of criticism and how to handle it, we talked about ways we can pray for those that treat us poorly, and finally, I want to talk for a moment about something that ties into last week as well. So you remember we talked about the burning dung gate, and the flaming poop mountain? How we are little different apart from Christ, were rotten to the core, and smell little better to God than the flaming mountain of dung or the dung gate smelled to the residents of the city. Scripture says apart from God we're incapable of doing anything good at all. That the best we can do is dirty filthy rags. That we are dead apart from Christ according to Ephesians 2. What can a dead man do? Tell me? What can a dead man do? Lie there? Stink? Exactly. He would be incapable of doing anything else for himself, including realizing he were thirsty, or wanting to get a drink. We were stinking nasty corpses. Incapable of choosing, or even desiring to choose God in our own power scripture says. Are we not like the walls of Jerusalem? Busted, broken, burned hulking wrecks that smelled bad and were incapable of choosing to repair themselves, much less actually repairing themselves?

Remember last week we talked about Ordo Salutis, or the order of salvation. We touched on the first two, let's jump into the third element of that. To remind you, the passage that talks about that is Romans 8:29-30: "For those whom He foreknew, He also predestined to become conformed to the image of His Son, so that He would be the firstborn among many brethren; and these whom He predestined, He also called; and these whom He called, He also justified; and these whom He justified, He also glorified." So we talked about foreknowledge, that God knows all things and loves us anyway, and we talked a touch about predestination. Let's jump right into conformation. Conforming something is the act of taking a thing that is in one shape, and forcing it into a different shape. Any of you ever play with Play-Doh when you were kids? Or clay? I took a clay class, and enjoyed it. We made bird houses one summer, and fired them in a kiln. We had to take the clay and roll it in our hands until it was absolutely perfect. It had to be a certain thickness, a certain length, the same thickness throughout. Then we molded these long strings of clay into the shape we wanted – into a bird house. God does the same thing for us. Except we give him less to work with. We're basically dead corpses apart from Christ, incapable of anything but laying there and stinking, and God walks up to us and says… you, I choose you. Imagine what your parents would have done if you'd brought home some stinking nasty dead creature? Yet Christ not only chose to bring us to his Father's house, but gave his life so that we could have the privilege. That's not where it ends though. When we become believers in Jesus Christ, we're basically resurrected. Like Lazarus. Remember Lazarus? So when Christ calls us we too are made alive once more. Ephesians 2 says "But because of his great love for us, God, who is rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ even when we were dead in transgressions—it is by grace you have been saved. And God raised us up with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms in Christ Jesus, in order that in the coming ages he might show the incomparable riches of his grace, expressed in his kindness to us in Christ Jesus. For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God—not by works, so that no one can boast."

Let me sum up this last point for you. Basically God gave us the ability to choose, and we've chosen badly. All of us, each and every one has made choices that tainted us with sin. We ruined God's perfect beautiful creation, and substituted instead our own stinking dead body. Romans 3 says we've all done this. Romans 6 says we deserve to stay that way for all of eternity. Fortunately God didn't leave it there. In the second half of Romans 6:23 Paul writes that the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus. While we deserved to be eternally dead, we're offered the promise of life. Jesus rebuilds us, like Nehemiah rebuilt the walls. No matter what adversity comes your way, Jesus is there conforming you to his image, the image we were created to bear and to reflect. Jesus has done all the hard parts. He went to the cross for you.

Your part is simple. Scripture says in Revelation 3:20 "Here I am! I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears my voice and opens the door, I will come in and eat with that person, and they with me." Likewise Romans 10 says "confess with your mouth the Lord Jesus Christ, and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead and you will be saved."

As the praise team comes up, if you've never come to a point in your spiritual life where you've answered Christ's knocking at the door of your heart, then today's the day. Won't you come?

CORRECTIONS LOG (added by Claude Code)
Verified against ESV, primary sources, and lexical references. Do not preach or republish
without resolving flagged items below.

1. PROVERBS 27:6 — PARAPHRASE, NOT ESV
   Sermon: "better to have wounds from your friend, than kisses from your enemy"
   ESV (Prov 27:6): "Faithful are the wounds of a friend; profuse are the kisses of an enemy."
   The sermon's version is a loose paraphrase. If quoting, use the ESV text; if paraphrasing,
   do not present it as a direct Scripture quote.

2. FOX / JACKAL CLAIM — INACCURATE
   The sermon states the Hebrew word in Nehemiah 4:3 "is usually translated as Jackal."
   This is incorrect. The Hebrew שׁוּעָל (shu'al) is translated "fox" in ESV, NIV, KJV, NASB,
   NRSV, CSB, and virtually every major English translation. BDB and HALOT do list "jackal"
   as a secondary possibility in a few contexts, but Neh 4:3 is universally rendered "fox."
   The claim should be removed or corrected to: "some scholars think this word could refer
   to a jackal in certain contexts, though most translations here read 'fox.'"

3. ROMANS 8:29-30 — NASB WORDING, NOT ESV
   Sermon uses: "become conformed," "firstborn among many brethren," capitalized pronouns.
   ESV reads: "predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son, in order that he might
   be the firstborn among many brothers."
   This appears to be NASB or a similar translation. Correct to ESV before publishing.

4. EPHESIANS 2:4-9 — NIV WORDING, NOT ESV
   Several phrases in the sermon match NIV, not ESV:
   - "incomparable riches of his grace" (NIV) → ESV: "immeasurable riches of his grace"
   - "expressed in his kindness to us" (NIV) → ESV: "in kindness toward us"
   - "this is not from yourselves" (NIV) → ESV: "this is not your own doing"
   Correct all three to ESV before publishing.

5. NEHEMIAH 4:4-5 — "TAKEN AS SLAVES" IS IMPRECISE
   Sermon: "that they be taken as slaves by a neighboring kingdom"
   ESV (Neh 4:4): "give them up to be plundered in a land where they are captives"
   The sense is broadly similar but "taken as slaves" is not what the text says. A closer
   rendering: "carried off as captives and plundered." Correct before republishing.

6. REVELATION 3:20 — NIV WORDING, NOT ESV
   Sermon: "Here I am! I stand at the door and knock..."
   ESV: "Behold, I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears my voice and opens the door,
   I will come in to him and eat with him, and he with me."
   "Here I am!" is NIV. The ESV opens with "Behold." Correct before publishing.
   Note: Revelation 3:20 is addressed to the church at Laodicea (a warning to a lukewarm
   congregation), not to unbelievers as a salvation invitation. This is the most common
   evangelical evangelistic use of the verse, and many preachers apply it evangelistically,
   but the original context is a call to a wayward church. Awareness of this is worthwhile
   when preaching it as an altar call text.

7. ROMANS 10:9 — KJV/NKJV WORDING, NOT ESV
   Sermon: "confess with your mouth the Lord Jesus Christ"
   ESV (Rom 10:9): "if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord"
   The sermon's phrasing matches KJV/NKJV. Correct to ESV before publishing.

8. ORDO SALUTIS — "CONFORMATION" AS THIRD STEP IS NON-STANDARD
   The sermon presents conformation as the third discrete element of the ordo salutis after
   foreknowledge and predestination. In Romans 8:29-30, conforming to the image of Christ
   is stated as the PURPOSE of predestination, not a separate step in the chain. The classic
   ordo from this passage runs: foreknowledge → predestination → calling → justification →
   glorification. Framing "conformation" as a numbered step may cause theological confusion.
   Consider clarifying that conforming is the goal/telos of God's redemptive work rather than
   a discrete step between predestination and calling.

9. "DIRTY FILTHY RAGS" — NO CITATION GIVEN
   The phrase "the best we can do is dirty filthy rags" alludes to Isaiah 64:6 (ESV: "all
   our righteous deeds are like a polluted garment"). No reference is cited in the sermon.
   Add citation if quoting or paraphrasing in print.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The sermon has genuine pastoral heat in the gospel section and flashes of real conviction, but the Weight of Glory is uneven — heavy at the close, thin in the middle, because the text's own gravity (prayer before action; God's sovereignty over every Tobiah) is never fully released.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Nehemiah 4 is narrative-historical. The rubric applies well. The passage has clear theological argument (pray, act, persevere) and redemptive-historical dimension (rebuilding as restoration under God's care). The rubric does not strain the genre. The sermon's weakness is not genre-related — it is a structural choice to impose an external framework on a passage with its own clear structure.
