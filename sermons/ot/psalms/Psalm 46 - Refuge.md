# Psalm 46 — Refuge
## God Is Our Refuge and Strength

*Sermon — Psalm 46 (ESV)*

*Developed from raw transcript. See Development Note in Integrity Log.*

---

I heard something interesting from preacher Steven Davey. He told the story of this little mining town in Arizona called Bisbee. Back then, the early 1900s before the first World War, conditions in this mining town had gotten pretty hard. The main ore vein had played out, and the only place left to mine was blasted into the face of a sheer rock wall. Dangerous work. People died doing it.

Things got bad enough that the miners decided to organize a union. When the mine owner caught wind of it, he made a promise: anyone who so much as breathed the word "union" would be fired and thrown out of the state. About 3,000 of them went on strike anyway. And roughly 1,200 of them were rounded up, loaded onto trains with whatever they had on their backs, and shipped out to New Mexico.

The newspaper covering the whole thing wrote that the miners were caught between a rock and a hard place. And they were right. They had to choose between risking their lives every day on a rock face for a paycheck that wasn't enough, or standing up for better conditions and getting deported. Either way — rock, hard place, no good options.

We know that feeling.

People try to fake their way through it. Some try to ignore it. But the reality is we are weak, sinful creatures, and we can really hurt when the hard things come. The pressures can get to us. Difficulties abound.

When you're stuck between a rock and a hard place — what do you do?

Martin Luther knew that feeling. He was a Catholic monk who went looking in Scripture for the gospel and found it. He didn't want to start a new church — he wanted to repair the damage done to the existing one. The Pope had other ideas. Luther was excommunicated. The Emperor declared him an outlaw and a heretic. The whole world he had known turned against him.

One of his close associates, Philip Melanchthon — arguably as important to the Reformation as Luther himself, though less well known — followed Luther around and wrote down what he said so he could study it later. Melanchthon recorded on several occasions that Luther, in the middle of all this, would say: *"Philip, let's sing the 46th Psalm."* And they would.

Luther was so shaped by this psalm that he later wrote the hymn "A Mighty Fortress Is Our God" — its first line drawn directly from Psalm 46:1.

Because this is a psalm for people between a rock and a hard place.

Let's read it. While you're turning there — our people of the week are the Moghal of Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Nepal.

After the British partition of India in 1947, the lines were drawn sometimes carelessly — along rivers and valleys rather than existing tribal or family structures. The Moghal people ended up divided across several countries. Wherever they landed, though, they kept their status. They are a dignified, proud people who believe — whether they say it plainly or not — that they are strong enough, smart enough, resilient enough to reach heaven on their own terms. Their confidence in themselves has not been shaken. They have never needed to look up and acknowledge they need God's help. There are more than 1.2 million of them without a single known believer in Jesus Christ. Who will pray for the Moghal?

## I. The Storm Is Real

I want you to notice something about the first three verses. The psalmist is not pretending.

He doesn't say: *if* the earth gives way. He says *though* it does. Though the mountains are moved into the heart of the sea. Though the waters roar and foam. Though the mountains tremble.

That is apocalyptic language. The psalmist is not talking about a bad week. He is talking about the cosmos coming unraveled. The most stable things you can imagine — bedrock, ancient mountains — heaved up and thrown into the ocean. The world as you know it, unrecognizable.

The psalmist isn't minimizing the trouble. He's staring it in the face and saying: *even so.*

Some of you have been there. You don't need me to illustrate the earth giving way. You know what it is to have the foundation underneath your life shift. The diagnosis. The phone call. The marriage that isn't what it was. The loss that made the world strange.

And what the psalmist is saying is: God does not become less of a refuge when the storm gets worse.

---

## II. The Claim

Look at verse 1 again.

*"God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble."*

Right off the bat, when I read this, I had to look up that word. *Refuge*.

In the Hebrew, the word is *machseh* — and its roots carry the meaning of: a place of safety. A covering. A shelter. Protection from the elements, from the danger, from the weather. The kind of place you run to when you need to get out of the storm. A place you can trust.

The same Hebrew root gets translated into Greek in several ways across the Old Testament — the ideas of help, support, trust, shelter, even hope. It's a word that covers the whole idea of: something you run to and find solid.

And what is that thing?

God. Not a plan. Not a church. Not a denomination. Not a set of circumstances that worked out. Not a position or a platform.

*God is our refuge.*

That's the claim. And the psalmist is about to spend the next ten verses proving it.

---

## III. Every Other Shelter Will Let You Down

When I was a teenager, I played football for the local police athletic league. One night it was storming badly. Thunder, lightning, rain coming down sideways. In the distance you could see little fingers pointing down from the sky, rolling turbulently, working their way from sky to earth. Lightning popping all around us.

The concessions stand offered a little cover, but not much.

So our coach, in his infinite brilliance, decided we should drag the metal bleachers over and set them up under the tall, scrawny pine tree at the edge of the field. Then he ordered us to sit there.

I was a kid. I did what I was told.

When my mother came early to pick me up, she was absolutely livid. All those kids sitting in the middle of an open field, on a giant metal bleacher — which is basically a lightning rod — under a tall pine tree — also a lightning rod — soaking wet, with tornadoes working in the distance.

I was very glad she came early that night.

Our coach wasn't dumb. He was trying to help. He was trying to find us shelter. But the shelter he found us put us in a worse position than if we'd just stood in the rain.

Aren't you glad my coach isn't God?

It's probably a good thing I'm not God either. Everyone except God will eventually let you down. Not usually out of bad intentions — sometimes out of good ones. But the shelter they build, or the shelter they point you toward, will not hold.

The psalmist has seen this. He's watched nations rise and kingdoms fall. He's watched what looked like permanent, unmovable earthly powers come apart. *"The nations rage, the kingdoms totter; he utters his voice, the earth melts."* (v.6)

God utters his voice. The earth melts.

That's not a God who is worried about what the nations are doing.

A friend of mine went to speak at a conference in Santiago, Chile a few years back. Right in the middle of it, an earthquake hit. And these buildings started swaying. My friend was terrified — because in Florida, when your building starts swaying in a hurricane, that's a very bad sign. That building doesn't have much time.

But in Santiago, the buildings were behaving exactly as they were designed to. Chile builds to strict seismic codes. The buildings are engineered to sway with the earth so that they stay standing when the ground moves. When that rock and hard place came, they bent — but they did not break.

*That's* what trustworthy shelter looks like. Not rigid and brittle. Not pretending the earthquake isn't happening. But rooted deep enough, designed well enough, that the sway doesn't bring it down.

That's the fortress the psalmist is pointing at.

Luther found it that way too. When the church cast him aside. When his whole world went upside down. When the Emperor declared him an outlaw. Because he had built his life on the Lord and not on the institution — because his foundation was not the church's approval but Christ — when the earth rumbled and shook, he was able to stand.

Philip, let's sing the 46th Psalm.

---

## IV. The Refrain

You may have noticed the psalm has a refrain. Twice it comes:

*"The LORD of hosts is with us; the God of Jacob is our fortress."* (vv.7, 11)

The psalmist wants this to echo. He wants it to be the thing you're hearing underneath everything else — every illustration, every upheaval, every collapsing nation, every roaring sea.

The LORD of *hosts*. Not just the LORD. The LORD of armies. The Lord who commands the angelic host, who holds the powers of the cosmos in his hand, who breaks the bow and shatters the spear, who burns the chariots with fire.

That LORD.

He is *with us.*

Not watching from a distance. Not observing from a safe remove while the world tears itself apart. *With us.* The same presence that opened the Red Sea. The same presence that shut the mouths of the lions. The same presence that raised Jesus from the dead on the third day.

With us.

---

## V. Be Still and Know

And then — after all of that — verse 10.

*"Be still, and know that I am God. I will be exalted among the nations, I will be exalted in the earth!"*

That is a command, not a suggestion.

The word for *know* here — in Hebrew, *yada* — is one of the deepest words in the Hebrew vocabulary. It means to know fully. To know every aspect and dimension of. But it also means to learn through relationship and experience. The Old Testament uses this same word for the intimate knowledge between a husband and a wife. It's the word used in Genesis 4 for what it means to truly *know* someone.

Those of you who have been married for any length of time know that knowing your spouse is not a one-time event. You are at once the biggest expert on that person and their primary student. You continue learning them. As they grow, as you both change, the knowing deepens.

The word *yada* carries all of that.

God is not asking for a theological acknowledgment. He is not asking you to sign a doctrinal statement. He is asking for you to *know him* — the way you know someone you've spent your whole life with. The way you know someone you've trusted in the dark.

*Be still. And know.*

Stop running. Stop spinning. Stop piling metal bleachers under pine trees trying to build your own shelter. Stop trusting the things that bend and break. Stop.

And know.

As you walk with the Lord — as your faith deepens, as you learn more of him — he allows you to see more of himself. He allows you to be more like him. And as a believer, you have a fortress you can run into and be safely hidden.

But here's the thing — and this is the most important sentence in the sermon — you have to actually *run into it.*

---

## VI. The Only Door

Hebrews 6:18 describes Christians as those *"who have fled for refuge."*

Fled for refuge.

Not people who have acknowledged that a fortress exists. Not people who appreciate the architecture of the fortress from a safe distance. People who fled. People who were in the storm and ran toward the only thing that would hold.

The question is not: *Is God a refuge?* He is. The psalm has made that plain. The nations rage, the kingdoms totter, he utters his voice and the earth melts — that's not a God who can't keep you.

The question is: *Have you fled to him?*

And if you haven't, there's something you need to know.

You can't enter the fortress on your own terms. God is holy. We have sinned. There is a gap between what God requires and what we are — and no amount of moral effort or religious performance closes it. The Moghal people believe their standing is strong enough, their goodness is sufficient, that they can reach heaven on their own. Most people in this room, if they were honest, have leaned on some version of that too.

But that's a metal bleacher under a pine tree. It looks like shelter. It puts you directly in the path of what you're trying to avoid.

The fortress is not entered by effort. It's entered through a door. And the door is Christ.

*"I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."* — John 14:6

God provided his own Son — the Lamb — so that sinners could have access to the Father. The cross is not merely a demonstration of love. It is the mechanism by which God's wrath against sin was satisfied, so that we could stand in his presence without being consumed. Christ took the storm. He took the judgment. He went into the darkness so that you and I could come into the shelter.

*"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."* — Romans 8:1

No condemnation. No storm of judgment. No separation from the God who is a very present help in trouble.

The rock and the hard place that the Bisbee miners faced — the rock and the hard place that all of us face — is the gap between our sin and a holy God. And Jesus Christ is not the hard place. He is the rock.

*"The LORD of hosts is with us; the God of Jacob is our fortress."*

If you have never come to a point in your life where you've fled to him — where you've acknowledged your sin and trusted Christ's death as the only thing that covers it — I want to tell you the door is open right now. You come to God not on the back of your own goodness, but through the cross of his Son.

That is the refuge.

There is no better shelter anywhere.

Be still.

And know that he is God.

---

*Let's pray.*
