We are going to be picking up where we left off two weeks ago in psalms 68, if you've brought your bible, and I hope you have. Psalms is more or less in the middle of your bible, perhaps a little to the left of the middle.

In any event, were going to pick right up where we left off. If you'll recall, we talked about how Our God is a God who can dash his enemies to bits, whether they are nuclear satellites, or chariots, or swords and spears or a virus, or any other thing or weapon that we can't even imagine yet. Nothing is greater than our God. While you're turning there, our people group of the week is the: Japanese of Japan. This people group is interesting, because it is a time when we massively dropped the ball as a national church. After world war two, the Japanese were desperate to learn more about the God that their conquerers worshipped. We had defeated them, and they took it as a sign, as a nation that their animistic Gods, and the buddha weren't powerful enough to protect them, so they obviously needed to learn more about the God that had defeated their religions. General MacArthur called for missionaries to go to japan en masse, and precious few answered his call for reasons related to the war we had just been in mostly. As a result of our refusal to share the gospel with them, they are almost universally Shinto nd buddist still nearly a century later.

They are a wealthy people that live rather comfortably, and so they see little need for any kind of religious change. They don't understand that apart from christ they are lost and heading for Hell.

Who will pray for the Japanese of Japan?

He is the single most powerful being in the universe. We read last week: 1 God shall arise, his enemies shall be scattered; and those who hate him shall flee before him! 2 As smoke is driven away, so you shall drive them away; as wax melts before fire, so the wicked shall perish before God! 3 But the righteous shall be glad; they shall exult before God; they shall be jubilant with joy! 4 Sing to God, sing praises to his name; lift up a song to him who rides through the deserts; his name is the LORD (Yahweh); exult before him! *(Note: "(Yahweh)" is a preacher's explanatory addition; ESV reads "his name is the Lord" — see flags.)*

One thing we didn't cover is that last phrase in vs 4, It mentions "him who rides through the desert". This is a kind of kicking sand in the eye of the baals. Baal you'll recall is a fertility idol. They believe that as Baal rode across the land with his consort Ashtaroth, the rains would come, and they would have fertile land.

So, this is basically saying, the God that brings fertility to desert places – to the places Baal won't or can't go, is named Yahweh. He has no limitations. He can go to the most desolate places and bring fertility with him. He can survive and thrive there without any resources besides himself.

He created the desolate places, he can ride across them if he likes. He can bring them fertility if he likes, he can wash them away and replace them with lush gardens if he wants. There is nothing to difficult for our God. No mountain too tall, no valley too deep. No sin to difficult to over come. There is no limit to his power. No chunk of wood, no idol, not even the idol the Israelites preferred over God a number of times could even hold a candle to the power of God.

Elijah and the Prophets of Baal. I remember a story about this. In fact Elijah's life makes a great illustration here. He started out obeying God, and was thrust into the national spotlight in a big way. God told him to go before the king, and tell the king that he's weak, and the real god is going to keep it from raining. He then storms off, and ends up in a desert place for a while. God has him sit there in the midst of harship and struggle for a long long time. Until the brook completely drys up. I think the /alone/ element is glossed over pretty easily here. This was alone, in a desert, for an extended stretch — the total drought ran three and a half years (James 5:17), though after the brook dried up Elijah was sent to Zarephath, so the full period wasn't all desert solitude. So many of those survival shows with contestants, frequently they are eating, and comfortable, and could make it to the end, but their own thoughts break them. but not elijah. He seems to make it through for a few years till the brook dries up. Its shortly after this that he has the encounter with the prophets of baal. Baal is gross. and so is baal worship. I won't get too deply into it, but its sexual in nature, and they believed that the things they did at the pagan temples caused increased fertility in the land. So the drought was a direct attack on the pagan structures around the nation of israel, and that had been adopted by some in within israel. The king of israels wife, had 850 personal prophets — 450 prophets of Baal and 400 prophets of Asherah — who ate at Jezebel's table. Eijah basically walks in, spits in the face of baal, and thunders out. Then a while later, we have this power encounter between 450 prophets of baal and elijah. The prophets of baal cut up a bull and put it on the alter, and they prayed and prayed and did all the baal worship … things … and still they were met with silence. So Elijah mocked them. Maybe baal is traveling? Maybe hes thinking about something so deeply that he just can't hear you. So ran around their alter, cutting themselves, yelling even louder than before, for hours and hours. Yet nothing happened. Naturally Elijah mocked them again, and I kid you not, its recorded in the bible, elijah says maybe he's using the bathroom? yell louder. So they did. But nothing happened. Finally when the time came and elijah had had enough. He called them all over to him, and said its my turn. So he repaired an alter to God that had existed there but that had been torn down by the prophets of baal, and ordered 4 barrels of water to be dumped on the alter. He had this done two more times. I researched to try and figure out how much water was in a barrel, but the results were unclear. on the small side, one source said 2 gallons to an ancient barrel, another said 4, so that there would have been almost 25- 50 gallons total. Another source said the barrels were closer to 70 gallons, and likely had been filled in the mediteranian. They mention a dry unit of measure called a seah, so they were probably using the smaller measurement of 2 gallons per barrel. Either way, the alter, the bull, the wood was all completely soaked, and the trench around the alter was filled to the brim. "O **LORD**, God of Abraham, Isaac, and Israel, let it be known this day that you are God in Israel, and that I am your servant, and that I have done all these things at your word. 37 Answer me, O **LORD**, answer me, that this people may know that you, O **LORD**, are God, and that you have turned their hearts back." 38 Then the fire of the **LORD** fell and consumed the burnt offering and the wood and the stones and the dust, and licked up the water that was in the trench. 39 And when all the people saw it, they fell on their faces and said, "The **LORD**, he is God; the **LORD**, he is God." 40 And Elijah said to them, "Seize the prophets of Baal; let not one of them escape." And they seized them. And Elijah brought them down to the brook Kishon and slaughtered them there. So God did what Elijah had requested. He didn't just send a little spark. He sent a firestorm that burned up the bull and the wood completely, and fully consumed the stones, and all of the water, even the water in the trench. Talk about a mic drop moment. This is the kind of thing I picture from the first part of this psalm, when the enemies of God are turned to dust. I have this picture of that column of fire that God sent, that even consumed the stone consuming the enemies of God. People talk about Hell being an eternal fire, always burning but never consuming. This passage comes to mind. Maybe God poured out a single drop of his wrath against baal followers on that idol, and what happened? I my minds eye I am envisioning singed beards and eyebrows. Scripture doesn't say so. But we know it was a sudden and severe firey inferno that came down hot enough to burn rocks maybe volcano style. This clearly put the fear of God into the people standing around. Imagine facing the wrath of God for eternity. A preacher I used to know talked about how there is more power in a fragment of God's fingernail clipping than in all of mankind. the alter being burned up was just a small fragment, a drop of water from the ocean. And hell is swimming in the ocean of God's wrath towards ungodliness … for eternity. Eternity is Long folks. Don't be wrong.

So at this point the passage changes tone a bit. Lets read a few more verses starting with vs 5.

With all that power, God is the Father to the Fatherless and the protector of widows.

Taking in children that arent' yours is a challenge. Some of you may know that challenge. I always think about Lindsay taking in Konner and Madison. Or my friend Josh, and his wife Sarah, who adopted two children internationally. You never really know what kind of trauma that child has been through, what genetic issues it will have, that will cause what medical problems. You and I, we just don't know! I remember reading stories of drug babies, born addicted to this drug or that. Going through withdraws in its first few precious days. Being exposed to traumas an infant should never have to know. Those things of course, lead to traumas, and behivors that you have to deal with the rest of the childs life. Adoption is a good thing, a noble thing. But its also a hard, scary, and complicated thing; and exactly the thing God does for us. When we are fatherless, he is our father. He is the father to all of those who follow him. We are born into an addiction to sin, and this world, and that addiction, our love for sin, cost His Son, his life. Our adoption was expensive. My friend, and his wife, payed about a years salary between the lawyers, the international agencies and so forth, and then there was a hickup and they had to start over from scratch and pay it all again. Its taken several years, but praise the Lord God has made their family whole. However whatever the price squeezed out of my friends, compared to the cost Christ paid for you and I to be able to be adopted is miniscule. We come into the relationship with baggage, problems, and sin. But God welcomes us in anyway. Our issues cost his son his life. A price they both willingly paid, so that we could all spend eternity in glory with Christ. With our savior, and with our father.

The second thing he calls himself is the protector of widows. Remember we are talking about a culture that was heavily partriarchical. In their society, a widow had no power, no defense, no income, no protection. No security or stability, or anything. It was a lonely, and desperate existence.

Orphans were equally vulnerable. What baby can protect himself or herself? What infant can survive without their mother feeding them? Nourishing them? Providing them with protection from the elements, the cold, the heat, their own body wastes. Babies are incredibly vulnerable. Like widows were in this culture.

To become a widow, was to be resigned to a life of hunger, and want. A life of difficulty and begging. A life a little like one lost in the desert. Little water, little food. Little in the way of comfort. Yet in that extreme poverty, we have an advocate. Someone who is watching out for us. Who adopts orphans, and seeks out widows. Someone who finds those living in the margins, and most at risk, and takes them into his home. Someone who uses his vast power to disintegrate enemies, to protect and preserve life, even life in the extreme circumstances of the desert, or being an orphan, or a widow.

Verse 6 is another verse that I love. Our saying here is taking strangers, making family. Someone read it for me? " God settles the solitary in a home; he leads out the prisoners to prosperity, but the rebellious dwell in a parched land. "

Settles the solitary makes the most sense in terms of people that don't have others in their life. Orphans, widows, Single parents, people who are alone. Taking strangers, making family. We have family in Christ, we are adopted into the family of the king of kings and lord of lords, as sons and daughters of the most high God.

Likewise he leads prisoners to prosperity. Apart from Christ what do we deserve? Were only owed punishment, and separation from God. One could call eternity in hell, and for that matter this life, enslaved to sin as a prison. But God sets prisoners free, and not just that, he makes them prosper. We who were due a punishment, are instead adopted. What a beautiful thing. All of our baggage and everything. I have this mental picture of being in glory with God. We have a comfy bed, warm meals, people we love, and in the presence of our God. The one who died, and came back to save us.

Those who live apart from God are in a dry and parched land. No support. Orphaned, or widowed, and alone.

Yet the psalm tells us circumstances are different for the Christian.

As God marches ahead of his people, the desert is rained on. The life giving rain falls, and moistens the soil, and rehydrates the seed. That's how many seeds work you know, they have to fall to the ground, wither, and die before they can bring forth whatever kinda of plant they really are. Once they wither and die, they begin to absorb moisture from the soil, and new life shoots forth. As Christians, and followers of Christ, do not be deceived we will still go through hard times. There will still be widows and orphans. There will still be times when we march through the desert. There will still be times where it feels like we are withering and dying.

But take heart little seed, God is doing a work. Hes doing a transformative work in you, and in the people around you. He allows us to go through times of hardship and pain, but were never alone. He's there trimming us where we need trimmed. Watering, and feeding us where we need it. Giving us times of peace, and prosperity, and pain in whatever measure we each individually need to be able to accomplish his plan for us. Ultimately, in my metaphor, his plan for us looks like a seed that has withered, died, rehydrated, grown, shot up little cotyledons, the little seed leaves, grown, and ultimately flowered, and is reproducing dropping seeds all over the place.

A seed doesn't grow into a flower without the hard times though.
One can't appreciate mountain tops without valleys.

The best tools we have to combat those hard times is our relationship with the Lord. When we struggle, read your bible, pray, fellowship with other believers, worship, and evangelism.
I think one knee jerk reaction to hard times that people have is to withdraw. We withdraw when things are hard, or when were ashamed etc. But is that whats best for us? What does the bible say? Proverbs 27:17 says "Iron sharpens iron, and one man sharpens another." Don't pull back, lean into to God, and to your christian brothers and sisters. Sometimes we need to hear from those we love that were being dumb. Sometimes we need to hear from those that love us that we need to improve here or there. Sometimes We need to hear from those same people to stay the course. How can we help each other if we don't fellowship with each other? Another knee jerk reaction people have is to hide whats going on. But does that help you? Its really another way of doing the same thing as when someone withdraws. I've been watching a show lately called alone. Its about a group of people that are individually dropped off alone, and expected to survive. They have a satelite phone, and can call for help if they end up hugging a bear, or something crazy. Otherwise they only eat and drink what they can find, and cook. They spend hours chipping little snails called limpets off rocks, and eating them. Probably takes more energy to gather them, than they gain from eating them. Withdrawing from God and from those that love you is like walking past the most delectable buffet, and hunting limpets while slowly starving.
We have so much in our world that needs prayer – our leadership, elections this year, things going on in each of our families, with our health. with our community, and on and on. So I think its important that we spend some time at the buffet table, or said differently in prayer this morning.

For anything and everything I've mentioned already, and that I should have mentioned but didn't. For anything that God puts on your heart.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Warmth With an Underdeveloped Exposition — The Gospel Arrives but Doesn't Land**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm, shepherd-hearted opening of Psalm 68:1-6 that connects God's power to his care for the vulnerable and draws a genuine gospel application, while leaving the text's own argument largely undeveloped and the conscience mostly unaddressed.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with qualification.**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's main point — that the all-powerful God is father to the fatherless and protector of widows — does arise from Psalm 68:5-6, which is the text's pivot. The sermon handles this legitimately. However, the first half of the sermon (on Baal, Elijah, 1 Kings 18) is a lengthy excursus that delays arrival at this point, and the text's own logic (vv. 1-4 as the theophanic march leading into vv. 5-6's tender care) is never explicitly traced. The main point passes; the argument of the passage is not fully honored.

*Authority Test*: Pass. The preacher submits to the text. He does not soften or editorialize against the passage's claims. The hard edges (hell, wrath, the exclusivity of Christ) are named, even if briefly.

**One flag triggered — score cap at 69 applies.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 13/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10)*: 6/10. The sermon covers the text's content but does not trace its argument. Psalm 68:1-6 is a theophanic march psalm — the God who scatters enemies (vv. 1-3) is the same God who fathers orphans (vv. 5-6). This contrast is the sermon's spine, and it is gestured at ("the passage changes tone a bit") but never unpacked with any exegetical force. Verse 4's anti-Baal polemic is handled well. The 1 Kings 18 illustration is long enough to become the sermon's body, displacing the text itself as the center of gravity.
- *Contextual Awareness (5)*: 3/5. The Baal polemic is handled with cultural intelligence. The covenantal context — Israel's march, the Sinai theophany, God's historical faithfulness — is absent. The psalm's place in the Psalter's theology of the divine warrior is untouched.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5)*: 2/5. No Greek or Hebrew engagement beyond naming "Yahweh" in passing. The word "settles" in v. 6 is significant (the Hebrew *mושׁיב*, to cause to dwell) but not opened. No explanation of why the psalm uses "Father" language for God.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5)*: 2/5. The Elijah illustration, while vivid and substantively accurate, runs approximately 700 words and displaces the text. The sermon's logic is more illustrative-associative than text-driven. The fallen condition (human vulnerability, lostness, fatherlessness — spiritual and physical) is implied but never named precisely.

**Category 2 — Theology & Doctrinal Integrity: 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7)*: 5/7. The adoption metaphor produces a genuine and moving gospel connection: "Our adoption was expensive... a price they both willingly paid." This is organic to the text — Psalm 68's "father to the fatherless" language points forward to adoption theology. The connection is real, not forced.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3)*: 1/3. The psalm's place in Israel's history — the exodus, the Sinai march, the ongoing divine-warrior motif — is absent. The sermon treats it as a timeless devotional rather than a covenantal text in a redemptive-historical arc.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4)*: 3/4. The gospel moments are genuine and mostly accurate. "Apart from Christ what do we deserve? We're only owed punishment and separation from God" is sound. The adoption language is theologically appropriate. Minor imprecision: "A price they both willingly paid" is accurate but could be slightly tightened to clarify the distinction between the Father's giving and the Son's offering.
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse (4)*: 3/4. No contradiction of confessional theology. The doctrines of grace are not explicitly engaged, which is understandable for this text. No drift into Arminian or moralistic framing.

**Category 3 — Gospel Centrality & Force: 9/15**

An unbeliever could hear this sermon and understand that Christ died for sinners, and that adoption is available — but the mechanics of how (faith, repentance, imputation) are not stated. The gospel is present but not fully proclaimed.
- *Necessity of Christ (5)*: 4/5. Christ is necessary to the sermon's argument — the adoption passage would not function without him. He is not merely decorative.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (5)*: 2/5. The sermon moves toward warmth and comfort rather than the cross. The hell/wrath section (Elijah, fire from heaven) is vivid but disconnected from the cross — the sermon describes God's wrath against Baal-worshippers but never explicitly connects that wrath to what Christ absorbed on behalf of those who were themselves Baal-worshippers by nature. The hammer lands; the balm relieves too quickly without sufficient law-work.

**Category 4 — Conscience & Applicatory Force: 7/15**

But the conscience is not searched — no one is cornered by the text. The hard-hearted member, the nominal Christian, the one who trusts in their own sufficiency — none is directly addressed.

The specific application of Psalm 68 — that the God of terrifying power is tenderly near to the most vulnerable — is not pressed into specific life circumstances beyond a general encouragement.

2. **The Baal polemic is culturally intelligent and exegetically grounded.** The explanation of "him who rides through the deserts" as a direct challenge to Baal's domain is accurate, clear, and interesting. The cultural background enriches the text rather than displacing it here.

3. **Congregational specificity and illustration are consistently drawn from real life.** Named families, the *Alone* show, limpets, specific prayer requests — this sermon is clearly addressed to an actual community, not a generic audience. The limpet-vs-buffet illustration is genuinely memorable.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The 1 Kings 18 Elijah narrative displaces the text as the sermon's body.** Running approximately 700 words, it occupies the central position in the sermon while Psalm 68:1-6 receives proportionally far less attention. The Elijah story illustrates a point made in Part 1 (God's power over enemies). In Part 2, it becomes the structural anchor — which means the sermon is largely built on an illustration of a prior sermon's point, not on the passage at hand. The text's own argument (theophanic march → tender care for the vulnerable) never fully emerges.

2. **The closing application is generic and text-detached.** "Read your bible, pray, fellowship with other believers, worship, and evangelism" (closing section) could close any sermon from any text. It does not arise from Psalm 68's specific claims. The text's powerful peculiarity — that the God of fire-from-heaven specifically seeks out widows and fatherless children — is not converted into specific, conscience-level application.

3. **The law is not allowed to work before the gospel relieves it.** The wrath/hell moment (fire consuming the altar, "imagine facing the wrath of God for eternity") is vivid, but it does not press the hearer personally before comfort arrives. The sequence moves from wrath-as-spectacle to gospel-comfort without a personal indictment of the hearer's own sin and need. The hammer does not land before the balm is applied.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how much of the sermon's work is being done by the Elijah narrative rather than by Psalm 68. The illustration feels like the body of the sermon because it is vivid and familiar — but in occupying that structural position, it has quietly demoted the text to a framing device. The preacher is also likely unaware that the congregation's most vulnerable members (those experiencing actual loneliness, grief, or abandonment) are addressed in the illustration (adoption, widowhood) but not directly — the sermon speaks *about* them rather than *to* them.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Elijah passage feels like the sermon's strongest section because it is the most detailed, vivid, and scripturally dense. The 1 Kings 18 material is accurate, well-researched, and preached with energy. But this is illustrative strength masking expositional weakness: the sermon is more confident about 1 Kings 18 than about Psalm 68. The congregation leaves with a vivid picture of Elijah's contest — and a somewhat thinner understanding of what Psalm 68 actually says and why it was written.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 6b — "he leads out the prisoners to prosperity" — is a profound gospel text. The Hebrew word translated "prosperity" (*כּוֹשָׁרוֹת*, kosharot) is unusual and disputed, but the image is striking: prisoners led to flourishing. This is not merely a picture of social liberation — it is an image of radical reversal, the kind of reversal the gospel produces. The sermon mentions this verse but does not open it. A full paragraph on the prisoner-to-prosperity reversal — applied both to Israel's history and to the gospel's work in the sinner's life — would have been this sermon's most powerful moment and would have directly strengthened the weakest category (conscience and applicatory force).

### 14. Surgical Fixes (4)

1. **Reduce the Elijah narrative by half and return that time to Psalm 68.** The key beats of 1 Kings 18 (drought, contest, fire, Baal's silence) can be communicated in 300 words. The remaining 400 words should be given to Psalm 68:5-6's specific argument: why does a God of devastating power call himself "father to the fatherless"? What is the psalm saying about God's character by juxtaposing these images? That contrast is the sermon's theological heart and it is currently underdeveloped.

2. **Add direct address to the widow, the orphan, and the abandoned in the congregation.** The sermon speaks *about* vulnerable people but not *to* them. After the adoption passage, add something like: "If you are alone today — if you have lost your husband, your father, your family — this text is not about someone else. This God is making a specific claim about you. He is not generically benevolent. He is your father. This is his promise to you." This moves the sermon from warm teaching to pastoral proclamation.

3. **Insert a law-work moment before the gospel.** Before the adoption illustration, press the hearer's personal guilt: "But notice — God doesn't adopt people who deserve it. He adopts people who are addicted to sin, who have worshipped Baals of their own making — comfort, money, approval, self. You and I are not the noble missionaries MacArthur called for. We are the Japanese. We were Baal-worshippers. And this God chose us anyway." This applies the law personally before the gospel relieves it.

4. **Replace the generic closing application with one text-specific imperative.** Instead of the general spiritual disciplines list, close with something arising directly from Psalm 68: "Because God is father to the fatherless, your job this week is to find one person in this church who is alone — one widow, one single parent, one person sitting at the edge of this community — and become the instrument of what this psalm promises. God fathers the fatherless through his people. Go be that." This is specific, exhortatory, corporate, and text-derived.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon does not score high enough in exposition or theology to trigger the dead-orthodoxy diagnostic. The pastoral warmth is genuine, not bloodless. The weaknesses here are developmental, not attitudinal.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The sermon carries genuine pastoral warmth and a real love for the congregation — named families, specific prayer requests, a shepherd's attention to actual people — but does not yet carry the full eternal weight of Psalm 68, a text written to make God's terrifying tenderness press on the soul. +2/5.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Psalm 68 is a theophanic hymn — a text that celebrates God's historical and eschatological march on behalf of his people. It belongs to the genre of the divine-warrior psalm, which means its logic is covenantal and historical before it is applicatory. The rubric's Exposition and Redemptive-Historical categories press appropriately on this text — a sermon on Psalm 68 that does not situate God's fatherhood within the covenantal march is genuinely missing something. The Conscience & Applicatory Force categories are slightly harder to score on a psalm than on an epistle, because psalms are primarily doxological — they are addressed to God, not to us. The rubric bends slightly here: the applicatory categories are legitimate but should be measured against the grain of the genre. A psalm sermon that produces worship and wonder alongside application is functioning well. This sermon produces warmth; it has not yet produced either the worship the psalm calls for or the specific application the congregation needs.
