### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a final sentence, not a proclamation.** "If you’re here and you don’t yet have a relationship with Jesus Christ, the place to start is the gospel" — this is a directional sign, not the gospel itself. The sermon ends here. The Barnhouse quote has already said something more specifically Christian than this closing line. The manuscript ends before the sermon’s most important moment.

2. **The psalm’s pilgrimage theology (vv.5-8) is not developed.** The valley of Baca section (v.6) is pastorally applied — the congregation’s hardships are identified. But the psalm’s own argument about the valley of Baca is that the pilgrim going through it finds it transformed into a place of springs, going from strength to strength until they appear before God (vv.6-7). This is one of the most pastorally powerful ideas in the Psalter: the journey through the valley of tears is itself the road to God’s presence, and God’s presence transforms the valley. The sermon applies the valley compassionately but does not preach the valley’s transformation.

3. **Verses 8-12 receive almost no treatment.** The second half of the psalm — the petition ("O LORD God of hosts, hear my prayer"), the declaration that God is a sun and shield, "no good thing does he withhold from those who walk uprightly," and the final beatitude "blessed is the one who trusts in you" — are the psalm’s own crescendo. These verses are the sermon’s missed climax: "no good thing does he withhold" directly answers the congregation’s unanswered needs (the unemployed woman, the abandoned boy). The sermon stops short of the psalm’s own answer to the valley of weeping.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher has a strong instinct for congregational care — he knows his people’s stories, he names their sufferings, he moves with warmth toward those who are hurting. The blind spot is that this pastoral warmth occasionally substitutes for the text’s own pastoral power. The psalm itself speaks to suffering, promises transformation of the valley of tears, and declares "no good thing does he withhold." The preacher reaches for this truth through his own pastoral illustrations rather than letting the text deliver it with its own authority. The congregation hears the preacher’s compassion but not always the text’s voice.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The 12 attributes section (via David Platt) creates the impression of theological density — a list of God’s characteristics enumerated with care. But listing attributes is not the same as declaring them with proclamatory force. "He’s the Lord who keeps his promises, and commands all of the heavenly hosts, heavenly armies, and he’s living and active, and beside us, and he’s my God" is accurate but runs together without development. The list feels like theological substance but functions more as an outline of what could be preached. The boyfriend/girlfriend illustration that follows it (v.4 — "My God, My King") is actually more effective at conveying covenant personal relationship than the list.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Psalm 84:11 — "For the LORD God is a sun and shield; the LORD bestows favor and honor. No good thing does he withhold from those who walk uprightly" — is the sermon’s greatest missed opportunity. This verse is the psalm’s direct answer to the valley of Baca congregation: the same God who is present in the temple is the God who withholds no good thing from those who trust Him. Applied to the unemployed woman, the abandoned boy, the grieving families at Christmas — this verse is the Word of God speaking with authority to the specific sorrows the preacher has named. "No good thing does he withhold" is a promise that would land with force precisely because the congregation’s needs have already been named. The sermon stops inches from the text’s own answer.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach vv. 5-7 as the psalm’s pilgrimage theology:** "The psalm doesn’t just tell us that God’s presence is better than anywhere else. It tells us how to get there. You go through the valley of Baca — the valley of tears. And here is what God does: He turns it into a place of springs. The one who passes through that valley goes from strength to strength. The journey through your suffering is itself the road to His presence. God is not absent in the valley. He is turning it into a fountain."

2. **Preach v.11 directly to the congregation’s named needs:** "For the LORD God is a sun and shield — the LORD bestows favor and honor. No good thing does he withhold from those who walk uprightly. For the woman who needs groceries this week, and doesn’t know how rent will be paid — no good thing does He withhold. For the boy whose mother left — no good thing does He withhold. The God who is better than a thousand days anywhere else is the God who withholds no good thing from those who trust Him."

3. **Develop the Barnhouse quote into a gospel proclamation:** "Barnhouse said ‘here are the sparrows who find their nest at the cross of Jesus Christ — worthlessness that finds its worth because the Savior died.’ That is the gospel. You come to the courts of God not because you are worthy, but because a sparrow has worth in His presence. You come not because you have earned it, but because Christ paid for it. He went to the cross so that the restless could find rest. He was forsaken so that you would not be forsaken. The place to find rest is not just ‘the gospel’ — it is the cross of Jesus Christ, where the Son of God died for sparrows."

4. **Close with an exhortation:** "So here is what Psalm 84 calls you to: long for God’s presence more than you long for anything else. And if you have never entered that presence — if you are still on the outside, watching the door — the way in is through Jesus Christ. He is the true temple, the true dwelling place of God with man. Come. The doors are open. Better is one day in His courts than thousands anywhere else."

5. **Trim the worship discussion** (vv.2 Baptist emotional reserve section) from 4 paragraphs to 2. The insight is valid but it interrupts the psalm’s movement and consumes homiletical time better spent on vv.8-12.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon has genuine pastoral fire and personal investment. The failure is structural (incomplete, gospel as final sentence) rather than cold or bloodless. The preacher loves his congregation and his text. The doctrine is not the problem.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries genuine pastoral weight — the murdered baby, the grieving families, the woman without groceries at Christmas — and reaches toward the God who is present in the valley of tears with real affection. But the psalm’s own doxological crescendo (vv.10-12) and its New Testament fulfillment in Christ as the true temple are not proclaimed, and the sermon ends before the full weight of "no good thing does he withhold" and "blessed is the one who trusts in you" can land on the congregation.

**Bonus: +2/5**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 84 is a pilgrimage psalm — a song of longing and ascent, marked by affective intensity ("my soul longs, my heart and flesh cry out") and pilgrimage theology (the journey through the valley of Baca). The rubric applies well, though two adjustments are worth noting. First, the Illustration & Imagination category should be read with the understanding that a pilgrimage psalm’s primary mode is lyric rather than narrative — the sparrow and swallow images are the text’s own illustrations, not additions, and treating them as such rewards the preacher who draws them out rather than importing external imagery. The sermon does this well. Second, the Sermonic Force category should note that pilgrimage psalms have a natural climactic arc (longing → journey → arrival → petition → beatitude) that invites the sermon to mirror the psalm’s own emotional movement — from longing, through the valley, to rest. This arc is present in the psalm but not fully traced in the sermon. No rubric category is strained by this genre; the pilgrimage shape actually clarifies what the exposition category is looking for.
