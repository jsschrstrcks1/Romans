### 1. Verdict Title

**"Warm Pastoral Affection with Uneven Exposition — The Shepherd Is Present but the Gospel Is a Footnote"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A conversationally warm continuation of Psalm 23 that contains genuine pastoral illustrations and affection for the congregation, but lacks the exegetical depth and gospel clarity that the text's rich imagery could support, and concludes with a placeholder ("Gospel.") rather than a proclamation.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**PASS — tentatively.**

**Scopus Test:** The sermon's thesis — the shepherd's active care, provision, and guidance for His sheep, culminating in the assurance that goodness and mercy will follow us all our days — arises genuinely from Psalm 23:4-6. The staff/rod imagery, the table, the anointed head, the overflowing cup, and the goodness/mercy conclusion are all developed to varying degrees.

**Authority Test:** The preacher does not contradict the text. He submits to its pastoral claims. However, the text's richest theological content — the valley of the shadow of death, the rod and staff that comfort in the presence of genuine threat, the enemy's presence at the table — is touched lightly rather than proclaimed with the weight the imagery deserves.

**Flag 1 triggered:** "Gospel." as a single word at the sermon's end is not a gospel presentation. It indicates where the gospel was intended to appear, not where it was proclaimed. A congregation hearing this sermon did not receive the gospel. Score cap: maximum 69.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a placeholder, not a proclamation:** "Gospel." is the sermon's final word before the closing affirmation. This is not a gospel presentation — it is a note to the preacher indicating where the gospel should go. A congregation sitting through this sermon on a Sunday morning did not hear the gospel. No Christ, no cross, no sin, no substitution, no call to faith. This is the sermon's most serious structural failure.

2. **"The valley of the shadow of death" receives insufficient weight:** This is the psalm's most famous and most searched verse — the verse most people know, and the verse most people need. "I will fear no evil, for you are with me" — this is the psalm's own gospel moment, the assurance of God's presence in the worst possible circumstances. The sermon names the verse and references it in passing but does not dwell there. The people in this congregation who are walking through actual valleys need this verse to be proclaimed with force.

3. **The anointing of the head (v.5) is completely absent:** "You anoint my head with oil" appears in the psalm but not in the sermon. The anointing is rich with significance — hospitality, healing, the anointing of kings and priests, and ultimately the Messianic title ("Messiah" = "Anointed One"). This is the verse with the most direct Christological resonance in the entire psalm, and it goes unaddressed.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher's strength is pastoral warmth and genuine affection for his congregation. The blind spot is the assumption that warmth *about* the Shepherd constitutes proclamation *of* the Shepherd. The congregation hears how good and caring God is — which is true and needed — but does not hear why His goodness is available to them specifically, and on what basis. Psalm 23 is a resurrection-side psalm: the comfort it promises is not available in the abstract — it is available because the Good Shepherd has gone through the valley of death and come out the other side. This is the theological ground of the psalm's assurance, and the sermon does not reach it.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The warm pastoral tone and the attractive illustrations (mesa, sheep dogs, overflowing gas pump) create the impression of a complete, nourishing sermon. The congregation leaves feeling good about God's care. This warmth masks the fact that the deepest verse of the psalm (v.4) is underpreached, the Christological center is absent, and the gospel itself is a missing placeholder. A congregation that leaves feeling warm but has not heard the gospel has not been fully served.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

"Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me" — this is the missed opportunity of the sermon, and perhaps of the entire batch. This verse is not primarily about difficult circumstances. It is about the presence of God in the face of *death itself*. And the New Testament's answer to it is specific: the reason a believer can walk through the shadow of death without fear is that Jesus Christ has walked through death and emerged on the other side. "O death, where is your victory? O death, where is your sting?" (1 Cor 15:55) is the New Testament expansion of Psalm 23:4. This is the sermon's natural climax, and it was not reached.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Develop v.4 with the weight it deserves:** "This isn't 'hard times.' The Hebrew is *tsalmaveth* — shadow of death. David is walking through a situation where death is genuinely near. And in that moment of mortal threat, he doesn't say 'I will fear no evil, because I'm tough.' He says 'I will fear no evil, for *you are with me*.'" Develop the nature of this comfort — God's actual presence — and then connect it to Christ: "The Good Shepherd doesn't send comfort from a distance. He walks the valley with His sheep. And He knows what that valley costs — because He walked through it Himself. And He didn't come out defeated."

2. **Add at least one sentence on the anointing of the head:** "David says, 'You anoint my head with oil.' In the ancient world, a host anointed a guest's head with oil as a sign of honor, welcome, and blessing. The Messiah — the 'Anointed One' — is named because God's anointing rested on Him. And through Him, that anointing now rests on you. You are not just a guest at the table. You are an honored, welcomed, anointed son or daughter."

3. **Replace the "Gospel." placeholder with an actual gospel paragraph:** Even two or three sentences: "The reason you can say 'The Lord is MY shepherd' is because of what happened at the cross. Jesus Christ, the Good Shepherd, said 'I lay down my life for the sheep' (John 10:15) — and He did. He walked through the valley of the shadow of death in your place, so that death has lost its final sting for everyone who is in Him. If you are His sheep — if you've trusted in Him — then these promises are yours. If you haven't — this is the invitation: come to the Shepherd who laid down His life for you."

4. **Add a closing exhortation:** The sermon ends with warmth but no imperative. Close with: "This week, when you're tempted to want what God hasn't given — remember: the Lord is your shepherd, you shall not want. When you're in a dark place — remember: He is with you. When your cup runs over — tell someone. Tell your children. And if the Shepherd is not yet yours — He is calling your name."
