- ⚠️ **Edwards quote** ("just the sin that made salvation necessary") *(researched March 2026)*: Full circulating form: "You contribute nothing to your salvation except the sin that made it necessary." **Attribution to Edwards is almost certainly false.** Exhaustive search of the Yale critical edition (26 volumes, edwards.yale.edu) — no result. The idea is sound Reformed theology; either state it as such or attribute to Temple with the primary source.**
- ⚠️ **MacArthur quote** ("Christians should serve righteousness exactly like they served sin — as slaves"): Not a recoverable direct MacArthur quote. Searched GTY sermons 45-48, 45-49 (Romans 6:15–23, Jan 1983) and 90-302 (Romans 6:15–23, Nov 2005) — none contain this phrasing. MacArthur's actual documented language: *"As you yielded your bodily parts slaves to uncleanness before Christ, so they should now be 100 percent yielded to righteousness since Christ"* (90-302). The "exactly like … as slaves" formulation is a reader's paraphrase that circulates with his name attached. **Attribute to Romans 6:18–19 directly, or quote MacArthur's actual language from 90-302 if a MacArthur reference is wanted.**

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The sermon is incomplete.** This is the dominant reality. The sermon announces the gospel in its final line and stops. Hebrews 2:8-9 — the christological climax of both texts — is not expounded. ### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that Hebrews 2:6-9 is the sermon's engine and has been left idling. The passage is identified and a listener is asked to turn there — and then it is not opened. The reason Hebrews quotes Psalm 8 is the sermon's entire christological point: the psalm says "you have made him a little lower than the heavenly beings and crowned him with glory and honor," and Hebrews says this refers to Jesus — humiliated in death, exalted in resurrection, the true human who accomplished what Adam failed to do. This is the sermon's gold. The preacher is standing next to it without mining it.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The opening (Caño Cristales, coral reef, Notre Dame, "if you think majesty can be summed up in things that man can make...") is strong enough that the sermon feels like it has arrived when it has only just begun. The illustrations give a sense of fullness and momentum. But they are all pre-text — they set the stage without ever raising the curtain. The sermon's apparent strength in its first third masks the fact that its theological core (Heb 2:8-9; the gospel) has not yet appeared.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Hebrews 2:9 is one of the most compressed and powerful christological statements in the New Testament: "but we see him who for a little while was made lower than the angels, namely Jesus, crowned with glory and honor because of the suffering of death, so that by the grace of God he might taste death for everyone." The phrase "taste death for everyone" (ὑπὲρ παντὸς γεύσηται θανάτου) is the gospel in a sentence — the eternal Son, made lower than the angels, tasting the full cup of death in the place of every person who would believe. The entire sermon's opening movement — creation's grandeur, human smallness, God's condescension — reaches its climax here. The sermon walks up to this verse and stops.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (5)

1. **Complete the sermon.** The most urgent surgical fix is not a fix — it is a completion. The gospel section must be written. Hebrews 2:8-9 must be expounded. The movement should be: (a) We are small (Ps 8:4); (b) We were made for glory but fell (Heb 2:7-8a); (c) We do not yet see that glory fulfilled in humanity — but we see Jesus (Heb 2:8b-9); (d) He tasted death so we would not have to — crown given through cross; (e) This is the only answer to human smallness and guilt: not self-improvement, but the exalted Son who was first humiliated for you.

2. **Remove or correct the Adonai/Trinity explanation.** Change the line about Adonai "meaning 'your majesties' and referring to the Trinity" to something accurate: "Adonai is God's formal title — it's the word for 'Lord' as authority and ruler. The Jews eventually used it as a substitute for God's personal name Yahweh because they considered that name too holy to speak. And so in one breath David is calling out to God intimately — 'Yahweh, my God, my Savior' — and formally — 'my King and Lord.'"

3. **State the sermon's main point explicitly within the first three minutes.** After the Caño Cristales opening, before the Platt story, state the thesis: "Psalm 8 asks a question that the whole Bible is answering: who is man, that God is mindful of him? And the answer, in the end, is not what you expect. The answer is Jesus." This gives the congregation a spine to hang everything on.

4. **Connect each illustration explicitly to its verse.** The Platt story illustrates the gospel's transforming power (death-to-life) — say so explicitly and name the verse it serves. The dying neighbor illustrates the reality of hell and the urgency of the gospel — say so and name the verse. "This is what Psalm 8:4 is asking — who is this small, mortal creature that God bothers with him? And the neighbor's father is the answer to why that question matters."

5. **Add a direct address to the person who attends church and considers themselves a Christian but has never been born again.** The Ryle quote ("cheap Christianity that costs nothing and is worth nothing") is the right diagnosis but it is currently aimed at the air. After quoting Ryle, add: "I want to speak directly to someone in this room who has been sitting in churches for years. You know the language. You've heard the Platt story before. You nodded when I quoted Ryle. But you have never actually trusted Christ. You have been a student of religion, not a slave of righteousness. This is for you." This is Perkins' category three — the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled — and it is currently unaddressed.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not applicable.** The sermon does not score high enough in any category to trigger the dead-orthodoxy pattern. The problem is incompleteness, not bloodlessness. Where the sermon does preach — the Yahweh/Adonai section, the dying neighbor, the Ryle quote — there is genuine pastoral warmth.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The Caño Cristales opening and the Yahweh/Adonai section carry genuine wonder and reverence — the preacher is moved by the majesty he is describing, and it shows. But the sermon does not yet carry eternal weight because it does not yet preach the eternal transaction: the Son of God made lower than the angels, tasting death, rising in glory. When that section is written, this sermon could carry significant weight. +1/5 for what is present.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Psalm 8 is a creation hymn and the rubric handles it appropriately — the Exposition categories press legitimately on whether the psalm's own argument (grandeur → smallness → dignity → dominion) is traced. The challenge with this particular sermon is that the preacher has paired the psalm with Hebrews 2:6-9, which is itself an expository sermon on the psalm (the New Testament's own handling of it). This is an excellent homiletical choice and a demanding one: the preacher is not just preaching Psalm 8, he is preaching the New Testament's christological reading of it. The rubric's Gospel Centrality and Christ-Centeredness categories are, if anything, more pressing here than usual — because Hebrews 2 removes any ambiguity about whether Psalm 8 is about Christ. It is. A sermon that brings these two texts together and does not arrive at Hebrews 2:9 has declined the New Testament's own invitation. The rubric fits this text well. The genre is not straining it.
