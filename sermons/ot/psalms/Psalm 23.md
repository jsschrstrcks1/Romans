Psalm 23

The LORD Is My Shepherd

A Psalm of David.

[1] The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want.
[2] 	He makes me lie down in green pastures.
He leads me beside still waters.
[3] 	He restores my soul.
He leads me in paths of righteousness
for his name’s sake.
[4] Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death,
I will fear no evil,
for you are with me;
your rod and your staff,
they comfort me.
[5] You prepare a table before me
in the presence of my enemies;
you anoint my head with oil;
my cup overflows.
[6] Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me
all the days of my life,
and I shall dwell in the house of the LORD
forever. (ESV)

Today that is exactly where Barbara is. I’ve had the priveledge of being her pastor for the past several years now. In fact, her first year at the church, she’d only been a handful of times, and I got a call that she had experienced a stroke. She was just over here at bayonet point, so I ran over to visit her. I had no idea that the only word she could say at this point was no. 

So I walked in and asked her how she was doing, and she told me empahtically NO. I asked how the food was, and once more no. I tried to make small talk for a few minutes but every question, she told me no. We laughed about it later, but I left thinking I had offended her. 

The last time I saw her was when we did a church service at the assisted living facility she has been living at. When I went to make sure they were up and ready her whole face lit up. Barbara had a smile that could lite up a room. 

One thing I know for sure is that today for her, her faith has become sight. Because I know Barbara I can say with certainty when she took that last breath she woke up in the presence of our savior, and Jesus said to her “well done my good and faithful servant”. Shes finally home where her lungs aren’t betraying her any more. She can walk and move and breathe freely without issue. I doubt she would be inclined to, but if she wanted to with her new body she could run and jump and play. No more tears. No more sorrow. No sickness. Just joy and the presence of God. Up there with Jesus who as our passage says leads us beside the still waters, through the green pastures. He restores our soul. He knows what we need, and he leads us to those things. People have asked why a rod and staff, is Jesus going to hit his sheep? The rod and staff are there ot protect the sheep. If a wolf comes around, the rod can be used to fight off the wolf. The staff is for sheep that are prone to wander. I have sheep, and we use it when we need to treat a sheep too. We have one now that has injured himself, and he’s just heatlhy enough that he doesn’t think he needs any more antibiotics, but we know, if he doesn’t get a few more days worth of treatments, that it can come back with a vengeance. So when hes feeling sassy, we can use the staff to rein him in, and give him the medicine that he needs. Its like that for us too. Sometmies we are prone to wander, and God lovingly, and gently, draws us back to himself. God is so good to us, even in the midst of hard things. And he loves us so much. 

And One thing I know Barbara  wanted more than anything else is for all of you to be up there with her one day. If you follow Jesus than one day you will be. Maybe you’re here today and you want to become a Christian, and you want to go where Barbara went. That starts with the gospel. Or maybe youre here today and you realize like my sheep, you’re prone to wander, and you want God to help you stay away from this or that, then I think that starts with the gospel too. 

All of that starts with the gospel. 

Repent and Believe.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Pastoral Eulogy With Gospel Instincts But No Sermonic Substance

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A graveside homily for Barbara that leans on Psalm 23's comfort and gestures toward the gospel, but does not open the text, press the conscience, or preach with the weight the passage demands.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**RESULT: CONDITIONAL PASS — with significant reservation.**

- **Scopus Test**: The sermon does not articulate a thesis that arises from Psalm 23's argument. The passage's movement from provision (v.1–2) → restoration (v.3) → fearlessness in death (v.4) → covenant feast (v.5) → eternal covenant faithfulness (v.6) is never traced. The sermon mentions several images from the text but does not expose the psalm's own main point. The closest the sermon comes to a claim is "God loves us and leads us even through hard things" — which is gestured at rather than argued from the text.
- **Authority Test**: The preacher does not stand above the text as editor, but he also does not really stand under it as herald. He stands beside it, using it as ambient backdrop for a pastoral tribute. The psalm is referenced but not exposited. It functions as a frame, not a foundation.

**Ruling**: This is a funeral homily, not a sermon. The Berean Gate is passed in the weakest possible sense — the sermon does not contradict the text — but it does not arise from it. No score cap is imposed, but the funeral genre must be noted, and the evaluation proceeds with full awareness that this is not a standard expository sermon. The Rubric Fit Note (element 17) will address this directly.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Three flags triggered. Score capped at 69.**

- **Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: "That starts with the gospel" and "Repent and Believe" are present, but no specific application is made to different hearers present. No one is cornered. No conscience is pressed. The call is gestured at but not made.
- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon tells the congregation what they might want (to be where Barbara is) and points vaguely to the gospel, but it never summons anyone. "Repent and Believe" is appended as a phrase, not proclaimed as a call. The congregation was not summoned; they were informed.
- **Flag 2 (partial) — Main point does not equal text's point**: The text's main point is the LORD's covenant faithfulness as shepherd across all of life including death, culminating in eternal dwelling. The sermon's main point is essentially "Barbara is with Jesus, and you can be too." This is a true statement, but it is not Psalm 23's statement. The text's own argument is not followed.

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 9 / 25**

Verses 5 and 6 — the feast in the presence of enemies and the declaration of eternal covenant dwelling — receive no treatment at all. The psalm ends with "I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever," which is the climactic anchor for the entire message of the day, and it is passed over.

- *Exegetical Clarity*: 2/5 — The rod and staff explanation is the most exegetical moment in the sermon. It is functional and genuine (particularly the personal illustration about the injured sheep), but it is isolated from the text's flow and amounts to a word study without structural purpose.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 10 / 18**

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

**Strength 1 — Genuine pastoral warmth and authentic relationship.**
The Barbara narrative is not a manufactured illustration — it is real pastoral care on display. "I got a call that she had experienced a stroke... This is native, memorable, and genuinely illuminates the pastoral and protective function of rod and staff. A preacher who actually owns sheep has a gift most expositors of this psalm lack. This illustration should be expanded, not discarded.

**Strength 3 — The gospel call is present and directionally correct.**
"Repent and Believe" is the right answer. The recognition that both the unbeliever and the wandering believer need the same gospel is a sound pastoral instinct. The seed is there. The problem is not the direction of the call but the absence of the call's substance and force.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

**Weakness 1 — Psalm 23 is never opened.**
The sermon quotes the psalm in full at the top, then selectively references a few images, but the psalm's own argument is never traced. The climactic verse — "I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever" (v.6) — is not exposited at all, even though it is the most directly relevant verse for a funeral sermon and provides the theological foundation for everything the preacher wants to say about Barbara. This is the psalm's main declaration. The sermon ends without reaching it as a text.

**Weakness 2 — The gospel is named but not proclaimed.**
"All of that starts with the gospel" appears twice. The cross appears zero times. Christ's atonement is not mentioned. Justification is not explained. An attentive visitor who does not know what the gospel is would leave knowing it involves repentance and belief but not knowing why, what, or how. At a funeral — when unbelievers are in the room, grief has softened the ground, and the Holy Spirit has prepared hearts — this is the supreme missed opportunity. The gospel must not merely be referenced; it must be declared.

**Weakness 3 — No specific address to the varieties of mourners present.**
A funeral gathers the full range of Perkins' hearers in one room. The hardened unbeliever who came for the reception. The believing widow in grief. The doubter who is watching death and wondering if God is real. The backslider who is feeling the pull of mortality. The faithful friend of Barbara whose faith is being tested by the loss. None of these people are addressed specifically. The sermon sees two categories (unbeliever and wanderer) and addresses both generically.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's emotional success (genuine warmth, real connection, visible pastoral love) is masking its theological thinness. The congregation will feel cared for — and they should, because they are — but they will leave with sentiment rather than covenant truth. The pastor's relationship with Barbara and his genuine grief create a pastoral credibility that can carry a moment that the text alone has not carried. That is a gift in the room, but it is not the same as the Word doing the work.

The preacher also likely does not see that Psalm 23's covenant structure (the LORD as the covenant God who has bound Himself to His sheep) is the most powerful theological resource available for the moment he is in — and he has not touched it. "Goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life" is hesed — covenant lovingkindness — not just general kindness. That word carries the full weight of God's binding covenant faithfulness. It is exactly the word grieving people need to hear on a day like this.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon feels strong because it is emotionally resonant and the occasion is grave. The Barbara stories are genuine, the shepherd illustration is memorable, and the direction toward the gospel is present. This can feel like a complete homily — and for a graveside word, it may function adequately. But the illusion is this: *the emotional weight of the occasion is doing the work that the text should be doing.* The sermon would be similarly effective if the text were Psalm 46, or John 14, or Romans 8 — or no text at all. Psalm 23 is present but not load-bearing. When the emotional occasion does what the exposition should do, it creates the illusion of a text-driven sermon where one does not exist.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The single greatest missed opportunity is verse 6: *"Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life, and I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever."*

This verse is the psalm's climactic declaration. It is where the shepherd metaphor gives way to the image of the covenant home. "The house of the LORD forever" is the answer to every fear named in the psalm — want, danger, death, enemies. And it is the direct theological ground for every claim the preacher wants to make about Barbara. "Today that is exactly where Barbara is" — yes, but the preacher never showed from the text *why* that is true, *what it means*, or *what it cost*.

The covenant word *hesed* (mercy/lovingkindness in v.6) — the word that captures God's binding, pursuing, oath-keeping love — is the theological heartbeat of this moment. It would have grounded the comfort in covenant rather than sentiment, and it was one word away from the text the preacher was already holding.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific Actions)

**Fix 1 — Open verse 6 as the climactic anchor of the sermon.**
"I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever" is where everything lands. Build the whole sermon toward this verse. Barbara is in the house of the LORD because the Shepherd who led her through the valley of the shadow of death brought her all the way home. The sermon ends where the psalm ends.

**Fix 2 — Name and briefly explain *hesed* at verse 6.**
"Goodness and mercy shall follow me" — the word is *hesed*, God's covenant lovingkindness. It does not mean God will generally be nice to His sheep. It means He has sworn an oath. He will not let His own go. For a grieving congregation, this is not a platitude — it is a binding promise. Two sentences would transform this moment.

**Fix 3 — Name the cross when you name the gospel.**
"All of that starts with the gospel" must be followed by *what the gospel is*: Christ died in the place of sinners, bore the wrath that their wandering deserved, and rose so that those who repent and trust Him inherit what the psalm promises — eternal dwelling with the Shepherd. The gospel must be declared, not referenced.

**Fix 4 — Address the hardened mourner directly before the gospel call.**
Add one direct address to the unbeliever who is in the room today: "Some of you are here today and you are watching death, and you are wondering if any of this is real. Barbara believed it. And she walked through the valley of the shadow of death without fearing evil — because the Shepherd was with her. You can know what she knew. But it starts not with being good enough to deserve it, but with knowing you never can — and turning to the One who was."

**Fix 5 — Reframe the structure around the psalm's own movement.**
Introduction: Barbara and this day. Main body: Three movements in Psalm 23 — the Shepherd provides (v.1–3), the Shepherd accompanies in the darkest valley (v.4), the Shepherd brings His sheep all the way home to the covenant feast (v.5–6). Application: What this means for Barbara. What this demands of us. Exhortation: Repent and believe in this Shepherd — who is Jesus Christ.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
**This rubric strains significantly against a funeral homily.** Psalm 23 is a wisdom psalm of trust, and this sermon appears to be a pastoral eulogy delivered at a graveside or memorial service for a named congregant (Barbara). The rubric is designed for a prepared expository sermon delivered to a congregation in a regular worship context. Applied to a funeral homily:

- The low exposition score is real but must be understood in context: funeral homilies are a distinct genre, and even the best Reformed preachers do not deliver full expository sermons at gravesides.
- The fatal flaw flags for generic application and absent exhortation are partially mitigated by the genre — a graveside address to a grieving congregation is not the same rhetorical act as a Sunday morning sermon.
- The illustration and sermonic force scores are relatively high because the pastor's voice and relational presence are genuine — which is exactly what this genre requires.

**The evaluation stands and the scores are accurate as measures of sermon quality**, but the reader should understand: this is a faithful pastor doing pastoral work with the tools of a brief funeral homily. The surgical fixes above are offered not to condemn but to show what this moment could have been — and what it still can be whenever this pastor preaches Psalm 23 in a full expository setting. The pastor's instincts are right. The toolbox needs sharpening.
