## The Puppy

I recently got a puppy, and as I was reading our passage today she kept coming to mind. She's a cute little dog. We're calling her Tiki. Dad says she's cuter than a speckled pup — but she is a speckled pup. She's black and that light grey they call blue.

We met my breeder friend — we've known each other five years or so — and had a great time. We just loved her, and all her dogs, and her pups. Little Tiki had thirty-seven brothers and sisters living in the yard. I can't even imagine that many dogs in my yard. Can you?

When we drove off, my little puppy looked so forlorn. You could see the sadness in her eyes. She wasn't afraid — she was just already missing her family. I was grateful she wasn't barking and crying the way Elisha's new pup was. That pup laid there crying and calling for her family all night.

Tiki will use her voice, though. I put her in her kennel all by herself for a little while. She had everything she needed in there: food, water, a couple of toys. I picked up an elephant toy — the ears are made of rope, and when two dogs, or a dog and a person, tug at it, it squeaks like crazy. She had a little ball, and a circle made of rope. She had everything that she needed.

And yet — when I went inside to use the bathroom Saturday afternoon, she cried and whined and complained. She sat out there making the most pathetic sounds I've ever heard her make for a solid five minutes. At that point, how can you not go out and comfort the puppy?

She wanted her master.

## Do You Want Your Master?

Everyone has a master. We know this.

For some their master is sports. For some it's the bottle, or money, or drugs. For some it's sex. Some of you may be mastered by your television. Who knows — outside of you and the Lord. At the end of the day, TV may seem like a benign master. And TV can be a good thing. Social media can be a good thing. Sports are fun, fun to watch and fun to play.

But at the end of the day, if your master isn't the Lord, your master is the devil.

Do you love your master?

## Psalm 42

Let's read our passage today — Psalm 42.

*[Read Psalm 42.]*

It's interesting to me that the word "pants" here can also be translated as "brays" — meaning a loud, urgent, vocal cry. According to Barnes, the word literally means to rise up and look upward toward something with longing. The only other place this root is used in the Old Testament is in Joel 1:20, where the animals of the field cry out to God for relief from a drought: *"Even the beasts of the field pant for you because the water brooks are dried up, and fire has devoured the open pastures."*

Do you long after God? Does your soul long after him? Because if not, it's possible you have an idolatry issue.

I read a quote by a scholar with his PhD in studies of the East and Near East who said that in the Syrian desert, herds of these particular deer would gather around where they used to find water and bray as loud as they can — so loud that you can walk right up to them without the deer even noticing you're there until you could reach out and touch one. For this type of deer, water is life.

Their focus is fully on their thirst. Their water.

What does your soul long after? What do you thirst for?

If you desire anything in your life like this that isn't God, that's idolatry.

Augustine — whom the Catholics call Saint Augustine — wrote this in his *Confessions*: "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee." There is no other thing that will satisfy our souls for more than a fleeting moment. Nothing can fill that emptiness. Nothing can bring lasting satisfaction or joy like our Savior can.

I was traveling one time and ran into the gas station to grab a soda — and on accident grabbed the diet version. I paid for it, was back on the road and driving before I knew it. I reached down and grabbed that ice cold soda and went to take a refreshing sip, only to be shocked by the nasty flavor of diet soda. It didn't satisfy like I thought it would. It didn't refresh me. It didn't even taste good. Yuck. The only can of diet Mountain Dew I've ever finished was in memory of David's dad — and that one didn't taste good either. Instead of something refreshing, I took a swig of something unfit for human consumption.

Anything we elevate in our hearts higher than God is like that. Instead of the peace of God, we end up with the emptiness of sin. Instead of the joy that can only come from God, we run the risk of addiction and shame, of having our families fall apart because we desire these empty pleasures. Instead of our thirst being satisfied, our thirsts are amplified — and can, in the case of drugs or alcohol, consume us entirely.

That's the trouble with lost people. They obey their master. They lust after anything they see. And at the end it leaves them less happy, less fulfilled, less satisfied, and less rested than before they started. More empty. More restless. More hungry than before they ever tried to satisfy their sinful thirsts apart from God.

## Water Is Life

There's another reason this particular species of deer desires water. It turns out the predators that hunt them won't chase them into the water — so the deer's knee-jerk reaction when danger comes is to head directly for it. Water is life. Water fulfills. Water is safety.

Imagine being a prey animal and waking up one morning to find that your security — your lake — has dried up. It's little wonder why they cry. They know what it means.

Water helps them in so many ways.

Let me ask you this: Where does your life come from? Your security? Where does your help come from?

## Psalm 121

Psalm 121 gives us the answer.

> *I lift up my eyes to the hills.*
> *From where does my help come?*
> *My help comes from the LORD, who made heaven and earth.*
> *He will not let your foot be moved;*
> *he who keeps you will not slumber.*
> *Behold, he who keeps Israel will neither slumber nor sleep.*
> *The LORD is your keeper;*
> *the LORD is your shade on your right hand.*
> *The sun shall not strike you by day,*
> *nor the moon by night.*
> *The LORD will keep you from all evil;*
> *he will keep your life.*
> *The LORD will keep your going out and your coming in*
> *from this time forth and forevermore.*
>
> — Psalm 121:1-8 (ESV)

What a wonderful promise. I count several right there. He promises to help. He reveals who he is — directly, and through creation. He will keep us from stumbling. He will be watchful day and night, even while we are at our most vulnerable. He will keep you — he will be your shade, your peace, your comfort. He will protect you from evil and keep your life. He will guide your going out and your coming in, forever and ever, without rest or interruption.

What a wonderful Master. What a wonderful Shepherd we can have.

## The Danger of Small Compromises

I think most people stumble into idolatry — not intentionally. They don't wake up one morning and say, *How can I rebel against God today?* For long-time Christians, they lose sight of God gradually. Sometimes they think, *I've read the Bible — I don't need to keep reading every day.* Or they allow the business of life to crowd God out of their mind. And it's easy to do.

Friday morning, I woke up at 5:45. Took my puppy out. She was coughing her head off, and I was worried — so instead of spending time reading my Bible, I spent the next two hours reading about puppy coughs. At 8 I ran to the store to get some medicine. At 9 we packed up and left for breakfast, and I spent much of the rest of the day driving. I did get a short nap in, and I do wish I had spent more time with the Lord. I allowed important things to crowd out the most important things. That night I crawled into bed around 12:30 or 1, exhausted. I didn't open my Bible like I should have. I just went to sleep.

Now — God's not going to damn you to hell over missing one day in the Word. But it sets a dangerous precedent. It starts calluses forming over your heart. And it's not just skipping Bible reading that can do that. Skipping prayer can. Skipping fellowship with other believers. Skipping worship.

Christian, there is a reason we are the way we are — and it's not because God has changed. It's because we pull back. Not even intentionally. It's a death by a thousand cuts. We allow things to happen. Slowly we let these things bleed our joy and our contentment and our happiness out of our lives. These little compromises are what cause a Christian not to long after God.

The deer can't make compromises. If they don't drink, they die. If they don't find their security in the water, they die. If they don't find their life and their help there, they die. God has been more gracious with us than that. We don't die quite so quickly as a deer in the desert. But the thirst is no less real.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with reservations.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — that the soul thirsts for God alone and that substitutes for God leave the soul emptier than before — genuinely arises from Psalm 42's own argument. The psalmist's longing, the soul's restless questioning, and the theme of thirst are all organically textual. PASS.

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text's emotional register and the text's diagnosis of human longing. However, the sermon does not engage Psalm 42's most distinct and demanding features: the psalmist's deep dejection ("Why are you cast down, O my soul?"), the accusation-level questioning of God ("Where is your God?"), and the recurring refrain of faith wrestling with darkness. The text contains both lament and hope in tension — the sermon flattens this into a warm encouragement homily. The hard parts are quietly bypassed rather than directly contradicted, which passes the gate technically while raising concerns about completeness. PASS (marginal).

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Two flags are triggered. Score capped at 69.**

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation.** Christ's finished work is absent from this sermon. The name of Jesus appears nowhere in the manuscript. The gospel is not proclaimed — not justification by faith, not the cross, not the resurrection. Augustine is quoted. Psalm 121 is added. But the mechanism by which a thirsty soul finds water in God — the atoning work of Christ — is entirely absent. A devout person of any monotheistic religion could sit through this sermon without offense. The Synagogue Test is triggered.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The sermon explains the problem (idolatry, spiritual thirst, small compromises) and offers an application (individual confession in prayer, corporate praise). But it never calls the congregation to specific believing, repenting, or obeying. The closing moves directly from application to prayer instruction without a "therefore" moment — without a summoning declaration. The congregation is invited to pray but not commanded by the Word. Explanation without exhortation is instruction, not preaching.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

This is the text's most practical and pastorally potent movement, and it is entirely absent from the sermon.

They heard a description of the problem (idolatry, small compromises) and an invitation to pray — but not the text's own prescription: speak the truth about God to your own soul, even when your circumstances have not changed.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific)

**Fix 1: Name Christ. Explicitly. At least once.**
After the small-compromises section — or in the development of Psalm 42's thirst image — preach John 7:37-38: "If anyone thirsts, let him come to me and drink." The psalm's thirst finds its antitype in Christ. This does not require a forced Christological jump — it is the text's own organic trajectory, confirmed by Jesus' own use of similar imagery. The sermon currently ends with thirst and prayer; it needs to arrive at the fountain.

**Fix 2: Preach the refrain — self-counsel under depression.**
Psalm 42:5 ("Why are you cast down, O my soul? Hope in God") is quoted in the source notes but not preached in the body of the sermon. This verse is the psalm's structural and pastoral backbone. The sermon should make this its central applicatory thrust: here is how a believer fights the spiritual dryness they just diagnosed. "Talk to your soul. Preach truth to yourself in the dark. The psalmist did not wait for his circumstances to change — he commanded his own heart."

**Fix 3: Distinguish the lost hearer from the drifting believer. Address both directly.**

**Fix 4: Replace the prayer prompt with an exhortation, then close in prayer.**

**Fix 5: Let the text's own image bear more weight than the illustrations.**
The deer in the Syrian desert braying at a dried-up spring — unable to hear or see anything around them because the thirst has consumed all their attention — is a more powerful image than the puppy or the diet soda. Develop this image from the text itself. The congregation should go home with the deer in their minds, not the puppy.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Psalm 42 is a lament psalm, and the rubric's categories bear some stress when applied to lament poetry. Lament psalms do not follow a simple doctrine-to-application logic — they are exercises in honest wrestling with God, where the theological movement is from crisis through memory to fragile hope. The rubric's categories for Exposition, Gospel Centrality, and Conscience are all applicable, but the evaluator should note that a faithful sermon on Psalm 42 might legitimately dwell longer in the psalmist's darkness than a sermon on Romans 8 would — and that the "exhortation" appropriate to a lament psalm may be the quieter work of naming grief and giving it permission before offering hope.

That said: even accounting for genre flexibility, the absence of Christ and the absence of the refrain's self-counsel remain genuine failures, not artifacts of genre strain. The rubric is bent slightly — but the core findings hold.
