# Colossians 4:2 — Prayer Service

We havent had a prayer service in a while, and I think today is a good day for one. 

I can’t help but preach a bit, so were going to be in Col 4:2, if you’ve brought your bible and I hope you have. 

While you’re turning there, It occurs to me that almost exactly a year ago today at this time we were disembarking on the beautiful island of Tahiit. We toured the island, and went inside a dormant volcano full of waterfalls. We even saw the beach where the mutiny on the bounty happened. An absolutely beautiful island. 

relatedly the author of the famous pirate book treasure island, Robert Louis Stevenson was said to be talking to his mother one day. Where he remarked: "Momma, you can't be good without praying." "How do you know, Robert?" she asked. "Because I've tried!" he answered. This brings to mind a story about another little fellow -- one who had been sent to his room because he had been bad. A short time later he came out and said to his mother, "I've been thinking about what I did and I said a prayer." "That's fine," she said, "if you ask God to make you good, He will help you." "Oh, I didn't ask Him to help me be good," replied the boy. "I asked Him to help you put up with me."

As cute as that is, I think its true. You can’t be good without praying. Charles Spurgeon said to neglect prayer when things are hard is like rejecting medicine when you’re sick. 

Or as owen said:”until you have prayed you can do no more”. 
Spurgeon says neglect of prayer is the locust devouring the church. 

Prayer is as vital as air. How long would you live without air? 

Lets read our passage. 

devote your self to prayer. What does devote mean? The dictionary for the English word says to spend a large amount or all of your time doing something. If you’re devoted to football you’d spend a large amount of your time watching/playing/thinking about/engaging in football. Maybe even all your lfie. 

But as Christians we are told to devote ourselves to prayer. Based on how the Greek word is translated in other passages, its safe to say that the intent here is to start spending a lot of time on prayer, and continue doing it daily. 

Be devoted. Be engaged in prayer. Be continuous in your prayer. 

Being watchful. I looked that word up too. And it turns out its meaning calls to mind soldiers standing at the alert on watch. 

A number of years ago I got to see the changing of the guard at the tomb of the unknown soldier. Rain or shine. Drizzle or deluge. Snow or blizzard they have troops walk up and down the monument alert and on guard. Protecting, watching over, looking out for the lost soldiers buried there. I think that’s a good picture of what we are intended to do, and indeed what God has done for us. 

Next I want to draw your attention to vs 3 and 4. What does Paul ask us to be praying for? Effective evangelism. 

It seems to me that one of these naturally leads to the other. Mature Christians spend time in prayer. Prayer draws us deeper into our relationship with God. Which spurs us to among other things be sharing the gospel.
In fact I believe you can use sharing the gospel as a dipstick, or a thermometer, or a depth gauge for your relationship with God. The further down the road of maturity you are, the more like you are to be regularly sharing the gospel. 

Finally vs 6.  Let your conversation be always full of grace,seasoned with salt, so that you may know how to answer everyone.

That is a clear call to slow down the speed with which we open and run our mouths. Be contemplative. Consider whether a thing brings glory and honor to God or not. Whether it brings people into the kingdom. 

There is an acronym people use – is it true, helpful, inspiring, necessary and kind? They continue if not then you shouldn’t say it. 

But I think the acronym falls short. Its not so pithy, but still adding a few things makes it better. Things like “Does it Honor God”, and “Will it help or hinder the gospel”. 

Long story short, we need to be praying, and sharing the gospel. Today I want to spend the rest of our time praying. As you may or may not have heard, Israel was attacked last night, and that may well draw us into  war with iran. Seems like a good thing to spend some time praying for. Were in an election year, pray fo our leaders, oray they are drawn closer to the Lord. For God’s man to be elected. For local leaders. For our church family. For Gloria whose dear friend died. For dalia and her health. For donna and kurt as they make hard decisions. For Bob’s daughter in law as she deals wth cancer. For God’s will to be done on earth. 

There is an acrostic I like to use. Its “acts”. ACTS is an acronym that stands for adoration, confession, thanksgiving, and supplication (or supply needs).
Adoration: Praising God for who he is and what he has done.
Confession: Saying sorry for the hurt we’ve caused God and others.
Thanksgiving: Thanking God for all he has done for us.
Supplication: Asking God to grant us what we need.
A trap Christians so often fall into when praying, is we ask God for what we want, and forget just who it is were talking to. 

I remember at thanksgiving time growing up, we had an aunt that seemed like she always prayed for 45 minutes. When you’re ten and hungry that is tough.  Anytime they asked me to pray back then it was basically two things, bless the food, help grandma stop smoking, amen. Sometimes just bless the food amen. No time or consideration given to just who I am making requests of. 

Some of us knew a child that couldn’t use words like we use words, and when he wanted something, he just squealed for it. I wonder how often our prayers sound like that to God. The impatient demands of a hungry child, and yet God still chooses to answer our pleas. We serve a good God. God is good!

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Warm Pastoral Gathering That Never Becomes Preaching

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A brief, affectionate opening of Colossians 4:2-6 that rightly calls the congregation to devoted prayer and gospel-seasoned speech, but remains a devotional prompt rather than a textual sermon — warm in spirit, thin in exposition, and absent of gospel proclamation.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — narrowly.**

- **Scopus Test**: The main point (devote yourself to prayer, as this is the posture of the maturing Christian and the seedbed of evangelism) does arise from Colossians 4:2-4. The passage does say what the sermon says.
- **Authority Test**: The preacher submits to the text's claims rather than editing them. He does not soften the imperative.

*Caution*: The gate barely holds. The text's own argument — continuance, watchfulness, thankfulness, and intercession for gospel proclamation — is touched but not opened. The sermon identifies the commands without inhabiting them. It passes the gate, but barely clears the threshold.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Three fatal flags triggered. Score capped at 69.**

- **Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation**: Christ's finished work is entirely absent. The sermon calls for prayer and moral speech but never names why prayer is possible (the mediation of Christ), nor what the "gospel" to be shared actually is. Evangelism is mentioned as a goal; the gospel is never articulated.
- **Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary**: The sermon could survive intact without Jesus. Every point could be delivered in a synagogue. The name of Christ appears nowhere in the manuscript.
- **Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: "We need to be praying and sharing the gospel" is the sum of the application. No specific hearer is addressed, no conscience is cornered, no distinction is made between the prayerless believer, the fearful witness, or the unbeliever in the room.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 10/25**
- *Textual Fidelity*: 4/10 — The text is present and quoted. "Devote yourself" and "watchful" are defined. But the sermon covers only surface-level word study. The structure of the passage (devote → watch → thanksgiving → intercession for Paul's gospel work → gracious speech) is noted but not opened. No exegetical argument is developed.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 2/5 — No reference to Colossians as a whole, to the Christological battle of the letter, or to why Paul would request prayer for gospel doors specifically in this context.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 2/5 — The Greek is gestured at ("the Greek word…") but the insight is minimal. The hearer cannot reconstruct the passage's own argument from this sermon.
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 2/5 — No eisegesis, but the jump from vs. 2 to vs. 3-4 to vs. 6 lacks the development that would show how they connect. The Fallen Condition Focus is never named: What is the specific condition this text addresses? Prayerlessness? Distracted speech? It is implied but never named, which weakens the sermon's diagnostic force.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 9/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness*: 1/7 — Christ is not mentioned. Not once. This is the most significant single failure.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness*: 2/3 — The text's imperative nature is honored, but no placement within the story of redemption is offered.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 4/15**

- *Cross-Centered Gravity*: 4/5 — Partial credit: the sermon does gesture toward the gospel being shared, even if it doesn't preach it.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 6/15**
- *Heart Penetration*: 2/5 — The anecdote about the child praying for God to help his mother "put up with me" and the squealing child metaphor briefly touch the conscience. But they entertain more than they search.
- *Specificity*: 2/5 — The prayer list (Israel, elections, Gloria, Dalia, Donna, Bob's daughter-in-law) shows pastoral specificity in the prayer service itself, which is genuinely good. But as applicatory force in the sermon, the people are directed to pray without their hearts being searched about why they don't.
- *Spiritual Differentiation*: 2/5 — No Perkins categories addressed distinctly. The sermon speaks to the congregation as one body, without distinguishing the prayerless believer from the new convert from the one who has given up on prayer entirely.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness*: 2/4 — ACTS acrostic is useful. The mention of gospel-sharing as a "thermometer" is an equipping metaphor. But neither tool is developed with enough depth to truly equip.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**

- *Progression*: 2/3 — Moves forward without wandering badly. The Tahiti story at the opening is a detour that establishes rapport but not stakes.
- *Unity*: 1/2 — Two ideas compete slightly: prayer as a discipline vs. speech as a discipline. In Colossians 4:2-6 they are linked; here they feel like separate topics.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**

- *Memorability*: 1/1 — "God still chooses to answer our pleas" after the squealing child image is the sermon's most affecting line. It will be remembered.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Conviction on the Page*: 0/2 — Little Scripture-driven assertion. More pastoral musing than proclamation.
- *Cadence and Breath*: 1/1 — Short sentences, approachable register, appropriate for a prayer service context.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Neither, primarily. It makes the hearer think about their own prayer life. Christ is not named.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** The prayer service that follows may produce encounter. The sermon itself offers ideas about prayer more than the God to whom prayer is addressed.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Mildly informed and warmly invited. Not convicted.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Pastoral warmth and congregational love**: The prayer list — Gloria, Dalia, Donna and Kurt, Bob's daughter-in-law — reveals a pastor who knows his people by name. This is not a weakness dressed as a strength. It is a genuine pastoral virtue. The sermon becomes a prayer service, which is exactly what the passage calls for.
2. **The squealing child image**: "I wonder how often our prayers sound like that to God. The impatient demands of a hungry child, and yet God still chooses to answer our pleas." This is the sermon's best moment — brief, earthy, conscience-touching, and it closes with doxology ("We serve a good God"). This is a seed of real preaching.
3. **Correct identification of prayer-as-evangelism connection**: The linkage between vs. 2 and vs. 3-4 — that devoted prayer leads to gospel urgency — is textually sound and pastorally useful. The "dipstick/thermometer" metaphor for gospel-sharing as a spiritual maturity indicator is memorable and accurate.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **Christ is entirely absent**: From opening to close, the name of Jesus Christ does not appear. In a sermon on prayer to God through Christ, this is not a peripheral omission — it is the structural collapse. Why can we pray? Because we have a mediator. Who is the "open door" Paul asks for in vs. 3? The gospel of Christ. What makes speech "full of grace"? The grace of God in the cross. Every connecting beam runs through Jesus, and every beam is missing.
2. **No law before the gospel, and no gospel at all**: The ACTS acrostic introduces "confession" without telling the congregation what sin is or what it costs. Adoration is mentioned without the prior work of understanding who God is in his holiness. The sermon lands on "God is good!" without the cross being the reason why that is true for sinners. The result is a pleasantness that does not press.
3. **The introduction does not serve the text**: Three paragraphs about Tahiti, the Bounty mutiny, Robert Louis Stevenson's mother, and a joke about a boy asking God to help his mother "put up with me" — none of this creates stakes for Colossians 4:2. The anecdotes create warmth but no urgency. The congregation is not told what is at risk if they fail to be devoted to prayer. An introduction that establishes the weight of the text would serve the sermon far better than one that establishes the preacher's vacation.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely does not see that the very warmth of this sermon — its relational ease, its humor, its pastoral familiarity — functions as a substitute for proclamation. It is possible for a congregation to leave a prayer service feeling that they have heard from God when they have only heard from their pastor about God. The absence of Christ in this sermon is almost certainly not intentional — it is the blindspot of a man who assumes the gospel rather than preaching it. The gospel is in the preacher's heart. It did not make it to the page.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The prayer list at the end creates the impression of pastoral depth and sermonic completeness. It is moving. It feels like the sermon has arrived. But this is the illusion: the sermon reached its emotional apex not through the proclamation of Christ but through the enumeration of congregational needs. A list of prayer requests, however pastorally faithful, is not an exhortation grounded in the gospel. The sermon feels finished because the people feel loved — not because Christ has been exalted.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Colossians 4:3 is one of the most striking prayer requests in the New Testament: Paul, in chains, asks not for his own release but for "a door for the word." This is a profound display of gospel-centered prayer — a man who measures his situation not by his comfort but by gospel advance. This is the sermon's heart and its best illustration of devoted, watchful, thankful prayer. It was mentioned but never inhabited. A sermon built on Paul's chains could have cornered every prayerless, self-focused pray-er in the room and shown them what prayer looks like when Christ is everything.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3-5)
1. **Name Christ in the first point**: After defining "devoted to prayer," ask why we can pray at all. The answer is Hebrews 4:14-16 — we have a great high priest. Prayer is not a discipline we practice; it is an audience we are granted through blood. One sentence changes everything.
2. **Build the sermon around Paul in chains in vs. 3**: "An open door for the word" — not "get me out." This is the text's own illustration of devoted, self-forgetful, gospel-centered prayer. Use it. It is more powerful than Tahiti.
3. **Add direct address to the prayerless**: "Some of you have not prayed in three days. Some of you pray only when you are desperate. The text's word for 'devote' means to continue — you cannot continue what you have not started." Name the condition before issuing the command.
4. **Clarify what the gospel is before asking people to share it**: The sermon tells the congregation to share the gospel. It never says what the gospel is. One sentence: "The gospel is the news that Christ died for sinners, rose from the dead, and everyone who trusts in him will be saved." Then the call to pray for gospel doors has content.
5. **Replace the Tahiti introduction with the stakes**: Open with the weight of the command. "Paul writes this from prison. He is in chains. And from prison he says: devote yourself to prayer. Watch. Give thanks. Pray for the gospel to go forward. What does it mean to be a Christian who prays? And what does it cost to be one who doesn't?" This creates urgency the vacation story cannot.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — the inverse is present. This sermon is not dead orthodoxy (correct theology without heart); it has warmth without doctrine. The affections are present. The theological skeleton is absent. This is not cold precision — it is warm imprecision.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
There is genuine pastoral love in this manuscript, and the squealing-child image carries a brief flash of eternal gravity — but the sermon does not carry the weight of glory because it does not carry Christ, and no sermon can be weighty with God absent from it.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Colossians 4:2-6 is straightforward imperative/hortatory prose — this rubric fits it well. No genre strain. The passage is not apocalyptic, lament, or narrative. It is exactly the kind of text where the rubric's demands are most appropriate: a clear command that requires exposition, gospel grounding, and conscience-level application. The sermon's weakness is not a genre problem — it is a content problem. The rubric is not bending the text; the text is demanding more than the sermon gave it.
