### 1. Verdict Title
Warm Gospel Heart in a Structurally Loose Frame — Propitiation Preached, Obedience Under-Resolved

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and pastorally instinctive opening of 1 John 2:1–6 that reaches the gospel with genuine force, but whose structural looseness, under-developed law movement, and absence of differentiated application prevent it from searching the conscience with the precision the passage demands.

- **Authority Test:** The preacher submits to the text's claims, including the hard ones about the commandment-keeping test of genuine knowledge. The section on "whoever says I know him but does not keep his commandments is a liar" is engaged honestly, not softened.

### 6. Cognitive Load Modifier
**0** — The sermon is accessible throughout. No seminary jargon. Greek is explained adequately. The congregation can follow the main argument.

### 7. Total Score
**67/100 — Concerning Gaps**
The sermon's gospel instincts are good. The propitiation and election material is confessionally sharp. But structural looseness, an under-pressed law movement, and generic application (rather than differentiated, conscience-pressing specificity) hold this back from the "Solid but Lacking" tier. The sermon is warmer than it is precise.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself? **Mostly Christ — the propitiation and election paragraphs are genuinely Christ-centered.**
2. Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him? **Partially — the naming of congregants by God's electing voice is a genuinely moving moment.**
3. Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed? **Comforted more than convicted. The conscience-pressing question early in the sermon is not followed through.**

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Propitiation explained with genuine pastoral weight.** The "cup of God's wrath" passage and the explanation that Christ "didn't find a sleazy loophole but personally took the punishment" is clear, warm, and cross-centered.
2. **Limited atonement handled with textual grounding.** The Greek "cosmos" discussion, paired with John 6:44 and John 10:11, is confessionally sound and textually honest — a real strength in a sermon addressed to a Reformed Baptist congregation.
3. **God's electing love named personally.** "I want Richard. I want Dottie. I'll take Willie too..." is an unusually pastorally effective moment — the doctrine of election preached as comfort, not abstraction.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The obedience test of vv. 3–5 is never resolved.** The sermon quotes "if you keep his commandments" as a test of genuine knowledge, then moves immediately to the comfort of propitiation. The hard question — "What do I do if my commandment-keeping is poor? Am I a genuine believer?" — is raised in the observation question but never pastorally walked through.
2. **The law does not land before the gospel relieves.** The Brett and insubordination illustrations establish the concept of sin but do not press the weight of divine wrath. The sermon moves too quickly from "we sinned" to "we have an advocate." The hammer swings, but it doesn't hit hard enough for the balm to feel like relief.
3. **No differentiated address to specific hearers.** The sermon treats the congregation as a uniform body of the converted. No word to the person who doubts their assurance because their obedience is weak. No word to the openly disobedient church member. No word to the unbeliever who may be present. Perkins' categories are almost entirely absent.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher's warmth and relational instincts are genuine strengths, but they may be operating as a substitute for precision. The most pressing question this passage raises — "Is commandment-keeping the test of genuine salvation, and if so, what do I do with my ongoing failures?" — is the theological and pastoral center of 1 John 2:3–5, and the sermon addresses it obliquely rather than directly.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The Greek word study on "cosmos" and the confessional clarity on limited atonement create an impression of exegetical rigor. But the sermon's actual textual engagement with vv. 3–6 (the commandment-keeping section) is minimal. The strongest theological content addresses vv. 1–2; vv. 3–6 receive mostly illustrative treatment.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
The structure of 1 John 2:1–6 is a rhetorical masterpiece: the purpose clause ("that you may not sin"), followed immediately by the comfort clause ("but if anyone does sin"), followed by the indicative of assurance ("we have an advocate"), followed by the test clause ("by this we know"). John himself provides the law/gospel/assurance structure the sermon needs. The preacher has the right instincts but did not follow John's own rhetorical architecture, which would have both organized the sermon and deepened its pastoral force.

### 14. Surgical Fixes
- **Follow John's rhetorical architecture directly.** Let each clause of vv. 1–2 become a movement: (1) the purpose — "that you may not sin" — law; (2) the comfort — "but if anyone does sin" — gospel address to the failing saint; (3) the ground — "Jesus Christ the righteous" — who he is; (4) propitiation — what he did. The sermon currently jumbles these.
- **Press the commandment-keeping test harder before offering comfort.** Ask: "Are you obeying? Is your life tracking toward holiness? John says the test of genuine knowledge is keeping his commands. Does that terrify you?" Let it land. Then bring the propitiation.
- **Add a direct address to the assurance-doubter.** "Some of you are here today wondering if you're really saved. Your obedience feels weak. John writes to you: you have an advocate. Your advocate's righteousness, not your obedience, is the ground of your standing."
- **Replace "Gospel." at the end with an actual exhortation.** What must a person who has heard this sermon now do? Believe? Confess? Examine themselves? Tell the congregation explicitly.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — the sermon has genuine warmth and pastoral heat. The gospel is proclaimed with real feeling. The issue is precision, not temperature.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The electing love of God paragraph and the propitiation section carry eternal weight; the sermon carries genuine if uneven unction, strongest when it names what Christ bore and who God chose.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
The rubric fits this passage well. 1 John 2:1–6 is an epistle with clear propositional content, law/gospel dynamics, and direct address to the congregation — all categories the rubric is built to assess. No genre-based adjustment is needed.
