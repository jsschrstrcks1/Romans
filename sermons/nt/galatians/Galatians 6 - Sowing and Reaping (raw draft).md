- Tribal chief suspicious, only allowed one boy to sell them eggs/chicken twice weekly

- Month before due: boy overcome by grace, prays sinner's prayer — first and only Christian in village
- Chief allows midwife for birth; after week of labor, baby born alive, Svea dies hours later at age 27
- David digs grave, makes white cross marked "Svea Flood"
- David declares "God has ruined my life," gives baby away, returns to Sweden
- Baby (Aggie) raised by Ericksons (who later die of malaria), then American couple
- After high school, Swedish magazine arrives — photo of white cross "Svea Flood"
- College professor translates article: the boy went back to village, told parents, they believed — friends/neighbors — chief — entire village became believers
- Aggie tracks down father who had remarried; siblings warn "never mention God"
- Aggie tells David the whole story — 600 in village became believers because of Svea *(Corrected March 2026 — "600+" → "600"; the Swedish magazine article cited by all major retellings reports "six hundred Christian believers in that one village." - David re-dedicates life to Lord, dies within a few weeks surrounded by family *(Corrected March 2026 — draft said "a week later"; authoritative retellings consistently say "within a few weeks" after Aggie and her husband returned to America.)*
- Aggie sent to Africa, meets the boy (now man) — he leads national church of 110,000 people
- Aggie kneels at mother's grave, fingers in soil, thanks God

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**FAIL — Evaluation halted at Berean Gate.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's main point cannot be derived from the text's own argument because the text is never exegeted. The passage (Gal. 6:7–10) makes a structured, logical argument: (1) God is not mocked — the law of sowing and reaping is inescapable; (2) sowing to the flesh yields corruption; (3) sowing to the Spirit yields eternal life; (4) therefore: do not grow weary; (5) do good to all, especially the household of faith. The sermon takes the *metaphor* (sowing/reaping) and repurposes it as a missions motivational frame without engaging the text's actual argument. The main point of the sermon — *Svea Flood sowed faithfully and God reaped a harvest* — is a valid sermon illustration; it is not the main point of Galatians 6:7–10.

*Authority Test:* The preacher cannot be said to stand under or above the text because the text is not substantively engaged. There is no exegesis to evaluate.

**Because this is summary notes and not a full draft, the Berean Gate failure is noted but not treated as terminal in the same way it would be for a developed manuscript. The evaluation continues below in diagnostic mode to serve the development of the full sermon — but no score is assigned, and the gate failure governs every structural observation that follows.**

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Multiple flags triggered. Score capped at 69 — though because no score is assigned (Berean Gate failure), the cap is noted diagnostically.**

- **Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The notes contain no articulation of the gospel. Svea's death is moving. The village's conversion is referenced. But *how* those 600 came to faith, what the gospel content was, what the boy heard and believed — these are absent. The story is told as a harvest story, not a gospel story.
- **Flag 2 — Main point does not equal text's point**: As noted above. The passage argues that the law of sowing and reaping is morally inescapable, that flesh-sowing yields corruption, and Spirit-sowing yields eternal life — and therefore believers should not grow weary in doing good. The sermon's main point is a missions-motivation narrative. These are not the same.
- **Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary**: Remove Christ from these notes and the sermon still functions. The Svea Flood story, the Toplady/Spurgeon/Luther quotes, the Titanic illustration — none of them require Christ's atoning work. The quotes are about free will and depravity (theologically sound but disconnected from the text). Christ is not the hero of this sermon; faithfulness-rewarded-by-harvest is.
- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: The notes move toward a missions appeal ("Will you pursue the diamonds of the world or the oranges of heaven?") but there is no clear summons to a specific response. No one is told what to *believe*, *repent of*, *obey*, or *do* on Monday morning.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely does not see that the **Svea Flood story has functionally replaced the sermon**. The story is so emotionally powerful that it fills the space that the text is supposed to fill. This is a common and understandable mistake — when an illustration is this good, it becomes the sermon. But the text of Galatians 6 is actually *richer* than the illustration. Paul's argument about flesh vs. Spirit, the moral inescapability of the harvest, and the command not to grow weary — these are more searching than the story alone. The preacher may also be unaware that the David/Svea contrast maps naturally onto Paul's two categories (sowing to flesh vs. sowing to Spirit) — which means the illustration and the text *want* to be integrated, not run in parallel.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The theological quotes (Toplady, Spurgeon, Luther) create the *feeling* of doctrinal depth. They are doctrinally sound. But in this sermon, they are not exegetically connected to the passage. A preacher can quote Augustine, Calvin, and Owen in a sermon on Psalm 23 and produce a theologically impressive but hermeneutically hollow message. The quotes here address free will and the necessity of divine power — themes that are *adjacent* to Galatians 6:8 ("sowing to the Spirit") but never *integrated* with it. The quotes feel like anchors; they are currently decorations.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
**Galatians 6:7 — "Do not be deceived: God is not mocked."** This phrase is the sermon's actual opening thunder. Paul is not merely making an agricultural observation — he is warning the Galatian churches, in the context of a letter about the danger of returning to law-works, that there are consequences. The Galatians had been seduced into sowing to the flesh (the flesh-effort of legalism, Gal. 3:3) while thinking they were sowing to the Spirit. The rebuke is pointed. The sermon could land this passage on every Galatian-style Christian in the room — the person who serves in the church but has never ceased trusting their own effort, the person who gives financially while harboring private flesh-patterns, the person who thinks their outward religious fruit exempts them from the inward harvest of unrepented sin. This is the text's conscience-edge, and the sermon's notes never approach it.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific)
1. **Begin the sermon by opening Galatians 6:7–10 verse by verse.** Before the Titanic illustration, before the Svea Flood story, before the quotes — preach the text. Establish the argument: What does "God is not mocked" mean in context? What is the flesh/Spirit contrast in the flow of Galatians? What does it mean that sowing to flesh produces *corruption*? Why does Paul command them not to grow weary — what weariness was present in the Galatian churches? The story and the quotes serve this argument; they do not replace it.

2. **Replace the fabricated Titanic illustration with the verified Peuchen account.** The real version (Major Arthur Peuchen, bonds and stocks in a tin box, three oranges, documented in Walter Lord's *A Night to Remember*, Ch. III) is equally vivid and more defensible. If desired, the preacher can note: "A man named Arthur Peuchen — first-class passenger — left $300,000 in bonds in his cabin and took three oranges." The gender change and casino fabrication should be retired permanently.

3. **Integrate the free will quotes into the exposition of verse 8, not as a standalone cluster.** The Toplady/Spurgeon/Luther quotes address the inability of the flesh to save — this maps directly onto "sowing to the flesh will from the flesh reap corruption." Use them to drive home what Paul means: sowing to the flesh is not merely moral failure — it is trusting a dead engine. Then the gospel: sowing to the Spirit is possible only because the Spirit has been given to those united to Christ. That move requires stating the gospel explicitly.

4. **Add a specific gospel proclamation before the Svea Flood story.** The story of 600 people coming to faith is only meaningful if the congregation understands *what* they came to faith in. State it: Christ crucified for sinners, risen, Lord — the only ground of justification. Then tell the story as the fruit of a seed that is Christ himself, planted by Svea's faithful witness.

5. **Close with Perkins-differentiated exhortation.** Address three hearers explicitly: (a) The burned-out believer who is about to quit doing good — "Do not grow weary... in due season you will reap if you do not give up." (b) The Galatian-style believer sowing to flesh-effort while appearing religious — "God is not mocked. What are you actually sowing to?" (c) The unbeliever in the room — "You have been sowing to the flesh all your life. The harvest is coming. There is a Spirit-sowing that is available to you right now — it is called repentance and faith in Christ."
