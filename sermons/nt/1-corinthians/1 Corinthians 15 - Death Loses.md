# 1 Corinthians 15 — Death Loses

**Easter Sunday — April 5, 2026**
**Text:** 1 Corinthians 15:20–28, 50–58
**Status:** Polished

---

Not because it's Easter and that's what you're supposed to do. But because there are people in this room for whom death is not a theological category — it's a clock. A diagnosis on a sheet of paper. A name they can't say without their voice breaking. A chair that's empty this morning that wasn't empty last Easter. There are plenty of us, including my family this week. As you know, my cousin was in a car accident this week and passed away at 18 years old.

And if we rush past that into celebration, we've cheated those people. We've given them a holiday when what they need is a verdict.

So. Death is real. You know it is. And if you don't know it personally yet, you will.

Before we go any further, let's talk about our people group of the week.

---

## Missions Moment: The Futa Fulbe

Our people group of the week is the Futa Fulbe — that's FOOL-bay FOO-tah.

The Fulbe are a West African people whose children grow up in age-sets — groups of all the children born within a three or four year span. Those children go to school together, work together, and eventually help each other pay bride prices when it comes time to marry. Their villages are often scattered across the landscape, but each one centers around a court and a mosque. Islam is the organizing reality of Fulbe life. It is not an add-on. It is their identity, their community structure, their answer to every question about meaning and duty and what happens after death.

And that last part matters this morning. Because the Fulbe answer to what happens after death is the same answer Islam gives everywhere: it depends on whether your good deeds outweigh your bad ones. Salvation by the scale. Mercy measured out on a balance.

Efforts to bring the gospel to the Fulbe have produced only a handful of believers. They are deeply devoted to their faith and see it as sufficient — as the fulfillment of everything they need.

But here is what we are about to celebrate at this table and in this sermon: Christ has been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep. That changes everything. The resurrection is not advice. It is not a system of weights and measures. It is an announcement — death has been defeated, and every man and woman who belongs to Christ will be raised. Not because the scale tipped in their favor, but because the last enemy has already lost.

The Fulbe need to hear that. A people who organize their whole lives around duty and community and obligation — they need to hear that there is a King who has already done what no amount of obligation can accomplish. He has swallowed up death in victory.

Let's pray for them.

Father, we thank you that the tomb is empty and that death does not get the last word. We pray now for the Futa Fulbe. Open their eyes to see that salvation built on works will always leave them uncertain, but that Christ's resurrection leaves nothing uncertain. Raise up workers — full-time missionaries who will go and live among the Fulbe and love them and tell them plainly what happened on the third day. Give boldness to the small number of Fulbe believers. Start something among them, Lord — not just scattered conversions but a movement, a church, a Christ-shaped community that the scattered villages can see and be drawn to. We ask you to do what only you can do: take a people who believe they already have the answer, and show them the Answer they never expected. In Christ's name, amen.

Who would like to pray for the Futa Fulbe?

Now let's talk about what happened to it.

---

Open your Bibles to 1 Corinthians 15. We're going to cover a lot of ground this morning, but I want you to see the argument Paul is making — because this isn't sentiment. This is case-building. This is a man who laid out the evidence, stacked it carefully, and then dared anyone to come at it.

Paul wrote this letter to the church in Corinth — a church he planted, a church he loved, a church that was also a mess. And one of the things the Corinthians had gotten confused about was the resurrection. Some of them were saying there is no resurrection of the dead. Now, you need to understand why. Corinth was a Greek city, and Greeks had been breathing in Platonic philosophy for centuries — the idea that the soul is the real you and the body is a disposable container you're better off without. So when Paul preached *bodily* resurrection, some of them heard it as nonsense. Why would you want the body back?

Paul's response starts at the beginning of the chapter and runs all the way to the end. He catalogs witnesses: Peter, the twelve, five hundred people at once — most of whom were still alive when Paul was writing, meaning go ask them if you don't believe me. Then himself — last of all, like an abnormal birth, because he met the risen Christ on a road to Damascus after the resurrection.

His argument in verses 12–19 is brutal. If there's no resurrection, then Christ wasn't raised. And if Christ wasn't raised, your faith is worthless. You're still in your sins. The people who have died in Christ are gone. We are, as Paul puts it, *"of all people most to be pitied."*

That's the foundation Paul lays before he gets to where we're reading.

He builds the full weight of *if resurrection is false*. He lets it sit there. He wants you to feel the full horror of a world where death wins.

And then verse 20.

*"But in fact Christ has been raised from the dead."*

Three words in Greek: *Νυνὶ δὲ Χριστὸς ἐγήγερται* — "But now, Christ has been raised." The word *νυνί* (nuni) is not just a time marker. It's an adversative — a sharp pivot. *Nevertheless.* It's the turn after the long darkness.

*But in fact.*

---

The first thing Paul calls Jesus in verse 20 is *ἀπαρχή* — *firstfruits*.

If you grew up in a farming family, you know what firstfruits means. The firstfruits were the first sheaf of the harvest — brought to God as an offering before the rest was taken in. The firstfruits were a pledge. A sample. A guarantee. When a farmer held up the firstfruits, he was declaring: *the rest is coming*. The full harvest is on its way. In Leviticus 23, God told Israel to bring that firstfruits sheaf on the day after the Sabbath — and Christ walked out of his tomb on the day after the Sabbath, on Sunday morning, the exact day the sheaf was waved before the Lord.

Jesus is the firstfruits of resurrection.

His resurrection is not the whole thing. It's the pledge of the whole thing. There is a harvest coming — a full harvest — of everyone who belongs to him. His body coming out of that tomb on Sunday morning was God holding up the first sheaf and saying: *the rest is coming*. Job knew it — centuries before Corinth, centuries before Paul, he said it from the ash heap with his skin falling off: *"I know that my Redeemer lives, and at the last he will stand upon the earth"* (Job 19:25). The firstfruits promise is older than the church.

If you have buried someone you loved in Christ, that is the promise. They are not the whole harvest. They are the grain still in the ground. The firstfruits already walked out of the tomb. The rest is coming.

---

Now verse 21–22.

*"For as by a man came death, by a man has come also the resurrection of the dead. For as in Adam all die, so also in Christ shall all be made alive."*

We spent 39 weeks in Romans. Many of you have Romans 5:12–21 still running somewhere in your minds. *"Therefore, just as sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men."* One man. Adam. Federal head of the human race. His act, his fall, his death — all of ours.

Paul is saying: same structure. Same logic. One man opens the door to death. One man opens the door to life. The Last Adam does what the first Adam undid.

Adam's sin reached everyone who was in him. Christ's resurrection reaches everyone who is in him.

This is not a self-improvement project. You cannot resurrect yourself. You cannot decide to be on the right side of this equation by willpower or effort or resolve. You are either in Adam or in Christ. There's no third option.

And if you are in Christ — if you have confessed with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believed in your heart that God raised him from the dead — then Paul is telling you: the same power that rolled the stone away is the same power that has hold of you.

---

Verses 24–26: the order of what happens next.

Christ is raised first. Then, at his coming, those who belong to him. Then the end — when he hands over the kingdom to the Father, having destroyed every rule, every authority, every power. And listen to verse 26:

*"The last enemy to be destroyed is death."*

Every throne. Every power. Every stronghold. Every dictator, every empire, every force that opposes God — destroyed. And the last one is death.

That's specific. Death is the last enemy, not the first. It means there's a sequence here. Things are being taken apart in order. History is moving toward a verdict. Death is not winning. Death is on the list.

Now — I know what some of you are thinking. *It doesn't look like that.* It looks like death is winning. You have a name in your head right now, or a face. It looks like death takes what it wants, when it wants.

You're not wrong about the observation. You're wrong about the timeline.

In 1945, a Japanese soldier named Hiroo Onoda was stationed in the Philippines. When the war ended, he didn't know. Nobody told him. His commanding officer had died. The radio went silent. He stayed in the jungle for 29 years — until 1974 — still fighting a war that had been over for three decades. He ambushed supply convoys. He evaded patrols. He stayed loyal to an empire that had already surrendered.

Death is Hiroo Onoda. It doesn't know the war is over — or it acts as if it doesn't — and that's exactly what makes it dangerous and exactly what makes it doomed.

The war ended on Easter Sunday. The enemy is still fighting — still taking people, still breaking bodies, still turning off lights. But the verdict is in. The sentence has been handed down. Christ has been raised, and every enemy is being destroyed in order, and death is last on the list.

The last enemy. Not the final winner. The last one to fall.

---

Verse 28 tells us that when the last enemy falls, God will be *"all in all."* Between here and the climax, Paul spends twenty verses on what the resurrection body will be like — the seed that dies and is raised something new, the contrast between the perishable and the imperishable, the natural body and the spiritual body. We're going to jump past that argument to where it lands, because Paul is building to a crescendo and I want you to hear it hit.

Verse 50.

Verses 51–53: *"Behold! I tell you a mystery."* When Paul says *mystery* in his letters, he doesn't mean something puzzling or obscure. He means something that was hidden and has now been revealed. *"We shall not all sleep, but we shall all be changed, in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trumpet."*

The trumpet. The change. The imperishable putting on the imperishable. Flesh and blood cannot inherit the kingdom — but something will. Something transformed, something raised, something that keeps the continuity of *you* while shedding everything that death could touch.

And then verse 54. Paul reaches back to Isaiah — chapter 25, verse 8. Written seven centuries before Christ:

*"He will swallow up death forever."*

Paul quotes it as accomplished fact: *"Death is swallowed up in victory."*

And then — this is my favorite move in the whole chapter — he taunts it.

*"O death, where is your victory? O death, where is your sting?"*

That's Hosea 13:14. Paul is not asking genuinely. He is mocking. He is standing in the ruins of death's power and pointing at the rubble and laughing. *Where is it?* You said you had victory. Where is it? You said you had a sting. Bring it. The man who wrote that sentence had watched Stephen stoned, had been left for dead himself, had planted churches in every city that might kill him for it. He is not writing triumphalism from a safe desk. He wrote it from prison. He wrote it because he had seen the risen Christ with his own eyes and he knew — *he knew* — that what he was saying was true.

Verse 56: *"The sting of death is sin, and the power of sin is the law."*

There it is. The sting. You know this. Thirty-nine weeks of Romans. Sin earns death. The law names the sin and pronounces the sentence. That is the sting — sin plus condemnation. Take away the condemnation and death loses its venom.

How do you take away the condemnation? Romans 8:1. *"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."*

The sting has been pulled. Christ absorbed it on the cross. He took the law's full sentence into his own body, exhausted it, finished it — *tetelestai*, it is finished — and walked out of the tomb on the third day, demonstrating that death had spent everything it had and couldn't hold him.

*"But thanks be to God, who gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ."*

Not earns. Gives. You don't win this victory. You receive it. The victory is his. The gift is yours.

---

Verse 58. After the most massive theological argument in the New Testament — after the witnesses, the logic, the firstfruits, the Last Adam, the list of enemies, the taunt of death, the Isaiah quotation, the Hosea quotation, the sting and its removal — Paul ends here:

*"Therefore, my beloved brothers, be steadfast, immovable, always abounding in the work of the Lord, knowing that in the Lord your labor is not in vain."*

*Not in vain.*

Some of you are fighting for a marriage that isn't getting better. You've been at it for years. You're tired. Nobody can see what it costs you to come home and try again. *It is not in vain.*

Some of you are white-knuckling through a week, through a day, through an hour — and you stumble, and you get back up, and you stumble again, and you get back up again. It feels like you're losing ground. *It is not in vain.*

Some of you have a body that is breaking down. A diagnosis that changed the sentence your life was going to be. And you get up on Sunday and you come to church and you sing and you're faithful and you grieve quietly. *It is not in vain.*

The resurrection is the reason that word is available. Without Easter, everything is in vain. Paul said so — *"if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile."* Everything evaporates. Every sacrifice, every act of faithfulness, every prayer, every act of love that cost something — gone. The grave swallows it all.

But Christ has been raised.

And that means the things you do in him do not disappear. They are being held. They are counted. The same God who raised Christ from the dead is the God who sees the faithfulness nobody else sees.

*Your labor is not in vain.*

---

If you are in Christ this morning — if you know what it means to have the condemnation lifted and the sting removed — then Easter is not just a holiday. It's the ground you stand on. Every day. When the body fails and the marriage strains and the addiction claws and the grief doesn't lift. The last enemy is losing. The verdict is in. The harvest is coming.

Steadfast. Immovable. Abounding.

Not because you feel like it. Because it is not in vain.

---

And if you don't know him — if you're sitting here this morning and Easter is something that happens to other people's faith —

Paul told the Corinthians the gospel at the very start of this chapter. Verses 3–4: *"Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures."* That's it. That's the gospel. Died, buried, raised. Everything else in this chapter flows from those three verbs.

Paul wrote to a city full of people who had plenty of options. Corinth had temples on every corner. Every god imaginable. And he laid out the evidence for the resurrection — the eyewitnesses, the logic, the consequence if it's false — and he said: *But in fact.*

It is a factual claim. The tomb was empty. The witnesses were real. The church that formed around the resurrection was real people who went to real deaths rather than say it hadn't happened. History cannot explain the church without the resurrection.

You don't have to clean yourself up to come. You don't have to understand all fifteen chapters first. The thief on the cross had hours to live and couldn't move his hands — he had no time for theology, no time for obedience, no time to prove anything — and he received the same promise the twelve received: *"Today you will be with me in paradise."* He heard. He believed. He was in Christ. The same firstfruits. The same harvest. The same *not in vain*.

Come find me after the service if you want to talk about it.

---

Last enemy. Not final winner.

*But in fact Christ has been raised from the dead.*

*Pray.*
