## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Warm Bibliological Survey That Stops Short of Preaching

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A well-intentioned survey of the doctrine of Scripture that accumulates historical breadth and pastoral warmth while stopping short of driving 2 Timothy 3:14–17 into the conscience with gospel force and clear exhortation.

### 3. Berean Gate
**Scopus Test: Conditional Pass.**
The sermon's thesis — Scripture is God's inerrant Word and the measuring stick for our lives — does arise from 2 Timothy 3:15–17, particularly from the "God-breathed" and "profitable" language of vv. 16–17. However, the sermon barely inhabits the text. Verses 14–15 receive only a single passing comment about a Greek verb. The passage's own argument — that Timothy must *continue* in Scripture precisely because of who taught him, the sacred writings that lead to *salvation through faith in Christ Jesus*, and the sufficiency of Scripture to make the man of God *complete* — is largely bypassed in favor of a historical survey of Baptist confessions.

**Authority Test: Pass.**
The preacher does not soften or edit the text. He submits to its authority and even presses it against cultural resistance to divine lordship.

**Ruling: Conditional pass. The passage is the stated occasion, not the controlling argument. The sermon risks being a topical lecture on bibliology anchored loosely to a text rather than an exposition that discovers its argument within the text's own structure and movement.**

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Flag 3 is triggered: Christ is absent or unnecessary.**
The Baptist Faith and Message is quoted ("All Scripture is a testimony to Christ"), but Christ Himself is never proclaimed in this sermon. The gospel reduces to a one-word note ("Gospel.") tacked on before communion. There is no proclamation of Christ's person or work, no articulation of justification by faith, no call to trust in Christ. The sermon would survive almost entirely intact in a synagogue, a mosque, or a Mormon meeting house — any community committed to the authority and inerrancy of its scriptures. The cross is a decoration, not the melody.

**Flag 5 is triggered: No actual exhortation.**
The sermon explains and applies but never summons. The congregation is told Scripture is the measuring stick. They are not told what they must now do — repent of neglect, trust Christ through the Word, commit to daily reading as a spiritual discipline grounded in union with Christ. The re-commitment to the Bible reading plan appears at the close but functions as an organizational nudge, not a herald's summons.

**Score is capped at 69.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 11/25**
- *Textual Fidelity: 4/10.* The sermon identifies the text (2 Tim 3:14–17) but spends the overwhelming majority of its length in Baptist confessional history and cultural commentary. Verse 14's Greek imperative is noted but not developed. Verses 15, 16, and 17 are mentioned but not inhabited. The text's own argument — that the Scriptures are able to make one wise unto *salvation through faith in Christ Jesus* (v.15) — is named in the BFM quotation but never preached from the text itself.
- *Contextual Awareness: 2/5.* No treatment of the surrounding context: the perilous times of ch. 3, Paul's awareness of his own approaching death (4:6), the personal commission to Timothy, or the redemptive-historical weight of "from childhood you have known the sacred writings." The Fallen Condition Focus (FCF) is largely absent — the sermon never names the specific human condition (ignorance of Scripture, vulnerability to deception, drift from truth) that this passage was written to address.
- *Exegetical Clarity: 3/5.* The Greek imperative in v.14 is a helpful moment of exegetical transparency. The explanation of "canon" from the word for "reed" is illuminating. Beyond these two moments, the text is more quoted than opened.
- *Logical Faithfulness: 2/5.* The historical survey of confessions, while historically accurate, is imported material that surrounds rather than serves the text. The sermon explains what the confessions say about Scripture more than what this text says about Scripture.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness: 2/7.* Christ is mentioned as the "focus of divine revelation" in the BFM quotation but is never proclaimed as the Savior this text points to. The sermon's content could stand without Christ at its center.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 2/3.* Some awareness that Scripture points to Christ is present via the BFM quote, but 2 Timothy 3:15's explicit phrase "salvation through faith in Christ Jesus" is not expounded.
- *Doctrinal Precision: 4/4.* The doctrine of inerrancy is handled with care, historical range, and confessional grounding. The use of Mohler and the Chicago Statement is apt. This section is the sermon's strongest.
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse: 3/4.* Strong alignment on bibliology. The 1689 LBCF is quoted approvingly and accurately. No doctrinal contradiction detected.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 4/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel: 0/5.* There is no clear articulation of justification by faith. The word "Gospel" appears once as a section heading before communion with no development.
- *Necessity of Christ: 2/5.* Christ is mentioned as the focus of Scripture, but His necessity for salvation is assumed, not proclaimed.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5.* The sermon does not orbit morality — it is about the authority of Scripture. But neither does it move toward the cross. Scripture is presented as an end (or near-end) rather than the instrument by which God brings sinners to Christ.
- *Synagogue Test: TRIGGERED.* A devout Jew, a Muslim, or a Mormon could hear this entire sermon and not be offended by the necessity of Christ's blood. The doctrine of Scripture is proclaimed; the gospel the Scriptures proclaim is absent.

- *Spiritual Differentiation: 2/5.* The sermon largely addresses the assembled congregation as a single unit. The JW illustration speaks to the uninstructed and vulnerable. The identity-struggle illustration touches the morally compromised. - *Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 2/4.* The bowling bumper metaphor and the measuring-stick imagery equip for discernment. But the congregation is not sent out — they are encouraged to continue doing what they are already (perhaps) doing.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* Primarily of myself, my Bible reading habits, and Baptist confessional history. Christ is gestured toward but not magnified.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Ideas about Scripture's authority, and about God's nature via the Tim Keller section. The encounter is intermittent.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Primarily informed, with brief moments of conviction in the lordship passage. Not genuinely comforted — there is no gospel comfort offered.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Historical confessional breadth.** The survey from Thomas Helwys (1611) through the Chicago Statement is genuinely instructive and shows pastoral care for grounding the congregation in a tradition larger than themselves. The 1689 LBCF quote is apt and well-introduced.
2. **The Tim Keller passage on divine lordship.** The extended treatment of why God must disagree with us, rooted in His omniscience, holiness, and timelessness, is the sermon's most theologically substantive and pastorally searching moment. "If your god never disagrees with you, you might just be worshiping an idealized version of yourself" earns its place.
3. **The measuring-stick / canon etymology.** Connecting the word "canon" to the reed-measuring-stick and then to the image of God taking our measure is the sermon's most memorable and textually connected homiletical moment.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The gospel is absent.** Second Timothy 3:15 explicitly says the sacred writings are "able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus." This phrase — arguably the theological heart of the passage — receives no exposition and no proclamation. The sermon teaches that Scripture is the measuring stick but does not proclaim what the Scripture measures us against: the righteousness of Christ, received by faith. The gospel note before communion is a one-word placeholder, not a proclamation.
2. **The text is the occasion, not the governor.** The sermon uses 2 Timothy 3:14–17 as a launching pad for a well-organized Baptist bibliological survey. The passage's own argument — continue, because of who taught you, because of how long you have known it, because it points to salvation in Christ, because it is God-breathed and sufficient — is never traced or preached. The text could be removed and most of the sermon would remain intact.
3. **No exhortation.** The congregation is invited to re-commit to the Bible reading plan. They are not summoned to anything. There is no clear "therefore" — no moment where the preacher looks the congregation in the eye and says: "You must now read this Word because your life depends on it, because Christ is found in it, because the judgment seat awaits and you will give account." The sermon ends before the summons.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely does not see that the doctrine of Scripture, preached without the gospel the Scripture contains, is bibliological moralism — a call to submit to the Bible's authority that provides no power for that submission. The sermon implicitly assumes that telling people Scripture is authoritative will produce love for Scripture. But the text itself (v.15) locates the power of Scripture in its ability to make one wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus. The Scripture is not the destination — Christ is. The preacher may also be unaware of how much the confession-survey section crowds the text out of its own sermon.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The historical sweep through Baptist confessions from 1611 to the Chicago Statement *feels* like depth. It communicates seriousness, tradition, and theological rootedness. But it functions as scaffolding that never supports a building. The confessions all say Scripture points to Christ. The sermon never arrives at Christ. The breadth of the survey creates the impression of substance while the text itself — and the gospel it contains — go largely unexpounded.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Second Timothy 3:15 is the sermon's unclaimed gold: "the sacred writings, which are able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus." The text does not merely say Scripture is authoritative — it says Scripture's *telos* is salvation in Christ. This is the Reformed-Baptist understanding of the whole canon: the Scriptures are Christotelic. Every confession cited in the sermon agrees on this point. The BFM itself says "All Scripture is a testimony to Christ, who is Himself the focus of divine revelation." But the sermon never arrived there. The measuring stick measures us against Christ — who alone has kept the standard perfectly, and whose righteousness alone is imputed to those who trust Him. That is the sermon's missing center, and it was sitting in the text the whole time.

### 14. Surgical Fixes
1. **Rebuild around 2 Timothy 3:15.** Make "salvation through faith in Christ Jesus" the hinge of the sermon. The authority of Scripture (v.16–17) flows from the salvation Scripture reveals. Restructure: *What Scripture is* (vv.16–17) → *What Scripture does* (v.15: makes wise for salvation in Christ) → *Therefore, what we must do* (v.14: continue in it, because it leads you to the Savior).
2. **Add a gospel proclamation — not a note, but a paragraph.** After the measuring-stick application, preach: "But here is the good news — the measuring stick has been met. Jesus Christ kept every commandment, fulfilled every type, satisfied every legal demand. And God offers His righteousness to everyone who trusts in Christ alone. That is what your Bible is pointing you to every page."
3. **Cut the Sanaani Arab prayer insert or relocate it entirely.** As positioned, it interrupts the sermonic momentum at the worst possible moment and asks the congregation to shift cognitive registers without preparation.
4. **Reduce the confessional survey by half.** Choose two confessions — the 1689 LBCF and the BFM — and use the others as a footnote reference. The survey as it stands consumes too much of the sermon's energy and gives the impression that Baptist consensus, not the text itself, is the authority being appealed to.
5. **Add a direct summons at the close.** After the re-commitment to Bible reading, say: "Do not read the Bible merely to be informed. Read it to find Christ. Read it because a day is coming when God will take your measure — and the only measure that will stand is the righteousness of His Son, trusted by faith, found in His Word."
6. **Address the humbled-but-not-yet-believing hearer.** The sermon as it stands addresses the convinced Baptist congregation. Add a direct word: "Some of you are here today and you are not yet sure about any of this. You are not sure God exists, or that this Book can be trusted. Let me tell you what the Book itself claims about why you are not yet sure..."

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Triggered.** The theology score (11/18) is moderate; the Gospel Centrality score (4/15) is critically low. The sermon is not dead — there are pastoral moments of real warmth and conviction. But it presents the authority of the Bible without the gospel the Bible proclaims, which is precisely the error it warns against (the JW woman had a Bible and no Christ). The doctrine is largely right. The gospel melody is silent.

> "This sermon may be doctrinally careful about the authority of Scripture but is pastorally inert at the gospel level. It calls people to trust the Book without clearly proclaiming the Christ the Book reveals. Correct bibliolog without gospel proclamation has not yet become preaching."

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
There are flashes of genuine pastoral gravity — particularly the lordship passage and the image of God taking our final measure — but the sermon does not culminate in worship or in Christ, and the eternal weight it gestures toward is never fully carried.
