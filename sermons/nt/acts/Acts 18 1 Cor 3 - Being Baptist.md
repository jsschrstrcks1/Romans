## Landmarkism and the Danger of Backwards Thinking

Landmarkism says we trace our history as Baptists back to John the Baptist, and that as a result we are not part of the Reformation because we never strayed from God and so never needed a reformation. This is all traced back to a guy who wrote a hugely popular book — almost certainly J. R. Graves and the movement that followed, popularized by J. M. Carroll's *The Trail of Blood* — that was based on some research that was... let's call it optimistic.

When you read the Bible, you must take what it says and let it shape your opinions. If you do that backwards — shape what you think of the Bible by your opinions — you can end up in dangerous territory. The same is true in any discipline. If you let your opinions bias your science, you end up with something other than science. If you let your opinions shape your truth, rather than allowing THE truth to shape your opinions, you end up holding to something that isn't true.

There was a guy — an early philosopher thousands of years ago, Heraclitus of Ephesus — who believed everything in the universe was made of fire, and that we were living inside a — not his words, but his idea — rubber-esque tire, and the sun, moon, and stars were places where the fire of the universe had burned through. Eventually he believed everything would be consumed by the fire. As you can tell, he really liked fire. He spent a lot of time thinking about fire, and instead of allowing the truth to shape his thinking, he went around telling everyone fire is the start of everything, everything is made of fire, and fire will be our doom. Everyone knew that people were not made of fire — that is one of those things that is obvious. What happens when you touch fire? What happens when you touch a person? It's obvious at that point that we aren't made of fire. He was so obscure in his reasoning that the ancient world called him "the Obscure" — and in part that is because he took his own thinking and elevated it above what is true.

When you approach any discipline that way, you have problems. In computers, if piece A is broken, but you decide it's piece B, it doesn't matter how many times you replace piece B, or C, or D — if you don't replace piece A, the broken piece, it's not going to fix the computer. Because the truth is that A is the piece that is broken. Without replacing A, it's never going to fix the problem.

## Landmarkism and the Donatists

Landmarkism does the same thing. They started with the idea that Baptists maybe had never strayed. So with that idea in mind, they tried to warp history to reflect this. They spent thousands of hours doing research and studying groups across a couple thousand years trying to find evidence of a church existing outside the Catholics. They did find some disparate groups here and there. Groups that rejected this or that teaching of the Catholic church. Groups that were vaguely — but not always — baptistic in their practice. That is, they dunked people. Frequently that was the only thing in our theology and practice that we shared with a given group. Some of the groups denied basic tenets of the faith, and some did that AND rejected believers' baptism.

We don't have time to talk about all of them, but we will talk about the Donatists. The Donatists are a group the Landmarkers trace their history through. The Donatists were a fourth-century group in North Africa who split from the Catholic church over the question of whether clergy who had buckled during Roman persecution — men who had handed over copies of Scripture to be burned to save their own skin — could still validly administer the sacraments. They said no. If your bishop was a coward or a traitor, then every baptism he performed and every communion he served was invalid.

Now — does that sound like Baptists? They were still Catholic in most of their theology. They held to the same creeds, the same sacramental system. They weren't arguing for sola scriptura. They weren't practicing believers' baptism by immersion. They weren't holding to congregational church governance. Their one distinctive was an insistence on the moral purity of clergy — and even Augustine opposed them, arguing that the validity of the sacraments depends on Christ, not on the minister's holiness.

According to Landmarkism, these Donatists carried the torch of the true church for nearly two hundred years. Do they even look vaguely like us? They did read the Bible. But the similarities mostly stopped there. The imaginative story told by the Landmarkers includes a number of groups like this, where their story is supported more by hopes and dreams than truth and facts.

In fact, there is almost never any link between the groups they drag our history through, and several times the groups hated each other. Frequently they were theologically at war. Yet somehow the theory believes one gave birth to the other. Crazy, isn't it? The impossibility of the timeline is not the only issue.

## The True Foundation of the Church

Do you remember who I mentioned they credit with the foundation of the church? John the Baptist. But who is the church really based upon? Jesus. Jesus was the stone the builders rejected but that became the cornerstone. Jesus did all the work of creating and redeeming us. Jesus was the one who veiled his glory, willingly, came to earth to be fully God and fully man, who was born of a virgin. Jesus was the one who lived a perfect and sinless life. Can you imagine getting through puberty sinlessly? Jesus did. Jesus was the only one who was able to make the payment God's holiness and justice demanded for sin, and Jesus paid that through his death on the cross.

John the Baptist was a good guy. He was largely obedient. History remembers him in those terms. But what right has John the Baptist to be the foundation of the church? None.

The point is this: the foundation laid by John the Baptist — which pointed to Christ — was not bad, but it wasn't the full, lifesaving gospel of truth that Jesus gave us.

Just Jesus — as revealed to us in the Word of God we call the Bible. Not the Bible and other books. Not the Bible and man's interpretation of the books. Just the Bible, and what it meant when it was written.

## Our Actual History

In truth our history is a bit more recent. The Baptist movement emerged from the English Separatists — men like John Smyth and Thomas Helwys, around 1609 to 1612 — who were themselves shaped by the Reformation. We have roots in the Reformation in Geneva under John Calvin, and we have roots in the Reformation in Germany under Martin Luther. Through Luther and some of the Anabaptists we recovered a biblical model of baptism and the gospel. Calvin and Luther agreed on the gospel and on grace. They both taught many similar things. Some of the Anabaptists traveled to Switzerland, and then across Europe, to the UK, and to America. In America these Calvinistic Anabaptists grew and grew. They were evangelistic. They were devoted to the Word of God and its teaching. They fought to be as close to the Bible in word and deed as they could be. The Calvinistic Puritans already in America had been preaching the gospel pretty thoroughly.

At the foundation of the Baptist denomination, the Particular Baptists — who were Calvinist — became the dominant stream in America, and all of them held to believers' baptism. There were also General Baptists, who were Arminian, but the Calvinist wing grew fastest and shaped most of what we know as Baptist identity today. There has been an ebb and flow to our history ever since. When we are closer to Scripture, we seem to grow, and when we wander and give priority to man's ability, we seem to ebb. Right now as a denomination we have been ebbing a bit. But the resurgence of biblical Christianity will see us through.

That's sort of our history in a nutshell. We are related to most of the Reformers, the Puritans, and the Anabaptists — all of whom were very gospel-centered, focused on the Word of God above all else, and determined to believe what the Bible teaches.

## Baptist Distinctives

We have a number of Baptist distinctives — at least seven.

**First: Biblical Authority.** The Bible is our chief authority in our theology and practice. We want to do what the Bible teaches. We want to teach what the Bible teaches. We want to reflect what the Bible says about God and the church.

**Second: Believers' Baptism.** Lots of other churches baptize basically anyone — even babies. They say it's symbolic of Old Testament circumcision, but that isn't the model Scripture gives us. The biblical model is that we are first saved by God, and then baptized as an outward sign of an inward change. In other denominations, baptizing anyone causes confusion. I've met people on the street that say they know they are a Christian because their parents baptized them as kids. I knew a guy whose parents were baptized Lutheran, as an example, and they were proud of their certificates of baptism and kept them in a place of prominence. They had never been to church. Did not have a personal relationship with Jesus. Were lost as lost — in fact were antagonistic any time you tried to talk to them about God. But they believed they were Christians because they had those certificates. In addition to being the biblical model, believers' baptism is also the best practice. It doesn't lead to people becoming confused like my friend's parents.

**Third: Regenerate Church Membership.** In order to join a Baptist church, your pastor has to be convinced you are a believer in Jesus Christ. In order to be baptized in a Baptist church, your pastor has to be convinced you are a believer in Jesus Christ. In other denominations this isn't so. Anyone can join, anyone can be a member, anyone can vote and shape the future of the denomination. Why would we want people who are currently rebelling against God to have a voice in the shaping of the direction of our denomination?

So it's definitely a blessing that our denomination isn't led the way many others are. Let's be frank — in every denomination there are lost people. Lost people in the church, lost people as deacons, as elders, and so on. There are lost Bible college students, lost seminary students, lost folks teaching in Bible colleges and seminaries, and heaven forbid there are also lost pastors out there in the world as well. Some of us may have even met one or more of these in our lives. The Baptists actively try to prevent this from happening. It makes us distinct.

**Fourth: Local Church Autonomy.** Many other churches followed the Catholic style of top-down leadership. One guy or group of guys at the helm that can dictate what happens to everything downstream. The problem is those same groups are the ones affected by the previous Baptist distinctive. Once that happens — once a denomination starts living for the world — without this ideal of local church autonomy, it's a lot harder to pull a denomination back from the brink of liberalism.

The Methodists were struggling with this at their last denominational meeting. They are at odds with Scripture on women pastors. They are at odds with Scripture on the issue of homosexuality. They are at odds with Scripture on a great deal of issues, and at the root is the issue of the authority of Scripture. But because of their top-down approach to church polity, they have a much greater degree of difficulty in bringing the denomination back from the edge.

In our denomination, we believe in congregational governance. So as the local churches go, so go our denominations. As a result, the seminaries hold an awful lot of power. As the seminaries go, so go the pastors, and so go the congregations, which then affect the denomination at the annual convention. This is an area we have neglected for as long as we have been Lakeview, and something we should change. Every year we have the opportunity to elect messengers to go to the convention and help shape the course of things for the following year. We should become involved going forward.

## The Conservative Resurgence

In fact, we as a denomination have already been pulled back from the edge once. A few decades ago we started flirting with liberalism — allowing our biblical stances to be eroded and replaced with what culture said was popular. We had lesbian women training for the pastorate. We had people teaching in the seminaries and in the churches things that were contrary to Scripture in every way you can imagine. It was a dark time, but because godly men stood up for what they believed, in a few short years the entire denomination was pulled back toward orthodoxy.

---

## Don't Elevate the Denomination Over Christ

In our second passage, Paul writes:

> "For when one says, 'I follow Paul,' and another, 'I follow Apollos,' are you not being merely human? What then is Apollos? What is Paul? Servants through whom you believed, as the Lord assigned to each."
> — 1 Corinthians 3:4-5 (ESV)

The same is true of our denominations today. What, after all, is a Baptist, or a Presbyterian, or Apollos, or Paul? Just servants through whom you came to believe. We need to be careful not to elevate denomination over being followers of Christ.

We need to be careful not to become puffed up or depressed because of what God does. Sometimes God sends blessings, and sometimes God leads us into the desert. Sometimes there will be standing room only, and sometimes it's just us. So long as we are doing what God wants, the rest is up to God. God brings here whom he wants, when he wants, for his own reasons. We just have to be faithful to share the gospel with those that do come.

2. **The "True Foundation" section (pp. "Do you remember who I mentioned...").** The Christ-centered paragraph — virgin birth, sinless life, atoning death as "the payment God's holiness and justice demanded" — is theologically tight and well-stated. It is the sermon's theological peak.

3. **The Lutheran baptism certificate anecdote.** This is the sermon's most effective illustration. It is concrete, personally witnessed, and shows precisely why believers' baptism matters — without being abstract or academic.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The texts are never opened — only cited.** Neither Acts 18:24-28 nor 1 Corinthians 3:4-11 is expounded. They are quoted and then applied as proof-texts for a predetermined argument. 1 Corinthians 3:5-10 — the argument that God alone gives growth, that planters and waterers are nothing, that the field belongs to God — is never preached. These verses contain the theological energy the sermon needs but does not use.

2. **No gospel proclamation — only gospel statement.** The gospel appears once, clearly and correctly, in the "True Foundation" section. But it is stated as historical fact in service of a denominational argument, not proclaimed as urgent news for sinners in the room. The cross is decoration here. It should be the destination.

3. **The closing collapses.** "I had more planned to say, but I don't think I can finish it in the time remaining" is a jarring admission that the sermon did not land anywhere. A congregation cannot be left with a prayer request for denominational politics as the final word of a sermon. The closing reveals that exhortation was never planned — the sermon was organized as a lecture from the beginning.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher almost certainly does not see that this teaching, despite being labeled as such, is functioning as a denominational apologetic and not as preaching. The passion for Baptist distinctives and historical accuracy is evident and good — but it has displaced the question: *What must this congregation do with what they have heard?* The sermon assumes that correct historical information produces right spiritual orientation. It does not. Correct information must be driven into the conscience before it reshapes the soul.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The historical accuracy and careful factual correction create an impression of depth. The Donatist discussion, the Smyth/Helwys origins, the precision on Calvinist vs. Arminian Baptist streams — this feels like substantive theological work. And it is substantive *historical* work. But historical accuracy is not the same as expositional faithfulness or gospel proclamation. The sermon feels weighty because it is well-researched. It is not weighty in the sense the rubric requires.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

1 Corinthians 3:5-10 is never preached. Paul's point — "neither he who plants nor he who waters is anything, but only God who gives the growth" — is the precise antidote to Baptist pride, denominational self-congratulation, and any form of institutionalism. The sermon applies the *conclusion* of Paul's argument (v. 11: Christ is the only foundation) but ignores the *engine* of his argument (vv. 5-10: the church belongs entirely to God). This would have been the natural bridge from Baptist history to pastoral humility to gospel dependence — the sermon's most natural climax, and it was left untouched.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach vv. 5-10 before v. 11.** The argument of 1 Corinthians 3 is the sermon's best material and it was skipped. Spend five minutes on "neither he who plants nor he who waters is anything, but only God who gives the growth" — and let that level every form of Baptist pride before landing on Christ as the only foundation.

2. **Add a gospel moment that is proclamation, not statement.** After the "True Foundation" paragraph, pause and address the congregation directly: "If you have never rested your soul on this foundation — not on your baptism, not on your church membership, not on your denomination, but on Christ alone — today is the day." This is the natural gospel moment and the sermon walks past it.

3. **Differentiate the hearers at the Baptist distinctives section.** After explaining believers' baptism, turn directly to the person who was baptized as an infant and has never repented and believed. After explaining regenerate church membership, speak to the person sitting in the pew who has never been born again. The material is there — the application to specific hearers is absent.

4. **Replace the closing with an exhortation.** Cut "I had more planned to say" entirely. End on 1 Corinthians 3:11 — state it again, ask the congregation to look at what their own life is built on, and call them to examine themselves (2 Cor. 13:5). The prayer for the denomination can follow the exhortation, not replace it.

5. **Unify the sermon under one proposition.** The sermon currently runs two parallel arguments: (a) Landmarkism is historically false, and (b) here is what Baptists actually believe. Unify them under a single controlling idea, e.g.: *"Because Jesus Christ alone is the foundation of the church, our identity must be built on him — not on our history, our tradition, or our distinctives."* Every section then serves that one point.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered** — the Dead Orthodoxy diagnostic requires high exposition scores alongside low conscience scores. Here the exposition score is itself modest. The diagnosis is not dead orthodoxy but *category confusion*: this is a catechetical teaching, not a sermon, and it was delivered without recognizing the difference.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The "True Foundation" section carries genuine gravity — the incarnation, the sinless life, and the atoning death are stated with force — but this weight is not sustained, and the sermon as a whole does not leave the congregation in the presence of God.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This piece explicitly identifies itself as a "teaching" rather than a sermon, and the rubric strains slightly at that boundary. Catechetical and apologetic teachings have a different generic function — they instruct and defend rather than primarily proclaim and exhort. The rubric's fatal flaw flags for absent exhortation and generic application are accurate and fair, but the evaluator acknowledges that a teaching on Baptist distinctives will naturally carry lower exhortation scores than an evangelistic or devotional sermon. The core critique stands regardless of genre: even a teaching must bring its material to bear on the conscience and close with a response the congregation can actually make. The rubric is appropriate here — it simply reveals what is missing from a piece that calls itself a teaching but stops short of the pastoral work that teaching, at its best, also performs.
