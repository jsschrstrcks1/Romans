## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Charming Storm Story in Search of a Sermon**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warmly delivered and personally engaging opening of Luke 8:22–25 that marshals good illustrative instincts and genuine pastoral affection while stopping short of becoming a sermon — the exposition is incomplete, the gospel is a single bracketed word, and the congregation is never addressed with the conscience-level force the text demands.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — but narrowly.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis is roughly: "The disciples discovered who Jesus truly was through the storm, and sometimes our faith needs to be stretched the same way." This does arise from the passage's own movement (fear → rebuke → greater fear/awe), so the gate passes. However, the sermon does not fully track the text's *argument* — it circles the passage's atmosphere more than it exposes its logic.

*Authority Test:* Pass. The preacher does not edit the text's claims. Jesus is presented as Lord over nature. The uncomfortable question ("Where is your faith?") is noted, though softened (see Weaknesses).

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**FLAG TRIGGERED — Score capped at 69.**

- **Fatal Flaw #1: No clear gospel presentation.** The gospel is literally the word "Gospel." appended as a single period-terminated word at the end of the manuscript. Christ's finished work — his death, resurrection, atonement — is entirely absent. The sermon does not explain how a sinner is saved or why Christ's lordship matters to them eternally.
- **Fatal Flaw #4: Application is entirely generic.** The one explicit application is: "Sometimes like the disciples our faith needs to be stretched." This is untextured, undifferentiated, and could be said at the end of any sermon preached anywhere on any Sunday. No specific call is issued. No particular sinner is addressed. No conscience is pressed.
- **Fatal Flaw #5: No actual exhortation.** The sermon explains, illustrates, and then ends. There is no "therefore" moment. The congregation is never told what to believe, repent of, obey, or pursue. The closing line is an observation about faith-stretching, not a summons to anything.

*A score above 69 is not possible.*

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 13/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (10):* 6/10. The sermon stays near the text and references key elements (Jesus sleeping, the storm's severity, Jesus' rebuke, the disciples' fear). However, the climactic verse — "Who then is this, that he commands even winds and water, and they obey him?" (v.25) — receives almost no expositional treatment. The Christological payoff of the passage is gestured at but not opened. Verses 22–25 are treated unevenly; vs. 24 (the actual command and its result) is barely present in the body.
- *Contextual Awareness (5):* 3/5. The preacher references Mark 4 (parallel account) and situates the event within "Jesus' longest day." This is useful. The book-level context of Luke 8 — the sequence of kingdom parables, the identity question building across the chapter — is not addressed.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5):* 3/5. Three Greek word studies are offered (katebē, lailaps/syneplerou, and the filling of the boat). These are the sermon's strongest expositional moments and add genuine clarity. However, the exegetical depth is deployed almost entirely in service of establishing the storm's severity rather than unlocking the text's theological argument.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5):* 1/5. The Fallen Condition Focus is never identified. The text exists to expose the disciples' deficient faith and reveal Christ's divine authority — but the sermon does not name the FCF: *we, like the disciples, live as though Jesus is merely present in the boat rather than sovereign over all that threatens it.* That gap is never named and therefore never bridged.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 10/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness (7):* 4/7. The sermon does reach a Christological statement ("This was Jesus the Son of God") and distinguishes between Jesus as teacher/healer and Jesus as Lord of creation. The movement is right but thin. The divine identity claim — that Jesus' act of commanding the winds is an act that in the OT belongs to Yahweh alone (Ps. 107:23–32) — is entirely missed.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3):* 1/3. The sermon treats this pericope in relative isolation. There is no placement within redemptive history, no connection to what Jesus is on His way to do, no sense that the One sleeping in the stern is walking toward Golgotha where His authority over all things will be purchased.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4):* 3/4. No doctrinal errors are present. The description of Jesus' divine authority is accurate but undeveloped. The Freud quotation is interesting but theologically thin as a diagnostic tool.
- *Confessional Alignment (4):* 2/4. No contradictions of confessional theology. But the sermon's one explicit anthropological claim — that Jesus "knew they were little and growing into the men of God they would one day be" — risks presenting faith-growth as a natural developmental process rather than a Spirit-wrought transformation.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 3/15**
The gospel is a single word at the end of the manuscript. This is not a gospel presentation. It is a placeholder. No justification is explained. No cross is presented. No substitution is articulated. The Synagogue Test fails: a devout Jewish person could sit through this sermon and leave with nothing more than "Jesus is more powerful than storms, which should encourage faith." No blood. No necessity of Christ's death. No way of salvation offered or explained.
Score: 3/15 (credit only for the Christological assertion in the body).

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 4/15**
- *Heart Penetration (5):* 2/5. The illustrations are emotionally warm and the personal vulnerability (flood damage, near-sinking) creates genuine connection. But the conscience is never targeted. The heart is warmed but not searched.
- *Specificity (5):* 1/5. The single application ("sometimes our faith needs to be stretched") is maximally generic.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (5):* 1/5. No categories of Perkins' taxonomy are addressed distinctly. The unbeliever is not addressed. The believer in genuine fear is not addressed. The person whose "boat" is currently sinking (illness, divorce, loss) is not addressed with anything beyond ambient comfort. The sermon speaks to everyone generally and therefore to no one specifically.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 1/8**
There is no exhortation. The sermon does not tell the congregation what to do, believe, repent of, or pursue. The final line is a gentle observation. One point is granted for the implicit encouragement toward trust embedded in the Christological assertion.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**
- *Clarity of Outline (4):* 3/4. The sermon has a discernible shape: extended personal illustration → Greek word studies → Christological identification → light application. This is traceable, even if informal.
- *Progression (3):* 1/3. The sermon does not build toward anything. It crescendos emotionally with the personal storm story and then continues at approximately the same register. The text's own climax (v.25, the identity question) is not the sermon's climax.
- *Unity (2):* 1/2. The three children-saying-awkward-things illustrations (chocolate skin, death warnings, heaviest woman) appear without clear integration into the sermon's argument and collectively run four times longer than the sermon's only application.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**
The personal storm narrative is genuinely excellent — specific, embodied, sensory, native to the text. The detail about the masks to keep their eyes open, the water over the bow, the bilge motor burning up — this is strong illustrative work. The three children's-humor illustrations are warm but redundant and crowd out substance. The meteorological data (Lake Erie comparison, Dr. DeYoung reference) is useful and textually anchored.
Score: 3/5

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**
The manuscript reads with authentic pastoral voice — conversational, honest, unpretentious. There are moments of genuine warmth and directness. But there is no moment where the manuscript *roars*. The climactic assertion about Jesus being "King of Kings and Lord of Lords" lands in a paragraph about farting and burping with childhood story humor immediately following. The tone-text incongruence is significant: the text thunders ("Who then is this?") — the sermon chuckles.
Score: 3/5

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Mostly myself and the preacher. The personal storm narrative, while well-told, consumes the first third of the sermon. The Christological statement is present but brief. The congregation leaves with a vivid picture of the preacher's near-sinking more than with an enlarged vision of Christ.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas. Warm, personable ideas delivered with genuine affection — but the sermon does not create the conditions for an encounter with the holy. The moment that could produce that encounter (v.25: "Who then is this?") is not developed.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Mildly informed and emotionally warmed. Not comforted with gospel comfort (no cross is present). Not convicted (no conscience is pressed). The congregation receives interesting weather meteorology, a good personal story, and a gentle encouragement that faith grows.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Personal storm illustration (paragraphs 2–15):** This is genuinely excellent illustrative material — embodied, specific, sensory, and directly native to the passage's subject. The detail about the bilge motor burning up at the precise right moment ("Nothing but the hand of God kept us from sinking that day") is the sermon's strongest moment and functions as authentic testimony to providence.

2. **Greek word studies (paragraphs 23–39):** The three word studies — katebē (ominous descent), the double storm descriptor (rotating and multi-directional), and the "filled" indicating a swamped boat — are accessible, textually grounded, and add genuine illuminative force. This is a preacher who does his homework.

3. **Christological identification moment (paragraph 61–65):** The distinction between "Jesus the military leader," "Jesus the great teacher," "Jesus who could heal the sick," and Jesus who moves with the power of God against creation's most terrifying forces is a theologically meaningful escalation. It is underdeveloped, but the instinct is right.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a word, not a proclamation (final line):** The single word "Gospel." appended to the manuscript is not a gospel presentation — it is a note-to-self. An entire congregation heard this sermon and received no articulation of Christ's atoning work, no explanation of justification by faith, no call to believe. If a person in that room had died that afternoon, they would have heard nothing about how to be reconciled to God.

2. **The text's climax is abandoned (v.25):** "Who then is this, that he commands even winds and water, and they obey him?" is the single most important line in the passage — the disciples' own terrified Christological question. The sermon identifies this implicitly ("they really realized who they had in the boat with them") but never exegetes the question, never presses it toward the congregation, never asks them to answer it. The entire sermon builds toward a question that is never put to the hearer.

3. **Three children's humor illustrations crowd out substance (paragraphs 67–73):** The chocolate/death-warning/heaviest-woman cluster is three separate illustrations making approximately the same point (it is uncomfortable to be in the presence of one whose magnitude you have underestimated). One of these would suffice — even then, the connection to the text is loose. Together, they occupy more manuscript space than the entire Christological section and shift the sermon's register from awe to chuckle at precisely the moment the text is calling for trembling.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how completely the personal illustrations — however excellent — have displaced the text. The structural logic of the sermon places the preacher's storm first, longest, and most vividly. The congregation's memory of this sermon will be the boat under the bridge, not the disciples before Christ. The sermon talks *about* the passage more than it *opens* it.

There is also a blind spot on the law/gospel dynamic: the sermon is almost entirely descriptive and warmly encouraging. There is no moment of exposure — no naming of the specific unbelief or fear or functional atheism that the passage is designed to confront. The FCF (the disciples' — and our — tendency to be terrified by circumstances that are sovereignly ordered by the One we claim to follow) is never named, so it is never addressed, so the gospel can never resolve it.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal illustration is so strong — and the Greek word studies are so confidently delivered — that the sermon *feels* more substantial than it is. A listener could leave feeling they received a rich, well-researched, personally authentic message and not notice that the gospel was absent, that v.25 was never opened, and that they were given no specific call to action whatsoever. The warmth and vulnerability of the preacher creates a trust that the content does not fully warrant.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Psalm 107:23–32 is the OT backdrop to this passage and one of the most vivid: "He commanded and raised the stormy wind, which lifted up the waves of the sea... they cried to the Lord in their trouble, and he delivered them from their distress. He made the storm be still... He brought them to their desired haven." When Jesus does in Luke 8 what the Psalm says only God can do, the disciples' question ("Who then is this?") is not unanswerable — it is answered by the OT they knew. The preacher had the meteorological data, the Greek, the personal illustration — he was one Psalm away from a Christological moment that would have grounded the entire sermon in redemptive history and answered the text's own climactic question.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach v.25 as the sermon's climax.** The current structure climaxes with the storm narrative and then declines. Rebuild the sermon so that every element — Greek words, personal story, meteorological data — is building toward the disciples' terrified question: "Who then is this?" Then answer it, and put that question to the congregation. Let the sermon end with that question pressed on every hearer.

2. **Name the Fallen Condition Focus explicitly.** The FCF here is: *We are like the disciples — we have Jesus in the boat, we know his name, we may even confess his lordship, and yet when the storm rises, we live as though he is asleep and indifferent and unable to save.* Name that. Say it plainly. Then let the text's answer address it.

3. **Write a real gospel presentation — even two paragraphs.** The One who commands winds and waves is the One who walked into Gethsemane, was nailed to a cross, and bore the storm of God's wrath that we deserved. His authority over nature is the same authority by which he absorbs the curse. Preach that. The "Gospel." note must become a proclamation.

4. **Cut two of the three children's humor illustrations.** Keep the best one if needed; cut the rest. Replace that space with direct address to the congregation at their point of fear. "Some of you are in that boat right now. The diagnosis, the marriage, the prodigal — the water is coming over the bow. The question this text forces on you is not 'Will Jesus wake up?' but 'Do you actually believe he is who he says he is?'"

5. **Add differentiated application using Perkins' taxonomy.** Address at minimum three categories: (a) the believer in genuine crisis — "Jesus is not asleep to your circumstances"; (b) the unbeliever in the room — "You are in the boat. You have not yet answered the question the disciples answered"; (c) the comfortable professor of faith — "Your faith has never been tested because you have never truly trusted him with anything that cost you."

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — the concern here is the inverse. This sermon does not risk dead orthodoxy (correct but bloodless). The risk is warm relational preaching that never becomes preaching at all — storytelling with a theological wrapper, producing affection for the preacher rather than encounter with the text's Christ. The fire is present in the delivery instinct; the doctrine is missing beneath it.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries the warmth of a man who loves his people and has genuinely known God's providence in storms — but the eternal weight of the text (the disciples' terror before the divine Son, the question that names him Lord, the cross toward which he is sailing) does not press through the manuscript; a congregation could receive this sermon and feel encouraged without having stood before the holiness of Christ. **Bonus: +1/5.**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Luke 8:22–25 is a narrative pericope — a miracle/epiphany account — and the rubric applies well to it. The text has a clear theological argument (the storm reveals Jesus' divine identity and exposes the disciples' deficient faith), a clear FCF, a natural law/gospel structure (the terror of the storm → the insufficiency of human ability → the sufficiency of Christ's word), and a climactic identity declaration that demands applicatory response. No rubric strain. The passage is well-served by this framework.
