# Luke 19:1–10 — The Thing Between You and God

**Text:** Luke 19:1–10
**Status:** Draft

---

Everybody has one.

Something they hold tighter than they hold onto God. Something that — if Christ asked them to lay it down — they would hesitate. Maybe money. Maybe comfort. Maybe control. Maybe the opinion of someone they're afraid to lose.

Maybe you're sitting here right now and you know exactly what yours is.

We're going to look this morning at a man who had the same obstacle as a man we met one chapter earlier — and a completely different outcome. Same city. Same roadside. Same Jesus. Same idol on the table. One man walked away rich and damned. The other climbed down poor and saved.

Open your Bibles to Luke chapter 19. We're going to read verses 1 through 10. Luke is in the New Testament — after Mark, before John. Chapter 19, right at the top of the page.

While you're turning there — let me remind you of where we just were. In Luke 18, starting in verse 18, a ruler came to Jesus and asked, *"Good Teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?"* Jesus told him to sell everything he had and follow. The man couldn't do it. He went away very sad, because he was extremely rich. And Jesus said this — verse 24: *"How difficult it is for those who have wealth to enter the kingdom of God!"* The disciples were stunned. *"Then who can be saved?"* And Jesus answered: *"What is impossible with man is possible with God."*

Hold that line. We're going to need it.

Now read with me. Luke 19, verse 1.

For the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost."

---

## I. The Man in the Tree (vv. 1–4)

Luke doesn't waste a word. He opens with the location — Jericho. And if you know your Old Testament, that name carries weight. Jericho was a cursed city. Joshua pronounced a curse on it after its walls fell (Joshua 6:26). It was rebuilt in defiance of that curse (1 Kings 16:34). And now Jesus walks into the city of the curse on his way to the cross — and the walls of a man's heart are about to fall.

Verse 2 tells you three things about this man, and every one of them matters.

*He was a chief tax collector.* Not just a tax collector — the chief. The head of the operation. Tax collectors in first-century Palestine were despised for good reason. They were Jews who had sold out to Rome. They collected Roman taxes from their own people, and Rome let them skim whatever extra they could squeeze. The more ruthless you were, the richer you got. A tax collector was a traitor to his nation and a thief from his neighbors. A *chief* tax collector was the one who organized the whole ring.

*And was rich.* Luke makes sure you see the connection. The money wasn't clean. It was blood money — taken from his own people at the point of Roman authority. Every denarius in his purse represented someone he'd defrauded. This wasn't generational wealth or the fruit of honest labor. This was the payoff for betraying your own.

Now — Luke has just told you in chapter 18 that it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person to enter the kingdom of God. And here, immediately, he introduces a man who is not just rich, but rich from theft, rich from treason, rich from crushing the poor. If the rich young ruler was a hard case, Zacchaeus is an impossible one.

And then this detail — verse 3: *he was seeking to see who Jesus was.* Something was pulling him. The text doesn't tell us what. Curiosity? Conviction? A rumble in the conscience of a man whose money had stopped buying him peace? We don't know. But something was drawing this man toward Jesus.

There's a crowd in the way. And there's a problem — he was small in stature. So this wealthy, powerful, hated man does something no dignified person in that culture would ever do. He runs. In the ancient Near East, a man of standing did not run. It was considered shameful — undignified. You gathered your robes, you walked with authority, you let people come to you. Running was for servants and children. And here is the chief tax collector of Jericho — a man who had built his entire reputation on power and intimidation — hiking up his robes and sprinting down the road like a boy.

And he climbs a tree. A sycamore tree — a fig-mulberry, low-hanging branches, the kind of tree kids climb. Not the kind of tree a chief tax collector climbs. Not in public. Not in front of the crowd that despises him.

Do you see what Luke is showing you? Before Jesus says a word, something has already begun to break down Zacchaeus's dignity. His position. His image. A man who built his whole identity on power and wealth is now sitting in a tree like a child, straining to catch a glimpse of a traveling rabbi.

Something is happening to this man. And Jesus hasn't even looked at him yet.

---

## II. The Word That Changes Everything (v. 5)

Verse 5. This is the hinge of the whole passage.

*"And when Jesus came to the place, he looked up and said to him, 'Zacchaeus, hurry and come down, for I must stay at your house today.'"*

Three things here.

**First — Jesus looked up.** Spurgeon preached on this verse and said something I haven't been able to shake: "I have thought it one of the greatest instances of God's condescension that He can look down on man. But I will tell you there was a greater condescension than that, when Christ looked up to see Zacchaeus. For God to look down on His creatures — that is mercy, but for Christ so to humble Himself that He has to look up to one of His own creatures, that becomes mercy indeed." — C.H. Spurgeon, *Effectual Calling*, Sermon No. 73, March 30, 1856

The God of the universe looked *up* at a crooked tax collector hiding in a tree. That's the posture of grace.

**Second — Jesus called him by name.** Nobody introduced them. The text gives no indication they had ever met. Jesus walks into Jericho and calls out a man by name — a man in a tree, a man the whole city hated, a man no respectable rabbi would touch. He doesn't call out to the crowd. He doesn't address the Pharisees or the city leaders. He calls the worst man in town.

This is effectual calling. This is what it looks like when sovereign grace arrives. Jesus didn't wait for Zacchaeus to come to him. He stopped. He looked up. He spoke his name.

**Third — and this is the one that should stop you cold — Jesus said "I must."** *Dei* (δεῖ) in the Greek. Divine necessity. Luke uses this word throughout his Gospel for things that *must* happen according to God's redemptive plan. It's the same word Jesus used in Luke 4:43 — *"I must preach the good news of the kingdom of God."* The same word in Luke 24:7 — *"The Son of Man must be delivered into the hands of sinful men and be crucified."* This isn't a social call. This isn't Jesus being polite. This is the eternal purpose of God intersecting with a man in a sycamore tree.

*I must stay at your house today.*

Not "I'd like to." Not "Would you mind?" *I must.* The salvation of Zacchaeus was as certain as the cross. It was planned. It was purposed. And it was happening — right now, today, whether the crowd approved or not.

## III. Jesus Puts His Finger on the Thing

Now — here's where the pattern gets sharp, and here's why Luke arranged his Gospel the way he did.

In Luke 18, the rich young ruler came to Jesus asking the right question. He had the right theology. He had the right morality — he'd kept the commandments from his youth. And Jesus looked at him and said: *"One thing you still lack. Sell all that you have and distribute to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven; and come, follow me"* (Luke 18:22).

Jesus didn't say that to everyone. He didn't make poverty a universal requirement. He put his finger on the *one thing* that man loved more than God. For the rich young ruler, money was the idol. And when Jesus named it, the man walked away.

Now look across Jesus's ministry and watch the pattern:

In Luke 9, a man volunteers to follow Jesus — and Jesus says, *"Foxes have holes, and birds of the air have nests, but the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head"* (Luke 9:58). Comfort. That was the thing. Another man says, *"Lord, let me first go and bury my father"* — and Jesus says, *"Leave the dead to bury their own dead. But as for you, go and proclaim the kingdom of God"* (Luke 9:59–60). Family loyalty. That was the thing. A third says, *"Let me first say farewell to those at my home"* — and Jesus replies, *"No one who puts his hand to the plow and looks back is fit for the kingdom of God"* (Luke 9:61–62). The past. The familiar. That was the thing.

And think about Levi — another tax collector. Mark 2:14: *"And as he passed by, he saw Levi the son of Alphaeus sitting at the tax booth, and he said to him, 'Follow me.' And he rose and followed him."* Luke's version adds a detail: *"And leaving everything, he rose and followed him"* (Luke 5:27–28). Leaving everything. The booth. The ledger. The income. The whole operation. One word from Jesus and the man walked away from the table with money still on it. Same profession as Zacchaeus. Same idol. Same immediate, total surrender. And nobody stood over either of them with a checklist. Grace moved and the money lost its grip.

In John 4, Jesus meets the woman at the well. She's there at noon — the heat of the day — because she's avoided the other women. She's had five husbands and is living with a sixth man. Jesus steers the conversation toward living water, and then — right when it's getting theological — he says, *"Go, call your husband, and come here"* (John 4:16). He doesn't demand she leave the man. He *reveals* the thing she was hiding — and the exposure itself breaks her open. Her sin. Her shame. That was the thing.

In Luke 14, Jesus turns to the great crowds following him and says, *"Any one of you who does not renounce all that he has cannot be my disciple"* (Luke 14:33). Everything. All of it. Whatever it is. That's the thing.

Do you see the pattern? Jesus never leaves the idol unnamed. He walks up to every person and puts his finger directly on the one thing standing between them and God. Not the obvious sins. Not the public ones. The *cherished* one. The one you've justified. The one you've made peace with. The one you've told yourself isn't really that big a deal.

Jesus doesn't negotiate. He doesn't say, "You can keep most of your idols if you add me to the collection." He says: *that thing.* The thing you're clutching. That's what has to go. Because anything you hold tighter than you hold me is your functional god. And I will not share the throne.

## IV. Grace Produces What Law Demands (vv. 6–8)

Now watch what happens with Zacchaeus. Verse 6:

*"So he hurried and came down and received him joyfully."*

Not reluctantly. Not after a negotiation. Joyfully. The man scrambles down the tree and opens his house to Jesus with delight.

But look at verse 7: *"And when they saw it, they all grumbled, 'He has gone in to be the guest of a man who is a sinner.'"*

Don't miss that grumbling. The whole crowd — the respectable, the religious, the ones who thought they knew who deserved God's attention — are offended. Not that Jesus taught something controversial. They're offended that he walked into the *wrong house.* They had already decided who was worth saving and who wasn't. And the irony is thick: the people grumbling about grace are the ones who need it most and recognize it least.

Some of you do that. You look at someone — maybe someone in this room, maybe someone in your family — and you've already decided they're too far gone. Too stubborn. Too dirty. You've written them off. And Jesus walks right past your good reputation and into their house. That should terrify you more than it offends you.

You know what's remarkable about this grumbling? The text says *they all grumbled.* Not some. Not a few Pharisees in the back. All of them. The entire crowd had decided that Zacchaeus was beyond the reach of God. And if you had asked any one of them, they'd have told you they were right. He *was* a sinner. He *had* stolen from them. They weren't wrong about the facts. They were wrong about grace. They thought it had limits. They thought it had a guest list. They thought the door to God's house had a velvet rope, and they were on the right side of it. Sound familiar?

And then — verse 8 — without Jesus asking him to, without anyone demanding it:

*"And Zacchaeus stood and said to the Lord, 'Behold, Lord, the half of my goods I give to the poor. And if I have defrauded anyone of anything, I restore it fourfold.'"*

Stop. Do you see what just happened?

Nobody told Zacchaeus to give his money away. Jesus didn't say, "Sell all that you have." He didn't give the same command he gave the rich young ruler. He walked into the man's house, and the man's heart changed — and the evidence was his wallet.

Now — some will point out that the Greek verbs in verse 8 are present tense: *"I am giving... I am restoring."* Some read this as Zacchaeus defending his existing practice — "I already do these things." But Jesus's response settles it: *"Today salvation has come to this house."* Today. Not "has been here all along." *Today.* Something new has happened. Something decisive. The σήμερον — the *today* — is emphatic. This is a man being transformed in real time.

Half his goods to the poor. Fourfold restitution to anyone he'd cheated. Under Mosaic law, fourfold was the penalty for theft — Exodus 22:1. Zacchaeus isn't just being generous. He is confessing, publicly, that he is a thief. And he is making it right at enormous personal cost. This man is about to become very, very poor.

Spurgeon saw it: "Ah! Zacchaeus, you will go to bed tonight a great deal poorer than when you got up this morning — but infinitely richer, too." — C.H. Spurgeon, *Effectual Calling*, Sermon No. 73, March 30, 1856

Here is the theological weight of this moment. The rich young ruler was told to give up his money and couldn't. Zacchaeus was never told to give up his money and did it anyway. What's the difference?

Grace.

The law says, "Give it up." And the sinner clutches tighter. Grace says, "I must stay at your house today" — and the sinner opens his hands.

This is what Paul would later write in Romans 8:3–4 — *"For God has done what the law, weakened by the flesh, could not do."* The law couldn't pry money out of the rich young ruler's hands. Grace opened Zacchaeus's fists like a child's.

This is monergistic regeneration. This is effectual calling producing real fruit. Nobody coached Zacchaeus on what to say. Nobody gave him a list of things to do to prove he was serious. The Holy Spirit moved, and the evidence was immediate: the thing that had been his god was now being given away with joy.

That's how you know it's real. When the thing you held tightest becomes the thing you release fastest — that is the fingerprint of God.

## V. Salvation Comes to the House (vv. 9–10)

Verse 9:

*"And Jesus said to him, 'Today salvation has come to this house, since he also is a son of Abraham.'"*

Notice the grammar. Jesus didn't say, "Today Zacchaeus earned salvation." He didn't say, "Because you gave your money away, you're now saved." He said salvation *came*. It arrived. It showed up. Salvation came to the house — and the giving was the evidence, not the cause.

"Son of Abraham" — not by blood. The crowd would have choked on that. This man was a traitor. He had sold out his heritage for Roman money. But Jesus declares him a true son of Abraham — a man of faith. Paul would later write the theology underneath this moment: *"Know then that it is those of faith who are the sons of Abraham"* (Galatians 3:7). Abraham's true children are defined by faith, not bloodline. And here is Zacchaeus — a man the whole city said had forfeited his place in Israel — declared a son of the promise. Not sons who clutch their inheritance, but sons who trust God enough to let it go.

And then — the thesis statement of the entire passage. Verse 10:

*"For the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost."*

Not to reward the righteous. Not to honor the morally impressive. Not to call the ones who had their lives together. He came to *seek* — actively, deliberately, with purpose — and to *save* — effectually, completely, irrevocably — the lost.

Zacchaeus didn't find Jesus. Jesus found Zacchaeus. Yes — the man climbed a tree. He was seeking. But seeking to *see* is not the same as seeking to *be saved*. Curiosity brought him to the sycamore. Sovereign grace brought him down from it.

Spurgeon put it plainly: "Sheep go astray, but they do not come back again of themselves. Ask the shepherd whether his sheep come back and he will tell you, 'No, sir, they will wander, but they never return.' When you find a sheep that ever came back of himself, then you may hope to find a sinner that will come to Christ of himself. No, it must be sovereign grace that must seek the sinner and bring him home." — C.H. Spurgeon, *The Mission of the Son of Man*, Sermon No. 204, July 11, 1858

Zacchaeus was a wandering sheep who thought he was just watching from a tree. But the Shepherd had already come for him. He was a thief and a traitor sitting in a sycamore, and the Son of God walked into his city, stopped under his branch, called him by name, and said *I must.*

That's the gospel. Not a man climbing high enough to reach God. A God who stops under the tree and looks up.

## The Pattern and the Warning

Here's what I want you to see this morning. Across the Gospels, Jesus does the same thing again and again. He finds people, He names the idol, and one of two things happens.

Some surrender it. Zacchaeus. Levi, who *"left everything and rose and followed him"* (Luke 5:28). The woman at the well, who dropped her water jar and ran to tell the town about the man who told her everything she ever did. These are the trophies of grace — not because they were strong enough to let go, but because God's hand was strong enough to pry their fingers open.

And some walk away. The rich young ruler. The would-be followers in Luke 9. The crowds in John 6 who heard hard teaching and left.

Same Jesus. Same invitation. Same finger on the same idol.

Different response. And the difference — every time — is grace.

Some of you know exactly what your thing is. You've known for years. Maybe it's money — not Zacchaeus money, but the low-grade worship of financial security that makes you anxious when the account dips and generous only when it's convenient. Maybe it's a relationship you won't surrender to God's authority because you're afraid of what he'll do with it. Maybe it's comfort — the refusal to be inconvenienced for the kingdom because your schedule, your routine, your rest is the thing you protect most fiercely.

Maybe it's politics. I don't care which side of the aisle you sit on — that's between you and God and the ballot box. But when your blood pressure rises higher over an election result than it does over the lostness of your neighbor — that's an idol. When you can name every member of the Supreme Court but you can't name a single unreached people group — that's an idol. When you spend more time defending a political figure than you do defending the faith once delivered to the saints — Jesus would put his finger right there. Right there. Not because politics don't matter. They do. But because the kingdom of God is not arriving on Air Force One, and it never was. And some of you have let a flag fly in your heart where only a cross belongs.

Maybe it's control. You'll follow Jesus as long as he goes where you were already headed. The moment he asks you to turn, to give, to forgive, to stay, to leave — you freeze. Because the thing you really worship isn't Jesus. It's the version of your life where you make the calls.

Hear me. Jesus is not in the business of sharing the throne. He will not be added to your collection of priorities. He is Lord — or he is nothing to you. And he has a way of walking into your life and putting his finger on the exact thing you've been protecting, and saying: *That. That's the one. Let it go.*

---

Some of you are the rich young ruler this morning. You know the right answers. You've kept the commandments — at least the visible ones. You're moral. You're decent. You're in church. And there is one thing you will not give him. And that one thing is the door you've locked against God.

Some of you are the grumbling crowd. You've been in church long enough to have opinions about who deserves grace and who doesn't. You've looked at someone's mess and thought, *Why would God waste his time on them?* You've ranked sins — theirs above yours, always. And you've missed the fact that the grumbling crowd in Jericho was standing six feet from Jesus and didn't recognize what was happening. They were closer to him than Zacchaeus was and further from salvation. Proximity to Jesus is not the same thing as surrender to Jesus. Some of you have been sitting in the pew so long you've confused attendance with obedience.

Some of you are the backslider. You were on fire once. You gave. You served. You read the Word. And then life hit — or sin crept — or the world just wore you down. And the thing you laid at the foot of the cross five or ten years ago, you quietly picked back up. You know it. God knows it. And you've been pretending it's fine. It's not fine. But the same grace that found Zacchaeus in a tree can find you in whatever hole you've crawled into.

Some of you are Zacchaeus. You're in the tree. You're curious. Maybe desperate. Maybe you've been running from God for years and you climbed up here today just to get a look. You don't know what you're looking for, but something pulled you in.

He sees you. He knows your name. And the word he speaks over you is the same word he spoke over a crooked tax collector in Jericho two thousand years ago: *I must stay at your house today.*

Not because you earned it. Not because you cleaned up first. Because the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost.

If you have never turned from your sin and trusted Christ — if you've been sitting in the tree your whole life, watching from a distance, curious but uncommitted — today is the day.

Here is what he did for you. The same Jesus who stopped under a sycamore tree in Jericho walked into Jerusalem and was nailed to a Roman cross. On that cross, he bore the full weight of God's wrath against sin — not his own sin, because he had none — but yours. *"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God"* (2 Corinthians 5:21). That is penal substitution. That is the heart of the gospel. Your sin placed on him. His righteousness placed on you. And three days later he walked out of the grave — alive, risen, victorious over death itself.

He offers full pardon to anyone who will turn from their sin and trust him. Not to those who clean up first. Not to those who have earned it. Not to the ones the crowd approves of. To the lost. To the ones in the tree. To the ones who know they have no business being this close to a holy God and came anyway.

He's not asking you to fix yourself first. He's not waiting for you to become respectable. He stopped. He looked up. He called your name.

Whatever your thing is — the money, the relationship, the comfort, the politics, the control, the sin you've made peace with — he's putting his finger on it right now. You feel it. You know what it is.

The question is not whether Jesus sees it. He's always seen it. The question is whether you'll climb down — or whether you'll grip that branch until it breaks under you.

Come down.

*Let's pray.*
