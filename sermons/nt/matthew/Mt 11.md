When you’re training oxen you take your biggest best trained ox, and you yoke him to your little baby tiny untrained ox. In that situation, little tiny ox, and great big daddy ox, which ox do you think is actually doing the pulling? Baby ox or daddy ox? Naturally the big ox carries the load, and the little ox follows along with the big ox doing the work. This teaches the little ox to trust the big ox, and the little ox learns how to be the big ox over time.

To spend time with him in prayer. To spend time with his word. To spend time fellowshipping with other believers. Trying to do anything else will just leave a bruise as we try and shoulder a load not intended for us to carry alone.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Date Evaluated:** 2026-03-21
**Evaluator:** Thus Says the Lord Rubric (Reformed Baptist)

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Pastorally Warm Burden-Bearer That Proclaims Comfort Without Completing the Gospel"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A generous, illustration-rich handling of Matthew 11:25–30 that draws the weary hearer toward Christ with genuine pastoral tenderness and the well-chosen oxen yoke image, but ends again with "Gospel" as a placeholder — leaving the sermon without its indispensable proclamatory conclusion and the implication of Christ's person-and-work underdeveloped.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Pass.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — that Christ carries what we cannot, and his yoke is easy precisely because he is the one doing the pulling — does arise from Matthew 11:28–30 and its context. The sermon traces the surrounding context of weariness (v.20–24 as backdrop) and the Gethsemane/prison/suffering context for "my burden is light." The main point of the text (the invitation of Christ to the weary; his yoke as rest) is the main point of the sermon. Pass.

*Authority Test:* The preacher does not soften the text. He takes seriously that the "easy yoke" is not a promise of easy circumstances (Dimitri, Nik Ripken, beheadings, Peter upside-down) and refuses to preach cheap comfort. Pass.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation: TRIGGERED.**
The sermon ends with the word "Gospel." — a period and nothing else. The manuscript contains no articulation of sin, substitutionary atonement, resurrection, faith, or repentance. The promise that "a few minutes we will give you that opportunity" appears in the body but the opportunity is never written. The sermon's entire structure leads toward a gospel presentation that does not arrive. Score capped at 69.

**No other fatal flaw flags triggered.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (7/10):* The sermon stays in genuine orbit around Matthew 11:28–30. The oxen yoke illustration (trained ox + young ox) is textually grounded, culturally explained, and exegetically sound. The contextual framing of vv.20–24 (woe to the cities) as contrast is valid. However, the sermon gives significant space to vv.25–27 ("hidden from the wise and revealed to babes") only by implication — the christological claim of v.27 ("no one knows the Son except the Father…and no one knows the Father except the Son") is the theological foundation for why Christ can make such an invitation, and it is not treated.
- *Contextual Awareness (4/5):* The preceding verses' "woe" context is identified. John the Baptist's doubt (opening of ch. 11) is brought in appropriately as an example of faith-in-struggle. Good contextual awareness.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5):* The oxen yoke is the sermon's exegetical crown and is explained clearly. The transition from "yoke is easy" to the broader suffering context is handled with care. The sermon does not engage the Greek of the passage, which is appropriate for the congregation — no marks lost for absence, but no marks gained either.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5):* No eisegesis. The David Platt material (Matt 10:38, Matt 28, "sheep among wolves") is used in support of the main point — the yoke being difficult — and is contextually valid, though it ranges significantly beyond the primary text.
- *FCF:* Clearly identified — the weary, burden-bearing person who needs to hear that Jesus carries the load. This is the Fallen Condition Focus of the passage and the sermon names it consistently.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (5/7):* Christ is the sermon's center — as the great ox who carries the load, as the indwelling Spirit who leads from within, as the one who has overcome the world. This is organic to the text. The implication of divine sovereignty over suffering is treated with pastoral sensitivity (the Dimitri illustration; "God is moving in these things").
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* The passage is placed in its Matthean context (between the woes of vv.20–24 and the Sabbath controversies of ch. 12). John the Baptist's doubt is used as a redemptive-historical marker of faith under pressure. The OT law burden (500+ laws + rabbinic additions) is explained as historical context. Adequate.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4):* The oxen yoke image is theologically sound and not overextended. The indwelling Spirit point is well-made. The statement "faith isn't hard — apart from Jesus Christ faith is impossible" is precise and Reformed. Good.
- *Confessional Alignment (2/4):* The total depravity implication ("apart from Jesus Christ faith is impossible") is present and sound. But the yoke/rest promise could use explicit grounding in the indicative of what Christ has done, not merely what he will do. The passive voice of grace (what Christ accomplished at the cross, not just what he enables in the present) is absent from the body of the sermon.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 5/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (1/5):* "Gospel." is a period, not a proclamation. Nothing in the body of the sermon constitutes a gospel presentation. The closest it comes is "Jesus is the only major religious figure that came back" (from the Mk 14 sermon — wait, that is a different sermon). In this sermon: "He's already overcome all of that for us" and "cast our cares upon him" are the closest gospel notes. They are true but are not articulations of the gospel.
- *Necessity of Christ (3/5):* Christ is essential — without him the burden is impossible to carry. This is the sermon's entire argument. But why Christ can carry the burden (his finished work, the cross) is not explained.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (1/5):* The cross is completely absent from the manuscript as written. The sermon is about the present ministry of Christ (carrying the yoke, indwelling the believer) without grounding that ministry in the finished work that makes it possible.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 11/15**

- *Heart Penetration (4/5):* The Dimitri illustration is extended and powerful. The comparison between Dimitri's "little struggles" and the congregation's FEMA appeals, health battles, and grandchildren's struggles is precisely calibrated to land without diminishing their real pain. Good pastoral conscience work.
- *Specificity (4/5):* The sermon is unusually specific — named people (George, Dalia), specific circumstances (FEMA appeal, chemo, flooded books), specific cultural references (Bring the Rain song). The congregation is genuinely addressed as a congregation, not as a generic audience.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (3/5):* The sermon addresses the weary believer (the primary audience), the doubter (through John the Baptist), and briefly gestures toward the unbeliever. The self-sufficient and the backslider are not explicitly addressed.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Call to Response (1/4):* The call to response is deferred to the missing "Gospel" section. Within the body, the closest thing to exhortation is "we need to slow down, and rely on Him" and "spend time with him in prayer... his word... fellowshipping." These are good but underdeveloped. There is no clear imperative moment.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (3/4):* The description of what staying close to Christ looks like (prayer, word, fellowship) functions as genuine equipping. The Dimitri illustration equips the congregation to endure suffering with faith.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* The sermon has a traceable structure: (1) Our burdens are real → (2) Context of struggle in Matthew 11 → (3) Even John the Baptist doubted → (4) The yoke is not easy in the world's terms → (5) But Jesus is the great ox carrying the load → (6) He indwells us. This is more coherent than most sermons in this batch.

- *Unity (1/2):* One controlling idea (Christ carries your burden; stay close to him) governs the sermon throughout. The faith/doubt distinction via Alister McGrath adds a useful nuance.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

- *Relevance (2/2):* FEMA appeal, flooded books, community struggles — the preacher's personal context grounds the sermon in real life. The Dimitri story (while extended) is the right kind of illustration: it doesn't make the congregation's struggles seem small, it reframes them.
- *Clarity (1/2):* The Dimitri story is long enough to risk losing the point before arriving at it. The David Platt/Duane "The Rock" Johnson illustration is accessible and funny without being trivializing.
- *Memorability (1/1):* The great ox and little ox image is memorable and well-developed.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2):* This sermon has genuine oral presence. The personal disclosures, the rhetorical questions, and the direct address give it pulpit energy.

- *Cadence and Breath (0/1):* Several paragraphs run very long without pause or pivot. The "Is it easy to do that in your workplace?" sequence (Platt) is choppy rather than rhythmic.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* The oxen image and the "He carries the load" argument keep Christ central. The pastoral specificity keeps the congregation in view without self-absorption. This is well-balanced.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* The Dimitri illustration and the "little struggles" moment create genuine encounter. The sermon does not merely inform.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* The weary hearer is comforted. The self-sufficient is mildly convicted by implication (you're trying to carry what he offered to carry). The unbeliever is not addressed in the body of the sermon — that was apparently the "Gospel" section.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The oxen yoke illustration is exegetically, pastorally, and imaginatively excellent.** It is text-native (Matthew 11:29–30), culturally grounded (explained for a modern congregation unfamiliar with ox training), and theologically precise (the big ox does the work; the small ox just stays close). The Duane Johnson version extends it accessibly. This image will be remembered.
2. **The Dimitri story is used with pastoral precision.** The preacher does not use it to minimize the congregation's real suffering — he uses it to reframe it ("he thanked the Lord for these little struggles"). The contrast between Dimitri's "little struggles" and the congregation's real but smaller burdens is well-calibrated.
3. **Textual awareness of what "easy yoke" cannot mean.** Rather than offering cheap comfort ("follow Jesus and it gets easier"), the sermon honestly engages the suffering of faithful Christians (Nik Ripken, Peter upside-down, missionaries' dead children) before arriving at the text's promise. This is expositional honesty.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a placeholder.** "Gospel." with a period is not a gospel presentation. The sermon builds masterfully toward an invitation — "I will give you rest" — without ever explaining what rest costs and how it was purchased. An unbeliever in the congregation hears "stay close to Christ and he'll carry your load" without being told who Christ is in the redemptive sense, what he has done, and why they must trust him and not merely adopt his method of burden-management.
2. **Matthew 11:27 is completely absent.** This verse ("All things have been handed over to me by my Father, and no one knows the Son except the Father, and no one knows the Father except the Son and anyone to whom the Son chooses to reveal him") is the christological foundation for the invitation of vv.28–30. It is the reason Christ can make such an audacious offer — he has the authority and the exclusive mediatorial position. Skipping it leaves the yoke invitation floating without its doctrinal tether.
3. **The exhortation is deferred and never arrived at.** The sermon's call to response — "in a few minutes we will give you that opportunity" — never arrives in the manuscript. What specific action is the weary believer called to? What does "staying close" look like this week, concretely? The congregation leaves with a beautiful image and a deferred call.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how much the sermon's comfort is built on present experience of Christ (the indwelling Spirit, staying close) without grounding that experience in the finished work of Christ at the cross. The invitation of Matthew 11:28–30 is not simply "let Jesus manage your burdens" — it is the invitation of the one who has accomplished redemption and now invites the weary to enter his rest. The rest is not just practical; it is eschatological and soteriological. The sermon's comfort is real but thinner than the text warrants.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Dimitri story and the oxen image are so effective that they can feel like the sermon has said everything it needs to say. But the cumulative effect is: "Your burdens are real, but Christ can carry them." This is true but incomplete. The deeper question is: why can Christ carry what sin and death have made of your life? What has he done that means you can actually lay it down? That question is the gospel, and it is the answer the text's own invitation was designed to deliver. The illustration illuminates a true point while leaving the deeper point unstated.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Matthew 11:25–27 — the Christ-hymn ("I thank you Father, Lord of heaven and earth...") — precedes the invitation and is the text's theological foundation. Verse 27's exclusive claim ("no one knows the Father except the Son and anyone to whom the Son chooses to reveal him") is both the basis for the invitation and the proclamation of the gospel: that access to God comes through Christ alone, by his gift, not by effort. The sermon used verses 28–30 without the foundation of verses 25–27. That foundation would have grounded the oxen image in something far more weighty than a burden-management strategy — it would have grounded it in the divine prerogative of the Son to grant rest.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Write out the full gospel presentation.** The invitation of Matthew 11:28–30 is a gospel invitation — "come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest." Complete the proclamation: the burden of sin and its weight, Christ's substitutionary bearing of that burden at the cross, the resurrection as proof of his authority to give rest, and the call to come to him in repentance and faith.
2. **Treat Matthew 11:27.** One paragraph on verse 27 would anchor the entire sermon: "Jesus didn't just offer to help carry your load. He is the only one who knows the Father, and the only one who can bring you to him. No other religion, no self-help program, no philosophical system offers what he offers — because none of them have what he has. He is the exclusive mediator of rest that lasts forever."
3. **Add a "therefore" moment before the gospel.** After the oxen image, pivot: "So what do you need to do? Here is what staying close looks like: it begins with coming to him. And for some of you, you have never come. Tonight is the night."
4. **Trim the Dimitri story by one-third.** The story earns its length, but two paragraphs of the five can be cut without losing the emotional and theological point. Lean on the key line: "he thanked the Lord for these little struggles."
5. **Integrate Matthew 11:25–27 into the introduction.** Frame the sermon from the outset: "Jesus is about to make an extraordinary promise — 'I will give you rest.' Before he makes it, he explains why he can: 'All things have been handed to me by my Father.' This is not a motivational offer from a life coach. This is a divine invitation from the Son of God."

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** This sermon has genuine pastoral warmth and real applicatory force. Its deficit is proclamation, not passion.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The oxen image and the Dimitri story give this sermon genuine eternal gravity — the congregation is being invited to rest in the infinite strength of the divine Son — but the missing gospel proclamation means the sermon's weight is pastoral rather than redemptive, comforting rather than saving.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Matthew 11:25–30 is a mixed genre — dominical hymn (vv.25–27) followed by dominical invitation (vv.28–30). The invitation genre fits the rubric's Gospel Centrality and Exhortation categories especially well. The hymn genre (vv.25–27) rewards close christological attention that the sermon currently skips. No genre strain — the rubric fits this passage directly.
