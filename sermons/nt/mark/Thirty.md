Thirty-Three: What If He Is Alive?
Deceptive Finality
Mark 15:42-47
One of the most troubling parts of the gospel story is how fast things change from Sunday
to Friday during the days leading up to Jesus’ crucifixion. On Sunday of that week, Jesus
makes his way into Jerusalem among cheering and adoring crowds. He is celebrated as a
hero and welcomed as a friend. Today, we call that day “Palm Sunday.” In a matter of
days, however, there is a dramatic turn. The cheering is replaced by jeering. And the
crowds that cry out their adoration quickly give way to crowds that call for crucifixion.
By the end of the week, Jesus is dead. If we were to put a title on Mark 15:42-47, we
might call this part of the story, “It Sure Looks Like It’s Over to Me.”
Some scholars are critical of Joseph of Arimathea. They say that he should have spoken
up sooner and that he should have done something before Jesus’ death. On the other
hand, his behavior at this point in the story is remarkably courageous. He is one of the
Jewish leaders – and he stands as evidence that not all of the Jewish leaders wanted Jesus
to be put to death. Joseph went boldly to Pilate and dared to ask for Jesus’ body.
Eventually, the request was granted. Joseph prepared Jesus’ body for burial and then
placed the body in a cave-like tomb. He then rolled a stone against the entrance of the
tomb.
There is nothing like a burial to tell us that the story is over.
Earlier in Chapter Sixteen of The Insanity of God, we read about the death of sixteen-
year-old Tim, the son of Nik and Ruth. Tim died suddenly in Nairobi as he was rushed to
the hospital early on Easter morning of 1997. About a week or so later, hundreds of
people gathered for a memorial service in the outdoor amphitheater at the school where
Tim had been a high school sophomore. Rain started to fall just as the service came to an
end. At the conclusion of the service, the gathered group walked down the hill to the
place where Tim was to be buried, right there on the school campus.
Here are some reflections from a journal entry written by someone who was there that
day:
After we prayed and sang down there at the bottom of the hill, Nik and Ruth led
about six hundred people back up the hill for a time of fellowship and reception.
For some reason, I stayed there at the graveside. There were about twelve of us
who stayed there, the others Kenyan workers and helpers, friends of Nik and
Ruth. I felt that I was there representing the family. Nik and Ruth, I am sure,
knew what was about to happen, but I was completely unaware of Kenyan
tradition. Nik and Ruth had to be back at the school with the people, but I was
able to stay by the graveside.
In just a few moments, I realized what was about to happen. I will never forget
the instant when it dawned on me that we were going to bury Tim.
The casket was lowered into the grave – with just ropes and hands – and then four
people picked up shovels and began filling the grave. When they were tired,
others took their places. I found myself doing what everybody else was doing.
Shovels were passed around. Sometimes people simply used their hands. The
rain, the tears, the sweat; who could tell the difference? Sometimes we worked in
silence. Sometimes there was a story or two. Sometimes there was even laughter.
Sometimes there were wails of grief. Red, wet Kenyan mud was everywhere. And
together, literally, we buried Tim.
There was no stone, but we did exactly what Joseph of Arimathea did.
There is nothing quite like a burial to tell us that the story is over.
Except . . . that is not quite true. The truth of this story – and the truth of every good
burial – is that the finality is a deception. The story is not over at all! No stone is ever
the last act when it is rolled up against something in which God has a part. And that is
not just true about death. There are so many things that we are inclined to put away.
“Well, that’s the end of that!” we say. “That’s the end of that chapter. That’s finally
over.”
Get a shovel. Roll a stone. Close it up. It’s over.
But God does not pay much attention to our finalities. He has so many ways of opening
up things that are sealed up tight. And the fact that he can do that here with Jesus . . .
means that he can do that with Tim . . . and it means that he can do that with you and me
too.
And the fact that God can do that with death means that he can do that with all of our
other finalities as well. All those places where we have decided that God is not going to
work. All those people we have labeled as beyond God’s power to reach. All those
relationships that we have written off.
We simply must understand this: we think we know what the finalities are . . . but God
barely even notices.
There is nothing quite like a burial to tell us that the story is over.
Except the story is not over at all.
Joseph rolls a stone against the entrance to the tomb. But we should not pay too much
attention to the stone. God barely notices the stone at all.
Life-giving,
Death-defying
God,
forgive me for saying that the story is over.
I look at the world and I see only
impossibility.
I know when something is over.
I know when it is time to give up hope.
I know what a burial means.
But you shake your head
at my foolish pronouncements.
You tell me quietly
that you hold every story in your hands.
You assure me that no story is finished
until you say that it is finished.
Forgive me for giving up.
Forgive me for walking away.
Forgive me for deciding
exactly where you can work –
and where you cannot work.
Forgive me for declaring that certain people
are beyond your reach.
Indeed, you are the God who
gives life and defies death.
Despite my convictions
about finalities,
I trust you.
Amen
Questions for reflection:
1. What are some of the finalities in your life right now? In your heart, what are
the limits that define what God can do? Are there people in your life who
seem to be beyond the reach of God? Are there situations that seem so
difficult that are pretty sure that they will never be fixed? Are there places in
the world that seem too far away for God’s activity? What is it that you
consider “impossible” in your life right now? What do you think God would
say to you in response to that?
2. What is the difference between “living for Jesus” and “living with Jesus”? If
you had to choose one of those ways of living, which one would you choose?
Why?

# Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

## 1. Verdict Title

**A Warm Devotional Built on a Borrowed Text — Gospel Absent, Christ Peripheral, Preaching Never Begins**

---

## 2. One-Sentence Summary

A meditative devotional on Mark 15:42–47 that uses the burial of Jesus as a springboard for a broadly theistic encouragement about God overcoming human “finalities,” while failing to open the passage's actual content, present the gospel, or summon any response from the hearer.

---

## 3. Berean Gate

**FAIL — Partial.**

- **Scopus Test**: Fail. The sermon's operative thesis — “God overcomes our perceived finalities” — does not arise from the main point of Mark 15:42–47. The passage's main point is the historical reality and eyewitness verification of Jesus' burial: that He truly died, was truly buried, and that specific named witnesses confirmed both facts. This is Mark's apologetic groundwork for the resurrection. It establishes the reality of death so that the reality of resurrection cannot be dismissed. The sermon bypasses this entirely and pivots to a motivational application about not giving up. The text is not the source of the sermon — it is the occasion for it.
- **Authority Test**: Inconclusive. The preacher does not stand above the text as editor in a manipulative sense, but the text is functionally abandoned after the first paragraph. The sermon's argument would survive the removal of Mark 15:42–47 entirely and proceed unchanged.

**Because the Scopus Test fails, the evaluation continues for diagnostic purposes but no score is assigned above the gate.**

However, the rubric instructs: *”Report: 'This sermon is not text-driven. The main point of the sermon does not arise from the main point of the passage. No score is assigned. The sermon must be rebuilt from the text, not decorated with it.'”*

That report stands. A full evaluation follows for surgical usefulness.

---

## 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Multiple flags triggered. Score capped at 69 — and the Berean Gate failure overrides scoring entirely.**

1. **Flag #1 — No clear gospel presentation**: Christ's finished work is entirely absent. The resurrection is gestured at (“What if He is alive?”) but never proclaimed. Justification, substitution, and the means of salvation are nowhere in the text.
2. **Flag #3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary**: This sermon could be preached at a Jewish synagogue, a Muslim mosque, or a secular memorial service about a good man who died. God's power over death is affirmed in generic theistic terms. Christ's identity as the one who *conquered* death — not merely experienced it — is never proclaimed. The name “Jesus” appears in passing. The risen Lord does not appear at all.
3. **Flag #4 — Application is entirely generic**: “All those places where we have decided that God is not going to work.” “All those relationships that we have written off.” These are the broadest possible categories. No specific conscience is pressed. No particular sin is named. No concrete action is called for.
4. **Flag #5 — No actual exhortation**: The piece ends with a prayer and two reflection questions. Neither the prayer nor the questions constitute a summons. The congregation is never told what to believe, repent of, or do. The closest the piece comes is the prayer's “Forgive me for giving up” — but this is aspirational language, not a command addressed to the hearer.

### 1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 8/25

- **Textual Fidelity (3/10)**: The passage is narrated — Joseph's courage is noted, the burial mechanics are described — but the passage's exegetical content is not opened. The apologetic function of the burial account (establishing the reality of death as the precondition for the reality of resurrection) is completely missed. The passage's named witnesses (Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of Joses, v.47) — who serve as Mark's eyewitness chain — are not mentioned. The stone's sealing function (which matters enormously for the resurrection account) is referenced only as a metaphor for human finalities.
- **Contextual Awareness (2/5)**: The Palm Sunday to Good Friday transition is accurately described but functions as scene-setting, not exegesis. No engagement with Mark's narrative purpose, his Christology, or where this passage sits in the Gospel's argument.
- **Exegetical Clarity (2/5)**: No Greek. No attention to specific textual details. The passage's key exegetical moments — Pilate's surprise that Jesus is already dead (v.44), the centurion's confirmation (v.45), the named women witnesses (v.47) — are entirely absent.

## 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely believes the resurrection is implied throughout — the title “What If He Is Alive?” signals it, the text anticipates it, the Kenya illustration connects to it. But implication is not proclamation. A sermon on the burial of Jesus that does not proclaim the resurrection has not arrived at its destination. The piece hovers at the edge of the gospel without entering it.

The preacher also may not see that the Kenya illustration, as moving as it is, is doing most of the pastoral work that the text should be doing. The illustration is the sermon; the text is the occasion. That inversion is the structural problem.

## 12. Illusion of Strength

The devotional prose poem at the end creates the impression of depth and godward orientation. “Forgive me for deciding / exactly where you can work — / and where you cannot work.” This is good language. But it is addressed to God, not to the hearer. A prayer that voices the right orientation does not produce that orientation in the listener. The piece mistakes a beautiful prayer for a completed application.

The opening contrast (Palm Sunday to Good Friday) also feels strong — it moves, it frames the stakes — but it is scene-setting borrowed from the broader passion narrative, not exegesis of the assigned text.

---

## 13. Missed Opportunity

Mark 15:42–47 is Mark's eyewitness verification unit. Pilate's surprise (v.44 — “Pilate was surprised to hear that he should have already died”) and the centurion's confirmation (v.45) are Mark's apologetic anchors: this death was real, witnessed, and verified. Joseph's request is courageous precisely because he publicly identifies with a condemned criminal. The named women witnesses (v.47 — “Mary Magdalene and Mary the mother of Joses saw where he was laid”) establish the eyewitness chain that will make the resurrection account credible. This is historical-apologetic preaching material of the highest order — and it is entirely ignored.

A sermon that opened *this* — the verification of death, the named witnesses, the centurion's confirmation — would be far more powerful than one that uses the burial as a springboard to a devotional about human finalities.

---

## 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild from the passage's actual argument**: Mark 15:42–47 is the death-verification unit. Preach it as such. The main point: *Jesus was truly dead, buried, witnessed, and sealed — so that the resurrection would be undeniable.* Everything in the passage serves that point. Start there.
2. **Proclaim the resurrection explicitly**: The title promises it (“What If He Is Alive?”) but the piece never delivers it. Answer the question. He is alive. He rose on the third day. The stone was rolled away not to let Jesus out but to let witnesses in. Preach that.
3. **Name substitutionary atonement**: If you are going to preach this burial, you must explain why it matters that God was buried. He died in the place of sinners. He bore the curse of death so that those who trust Him would never bear it themselves. Without this, the resurrection is inspiring but not saving.
4. **Cut or subordinate the Kenya illustration**: It is excellent as illustration, but it is currently carrying the sermon. Reduce it to two paragraphs, use it to illuminate the text's point (the reality of burial, the weight of death), then drive past it to the resurrection proclamation.
5. **Add a direct exhortation before the closing prayer**: “Therefore — trust the risen Christ. Not trust in God's ability in general — trust the specific man who died and rose. Confess that His death was for you. Believe that His resurrection guarantees yours. Do not settle for a God who can overcome difficulties when the text is offering you a God who conquered death.”
6. **Cut the final discussion question (living for Jesus vs. living with Jesus)**: This question is unconnected to the passage and introduces an entirely new framework in the last line of the piece. It weakens the ending and suggests the piece itself does not know where it is going.

## 17. Rubric Fit Note

Mark 15:42–47 is narrative — specifically, an eyewitness verification pericope embedded in a passion narrative. The rubric fits well here: narrative passages must be preached as narrative (character, scene, conflict, resolution) but must also yield their theological cargo. The rubric's categories are all applicable. What the genre demands here is that the preacher follow Mark's narrative logic — burial as proof of death, death as precondition for resurrection — rather than treating the burial as a free-floating symbol. The rubric does not strain against this passage; the passage itself was not used to its potential.
