## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date:** 2026-03-21
**Sermon File:** mark 4 (5-30-19).md
**Passage:** Mark 4:35–41 (Jesus calms the storm), with Baptist Faith & Message statement on God the Son

---

### 1. Verdict Title
**Two Natures, One Savior — Solid Theological Foundation With a Gospel Announced But Not Opened**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warmly pastoral sermon on the full humanity and full divinity of Jesus from Mark 4:35–41 that handles the passage's two great movements with clarity and appropriate awe, but treats the gospel as a label rather than a proclamation and leans on the Baptist Faith and Message statement in ways that substitute creedal recitation for expository development.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS**
The main point of the sermon — Jesus is fully man and fully God, and the disciples' terror before the calmed storm is the appropriate response to who he is — arises from Mark 4:35–41 itself. Verse 41 ("Who is this? Even the wind and the waves obey him!") is the passage's climax and the sermon's governing question. The sermon follows the text's own argument.

**Authority Test: PASS**
The preacher submits to the text. The uncomfortable demand of v.41 — that right knowledge of Christ produces fear, not comfort — is honored rather than domesticated. The application (would we sin if we truly knew who God is?) submits to the text's claim.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation (TRIGGERED):**
The sermon announces "Gospel" as a section heading after the BFM reading, but does not proclaim the gospel content. The BFM statement includes references to Christ's substitutionary death and resurrection, and the sermon reads it aloud, but the preacher does not explain, apply, or press these truths. The warning about rough seas ("this is the warning call over your radio") is gospel-adjacent, but an unbeliever would not leave knowing what the gospel is or how to be saved. The Communion passage (1 Corinthians 11:23–26) appended at the end suggests this was a Communion Sunday — an occasion that should have demanded a full gospel proclamation and did not receive one.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (7/10):* The sermon draws three observations from Mark 4:35–41 — Jesus' humanity (he slept), Jesus' divinity (he commanded the storm), and the disciples' escalating fear (v.41). These arise from the text's own structure. The exegetical observation that professional fishermen would not have been easily frightened — requiring a Katrina-level storm to panic them — is an excellent contextual inference that illuminates the disciples' desperation.
- *Contextual Awareness (4/5):* The historical context of the passage is engaged well. The Teli of India people-group section and the BFM reading interrupt the exposition, but the passage itself is treated with attentiveness.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5):* The movement from humanity to divinity is clear and accessible. However, the FCF is not named precisely. Mark 4:35–41 addresses the fallen condition of unbelief and misunderstanding about who Jesus is — the disciples' rebuke ("don't you care if we drown?") exposes a failure of trust in a present Savior. This goes unnamed.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5):* No eisegesis. However, the BFM reading creates a structural problem — it inserts a creedal statement into the middle of an expository sermon in a way that can substitute for development rather than supplement it.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 14/18**

- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* The sermon notes that "Jesus honored the divine law by his personal obedience, and in his substitutionary death on the cross he made provision for the redemption of men from sin" — via the BFM reading. But this is not developed organically from the text.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7/15**

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* More of Christ — the two-natures focus keeps him central.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Closer to encounter than most — the fear-of-God application and the storm illustration are genuinely awe-inducing moments.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Convicted (the "desire for change" test), informed (two natures), with some awe. Not fully comforted — the gospel comfort is absent.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The fishermen-wouldn't-be-afraid contextual inference.** Establishing that experienced fishermen required a Katrina-level storm to panic is excellent contextual work that makes the disciples' terror readable as genuine severity, not timidity. It illuminates the passage rather than decorating it.
2. **"If knowing Jesus hasn't caused a change in your life, you don't yet know Jesus."** This single sentence is the most diagnostically sharp moment in the sermon. It functions as a genuine self-examination tool for the nominally religious and is applied with appropriate directness.
3. **The fear-of-God application from v.41.** Taking the disciples' escalating fear as the model for appropriate response to Christ's identity is textually faithful and counter-cultural. The speed-trap illustration grounds the abstract into the concrete.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The BFM statement substitutes for expository development.** Reading the confessional statement on Christ is not wrong, but it is not exposition. The sermon's Christological work should arise from the text itself, with the BFM as a supplement, not a replacement for the preacher's own engagement with the passage.
2. **The gospel is in the sermon only as a label and a creedal reading.** On a Communion Sunday — when the congregation is about to proclaim the Lord's death — the gospel content should be the sermon's deepest well. "Gospel" and the 1 Corinthians 11 text appended at the end are not a proclamation.
3. **The Fallen Condition Focus is unnamed.** The passage diagnoses the disciples' failure to trust in the present Christ — "do you still have no faith?" (v.40) is the text's own diagnostic. This should be named as the FCF and applied directly: the congregation's failure to trust the same present Christ in their own storms.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see that the BFM reading, while doctrinally excellent, can function as a pastoral shortcut. Reading a confessional statement is not the same as preaching the Christ that statement describes. The congregation may leave knowing the orthodox formulas without having been pressed by the living Christ whom the formulas point to.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The doctrinal precision of the BFM statement and the two-natures framework give the sermon an appearance of theological depth. But doctrine read is not the same as doctrine preached. The sermon has a high theological ceiling but the preacher does not fully occupy it.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Mark 4:40 — "Why are you so afraid? Have you still no faith?" — is the passage's most searching moment. The disciples had Jesus in the boat. He was present with them. And they still despaired. This is the FCF: the failure of faith in a present but sleeping Savior. The sermon never presses this: Are you in the storm right now, with Jesus in the boat, and still convinced you are going to drown? That would have been a powerful, textually faithful, and pastorally searching application that the sermon approaches but never lands.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach the gospel from the BFM, don't just read it.** Take "substitutionary death on the cross" and "raised from the dead with a glorified body" and spend 4–5 minutes explaining and proclaiming each. Especially on a Communion Sunday — the Lord's Table demands it.
2. **Name the Fallen Condition Focus.** After the exposition, identify the FCF explicitly: "The disciples had Jesus in the boat and still thought they were going to drown. How often do we do the same — pray, attend, even preach, while living as if Christ is asleep and not enough?"
3. **Preach v.40 ("Have you still no faith?") as the sermon's conscience-pressing center.** This is the text's own diagnostic. It should be the moment the sermon looks the congregation in the eye.
4. **Integrate or separate the BFM reading.** Either weave the BFM affirmations into the expository flow as supporting doctrinal beats, or have a separate BFM teaching moment at a different time. As currently structured, it interrupts the expository momentum.
5. **Develop the "desire for change" diagnostic more fully.** Add: conviction (what does lack of desire for change reveal about your heart?), direction (what change should a person who truly knows Christ expect to see?), consolation (for those who see the change but fear it's not enough — the righteousness is Christ's, not yours).

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Mark 4:35–41 is Gospel narrative of the miracle genre. The rubric applies well, with one note: the passage's Christological claim (Jesus calms the storm, therefore who is he?) is primarily doxological and revelatory rather than didactic. The rubric's application categories press toward concrete behavioral change, but this passage's primary demand is worship — awe before Christ. The evaluation honors that; the rubric bends slightly toward the passage's doxological register here.
