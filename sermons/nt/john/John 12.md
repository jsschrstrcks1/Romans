## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**"Sincere Pastoral Heart, Absent Gospel Architecture — A Devotional Sketch That Does Not Yet Qualify as a Sermon"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A well-intentioned opening of John 12 that drew a real contrast between Mary and Judas, but never exposed the text’s own argument, never clearly proclaimed the gospel, and substituted statistics and cultural commentary for biblical application.

---

### 3. Berean Gate: FAIL (Conditional)

**Scopus Test:** The sermon’s thesis appears to be: "Give everything to Jesus like Mary did, not just superficially like Judas." This thesis is morally derived from the narrative but does not arise from the text’s own argument. John 12 is not primarily a call to imitative generosity — it is an anointing that points to Jesus’ burial (v. 7), a foreshadowing of the cross, and a declaration that Mary understood what the disciples did not. The text’s main point concerns who Jesus is and where He is going. The sermon’s main point is about who you are and where your commitment level is. These are different sermons.

**Authority Test:** The text’s hardest declarations — that Jesus deflects honor back to His death ("She has kept it for the day of my burial"), that the poor will always be with them (a statement about the uniqueness of this moment, not a social policy), that this anointing is explicitly linked to His sacrifice — are all quietly omitted. The sermon reduces a Christological passage to an exhortation about generous devotion.

**Ruling:** The sermon does not fully pass the Berean Gate. The main point of the sermon is not the main point of the passage. The passage points toward Christ’s atoning death; the sermon points toward the listener’s devotional life. This is a soft failure — the text is present, read, and referenced — but its actual argument is not preached.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG 1 — No clear gospel presentation.** The sermon repeatedly asks whether listeners love Jesus and warns that Judas-like people will go to hell, but never explains the mechanism of salvation. How does one move from being "like Judas" to being "like Mary"? The sermon does not answer this. Regeneration is not mentioned. Justification is not explained. The cross is invoked in the communion liturgy at the end, but not in the body of the sermon as a proclamation.

**FLAG 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary in the sermon’s argument.** The sermon’s core contrast (Mary vs. Judas = wholehearted vs. halfhearted) could function as a motivational talk in a secular context. The unique, unrepeatable significance of this anointing — that it anticipates the death of the Son of God — is not the engine of the sermon. Jesus is the object of devotion but not the ground of it.

**FLAG 4 — Application is entirely generic.** "Are you like Mary or Judas?" is a binary that does not press the conscience with specificity. There is no differentiated address to the backslider, the doubter, the self-righteous churchgoer, the grieving. The application stays at the surface level of commitment intensity.

**Score cap: 69 maximum.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 11/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (5/10):* The text is read, and the narrative characters are correctly identified. However, the central exegetical point — that the anointing is oriented toward Christ’s burial (v. 7), not primarily toward Mary’s spiritual state — receives no treatment. The key verse is skipped. Lazarus is mentioned in the outline as a subordinate detail. The section on Judas stealing from the money box is accurate but underdeveloped.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5):* No treatment of the book of John’s Christological argument. No placement within the Passion Narrative. No connection to John’s theological purpose (20:31).
- *Exegetical Clarity (2/5):* No Greek engagement. No explanation of the nature of the nard (a burial ointment), the significance of the foot-washing posture, or why this moment is unrepeatable.
- *Logical Faithfulness (2/5):* The Judas material wanders from the text into cultural commentary (Hugh Hefner, statistics) for several paragraphs. The Fallen Condition Focus is implicit (we are more like Judas than we realize) but never named or anchored in the text itself.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 9/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (3/7):* Christ is mentioned frequently but as the object of devotion rather than as the one whose death this anointing anticipates. The sermon is affectionately Christ-adjacent rather than Christ-centered.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (1/3):* Zero treatment. The anointing’s connection to the Passion is the textual engine; it is not engaged.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4):* No serious doctrinal errors are present. The mention of Hell is appropriately sober. But justification, atonement, and new birth are entirely absent. Charles Wesley’s own conversion story (he "realized he was lost and accepted Christ") is used illustratively but the mechanism of that conversion is not explained.
- *Confessional Alignment (2/4):* No contradictions of confessional theology. But there is no positive engagement either. The sermon is generically evangelical.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 5/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (1/5):* The communion liturgy at the close contains the clearest gospel content in the sermon — but that is liturgy, not proclamation. Within the sermon’s argument, the gospel is background noise.
- *Necessity of Christ (2/5):* Christ is invoked, but His death is not explained as the basis for anything. Why did He have to die? What does His death accomplish? These questions are not answered.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5):* The sermon moves toward Mary’s devotion, not toward the cross. The Synagogue Test is partially triggered: a moral person from another tradition could sit through most of this sermon without offense. The cross scandal is absent from the sermon’s argument.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 6/15**

- *Heart Penetration (2/5):* The binary question ("Are you like Mary or Judas?") has emotional weight but does not go deep enough to corner the conscience. The Hugh Hefner illustration probes cultural sin but does not press the hearer into self-examination before God.
- *Specificity (2/5):* The statistics section is the most specific moment, but specificity about cultural lostness is not the same as specificity about the listener’s own spiritual condition. "Are you here for the social benefits?" is a start, but it names the sin shallowly.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon essentially addresses two categories — the committed (like Mary) and the nominal/false (like Judas). Perkins’ other categories — the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, the knowledgeable-but-unmoved, the backslider — receive no address.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Call to Response (2/4):* There is a response moment — raise your hand, come talk to me. This is genuine and pastoral. But "raise your hand if you don’t love God as much as you should" is not a gospel call. What should they do after the hand goes up? The mechanism of response is undefined.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The statistics section implies urgency for witness, and "How can we stand by and watch?" is a genuine exhortation. But it is not grounded in the text, not connected to the gospel, and not developed into a concrete equipping moment.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (2/4):* The outline is visible: (1) Mary gave everything; (2) We are statistically like Judas; (3) Which one are you? This is a clear three-part structure but point 2 wanders significantly (statistics, Hugh Hefner, Wesley) and loses coherence.
- *Progression (2/3):* The sermon does move from narrative exposition to self-examination to response. The progression is logical but interrupted.
- *Unity (1/2):* The Hugh Hefner section and the statistics section feel like two different sermons grafted in. The unity suffers.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**

- *Relevance (2/2):* Charles Wesley’s conversion story is genuinely useful. The Hugh Hefner illustration connects to the congregation’s cultural moment.
- *Clarity (1/2):* The Wesley illustration clarifies the Judas/Mary contrast well. The Hugh Hefner section, however, dilutes the sermon’s focus.
- *Memorability (0/1):* No single image is crafted to carry home. The scent filling the house (from the text itself) is the strongest image and it is mentioned only in passing.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (1/2):* Conversational and warm. The direct address is genuine. But the manuscript is a series of notes rather than a composed sermon — it reads as preparation for talking, not as a written-to-be-preached document.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The conviction present is pastoral and personal. It is not textually grounded. The sermon asserts concern more than it grounds assertion in Scripture.
- *Cadence and Breath (1/1):* The natural oral register is present. The preacher’s voice is authentic.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily myself. The Mary/Judas binary directs attention inward to commitment level, not upward to Christ’s person and work.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about devotion and statistics about religious practice. The sermon does not create an encounter.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** The statistics section is alarming but not convicting in the biblical sense. There is no gospel comfort. The sermon lands in mild concern rather than either true conviction or true comfort.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Pastoral authenticity and genuine care.** The repeated invitation — "slip your hand up and I’ll spend a few minutes talking with you" — communicates real pastoral warmth. The preacher clearly loves his people and wants none to perish.
2. **The Judas comparison has genuine force.** The observation that we are "statistically most like Judas" and the specific characterization of Judas’s possible self-deceptions (the money, the social elevation, the political miscalculation) is the most exegetically honest and interesting moment in the sermon.
3. **Charles Wesley’s conversion narrative is well-chosen.** The illustration that a man who did so much for Christ discovered he was lost captures the sermon’s burden memorably and illustrates the Judas problem with real biographical force.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text’s actual main point is never preached.** John 12:7 — "Leave her alone, so that she may keep it for the day of my burial" — is the hermeneutical key to the entire passage. Jesus reframes the anointing as a burial preparation. The sermon does not engage this verse at all. The anointing is treated as a picture of general devotion rather than as a Christological sign pointing to the cross. This is a first-order failure. (Location: The entire sermon — absence is the problem.)
2. **The gospel is never proclaimed within the sermon’s argument.** How does a person move from Judas-like religion to Mary-like love? The sermon’s implicit answer appears to be: recognize your condition and make a commitment. This is not the gospel. The new birth, regeneration, and justification by grace through faith are entirely absent. The mechanism of the transformation the sermon calls for is never explained. (Location: Points 2 and 3 — the entire applicatory section.)
3. **The Hugh Hefner section fractures the sermon’s coherence.** Three paragraphs on Hugh Hefner’s exploitation of women, while morally serious, are not tethered to John 12 in any textual or logical way. The section functions as a culture-war illustration imported into a passage that has nothing to say about pornography. It distracts from the text, inflates the sermon’s length, and does not serve the main point. (Location: Point 2, the extended cultural commentary section.)

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see that his pastoral instinct — which is genuine and good — is operating without theological architecture beneath it. The sermon feels loving because the preacher is loving, not because the structure is built to deliver love through truth. The warmth is real; the scaffold is absent. Additionally, the preacher may not see that calling people to "be like Mary" without explaining how the cross makes that possible is moralism dressed in devotional clothing. Good desire without gospel foundation produces guilt, not transformation.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The statistics section *feels* powerful because the numbers are striking and the urgency is real. But statistics about American religious decline are not exposition of John 12. The section creates the impression of prophetic cultural engagement while actually substituting sociological data for biblical preaching. The congregation may have been moved by the numbers while receiving no bread from the text.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The text itself offers one of the most emotionally charged images in the Gospel of John — a woman pouring out a year’s wages on the feet of a man she knows is about to die, wiping them with her hair, filling the house with the scent of burial perfume while Judas calculates the waste. This is a scene of extraordinary dramatic and theological power. The text sets up a stunning contrast: Mary anoints Jesus for burial while Judas plots His betrayal, and both are in the same room. This dramatic irony — the woman who understood and the disciple who didn’t — is the heartbeat of the passage. It is barely touched. The scent that "filled the house" is mentioned in the outline but never developed as an image of the gospel’s inescapable reach or the extravagance of grace.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild the sermon’s thesis from v. 7.** The sermon’s controlling idea should be: "Mary understood what the disciples didn’t — that Jesus was about to die for sinners, and she responded with everything she had." This grounds the devotion in the cross, not in personality type.
2. **Preach the gospel explicitly before the application.** After identifying the Judas-condition (nominal, self-serving religion), explain what God does to change it: regeneration, the new birth, justification by faith in Christ’s finished work. The transformation from Judas to Mary is not a decision — it is a divine work. Name it.
3. **Cut the Hugh Hefner section entirely or reposition it.** If cultural lostness must be addressed, it should be brief and connected to the text’s own pathos. Three paragraphs on Hefner break the sermon’s spine.
4. **Develop the burial anointing as the sermon’s climax.** "She has kept it for the day of my burial" (v. 7) should be the sermon’s turning point — the moment where the preacher shows that Mary’s extravagant gift is inseparable from Christ’s extravagant sacrifice. Devotion flows from the cross.
5. **Add differentiated address.** Address at minimum: (a) the person who has been in church for years but has never believed; (b) the new or young believer who wonders if their love for Christ is enough; (c) the discouraged saint whose affections have gone cold. Each needs a different word from this text.
6. **The response moment needs gospel content.** "Slip your hand up" is fine as a mechanism, but the listener needs to know *what* they are responding to. State the gospel before the invitation: Christ died in your place, rose again, and offers forgiveness and new life to all who trust Him — not to those who try harder.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered — the score pattern does not reflect high doctrinal precision alongside cold application. The problem here is the inverse: pastoral warmth without doctrinal structure.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

There are moments of genuine pastoral tenderness — especially the repeated invitation and the Charles Wesley illustration — but the sermon does not carry the weight of eternity because it never grounds its urgency in the actual gospel of Jesus Christ’s death and resurrection.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This is a Gospel narrative passage — not an epistle. The rubric’s emphasis on law/gospel dynamics and propositional structure must be applied with narrative sensitivity. A narrative sermon can legitimately move through character and scene. However, the rubric’s core demands — that the text’s main point govern the sermon, that Christ’s finished work be proclaimed, that the conscience be searched — apply fully here. John’s Gospel is explicitly theological narrative (20:31), and this passage is Christologically loaded. The rubric is not strained by this genre; the sermon simply does not engage the passage’s own theological content.
