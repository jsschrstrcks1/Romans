## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Warm Pastoral Heart in Search of a Sermon: Genuine Illustrations, Underdeveloped Gospel, and a Missing Exhortation

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm, farm-grounded opening of John 10 that communicated the security of the sheep and the faithfulness of the Shepherd while leaving the gospel unformed, the conscience mostly unexamined, and the congregation without a clear call to response.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with reservation**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's main point — Christ is the Good Shepherd who knows, secures, and guards His sheep — is the main point of John 10. Pass.

*Authority Test*: The preacher submits to the text without visible distortion. There is no softening of hard doctrines and no obvious eisegesis. However, the sermon handles the text more as a theme source than an argument to follow. Verses 1–6 and 7–21 are summarized rather than opened. The third movement (vv. 22–30) receives the most careful treatment but still doesn't fully expose the text's own logic. Pass, but barely — the sermon skims the surface of a very deep passage.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Two flags are present. Score capped at 69.**

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: The application section identifies who Christ's sheep are in three bullet-point phrases but never presses on the conscience with specificity. "Follow Christ" and "hear his voice" are stated without ever naming what that looks like in concrete terms for this congregation.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon informs the congregation of the security of the believer and ends with a brief evangelistic invite. There is no moment where the preacher looks the believers in the eye and says, "Therefore, do this." The congregation is comforted but never summoned. The close addresses the unbeliever adequately in passing, but the main body of the sermon — addressed to believers — never produces an imperative edge.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1: Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**

- *Textual Fidelity* (10): 6/10. The sermon tracks the three-part structure of the chapter faithfully (illustration, explanation, application), but individual verses are mostly paraphrased or bypassed. The critical content of vv. 11–15 (the Good Shepherd laying down his life) is mentioned but underdeveloped. The distinction between "door" (v. 7, 9) and "shepherd" (v. 11, 14) — two separate metaphors — is blurred. Verses 19–21 receive no treatment.
- *Contextual Awareness* (5): 3/5. The context of the Feast of Dedication (v. 22) is mentioned, and the communal sheep pen background is well-drawn and helpful. But the placement within John's Gospel — the healing of the man born blind in John 9 as the immediate trigger for this discourse — is absent, which matters for understanding why Jesus uses this imagery.
- *Exegetical Clarity* (5): 2/5. No engagement with the Greek. No opening of key terms (e.g., "I am the door," the two uses of "I am," the verb "ginosko" in v. 14). The text is handled as accessible narrative rather than as a word to be mined.
- *Logical Faithfulness* (5): 3/5. No clear eisegesis, but the handling of the "other sheep" in v. 16 is abbreviated in a way that risks obscuring the Gentile mission and the unity of the church.

*FCF Assessment*: The Fallen Condition Focus is present but undeveloped. The sermon gestures toward the problem of false teachers and thieves, and the need for genuine saving faith ("fake Christians"). But the existential problem this text was written to address — the sheep who are confused about where to find life, who to follow, and whether their salvation is secure — is never named as the human condition being met by Christ's claims.

**Category 2: Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness* (7): 5/7. Christ is clearly the center — as Good Shepherd, as the Door, as the one who holds the sheep. The predestination reference (Eph. 1:4–5) and the doctrine of perseverance are real theological freight. But Christ's atoning work is mentioned only in passing ("even death on the cross") without explanation or weight. The Person of Christ is present; the Work of Christ is nearly absent.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* (3): 1/3. No placement of this text in redemptive history. The connection to Psalm 23, Ezekiel 34, or the Davidic shepherd motif would have grounded the text organically. The "I AM" language goes unmarked.
- *Doctrinal Precision* (4): 3/4. The doctrine of perseverance of the saints is presented clearly and accurately (vv. 27–30). The election reference from Ephesians 1 is sound. The distinction between genuine and false believers is present. No doctrinal errors are detectable.
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse* (4): 3/4. No contradictions of confessional theology. The treatment of election, preservation, and the necessity of Christ is consistent with the 1689 Baptist Confession. But the covenant framework underlying this "I AM" claim goes unexplored.

**Category 3: Gospel Centrality & Force — 7/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel* (5): 2/5. The gospel is gestured at but never proclaimed. The phrase "death on the cross" appears once, embedded in a comparison about why the owner cares more than the hired hand. Justification is absent. The mechanism of salvation — what it means for Christ to lay down His life for the sheep — is never explained. An unbeliever could sit through this sermon and understand that Jesus is protective and that following Him matters, but not understand why the cross was necessary or what it accomplishes.
- *Necessity of Christ* (5): 3/5. Christ is necessary to the sermon's structure — you cannot remove Him. But the necessity is framed mainly in terms of His power and knowledge, not His atoning mediation. "Nothing else is strong enough to overpower Christ" addresses sovereignty but not substitution.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity* (5): 2/5. The sermon does not move toward the cross. The climax is the security of the believer (vv. 27–30), which is the right destination for this text — but the path through the cross is bypassed. "The Good Shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" (v. 11) is the theological hinge of the chapter, and this sermon does not stop there.

*Synagogue Test*: A devout non-Christian could sit through this sermon without being offended by the necessity of Christ's blood. The sermon presents Christ as powerful protector and knowledgeable shepherd — both are true — but does not make the cross a scandal. **Flag: Moralistic drift toward comfort without substitution.**

**Category 4: Conscience & Applicatory Force — 6/15**

The most under-served hearer in this sermon is the person who attends church, knows the categories, but has never genuinely come through the door. That person is in nearly every congregation and this text directly addresses them — but the sermon does not press them with specificity.

*Perkins Assessment*: Two of seven categories addressed. The sermon does not distinguish or differentiate with pastoral precision.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
   Largely ideas. The warmth is present, but there is no moment of divine weight — no place where God seems near, fearsome, or uncontainably glorious. The sermon is informative and comfortable. It does not tremble.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
   Comforted (believers are secure) and informed (three categories of who Christ's sheep are). Not convicted. The conscience is not pressed. The text's own confrontational edge — the thief, the false shepherd, the religious leaders who do not believe (v. 26) — is softened into background.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Authentic, congregation-native illustration**: The farm illustrations are not borrowed material — they arise from the preacher's own life and directly serve the text. Hope learning the sound of the feed bucket lid as a picture of hearing the Shepherd's voice is the kind of illustration that sticks for years. This is a genuine gift that most preachers must work to develop; this preacher has it naturally.

2. **Doctrinally sound on perseverance**: The treatment of John 10:27–30 is the sermon's strongest moment. The double-handed security ("no one can snatch them out of My hand... no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand") is explained accurately, and the rhetorical challenge — "Are you stronger than God?" — is the sermon's sharpest moment of direct address.

3. **Readable three-part structure**: The division follows the text's own movement (illustration → explanation → application), is clearly signposted, and allows an ordinary listener to track the sermon without effort. Structure serves the congregation here rather than impressing them.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is absent in any saving form**: The phrase "death on the cross" appears once, inside a comparison about owner vs. hired hand, and is not unpacked. The text's own center — "I am the good shepherd; the good shepherd lays down His life for the sheep" (v. 11) — is the hinge on which the entire chapter turns, and this sermon does not stop there. An unbeliever could hear this sermon and understand that Jesus is protective and worth following without understanding *why He died*, *what His death accomplished*, or *how a sinner is actually saved*. This is a structural failure, not a minor omission.

2. **No conscience-pressing application**: The application section (vv. 22–30) identifies three markers of being Christ's sheep (hear His voice, know and are known by Christ, follow Christ) but never deploys these as diagnostic tools to press the congregation. The preacher knows this text has an edge — "fake Christians" are mentioned in the illustration section — but that edge is never brought back to bear in application. The man who thinks he is a sheep but is not is never cornered.

3. **No exhortation to the believer**: The close addresses the unbeliever (rightly) but leaves the believer with a fact (you are secure) and no imperative. The believers were never told what to *do*. Should they be examining themselves? Should they be sharing this security with others who are afraid? Should they be guarding against false shepherds in their lives? The text supports all three — and none are given.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how much theological weight vv. 11–15 carry. The Good Shepherd laying down His life is not a brief transitional point on the way to perseverance — it is the engine of everything. The security of vv. 27–30 is grounded in the atonement of vv. 11–15. Without stopping at the cross, the comfort of election floats free from its anchor. The sermon comforts without first establishing *why* the comfort is warranted.

Additionally, the preacher may not realize that the congregation most at risk — the person who attends faithfully, knows the vocabulary, and has never genuinely been born again — is the precise target of this passage. Jesus is not speaking to pagans here; He is speaking to religious people who are in the wrong fold. That pastoral edge is the text's gift to a preacher with a mixed congregation, and it goes unused.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The farm illustrations feel strong — and they are genuinely good. But they risk becoming a substitute for exegesis. The sermon's warm and personal opening creates an impression of pastoral depth that the body of the sermon does not fully sustain. A listener may leave feeling that the sermon was rich and grounded because the illustrations were vivid and personal, without noticing that the text's own argument was never carefully followed. The illustrations are real; the exegesis is thin. The warmth of the former can mask the weakness of the latter.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The text's most dramatic moment is v. 11: "I am the good shepherd; the good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep." This is one of the seven "I AM" statements in John's Gospel — each one an echo of Exodus 3:14, a claim to divine identity. The Good Shepherd is not just a caring leader; He is YHWH Himself taking up the Davidic role promised in Ezekiel 34 ("I myself will search for my sheep and look after them") and fulfilling it in the flesh. The sermon does not go near this. The substitutionary death implied in v. 11, the voluntariness of it in vv. 17–18 ("No one takes it from me, but I lay it down of my own accord"), and the Trinitarian authority behind it ("This command I received from my Father") are all left on the table. This is the theological center of gravity of the entire chapter, and the sermon treats it as a supporting detail.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Stop at v. 11 and open the atonement**: Dedicate a full section to "lays down his life for the sheep." What does it mean that the Shepherd died *for* the sheep? What does "for" mean — in place of? On behalf of? Explain penal substitution here. The security of vv. 27–30 is only glorious if the hearer understands *what it cost* the Shepherd to secure it.

2. **Deploy the three markers of vv. 27–28 as a diagnostic, not just a comfort**: "My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me." Reframe this not only as a comfort for the assured but as a searchlight for the congregation. "Do you hear His voice? Not just have you heard *about* Him — but does His Word arrest you, search you, move you? Do you *follow* — not perfectly, but genuinely, directionlessly toward Him? Let these be the lamps by which you examine your own soul tonight."

3. **Add a clear gospel statement to the explanation section**: Before moving to the application of perseverance, name what the Shepherd's death accomplished. "He did not die as a hero who inspired us to be better. He died as a substitute — taking the judgment that the sheep deserved, so that they could be brought safely into the fold. That is what 'laying down his life' means. And that is the only basis for the security that follows." One paragraph. It transforms the sermon.

4. **Add a believer-directed exhortation at the close**: After addressing the unbeliever, turn back to the saints: "For you who are His sheep — you are not called only to rest in this. You are called to witness to it. There are other sheep not yet in this fold (v. 16). Go and tell them where the Gate is. Your security is not a secret to hoard; it is a treasure to share. Go."

5. **Cut the Pilgrim's Progress reference or develop it**: The Wicket Gate allusion at the close is dropped in without explanation. Either omit it, or give it one sentence of context — enough that a new believer who has never read Bunyan can follow the picture.
