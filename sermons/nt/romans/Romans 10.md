**The Gospel That Reformed the World**
*Romans 10:1–13*
**Introduction — One Truth in Many Voices**
If you’ve brought your Bible—and I hope you have—please open with me to Romans chapter 10, beginning in verse 1.
It’s remarkable how the Lord unites His people around a single truth through many different voices. The women of our church, in their Bible study, are in Galatians 3—discovering how the law was a guardian until Christ came, and how we are sons and daughters of God through faith alone. The men, in their study of *A Journey in Grace*, are tracing a young man’s awakening to the doctrines of grace—the rediscovery of God’s sovereign mercy in salvation.
And here we are in Romans 10, where Paul pleads with tears that Israel—his own kinsmen according to the flesh—might be saved.
They had zeal, but no knowledge; passion, but no peace; religion, but no righteousness.
In other words, they had everything but Christ.
And as we read, we’ll hear echoes not only of Paul’s own heartache but of a German monk standing before a wooden door in Wittenberg over fifteen hundred years later.
The hammer that struck the church door in 1517 was the echo of Paul’s pen in Romans 10.
Let’s read together:
“Brothers, my heart’s desire and prayer to God for them is that they may be saved. For I bear them witness that they have a zeal for God, but not according to knowledge. For, being ignorant of the righteousness of God, and seeking to establish their own, they did not submit to God’s righteousness. For Christ is the end of the law for righteousness to everyone who believes.”
— *Romans 10:1–4 (ESV)*
Before he ever nailed his theses to the door, Luther tried to find peace through confession, penance, and prayer—until he discovered that the Word of God was not far off in relics or rituals, but near him, in the gospel.
That same Word is near us this morning.
**I. Sola Scriptura — The Word Near You**
*(Romans 10:5–8; cf. Galatians 3:8)*
Paul begins not with speculation but with Scripture. He quotes Moses to show that the gospel was never a new idea—it was always near.
“The word is near you, in your mouth and in your heart.”
The same Word that Abraham believed, the same promise that Moses proclaimed, is the Word that saves today.
The Reformers called this *Sola Scriptura*—*Scripture alone*—because they knew that the Word of God is sufficient for life and salvation.
Luther said, “Scripture is the cradle wherein Christ lies.”
That means every page, every promise, every prophecy points toward the same Savior Paul is preaching here.
When human traditions overshadow the Word, the gospel becomes distant—buried beneath rituals and systems and rules. But Paul says, “The Word is near you.”
Near enough to hear. Near enough to believe. Near enough to save.
The same temptation that led Israel to seek righteousness by their own effort now drives many in our own age to look beyond the Word that is already near. The human heart has not changed—it still prefers mystery over obedience, spectacle over Scripture.
That’s why so many chase spectacle—“prophets” and supposed Bible “codes”—looking past the Bible they already hold; it’s the same whisper from Eden: *“Did God really say?”*
We’ve seen what happens when people seek truth outside of God’s Word. In Jeremiah’s day, the people of Judah baked cakes and poured out drink offerings to what they called “the queen of heaven.” They thought they were honoring God with extra devotion—but they were, in fact, grieving Him with borrowed worship.
And centuries later, the church would make the same mistake again—lifting up Mary, not as the humble servant who magnified the Lord, but as a co-redeemer who must be magnified herself.
The same impulse that drove Judah to the queen of heaven drives hearts today to elevate tradition above truth, and creature above Creator.
But Scripture alone guards us from such error.
Beloved, when we stay close to the Word, we stay close to Christ.
He is not hidden in rites, relics, or rituals.
The Word is near you—near enough to hear, near enough to believe, near enough to save.
Friends, God’s Word does not need to be improved; it needs to be believed.
**II. Sola Gratia — The Grace That Reaches Don**
*(Romans 10:1–4 revisited; Galatians 3:24*
Paul’s heart in these opening verses is soaked in grace.
“Brothers, my heart’s desire and prayer to God for them is that they may be saved.”
You can almost hear the break in his voice. He loves his people.
He remembers what it was like to chase righteousness through rule-keeping. He had the pedigree, the training, the zeal—and yet all of it left him bankrupt before a holy God.
That’s why verse 4 lands with such thunderous hope:
“For Christ is the end of the law for righteousness to everyone who believes.”
Grace means that God has done for us what we could never do for ourselves.
The law was never a ladder to climb; it was a mirror to show our need.
Paul says in Galatians 3:24, “The law was our guardian until Christ came, in order that we might be justified by faith.”
The law guards but cannot give life. It convicts but cannot convert. It exposes sin but cannot erase it.
Israel’s tragedy—and ours whenever we trust our performance—is that they mistook the law’s diagnosis for its cure.
They were, as Paul says, “ignorant of the righteousness of God and seeking to establish their own.”
That same self-righteous impulse hides in all of us.
It’s what drives the moralist to boast, the religious man to compare, and the weary Christian to try harder instead of resting deeper.
But grace descends.
Grace doesn’t stand at the top of the staircase shouting, “Climb higher.”
Grace walks down the steps, takes our trembling hand, and carries us home.
Thomas Watson said, “Grace does not find men worthy; it makes them so.”
That’s the scandal of it—God giving to the undeserving what can never be earned.

And that’s what Paul discovered on the Damascus road. The very law he thought he was upholding condemned him, until grace stopped him in his tracks with a blinding light and a merciful voice: *“Saul, Saul, why are you persecuting Me?”*
In that moment, the persecutor met the pursued Savior. The hunter became the hunted, and mercy overtook him.
Grace doesn’t just wait politely at the door — it runs us down.
It overtakes the rebel, embraces the wanderer, and reforms the heart it captures.
That’s what it did for Paul, and that’s what it still does for us.
Grace doesn’t sit around wringing its hands, hoping you’ll someday turn to Jesus.
Grace is a cheetah, and we are a limping gazelle. The outcome is certain.
It doesn’t chase to toy with us; it chases to rescue us. And when it catches us, it doesn’t tear us apart — it puts us back together.
That’s the power of *Sola Gratia*: grace always reaches the one it pursues, and when it does, it leaves them changed forever.
**So what does grace do in people like us?**
Beloved, grace isn’t God looking the other way. It’s God looking directly—unflinchingly—at our sin and choosing to deal with it Himself.
It’s not leniency; it’s substitution. Grace doesn’t excuse sin—it overthrows it, like patriots tossing tea into Boston Harbor. That tea never stood a chance.
Maybe you’re here today feeling like you’ve fallen too far, that your zeal has burned out, that you’ve failed too often.
The good news of *Sola Gratia* is that your failures don’t repel God’s mercy—they attract it.
As the Puritans used to say, “Our sins are the wounds into which the balm of Christ is poured.”
And when grace takes aim, it doesn’t miss.
I think of that Olympic marksman—steady hand, clear eye, no panic, no wasted motion. While others trembled and overcorrected, he simply lifted the rifle, exhaled, and struck the target again and again.
That’s grace. It doesn’t scramble or strain; it simply hits what it’s after—your heart.
When Paul says, “Christ is the end of the law for righteousness,” he means the striving is over.
The endless measuring, comparing, performing—over.
Because grace has reached down… and when grace reaches down, it never misses its mark.

**III. Sola Fide — The Faith That Receives**
*(Romans 10:9–10; Galatians 3:11)*
When grace captures the heart, faith opens the hand.
“If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved.”
Do you hear the simplicity in that?
No rituals. No ceremonies. No endless ladder of merit.
Just belief — and even that belief is the gift of God.
No rites, no relics, no rituals. No secret codes. Just Jesus.
Faith is not a work.
It’s the end of working.
It’s not an accomplishment to boast in, but the surrender of boasting altogether.
It’s the humble confession that we can’t do it on our own. We’re too weak. We can’t make it.
We’re a lot like Florence Chadwick, who tried to swim from Catalina Island to the California mainland. She was the best of her day—strong, disciplined, determined. She swam for fifteen hours straight through cold, fog, and fatigue. But when she was only a mile from shore, the fog thickened. She couldn’t see the coastline anymore, and she gave up.
Later she said, “If I could have seen the shore, I would have made it.”
That’s the human story. We swim hard, but the fog of sin blinds us. We can’t see the shore. And no amount of self-confidence can part that fog.
We don’t need more faith in ourselves — we need faith from God.
A faith He supplies.
Faith is the open hand of a beggar reaching out to receive what grace has already purchased.
It’s the untying of the white flag that’s been clenched in our fist, finally raising it to the One who’s been gently chasing us all along.

That’s why Paul writes elsewhere, *“The righteous shall live by faith.”* (Galatians 3:11)
That verse lit the torch of the Reformation. Martin Luther called it “the gate to paradise.”=
Before he understood justification by faith, Luther feared the righteousness of God. He saw it only as judgment. But when he read Romans 1:17—*“**The just shall live by faith”*—his world turned upside down. He said, “I felt myself altogether born again and entered paradise itself through open gates.”
Luther’s concern was that he could never be good enough.
But faith says, “I don’t have to be enough, because God is.”
As Colossians 3 reminds us, “I am hidden with Christ in God.”
That’s the assurance of *Sola Fide*—my life is secure not because I hold on tightly to Christ, but because He holds tightly to me.
Faith doesn’t earn righteousness; it receives it.
It’s not the cause of salvation but the channel through which salvation flows.
Spurgeon said, “Faith is the silver channel through which the mercy of God flows.”
It’s not the well; it’s the pipe.
Not the light; it’s the window.
Beloved, we live in a world allergic to faith because faith admits dependence. Our culture loves autonomy, control, and self-confidence. We hear it every day: “Pull yourself up by your bootstraps.” But faith says, “I cannot save myself.”
And that confession is the beginning of true rest.
That’s why Jesus said, *“Come to Me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest.”* (Matthew 11:28)
Faith isn’t a new kind of labor; it’s the end of labor.
It’s dropping the tools of self-righteous self-salvation and resting in the finished work of Christ.
Friends, saving faith is not believing in spite of evidence; it’s believing because of evidence—because the tomb is empty, the cross is sufficient, and the Savior is alive.

As Paul says here, “If you confess with your mouth and believe in your heart that God raised Him from the dead, you will be saved.”
That’s a promise, not a probability.
Faith receives what grace provides, and both point to Christ alone.
**IV. Solus Christus — The Person Who Saves**
*(Romans 10:11–12; John 14:6; Acts 4:12; Ephesians 2:1–5; 1 Timothy 2:5)*
Every road of redemption leads to one destination—Christ.
Grace comes from Him.
Faith rests in Him.
Salvation stands or falls with Him.
“For the Scripture says, ‘Everyone who believes in Him will not be put to shame.’ For there is no distinction between Jew and Greek; for the same Lord is Lord of all, bestowing His riches on all who call on Him.”
Church, this is our hope on Monday morning as much as on Sunday.
That little phrase—“in Him”—is the center of the Christian universe.
The gospel is not *“be better”*; it’s *“be in Christ.”*
Religion says, “Try harder.” Christ says, “It is finished.” The world says, “Find your truth.”
Christ says, “I am the Truth.” Philosophers build systems. Prophets point ahead. Priests stand between.
I’m reminded of Ephesians 2—“You were dead in your trespasses and sins.”
What can a dead man do to be better, to try harder, to find his truth?
Nothing. Such a man doesn’t need advice; he needs resurrection. He needs Jesus.
Because only Jesus saves—because only Jesus became the sacrifice.
Only Jesus could drink down the cup of God’s wrath to its bitter dregs and then cry out in triumph, “It is finished.”
There are no ladders to God; there is one cross from God to man.
One Mediator between God and man—the man Christ Jesus (1 Timothy 2:5).
When Israel brought sacrifices, they were shadows pointing to a substance—the Lamb who would come.
Every drop of blood on the altar whispered, “Someone must die.”
At Calvary, the whisper became a roar.
*“He was pierced for our transgressions, crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was upon Him, and by His wounds we are healed.”* (Isaiah 53:5)
There is no other name under heaven by which we might be saved, because no one else was worthy or able to drink down the cup of almighty God’s eternal wrath and declare, *“It is finished.”*
No one else can take our sinful record and declare us not guilty while taking the judgment Himself.
No one else loved us so much as to die on the cross for our sins.
No one.
He did not come to make salvation possible—He came to make it certain for His people.
As Thomas Brooks wrote, “Christ did not die to make men salvable, but to make salvation sure.”
This is the scandal of the gospel: that the sinless One would take the sinner’s place, that the Judge would bear the judgment.
We call that substitution.
Luther called it the great exchange—our sin for His righteousness, our shame for His glory, our death for His life.
**The Sufficiency That Ends All Additions
**The Reformers fought for this truth: Christ plus anything ruins everything.
Christ plus Mary? Idolatry.
Christ plus sacraments? Bondage.
Christ plus works? Condemnation.
But Christ alone is enough.
His righteousness doesn’t need our polish; His cross doesn’t need our nails.
There’s no co-mediator, no purgatory, no celestial bureaucracy.
Just the risen Son of God, interceding for His people even now.
Hebrews 7 says, *“He is able to save to the uttermost those who draw near to God through Him, since He always lives to make intercession for them.”*
That means when you fail—and you will—Christ’s prayers are stronger than your sin.
**Application**
Beloved, in a world overflowing with saviors—political, religious, self-help, and digital—there is still only one Name by which we must be saved.
And spoiler alert—it’s not the name of any politician.
Our culture wants a customizable Christ—one who agrees with our desires and blesses our idols.
But the Christ of Scripture doesn’t conform to us; He transforms us.
He doesn’t bless every path; He is the path.
He doesn’t share His throne; He alone is Lord.
As Tim Keller once said, “If your god never disagrees with you, you might just be worshipping an idealized version of yourself.”
If salvation depended on us, we’d lose it before sunset—
like a toddler trying to keep white clothes clean while playing in the mud.
But praise God, our salvation depends not on our grip of Him, but His grip of us.
And that’s good news—because the exclusivity of Christ is the safety of the sinner.
If salvation were in our hands, we’d fumble it.
But since it’s in His, it’s as secure as His throne in heaven.
“Grace comes from Christ. Faith rests in Christ. Salvation belongs to Christ. And all of it exists for one purpose—to magnify the glory of God.
That brings us to the final cry of the Reformation: *Soli Deo Gloria* — To the Glory of God Alone.”

**Closing — The Gospel That Still Reforms Hearts
**“Grace alone. Faith alone. Christ alone. According to Scripture alone. To the glory of God alone.”
That’s not just a slogan of the past — it’s the heartbeat of heaven.
It’s the melody that began at creation, thundered at Calvary, and will echo forever around the throne of the Lamb.

Beloved, the gospel is not an idea to admire; it’s a Savior to adore.
It is the story of a holy God who would not leave rebels to die in their sin,
but stepped down from glory, wrapped Himself in flesh,
and lived the life we could never live.
He perfectly obeyed every word of the law we shattered.
He took our guilt, our shame, our wrath — and nailed it to the cross.
There, on that dark hill outside Jerusalem, the sin of man met the mercy of God.
The wrath we deserved was poured out on the Son who did not deserve it.
The justice of heaven was satisfied, and the gates of paradise swung open to the unworthy.
Three days later, the tomb was empty — because grace doesn’t stay buried.
Faith was vindicated, death was defeated, and hell was silenced.
And even now, the risen Christ reigns, calling sinners to Himself with the same invitation Paul preached:
“Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved.” *(Romans 10:13)*
That is the gospel that reformed the world.
And it’s still the gospel that saves today — the power of God unto salvation for everyone who believes.
So, yes — while the world at the end of October celebrates shadows,
we celebrate the Light that no darkness can overcome.
We celebrate not ghosts, but the Holy Ghost —
not costumes, but the covering of Christ’s righteousness —
not death, but the One who conquered death and offers life eternal.
Brothers and sisters, may our hearts never tire of this truth:
We bring nothing to the table but our need.
Christ brings everything — His blood, His righteousness, His Spirit, His glory.
And because of Him, the sinner can be declared righteous, the rebel can be made a son,
and the wanderer can come home forgiven, free, and forever loved.
So let us join the reformers, the apostles, the angels, and the redeemed of every nation in one eternal song:
*“To Him who loves us and has freed us from our sins by His blood,*
*and made us a kingdom, priests to His God and Father —*
*to Him be glory and dominion forever and ever. Amen.”* *(Revelation 1:5–6)*
That’s the gospel that reformed the world.
And it’s still the gospel that saves today.
That, friends, is a far better reason to celebrate the end of October
than candy, ghosts, and witches.
As we close, I want to do so with the lyrics of a song in view. 
It was an old African spiritual song, written while cruel whips tore their backs. They wrote:

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Reformation Doxology With a Thin Expository Floor**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A gospel-saturated celebration of the *sola*s that carries genuine evangelical heat while treating Romans 10:1–13 more as a launching pad for Reformation themes than as the controlling authority of the sermon's argument.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS (marginal)**
The sermon's thesis — that salvation is by grace alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone, according to Scripture alone — is defensible from Romans 10, but the passage's own *dominant argument* (the tragedy of Israel's self-righteousness vs. the righteousness of faith, culminating in the universal call of 10:13) is subordinated to a Reformation framework imported from outside the text. The five *sola*s organize the sermon; the passage illustrates them. That is a near-miss on the scopus test.

**Authority Test: PASS**
The preacher submits to the text's claims. No softening of uncomfortable declarations, no editorializing away from hard truths. The text is honored, even if it is not always the driver.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

No fatal flaws triggered. Gospel is clearly presented. Christ is necessary throughout. Application, while mostly generic, has specific moments. Exhortation is present. The sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers.

**Score cap not applied.**

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity (7/10)*: The sermon draws real meaning from the passage — Christ as the end of the law (v.4), confession and belief (vv.9–10), the universality of the call (vv.11–13). But significant portions of the sermon (Sola Scriptura section, Mary critique, queen of heaven excursus, Olympic marksman, Florence Chadwick) are extended distance from the text. Verses 5–8 (the Moses quotation, the "word near you") receive only passing treatment despite being structurally central to Paul's argument. Verses 14–17, which complete Paul's logic, are entirely absent.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5)*: The Romans 9–11 argument about Israel's unbelief is acknowledged in the introduction but then largely abandoned as the sermon pivots to Reformation history. The covenantal logic of Paul's Moses quotation (Deut. 30) is not unpacked.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5)*: Where the preacher touches the text, the meaning is explained clearly and faithfully. "Christ is the *telos* of the law" is handled well. The confession/belief dynamic in vv.9–10 is solid.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5)*: No eisegesis in the strict sense, but the sermon's organizational spine (five *sola*s) imposes a sixteenth-century theological grid on a first-century apostolic argument. The text's own logic — that Israel's problem was seeking to establish their *own* righteousness, not submitting to God's — is the engine Paul drives, but the sermon runs on a different engine.
- *FCF (Fallen Condition Focus)*: Partially identified — the self-righteous impulse that trusts human merit — but not named with surgical precision. The FCF floats rather than cuts.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 15 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (6/7)*: Christ is genuinely central and organically present. The great exchange (Luther), penal substitution, and the interceding Christ are all present and clear. Slightly formulaic in places.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3)*: The sermon gestures toward the OT/NT continuity (Abraham, Moses, Israel's sacrifices pointing to Christ) but does not develop the covenantal architecture with precision.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4/4)*: Justification by faith is clearly articulated. Grace as substitution (not leniency) is explicit and well-stated. No theological slippage detected.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4)*: Consistent with 1689 theology. The doctrines of grace are present by implication (grace as the pursuing cheetah, faith as gift). Explicit confessional precision on the Covenant of Grace is not developed, but neither is it contradicted.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 12 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (5/5)*: The gospel is plainly proclaimed — Christ's substitutionary death, the resurrection, justification by faith. An unbeliever could understand how to be saved from this sermon.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5)*: Christ is essential, not decorative. The sermon cannot survive without Jesus. The *Solus Christus* section is the strongest in the sermon.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5)*: The sermon moves toward the cross but somewhat circuitously. The Reformation historical framework sometimes acts as a detour. The closing doxology lands powerfully, but the cross-centered gravity builds unevenly.
- *Synagogue Test*: FAILS. A devout Jewish synagogue member would be offended by the necessity of Christ's blood — the scandal is present. Gospel is not merely decorative. Test passed in the right direction.
- *Law/Gospel Dialectic*: The law as mirror (hammer) is present (Section II). The gospel as balm follows clearly. Order is correct.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration (3/5)*: The sermon makes genuine appeals to the heart — the weary Christian who has burned out, the moralist who compares, the failure who feels too far gone. These are real moments. But they are interspersed rather than sustained, and the piercing often gives way to encouragement before the wound fully opens.
- *Specificity (3/5)*: Some specificity present (the moralist, the weary Christian, the person chasing spectacle). But most applications are general exhortations that could belong in any evangelical sermon on any text. The congregation's specific situation is addressed only at the broadest level.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (3/5)*: The sermon addresses believers (direct address throughout), the self-righteous (indirectly), and the weary/failing. The hardened unbeliever, the backslider, and the humbled-but-not-yet-believing are not distinctly addressed. Perkins' taxonomy is touched but not systematically employed.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5 / 8**

- *Call to Response (3/4)*: The closing contains a genuine gospel call. Romans 10:13 is the climax and functions as an invitation. The "everyone who calls" language is explicit. However, the sermon does not produce a clear, differentiated call to response for distinct hearers — the exhortation is primarily doxological rather than directive.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4)*: The sermon comforts the congregation where they sit more than it sends them out. There is no specific "therefore, go and..." moment. The corporate calling of the church — to proclaim this gospel to others (which Romans 10:14–17 demands) — is entirely absent because those verses were not included.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 7 / 9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4)*: The four *sola*s plus a closing provide a clear, memorable framework. A listener can sketch it. The headings are traceable.
- *Progression (3/3)*: The sermon builds — from the Word near you, to grace reaching down, to faith receiving, to Christ alone, to glory. The trajectory is sound.
- *Unity (1/2)*: The dominant idea is almost singular — the *sola*s as the gospel framework — but the Sola Scriptura section drifts (queen of heaven, Marian devotion, prophets and Bible codes) in ways that feel like a separate agenda rather than a development of the main point. The sermon has two competing centers: Reformation theology and Romans 10.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4 / 5**

- *Relevance (2/2)*: The illustrations are drawn from accessible worlds — athletics (Olympic marksman, Florence Chadwick), American idiom (tea in Boston Harbor, toddler in white clothes), historical narrative (Luther, Paul on Damascus road).
- *Clarity (1/2)*: Most illustrations clarify. The cheetah/gazelle metaphor for irresistible grace is vivid and theologically accurate but slightly overwrought. The Olympic marksman illustration is somewhat generic and could be transplanted to almost any sermon.
- *Memorability (1/1)*: The "grace doesn't stay buried" line and the Florence Chadwick fog illustration will likely be remembered. The closing African spiritual is a powerful, well-earned image.

**8. Sermonic Force — 4 / 5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2)*: This manuscript reads as oral proclamation. Sentence variation, direct address, breath-length rhythm — all present.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2)*: The conviction is real but somewhat formulaic. Certain phrases feel rehearsed rather than discovered ("Christ plus anything ruins everything," "Sola Scriptura" sections). The manuscript is strongest when the preacher stays close to Paul's actual argument and weakest when it becomes a Reformation survey course.
- *Cadence and Breath (1/1)*: The closing section has genuine cadence — the parallel construction in the final doxology builds and releases well.

---

### 6. Cognitive Load Modifier — −1

The sermon is well-organized and broadly accessible. The five *sola*s serve as helpful signposts. Deduction of one point for the density of historical references (Luther, Reformation history, Jeremiah, Mary, Paul's Damascus road) layered over the text, which slightly overloads the ordinary hearer who needs to track both the passage *and* the historical framework simultaneously.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Penal substitution is explicit and well-stated** (Section IV, *Solus Christus*): "The sinless One would take the sinner's place, that the Judge would bear the judgment... our sin for His righteousness, our shame for His glory, our death for His life." This is clear, precise, and gospel-declarative.
2. **The grace-as-cheetah metaphor and the Damascus road illustration work together** (Section II): The irresistible nature of saving grace is communicated with both doctrinal accuracy and imaginative vividness. The sequence from Paul's self-righteousness to his overtaking by grace illustrates *Sola Gratia* with scriptural and experiential grounding.
3. **The closing doxology earns its place** (Closing): The parallel construction ("He took our guilt, our shame, our wrath — and nailed it to the cross"), the pivot from the "white clothes / mud" illustration to the security of Christ's grip, and the African spiritual close represent the sermon's best writing. The doxological ending is appropriately exuberant for a Reformation Sunday text.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Romans 10:5–8 (the Moses quotation and "the word near you") is the heart of Paul's argument and receives the sermon's thinnest treatment.** The Sola Scriptura section borrows the "word near you" phrase but immediately pivots to anti-Catholic polemic and a Jeremiah excursus. Paul's actual point — that the righteousness of faith *does not require ascent or descent*, that the word is already near — is never exegetically developed. This is the passage's most distinctive and theologically rich section, and it is treated as a slogan.
2. **The conscience is informed but not cornered.** The "self-righteous impulse hides in all of us" (Section II) is the moment to press — but the sermon names it, gestures at it, and then moves to grace before the hearer has felt the exposure. The Puritan pattern (conviction, direction, consolation) is compressed to the point that conviction is decorative. A hardened moralist in the congregation could sit through this sermon nod approvingly and feel largely comfortable.
3. **Romans 10:14–17 is entirely absent.** Paul's argument in these verses — that faith requires hearing, hearing requires preaching, preaching requires sending — is the *missional climax* of the passage. Its omission means the sermon never reaches the exhortative imperative the text itself provides. The congregation hears the gospel but is never summoned to proclaim it. This is the sermon's most significant structural omission.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

- **The preacher likely does not see that the Reformation framework is driving the sermon rather than the text.** The five *sola*s are a faithful theological summary, but they are not Romans 10's own argument. The text argues from Israel's failure to the universality of the gospel call; the sermon argues from the *sola*s to each other. These produce similar destinations but different journeys — and the preacher's journey bypasses much of Paul's.
- **The polemical sections (queen of heaven, Marian co-redemptrix, Bible codes) likely feel like application to the preacher but register as distractions to the text.** They are not unimportant topics, but they are being answered by a passage that is not primarily addressing them.
- **The "already comforted" congregation is not challenged.** The sermon assumes hearers who need reassurance and warmth. It does not reckon with hearers who are self-satisfied, morally confident, or spiritually asleep — the very profile Paul is diagnosing in Israel in vv.1–3.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

**The Reformation historical framework creates the *feeling* of expository depth without delivering it.** Luther, Wittenberg, the five *sola*s, the great exchange — these are legitimate, emotionally resonant, and theologically accurate. But they can create the impression that the sermon has engaged the passage's argument when it has largely used the passage as a proof-text for a pre-existing theological framework. The preacher may feel the exposition was strong because the theology was sound. But sound theology is not the same as expository faithfulness to *this* text's *own* argument.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**The Moses quotation in vv.5–8 (from Deuteronomy 30) is one of the most theologically daring moves Paul makes in Romans.** He takes a passage about Israel's covenant obligations and re-reads it as a description of the gospel's accessibility — the word is not in heaven (you don't need to ascend to Christ) or in the abyss (you don't need to bring Christ up). This is Paul doing *christological hermeneutics on the Torah*, and it is breathtaking. The sermon uses the phrase "the word is near you" as a slogan for Sola Scriptura — a legitimate application — but the *exegetical wonder* of what Paul is doing with Deuteronomy 30 is entirely missed. That passage deserved at minimum two minutes of unhurried explanation.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild Section I around the Moses quotation (vv.5–8).** Spend 3–4 minutes unpacking what Paul is doing with Deuteronomy 30. Show the congregation that Paul is not quoting Moses as background color — he is *re-reading the Torah through the lens of Christ's resurrection*. This is the exegetical wonder the congregation deserves to see.
2. **Add Romans 10:14–17 as a fifth section (or extend the closing).** Paul's own conclusion is missional: you cannot call on one you have not believed; you cannot believe one you have not heard; you cannot hear without a preacher. The sermon's doxological close is beautiful, but it should be preceded by a summons: *"Therefore, someone must go. Someone must speak. That someone is you."*
3. **After naming the self-righteous impulse in Section II, let the law land before grace arrives.** Give the moralist, the performance-driven church member, and the "weary Christian trying harder" thirty unrelieved seconds of exposure before grace relieves the pressure. Currently the conviction is too brief to do its work.
4. **Differentiate the closing exhortation by hearer type.** Address at least three distinct categories: the unbeliever who has intellectual assent but not heart surrender; the believer who is resting in grace genuinely; and the backslider who once knew this gospel and has drifted. Each hears Romans 10:13 differently and deserves a distinct word.
5. **The Sola Scriptura section's polemical material (queen of heaven, Mary as co-redeemer) should either be integrated as explicit application of the *text's* argument about seeking one's own righteousness, or removed.** As it stands, it reads as a topical insert. If kept, frame it as: "Israel's self-righteousness took the form of religious zeal without submission to God's righteousness. We do the same whenever we supplement Christ with human additions." Then the polemic becomes exposition.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** Exposition scores 17 (below the 20 threshold for trigger). Gospel Centrality is 12 (above 9). The sermon has pastoral warmth and genuine evangelical heat. The fire is present, if not always well-aimed.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The closing doxology — culminating in the African spiritual — carries genuine eternal gravity, and the sermon's consistent Christ-exaltation earns a partial bonus; **+3 of 5** awarded, withheld fully because the sermon's best moments of glory are compressed into the close rather than sustained throughout.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Romans 10:1–13 is a doctrinal-epistolary passage, which this rubric is well-suited to evaluate. The passage is didactic, argumentative, and gospel-declarative — all categories the rubric measures directly. No genre strain. The one note: this sermon is structured thematically (five *sola*s) rather than sequentially (verse-by-verse), which is a legitimate homiletical choice for this passage. The rubric does not penalize thematic structure per se — but it does penalize when the theme overrides the text's own argument, which has occurred here.
