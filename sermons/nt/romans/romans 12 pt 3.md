**FULL SERMON MANUSCRIPT — WEEK 3**
**Romans 12:9–13 — Love Without Hypocrisy**
*(45–55 minutes, single-spaced)*

**INTRODUCTION — FROM SACRIFICE TO SHARED LIFE**
If you’ve brought your Bible—and I hope you have—please open with me to Romans 12. We are continuing to walk through what many have called the most practical chapter in all of Scripture. But do not mistake “practical” for shallow. Romans 12 is only practical because Romans 1–11 is true.
As a reminder, this is our third week in romans 12 - In Week 1, Paul called us to the altar: present your bodies as living sacrifices. Abraham, Isaac and mt Moiah. A ram caught in the thicket.
In Week 2, he placed those surrendered lives into the body: many members, one body, gifts given by grace for service. All working together for the glory of God!
Now in verses 9–13, Paul shows us what life together actually looks like when the gospel is real.
This is not theory. This is not idealism. This is not sentimental Christianity. his is the **culture of a redeemed people**. Let’s read the text.
“Let love be genuine. Abhor what is evil; hold fast to what is good. Love one another with brotherly affection. Outdo one another in showing honor. Do not be slothful in zeal, be fervent in spirit, serve the Lord. Rejoice in hope, be patient in tribulation, be constant in prayer. Contribute to the needs of the saints and seek to show hospitality.”
**POINT 1 — LOVE WITHOUT A MASK (v. 9a)**
“Let love be genuine.”
The word Paul uses means **without hypocrisy**, without a mask, without pretending. Paul does not say, “Let love feel warm,” or “Let love be polite,” or “Let love avoid conflict.” He says, let it be real.
Which immediately tells us something uncomfortable: Real, true, gospel, covenant love does not come naturally to us. If genuine love were instinctive, Paul would not need to command it. That’s why sometimes you have to make yourself get up and go do the loving thing for someone. That’s why sometimes you lack grace with your spouse. That’s why we fight. Because if we are left to ourselves, our love is conditional. It is selective. It is self-protective. It loves when it is reciprocated and it withdraws when it is costly.

But let me draw an important line of distinction here. Paul couches this command in a lot of other language across the passage. In verse 9 he also commands us to *“abhor what is evil; hold fast to what is good.”* In verses 17–19 he tells us, *“Repay no one evil for evil… never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God.”* And in verse 18 he caps it off with, *“If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all.”*

That little phrase *“if possible, so far as it depends on you”* means there will be times, and seasons, and situations where peace does **not** fully depend on you. Where the ball is in someone else’s court. Where hardness of heart, or ongoing sin, or even abuse is happening. In those cases, real love is not the same as passive enabling. Genuine love does not pretend evil is good, and it does not quietly remain in harm’s way while calling that “faithfulness.”

If you are being abused, real love—biblical, Romans 12 love—does not mean you simply stay and keep taking the blows. It may mean you seek safety. “And it may mean involving pastors, trusted family, and when necessary, the proper civil authorities—because God is not honored by covering evil.”
It always means you refuse revenge, you refuse to return evil for evil, but it does **not** mean you offer your body and soul as a doormat for someone else’s sin. There are situations where the most loving, God-honoring thing you can do is to step out of the line of fire, entrust justice to the Lord, and pray that His kindness would lead that person to repentance.

So when Paul says, *“Let love be genuine,”* he is not calling you to be a victim; he is calling you to a Christ-like love that hates evil, clings to what is good, refuses revenge, and—*as far as it depends on you*—pursues peace without sacrificing truth.
That’s why the gospel matters so deeply here. Paul assumes that something decisive has already happened in these believers. They are no longer who they were in Romans 1. God has acted. God has changed them. God has given them new hearts capable of real love. Christian love is not personality-driven. It is not temperament-based. It is not rooted in natural compatibility. It is **supernatural fruit**. And fake love never lasts. It collapses under pressure. Genuine love survives disappointment because it is rooted in grace, not performance.
**POINT 2 — LOVE WITH A MORAL BACKBONE (v. 9b)**
“Abhor what is evil; hold fast to what is good.”
Do you see how that connects to what we just said? If genuine love doesn’t mean quietly remaining in abuse, it’s because Paul refuses to separate love from holiness. When he says, *“Let love be genuine,”* the very next breath is, *“Abhor what is evil; hold fast to what is good.”* In other words, real love doesn’t just feel warmly toward people; it takes sides—God’s side—against whatever is evil, destructive, and dehumanizing in their life or in yours.
Biblical love is not morally neutral. It does not bless what destroys people. It does not affirm what corrodes souls. It hates evil—not because it is harsh, but because it is loving.
Love that never confronts sin is not love. Truth that never bleeds compassion is not Christ.
Paul binds them together. Love hates what God hates and clings to what God calls good. This guards us from a sentimental Christianity that redefines love to mean approval, tolerance, or silence.
And it also guards us from a twisted version of “love” that says, “If you really loved me, you would let me keep sinning against you. You would stay, no matter what I do.” No—*that* is not Romans 12 love. A renewed mind—Romans 12:2—produces moral clarity. And moral clarity is not the enemy of love; it is the backbone of it.
**POINT 3 — FAMILY AFFECTION, NOT RELIGIOUS DISTANCE (v. 10a)**
“Love one another with brotherly affection.”
Do you see the balance Paul is striking? In verse 9 he tells us to hate what is evil and cling to what is good—that’s moral backbone. In verse 10 he leans in and says, *“Love one another with brotherly affection.”* That’s family language. Philadelphia. This is not cold suspicion with a moral checklist in our hands. This is covenant affection with our arms open, our hearts engaged, our eyes wet.
The church is not a volunteer association; it is a family created by grace.
You did not choose this family. God did. You did not earn your place. Christ did.
That changes how we relate to one another. Family love is patient. Family love forgives. Family love stays when it would be easier to leave.
But don’t miss this: **family affection doesn’t cancel moral clarity; it sits on top of it.** In a healthy family, love refuses to pretend evil isn’t evil. Love refuses to baptize abuse as “faithfulness.” Love refuses to call enabling “grace.” At the same time, love also refuses to write people off forever, to sentence them in our hearts, or to treat brothers and sisters as enemies.

We heard last week from our guest preacher that love is patient and kind; it does not envy or boast; it is not proud; it does not dishonor others; it is not self-seeking or easily angered; it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres. Love never fails.

If love *rejoices with the truth*, it is not going to settle for a lie. It will not call darkness “light” just to keep the peace. It will not rewrite history so no one has to repent. Real love would rather face hard truth with tears and repentance than live comfortably in denial.

And yet that same love, when truth is faced and sin is confessed, does not cling to old records of wrongs as a weapon. It protects. It hopes. It perseveres. It says, “I will not pretend nothing happened—but I also will not close the door on what God can do if there is real repentance.”
Some of you may have grown up in families where “love” meant “keep the secret and protect the image.” Paul is telling us: **biblical love refuses that bargain.**
If God adopted us in Christ, then we must learn to treat one another as brothers and sisters—not competitors, not strangers, not irritations, but family. That may mean leaning in with costly patience. It may, in some situations, mean creating space and boundaries while we pray and wait for repentance. But even then, the posture God is after is not cold indifference; it is wounded, watchful, hopeful family love—eyes fixed on Christ, ready to move toward peace *“if possible, so far as it depends on you.”*

None of that says, “Be a doormat,” or, “Stay and accept abuse.” Neither does it say, “Run and hide from those you have wronged, or those who have wronged you.” Gospel love refuses both extremes. It will not quietly endure ongoing evil and call that virtue, and it will not harden its heart and slam the door forever. It stands in the tension—hating what is evil, holding fast to what is good, and staying ready, by grace, to move toward real reconciliation when God opens that door.
After all, as Christians we are called to be Christlike—that’s what the name means: little Christs, followers of His example. This is the same Christ who, in righteous anger, overturned the money-changers’ tables in the temple, and yet meekly went to the cross to receive the holy wrath of God in the place of sinners like you and me. So Christlike love knows how to stand up against evil and also how to lay itself down in sacrificial grace.
POINT 4 — COMPETITION REDEEMED (v. 10b)

“Outdo one another in showing honor.”

Paul does something remarkable here: he redeems competition. The world competes for recognition, platform, attention, and praise. Paul tells the church to compete—but for honor given away.

Pride seeks visibility.
Love seeks to elevate others.

In other words, in a gospel-shaped family, I am not secretly asking, “Why didn’t I get that role? Why didn’t I get that attention?” Instead, I am asking, “How can I publicly thank, elevate, and encourage the brothers and sisters God has put around me?”

This cuts directly against comparison, envy, and insecurity. In the church, honor is not something we grasp—it is something we give. Gifts are not for platform; they are for building others up.

And notice how this ties back to everything we’ve said: genuine love with a moral backbone doesn’t just refuse to enable evil; it also refuses to poison the family with quiet jealousy. It refuses to punish people with coldness, distance, or silence when they are honored instead of us. A mind renewed by grace learns to rejoice when God uses someone else, to speak well of them, to step aside when needed—not as a doormat, but as a brother or sister who trusts that the Father has not forgotten them.

A church shaped by grace becomes a place where people are celebrated, not sidelined—where we don’t compete for the microphone, but we gladly compete to show honor.

POINT 5 — LOVE THAT ENDURES (vv. 11–12)
Paul now shows us how this love survives over time.
“Do not be slothful in zeal, be fervent in spirit, serve the Lord.” “Rejoice in hope.” “Be patient in tribulation.” “Be constant in prayer.” This is not love for a moment. This is love for the long haul. This is love that survives disappointment, friction, suffering, and unmet expectations. And notice where Paul points us:
•	“Serve the Lord” – ultimately, our love for people is anchored in our love for Christ. If we look only at them—their failures, their sins, their coldness—we will run out of fuel. But when we remember, “I am serving the Lord here,” zeal is rekindled.
•	“Rejoice in hope” – hope looks past the present mess. Hope says, “God is not finished with them. God is not finished with me. God is not finished with this story.”
•	“Be patient in tribulation” – that includes the tribulation of strained relationships, of family conflict, of being misunderstood or mistreated. Patience does not mean pretending it doesn’t hurt; it means refusing to give sin the last word.
•	“Be constant in prayer” – because there will be people you cannot fix, situations you cannot untangle, hearts you cannot soften—including your own. So you carry them, again and again, into the presence of the One who can.

You cannot love like this in your own strength. This kind of love is sustained by hope, refined through hardship, and fueled by prayer. God does not merely command endurance—He supplies it.

And remember what we have already said: enduring love is not the same as endless enabling. “Be patient in tribulation” does not mean, “Remain indefinitely in abuse and call that faithfulness.” It means that even when you must step back, even when you must set boundaries, even when the ball is in someone else’s court, your heart does not give up praying, does not give up hoping, does not give up believing that the grace which saved you is able to rescue them.

Those whom God saves, He sustains. And that sustaining grace shows itself in continued love, even when it is costly—not a love that denies evil or erases truth, but a love that keeps its eyes on Christ, keeps its knees bent in prayer, and keeps its hope anchored in the God who raises the dead.

All of this only makes sense if what Paul says in Colossians 3 is true of you: *“For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God”* (Col. 3:3).

If Romans 12 is Paul saying, “Live out the new life.”
Then Colossians 3 is Paul saying, “Here’s why you *can* live that way:
### If my real life is hidden with Christ in God—safe, secure, untouchable—then I don’t have to claw for recognition, I don’t have to stay in destruction to prove I’m loving, and I don’t have to run forever from hard relationships in fear. I can hate what is evil and cling to what is good; I can set boundaries where I must; I can keep loving, keep hoping, keep praying, because my identity is not finally in how other people treat me. It is in Christ. And when Christ who is my life appears, Paul says, “then you also will appear with him in glory” (Col. 3:4). That future glory, that hidden security, is what frees us to live this Romans 12 kind of love in the middle of very imperfect families, very imperfect churches, and our very imperfect communities. 

We can only love with this kind of love, if we are truly connected to God. A wise pastor I know once preached that we are a bit like a flashlight. On its own, without batteries, how well does that flashlight work in the dark? We need to be connected to the power source, or we ephesians 2 – dead – in our trespasses and sins. 

This enables ordinary faithfulness. 

POINT 6 — LOVE MADE VISIBLE (v. 13)
“Contribute to the needs of the saints and seek to show hospitality.”
Paul lands love on the ground. Love opens hands. Love opens homes. Love is not abstract. It is visible. Love means visiting someone in the hospital. Even when we are tired. Even if its hard. Love costs something.
This is ordinary faithfulness: noticing needs, sharing resources, welcoming others into your life. It is not flashy, but it is powerful. This is how the early church turned the world upside down—not through influence, but through love. 

And yes, love matters here. Not as a substitute for the gospel, but as an adornment to it. In hard places—whether across the street or across the world—visible, sacrificial love often opens doors and earns a hearing. **And when that love adorns the clear proclamation of Christ crucified and risen, attended by the Spirit of God,** counterfeit hopes begin to lose their shine.

Let me pause here—not to accuse, but to help us examine ourselves honestly.
Paul is not describing perfection. He is describing direction.
So don’t ask yourself, *“Do I do this flawlessly?”*
Ask instead:
Is my love becoming more genuine over time?
Am I learning to hate what harms others and cling to what gives life?
Do I see other believers as family, even when they are difficult?
Am I quicker to honor than to critique?
Does my love endure when it is inconvenient?
Is my faith visible in generosity and hospitality?
If you see growth—even slow, imperfect growth (like mine) then —that is evidence of grace at work.
And if you see resistance—hardness, isolation, indifference—don’t despair. Bring it to the Lord. Grace does not expose us to shame us; it exposes us to heal us.
A bruised reed He will not break.
A smoldering wick He will not snuff out.
But if there is **no desire** for this love, no movement toward it, no hunger for it—then Paul would have us ask whether the gospel has truly taken root.

**LOVE THAT MAKES CHRIST VISIBLE**

Romans 12:9–13 is not a checklist. It is a portrait. It is what the church looks like when the gospel is real—when people whose lives are hidden with Christ in God begin to live like that is actually true.

This love does not make much of us.
It makes much of Christ.

It hates what is evil and holds fast to what is good.
It refuses abuse and refuses revenge.
It loves with family affection, not religious distance.
It competes—not for platform or praise—but to outdo one another in showing honor.
It endures in hope, through tribulation, on its knees in prayer.
It opens hands, opens homes, and turns invisible grace into visible mercy.

And next week, Paul will take this love outside the church—to enemies, persecutors, and those who wound us. But it begins here, among the family of God, as men and women who know that their lives are hidden with Christ in God learn, by the Spirit, to love as they have been loved.

**Let love be genuine.**
And all that starts with a relationship with Jesus, by being plugged into the power source. 

Gospel.

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Warmth With a Truncated Gospel and an Underdeveloped Conscience**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A genuinely pastoral opening of Romans 12:9–13 that demonstrated real exegetical care and community-shaping application while consistently stopping short of a full gospel proclamation, pressing the conscience with insufficient specificity, and ending with a closing that names "Gospel" without actually preaching it.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS**

- **Scopus Test**: Pass. The sermon's controlling thesis — that genuine, gospel-shaped love is morally courageous, family-warm, and enduring — arises directly from Paul's argument in vv. 9–13. The main point of the sermon is the main point of the text.
- **Authority Test**: Pass, with a minor caution. The preacher handles the text honestly and does not soften its demands. The extended domestic-abuse pastoral application (while pastorally sensitive) runs the risk of allowing a contextual concern to dominate the passage's primary movement. This is a structural judgment call, not a failure of authority.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**One flag triggered — score capped at 69.**

- **Flag 4 — Application is largely generic**: The self-examination questions at the end of Point 6 ("Is my love becoming more genuine over time?", "Am I quicker to honor than to critique?") read as diagnostic prompts without differentiated address. They are appropriate but fail to corner the self-righteous, distinguish the sufferer from the hardened, or specifically address the categories of hearers present. The conscience is nudged, not pressed.
- **Flag 5 — Exhortation is present but weak**: The sermon does attempt a call toward the end ("bring it to the Lord," "Paul would have us ask whether the gospel has truly taken root"), and this is enough to keep the flag from fully triggering. However, the final "Gospel." is a label, not a proclamation. The sermon ends before actually preaching the gospel it references throughout.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 8/15**

The self-righteous, the religiously comfortable, and the church member who is harboring pride while performing service are not specifically addressed.

- *Spiritual Differentiation* — 2/5: Perkins' taxonomy is largely collapsed into two categories: the believer (being encouraged) and the sufferer (being protected). The hardened, the knowledgeable-but-unmoved, the backslider, and the humbled-but-not-yet-believing are not reached. The sermon lacks a moment where a particular sinner is cornered.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5/8**

The invitation to "bring it to the Lord" and the warning about whether the gospel has truly taken root are present but underweight. **6. Structure & Logical Flow — 7/9**

The Christ-at-the-temple/Christ-at-the-cross contrast is the sermon's strongest illustrative moment. The "bruised reed / smoldering wick" quotation is well-placed and emotionally effective. ### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
   Partially yes. The sermon points to Christ as the hidden life, as the Christlike model, as the power source. But because the gospel is never fully proclaimed, the hearer is left more with a vision of what their love should look like than with a fresh encounter with the One who loved them first. The mirror is cleaner than the window.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
   Ideas, mostly. The sermon is warm and pastoral. But the moments where God's character becomes weighty — His holiness, His specific wrath against sin, His particular love for the suffering — are gestured at rather than pressed. The "bruised reed" quotation is the closest the sermon comes to landing with divine weight.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
   The suffering hearer is comforted. The curious hearer is informed. The complacent, the self-righteous, and the church member going through the motions are not meaningfully convicted.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Pastoral courage on domestic abuse**: The willingness to address abuse directly — naming that "genuine love does not mean you simply stay and keep taking the blows" — is both textually grounded (using the "if possible, so far as it depends on you" qualifier) and pastorally necessary. This is not an addition to the text; it is faithful application of the text's own qualifiers. Few preachers do this as carefully.

2. **Indicative/Imperative structure maintained throughout**: The sermon consistently returns to the foundation — "Paul assumes that something decisive has already happened in these believers" — and roots each imperative in prior grace. This prevents the sermon from sliding into moralism and reflects genuine theological instinct.

3. **Family-over-association ecclesiology**: The "church is not a volunteer association, it is a family created by grace" theme is developed naturally across Points 3–4 and given real teeth. The distinction between cold suspicion with a moral checklist and covenant affection with arms open is well-drawn and memorable.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is named but never preached**: The sermon's final word is "Gospel." — a caption for what was never shown. Throughout the sermon, grace is referenced, the cross is mentioned once, and Christ's work is gestured at. But there is no point at which a genuinely lost person hears what they must believe, why Christ's death is necessary, how they can be right with God, or what the good news actually is. A sermon on Christian ethics must still contain the gospel that makes Christian ethics possible — and here it is missing as proclamation.

2. **Conscience application is insufficiently differentiated**: The self-examination prompts at the close (Point 6) address only one type of hearer: the sincere believer who may be growing slowly. The person who is not growing, the person using "grace" as a cover for ongoing indifference, the prideful elder, the person whose "genuine love" is really performance, the person sitting in church wondering if they've ever been truly converted — none of these are specifically addressed. Perkins' taxonomy is barely touched.

3. **Point 5 (vv. 11–12) is rushed**: Four distinct imperatives — "fervent in spirit, serve the Lord / rejoice in hope / patient in tribulation / constant in prayer" — are treated in a single point with bullet-point brevity. Each of these contains enough weight for a full point. The cumulative effect is a section that names truths without opening them. The congregation is told what Paul says but not walked through what Paul means.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears to be preaching primarily to the suffering — those in difficult relationships, those navigating abuse, those tempted to isolate. This is admirable pastoral instinct. But it creates a blind spot for the comfortable, the self-satisfied, and the morally confident. The sermon does not ask the prosperous, relationally stable, and doctrinally confident churchgoer to examine whether their love is genuine in the sense Paul means — whether they actually hate what God hates and cling to what God calls good in their own lives, not just in others'.

Additionally, the FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is never named with theological precision. The sermon identifies symptoms ("our love is conditional, self-protective") but does not name the root: the heart's fundamental orientation toward self rather than God as the worship problem that gospel grace addresses. Without naming the disease precisely, the medicine feels like good advice rather than necessary grace.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The domestic abuse pastoral application reads as the sermon's most specific and courageous section — and it is genuinely good. But its length and prominence create the impression that the sermon has done deep conscience work when in fact it has addressed one category of hearer (the sufferer) at length while leaving the other six largely untouched. The specificity in this section masks the generality everywhere else. A reader who finishes the sermon may feel that hard things were said — and some were — but the hard things said were largely limited to one pastoral situation.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The command "Outdo one another in showing honor" (v. 10b, Point 4) is the passage's most countercultural and corporate demand — and the sermon treats it in the fewest words. This imperative strikes directly at the comparison, platform-seeking, and recognition-hunger that characterize not just the world but evangelical church culture. The preacher notes the redeemed competition correctly but does not press it into the congregation's specific experience: the musician who feels overlooked, the elder who resents a newer voice, the volunteer who wonders why no one noticed. This was the sermon's sharpest edge and it was left mostly unswung.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach the gospel in the conclusion — actually preach it.** The word "Gospel." at the end is a marker, not a proclamation. Add 4–6 sentences that explain what the gospel is: that the love described in Romans 12:9–13 is only possible because Christ lived the life we couldn't live, died the death we deserved, rose in vindication, and now gives His Spirit to produce this love in us. Make justification by faith explicit. Give the lost hearer something to believe, not just something to admire.

2. **Differentiate the conscience application across at least three hearer categories.** After the self-examination prompts, add a paragraph that directly addresses: (a) the person who is performing Romans 12 love for approval — your love is not genuine, it is strategic; (b) the person who has hardened toward a brother or sister while calling it "discernment" — you may be holding a grudge in God's name; (c) the unconverted hearer — you cannot produce this love from moral effort, only from a new heart that Christ alone can give.

3. **Give Point 5 (vv. 11–12) its own weight.** Either expand this point significantly or choose one of the four imperatives ("constant in prayer") to develop fully. The current treatment skims the surface of four rich commands. One deep well is more useful than four shallow ones.

4. **Tighten the domestic abuse theme to one point.** The application is valuable but returns in Points 1, 2, 3, and 5. Once developed fully (in Point 1 or Point 2), subsequent references can be brief callbacks rather than re-explanations. This will sharpen the sermon's overall unity and allow the other points to carry their own weight.

5. **Name the Fallen Condition Focus with precision.** Before entering the exposition, identify specifically what aspect of fallen humanity this text addresses: we are naturally incapable of genuine, other-centered love because we are oriented by nature toward self-worship and self-protection. Name this clearly and let the gospel answer it directly.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Romans 12:9–13 is an ethical/paraenetic passage — a list of imperatives built on prior indicatives. The rubric applies well here, with the standard caveat that ethical passages require extra care to ensure the gospel-indicative foundation is explicitly proclaimed, not merely assumed. The sermon's primary structural challenge is that it applies the imperatives carefully but underpreaches the indicatives that make them possible. The rubric's Gospel Centrality and Conscience categories are the appropriate pressure points for this genre. No genre strain detected.
