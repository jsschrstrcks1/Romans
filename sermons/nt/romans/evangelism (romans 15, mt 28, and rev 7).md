## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Verdict Title:** Genuine Pastoral Heart, Shallow Exposition — A Missions Appeal That Nearly Misses the Gospel

**One-Sentence Summary:** A warmly motivated sermon on evangelism that draws real urgency from the Great Commission and Revelation 7 but substitutes personal anecdotes and missions statistics for sustained textual exposition, and ends before it delivers the gospel it promises.

---

### Berean Gate

**Scopus Test:** The sermon's thesis — "God has commanded us to take the gospel to all nations, and we must obey" — is broadly defensible from Matthew 28 and Romans 15. It passes, barely. The thesis is present but floats above the text rather than arising from close reading of any of the three passages.

**Authority Test:** Pass with caveat. The preacher does not soften or edit the text's demands. He is willing to apply the command directly and name disobedience. However, Romans 15 and Revelation 7 receive almost no exegetical treatment — they are cited, not opened. The Berean Gate passes on the strength of the Matthew 28 text and the sermon's honest confrontation of the church's missions failure.

**Result: PASS (narrow)**

---

### Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation:** TRIGGERED. The sermon builds to a gospel presentation at the end and then stops. The final line reads: "The gospel. Communion." This is not a gospel presentation — it is a placeholder. An unbeliever sitting in the room who responded to the closing exhortation ("Perhaps you're here today and all of this is new for you, and you don't know what the gospel is. Its easy, I'll tell you!") received nothing. The sermon ends before delivering what it promised. This is the single most serious flaw in the manuscript.

**Flag 4 — Application is generic:** PARTIAL. The closing exhortation ("Will you respond like Jonah or like Paul?") is binary and broadly applicable but lacks differentiated specificity. The congregation is not addressed as distinct types of hearers.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation with content:** TRIGGERED in part. The "go" is issued — "Are you going to honor God, or will you now knowingly live in disobedience?" — but what "go" looks like concretely is never named. The church is summoned but not equipped. There is no "therefore, here is what you do Monday morning."

### Strengths (Top 3)

3. **The *panta ethne* word study.** Clear, accessible, exegetically grounded, and directly connected to the sermon's argument. This is how a word study should function — as a lock that opens a door, not a display of scholarship.

### Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is promised and not delivered.** The final paragraph explicitly sets up a gospel presentation: "Perhaps you're here today and all of this is new for you, and you don't know what the gospel is. Its easy, I'll tell you!" The sermon then ends. "The gospel. Communion." are not a gospel presentation. An unbeliever in that room received nothing. This is not a minor editorial gap — it is a structural failure that invalidates the sermon's evangelistic purpose. A sermon calling the church to evangelize cannot itself fail to evangelize.

2. **Romans 15 and Revelation 7 are cited but not opened.** The preacher references these texts but the congregation receives no understanding of what they actually say or why they matter beyond their surface content. Romans 15:18-21 contains Paul's remarkable priestly theology of the Gentile mission — that Paul sees himself as a priest offering the nations as a sacrifice to God. This is a theologically explosive image that goes entirely untouched. Revelation 7:9-17 is a vision of the eschatological completion of the missionary task — the gathered church of every nation before the throne. The preacher uses it correctly but without opening it.

3. **The sermon mistakes inspiration for equipping.** The congregation is told to care about missions and challenged to not be like Jonah. But they are never told *what to do*. The gap between "you should evangelize" and "here is how to share the gospel in your actual life" goes unbridged. This is not merely an applicatory weakness — it is a failure of the sermon's own stated purpose. If the sermon aims to mobilize people for evangelism, it must equip them, not only challenge them.

---

### Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that the sermon's structure actually undermines its own evangelistic appeal. By ending before delivering the gospel, the sermon models precisely the failure it is preaching against — beginning the witness and not completing it. This is not intentional irony; it is a blind spot. The preacher likely ended here because the communion service followed and the gospel would be implicit in the table. But the manuscript as a standalone document does not know this, and an unbeliever reading or hearing it has been offered a promise that was never kept.

There is also a blind spot regarding the sovereignty of God and missions. Revelation 7 — every tribe, tongue, people, and nation before the throne — is an eschatological certainty grounded in God's sovereign election, not merely a missionary goal. The preacher uses it as a target to work toward rather than a promise that grounds the missionary's confidence. The result is a missions appeal that feels more like burden than privilege.

---

### Illusion of Strength

The repeated refrain — "Jesus, the one we love and serve, in whom lies our only hope for salvation, the one who loved us enough to die on the cross for us, he commissioned us" — feels like a solid christological anchor. It is used three times. But this repetition creates the *feeling* of gospel substance without providing it. The phrase is true, but it is a caption, not a proclamation. Justification is not explained. Atonement is not opened. The nature of sin and judgment is assumed. The refrain becomes a rhetorical device that substitutes for the gospel rather than delivering it.

---

### Missed Opportunity

Romans 15:16 — Paul's description of himself as "a minister of Christ Jesus to the Gentiles, serving as a priest of God's gospel, so that the Gentiles might become an offering acceptable to God, sanctified by the Holy Spirit" — is the most theologically rich verse in the passage and receives no treatment. This is the text's own climax. Paul does not merely obey the Great Commission — he understands his missionary work as priestly liturgy. The nations are the sacrifice. The gospel is the act of consecration. This image would have given the sermon a theological center of gravity it currently lacks, and it would have organically connected evangelism to worship — a connection the preacher wants to make but only reaches through assertion.

---

### Surgical Fixes

1. **Deliver the gospel.** Before the communion section, insert a clear three-to-five minute gospel presentation: the problem of sin, the holiness of God, the substitutionary death of Christ, the resurrection, and the call to repent and believe. The sermon sets this up explicitly — do not leave it empty. This single fix addresses the fatal flaw.

2. **Open Romans 15:16 — the priestly image.** Replace one of the three repetitions of the refrain with an exposition of Paul's self-description as a priest offering the nations to God. This connects evangelism to worship at a textual level rather than a rhetorical one, and gives the sermon an exegetical center it currently lacks.

3. **Anchor the Revelation 7 vision in sovereign promise, not human task.** Reframe the *panta ethne* word study: these nations *will* be there — God has promised it — and therefore the missionary labors with confidence, not desperation. This resolves the implicit tension between the enormity of the task (the missions map) and the church's finite resources.

4. **Add one concrete application for the stay-at-home.** The Jonah/Paul binary is bold but abstract. Add a third option: "Maybe you're not called overseas. Maybe you're called to your street. Here is what that looks like..." The Anthony Cox illustration (buying lunch and sharing the gospel) is already in the sermon — use it as the concrete model for the ordinary congregant.

5. **Address the backslider and the missions-cynical explicitly.** Some in the congregation have tried to share the gospel and been hurt, or have given to missions and seen the funds wasted, or are exhausted by the burden of the unreached. The sermon does not reach these people. A short paragraph addressing the discouraged witness ("Some of you have tried this and felt like you failed...") would open the sermon's pastoral range considerably.

6. **Cut the Revelation 7 chapter reference error.** The manuscript states "Thirdly Romans 7:9-end" — the text being discussed is Revelation 7:9-end, not Romans 7. This should be corrected in the manuscript.
