### Opening Illustration — The Lieutenant and the Sergeant

*(⚠️ VERIFY BEFORE USE: Source of this story is unconfirmed — circulated in preaching contexts but origin not traced to a named account or publication. Verify against Gulf War literature or remove attribution before pulpit use.)*

I heard a story about a man serving in the first Gulf War. A small group was sent behind enemy lines to scout locations. They were hit. Fierce firefight. When the smoke cleared, every man was dead except two — both wounded. One seriously. The stronger of the two, a lieutenant, picked up the other man — a sergeant — and carried him.

He hauled him as far as he could into the desert. Away from the enemy. Away from safety. He finally found a patch of grass and a few small shrubs, dug a foxhole. Found a trickle of water at the bottom — but not enough for both of them.

So he searched.

About twelve miles away he found an oasis. And every day, for weeks, the lieutenant would wait until dark — and run twelve miles through the desert to the oasis and carry water back to his wounded sergeant. He found materials, made a splint, kept him alive. Until finally, on one of his treks for water, he stumbled on a group of friendly soldiers, got their location to the allies, and got his injured friend home.

I want to talk this morning about carrying water for the weak.

---

### Scripture Reading

*Turn with me to Romans 15, starting at verse 1.*

> **Romans 15:1-3 (ESV)**
>
> We who are strong have an obligation to bear with the failings of the weak, and not to please ourselves. Let each of us please his neighbor for his good, to build him up. For Christ did not please himself, but as it is written, "The reproaches of those who reproached you fell on me."

---

### The Command — Bear With the Weak

This is a pillar of Paul's argument in these final chapters. Watch out for the weaker brother.

Why does it matter? You don't know if the person you've invited over struggles with alcohol. Why risk it? Why pour a drink when it might make your neighbor stumble? For your neighbor's good. Last week we were in chapter 14, verse 20: *"Do not, for the sake of food, destroy the work of God."* The command this week carries the same weight in the other direction — not just avoid destroying, but actively build up.

Proverbs 27:17 puts it this way: *"Iron sharpens iron, and one man sharpens another."*

Sometimes the strong have to carry water for the weak. In doing so, you become like that lieutenant. He hauled water for days, went hungry for days, helped his friend for weeks — until he found help. God may use you to help mature a weaker believer. We are commanded to be involved in discipleship. To live a life before God that is worth reproducing in others.

For your benefit, for another person's benefit, and for the glory of God — we are to carry water for the weaker brother. We are to be patient in their stumbling. Longsuffering when, like a little puppy, they get into the garbage again. This strengthens the strong, supports the weak, and glorifies God.

---

### Paul's Example

This was Paul's own practice.

God had commissioned Paul as the apostle to the Gentiles — the one sent specifically to carry the gospel beyond Jerusalem. When Paul began his ministry, the church in Jerusalem was largely an insular community. The light of the gospel burned brightly there — if you could have looked from the sky, Jerusalem would have been a spotlight. The rest of the world dark.

Paul's intentional ministry — and the unintentional scattering of believers through persecution — spread the gospel to most of the known world. The scattering that followed Stephen's death, which Paul himself had approved, turned out to be the mechanism God used to push the light outward. What Paul had meant for harm, God meant for good.

It is very likely that if you could trace your spiritual family tree — who led you to the Lord, who led them to the Lord, and so on up the line — somewhere back in that chain is Paul. He carried water for the Gentiles. When we were spiritually lost and wounded, he patched us up and hauled water to us in the desert.

---

### Christ's Example

But Paul was only following someone else's lead.

He came for the Jew first, then the Gentile. We Gentiles were adopted in. Colossians 3:3 says: *"For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God."*

We have all sinned. All missed the mark. All fallen short. We are like that sergeant with the broken legs — hopeless, broken, easier to leave for dead. But Christ carried water for us.

He endured a trial that was a farce from the beginning. They had to pay witnesses, and still had trouble finding two whose stories matched. He was scourged within an inch of his life. Beaten until — and there is no gentle way to say this — the flesh was laid bare. He was mocked, spat on, and nailed to a cross.

At any moment he could have stopped it. Matthew 26:53 — he said it himself: *"Do you think that I cannot appeal to my Father, and he will at once send me more than twelve legions of angels?"* A gesture would have done it. He could have called them down. He chose not to.

He owed us nothing. We owed him everything. We owed a debt we could not pay. He paid a debt he did not owe. He carried water for us while we were behind enemy lines, lost in the desert, wounded and broken.

---

### The Difference — He Is the Water

But there is one difference between Christ and that lieutenant.

The lieutenant carried water to his friend. Christ *is* the water.

Psalm 34:8 says *"taste and see that the Lord is good."* And in John 4 — the woman at the well — Jesus says: *"Whoever drinks of the water that I will give him will never be thirsty again. The water that I will give him will become in him a spring of water welling up to eternal life."* (John 4:14)

He didn't just haul it to us. He became it for us.

---

### Close — Strategic Discipleship

As we close this morning — believers, look at the people around you. Some of them are people for whom you are called to carry water.

Some don't know Jesus, and you may be the one God is going to use to share the gospel with them for the first time. Some are weaker brothers and sisters, and God may use you to help disciple them. Be intentional. Live in a way that puts you in contact with lost people. Find someone you can pour your life into — if you haven't already.

A lot of people want monuments. Buildings named after them. Libraries. Something to keep their name alive. I say it's far better to leave a legacy that lives on through people you've discipled.

---

### Gospel

If you don't know Jesus — that's where we start.

*"I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."* (John 14:6)

Jesus is the living water. He is the one who ran into the dark for you, not twelve miles but from eternity — and he did not come back empty-handed. If you don't have a relationship with him, come talk to me after the service. I would love to tell you more about how he has carried water for you.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm Pastoral Sketch That Carries Water to the Surface but Never Breaks Ground**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A winsome opening of Romans 15:1-3 that effectively deploys the lieutenant illustration and lands a moving Christ-as-water image, while remaining too thin on exposition, too light on conscience, and too close to moralistic discipleship-culture exhortation to constitute full-weight preaching.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS (with reservation)**
The sermon's thesis — "the strong bear with and actively serve the weak, as Christ served us" — does arise from the text's argument in Romans 15:1-3. The passage is the source of the main idea.

**Authority Test: PASS (with reservation)**
The preacher does not edit out the text's demands. However, the sermon handles the passage's Christological basis (v.3, quoting Psalm 69:9) only in passing. The quotation "the reproaches of those who reproached you fell on me" is the hermeneutical engine of Paul's argument — Christ bore the reproach that was directed at God — and the sermon substitutes the water illustration for the actual exegetical weight Paul is carrying here. The passage stands under the sermon, but the passage's own logic is partially bypassed rather than followed.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — Christ is present but functions decoratively at points.** The sermon does present Christ as essential — the water image lands well, and the gospel close is genuine. However, in the "Strategic Discipleship" close, the application slides into motivational language ("live a life worth reproducing," "leave a legacy through people you've discipled") that does not require Christ to be true. A life-coach could say the same things. Flag is a warning, not a full trigger — the gospel close partially redeems this.

**Flag 4 — Application is substantially generic.** "Find someone you can pour your life into," "be intentional," "live in a way that puts you in contact with lost people" — these are discipleship-culture mantras that could appear in any sermon in any church at any time. No specific conscience is cornered. No particular failure of the strong is named. No concrete situation in this congregation is identified. The application does not differentiate hearers. This flag is triggered.

**Score capped at 69 due to Flag 4.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10 pts): 7* — The command of v.1 and the neighbor-building of v.2 are covered, and Christ's example from v.3 is noted. But Psalm 69:9 — which Paul actually cites — is completely absent. Paul's point is that Christ absorbed the reproach directed at God, making him the supreme model of bearing with others' failures toward God. The sermon replaces this with a generic "Christ serves us" theme. The specific mechanism of the text — the OT quotation and its redemptive-historical logic — goes unexplained.
- *Contextual Awareness (5 pts): 3* — The link to Romans 14 is made (food/drink, not destroying the work of God). The broader argument of Romans 14-15 as a unit on Jew-Gentile relations within the Roman church is not engaged, though it would have enriched the "Paul carried water to Gentiles" section.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5 pts): 2* — The Greek is not engaged. The key distinction between "bear with" (ἀνέχεσθαι) as patient tolerance and active building-up is not made. The Fallen Condition Focus (the strong's tendency toward self-pleasing, the ecclesial fracture this causes) is implied but not named concretely.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5 pts): 2* — The detour into Paul's missionary biography is loosely connected to the text. The "if you trace your spiritual family tree back, Paul is there" section, while evocative, is an idea-import that doesn't arise from Romans 15:1-3.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7 pts): 5* — The Christ-as-water image is genuinely moving and Christologically sound. The passion narrative details (scourging, mock trial, choosing not to call angels) are vivid and appropriate. However, the atonement mechanism is not named — why did Christ bear the reproach? What was accomplished? The cross is described experientially, not theologically.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3 pts): 2* — The Gentile inclusion theme in the Paul section is a natural redemptive-historical bridge. The woman at the well (John 4) as a Gentile/Samaritan carrying water is an organic choice. Not fully developed but present.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4 pts): 3* — No serious theological errors. "We owed a debt we could not pay; he paid a debt he did not owe" is a serviceable summary. Colossians 3:3 ("your life is hidden with Christ in God") is cited without explanation in a context (Gentile adoption) where its meaning is not self-evident to a listener.
- *Confessional Alignment (4 pts): 2* — The gospel close gestures at Christ as the only way (John 14:6), but justification by faith is not stated. The category of union with Christ appears in the Colossians citation but is not developed. No fatal contradiction, but confessional substance is thin.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (5 pts): 3* — The gospel close is present and warm: Jesus is the living water, he came from eternity into the dark, come talk to me. But justification by faith, the necessity of repentance, and the nature of the new birth are absent. An unbeliever would know something is on offer but not precisely what.
- *Necessity of Christ (5 pts): 3* — The water-as-Christ image makes Christ necessary to the metaphor. However, the application section ("be intentional," "find someone to pour into," "leave a legacy") does not require Christ for its logic. The discipleship call is functionally separable from the gospel.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (5 pts): 3* — The passion narrative is the sermon's strongest moment. It moves toward the cross with genuine force. The sermon does not, however, build the application from the cross — the imperative ("carry water for others") flows from the illustration and example more than from what the cross accomplished for us and in us.

**Synagogue Test:** A devout non-Christian could hear most of this sermon — including the discipleship application — without deep offense. The closing gospel appeal is pointed, but the body of the sermon operates largely in the mode of moral example (Christ and Paul as models to emulate) rather than as proclamation of the uniquely scandalous good news. Minor flag for moralistic drift in the application.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15**

- *Heart Penetration (5 pts): 2* — The sermon does not search the heart. No specific failure of the strong is named. The self-righteous, the spiritually comfortable, the person who has been passive in discipleship for years — none of them are cornered. The text offers this: the strong person who refuses to bear with the weak is implicitly sinning against both neighbor and Christ. That confrontation never arrives.
- *Specificity (5 pts): 2* — "Find someone to pour your life into" is the application's entire load-bearing structure. There is no specificity about what bearing with the weak actually looks like in this congregation's context. No examples of what the weak person's "failings" look like in practice, no naming of what the strong person's self-pleasing looks like in practice.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (5 pts): 3* — The sermon distinguishes unbelievers (gospel close) from believers (discipleship close). That is about the extent of differentiation. Perkins' seven categories: only the unbeliever and the believing "strong" are addressed. The backslider, the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, the knowledgeable-but-unmoved strong person who has heard this discipleship call a hundred times and still isn't doing it — none addressed.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Call to Response (4 pts): 2* — The closing paragraph gestures toward action ("be intentional," "find someone") but does not issue a sharp imperative grounded in the text's own language. There is no "therefore" moment. No summoning. No direct address to the conscience at the point of demand.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (4 pts): 2* — The discipleship theme has witness potential (telling the gospel to someone), but the equipping is vague. The congregation is told *to* disciple but not *how* to engage, *what* to do in a specific situation, or *what truth to believe* as the ground for this action.

- *Clarity of Outline (4 pts): 3* — The structure is traceable: illustration → command → Paul's example → Christ's example → the difference → close. A listener could follow this. The section headers (Command, Paul's Example, Christ's Example) do the structural work.

- *Unity (2 pts): 1* — Two ideas compete: (a) bearing with the weak as discipleship, and (b) carrying the gospel to the lost. The close conflates discipleship of weaker believers with evangelism of unbelievers. These are related but distinct, and the sermon blurs them.

- *Clarity (2 pts): 2* — The illustration illuminates the main point and then is extended organically to Christ. The "he didn't carry water — he *is* the water" turn is the sermon's best line. It earns its place.
- *Memorability (1 pt): 0* — The illustration itself, given the unverified-source flag, cannot be leaned on. If the story doesn't check out, it undercuts the whole opening. The image of Christ as living water will persist (because John 4 persists). The lieutenant illustration may not.

- *Cadence and Breath (1 pt): 0* — There is no climactic compression. The sermon's best moment (Christ-as-water) is followed by a deflating generic application. The cadence doesn't build to a point of maximum pressure.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Christ-as-water turn** ("He didn't just haul it to us. He became it for us.") is the sermon's finest moment — theologically precise, imaginatively sharp, and organically arising from John 4. This is a line worth keeping and expanding.

2. **The passion narrative is concrete and vivid.** The trial details (paid witnesses who couldn't agree), the scourging, the Matthew 26:53 citation — these land with appropriate force and resist sentimentalism. The line "he owed us nothing, we owed him everything" is clean and true.

3. **The structural movement from illustration to Paul to Christ is coherent.** The three-stage escalation (the lieutenant, then Paul, then Christ) gives the sermon a clear logic that a listener can follow. The "someone else's lead" pivot into Christ's example is a clean transition.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Psalm 69:9 — the cited text in v.3 — is never touched.** Paul's quotation is the structural load-bearer of his argument: Christ bore the reproach meant for God. This is not a side detail; it is the basis for the command. The sermon replaces Paul's actual Christological argument with the water illustration. The preacher has illustrated around the text's center of gravity rather than through it.

2. **The application is generic and conscience-free.** "Find someone to pour your life into" is motivational-culture language, not applicatory preaching. No specific failure of the strong is named. No one in the congregation is cornered. The text explicitly names self-pleasing as the opposing sin — but self-pleasing in this congregation's actual forms (avoiding difficult people, protecting comfort, refusing to inconvenience yourself for the weak) is never identified.

3. **The discipleship close conflates two different things.** The text is about bearing with weaker *believers* (the "weak in faith" of Romans 14-15 — specifically those with scruples about food and holy days). The sermon's application slides between discipling weaker Christians and evangelizing unbelievers ("some don't know Jesus — you may be the one to share the gospel with them"). These are related but not identical, and the conflation muddles what the text is actually calling for.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how close the closing application comes to discipleship-movement moralism. "Live a life worth reproducing," "be intentional," "leave a legacy through people" — these are the lingua franca of para-church discipleship culture and could appear in a John Maxwell talk. The gospel close is genuine, but it arrives after the application has already shifted the sermon's center of gravity from grace-response to self-improvement-adjacent exhortation.

The preacher also may not see that the "Paul as water-carrier" section, while evocative, is essentially a detour. Paul's biography is not in Romans 15:1-3. The detour is not false, but it borrows time from the actual text.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The lieutenant illustration *feels* like the sermon's strongest section because it is the most memorable and the most emotionally engaging. But it actually does the least textual work. The real strength of the sermon is in the Christ-as-water image — which is derivative of the illustration rather than the other way around. The illustration may crowd out the very thing that makes the sermon worth hearing.

Similarly, the gospel close *feels* complete because it names Jesus as the living water and gives a clear invitation. But it is only two short paragraphs at the end of a sermon that has been primarily about discipleship culture. The gospel is present, but it functions as an appendix rather than a controlling logic.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Psalm 69:9 and the nature of Christ's bearing.** Paul quotes a psalm of David in which the psalmist suffers reproaches meant for God — and Paul applies this to Christ. The Christological point is that Christ bore God's reproach — the weight of human sin against God — as an act of bearing with the weak. This means the "bearing with the weak" in v.1-2 is not simply empathy or patience; it is patterned on an atoning, substitutionary bearing. The sermon never touches this. If it had, the application would be grounded in something far more powerful than example — it would be grounded in union with Christ, who bore the ultimate weight so that we might bear the lesser weight for one another.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Expose Psalm 69:9.** Spend sixty to ninety seconds on what Paul actually quotes in v.3. "The reproaches of those who reproached you fell on me" — this is David's psalm, applied by Paul to Christ. Christ absorbed the hostility that humanity directed at God. That is the ultimate bearing-with-the-weak. Build the application from this, not only from the illustration.

2. **Name the specific sin of the strong.** The text sets up self-pleasing as the opposing failure. Name what self-pleasing looks like in this congregation. "Some of you have avoided that person in the congregation who asks too many questions and has too many scruples — you've written them off as weak, and you've protected your own comfort. Paul says that is sin." Corner someone.

3. **Separate discipleship and evangelism in the close.** The text is about bearing with weaker *brothers* — those already in the church. The application should first press that call with specificity, then, secondarily, extend the principle outward to witness. Currently, the two are conflated in a way that dilutes both.

4. **Reorder the close.** End with the Christ-as-water image — not with the discipleship application. The gospel close is the right final note; let the application precede it rather than follow it. The congregation should exit with Christ before them, not with a to-do list.

5. **Add one moment of direct address to the self-sufficient strong.** "Some of you are the strong in this room — and you know it, and you've been using that as permission to distance yourself from the people who exhaust you. That is not strength. That is self-pleasing. And Paul says: that is not who Christ was."

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The Psalm 69:9 citation — the exegetical center of the passage — is never opened.** "The reproaches of those who reproached you fell on me" is Paul's entire argument in verse 3. He is not merely saying Christ suffered; he is saying Christ absorbed the cost others refused to bear, fulfilling a pattern from the Psalter. The sermon quotes the verse, applies it as a general statement about Christ's suffering, and moves on. The engine of Paul's argument is left running in the parking lot while the sermon drives somewhere else.

2. **Application is generic and interchangeable.** "Find someone you can pour your life into" and "Live in a way that puts you in contact with lost people" could appear in any evangelism or discipleship sermon. They do not arise specifically from Romans 15:1-3's argument about bearing with the scruples of the weak. The congregation is not told what bearing with the weak actually looks like — deferring personal freedoms, absorbing inconvenience without resentment, entering into someone's weakness rather than managing it from a distance.

3. **The strong believer's self-pleasing is not pressed.** The Fallen Condition Focus of this text is the instinct to please oneself at the expense of the weak. Paul names it directly: "not to please ourselves." The sermon quotes this and then moves to illustration, never pressing the congregation with the specific temptation — using your freedom as a right, being impatient with the spiritually immature, resenting those whose weakness costs you something. The law does not land before the gospel relieves it.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's most memorable content — the illustration — is doing the heavy lifting that the text should be doing. The structure is, in effect: illustration → text → illustration-continues → text as decoration. The danger going forward is a preaching style where the sermon lands emotionally but the congregation cannot reconstruct what the passage itself argued.

Additionally, the closing application's pivot to evangelism and discipleship broadly, rather than the specific burden of the passage (bearing with the scruples of the weak within the church), suggests the preacher may be more comfortable with outward-facing application (evangelism, discipleship) than inward-facing community accountability. The text presses community; the sermon deflects to mission.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon feels strong because the illustration is emotionally resonant and the "He is the water" move is theologically satisfying. A congregation will leave feeling like they heard good preaching. But the strength is in the illustration and the pivot, not in the exposition of the text. The passage's argument — Christ as the fulfillment of the Psalm 69 pattern of absorbing what the righteous suffer on behalf of others — is never actually preached. The sermon is emotionally richer than it is exegetically rich, and the emotional strength creates the impression of depth that the exegetical work has not fully earned.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 3b — the Psalm 69:9 quotation — is one of the richest redemptive-historical moments in the passage. Paul is reaching back into the Psalter and locating Jesus in the Psalm 69 sufferer — the one who bore the reproaches that rightly belong to God. Christ does not please himself because he absorbed the full weight of divine reproach that sinners deserved. The sermon glimpses this in the Christ section (the voluntariness of the cross, the debt language) but never arrives at the Psalm 69 logic. Preaching this would have given the Christ section actual exegetical grounding, connected the Old Testament to the New organically, and made the call to bear with the weak feel like an echo of cosmic redemption rather than a discipleship principle.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Open Psalm 69:9 before applying verse 3.** Spend 2-3 minutes explaining what Paul is doing with the citation — Christ absorbing the reproaches aimed at God, fulfilling the suffering-righteous pattern of the Psalms. Then show how this is Paul's argument: Christ didn't please himself because he took on the full weight of what we deserved. This transforms the Christ section from an illustration to an exegetical argument.

2. **Press the Fallen Condition Focus before the relief.** After reading the text, name the specific sin: self-pleasing, the instinct to treat personal freedom as a right rather than a resource for others. Give it a face — the strong believer who sighs at the weaker brother's questions, who resents the inconvenience of slower growth, who pours a drink and considers his liberty without considering his neighbor. Let the law land before Christ carries it.

3. **Narrow the application to the passage's actual burden.** Replace "find someone to pour your life into" with specific, differentiated application — what does it look like to bear with a weaker believer who thinks Sunday football is sinful? Who struggles with alcohol and sits at your dinner table? Who asks the same spiritual questions for the third year in a row? The text has specific address; the application should match it.

4. **Integrate the gospel section structurally, not appendicially.** The gospel is tacked on at the end in a separate headed section. Consider weaving the gospel moment into "The Difference" section — let "He is the water" become the full gospel presentation, with justification explicitly named, rather than a clever metaphor that then requires a gospel section appended after.

5. **Add direct address to the strong believer at the conscience-pressing moment.** After identifying the Fallen Condition Focus, address them directly: "Some of you are strong. You know you are. You've moved past the food debates and the calendar scruples. And you are impatient. You've earned that right, haven't you? Paul says you haven't." This kind of specific, cornering address is currently absent from the manuscript.
