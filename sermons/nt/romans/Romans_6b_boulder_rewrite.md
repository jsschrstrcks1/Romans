**Romans 6b – Freed From Sin, Slaves to Righteousness**

This is how most people talk about their sin—like it’s a little loose gravel on the road. But God sees it for what it is—a massive boulder blocking the way. A boulder you can’t move, can’t get around, and can’t ignore. And unless it’s removed, it will keep you from the destination He’s called you to.
**INTRODUCTION**
Before we dive into Romans 6, I want us to remember why this matters for the nations. Just as that boulder blocks a road, sin blocks the way to God for every people group still in darkness...
**Point 1 – Grace Exposes Your Master**
Grace doesn’t hand you a broom for the gravel—it sends the demolition crew for the boulder. When Christ saves you, He removes not just the penalty of sin, but the master you once served.
**CONCLUSION**
So the question is this—are you still camped at the foot of the boulder, pretending it’s just a minor inconvenience? Or have you followed the King who shattered it and brought you under His banner?

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**DEFERRED — Insufficient manuscript to evaluate**

The Berean Gate requires that the main point of the sermon demonstrably arise from the passage’s own argument. Romans 6:15–23 (the apparent source for "slaves to righteousness") contains a precise argument: freedom from sin does not mean freedom from all masters — you are always a slave, and the question is which master you serve. The introduction, one point, and a conclusion fragment that exist here gesture at that theme. But the sermon cannot be evaluated for Scopus coherence because the passage’s argument has not been exposed — it has only been gestured at with a metaphor.

The boulder illustration is not the problem. The absence of the text is the problem.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Three fatal flaw flags are triggered — score capped at 69 regardless of other elements. However, given the manuscript’s incompleteness, no meaningful score can be assigned at all.**

1. **No clear gospel presentation.** The conclusion mentions "the King who shattered" the boulder, and Point 1 mentions Christ removing the penalty of sin and the master. These are gestures toward the gospel. But justification by faith is absent, the cross is unnamed, and penal substitution is not articulated. An unbeliever attending this sermon would receive a vivid metaphor and no clear account of how Christ accomplishes the removal.

2. **Application is entirely generic.** The only differentiated address is the conclusion’s binary: "camped at the boulder" vs. "followed the King." No specific address to the struggling believer, the self-righteous, the discouraged, the backslider, or the hardened. No conscience is pressed on any particular point.

3. **No actual exhortation.** The concluding question ("are you still camped...?") is rhetorical but not exhortative. The congregation is asked a question; they are not summoned to a response. "Did the preacher tell the people what they must now believe, repent of, obey, or pursue?" — No. They were asked which camp they are in, without being told what to do next.

The sermon does not move toward the cross; it gestures at "the King who shattered it" without explaining what that shattering cost.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The boulder/gravel distinction is a genuine and useful insight.** The sermon’s opening move — reframing sin from minor inconvenience to impassable obstacle — is the kind of image that can reorient a congregation’s conscience on the nature of their bondage. This is not decoration; it maps accurately to Paul’s argument that sin is a master, not a management problem.

2. **Point 1 contains a theologically important claim stated clearly.** "Christ removes not just the penalty of sin, but the master you once served" — this is the distinction between justification and the beginning of sanctification, and it is accurate. It is the kind of sentence that a congregation needs to hear and will remember. If the sermon were complete, this would be one of its anchoring claims.

3. **The prose voice is oral and direct.** What exists reads like preaching, not like an essay. The sentences are shaped for the spoken word. The closing rhetorical question is structured correctly — direct address, binary choice, forward pressure. The voice is there; the sermon is not yet.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text is absent.** No verse of Romans 6 is quoted or examined in the existing manuscript. The boulder metaphor is imposed on the passage rather than drawn from it. Paul’s argument — "do you not know that to whom you present yourselves as slaves to obey, his slaves you are?" (v. 16), the shame/holiness progression (v. 21–22), "the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life" (v. 23) — none of this appears. A sermon outline about a boulder is not yet a sermon on Romans 6.

2. **The gospel is metaphor, not proclamation.** "The King who shattered it" is a vivid image, but it is not the gospel. It does not name the cross. It does not name the resurrection. It does not articulate how the shattering was accomplished or what it cost. An unbeliever attending this sermon would encounter a motivational framework with a Christian king in it — not the gospel of penal substitution and new creation.

3. **The sermon is structurally incomplete.** Introduction, one point, and a conclusion fragment do not constitute a sermon. The evaluation cannot be completed on what exists. This is not a critique of the preacher’s ability — it is a statement about the manuscript’s current state.

### 11. Blind Spots

The boulder metaphor, while vivid, risks making sin primarily an obstacle to be removed rather than a bondage of the will and affections from which only union with Christ in His death can liberate. Paul’s argument in Romans 6 is not that Christ clears the road — it is that the old self is buried with Christ and a new self is raised. The metaphor’s logic is closer to obstacle-removal than to death-and-resurrection. A preacher attached to the illustration may not notice that it subtly frames salvation as external road-clearing rather than the internal new-creation work Paul is describing.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The boulder metaphor gives the sermon an appearance of arresting and confident opening. It reads as though the sermon is going somewhere powerful. This is the illusion of strength: the illustration is vivid, the voice is confident, the structure sounds like it will pay off — but the payoff requires a complete sermon that does not yet exist. The strength of the opening image should not be mistaken for the strength of the theological content.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Romans 6:20–23 contains one of the most psychologically penetrating observations in all of Paul’s letters: "When you were slaves of sin, you were free from righteousness. But what fruit were you getting at that time from the things of which you are now ashamed?" Paul invites the congregation to remember their former slavery — to feel the shame of what they once called freedom. This is extraordinary preaching material. It is the moment when the boulder metaphor could be grounded in the congregation’s actual experience: "What was the thing you pursued that you now know was a millstone? What did sin promise you that it never delivered?" That moment of concrete, specific, shame-laden remembrance is entirely missing from the current outline.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Ground every point in the text before building the illustration.** For each structural movement, begin with the verse, state its claim in plain language, then deploy the boulder/illustration framework to help it land. The illustration should serve the text, not substitute for it. Quote Romans 6:16, 6:22, and 6:23 explicitly.

2. **Develop a Point 2 and Point 3 that trace Paul’s argument.** Possible structure: (1) Diagnose the master — you are always a slave, the question is to whom (vv. 15–18); (2) Consider the fruit — what did your former master pay you? (vv. 19–22); (3) Receive the wage and the gift — sin pays in death; God gives eternal life (v. 23). The boulder metaphor can thread through all three.

3. **Name the cross.** At minimum, add one paragraph in the body or conclusion that explicitly states: Christ was crushed under the weight of what you could not carry. The penalty was borne in His body. The master was dethroned through His resurrection. This is the ground of everything the boulder metaphor promises.

4. **Add differentiated application per point.** After each structural movement, address at least two categories of hearer: the person still in bondage who does not know it, and the believer who is living as if the boulder is still there. The Perkins’ taxonomy does not need to be exhaustive, but the sermon currently addresses only the undifferentiated "you."

5. **Convert the concluding question into an exhortation.** Replace "are you still camped at the foot of the boulder?" with a direct call: "Stop camping. The King has moved the stone. Walk. Today. In the name of Christ, walk — in newness of life, in the service of righteousness, toward the holiness that leads to eternal life." The congregation needs to be told what to do, not only asked where they are.
