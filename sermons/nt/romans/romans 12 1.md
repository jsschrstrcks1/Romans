**STRUCTURED SERMON MANUSCRIPT — WEEK 1**
**Romans 12:1–2 — A Life on the Altar**
**(Structure C: 5 major movements; 45–60 minutes, single-spaced)**
**INTRODUCTION — THE MOUNTAIN THAT FRAMES THE CHAPTER**
If you’ve brought your Bible—and I hope you have—please open with me to Romans 12:1–2. After eleven chapters of the deepest doctrine in Scripture, Paul now pivots. You can feel the hinge turn. Everything God has done now becomes everything we are called to do.
Before we read, let’s take our usual moment for the People Group of the Week. We do this because the grace that saves us is the grace that sends us. Today’s group is the ____________, with fewer than _____ percent believing. Many have never heard the gospel once. Would two of you pray for them?
(Prayer.)
Let’s read the text:
“I appeal to you therefore, brothers, by the mercies of God, to present your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and acceptable to God, which is your spiritual worship. Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind…”
This is the Word of the Lord.
We’ve read the Book—now let’s talk to the Author.
(Father… Amen.)
Before we walk into the text, I want to take you up a mountain in Genesis: Abraham and Isaac climbing Mount Moriah. Isaac carrying the wood, Abraham carrying the knife. Isaac trusting his father. Isaac laying down willingly. That picture — a living, breathing, conscious son offering himself to the will of his father — is the living illustration behind Romans 12:1–2.
That mountain becomes the spine of this entire passage.

**POINT 1 — THE MOUNTAIN OF MERCY (ROMANS 1–11)**
Paul begins with a thunderclap: **“Therefore.”**
This is not decorative — it is doctrinal.
You cannot obey Romans 12 until you’ve understood Romans 1–11.
Think of everything Paul has already shown us:
Romans 1–3 — mankind guilty, deserving wrath.
Romans 4 — Abraham justified by faith alone.
Romans 5 — peace with God through the blood of Christ.
Romans 6 — dead to sin, alive to God.
Romans 7 — the agony of remaining sin.
Romans 8 — no condemnation; adoption; the Spirit’s power.
Romans 9–11 — sovereign mercy choosing and saving a people.
After eleven chapters of mercy, Paul says:
**“Therefore… by the mercies of God…”**
Mercy is the fuel of obedience.
Mercy is what gets you up the mountain.
Just like Isaac climbed Moriah because he trusted his father, we climb the mountain of obedience because we trust ours.

**POINT 2 — THE CALL TO THE ALTAR (PRESENT YOUR BODIES)**
“Present your bodies.”
Not your intentions.
Not your spiritual feelings.
Not your Sunday version of yourself.
Your bodies — the whole embodied life:
Your habits.
Your eyes.
Your tongue.
Your schedule.
Your private world.
Your online world.
Your money.
Your speech.
Your reactions.
Everything.
And this is where Moriah comes back into view.
Isaac was not dragged up the mountain — he climbed it.
He carried the wood — the very wood he would lie upon.
He surrendered — willingly, consciously, trustingly.
He was a living sacrifice.
But Isaac did not die.
Why?
Because God provided a substitute — the ram in the thicket.
And here is the difference between Isaac and us:
**Isaac walked off the mountain because God provided a substitute.**
**You walk onto the mountain because God has already provided yours — Christ.**
So when Paul says your sacrifice is “holy and acceptable,” he means this:
You are accepted because Christ is accepted.
As Colossians 3 says — your life is **hidden with Christ in God**.
Covered in His righteousness.
Wrapped in His perfection.

**POINT 3 — THE NATURE OF WORSHIP (A LIFE SURRENDERED)**
“And this is your spiritual worship.”
Not emotional worship.
Not temporary worship.
Not a feeling — pizza can give you feelings.
Not a one-hour-a-week religious moment.
Not singing songs and reading Scripture and then living however we please.
Worship in Romans 12 is **a posture of life** — a lifestyle of surrender to our King.
A life bowed before God, not just a heart stirred for a moment.
Worship looks like submission in all seasons:
Submission when life is stable.
Submission when life is uncertain.
Submission when plans work.
Submission when plans fall apart.
Submission in strength.
Submission in weakness.
Submission in the quiet, mundane, unglamorous grind of everyday faithfulness.
Worship is not a mood.
Worship is not a moment.
**Worship is surrender.**

**POINT 4 — THE WAR FOR YOUR MIND (CONFORMED VS. TRANSFORMED)**
Paul continues:
**“Do not be conformed to this world.”**
The Greek word pictures being **pressed into a mold**.
And here’s the frightening truth:
Conformity requires zero effort.
It happens automatically if you do nothing.
If you turn off your mind, the world will disciple you.
Entertainment disciples you.
Politics disciples you.
Your friends disciple you.
Your desires disciple you.
Your phone disciples you.
Your fears disciple you.
So when someone says, “Well, everyone around me cusses, so I do too”—
that’s not an excuse.
That’s a confession.
You’ve allowed the world to disciple your mouth.
And not just your mouth:
Tell little white lies?
Conformity.
Avoid evangelism because your circle has shrunk to only Christians?
Conformity.

It feels safe, but it’s disobedience.
A silent Christian is a conformed Christian.
A comfortable Christian is often a conformed Christian.
Want to see worldly conformity in the wild?
Just look at this week’s Epstein headlines.

The documents show what Scripture already taught us:
People will excuse anything if it protects their access, influence, or power.
Deals in shadows.
Compromises made for advantage.
Sin defended because “our side” benefits.

And the church is not immune.
Many today trust in political maneuvering more than holiness,
in winning more than witnessing,
in strategy more than surrender.

But Psalm 20:7 cuts through the fog:
*Some trust in chariots and some in horses,*
*but we trust in the name of the LORD our God.*
John Flavel said,
“To preach the truth without offending carnal men is to attempt what Christ Himself could not do.”
Christ never bartered holiness for access.
He never traded truth for influence.
He needs no running mate and no political machinery.
When Paul says, “Do not be conformed,”
he is calling you to reject every idol,
every compromise,
every worldly strategy masquerading as wisdom.
Then comes the positive command:
**“But be transformed by the renewal of your mind.”**
Conformity is external pressure.
Transformation is internal power.
This is the Spirit’s work — metamorphosis.
And think of Moriah again:
Transformation happens **on the mountain**,
not before the climb.
Abraham walked down different than he walked up.
Isaac walked down changed forever.
The altar shapes the worshiper.
Obedience renews the mind.
Surrender reshapes the soul.

You don’t renew your mind so you can surrender.
You surrender so God can renew your mind.

**POINT 5 — THE CLARITY THAT COMES AFTER SURRENDER (DISCERNING GOD’S WILL)**
“…that by testing you may discern what is the will of God.”
Notice the order:
Surrender → Renewal → Discernment
Not:
Discernment → Then surrender if we like it.
Abraham did not see the ram at the bottom of the mountain.
He saw it with the knife raised in obedience.

Most Christians never discern God’s will
because they never go far enough up the mountain to see it.

They want revelation without obedience.
Clarity without surrender.
Guidance without sacrifice.
But Romans 12 tells us:
**Obedience precedes understanding.**
**Worship precedes wisdom.**
**Surrender precedes discernment.**
And when God renews your mind,
His will becomes “good and acceptable and perfect.”
Sin becomes bitter.
Obedience becomes sweet.
As John Owen wrote:
“He who has slight thoughts of sin never had great thoughts of God.”
**CONCLUSION — THE TRUE SON ON THE TRUE MOUNTAIN**
Back to Moriah.
Isaac carried the wood.
Isaac surrendered.
Isaac lay down.
But Isaac did not die.

Because God provided a substitute.

But centuries later, on a mountain not far from Moriah,
another Son carried the wood.
Another Son climbed the hill.
Another Son lay down on the altar.

But this time —
there was no voice from heaven saying, “Stop.”
There was no ram in the thicket.
No substitute.

Because **He** was the substitute.

Christ became the dying sacrifice
so that you could become the living one.

Christ stayed on His altar
so you could climb onto yours.

Christ surrendered His life
so you could surrender yours without fear.

So how do we respond?

To the struggling saint:
God is not asking for flawless climbing.
Just faithful climbing.
Your sacrifice is accepted because Christ’s was perfect.

To the self-assured sinner:
If there is no surrender,
no sacrifice,
no transformation,
no renewal—
then the mercy you claim may not be the mercy you possess.

To the seeking skeptic:
You don’t clean yourself up before coming.
You come as you are,
lay yourself down,
and God does the renewing.

Romans 12 is the call to climb the mountain.
To carry the wood.
To surrender your life.
Not to die for your sins—Christ has done that.
But to live for His glory.

A living sacrifice.
A renewed mind.
A life on the altar.

Because the King who carried His cross up His mountain
now calls you to carry your life up yours.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Preacher on the Mountain: Merciful Foundation, Strong Typology, Application That Corners but Doesn't Fully Distinguish**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A faithful and pastorally warm opening of Romans 12:1–2 that magnified the mercy-driven logic of surrender and leveraged the Moriah typology with genuine gospel force, while leaving several categories of hearer unaddressed and the mind-renewal transformation underdeveloped at the experiential level.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS**

**Scopus Test**: The sermon's thesis — that the mercies of God fuel and fund the living sacrifice of the whole embodied life — demonstrably arises from the passage's own "therefore" logic. The preacher correctly reads the hinge between Romans 1–11 and 12:1–2.

**Authority Test**: The preacher stands under the text. The uncomfortable demands — bodily presentation, non-conformity, the sharp implication that mercy claimed but not lived is mercy perhaps not possessed — are not softened. The self-assured sinner is addressed, not coddled.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No fatal flaws triggered. Score is uncapped.**

The gospel is proclaimed (not merely assumed), Christ is essential to the argument, application is specific (not entirely generic), the congregation is called to response, and the sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 20/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (8/10)*: The "therefore" as doctrinal hinge is well-handled. The progression from mercy to sacrifice to transformation to discernment faithfully tracks Paul's argument. However, the Greek word for "conformed" (syschematizesthe) is mentioned but the parallel word for "transformed" (metamorphousthe) is not developed with equal depth — the passive/divine-agency dimension of transformation receives only cursory treatment.
- *Contextual Awareness (4/5)*: The Romans 1–11 summary is appropriate and useful without being exhaustive. Colossians 3 is deployed legitimately.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5)*: The "bodies" exposition is excellent — concrete, embodied, refusing abstraction. The "spiritual worship" (logiken latreian) is helpfully demystified. Some more development of *logiken* as "rational" or "word-governed" would have served the renewal-of-mind point.
- *Logical Faithfulness (4/5)*: No eisegesis. The Moriah typology does not become allegory — it functions as legitimate typology rooted in covenantal logic. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is implicitly present — the human tendency to surrender to external pressure rather than internal transformation — but it is never explicitly named. The preacher knows what wound the text addresses; he does not quite name it for the hearer.

**Category 2 — Theology & Doctrinal Integrity: 14/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (6/7)*: The conclusion's Christological movement is the sermon's best theological moment. "Christ became the dying sacrifice so that you could become the living one" is precise and doxological. The typological movement from Moriah to Calvary is earned, not forced.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3)*: The Abraham/Isaac/Christ typological chain is organic. However, the broader redemptive-historical placement of Romans 12 — this is what new-covenant life looks like in contrast to old-covenant animal sacrifice — is not made explicit. The "living sacrifice" language vs. Levitical sacrifice would have deepened this.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4)*: "You are accepted because Christ is accepted" is excellent. The justification logic is present but understated. The transformation language would benefit from clearer Spirit-agency — the sermon says it is "the Spirit's work" once, but this needs more development, lest transformation sound like willpower.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4)*: No doctrinal contradiction. The grace-to-obedience logic is Reformed. Sovereign mercy is mentioned but not developed pastorally.

**Category 3 — Gospel Centrality & Force: 12/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (4/5)*: The substitution logic is clear and climactic. An unbeliever could understand that Christ died in our place and that our acceptance before God is grounded in His. Justification by faith is present but could be more explicitly stated.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5)*: Christ is structurally necessary — the typology cannot work without Him. The conclusion makes Him indispensable, not decorative.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (4/5)*: The sermon moves toward the cross throughout. The law does land (conformity is disobedience, the self-assured sinner is directly addressed) before the gospel relieves it.
- *Synagogue Test*: The sermon would give offense in a synagogue. The typological claim that the Moriah substitute points forward to Christ, and that Christ's death is the only ground of acceptance, is a scandal, not a decoration. Passed.

**Category 4 — Conscience & Applicatory Force: 10/15**

- *Heart Penetration (4/5)*: The conformity section genuinely presses the conscience — the Epstein illustration and the political-maneuvering application are specific and uncomfortable. "A comfortable Christian is often a conformed Christian" corners the complacent.
- *Specificity (4/5)*: The "bodies" list (habits, eyes, tongue, schedule, private world, online world, money, speech, reactions) is specific and embodied. The conformity applications are culturally grounded.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5)*: The conclusion addresses three categories — the struggling saint, the self-assured sinner, and the seeking skeptic — which is commendable. However, Perkins' other categories are not reached: the backslider (the one who once climbed the mountain and walked back down), the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled (the doctrinally fluent man who has never yielded his body), and the humbled-but-not-yet-believing receive no specific address. The congregation is largely addressed as a binary (believer/unbeliever).

**Category 5 — Exhortation & Response: 6/8**

- *Call to Response (3/4)*: The threefold conclusion address is a genuine exhortation, not merely application. The three-way differentiation is pastoral. However, the exhortation does not coalesce into one commanding "therefore" moment — it is distributive (three types of people) rather than gathered.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (3/4)*: The sermon does send the church outward — the mission reference at the start, the evangelism-conformity connection, the witness implication. However, corporate body-life application is absent. The sacrifice and transformation are addressed entirely individually; the covenant-community dimension of Romans 12 is untouched.

**Category 6 — Structure & Logical Flow: 8/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (4/4)*: Five clear movements, each with a named heading. The napkin test passes easily.
- *Progression (3/3)*: The sermon builds from mercy (fuel) → sacrifice (altar) → worship (posture) → transformation (war) → discernment (fruit). The logic is clean and cumulative.
- *Unity (1/2)*: The Epstein/political application in Point 4, while culturally grounded, risks becoming a competing center of gravity. It is the longest illustration in the sermon and threatens to shift the sermon's weight from transformation-of-mind to political commentary. One paragraph could be excised without structural loss.

**Category 7 — Illustration & Imagination: 4/5**

- *Relevance (2/2)*: The political/Epstein illustration is contemporary and specific. The Moriah typology is handled with pastoral imagination.
- *Clarity (2/2)*: The primary illustration (Moriah/Isaac) illuminates the point — it does not compete with it.
- *Memorability (0/1)*: The typological conclusion will be remembered. The Epstein reference, while sharp, risks becoming the memorable moment rather than the theological point — cultural currency at the cost of sermonic gravity.

**Category 8 — Sermonic Force: 4/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2)*: The parallel lists, direct address, breath-length repetition ("Submission when… Submission when…"), and second-person direct address are consistently oral.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2)*: The Scripture-grounded assertion ratio is strong in Points 1–3 and the conclusion. Points 4–5 carry more cultural assertion than textual assertion. The Owen and Flavel quotes are well-deployed, but the Epstein section carries the sermon's rhetorical weight without sufficient scriptural tethering.
- *Cadence and Breath (1/1)*: The conclusion earns its climactic compression. "There was no ram in the thicket. No substitute. Because He was the substitute." — this is preaching.

### 6. Cognitive Load Assessment
**Score: −1**

The sermon is accessible and well-paced. One note: the Epstein section in Point 4 introduces a competing conceptual frame (political commentary) that risks fragmenting the congregation's tracking. Those already engaged in political debate may follow that thread and lose the transformation thread. Slightly dense at that point — one fewer cultural concept would help.

### 7. Total Score
**20 + 14 + 12 + 10 + 6 + 8 + 4 + 4 = 78 (subtotal) − 1 (cognitive load) = 77/100**

**Interpretation: Solid but lacking — needs another pass, primarily in spiritual differentiation, Spirit-agency in transformation, and the corporate dimension of sacrifice.**

---

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?*
**Primarily Christ.** The Moriah-to-Calvary movement is doxological, not therapeutic. The sermon moves the hearer toward the cross, not toward self-improvement.

2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?*
**Mostly God, with pockets of ideas.** The conclusion carries genuine divine gravity. The Epstein section is ideas-about-culture, and the transformation section (Point 4) is more diagnostic than doxological.

3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?*
**Both comforted and convicted** — the threefold conclusion address is genuinely pastoral, and the conformity section is genuinely convicting. The hearer does not leave merely informed.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Moriah typology is organically developed and earns its Christological conclusion.** The progression from Isaac-who-did-not-die to Christ-who-did is covenantally grounded, not forced. "Christ became the dying sacrifice so that you could become the living one" — this is the sermon's highest moment, and it arises from the text's own logic rather than being imported.

2. **The "bodies" exposition in Point 2 is specific, embodied, and refuses pietistic abstraction.** The list (habits, eyes, tongue, schedule, private world, online world, money, speech, reactions) confronts the tendency to offer God a "spiritual version" of ourselves while keeping the embodied life under our own management. This is exactly what Paul's use of "bodies" (somata) demands.

3. **The mercy-to-obedience logic is clear and Reformed without being mechanical.** "Mercy is the fuel of obedience" is a compressed statement of the indicative-before-imperative structure of New Testament ethics. The sermon does not moralize — it motivates from grace, not law.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Spiritual differentiation is underdeveloped.** The conclusion addresses three categories but misses the most important missing hearer: the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled man who knows Reformed soteriology, attends faithfully, could reconstruct the argument of Romans 1–11, and has never surrendered his private world. This is the most likely listener in a Reformed congregation, and the sermon does not corner him. The application lands most directly on the openly conforming or openly skeptical.

2. **The Spirit's agency in transformation is asserted but not expounded.** Point 4 correctly identifies transformation as "the Spirit's work — metamorphosis," but this is a single clause in an otherwise human-agency-heavy section. The imperatives ("do not be conformed," "be transformed") are explained without sufficient grounding in the Spirit's sanctifying work. A listener could hear this section as: "Try harder not to be conformed." The Spirit as the agent of the metamorphosis — the same Spirit who raised Christ from the dead (Romans 8:11) — needs pastoral development here.

3. **The Epstein/political illustration carries disproportionate rhetorical weight and risks displacing the theological center.** It is the longest single illustration in the sermon, and it touches a topic that will activate strong emotional responses in the congregation. A hearer absorbed in the political application may miss the transformation application entirely. The illustration is legitimate, but its length and emotional temperature are not proportional to its weight in the text's argument.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's application tilts almost entirely toward *external* conformity (what you say, who you talk to, what news you follow) while the text's most searching question is about *internal* formation — what is shaping your mind's habits of judgment and desire at the level below conscious choice. "Be transformed by the renewal of your mind" is not primarily about resisting external pressure; it is about the reordering of the affections, the imagination, and the evaluative capacity. The sermon addresses the symptoms (cussing, silent witness, political trust) more than the disease (a mind whose default categories and loves are still shaped by the age).

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Moriah typology feels like the sermon's structural spine, and the preacher handles it with genuine skill. But it is worth noting: the typology does almost all the sermon's illustrative and emotional work. Every major point returns to it. This creates unity — but it also means the sermon's imaginative range is narrow. If the typology lands for a hearer, the sermon lands. If it does not (for the analytically inclined hearer who needs propositional traction, or the hearer for whom the Genesis narrative is remote), the sermon's emotional architecture has nowhere to pivot. The typology is a strength; its dominance is also a constraint.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The text's phrase "good and acceptable and perfect" (agathos, euarestos, teleios) describing God's will in verse 2 is given one sentence: "His will becomes 'good and acceptable and perfect.'" This is one of the most pastorally rich moments in the passage — the promise that the surrendered mind discovers God's will to be not onerous but beautiful, not burdensome but whole. For the hearer who is afraid to surrender because they fear what God might ask of them, this phrase is the text's answer. The sermon passes over it with a summary and misses the opportunity to address the fear of surrender directly.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Add a direct address to the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled after Point 2.** Something like: "Some of you can explain the doctrines of grace with precision. You know the difference between justification and sanctification. You know this text. And your private life — your online habits, your private thoughts, your financial priorities — has never come anywhere near the altar. The doctrine is right. The body is withheld. This sermon is for you first."

2. **Develop the Spirit's agency in transformation at the center of Point 4.** After stating "transformation is internal power," add: "And note the passive voice — *be transformed*. You do not transform yourself. The same Spirit who hovered over the waters at creation, who raised Christ from the dead, is the Agent of your metamorphosis. You are not being asked to generate change — you are being called to stop resisting the One who is already at work in you." This prevents the section from reading as moralistic effort.

3. **Trim the Epstein section by half and anchor it to the text before returning to it.** The illustration is valid, but re-anchor it with a verse before expanding: "Psalm 20:7 is not a political statement — it is a diagnostic for where your trust actually lives. Then here is what that looks like in the news cycle this week..." This keeps the text as the center of gravity and the illustration as servant, not master.

4. **Develop "good and acceptable and perfect" in Point 5.** Add: "You are afraid of the altar because you don't know what God will ask of you there. But Paul tells you in advance: what you will find on the other side of surrender is not loss — it is God's will revealed as *good, acceptable, and perfect.* The surrendered mind discovers that what God asks is not crushing — it is the only life worth living." This addresses the fear-of-surrender hearer who is not an open sinner but a hesitant saint.

5. **Add one corporate application.** Romans 12 is the opening of Paul's instruction on the body — the community of believers as a corporate sacrifice. Add one sentence after the individual application: "And this is not only personal. Paul is writing to a church. A congregation that presents its *bodies* — plural — together is a community of living sacrifices. What would it mean for this church, not just you as an individual, to be holy and acceptable to God?"

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.**

Exposition scores 20 (below the 20+ threshold for automatic concern), and Conscience scores 10/15 (above the 9-point floor). The sermon has pastoral warmth and genuine conscience-pressing throughout. The fire is present, though unevenly distributed.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
**+3/5**

The conclusion carries genuine eternal gravity — "there was no voice from heaven saying stop" is a moment of real doxological weight. The sermon does not merely inform; it moves toward worship. However, it does not quite end in worship — it ends in summons. The congregation is sent, not silenced before God. One more beat in the conclusion that allowed the hearer to rest in the cross before being sent to the mountain would have earned the full bonus.

**Total with bonus: 77 + 3 = 80/100**

**Final Score: 80/100 — Strong and faithful. Minor sharpening needed. Ready for the pulpit with targeted revisions.**
