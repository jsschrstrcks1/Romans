# The Names in the Light
## A Sermon on Romans 16:1-27

**Series**: The Night Is Far Gone
**Part V**: Walking in Daylight
**Previous**: Romans 15:14-33 (The Mission in the Early Light)

---

## Opening

Sixteen chapters.

That's how long this letter is. Sixteen chapters of the densest, most demanding theology in the New Testament. The wrath of God against all ungodliness. Justification by faith alone. Union with Christ. The sovereignty of mercy. The war inside. No condemnation. Election. Remnant. Living sacrifice. Love without hypocrisy. Submission. Liberty. Welcome. Mission.

And now Paul writes his final page.

And what does he put on it?

Names.

Not a theological summary. Not a creedal statement. Not a final warning — though he'll get to that. He writes names. Twenty-six of them. More, if you count the households. Real people. Specific people. People he knows, people he loves, people who risked their lives and wore out their hands and opened their homes for the gospel.

The letter that began with the wrath of God ends with the family of God.

And that's not an accident. That's the point. Fifteen chapters of theology — and this is what it produces. Not just doctrines believed. People changed. Communities formed. Names written down and remembered.

We are finishing our journey through Romans today. And we are going to finish the way Paul finishes — with names, a warning, and worship.

---

## People Group of the Week

Before we read, let's pause to pray for a people whose names the church has largely forgotten.

**The Tunisian Arabs** — over 12 million people living in the land that was once the heart of the early church. Tunisia is ancient Carthage. This is the soil that gave us Tertullian — the first major theologian to write in Latin, the man who coined the word *Trinity*. This is where Cyprian served as bishop and was martyred for the faith. Augustine grew up just across the border in Numidia. For centuries, North Africa was one of the most vibrant Christian regions on earth.

That is no longer true.

Today, Tunisia is over 99% Muslim. The Christian community is a tiny fraction of one percent — and most of those are expatriates, not Tunisian believers. The land that once produced some of the greatest names in church history is now a land where almost no one knows the name of Christ.

The irony cuts deep. In a sermon about names, here is a nation whose Christian heritage has been erased. The names are gone. The churches are gone. The Christ who was once proclaimed in Carthage is largely unknown in Tunis.

Would you pray with me?

*Father, you planted your church in North Africa before it ever reached most of Europe. You raised up Tertullian and Cyprian and countless unnamed saints in the land we now call Tunisia. The gospel flourished there once. We ask you to make it flourish again. Send workers. Open doors. Stir hearts. Let the name of Christ be spoken again in Carthage — not as ancient history, but as living hope. For your glory and for the sake of your name among all the nations. Amen.*

## I. The Named: The Gospel Has Faces (vv. 1-16)

Most people skim this section. Twenty-six names. Greet this person. Greet that person. It reads like the credits at the end of a movie — the part where everybody stands up and leaves.

Don't leave.

Because Paul didn't write filler. Paul wrote names. And names mean something.

In the ancient world, to be named in a letter like this was to be publicly honored. This letter would have been read aloud to the gathered church in Rome. Every name Paul wrote down would have been spoken in the hearing of the whole congregation. That is not a footnote. That is a roll call of the faithful.

And the people on this list are not the people you'd expect.

### Phoebe: The Woman Who Carried Romans (vv. 1-2)

*"I commend to you our sister Phoebe, a servant of the church at Cenchreae, that you may welcome her in the Lord in a way worthy of the saints, and help her in whatever she may need from you, for she has been a patron of many and of myself as well."*

Phoebe is the first name in the chapter. That's not random.

She is almost certainly the one who carried this letter from Corinth to Rome. The most important letter in the history of Christian theology — the letter we have spent over a year studying — was hand-delivered by a woman from a port town.

Paul calls her *diakonos* — servant. It's the same word he uses for himself in other letters. And he calls her *prostatis* — the ESV translates it "patron." She was a benefactor. She used her resources — her home, her money, her influence — to support the mission. Paul says she has been a patron "of many and of myself as well."

This woman bankrolled the gospel. And she carried it in her hands.

### Prisca and Aquila: The Couple Who Bled (vv. 3-5)

*"Greet Prisca and Aquila, my fellow workers in Christ Jesus, who risked their necks for my life, to whom not only I give thanks but all the churches of the Gentiles give thanks as well. Greet also the church in their house."*

We know this couple. Acts 18 tells us they were Jewish tentmakers, expelled from Rome when Emperor Claudius ordered all Jews out of the city. They landed in Corinth, where they met Paul and worked alongside him. Later, in Ephesus, they took aside a brilliant preacher named Apollos — a man who was eloquent and powerful in the Scriptures but only knew the baptism of John — and they "explained to him the way of God more accurately" (Acts 18:26).

They show up in Acts. In 1 Corinthians. In 2 Timothy. And here in Romans. Everywhere Paul went, this couple was either with him or ahead of him.

And Paul says they "risked their necks" for his life. The Greek is literal — they put their own throats on the line. We don't know the specific incident. But Paul does. And he hasn't forgotten.

Notice: Paul names Prisca first. In a culture where the husband's name came first by default, Paul puts the wife's name ahead — here and again in 2 Timothy 4:19. Not to make a statement about gender. Because Prisca was apparently the more prominent minister of the two. And Paul honored that without apology.

They also had a church in their house. Wherever Prisca and Aquila went, a church formed around them. Their home was not a retreat from the mission. It was the mission.

### The Women Who Worked Hard (vv. 6, 12)

Look at who Paul honors and how he honors them.

*"Greet Mary, who has worked hard for you."* (v. 6)

*"Greet those workers in the Lord, Tryphaena and Tryphosa. Greet the beloved Persis, who has worked hard in the Lord."* (v. 12)

The word is *kopiaō*. It doesn't mean "helped out." It means labored to the point of exhaustion. Toiled. Spent yourself. Paul uses this word for his own apostolic ministry elsewhere. And here he applies it to Mary, to Tryphaena, to Tryphosa, to Persis.

These women were not decorative. They were not standing at the edge of the ministry smiling. They were spent. Paul knew it. And he named them for it.

### Rufus and His Mother (v. 13)

*"Greet Rufus, chosen in the Lord; also his mother, who has been a mother to me as well."*

Here's a detail easy to miss. Mark 15:21 tells us that when Jesus was carrying the cross to Golgotha, the soldiers compelled "Simon of Cyrene, the father of Alexander and Rufus, to carry his cross."

Mark names Simon's sons — Alexander and Rufus — because his readers in Rome already knew them. They were part of the Roman church. If this is the same Rufus — and many scholars believe it is — then Paul is greeting the son of the man who carried the cross of Christ.

And Rufus's mother had been a mother to Paul. Somewhere in Paul's travels, this woman had taken care of him. Fed him. Sheltered him. Treated him like a son. And Paul remembers. He writes her name.

### Tertius: The Invisible Hand (v. 22)

And then, buried in the greetings from Paul's side, this:

*"I Tertius, who wrote this letter, greet you in the Lord."*

Tertius was the scribe. Paul dictated; Tertius wrote. Every word of Romans passed through this man's hand. And in the middle of recording someone else's theology, he slips in one sentence of his own. "I greet you too."

It's the only time in the entire letter that someone other than Paul speaks. Tertius — whose name literally means "Third" — who sat behind the scenes the whole time, writing down the most magnificent letter in Christian history, wanted the Roman church to know: I was here too. I served too. I belong to this family too.

### What the Names Mean

Church, here's what I want you to see.

Paul doesn't end Romans with a doctrinal summary. He ends it with a family portrait.

Twenty-six names. Slaves and freedmen. Jews and Gentiles. Men and women. Couples and singles. Household churches and individual laborers. People who risked their necks and people who risked their savings. People who worked to exhaustion and people who simply opened their homes.

This is what the gospel produces. Not just a theology believed — a community formed. Not just truth in the head — love in the room. Names known. Service honored. Family real.

*"Greet one another with a holy kiss. All the churches of Christ greet you."* (v. 16)

The holy kiss was a sign of family — of belonging to one another. Paul doesn't say "appreciate each other from a distance." He says greet each other the way a family greets each other. With warmth. With physical presence. With the unmistakable message: you are mine and I am yours.

Some of you are sitting in this room and you feel invisible. You serve and nobody notices. You show up and nobody asks how you are. You carry weight that nobody sees.

Paul would write your name down.

The gospel does not produce anonymous Christians. It produces named ones. Known ones. Family.

And if you've been treating the church like an audience to sit in rather than a family to belong to — this chapter is a rebuke. Paul could name twenty-six people in a church he'd never visited. Can you name ten people sitting in this room? Do you know who's exhausted? Who's struggling? Who's been a mother to someone who needed one?

## II. The Warning: Smooth Words and Flattering Speech (vv. 17-20)

Now Paul pivots. And the pivot is jarring.

He has just named twenty-six people he loves. He has called them beloved, fellow workers, fellow prisoners, kinsmen, patrons, chosen. The room is warm with affection.

And then:

*"I appeal to you, brothers, to watch out for those who cause divisions and create obstacles contrary to the doctrine that you have been taught; avoid them."* (v. 17)

After fifteen chapters building unity — Jew and Gentile, weak and strong, slave and free — Paul warns about people who tear it apart.

And look at how he describes them. Verse 18:

*"For such persons do not serve our Lord Christ, but their own appetites, and by smooth talk and flattery they deceive the hearts of the naive."*

"Their own appetites." The Greek is *koilia* — their belly. Their stomach. These people are not serving Christ. They are serving themselves. Their theology serves their comfort, their platform, their reputation. They wrap self-interest in spiritual language and call it ministry.

And their method? Smooth talk and flattery. Not crude error. Not obvious heresy. Polished, attractive, winsome deception. The false teacher doesn't walk in wearing a sign. He walks in with a smile and a compliment. He tells you what you want to hear. He scratches the itch. Paul warned Timothy about the same thing: *"The time is coming when people will not endure sound teaching, but having itching ears they will accumulate for themselves teachers to suit their own passions"* (2 Timothy 4:3).

And who do they deceive? "The hearts of the naive." Not the stupid. The naive. The unsuspecting. People who assume everyone who sounds spiritual is spiritual. People who measure a teacher by his warmth rather than his doctrine.

Some of you are listening to voices right now — online, in your earbuds, on your screens — that fit Paul's description exactly. Smooth talk. Flattering words. And you come away less sure of what the Bible says, not more. That is not growth. That is verse 18.

Now — notice what Paul says to do about it. Not "engage them in extended dialogue." Not "give them a platform so the congregation can hear both sides." Not "agree to disagree."

*Avoid them.*

That's it. Identify them. And avoid them. Titus 3:10-11 says the same thing: *"As for a person who stirs up division, after warning him once and then twice, have nothing more to do with him, knowing that such a person is warped and sinful; he is self-condemned."*

This is the hardest thing for our culture to hear. We have been trained to think that excluding anyone is the unforgivable sin. That every voice deserves a seat at the table. That tolerance is the highest virtue.

Paul says no. Some voices are dangerous. Some teachers serve their own belly. Some divisions are not honest disagreements — they are attacks on the doctrine. And the loving thing to do — the thing that protects Phoebe and Prisca and Mary and Rufus's mother and every other name on that list — is to identify the danger and shut the door.

You guard the family by guarding the doctrine. You protect the names by protecting the truth.

"But Paul — isn't that what you said *not* to do in Romans 14? Don't judge. Don't despise. Welcome the weak."

Right. And here's the difference. Romans 14 is about disputable matters — food laws, holy days, matters of conscience where the gospel is not at stake. Romans 16 is about "the doctrine that you have been taught" — the received body of apostolic teaching. The gospel itself.

There is a world of difference between a brother whose conscience disagrees with yours about a secondary matter and a teacher who is undermining the doctrine to serve his own appetites. The first one you welcome. The second one you avoid. Confuse the two and you will either split the church over nonsense or let wolves into the fold.

And then — verse 20. One of the most astonishing sentences in the letter:

*"The God of peace will soon crush Satan under your feet."*

Do you hear the echo?

Genesis 3:15. The first promise of the gospel, spoken in the wreckage of Eden: *"I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel."*

The seed of the woman will crush the serpent's head. That promise was made in a garden. And here, at the end of Romans, Paul says it is being fulfilled — through the church. The God of peace will crush Satan under *your* feet. Not an angel's feet. Not an apostle's feet. Yours.

The community that guards the gospel participates in the serpent's defeat. Every time the church identifies false teaching and refuses to let it in, the heel presses down. Every time the truth holds and the family stays intact, Eden's promise advances.

The God of peace. Not the God of niceness. The God of *peace* — the God who made peace through the blood of the cross, who reconciled enemies by absorbing the enmity in His own body. That God will crush Satan. The war is not in doubt. The outcome is certain.

And it happens under the feet of ordinary believers who love the truth enough to protect it.

## III. The Doxology: Where the Night Ends (vv. 25-27)

We've arrived.

This is the last paragraph of the letter. The last paragraph of the series. The final word of the most magnificent letter Paul ever wrote — arguably the most magnificent letter anyone ever wrote.

And it is worship.

*"Now to him who is able to strengthen you according to my gospel and the preaching of Jesus Christ, according to the revelation of the mystery that was kept secret for long ages but has now been disclosed and through the prophetic writings has been made known to all nations, according to the command of the eternal God, to bring about the obedience of faith — to the only wise God be glory forevermore through Jesus Christ! Amen."*

Read that again slowly. It's one sentence. And it carries the weight of the entire letter.

### "My Gospel"

Paul calls it "my gospel." Not because he invented it. Because it was entrusted to him. This is the same gospel he declared in chapter 1 — *"the gospel of God, which he promised beforehand through his prophets in the holy Scriptures, concerning his Son"* (Romans 1:1-3). Paul owns this gospel the way a soldier owns his orders. He didn't write them. But he will die carrying them out.

### "The Mystery Kept Secret for Long Ages"

*Mystērion*. Not a riddle. Not a secret code. A plan — God's eternal plan to save both Jew and Gentile through the death and resurrection of His Son. Hidden in the prophets. Hinted in the sacrifices. Whispered in the psalms. And now — *disclosed*. Blazing in the open. What Moses glimpsed and David sang and Isaiah ached for — the church now holds in its hands.

That mystery is what Romans has been laying open for sixteen chapters. The wrath was real — but the rescue was planned before the foundation of the world. The law was holy — but it was never the solution. The solution was a Son, sent in the fullness of time, who kept the law you couldn't keep and bore the curse you couldn't survive.

### "Made Known to All Nations... to Bring About the Obedience of Faith"

Now listen to this. Romans 1:5 — the opening of the letter — says Paul received grace and apostleship *"to bring about the obedience of faith for the sake of his name among all the nations."*

Romans 16:26 — the closing of the letter — says the mystery has been made known to all nations *"to bring about the obedience of faith."*

Same phrase. Same mission. The letter ends where it began.

Everything between those two phrases — every doctrine, every warning, every comfort, every command — serves one purpose: the obedience of faith among all the nations. Not obedience that earns salvation. Obedience that flows from faith. The kind of obedience you see in Phoebe, who carried the letter. In Prisca and Aquila, who risked their necks. In Mary and Persis, who worked to exhaustion. In Rufus's mother, who mothered an apostle.

The obedience of faith has names. It always has.

### "To the Only Wise God Be Glory Forevermore"

And the last word is glory.

Not an instruction. Not a warning. Not even a promise. Worship. Pure, unrestrained, final worship.

*"To the only wise God be glory forevermore through Jesus Christ! Amen."*

This is the second doxology in Romans. The first came at the end of chapter 11, after Paul finished the hardest section of the letter — election, sovereignty, Israel's future — and could do nothing but worship: *"Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments and how inscrutable his ways!"* (Romans 11:33).

That doxology came after theology overwhelmed the mind. This doxology comes after the gospel has filled a room with names.

Both lead to the same place: glory. When you see God's sovereignty — glory. When you see God's family — glory. When the mystery is too deep to fathom — glory. When the mystery shows up in Phoebe's hands and Prisca's scars and Tertius's pen — glory.

The only wise God. Not the only powerful God — though He is. Not the only holy God — though He is. The only *wise* God. The God whose plan was so deep that it hid in plain sight for long ages and then burst open in the person of Jesus Christ. Wise enough to save through a cross. Wise enough to build a family out of enemies. Wise enough to crush the serpent under the feet of ordinary saints.

To that God — glory. Forevermore. Through Jesus Christ.

Amen.

## What This Means for You

We've walked through Romans for over a year. And I want to leave you with this.

**If you've been coasting:**

Paul could name twenty-six people in a church he'd never visited. He knew their service, their sacrifice, their scars. What would Paul say about you? Not your attendance record. Your engagement. Are you a name in this family, or a face in the crowd?

The gospel does not produce spectators. It produces family. And family means knowing and being known. Serving and being served. Carrying and being carried.

**If you feel invisible:**

Paul saw people. He named them. He remembered who fed him, who bled for him, who wrote his letters, who opened their homes. The gospel does not produce anonymous Christians. You are not invisible to God. And you should not be invisible to this church.

If you have been serving quietly — in the nursery, in the kitchen, behind a mop, behind a checkbook — you are not less than the person on the platform. You are Phoebe carrying the letter. You are Tertius holding the pen. Your name is known.

**If you're worried about the church:**

The warning is real. There are people who cause divisions. There are teachers who serve their own appetites. And Paul says: watch out. Be wise as to what is good and innocent as to what is evil. Guard the doctrine. Protect the family.

But don't miss verse 20. The God of peace will crush Satan under your feet. The outcome is not in doubt. The church does not survive by our cleverness. It survives by God's power. And that power runs through ordinary believers who love the truth.

**If you don't know Christ:**

This whole letter has been building to one thing: the mystery, hidden for long ages, now disclosed. God sent His Son to die for sinners. Not for good people trying harder. For enemies. Romans 5:10 — *"while we were enemies we were reconciled to God by the death of his Son."*

That is the gospel. It is not advice. It is not a program. It is news — the best news in the history of the world. And it has a name on it. Yours, if you will turn from your sin and trust Christ today.

## Closing

We started this series in darkness.

Romans 1. The midnight of human rebellion. Suppressing truth. Exchanging glory for images. Given over to the consequences of our own choices. The wrath of God revealed from heaven.

And then — the alarm sounded. Romans 3: a righteousness apart from the law. Romans 4: faith counted as righteousness. Romans 5: peace with God. Love poured out while we were still sinners.

We got dressed. Romans 6: dead to sin, alive to God. Romans 7: the war within. Romans 8: no condemnation. Nothing — not death, not life, not angels, not rulers, not things present, not things to come — nothing can separate you from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord.

We saw the certainty of dawn. Romans 9: the Potter is sovereign. Romans 10: faith comes by hearing. Romans 11: the mystery of Israel and the grafting of the Gentiles. And then the first doxology — "Oh, the depth!" — because the mind could go no further and the only response left was worship.

We walked into daylight. Romans 12: a living sacrifice. Romans 13: submission before sunrise. The debt you can never repay. The night is far gone; the day is at hand.

Romans 14: the welcome that costs you nothing. Love that surrenders its rights. Romans 15: the welcome we've been given. The mission in the early light.

And now — Romans 16.

The names.

The family the gospel built. Twenty-six people — servants and patrons, prisoners and tentmakers, scribes and mothers — who gave their necks and their homes and their hands for the sake of the Name above every name.

And the only word left is glory.

*"To the only wise God be glory forevermore through Jesus Christ! Amen."*

## Benediction

Church —

The night is far gone. The day is at hand.

We have walked through this letter together — from the darkness we chose to the light we were given. From the wrath we deserved to the grace we did not. From enemies of God to children of God to names written down and remembered.

May the God who is able to strengthen you — strengthen you.

May the mystery now disclosed — the mystery of Christ crucified and risen for sinners — be the ground under your feet and the fire in your bones.

May your names be counted among the faithful — not because you were famous, but because you were spent.

And to the only wise God — through Jesus Christ — be glory forevermore.

The night is over. The names are written. Go in the light.

Amen.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Both, appropriately: the names-section turns me inward (am I a name?), the doxology turns me upward (to the only wise God). The inward turn serves the upward one.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** "To the only wise God be glory forevermore through Jesus Christ" — preached as worship, not narrated as conclusion. Yes.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Convicted ("can you name ten people?"), comforted (you are not invisible to God), moved (Tunisia, Rufus's mother, Tertius). Not merely informed.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Romans 1:5/16:26 inclusio.** "To bring about the obedience of faith among all the nations" — the letter begins and ends with the same mission. This exegetical observation ties the entire sixteen-chapter arc into one purpose, and the preacher deploys it not as a curiosity but as the key to everything between those two phrases.
2. **The Tunisia/North Africa People Group.** In a sermon about names, pointing to a nation whose Christian names have been almost entirely erased is both historically grounded and pastorally devastating. "The land that once gave us Tertullian is now a land where almost no one knows the name of Christ." That is not an illustration. It is a warning.
3. **Tertius.** "I was here too. I served too. I belong to this family too." — the scribe who slipped in one sentence while recording someone else's theology. The preacher handles it with precisely the pastoral warmth it deserves. The invisible servant in the congregation heard their name spoken.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The warning section (vv.17–20) is the least developed of the three.** It receives real treatment — *koilia*, smooth talk, the naïve, "avoid them" preached without apology, Titus 3:10–11, the distinction from Romans 14. But the Genesis 3:15 echo ("The God of peace will crush Satan under your feet") is the most theologically explosive sentence in the section and receives only two paragraphs. It deserved more.
2. **The application has no concrete story.** The Romans 14/15 sermons had Holly and Rami. This sermon has four categories but no person. One specific story — a Tertius-type servant in the congregation who has been invisible — would have made the "you are not invisible" section as powerful as the historical illustrations.
3. **The doctrinally-concerned application could be more specific.** "Some of you are listening to voices right now — online, in your earbuds, on your screens — that fit Paul's description exactly." This is accurate but general. Naming the type of voice (health-and-wealth preaching, therapeutic deism, progressive deconstruction) would make it more searchingly specific.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The sermon may not see that the warning section's hardest application is for the theologically confident — those who are certain they can always identify the false teacher. Paul's warning is for the "naive," but the theologically trained are not immune to flattery of a more sophisticated kind. A line aimed at the person who thinks they're too discerning to be deceived would close a gap.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The closing series-summary ("We started in darkness... The alarm sounded... We got dressed...") is deeply satisfying as a capstone — it earns its place. But it risks becoming the sermon's emotional climax at the expense of the doxology. The last word of the letter is "Amen" — worship. If the series-summary is the last thing the congregation feels emotionally, the doxology has been displaced. The summary should lead to the doxology, not follow it.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**The "holy kiss" (v.16).** "Greet one another with a holy kiss. All the churches of Christ greet you." The sermon notes this as "a sign of family" — but doesn't press the application: what does the holy kiss look like in this congregation today? This is the one moment in the passage that is explicitly communal and ecclesiological, and it passes relatively quickly. In a sermon about invisible servants and coasting Christians, "greet one another the way a family greets each other" had room to become a moment of real summons.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. Expand the Genesis 3:15/verse 20 section: "Ordinary believers. Faithful doctrine. Shut doors. And the heel presses down. You are not watching the battle from the stands — you are in it."
2. Add one specific Tertius-type person from the congregation's life (or pastoral experience) in the application.
3. Reorder the close: series-summary → leads into the doxology → benediction. The doxology should be the last word, not the summary.
4. Add one sentence to the warning application naming the type of voice: "The man who tells you your best days are ahead if you'll just believe harder. The teacher who makes you doubt the plain meaning of Scripture and feel sophisticated for doubting it."

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Romans 16 is a list of names plus a doxology — a genre that genuinely stresses the rubric's categories. The rubric notes that narrative and list-genre texts may stress the exposition categories differently. Here, the rubric is wisely applied: textual fidelity is measured by whether the preacher mines the names for their actual content, not by whether he produces an academic commentary. The sermon passes this test. The rubric fits.
