## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastoral Introduction That Wanders Before It Arrives — Gospel Gesture Without Gospel Proclamation**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm opening of Romans 15:14 that demonstrates genuine pastoral heart and engaging personality while failing to deliver a clear gospel proclamation, coherent expository argument, or conscience-searching application rooted in the text's own claims.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — Conditionally**

The Scopus Test is borderline. The sermon loosely orbits Paul's commendation of the Romans (goodness, knowledge, ability to instruct), and the main point — that these qualities should characterize believers today — does arise from the passage. The Authority Test is also marginally passed; the preacher does not edit or suppress the text. However, the sermon's actual center of gravity drifts far from Romans 15:14 into contemporary cultural comparison, anecdote, and Reformation trivia. The gate is passed, but the sermon nearly abandons the text shortly after reading it.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score is CAPPED at 69.**

The following fatal flaw flags are triggered:

- **Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The sermon gestures toward the gospel in its final two sentences ("it all starts with the gospel") but never actually presents it. Christ's finished work, justification, penal substitution, or saving grace is absent. The word "gospel" is invoked but its content is never proclaimed. A listener who did not already know the gospel would not learn it here.
- **Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: The closing application ("Are you choosing good? Are you spending time in his word? Are you teaching those God has put in your life?") is unspecific moral exhortation that could appear in virtually any self-help talk. No differentiated address. No one is cornered.
- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon closes with three rhetorical questions and a vague gesture toward the gospel. It never issues a direct, concrete summons: "Therefore, do this." The congregation is invited to self-reflect but never commanded to respond to anything specific.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The Gospel Is Invoked But Never Proclaimed**: The sermon ends with "it all starts with the gospel" — but no one in the room learned what the gospel is. This is the sermon's central failure. The cultural comparison in the introduction (Rome = America) sets up the need for something better, but that setup never arrives. Paul's point in Romans 15:14 is not that the Romans were good moral people — it is that they were grounded in grace, and Paul's confidence in them reflects the work of God in them. The sermon strips the theological foundation from the compliment and turns it into a behavioral checklist.

2. **The Introduction Is a Separate Sermon**: The Rome/America comparison, the Yazidi account, the Robert Kraft reference, and the prostitution statistics occupy approximately 40% of the sermon's length and do not organically connect to the exegesis of v.14 or the application that follows. This material may be appropriate as a brief contextual note, but as presented it dominates the sermon and establishes a cultural-moral frame that the text does not actually bear. The congregation is primed for a word about Rome's wickedness and America's parallels — but that is not what Romans 15:14 is about.

3. **Application Is Moralistic and Undifferentiated**: The closing application ("Are you choosing good? Are you spending time in his word? Are you teaching those God has put in your life?") is three generic moral questions that any motivational speaker could ask. No sinner is cornered by the text. No specific sin is named. No particular hearer — the backslider, the grieving, the self-righteous elder, the young person drifting — is addressed distinctly. The application leaves everyone in the same place and asks them to try harder without supplying the only power by which they can.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's most energetic section (the cultural comparison) is actually the section most distant from the text — and that its emotional weight (especially the Yazidi account) may crowd out the text's own emotional weight rather than serve it. The preacher's pastoral energy and warm personality may also mask from him how little of the text he actually opened: v.14 is cited, but its context within Paul's priestly self-understanding (vv.15-16), the strong/weak dynamic of the preceding chapters, and the eschatological mission horizon are entirely absent. He is explaining the praise without understanding why Paul gave it or what it was grounded in.

There is also a blind spot regarding the difference between moral encouragement and gospel proclamation. The sermon ends with the assumption that "relationship with God" and "the gospel" are ideas the congregation already possesses and simply needs to apply. But the text calls for proclamation, not assumption.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The extended cultural comparison (Rome/America) feels like substantial contextual grounding — the preacher clearly knows history, is aware of the culture, and draws vivid parallels. But this section is not exegetical background to Romans 15:14; it is background to Romans 1. The sermon appears to be preparing hearers for the book of Romans generally, not for this passage specifically. The confidence of this section gives the impression of thorough preparation when it is actually misdirected energy. Similarly, the McCheyney quote and the Luther quotations are interesting and well-deployed — but they are not connected to the text's argument. They decorate the sermon rather than illuminate it.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Romans 15:14 is Paul's pastoral confidence in the Romans rooted in his conviction that they are "full of goodness, filled with all knowledge, and able to instruct one another." This commendation immediately precedes Paul's description of his own apostolic calling as a priestly service (v.16 — "a minister of Christ Jesus to the Gentiles, acting as a priest of the gospel of God"). The passage is a doorway into Paul's understanding of gospel ministry, the grace that produces communities capable of mutual instruction, and the global mission horizon of Romans 15. The sermon missed the key theological question embedded in the passage: *What produces a church like this?* The answer Paul implies is not moral effort but gospel grace — and that grace is the very thing the sermon never names. The contrast between Corinth and Rome that the sermon wisely identifies (Paul praised Rome; he spent four letters correcting Corinth) is the sermon's best instinct, but it needed to answer: *Why was Rome different?* The answer is grace — and that answer is the gospel itself.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Compress the Introduction to 5 minutes; anchor it specifically to this passage's context.** The Rome/America cultural comparison can be retained briefly as a contextual note, but it should serve the text's argument. The relevant context is not Rome's general wickedness (that is Romans 1–3) but the remarkable fact that a grace-wrought community of believers existed *inside* that wickedness — which is what makes Paul's confidence in v.14 theologically stunning. Frame the introduction around that contrast.

2. **Preach the theological ground of Paul's confidence.** Why is Paul "satisfied" about them? Not because they tried hard, but because God had worked in them. Romans 15:14 is a pastoral compliment with a theological foundation. Locate it in the grace of God that Paul has been describing since Romans 1. The three commendations (goodness, knowledge, ability to instruct) are fruits of the Spirit, not human achievements. This reframes the application from "try harder" to "abide in the grace that produces this."

3. **Add a direct and full gospel proclamation before the application section.** The sermon's instinct — "it all starts with the gospel" — is exactly right. Follow through. What is the gospel? Who is Christ? What did He accomplish? Why does that matter for goodness, knowledge, and discipleship? Without this, the application is moralism with Christian vocabulary. With it, the application becomes the natural response to grace.

4. **Differentiate the application using Perkins' categories.** Address the believer who is growing ("this is the community God is building — keep going"), the backslider who once had this and lost it ("return to the Source that produced it"), the unbeliever who is hearing this for the first time ("this community exists because of grace — that grace is available to you today"), and the self-sufficient church member who is mistaking religious activity for the goodness Paul describes ("Paul is describing a heart transformation, not a behavior management program").

5. **Replace the rhetorical closing questions with a direct exhortation.** The current ending ("Can you?") is an invitation to self-evaluation, not a summons. A biblical close names the command and the resource: "Stop managing your Christianity at arm's length. Come to Christ freshly. Let the gospel do what moralism cannot. And then — because of what He has done — go and make disciples." The "therefore" must land.

The diagnosis here is not cold orthodoxy — it is insufficient depth in all categories simultaneously, combined with genuine but undisciplined pastoral warmth.

The garlic illustration and the McCheyney reference briefly open windows onto something eternal, but the sermon as a whole does not carry the weight of eternity — it carries the weight of a good man who loves his congregation and has not yet learned to let the text carry the sermon instead of his own personality.
