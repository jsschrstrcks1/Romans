# Romans 1:18-32 — "Without Excuse"

*Part I: Midnight — The Darkness We Chose*

---

## Opening

Last week we stood at the threshold.

Paul gave us the thesis of the entire letter — Romans 1:16-17. The gospel is the power of God for salvation. The righteousness of God is revealed from faith for faith. The righteous shall live by faith.

That was the promise of dawn. The announcement that light exists, that rescue is real, that power has come.

Now the lights go out.

Romans 1:18 begins the longest sustained indictment in all of Scripture. From here through Romans 3:20, Paul will systematically dismantle every defense, every excuse, every hiding place that humanity has ever constructed. And when he's finished, "every mouth" will be "stopped" and "the whole world" will be "held accountable to God" (Romans 3:19).

This is midnight. And it starts right here.

If you've brought your Bible, and I hope you have, open with me to Romans 1:18.

---

## Prayer Transition

Before we read, let me tell you about a people group we're praying for this week.

Turkey. Eighty-five million people. And less than one-tenth of one percent are evangelical Christians.

Now here's what makes Turkey remarkable. This is the land of Paul's missionary journeys. Antioch — where believers were first called Christians — is in Turkey. The seven churches of Revelation — Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia, Laodicea — all in Turkey. The Council of Nicaea, where the church defended the deity of Christ — Turkey. The Council of Chalcedon — Turkey.

Some of the most important moments in church history happened on Turkish soil. And today, the gospel light in that land has almost gone out.

That should sober us. Because it proves that what Paul describes in Romans 1 — the exchange, the suppression, the slow slide away from truth — doesn't just happen to individuals. It happens to civilizations. It happened to a land where Paul himself preached.

Pray for Turkey — that the ancient gospel would take root again in ancient soil. That Turkish believers would be raised up. That the land of the seven churches would hear the voice of Christ once more.

Let's pray.

## Introduction: Two Revelations

Notice something about the structure here. Verse 17 — the last verse of last week's sermon — says "the righteousness of God is *revealed.*" Now verse 18 says "the wrath of God is *revealed.*"

Same God. Same verb. Two revelations.

One is revealed in the gospel. The other is revealed from heaven against ungodliness.

The gospel is good news precisely *because* the wrath is real. If there is no disease, you don't need a cure. If there is no condemnation, you don't need a Savior. If there is no wrath, the cross is meaningless.

Paul understood this. He didn't start with wrath to scare people into heaven. He started with the gospel — "I am not ashamed" — and then showed why the gospel matters. The wrath makes the gospel urgent. The gospel makes the wrath survivable.

You cannot understand the glory of Romans 3:21 — "But now the righteousness of God has been manifested" — until you've sat in the darkness of Romans 1:18 through 3:20.

So let's sit in the darkness. Not because we enjoy it, but because we need to feel the weight of what Christ saved us from.

---

## I. The Wrath Revealed (vv. 18-20)

> "For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who by their unrighteousness suppress the truth."

### What Wrath Is

God's wrath is not a temper tantrum. It is not divine irritability. It is not God having a bad day.

God's wrath is His settled, holy, righteous opposition to everything that contradicts His nature. It is what a perfectly good God *must* feel toward evil. A God who looked at the abuse of children, the oppression of the weak, the corruption of justice, and felt *nothing* — that would not be a good God. That would be an indifferent God. And an indifferent God is no God at all.

The wrath of God is the other side of His love. Because He loves what is good, He hates what destroys it. Because He loves people, He burns against the sin that enslaves them.

And notice — this wrath is *revealed.* Not hidden. Not future only. Revealed. Active. Present. Paul will show us how in verses 24-32.

### What Humanity Does with the Truth

> "...who by their unrighteousness suppress the truth."

The word "suppress" means to hold down, to restrain by force. It's the image of someone pushing something underwater and holding it there.

Humanity doesn't merely ignore the truth. We *suppress* it. We know it's there. We feel it pushing upward. And we shove it back down because we don't want it.

This is not ignorance. This is rebellion.

### What Can Be Known

> "For what can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them. For his invisible attributes, namely, his eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world, in the things that have been made. So they are without excuse."

God has not hidden Himself. Creation screams Creator.

The Psalm 19 says, "The heavens declare the glory of God, and the sky above proclaims his handiwork. Day to day pours out speech, and night to night reveals knowledge" (Psalm 19:1-2).

You don't need a theology degree to see God's fingerprints. You need eyes.

Consider nuclear resonance — the speed at which atomic nuclei vibrate. If that speed changed by even a fraction — just one percent faster — every atom of carbon in the universe would become oxygen. Carbon is the building block of life. We are carbon-based creatures. A one-percent shift in nuclear resonance, and life as we know it is impossible.

Or consider where we sit in the solar system. A little closer to the sun, the oceans boil. A little farther, they freeze. A slight change in gravity, and our atmosphere is gone. A slight change in electromagnetism, and our sun tears itself apart.

One of my sister's professors once said that the more she studies science, the more she believes there must be an all-powerful God who designed everything.

She's right. And Paul says she doesn't even need the science. The evidence has been "clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world." The first humans who looked up at the stars knew. Every generation since has known.

And the verdict is absolute: *without excuse.*

No one will stand before God on the last day and say, "I didn't know You existed." Creation took that excuse off the table before you were born.

### A Critical Distinction

But here is something we must not miss. General revelation — what God shows through creation — is sufficient to *condemn*. It is not sufficient to *save.*

Creation tells you God exists. It tells you He is powerful. It tells you He is eternal. But it does not tell you how to be reconciled to Him. It does not tell you about the cross. It does not tell you the name of Jesus.

For that, you need special revelation. You need the gospel preached. You need the Word proclaimed.

This is why missions is not optional. This is why Paul says he is "under obligation" to preach (1:14). This is why we pray for unreached peoples. Because the Turks can look at the stars and know God is real — but unless someone tells them about Christ, they will die knowing God exists and never knowing how to reach Him.

Creation condemns. Only the gospel saves. And the gospel must be carried by human feet to human ears.

## II. The Exchange (vv. 21-23)

> "For although they knew God, they did not honor him as God or give thanks to him, but they became futile in their thinking, and their foolish hearts were darkened. Claiming to be wise, they became fools, and exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images resembling mortal man and birds and animals and creeping things."

Watch the downward spiral. It begins with something that doesn't look like rebellion at all.

**Step one: they did not honor Him as God.** They knew God. Verse 21 says so explicitly. The problem was never a lack of evidence. The problem was a refusal to respond to the evidence. They saw God's handiwork and chose not to give Him the honor due His name.

**Step two: they did not give thanks.** Ingratitude. It seems small. But ingratitude is the soil in which all idolatry grows. When you stop being thankful to the true God, you start looking for something else to worship.

**Step three: they became futile in their thinking.** The mind follows the heart. When the heart refuses to honor God, the mind cannot think straight. Futility sets in. Reasoning becomes circular. Arguments become elaborate justifications for what the heart has already decided.

**Step four: their foolish hearts were darkened.** Not just confused — *darkened.* The lights go out. And they don't even know the lights are out, because darkness cannot see itself.

**Step five: claiming to be wise, they became fools.** This is the cruelest irony. The further they drift from God, the smarter they think they are. Every generation that abandons God announces itself as the most enlightened generation in history. "Claiming to be wise" — while stumbling around in the dark.

**Step six: they exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images.** The exchange. The trade. The swap that no sane person would make.

John Calvin wrote that "the human heart is a perpetual factory of idols" (Institutes I.xi.8). We may not carve wooden statues. But we worship the idol reflected in a calm stream, or in a mirror. We worship comfort. We worship control. We worship reputation. We worship being right.

And every idol is a downgrade. Every exchange trades glory for ashes. You give up the immortal God and receive — what? "Images resembling mortal man and birds and animals and creeping things." The exchange always goes downward. Man. Birds. Animals. Creeping things. From the image of God to the image of a bug.

That is what idolatry does. It doesn't elevate you. It degrades you. You become like what you worship — and if you worship things that crawl on their bellies, you will find yourself on your belly soon enough.

---

## III. The Three-Fold Giving Over (vv. 24-32)

Now comes the most sobering structure in the passage. Three times God acts. Three times He steps back. And each time, the fall goes deeper.

This is not God pushing anyone into sin. This is God removing His restraining hand and letting people have what they demanded. It is the most terrifying form of judgment imaginable — not fire from heaven, not a flood, not a plague. Just God saying: *Fine. Have it your way.*

### A. "God Gave Them Up" — To Impurity (vv. 24-25)

> "Therefore God gave them up in the lusts of their hearts to impurity, to the dishonoring of their bodies among themselves, because they exchanged the truth about God for a lie and worshiped and served the creature rather than the Creator, who is blessed forever! Amen."

The first giving over. God removes restraining grace, and the lusts that were always in the heart are now unrestrained. Bodies are dishonored. Appetites run unchecked.

Notice: this happens *because they exchanged the truth about God for a lie.* The cause is theological, not merely moral. They didn't fall into sin because they lacked willpower. They fell because they traded God for an idol. The worship problem came first. The moral collapse followed.

This is the pattern Paul is establishing. And it is critical for understanding everything that follows. Every sin in this passage — every one — is downstream of a worship failure. When God is no longer on the throne of your heart, everything below the throne is in disorder.

And notice Paul's eruption of praise in the middle of the indictment: "who is blessed forever! Amen." Even while describing humanity's worst rebellion, Paul cannot mention the Creator without blessing Him. The glory of God is not diminished by man's rejection of it. God remains blessed. God remains God. Our rebellion changes us, not Him.

### B. "God Gave Them Up" — To Dishonorable Passions (vv. 26-27)

> "For this reason God gave them up to dishonorable passions."

The second giving over. Deeper still. The passions themselves become disordered. What was designed by God for one purpose is redirected toward another.

Paul describes the sexual dimensions of this disorder — passions that exchange what is natural for what is contrary to nature. The text is plain. I will not soften it, and I will not weaponize it.

Here is what we must understand about this passage:

**First, this is not the only sin in the list.** If you stop here, you have misread the passage. Paul is building a case that climaxes in verses 29-31 with a vice list that includes gossip, boasting, disobedience to parents, and faithlessness. Every person in this room will find themselves somewhere in that list. This passage is a mirror, not a window. It is about *us*, not *them.*

**Second, the point is the progression, not the catalog.** Paul is tracing the consequences of the exchange in verse 23. When the Creator is replaced by the creature, *everything* downstream becomes disordered — bodies, passions, minds, relationships, society. Sexual sin of every kind — heterosexual and otherwise — is a *symptom* of a worship problem. The disease is idolatry. The symptoms are legion.

**Third, truth and compassion are not opposites.** There are people in this room who carry real pain connected to these verses — pain on every side of the question. Some carry guilt. Some carry confusion. Some carry years of being bludgeoned with this text by people who used it as a weapon rather than a diagnosis.

Paul does not use this text as a weapon. He uses it as evidence in a courtroom. And the defendant is not one group of sinners. The defendant is *all of humanity.* That includes every person in this room.

The question Paul wants you to ask is not "How bad are *those* people?" The question is: "What have I exchanged the glory of God for?"

### C. "God Gave Them Over" — To a Debased Mind (vv. 28-31)

> "And since they did not see fit to acknowledge God, God gave them over to a debased mind to do what ought not to be done."

The third giving over. And this is the deepest. The mind itself is corrupted. The faculty God gave us to reason and discern — the very thing that separates us from the animals we were worshiping in verse 23 — is now broken.

A debased mind. The word in Greek carries the sense of something that has been tested and found worthless — like a coin that's been weighed and found to be counterfeit. The mind that refuses to acknowledge God becomes a mind incapable of thinking rightly about anything.

And then comes the list. And this list is the part that should terrify every person in this room:

> "They were filled with all manner of unrighteousness, evil, covetousness, malice. They are full of envy, murder, strife, deceit, maliciousness. They are gossips, slanderers, haters of God, insolent, haughty, boastful, inventors of evil, disobedient to parents, foolish, faithless, heartless, ruthless."

Twenty-one sins. And they are not exotic. They are ordinary.

Covetousness — wanting what isn't yours. You've done that this week. Envy — resenting someone else's blessing. Strife — stirring up conflict. Deceit — shading the truth. Gossip — talking about people instead of to them. Boastful — making sure everyone knows how good you are. Disobedient to parents — dishonoring the people who raised you. Foolish. Faithless. Heartless. Ruthless.

When you look at yourself in the mirror of this passage — apart from Christ — how do you look?

Jesus said in Matthew 5:21-22: "You have heard that it was said to those of old, 'You shall not murder; and whoever murders will be liable to judgment.' But I say to you that everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment."

Anger is the seed. Murder is the fruit. And most of us have the seed growing in our hearts right now.

This list is not describing the worst people you can imagine. It is describing humanity apart from the restraining grace of God. It is describing what we all are, at root, when God lets go.

### D. The Final Indictment (v. 32)

> "Though they know God's righteous decree that those who practice such things deserve to die, they not only do them but give approval to those who practice them."

This is the bottom.

It is one thing to sin. It is another thing to approve of sin in others. The first is weakness. The second is advocacy. The first falls short of the glory of God. The second redefines glory to accommodate the fall.

When a culture not only tolerates what God condemns but *celebrates* it — when approval becomes the expectation and disapproval becomes the offense — then the spiral described in this passage is not hypothetical. It is complete.

And notice: "they *know* God's righteous decree." They know. They are not ignorant. They suppress what they know because the truth is inconvenient, and the lie is comfortable.

This is where Paul began in verse 18 — suppressing the truth. And this is where he ends. The chapter opens with suppression and closes with approval. The truth has not merely been pushed down. It has been replaced. The conscience has not merely been ignored. It has been inverted.

---

## Closing: The Catastrophe and the Cure

Let me tell you where we are.

Paul has just described the human condition apart from God. Total rebellion. Total suppression. Total exchange. And a God who, in judgment, gives people over to what they demand.

This is the catastrophe. This is midnight. And if this passage were the end of the story, there would be no reason to get out of bed in the morning.

But remember where we started last week:

> "For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes."

You don't need power for a scrape. You need power for a catastrophe.

Romans 1:18-32 is the catastrophe. The gospel is the power.

The same God whose wrath is revealed in verse 18 has a righteousness revealed in verse 17. The same God who gives people over in judgment is the God who gave His Son in mercy. The same God who lets the darkness fall is the God who planned the dawn before the world began.

This indictment is not the end. Romans 3:21 is coming — two chapters from now — and it begins with two of the most important words in the Bible: "But now."

*But now* the righteousness of God has been manifested. *But now* apart from the law. *But now* through faith in Jesus Christ for all who believe.

The "but now" is coming. The dawn is coming. The power of God for salvation is real.

But you can't appreciate the dawn if you've never felt the dark. And tonight, you've felt it.

So what do you do with this passage?

You don't use it to condemn the person next to you. Paul will deal with that in chapter 2: "Therefore you have no excuse, O man, every one of you who judges. For in passing judgment on another you condemn yourself, because you, the judge, practice the very same things" (Romans 2:1).

You don't use it to despair. The wrath is real, but the gospel is real too — and the gospel is *power.*

You use it the way Paul intended. As a mirror. You look into it, you see what you are apart from Christ, and you run — not away from God, but *to* Him. To the cross. To the blood. To the righteousness that is not your own but is freely given to everyone who believes.

The night is far gone. The day is at hand.

And the God who planned the cross before the foundation of the world — who grew the tree and nurtured the soldiers and forged the iron — that God is not done with you.

He never was.

Let's pray.

### Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Both — and in the right order. The sermon cornered me first (the mirror), then pointed me to Christ (the cure). The self-examination serves the gospel, not the ego.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Encountered God — His holiness, His wrath, the terrifying mercy of "God gave them up." The eruption of praise mid-indictment is a moment of genuine encounter.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Convicted. The vice list personalization and the "mirror not window" framing are genuinely searching. The closing comforts — but only after the conviction has landed.

---

### Strengths (Top 3)

1. **"Mirror, not window" reframing** (Section III.B and Closing) — "This passage is a mirror, not a window. It is about *us*, not *them*." This single pastoral move prevents the self-righteous from weaponizing the text and forces every hearer into the indictment. It is the sermon's most important interpretive decision, and it is exactly right.

2. **The six-step downward spiral** (Section II, vv.21-23) — The progression from "did not honor" to "did not give thanks" to "futile in thinking" to "hearts darkened" to "claiming to be wise" to "the exchange" is a structural observation that arises from the text and preaches with devastating clarity. Step two — ingratitude as the soil of idolatry — is a pastoral insight that will change how people think about thankfulness.

3. **Vice list personalization** (Section III.C) — "Covetousness — wanting what isn't yours. You've done that this week." This transforms a catalog into a confrontation. The preacher walks through the list and makes it impossible for anyone in the room to remain a spectator. This is conscience-level application at its best.

---

### Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Gospel compression** — The indictment runs from the introduction through Section III.D — roughly 220 lines. The gospel turn occupies roughly 40 lines in the closing. The ratio is appropriate to the text (which is itself an indictment), but the gospel relief could be expanded without violating the passage. Specifically: the closing mentions "the cross" and "the blood" but does not unpack *how* the cross answers the specific indictment Paul has just delivered. How does the cross answer the exchange of verse 23? How does Christ's righteousness answer the debased mind of verse 28? Connecting the cure to the specific disease would strengthen the gospel section without adding length.

2. **Christ-connection could be more organic** — Christ appears primarily in the closing as the answer to the problem. But the text itself contains seeds of Christology: the "image of God" language in verse 23 echoes Colossians 1:15 (Christ as "the image of the invisible God"). Humanity exchanged the glory of the immortal God for degrading images — Christ *is* the image of the invisible God, the glory they traded away. This connection would make Christ present throughout the indictment, not just in the remedy.

3. **The "what does running to Christ look like?" gap** — The exhortation is clear: run to Christ, not away. But the sermon doesn't describe the road. What does a person who recognized themselves in the vice list *do* on Monday? Repent of what, specifically? Confess to whom? Pursue what? The direction is right; the specificity is missing.

---

### Blind Spots

The sermon handles the sexual ethics passage (vv.26-27) with remarkable pastoral wisdom — truth and compassion held together, the passage placed within the larger argument, the "not the only sin in the list" qualifier applied correctly. But the pastoral care shown to those "carrying pain connected to these verses" may inadvertently soften the text's own force. Paul's argument is not that homosexuality is *one sin among many* (though it is) — it is that disordered sexuality is a *particularly visible marker* of the exchange, placed between the first and third giving-over as the centerpiece of the spiral. The sermon's pastoral instinct to prevent weaponization is right. But the text's own emphasis on this particular disorder as *emblematic* of the exchange could be handled more explicitly without losing the pastoral tone.

---

### Illusion of Strength

The Turkey prayer section *feels* like it carries missional weight — and it does. But it may also function as emotional distance from the text. By beginning with a foreign nation's spiritual darkness, the hearer has a moment to think, "How tragic for *them*" before Paul's indictment arrives. The connection ("what Paul describes in Romans 1 doesn't just happen to individuals — it happens to civilizations") partially closes this gap, but the hearer's first instinct is still to look outward. The mirror doesn't arrive until later.

---

### Missed Opportunity

**The "image" language of verse 23** — "exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images resembling mortal man." This is loaded with Christological potential that the sermon does not tap. Christ is "the image of the invisible God" (Colossians 1:15), "the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature" (Hebrews 1:3). Humanity exchanged the glory of God for degrading images — and in Christ, God gave back the true Image. The exchange of verse 23 is answered by the incarnation. Naming this would make the gospel present in the indictment itself, not just in the closing.

**Verse 25's "who is blessed forever! Amen"** — The sermon notes Paul's eruption of praise but does not develop it. This is a remarkable moment: Paul is cataloging humanity's worst rebellion, and he *cannot mention the Creator without blessing Him.* This could have been developed into a meditation on God's impassibility — human rebellion does not diminish God's blessedness. He remains who He is. Our sin changes us, not Him. The sermon touches this ("God remains blessed. God remains God. Our rebellion changes us, not Him") but could have pressed harder.

---

### Surgical Fixes

1. **Connect the gospel cure to the specific disease.** In the closing, after "To the cross. To the blood," add: *"The cross answers the exchange. You traded the glory of God for images — and God gave you back His glory in Christ, who is the image of the invisible God. You were given over to a debased mind — and Christ renews the mind by His Spirit. The specific disease Paul diagnosed has a specific cure. And the cure is not moral improvement. The cure is a Person."*

2. **Develop the exhortation with specificity.** After "you run to Him," add: *"And running looks like this: you confess what the text exposed. You name the idol — the comfort, the control, the approval you've been worshiping. You bring it to the cross and leave it there. And you ask the God who gave you over to give you back — to restore the mind, the affections, the worship that idolatry corrupted."*

3. **Tap the Christological potential of verse 23.** When expounding the exchange — "exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images" — add a forward-pointing note: *"Hold this in mind. Because Paul will show us that the answer to the exchange is not better willpower. It is a better Image. Christ is the image of the invisible God — the glory they traded away, given back in flesh and blood."*

4. **Consider repositioning the Turkey prayer.** The prayer is excellent, but its placement before the text may allow the hearer emotional distance. If the prayer came *after* the indictment — "This is what happened to Turkey. A land where Paul preached. And it can happen here" — the mirror would be sharper.

### Weight of Glory Assessment

This sermon carries the weight of judgment and mercy held together. God's wrath is terrifying not because the preacher sensationalized it but because the text itself is terrifying and the sermon let it speak. The closing — "that God is not done with you. He never was" — lands with the force of grace arriving after a long night. The sermon leaves the hearer wanting mercy, which is exactly where Paul wants them before Romans 2.

### Rubric Fit Note

Romans 1:18-32 is an indictment passage — its genre naturally weights toward exposition of sin, not gospel proclamation. The rubric's Gospel Centrality category (15 pts) is slightly strained by a text whose purpose is to establish the *need* for the gospel rather than to *present* it. The lower gospel score (11/15) reflects a real compression, but the rubric should acknowledge that this text's own purpose is to make the gospel urgent, not to deliver it. Paul himself doesn't present the gospel in these verses — he presents the catastrophe. The sermon follows Paul's own strategy. The gospel compression is a legitimate critique but should be weighed against the text's genre.
