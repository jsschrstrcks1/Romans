## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastoral Warning on a Real Danger — Floating Free of Its Text**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A well-intentioned address on racial equality and cultural Marxism that assembles a useful collection of proof texts but fails to preach Romans 10:12-13 as a sustained argument, functioning more as a topical lecture with biblical garnish than a text-driven sermon.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**RESULT: CONDITIONAL PASS — WITH CAUTION**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — that Christ transcends all racial and ethnic categories, and that cultural Marxism's identity-based hierarchy is anti-biblical — is loosely consistent with the implication of Romans 10:12-13. However, the passage's own argument in context (Paul's anguish over Israel's rejection of Christ; that the gospel is for all who call; that the same Lord is Lord of all who call) is not exposited. The text is used as a launching pad, not a governing argument.

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text's conclusions but does not submit to the text's own argument. The sermon's main point could have been assembled without Romans 10:12-13 as the anchor. Romans 10 is about the universality of the gospel call in the context of Israel's stumbling — Paul's passion is for Jewish and Gentile salvation, not primarily a corrective to intersectionality. The passage is conscripted into a contemporary argument rather than allowed to set the agenda.

**The gate passes narrowly** on the basis that the theological conclusion drawn (no distinction between Jew and Greek; Christ is Lord of all) genuinely arises from the text's language. But the passage's own burden — the urgency of gospel proclamation to all nations — is largely absent. The sermon arrives at the text's destination by a different road.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG 1 — TRIGGERED: Main point does not fully equal text's point.**
Romans 10:12-13 in context is about the universal availability of salvation through calling on Christ — set within Paul's burden for Israel's salvation and his argument that the gospel crosses all ethnic lines precisely because *calling on the name of the Lord* is the only criterion. The sermon uses the "no distinction" language but strips it of its soteriological urgency. The text is about the gospel; the sermon is primarily about ideology.

**FLAG 2 — TRIGGERED (PARTIAL): No sustained gospel presentation.**
The gospel appears at the end (vv. 69-71), but it is brief, transitional, and not woven into the body of the sermon. For a passage whose central claim is that "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved," the mechanics and miracle of that salvation are conspicuously underdeveloped.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

**Weakness 1 — The Text is Never Preached**
Romans 10:12-13 is the stated text, but the sermon never exposits it. The passage is quoted twice — once at the beginning and once in the assembled proof-text section — but its own argument is never opened. Why does Paul say "no distinction between Jew and Greek" here? What is the preceding argument (10:5-11)? What is the chain of logic Paul is building (hearing → faith → calling → salvation)? What does "calling on the name of the Lord" mean in the context of a Jew who has the law but has not confessed Christ? None of this is addressed. The text decorates the sermon rather than generating it.

**Weakness 2 — The Gospel is Structurally Marginalized**
The sermon spends approximately 80% of its body on cultural analysis and perhaps 20% on biblical truth and gospel call. For a passage that climaxes with "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved," the salvation dynamics — the urgency of the call, the sufficiency of Christ, the necessity of faith, what "calling" means — receive almost no space. The Synagogue Test is not passed: a devout Muslim who opposes identity politics on principle could affirm most of this sermon's argument without confronting the offense of the cross.

**Weakness 3 — Hearers Are Not Differentiated**
The sermon addresses its congregation as a single unified group: theologically-aligned conservatives who need to be confirmed in their opposition to CRT. No address is made to the person who has experienced genuine racial injustice and may have been drawn toward CRT as a result. No address is made to the person who harbors private racial contempt — which is the actual sin the text's principle ("no distinction") would confront. No address is made to the unconverted who may be in the room. The closing invitation appears but is not prepared for by the sermon's body.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see that the sermon is functionally more comfortable to its congregation than confrontational. The cultural critique of CRT lands easily on a conservative Southern Baptist audience — it is the expected conclusion. But Romans 10's actual confrontation runs in a different direction: Paul's anguish is for the *lost*, and the "no distinction" principle is in service of *universal gospel urgency*, not primarily in service of anti-racism argument. The sermon has been unconsciously shaped by what the congregation already believes, rather than by what the text is actually doing.

There is also a blind spot regarding the legitimate concerns underneath CRT that the sermon does not acknowledge — the reality of historical racial injustice and the genuine suffering of image-bearers. The sermon is correct that CRT's framework is unbiblical, but it does not demonstrate that the preacher has *felt* the weight of racial suffering before critiquing its ideological response. This will limit the sermon's reach with any hearer who has experienced genuine racial harm.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Greg/Dottie illustration feels like the sermon's analytical backbone — it makes the argument clear and accessible. But this is precisely where the sermon's structural problem is most visible: the clearest, most developed argument in the entire sermon is about book selection, not about Christ. The illustration is doing more rhetorical work than the text. When the most memorable and persuasive section of a sermon is a thought experiment about fictional authors, the gospel has been structurally displaced.

Similarly, the assembly of proof texts (Gal 3:26-29, Col 3:11, Acts 10:34-35, Deut 10:17) feels comprehensive and may create an impression of biblical saturation. But proof-texting is not exposition. Assembling verses that confirm the conclusion is not the same as preaching through a passage's own argument. The biblical material is real, but its arrangement is more like a legal brief than a sermon.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Romans 10:12-13 contains one of the most powerful missionary statements in the New Testament — and it sits immediately before the sending passage: "How then will they call on him in whom they have not believed? And how are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard? And how are they to hear without someone preaching? And how are they to preach unless they are sent?" (vv. 14-15). The sermon opens with a people group that has never heard the gospel. It closes with an invitation to come to Christ. But it never makes the connection that the *reason* "there is no distinction between Jew and Greek" is that *all* of them are under the same urgent necessity of hearing and calling — and that the church's response to cultural tribalism is not merely a correct worldview but a missionary mandate. The Dhund Abbasi opening and the Romans 10 text were perfectly positioned to unite around this — and the opportunity was not taken.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3-5 Specific)

**Fix 1 — Anchor the introduction in the text's own argument before introducing CRT.**
Begin by opening Romans 10 in context: Paul's anguish for Israel (9:1-3), the universality of the gospel call (10:12-13), and the sending passage (10:14-15). Establish what the text is *doing* before bringing in the cultural application. This makes the text the lens rather than the decoration.

**Fix 2 — Preach the gospel mechanics from the text itself.**
"Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved" — what does *calling on the name* mean? What does it require? What does it cost? This is the text's own gospel invitation, and it deserves a full movement in the sermon. Move from law (all have sinned, there is no distinction in guilt as well as grace) to gospel (but the same Lord is Lord of all, bestowing riches on all who call). The current sermon gives the grace side without the guilt side.

**Fix 3 — Add a direct address to at least two distinct hearers: the person who harbors racial contempt, and the person who has been harmed by racial injustice.**
"Some of you have carried a contempt for people who don't look like you — and you've dressed it up in conservative politics, and it's still racism before God." And: "Some of you have been genuinely hurt. You've felt the weight of being seen as less. The answer to that pain is not an ideology that simply flips the hierarchy — it's a Savior who stands at the bottom with you." Both addresses are available from this text. Neither is currently present.

**Fix 4 — Connect the Dhund Abbasi opening to the Romans 10:14-15 sending passage at the close.**
The opening people group and the text's own missionary logic are a natural pair. Close not just with "where is your citizenship?" but with "and what are you doing about the Dhund? Because the same Lord who is Lord of all is waiting for someone to go." This unifies the sermon's disparate elements and gives the exhortation specific missional content.

**Fix 5 — Tighten the CRT explanation section by one-third.**
The Greg/Dottie illustration is effective but overlong. The intersectionality explanation (p. 45) is thorough — perhaps too thorough. The congregation needs enough to understand the problem, not a graduate seminar. Cut to the essential claims, spend the recovered time on the biblical text.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Romans 10:12-13 is an epistolary text in the middle of a dense theological argument (Romans 9-11). The rubric fits well — this passage invites exposition, gospel proclamation, and applicatory force. The genre does not strain the rubric; the strain comes from the fact that the sermon chose to use the passage as a topical proof text rather than preach it as an argument. The rubric is appropriate and well-matched to this text and preaching context. No genre accommodations required.
