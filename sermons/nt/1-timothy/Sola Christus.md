### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see the sermon as incomplete because the live delivery presumably continued beyond where the manuscript ends. The energy and care in the introduction suggest that the actual three-office treatment was preached extemporaneously or from notes not captured in this document. But the manuscript as submitted represents only the preparatory material.

The preacher may also not see how the extended anti-Catholic polemic (while doctrinally necessary) shapes the sermon's tone in a way that could feel more polemical than pastoral to the congregation. Not everyone in the room has a Catholic background requiring correction; the positive case for Christ's offices deserves as much energy as the negative case against their corruption.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Reformation vocabulary (Sola Christus, munus triplex, the five solas) creates the impression of theological depth. And the content is theologically sound. But the theological vocabulary is being used in the introduction — preparing to discuss the three offices — rather than actually developing them. The sermon *feels* substantive because the concepts are real; it is not yet substantive because the concepts are announced but not opened.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The JC Ryle quote at the opening — "Christ's cross and Christ's crown are the clue we must hold fast" — was the seed of the entire sermon and was not developed. A sermon on Sola Christus that built from Christ as the interpretive key to all Scripture, then developed each of the three offices from specific texts (Isaiah 61:1 for prophet; Hebrews 4:14–16 for priest; Psalm 2 or Revelation 19 for king), would have been exceptional. The architecture is present in the Ryle quote; the building was never constructed.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Assign a governing text and build from it.** Options: Isaiah 61:1 (prophet), Hebrews 4:14–16 (priest), or Psalm 110 (all three offices in one passage). The sermon's content should arise from the text, with the Reformation historical argument as supporting material.
2. **Complete the three-office section.** Each office needs at minimum: (a) what the role is; (b) how Christ fulfills it uniquely and finally; (c) what it means for the congregation right now. The prophet section especially needs to develop Christ as the final Word of God (Hebrews 1:1–2).
3. **Proclaim the gospel from within the three offices.** As priest: Christ offered himself as the sacrifice. "We needed a priest who would not just offer the sacrifice — he had to become the sacrifice." As king: Christ has defeated our enemies. "The war is over. Our king has won." As prophet: Christ is the final word. "He doesn't just speak for God — he is the Word of God made flesh."
4. **Shorten the Catholic polemic to one-third its current length.** The points about saintly intercession, priestly absolution, and church-administered grace can each be made in two sentences. The current treatment takes 60% of the sermon's space.
5. **Add application differentiated by hearer.** After the three offices: "If you have never come to Christ, the prophet is still speaking — hear him today. If you are a believer carrying guilt, the priest has already made the sacrifice — your debt is paid. If you are afraid of the future, the king has already won — you are on the winning side."
