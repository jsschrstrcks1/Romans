# The Hand That Won't Let Go
## A Sermon on 2 Chronicles 15

---

## Introduction

Our passage tonight is one that is sometimes twisted to try and show salvation can be lost. You guys are smarter than that though, and I know you know that some things had to happen in the Old Testament that were superseded by different things in the New. Just like we no longer have to sacrifice animals to cover our sin. Christ was sacrificed to remove our sin, and now we are to present our bodies a living sacrifice holy and acceptable to God. But aren't you glad you don't have to keep sheep just so your sins are covered? What happens if you had the flu the day you were supposed to make your sacrifice and died without having made it? The laws were copious and complex. The requirements were firm and unbending. The law was a harsh and brutal task mistress.

Likewise in the Old Testament economy of salvation there were other differences. God was in a way trying to teach humankind to look to him. He doesn't normally turn to force against an individual's free will, but he certainly has that within the scope of his power. Instead he prefers to use a carrot and stick approach. If you follow me you will be blessed, and if you rebel, there are punishments. This is pretty thoroughly illustrated in our text tonight. A people are given a set of options. Follow God, and have peace. Ignore God or worship other gods and have trouble. As has been illustrated in passage after passage in the Old Testament, God's punishments are severe, and his blessings are immeasurable.

Let's read 2 Chronicles 15.

## Scripture Reading

> The Spirit of God came upon Azariah the son of Oded, and he went out to meet Asa and said to him, "Hear me, Asa, and all Judah and Benjamin: The LORD is with you while you are with him. If you seek him, he will be found by you, but if you forsake him, he will forsake you."
> — 2 Chronicles 15:1-2 (ESV)

---

## 1. Old Testament Salvation

So in the Old Testament we actually have a few different models. Or you might say we have two models, one that was there right from the beginning, and the other that continued to develop across the Old Testament. Let's talk about the second one first. That of course is the sacrificial system. Instituted by God with Adam and Eve, we see this was already common practice by the time of the story of Cain and Abel. That's why the one killed the other, remember? One ignored the parameters of God in his arrogance and pride. The other obediently, lovingly gave his best. Little surprise one was rejected while the other embraced. In jealousy the rejected brother killed the brother God embraced. Humanity is in its infancy, and yet already we have people killing people. Humankind's depravity is on full display. In the human economy of evil, we generally put murder near the top of the list of evil things one person can do to another. God sees sin somewhat differently. But regardless, it's a heinous thing.

But this model continues to be developed. Turned out there were a lot of things that mankind could do that would displease God. The Old Testament has a total of 611-613 laws depending on how you want to count them. There were laws that governed ceremony, laws that governed their politics, their morality, diet, and a ton of things in between. God as our Father had to spell out for us in great detail exactly what we had done wrong as a people.

My nephew is like that. I had a conversation with him the other day. He had used one of his sister's toys. He played with it, I knew he had played with it. He left it out when he was done, and I knew only he had used it that particular day. He was home sick. There were no other kids home. Yet when I asked him about the toy, his response was "it's Lilly's toy." As though that made a difference as to whether or not he should pick up the toy. So I asked him again if he had used the toy. He looked me in the eye and said "But it's LILLY'S toy!" He declared it emphatically. Like that absolved him of having taken it out of where it belonged, played with it, and left it carelessly out. I had to sit there and in super careful, super explicit terms explain to him that because he had used the toy, not Lilly, that he was responsible for picking it up, and putting it away when HE was done with it. Even so, he said "but it's not mine!"

Mankind at this point in our history, and even still today, sometimes wants the rules spelled out in incredible detail. Moses' law was an example of that. God speaking through Moses spelled out 613 things that we must do — some timeless laws that ought to still be followed, and others were laws that applied to that time period alone. Things like the politics, or the dietary prohibitions have passed away, but others — the moral law remains constant. As far as I can tell all of the moral law of the Old Testament is repeated in the New. The ceremonial law, political law, and dietary law were not, while the dietary law was explicitly revoked.

The moral law is things like not killing, no raping, not stealing, being sober — which means not getting drunk, but I believe also means not imbibing substances that alter our mental state recreationally. Medicine prescribed by a doctor is one thing. Taking drugs for recreation is a whole different thing. If it alters your state — alcohol, pot, or worse — you're not sober, it's a sin. Anyway. Moral law covers a bunch of things. Breaking any of the laws in any of these categories was enough to require you to make a sacrifice. The sacrifice didn't remove your sin. It was still there. It was still on your record. But it covered it. Something innocent, and clean and good must be put to death. Must have its life blood poured out. Must cease to exist so that sinful man can have some of their sins covered. The death of the innocent was supposed to be heinous to us. It was supposed to be expensive, it was supposed to make us sick to our stomach to see something clean and unblemished, something untarnished and whole have to die to cover our sins. The whole experience is supposed to be repulsive, to remind us how repulsive our sin is. Our sin is so repulsive to God, and he loved us so much that he was willing to send his Son to die on the cross for us. We're going to come back to that, but for now let's look at the second way of OT salvation.

### Salvation by Faith

The second way of salvation in the Old Testament is quite similar to ours today, and yet in the Old Testament economy, people largely didn't follow it. Many perhaps thought they were. But what we have in the New Testament is a couple of statements indicating there were precious few that actually followed it. In fact I can only think of a few people. Still it was the result of God's work in their heart and life that they were able to. I say this regularly, but it's worth mentioning again — at the moment of salvation, what is man's contribution? Jonathan Edwards said the only thing we contribute to our salvation is the sin that makes it necessary. So we know it's the renewing work of God in the lives of these men that allowed them to follow this second way. Abraham was one of those few men. Paul tells us in Romans 4:

> For if Abraham was justified by works, he has something to boast about, but not before God. For what does the Scripture say? "Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness."
> — Romans 4:2-3 (ESV)

Paul is of course quoting Genesis 15:6. Let me set the stage for that a little bit. Abraham is walking around and all of a sudden God appears to him and promises him offspring, and a land. Wealth is implied as well.

The hardest part for Abraham to believe would have been the barrenness. Being barren as a couple was seen as a sign of condemnation from God. Imagine that for a moment — what would it be like to believe the God you believe in had condemned you, and so as a result you were childless? I think that is a bitter pill. Imagine going through life thinking God had condemned you, but choosing to follow him anyway? That would take faith! God recognizes this as well:

> And he believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness.
> — Genesis 15:6 (ESV)

There are precious few of whom this can be said. But this is a glimmer of what was to come. It's a tiny match at the top of the hill proclaiming that one day there would be another way to get to God, to be reconciled to God.

## 2. New Testament Model

Things as you know change somewhat in the New Testament.

The New Testament points back to the Old a bunch of times illustrating the futility. It is repulsive that something innocent and pure has to die on our behalf, and not just once and done. It was a thing that had to happen over and over and over and over and over in a person's life. It was a lifestyle. It cost a small fortune, and was generally an unpleasant experience. I read that during some of the big events where everyone offered a sacrifice, as many as a quarter million sheep might be sacrificed in Jerusalem. Maybe a half million birds. Millions of grain offerings. Yet the terrifying cost in life, and treasure and innocence merely covered the sin of the Old Testament individual.

It's sort of like buying a used car. Have you ever bought a used car? Used car sellers seem to try and cover up all the problems. Or an ad for a house. They do the same thing with houses. For instance, if they say it's "cozy," or a "condo alternative," you know it's going to be tiny! Or if they say the house is "as is" or for "investors" you know it's going to be a money pit — something that's going to need a lot of work. They cover the faults of the house or car with spin in order to deceive us, or keep the wool over our eyes until we've already bought the item. God wasn't deceived of course, but he chose to overlook any shortcoming covered by the proper sacrifice. Really all of this was a foreshadowing of what was to come. In the past many sacrifices were necessary to have an imperfect solution for sin. In the New Testament it only took one sacrifice to have a perfect solution for sin. In the Old Testament the sacrifice was moderately priced. In the New Testament the sacrifice was priceless. In the Old, the sacrifice was offered at a prescribed pace, and in the New, the sacrifice was once and for all.

Likewise in the Old Testament if an individual stopped or turned away from God, and the sacrificial system, they would be subject to the wrath of God unless they turned back to him.

### The Hand That Won't Let Go

That's a vital difference. Because in the New Testament God took some extra measures in order to ensure our salvation. You may have heard it called "once saved always saved" or sometimes called the preservation of the saints. Either way it's fundamentally the same thing. I need someone to come up here for a moment. In the Old Testament our salvation was at least partially dependent upon our own ability to cling to God. Fail to do so and doom would find you. Our passage this morning says if you seek God you will find him, but if you forsake him, he will forsake you. It's like this *(grasp person's arm)*. In the Old Testament, if you got sidetracked, or forgot, or had your head turned by some piece of wood, and decided to let go of God, to stop following and seeking him as you should, it was like letting go of God, forsaking God, and he would forsake you. In the New Testament *(have person grab me back)*, if you are truly a believer in Jesus Christ, it's not just dependent upon you. Jesus helps. Even when I let go, who's still there holding on?

> My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand. My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all, and no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand. I and the Father are one.
> — John 10:27-30 (ESV)

So put another way, if you are in God's hand, how would you get out? You would basically have to be stronger than God to escape his hand. But is anyone stronger than God? Certainly not. That means if you truly became a Christian, no matter what evil you do, or think of, if you were truly saved before, you will remain so. Like this person holding onto my arm — I'd have to be stronger than them to get loose, wouldn't I? But we have already established no one is stronger than God.

> Now to him who is able to keep you from stumbling and to present you blameless before the presence of his glory with great joy.
> — Jude 24 (ESV)

God's wrath was satisfied by the death of his Son. The cosmic exchange was made. The righteous for the unrighteous. In light of the price his perfect Son paid for me, and for you, God is going to keep us from falling, so that we can be presented to him one day — the precious gift paid for with the blood of his Son. On that day, we won't be that battered used car, or house whose problems are covered with clever spin. On that day we will be presented blameless and without fault. It will be a day of great joy.

## 3. Some Things Remain the Same — Who Are You?

**Lost person, at enmity with God.** Some people fall into this category. If you have never come to a point in your spiritual life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins, the time is now. Today is the day. The Bible says seek God, and he will allow you to find him. You might say it this way: seek God, and he will find you. I can't tell you how many times I've heard stories of people who were lost in sin, separated from God, and they prayed "God, if you're real..." God hears those prayers. He frequently responds to those prayers. Test him in this. Seek him, and you will find him.

**Believer who's backslidden.** Some people fall into a second category. Some people have prayed that prayer to follow Christ, and then returned — Scripture says like a dog to vomit. Some of you in this room know exactly what I mean. You've lived it. God freed you from something, and yet you run right back to it. It's like my dog. I gave her a bath the other day. She had started to smell like some kind of weird blend of gym socks and Doritos. So I bathed her, and she smelled a lot better. But as soon as I let her out in the yard she promptly found a mud hole, and rolled in it. Then she found something dead, and rolled in that too. Within an hour she stank more than she had before the bath. But I took my pup back in and cleaned her up again, and so will God with you. Maybe you've been away for a time, maybe you've been rolling in the filth of the world, but God's right there waiting to take you back. Some people argue that if you're truly a follower of God, truly a believer, he will keep you from wandering too far. So if someone starts backsliding too far, it's possible they aren't saved. I'm okay with that line of thinking as long as they keep it at "possible." We can't see the deep recesses of people's hearts. We can't know the thoughts of someone's head. But we don't have to. God can and does, and he KNOWS you. In any event, salvation in the New Testament begins with the gospel.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Pastoral Warmth Without Exegetical Ground: A Topical Survey Using 2 Chronicles 15 as a Launching Pad

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A conversational and warmly pastoral treatment of perseverance and Old/New Testament salvation that largely abandons the text of 2 Chronicles 15 in favor of a theological survey the passage does not itself generate.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**FAIL — Partial.**

The Scopus Test: The sermon's main point — that New Testament believers are held by God in a way Old Testament believers were not, making their salvation eternally secure — does not arise from 2 Chronicles 15 itself. The text is Azariah's prophetic word to Asa calling for national covenant fidelity. The text's own main point is a call to seek the LORD with urgency, illustrated by the history of Israel's apostasy and restoration. The sermon uses vv.1-2 as a springboard to depart the text almost entirely and deliver a theological lecture on dispensational differences in salvation. 2 Chronicles 15 is never opened, never worked through, never allowed to speak on its own terms.

The Authority Test: The preacher does not stand above the text as an adversary, but he does stand largely beside it — treating the text as an occasion rather than an authority. The passage's own solemn warning ("if you forsake him, he will forsake you") is addressed only to defuse it, not to receive it.

> Verdict: The main point of the sermon does not arise demonstrably from the main point of the passage. The sermon proceeds anyway. Evaluation continues, but the score is capped accordingly, and this structural problem governs all downstream assessment.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 — Main point does not equal the text's point.** The sermon derives its central argument from John 10, Jude 24, and Romans 4 — not from 2 Chronicles 15. The passage is listed in the header but functionally abandoned.

**Flag 4 — Application is significantly generic.** The applicatory section ("Lost person," "Believer who's backslidden") uses broad categories but never presses the conscience with specificity native to the text or the congregation's actual condition.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The sermon closes mid-sentence ("salvation in the New Testament begins with the gospel") without completing the thought, delivering a clear call, or summoning the congregation to any specific belief, repentance, or obedience. The manuscript appears to be incomplete.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 9 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity*: 3/10. Only vv.1-2 of 2 Chronicles 15 are read, and even those are used as a foil rather than a text. The rest of the chapter — Azariah's historical survey (vv.3-7), Asa's reform (vv.8-15), the great covenant assembly, the oath and the joy — receives no treatment whatsoever. The passage is named but not preached.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 2/5. There is no awareness of the book of Chronicles' purpose (encouraging the post-exilic community toward covenant fidelity), no awareness of Asa's specific reign, no connection to the Chronicler's theology of seeking and forsaking. The passage is stripped of its canonical context.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 2/5. No Greek or Hebrew engagement. No explanation of what "forsake" means covenantally. No treatment of what "seeking God" meant in its historical context.
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 2/5. The sermon imports the New/Old Testament contrast from outside the text. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is present implicitly — human inability to maintain covenant fidelity — but it is never named from the text itself. It floats without exegetical anchoring.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness*: 5/7. The move to Christ is real and genuinely grounded in the sacrificial typology. The cross is presented with some gravity: "The righteous for the unrighteous." The penal substitution language is present though underdeveloped. The typological connections (sacrifice → Christ) are legitimate, not manufactured.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness*: 2/3. There is genuine awareness of the two-testament structure, though it is applied too schematically. The sermon treats the difference as "Old = conditional, New = unconditional" without nuance — ignoring that Abraham's faith-righteousness (which the sermon cites) was also unconditional.
- *Doctrinal Precision*: 3/4. The perseverance argument is substantively correct and well-illustrated. The illustration (holding arms) is memorable and theologically functional. However, the statement "two models of Old Testament salvation" is problematic — it implies Abraham was saved by a different mechanism (or perhaps by the sacrificial system in addition to faith), which is not confessionally precise. The "carrot and stick approach" applied to God's providential dealings is imprecise framing for covenantal blessing/curse structure.
- *Confessional Alignment*: 2/4. Perseverance of the saints is affirmed clearly. However, the framing of Old Testament salvation as primarily national/conditional and New Testament salvation as individual/unconditional oversimplifies. The Covenant of Grace is not articulated. The statement "God doesn't normally turn to force against an individual's free will" edges toward open theism framing rather than Reformed anthropology.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel*: 3/5. The gospel is present: Christ's death as substitutionary sacrifice, justification by faith (Romans 4), God's wrath satisfied. But it is embedded in a broader theological survey rather than proclaimed with clarity. An unbeliever tracking the first half of this sermon would not find a clear path to Christ until the second half.
- *Necessity of Christ*: 4/5. Christ is necessary here — the argument literally depends on the distinction between Old and New covenant arrangements, and Christ is the hinge. This is genuine.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity*: 2/5. The cross appears but does not carry sermonic weight. The Jude 24 quotation produces the most Christ-centered moment, but the "battered used car" analogy dilutes rather than deepens the gravity of what Christ accomplished.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration*: 2/5. The sermon speaks warmly and personally, but it does not press on specific sins or specific failures. The uncle/nephew illustration is charming but does not corner the hearer. The sermon tells people *about* sin more than it exposes the sin *in them*.
- *Specificity*: 3/5. The "Lost person / Backslider" distinction is a genuine attempt at differentiation. The dog illustration for the backslider is vivid. But neither category is pressed with specificity — what does backsliding look like in *this* congregation? What specific sin is the comfortable believer excusing?
- *Spiritual Differentiation*: 2/5. The sermon reaches two of Perkins' seven categories (the unbeliever and the backslider). The knowledgeable-but-unhumbled, the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, the growing believer, and the self-righteous are entirely unaddressed.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 2 / 8**

- *Call to Response*: 1/4. The sermon ends mid-thought. The lost person is told "the time is now" but this is never completed with a clear gospel presentation or a specific call. The backslider is told God is waiting — but what must the backslider *do*? No concrete call is given.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness*: 1/4. There is no "therefore go" moment. The sermon does not send anyone anywhere. The congregation is warmed but not commissioned.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5 / 9**

- *Clarity of Outline*: 2/4. The three-point structure (OT Salvation / NT Model / Some Things Remain) is traceable on paper but loose in execution. Section 1 wanders through Cain and Abel, the 613 laws, the nephew illustration, Abraham's faith, and OT sacrifice in a way that a listener would struggle to follow.
- *Progression*: 2/3. There is movement from Old to New, which provides some forward momentum. But the progression runs off the rails when the manuscript appears to end abruptly.
- *Unity*: 1/2. The sermon attempts to do too much: (1) explain OT sacrifice, (2) explain OT faith-righteousness, (3) compare both testaments, (4) defend perseverance of the saints, (5) apply to the lost, (6) apply to backsliders. These compete rather than unify.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3 / 5**

- *Relevance*: 1/2. The nephew illustration is personal and accessible. The dog illustration lands well. The used car/real estate analogy is creative but diminishes the gravity of atonement — comparing God's treatment of covered sin to a deceptive home seller is theologically unfortunate.
- *Clarity*: 1/2. The arm-holding illustration for perseverance is the sermon's clearest and most effective moment — it is embodied, memorable, and doctrinally precise.
- *Memorability*: 1/1. The arm-holding demonstration will be remembered. The used car analogy may also be remembered, for the wrong reason.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3 / 5**

- *Reads as Preaching*: 2/2. This is clearly written for oral delivery, not for print. The direct address, the conversational tone, the rhetorical questions, and the congregational asides all read as spoken word. This is a genuine strength.
- *Conviction on the Page*: 1/2. The conviction is present but uneven — strongest at the perseverance section, absent at the conclusion.
- *Cadence and Breath*: 0/1. The sermon ends without cadence. There is no climax, no compression, no final note. The manuscript appears incomplete.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Partially Christ. The perseverance section and the Jude 24 quotation point genuinely toward Christ's sufficiency. But significant portions of the sermon are occupied with OT procedural description (laws, sacrifices, categories) rather than Christ himself.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas about Him. The sermon explains God's arrangements across redemptive history, but God himself — his holiness, his grace, his searching gaze — is rarely felt as present. The arm-holding illustration is the closest the sermon comes to making the doctrine *feel* real.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Informed, and partially comforted (for the believer concerned about perseverance). The conscience is not searched. No one is cornered before the cross.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Perseverance of the Saints, Embodied.** The arm-holding object lesson (Section 2, "The Hand That Won't Let Go") is the theological heart of the sermon and its most effective moment. It takes an abstract doctrine and makes it physically felt. The John 10:27-30 and Jude 24 couplet is well-selected and placed. This section alone justifies the sermon's title and its central pastoral aim.

2. **Accessible, Pastoral Voice.** The sermon is written in a genuinely conversational register that real people can follow. There is no performative scholarly vocabulary, no hedge-laden abstractions. The preacher sounds like a pastor who knows his congregation. The nephew illustration and the dog bath illustration are specific, warm, and recognizably human.

3. **Sacrificial Typology Handled with Genuine Weight.** The description of the Old Testament sacrifice — "something innocent, and clean and good must be put to death... to remind us how repulsive our sin is" — carries real theological substance. The emotional load placed on the sacrifice-as-costly is doctrinally sound and preached with appropriate gravity.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **2 Chronicles 15 Is Not Preached.** The passage is named, two verses are read, and it is never opened again. Azariah's speech (vv.3-7), the covenant renewal assembly (vv.8-15), Asa's removal of the idols, the oath of the people, the joy of the assembly — none of it is touched. The text's own theological richness (the Chronicler's theology of seeking/forsaking, the communal nature of covenant, the pattern of reform and renewal) is entirely ignored. A listener gains nothing from this text that they could not have gained from a systematic theology lecture.

2. **The Sermon Is Incomplete.** The manuscript ends mid-section, mid-thought. The applicatory third section ("Some Things Remain the Same") opens two categories and stops. There is no gospel presentation, no completed call to the lost, no exhortation to the backslider, no conclusion, no doxology. Whatever was preached beyond this manuscript is not captured here, and the evaluation is necessarily incomplete as a result. As written, the sermon does not reach any ending.

3. **Theological Imprecision on Old Testament Salvation.** The sermon's framing — "two models of Old Testament salvation" — implies that some OT believers were saved by sacrifice and others by faith, which is not confessionally accurate. The Westminster Standards and the 1689 are clear: there is one Covenant of Grace, administered differently across the testaments, but the ground of salvation for Abraham and for the Apostle Paul is identical — the righteousness of Christ received by faith. The sacrificial system was never a mechanism of salvation; it was a typological, pedagogical institution pointing forward. The sermon treats it as a functional salvation mechanism, which is a meaningful doctrinal error.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher does not appear to see that he has abandoned his text. The sermon *feels* text-driven because it references a passage and quotes the Bible frequently — but the passage selected is never actually opened. There is a conflation of "using Scripture" with "preaching a text." This is perhaps the most common failure in conservative evangelical preaching: the Bible is present everywhere as decoration and is absent as authority. The preacher clearly loves Scripture and knows it. What he lacks is the discipline of staying in the passage, letting the text itself generate the sermon's structure, and trusting that 2 Chronicles 15 has enough in it to preach a full sermon on perseverance, covenant renewal, and the grace of God.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The doctrinal content on perseverance is correct and the illustrations are vivid — this combination creates the impression of a sermon landing well. But this strength masks a significant structural problem: the sermon could have used any passage (or no passage) to make this same argument. When John 10, Jude 24, and Romans 4 are doing all the theological work and 2 Chronicles 15 is merely the occasion, the sermon has traded textual authority for topical convenience. The hearer feels taught but is not being formed by this particular word of God to this particular people.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

2 Chronicles 15:12-15 is one of the most joyful corporate covenant renewal scenes in the Old Testament: "They entered into a covenant to seek the LORD, the God of their fathers, with all their heart and with all their soul... And all Judah rejoiced over the oath, for they had sworn with all their heart and sought him with their whole desire, and he was found by them." This is a picture of a whole people, in gladness, binding themselves to the God who had already bound himself to them. It would have been the perfect biblical image to anchor the sermon's central claim about perseverance — not because it proves the doctrine directly, but because it shows what it looks like when a people are seized by the grace of God and respond with whole-hearted covenant commitment. The joy of verse 15 could have been the sermon's emotional apex: *this is what it looks like when God's hand won't let go and the people run to him rather than from him.* That moment was left untouched.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3-5)

1. **Rebuild the sermon from 2 Chronicles 15 itself.** Work through the passage in sequence: Azariah's word (vv.1-7), Asa's response (vv.8-11), the covenant assembly (vv.12-15), the ongoing reform (vv.16-19). The doctrine of perseverance can still be the sermon's destination — but it must arrive there by allowing the text's own logic to carry it. The question the text itself raises is: *What does it look like to seek God, and what is the fruit of seeking him?* That question, answered from the text, lands exactly where this sermon wants to go — only with more authority.

2. **Correct the "two models of OT salvation" framing.** Replace with: one Covenant of Grace, administered through types and shadows in the Old Testament and fulfilled in Christ in the New. Sacrifice was never a mechanism of salvation — it was a typological institution pointing forward to Christ. This is not a minor distinction; it shapes how the entire OT/NT contrast is presented.

3. **Complete the exhortation.** The manuscript appears to end mid-sermon. The call to the lost must include a clear gospel presentation: what Christ has done, why it is sufficient, how one receives it by faith. The call to the backslider must include a specific summons: *return now, this week, today.* A sermon without a completed exhortation is a sermon without its spine.

4. **Add differentiated application to at least one more category of hearer.** The sermon addresses the lost and the backslider. What about the believer who is in the hand of God but has never felt the grip — who is anxious about their standing, who doubts their own election? This person needs to hear that the question is not the quality of their grip but the strength of the One who holds them. This application is native to the text's argument and costs almost nothing to add.

5. **Replace the used car analogy with something that honors the weight of atonement.** God's overlooking of covered sin is not comparable to a deceptive seller concealing defects. The analogy, however well-intended, trivializes both God's holiness (as if he is "fooled") and the seriousness of sin. A better image: on that final day, we will not arrive battered and hiding our defects — we will arrive clothed in a righteousness not our own, because everything wrong with us was laid on another. The contrast is not between covered flaws and exposed flaws; it is between our condemnation and Christ's righteousness imputed to us.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered in its classical form.** The sermon is not doctrinally cold — it has pastoral warmth, personal illustration, and genuine affection for the congregation. What it lacks is not warmth but *textual depth*. The dead orthodoxy diagnostic fires when correct doctrine is delivered without pastoral searching power. This sermon's problem is different: incomplete doctrine delivered with pastoral warmth, but without the authority that comes from remaining in the text.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement

The sermon carries a genuine pastoral heartbeat — the preacher wants his people to know they are held — but the eternal gravity that comes from standing under the specific Word of God to this specific people is absent, because the sermon never fully submits to the text's own authority; it borrows the text's weight without bearing it.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

2 Chronicles 15 is Old Testament narrative with an embedded prophetic speech — a genre that rewards careful attention to the Chronicler's theological shaping of Israel's history. The rubric's exposition categories apply fully and are not strained by the genre. What is strained is the sermonic approach itself: the preacher treats an OT narrative as a proof-text occasion rather than as a text in its own right. The rubric is not bending here — the sermon is avoiding the text. A skilled preacher of OT narrative will find that the Chronicler's theology of "seeking and finding" is one of the richest veins in all of Scripture for preaching perseverance, covenant renewal, and the grace that meets the returning sinner. This passage deserved a sermon, not a platform.
