Thankfulness is hard when you're down. But it's appropriate.

I heard a story this week about a pastor whose little two-year-old daughter was diagnosed with terminal leukemia. Of course that reminded me of Bob Clark and his son Rob, who faced a terminal diagnosis of leukemia in Rob. If you know Bob, you know that God spared Rob, and he went on to get a Master's degree in counseling, and he's practicing in Texas. That story could have gone wildly differently but for the grace and mercy of God.

Likewise this little girl was diagnosed with terminal cancer, but at a much younger age — two years old. Worse, normally you have your immune system to help fight cancer, but in this case the baby was born with the cancer already growing in her little body. This meant that perhaps his wife had leukemia as well, and just hadn't discovered it yet. Testing was done the same day, while they were sitting there weeping with their little toddler given this death sentence. Come to find out, the mother had it too. Both were going to be a difficult match for donor marrow, both needed the same type. No one in their family was a match — none of the other four children, nor the brothers or sisters or parents were suitable donors. They dosed both with chemotherapy. They dosed both with radiation, trying to extend their lives long enough to find a match. Even with a match they were told that if both lived another five years, both would most likely make it an additional fifteen, short of some other tragedy happening. The chemo had limited effect. The radiation seemed to slow it, but not stop it. Things kept deteriorating.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5) — For When the Complete Manuscript Is Submitted

1. **Cite and open a text immediately upon returning to the pulpit after the narrative.** The congregation has been led into suffering. They need the Word now. The text should do the theological work of grounding thankfulness — whether Philippians 4:6–7, 1 Thessalonians 5:18, Habakkuk 3, or whatever text governs this sermon.

2. **Resolve the narrative before leaving it.** The sermon ends mid-story. The congregation deserves to know what happened to the pastor's daughter and her mother. If the outcome is tragic, that is precisely the territory where the gospel must be most powerful. Don't leave the wound open without the medicine.

3. **Turn the illustration inward.** At some point, the sermon must ask: *What about you?* Not "What about this pastor's family?" but "Where in your own life is thankfulness hard right now?" The submitted fragment keeps the suffering at a narrative distance. The sermon must eventually close that distance.

4. **Ground thankfulness in the gospel, not in outcomes.** The greatest danger in a sermon on thankfulness is allowing the conclusion to be: "God may heal you, like Rob Clark." The theological foundation for thankfulness in suffering is not "God might fix it" — it is "Christ has already borne the worst thing, and nothing can separate you from his love." Make sure the gospel provides the *basis* for thankfulness, not just the *hope* of relief.

5. **Distinguish hearers.** Who in the congregation is going through something like this? Who is grateful easily and needs to be deepened? Who has lost someone and cannot find gratitude? The sermon's setup lends itself to addressing the grieving specifically — don't let the application remain generic.
