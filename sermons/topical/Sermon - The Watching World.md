# The Watching World
## A Sermon on Nehemiah 5

---

## Opening

I read a story in high school — I don't know that I've ever forgotten it.

A boy was born in Africa. One night, a neighboring tribe attacked his village and kidnapped him along with everyone else they could take — men, women, boys, and girls. Those they didn't want to keep for themselves they sold. The boy ended up in Europe. He became a believer in Jesus Christ. And eventually he traveled back to Africa to tell his people what he had learned.

*[See end notes — this account closely matches the biography of Samuel Ajayi Crowther. Naming him would strengthen the illustration considerably.]*

Something very similar is happening in our passage tonight. Nehemiah 5.

---

## The Situation Inside the Wall

Now — slavery in America and Europe was different from slavery in biblical times. A slave in the biblical world was, at bottom, forced servitude — but there were standards enforced. They had to be fed. They couldn't be abused. They could purchase their freedom. It was also easier to end up in that situation. If you didn't pay your debts, your creditor could require you to work for them until the debt was cleared. Aren't we glad that doesn't happen today?

Here's the situation in brief.

They had the city back. But they didn't necessarily control the lands around it — not enough to feed everyone. And it had been an all-hands-on-the-wall situation to get this far. Those who had fields had been building, not farming. The wheat, grapes, and oil crops had gone untended.

What developed out of that was two groups of people. On one side: those who had resources in reserve — including people kept back from the wall to tend their fields. On the other side: people who had obeyed Nehemiah and put everything into the wall, and who were now facing hunger in a few months.

The wealthy Israelites with food in reserve were making predatory loans to the ones who didn't have any. When those loans reached a certain point, they were selling people — sometimes whole families — to their enemies. Nehemiah, meanwhile, was out spending his own money buying people back from those same enemies. But the enemies knew he would pay. So they were charging him forty or fifty percent above what they'd paid. They knew Nehemiah was wealthy. They knew he'd do what he had to do.

What the wealthy Israelites were doing, then, was essentially cutting off their nose to spite their face. If they had gone to Nehemiah directly, he probably would have paid the debt rather than buy the person back at a premium from an enemy — and lose a good worker in the meantime.

One commentator described this as a "Gotrocks family" situation. I didn't know what that meant, so I looked it up. In his 2004 annual shareholder letter, Warren Buffett described a hypothetical family — the Gotrocks — who happened to own all the companies in America. Each member could have simply collected their dividends and been content. Instead, some of them hired helpers to assist them in trading with each other. Others hired more expensive helpers with fancier titles. The cycle kept going, and before long a significant portion of their collective income was going to the helpers. They were trading their income and their people away in pursuit of security — and losing all three things.

This made Nehemiah angry.

---

## Righteous Anger — and How to Handle It

I don't know about you, but sometimes I get angry too. Anyone else?

We had a situation not long ago where a man we'd known for a while made a mistake — and instead of owning up to it, he tried to cover it up and even asked for extra money for tasks he'd been hired to complete. All of this happened on the day my dad had his heart surgery. I wanted to be at the hospital, but I stayed home to make sure this situation got handled. I'd love to tell you his goading didn't get to me, that I sat there and handled it like Christ. But I can't. I definitely lost my temper. I didn't curse at the man. My message was simply: *You promised A, B, and C. If you want to be paid for your work, you'll do A, B, and C.* Which he did not like. He argued and threatened and swore a pile of lemons tasted like lemonade, and I wouldn't give him an inch. Well — I gave him about six inches. But you understand me.

I think I would have been better off reaching a compromise, or calling him directly on his mistake, or taking a breath and letting things settle.

Nehemiah took a wiser course. Look at verse 6:

> I was very angry when I heard their outcry and these words.
> — Nehemiah 5:6 (ESV)

Nehemiah was angry. That part's the same. Here's where it diverges. Verse 7:

> I took counsel with myself, and I brought charges against the nobles and the officials. I said to them, "You are exacting interest, each from his brother." And I held a great assembly against them and said to them, "We, as far as we are able, have bought back our Jewish brothers who have been sold to the nations, but you even sell your brothers that they may be sold to us!" They were silent and could not find a word to say.
> — Nehemiah 5:7–8 (ESV)

He didn't go running his mouth to his friends. He didn't vent on anyone. He took a minute. He collected himself. He took counsel with himself. And then he confronted it head on — publicly, directly, without apology.

And they had nothing to say.

Then verse 9:

> So I said, "The thing that you are doing is not good. Ought you not to walk in the fear of our God to prevent the taunts of the nations our enemies?"
> — Nehemiah 5:9 (ESV)

His response to the sin of his countrymen — sin against their own people and against God — was to point them back to the fear of God. And to remind them: *the world is watching.*

I say this often, but it bears repeating. I have this mental picture of people marching blindfolded toward a cliff. We who believe in Christ can see where they're headed. Ought we not to be running around pulling off blindfolds? Instead we sometimes sit on the sidelines and argue about things that won't matter when we get to heaven.

---

## The SBC and the Watching World

The SBC held their annual meeting not long ago, and one of the things they addressed was the alt-right — a movement that essentially affirms white supremacy. It's a relatively small group with relatively little public influence, from what I can tell. It's not something I had heard of before this meeting. It's not something we encounter on a daily basis. But then, we're not Black — so it may be something we simply can't see from where we stand.

There was a group that was very vocal — if not particularly articulate — about the evils of this movement. They demanded, in a letter that wasn't exactly a model of clarity, that the denomination affirm once more that we stand against this form of evil. Because of the way it was written, and because of how few people had even heard of the alt-right, there was debate about whether to pass the resolution at all.

It is evil. And I'm glad the resolution passed. But we spent something like twenty-four hours talking about what it meant, what it said about the denomination, on and on. Couldn't we have better spent that time making much of Christ? Telling people they're wearing a blindfold and walking toward a cliff?

Danny Akin, the president of the seminary I attend, put it plainly: "We stand steadfastly against any and every form of racism, bigotry, and racial supremacy no matter where that may come from and no matter who may express it. If we don't, we have no reason to expect the blessing of God." *[See end notes on this quotation.]*

I'm glad we got it right in the end. Our system is messy, but it works.

---

## The Taunts of the Nations

Nehemiah's final warning in verse 9 is worth sitting with: *the nations are watching.* What would you do if you found out that one careless action on your part — witnessed by a lost friend — became their excuse for not coming to Christ, and as a result they spent eternity in hell?

When I was at Trinity, one of the papers I wrote was on obstacles Muslims face in becoming Christians. One of the things I found is that Muslims frequently say that if they had ever met a real Christian, they might have been convinced. Making it worse — in many places, their primary experience of Christians is what comes out of Nashville and Hollywood.

Britney Spears is a perfect example. She's faded from the headlines now, but when she first started her career she talked regularly about wanting to serve the Lord through her music and bring glory to his name. A few years later, she was doing something very different. The music videos, the scandals, the public breakdowns. At her lowest point, I found out she had been asked not to come to parties by other music stars because of her constant inappropriate behavior. But for years, she was perceived as a Christian ambassador. When Muslims thought about what a Christian looked like, they pictured her.

The Israelites were living their own version of this. Not necessarily to that extreme — but in their own way, they were a sign of contradiction. These loans they were making stripped the borrowers of the use of their land or their collateral until the debt was repaid. Think about what that meant. You're a farmer, your family is starving, you take a loan — and now you can't use your farm. You're functionally homeless. Maybe you have some money for a while to feed the kids. But eventually the money runs out, and in that kind of desperation, selling your child to a slaver starts to look like a mercy.

This wasn't a foreign empire doing this to them. This was Israelites doing it to each other. It was a sign of their unbelief. Just as our actions can be a sign of ours.

If someone came to you and said *let me tell you about my Jesus* — and then stole your credit card. How would that make you feel about Jesus and Christians? What if they cheated on their spouse and were telling you about how Jesus had made them a better person? What if they were abusive to their children and talking about God's love? What if they were telling you about the holiness of God while dropping curse words? Would any of that convince you?

Or would it push you further away?

The world watches how you treat people. Especially when they know you're a Christian. Are you living your life in such a way that what you do obscures Christ?

I think it's an easy trap to fall into. I think I may have fallen into it with the man who installed our drain field.

Look over your life for a minute. Are you treating others fairly — in your finances, in your dealings, in your words? Or are you saying you follow Christ with your lips and failing to follow him with your actions?

---

## Closing

Nehemiah's confrontation called the people back. Away from abusing each other. Toward a more godly way of life.

In a moment, when the praise team comes up to lead us in the final song — if you realize there are areas in your life that need work, come to the altar. We have one. If you think you don't need any work, that's pride, and a good thing to repent of as well.

**Opening story — Samuel Ajayi Crowther:**
The account in the notes — kidnapped by a neighboring tribe, sold into slavery, transported to Europe, became a Christian, returned to Africa as a missionary — closely matches the biography of Samuel Ajayi Crowther (c. 1809–1891). Crowther was seized in an 1821 Fulani slave raid on his Yoruba village in present-day Nigeria. He was sold and eventually rescued at sea by a British anti-slavery patrol, taken to Sierra Leone and later to England, where he was educated and baptized. He was ordained as an Anglican priest and later consecrated as the first African Anglican bishop. He returned to Nigeria and spent his life as a missionary to the Yoruba and along the Niger River. Naming him from the pulpit would give the illustration considerably more weight — and honor him appropriately. Verify the specific story the pastor read in high school before preaching to confirm it is Crowther's account.

**Gotrocks / Warren Buffett:**
The "Gotrocks" parable appeared in Buffett's letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders for fiscal year 2004, published in early 2005. Citing it as "his 2005 shareholder letter" is consistent with how it is commonly referenced. ✓ The illustration is real and verifiable.

**Britney Spears:**
Corrected from "Brittany Spears" and "Britany" throughout. Correct spelling: Britney Spears.

The specific interest percentage (mentioned in the application section) is not stated in the text of Nehemiah 5. These figures are presented as illustrative, not as historical data from the text. If more specificity is desired, Nehemiah 5:11 mentions "the hundredth" — interpreted by many as 1% per month (12% annually), which was considered usurious under Mosaic law. The higher figures in the notes appear to be rhetorical amplification.

**SBC Annual Meeting dating:**

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Instinct Without Gospel Architecture — A Faithful Conscience-Sermon Undermined by an Absent Christ**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and practically honest opening of Nehemiah 5 that faithfully presses the conscience on the watching world's judgment of Christian witness, while failing to ground that press in the gospel and leaving Christ functionally absent from both the diagnosis and the cure.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS (conditional)**
The sermon's thesis — that God's people must live in the fear of God because the watching world judges the credibility of their witness by their conduct — does arise from Nehemiah 5:9. The main idea is traceable to the text.

**Authority Test: PASS**
The preacher does not soften or edit the text's demands. He submits to Nehemiah's confrontation and allows it to confront the congregation. No obvious editorial softening of the hard parts is present.

Berean Gate: **PASS** — but the passage opens into far more than the preacher walked through.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG 1: No clear gospel presentation — TRIGGERED**
Christ's finished work is not proclaimed in this sermon. The name of Jesus appears twice in passing — "a boy who became a believer in Jesus Christ" and "let me tell you about my Jesus." Neither is a proclamation of the gospel. There is no mention of sin before God, atonement, justification, the cross, or faith. The sermon calls people to the altar to repent of failing to "follow Christ with your actions" — but Christ himself is never explained, his work never named. The call to ethical reform floats free of any gospel foundation.

**FLAG 2: No actual exhortation — PARTIAL TRIGGER**
The closing contains a call to the altar, which is a form of response. However, the altar call is moral rather than gospel-shaped: "if you realize there are areas in your life that need work, come." This is a call to self-improvement, not a summons to the cross. The exhortation does not tell the congregation what to believe, what Christ has done, or what repentance toward God looks like.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Primarily myself. The consistent frame is "how my conduct affects the watching world." Christ is named but not magnified. The dominant concern is Christian reputation, not Christ's glory — though the preacher appears to intend both.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas about how to behave. The fear of God is named (v. 9 is quoted), but God himself — his holiness, his covenant love, his wrath and mercy — is not encountered in this sermon. He is background.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Partially convicted. The contractor story and the direct questions in the application section produce genuine conscience pressure. But the conviction is free-floating — there is no gospel to run to, no cross to fall before. Conviction without gospel is a knife with no sheath.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Pastoral self-implication.** The contractor story (the day of the father's heart surgery) is genuinely vulnerable. The preacher does not arrive at the application clean — he admits his own failure first. This earns the congregation's trust and opens the conscience for what follows. This is Puritan-style self-application before other-application, and it is well-executed.

2. **Specificity of application.** "Are you treating others fairly — in your finances, in your dealings, in your words?" is more specific than most application sections achieve. The Britney Spears illustration, whatever its other limitations, makes the "watching world" motif concrete and culturally real rather than abstractly theological.

3. **Correct handling of Nehemiah's model of anger.** The contrast between Nehemiah's self-counsel (v. 7) and the preacher's own loss of temper is honest and exegetically legitimate. The text does model a righteous pattern for handling anger — and the sermon identifies it accurately. Verse 9's pivot to "the fear of God" as the frame for ethical conduct is handled with appropriate weight.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Christ is absent.** Not diminished — absent. The altar call at the close invites people to repent of "areas that need work." Repentance toward whom? Through what? On what basis? The name of Jesus appears in two passing references and neither is proclamation. A sermon that calls the congregation to Christian conduct without the gospel has handed them a standard without a Savior. This is not a minor omission — it is the structural failure of the sermon.

2. **The fear of God is invoked but not explained.** Verse 9 — "Ought you not to walk in the fear of our God?" — is the sermon's climactic verse, and the preacher quotes it. But what is the fear of God? How is it produced? How does the gospel deepen it rather than eliminate it? The text offers the preacher a genuine theological hinge, and the sermon passes over it in favor of the "watching world" application. The watching world is the consequence; the fear of God is the engine — and the engine receives no fuel.

3. **Nehemiah 5:14–19 is entirely missing.** Nehemiah's own renunciation of the governor's food allowance — his personal financial sacrifice that grounds his moral authority — is the passage's climax and its most powerful illustration. The sermon uses Nehemiah as a behavioral model without showing what made him that model: a man who feared God more than he feared poverty, and who bore the cost of others' welfare himself. This is not only a missed homiletical opportunity — it is an incomplete exposition of the text.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that **"the watching world" has become the sermon's controlling concern rather than the fear of God.** The text makes the watching world a secondary reason ("to prevent the taunts"); the primary reason is that what they were doing was "not good" before God. The sermon inverts this priority — it motivates ethical conduct primarily through external witness concern rather than through covenant obligation and the fear of the Lord. The result is a subtle but significant shift from theocentric to missional-pragmatic motivation.

Additionally, the preacher may not notice that **the sermon's application asks for behavior change without gospel resources**. Asking people to act like Christians without giving them Christ is the engine of moralism — even when the preacher is sincere, warm, and personally humble.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal vulnerability (the contractor story, the SBC section) creates the impression of pastoral depth and searching application. It is genuine — the preacher is not performing humility. But it may mask the absence of gospel architecture. A sermon can feel prophetic because it is honest about the preacher's own failures, while still being functionally moralistic in its structure. The congregation may leave feeling the sermon "got them" — without knowing that what they needed most (Christ and his cross) was never offered. The warmth of self-disclosure is real; the gospel beneath it is missing.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Nehemiah 5:9 and the fear of God as the only sufficient motivation for ethical conduct.** The text hands the preacher one of the most powerful arguments in the Old Testament for a gospel-grounded ethic: it is not reputation management, not social pressure, not even love of neighbor that finally produces just dealing — it is the fear of God, which in the New Testament is inseparable from the love of Christ who bore our judgment. The watching world cares not because PR matters, but because God is watching too — and his people's conduct either adorns or obscures his name. This could have been the gospel hinge that moved the sermon from ethics to worship. It was passed over for the Britney Spears illustration.

Additionally, **Samuel Ajayi Crowther** — kidnapped, enslaved, redeemed, ordained, sent back — is one of the most powerful images of the gospel in African church history. The sermon uses him anonymously as a structural parallel. Named and told properly, his story would have given the sermon its redemptive center: a man taken captive who became a herald of the One who sets captives free.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Insert a gospel movement before the altar call.** After "Look over your life for a minute," add a brief but explicit gospel statement: what sin before God looks like, what Christ has done to address it, and what repentance toward him means. The altar call cannot be a call to self-improvement — it must be a call to Christ. Even three sentences would transform the close.

2. **Preach Nehemiah 5:14–19.** The sermon ends at verse 9 exegetically. Nehemiah's personal sacrifice (refusing the governor's food allowance, bearing the cost himself) is both the climax of the passage and the most powerful illustration in it. Add a section: "What did Nehemiah's fear of God cost him?" This also mirrors the gospel — Christ bore the cost of our debt to God.

3. **Name Samuel Crowther.** If the story is verified as Crowther's biography (as the end notes indicate it likely is), name him from the pulpit. "A boy named Samuel Ajayi Crowther — kidnapped, sold, redeemed, and eventually sent back as a bishop to the people who had enslaved him." This becomes the sermon's most memorable image and its clearest gospel resonance.

4. **Reframe the fear of God section.** Currently, verse 9's "fear of our God" is used as a motivational trigger for ethical conduct. Expand this: What is the fear of God? How does the gospel produce rather than eliminate it? Fear of God is not anxiety — it is reverence before One who is holy and who has been merciful. This is the sermon's theological hinge and it is currently a hinge without a door.

5. **Clarify the exhortation's direction.** The altar call currently says "if you realize there are areas in your life that need work, come." Reframe: "If you realize you have been living as though Christ does not watch what you do — come to him. Not to fix yourself. To receive from him what you cannot produce yourself." This is a small change with a structural effect.

**Not triggered** — this sermon is not doctrinally precise enough for dead orthodoxy. Its failure is in the opposite direction: warm pastoral instinct without theological architecture. The issue is not cold correctness but structural gospel absence.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

There are flashes of genuine pastoral weight — the "blindfolded people walking toward a cliff" image, the direct questions in the application, the honest self-implication — but the sermon does not carry eternal gravity because it does not culminate in the eternal Christ. The congregation is pressed but not lifted; convicted but not shown the Savior.

**Bonus: +1 / 5** — Pastoral warmth is present; eternal weight is not.
