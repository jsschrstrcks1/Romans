# Don't Trust Your Heart
## A Sermon on 2 Kings 25:1-12

---

## Opening

We're going to be in the last chapter of 2nd Kings this week. 2 Kings chapter 25. But before you turn there, open your Bibles to the New Testament, the book of John, chapter 6.

We have been talking about obedience versus disobedience. Worshipping God versus worshipping idols. And in the midst of all this I saw a video by a popular preacher, a young preacher, a Baptist preacher, a graduate of a good Baptist school, that said God can't override unbelief. Not that he WOULD NOT — that he COULD NOT. Which I find striking. It took God 6 days to speak into existence an existence that has taken the rest of us thousands of years to try and understand. And yet we've only explored 5% of the world's oceans, and 1% of the sea floor. We have spent billions of man hours studying science and yet we still don't fully know why molecules stick together, or understand fully mechanics at the quantum level. How can a single atom be both positive and negatively charged until we catch it? Despite our best efforts we have way more questions than answers. Way more mysteries unsolved, and every mystery we solve reveals a ton more. Einstein famously was searching for his theory for everything, and yet one of the smartest men to ever have lived, whose math unlocked the power of the bomb that is capable of destroying the world, couldn't come up with a solution. When we only rely on ourselves we end up with problems. I think the preacher I mentioned before may have misspoken — he may have meant "wouldn't." That's a point I'd argue. But what he said was "couldn't" — what he said was God can't override man's free will. There are a bunch of times when God did just that in Scripture. Can any of you think of a time? Pharaoh. How about Lydia? Remember Lydia? I came across a list of verses where God talks about our will and his effect on it. Look with me first at John 6:

## Scripture Reading: 2 Kings 25:1-12

So you might be saying, "That's a weird passage, pastor, to pick for a Sunday morning." It is kind of weird — a king of Israel slain, the temple destroyed, hope lost, the only people that remained were the most destitute and they were ordered to maintain the vineyards.

This destruction of the temple would have been especially important to the Jews of the day. It had become the focal point of Israelite culture. You needed a job? You'd go to the temple. You wanted the news of the day? It could be found at the temple. It was the absolute center, the focal point of daily life in Israel. The closest analog I can think of to this would be a Muslim mosque. They offer continuing education type classes, social connections, political connections, news, jobs, deals for other Muslims. It's the epicenter of Muslim life. A Jew's life revolved around the temple more than our life revolves around the local church. So when the temple was torn up, it was like losing a large piece of their identity. It was devastating to the surviving Jews. A good word for you to know at this point is diaspora. The Jews that were scattered here — and it's used again of the Christians scattered in Acts — are called the diaspora. It just means the scattered ones. Or you could call it the remnant.

Do you remember the story of Shadrach, Meshach and Abednego? They would have been about 10-12 here and taken captive alongside Daniel.

For the Jew living in Israel it would have been like an American living in Pearl Harbor, or like living in Japan when we dropped the bomb. It left an indelible mark on their collective sense of identity.

---

## It Could Have Been Avoided

But it all could have been avoided. At this point in Israel's history Jeremiah was a prophet, and he received a warning from God. A warning that he took straight to the king. This is all in Jeremiah 38 — you should read this later. Jeremiah tells the king, look — God says surrender and our most important cultural treasure will be spared, not to mention all the lives that could be lost. Not to mention the king's own life. But the king thought he knew better than God, and better than Jeremiah, so he did his own thing.

How risky is that? It strikes me that the world upholds the heart of mankind as one of the chief earthly authorities. Have you ever seen a Disney movie that didn't have one of the people in the movie say "trust your heart"? Terrible advice. Jeremiah may have been thinking of King Zedekiah here when he says:

> The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?
> — Jeremiah 17:9 (ESV)

If the heart is deceitful above all things, and we can't trust it, then why do we tell our kids that? Anyway. King Zed trusted his heart instead of God, and the result was devastating. All of his kids were killed. He ends up dying in captivity at the hands of the enemy of his country.

---

## Where Did Your Heart Lead You?

I can't help but notice some parallels here. Think back on your own life, when you depended on you for answers and direction. Dad, think about that. When you were young, and you looked to what your heart wanted for guidance, where did you end up? What path did that set you on? Were you living for the Lord? Certainly not! The same is true of all of us. When we trust our heart for direction — our heart that Jeremiah says is "deceitful above all things, and desperately sick" — we all end up going down some dark paths. Kurt ended up in the motorcycle club, Kenny Duffield ended up becoming an alcoholic. Drugs, sex, lying, and anything else that seems good is the end result of trusting our hearts. Don't trust your heart — trust God! The end result of trusting your heart is death. Sometimes a physical death, but worse is the eternity you could spend apart from God. Your body dying is over in a minute or two, but eternity in hell is much, much worse!

Over and over in Scripture this principle is made clear:

> Whoever trusts in his own mind is a fool, but he who walks in wisdom will be delivered.
> — Proverbs 28:26 (ESV)

---

## The Crossroads

So we find ourselves at a crossroads, like King Zed found himself at a crossroads. He had been told by God the path he was on could only lead to death — his and his family's. He was told there was another way. I'm sure Jeremiah told him not to trust his heart! We know Jeremiah's feelings on the heart. In fact you and I know what the Bible says about our hearts. He knew, and we know that our hearts lead us to trouble just like they led Adam and Eve to trouble. The best of our earthly wisdom is far, far short of God's wisdom. The best of our knowledge falls far, far short of God's knowledge. The best of our righteousness is as filthy rags. But we like Zed are at a crossroads before Christ. We can either follow our own wants and desires, and end up having a life filled with our own attempts to satisfy ourselves and an eternity of drinking deep the wrath of God — OR we can choose to set aside our desires, our wisdom, the things our heart desires right now, and choose to follow Christ, and have an eternity of being fully satisfied in our Lord Jesus Christ.

I don't know about you, but for me that's an easy choice. The famous philosopher Pascal wrote in his book *Pensées* to an imagined atheist — if I am wrong, and you are right, I've lived a good life, helped other people, generally left the world a better place. But if you are wrong, and I am right, you'll spend an eternity separate from God in the crucible of his wrath.

That all starts with the gospel. That all starts with listening to God, over your own sinful heart. Because when we listen to our heart we end up making mistakes — sometimes that means we say silly things like Mr. Furtick. Other times it means rejecting Christ one time too many, and ending up in hell.

As the praise team comes, be thinking about your own life. Are you a believer in Christ? If not, YOU are at that same crossroads today. Ahead lies doom or salvation. Which way will you choose?

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date**: 2026-03-21
**Rubric**: Thus Says the Lord — Reformed Baptist Sermon Evaluation

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Sharp Warning with a Real Gospel Instinct, Undermined by Scattered Texts and a Thin Conclusion"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A pastorally direct sermon on the danger of self-trust that correctly identifies the fallen condition and reaches for the gospel but weakens its proclamatory force by using 2 Kings 25 as a thin framing device, opening with an extended sovereignty-of-the-will digression, and closing with Pascal rather than Christ.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Result: Conditional Pass — with reservation.**

**Scopus Test**: The announced text is 2 Kings 25:1-12, but the sermon's main point — "don't trust your heart; trust God" — is derived not from 2 Kings 25 but from Jeremiah 17:9 ("The heart is deceitful above all things"). 2 Kings 25 serves as a historical illustration for the Jeremiah point; it does not itself yield the main point. A sermon can use a narrative passage illustratively, but the rubric asks whether the sermon's thesis demonstrably arises from *the passage's own argument*. 2 Kings 25 is a narrative of judgment and exile — the fall of Jerusalem, the destruction of the temple, the deportation of the people. Its "main point" (to the degree narrative has one) is the culmination of covenant unfaithfulness and the execution of God's judgment. "Don't trust your heart" is a legitimate application of this narrative, but it requires Jeremiah 17:9 to make the connection explicit. The sermon is not dishonest, but it is not text-driven in the strict sense.

Additionally, the John 6 opening (sovereignty over the will) is textually legitimate but has no clear organic relationship to the announced 2 Kings text and takes up significant real estate before the sermon's actual argument begins.

**Authority Test**: Pass. The preacher does not soften the text's judgment. He is willing to name names (Furtick), describe the danger of hell, and press the crossroads choice.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Near-trigger: Fatal Flaw #1 (No clear gospel presentation)**

The gospel is gestured at but not preached with clarity. The sermon contains:
- "That all starts with the gospel. That all starts with listening to God, over your own sinful heart."
- "We can... choose to follow Christ, and have an eternity of being fully satisfied in our Lord Jesus Christ."
- A crossroads call: "Are you a believer in Christ?"

But what the gospel *is* — the substitutionary death of Christ, resurrection, justification by faith — is not stated. The Pascal's Wager closing substitutes a rationalist calculation for gospel proclamation. A hearer could understand: "choose God over self and be saved" — without understanding that their sin requires atonement, that Christ has provided it, or what it means to "choose Christ."

The flag is a near-trigger rather than a full trigger because the sermon's trajectory is toward the gospel and the crossroads framing makes a genuine call for response. However, the gospel content is thin enough to be a serious weakness.

**No full fatal flags triggered. No cap applied.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 12/25**

- *Textual Fidelity* (4/10): The Jeremiah 17:9 application to Zedekiah's failure is the sermon's strongest textual moment — it is an organic connection between Jeremiah (the prophet present at the fall described in 2 Kings 25) and the 2 Kings narrative. This shows genuine contextual awareness. But the John 6 section and the Proverbs 28:26 citation are appendages to the argument rather than development of the text. The main text (2 Kings 25) is never actually *exegeted* — it is summarized for context and then used as a launching pad for the Jeremiah 17 application.
- *Contextual Awareness* (3/5): The connection of Jeremiah to the 2 Kings 25 narrative is contextually sound and shows awareness of the prophetic literature's relationship to the Kings narrative. The temple-as-center-of-Jewish-life analogy (to a Muslim mosque) is helpful for congregational understanding.
- *Exegetical Clarity* (3/5): There is no Greek or Hebrew work, which is not required — but there is also no verse-by-verse development of the passage. The text is read and summarized, not opened.
- *Logical Faithfulness* (2/5): The sermon's logic is: 2 Kings 25 narrates Jerusalem's fall → Zedekiah's heart-trust caused it → Jeremiah 17:9 names the problem → therefore don't trust your heart. This is legitimate but indirect. The sermon imports its main thesis from a supporting text rather than deriving it from the announced text.

**FCF** (Fallen Condition Focus): Precisely identified — human self-trust, the deceitfulness of the heart as the root of all disobedience. This is the strongest element of the sermon's theological architecture. The specific fallen condition is named concretely: "we trust what we want instead of God."

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness* (4/7): Christ is present as the alternative to self-trust — "choose to follow Christ" — and as the end-goal of eternity. But the sermon does not show *why* Christ is the answer to a deceitful heart. The organic connection would be: your heart is deceitful and unable to trust rightly → only God can change your heart (regeneration) → Christ secured that change at the cross → therefore trust him. The sermon points to Christ without traversing this path.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* (2/3): The 2 Kings 25 context is correctly located in redemptive history — temple destruction, exile, the culmination of covenantal unfaithfulness. The "crossroads before Christ" framing in the application is redemptive-historically aware.
- *Doctrinal Precision* (4/4): The sovereignty section (John 6:44, Acts 13:48, Acts 16:14 etc.) is doctrinally precise and clearly Calvinistic. Jeremiah 17:9 is applied with precision — not as a motivational warning but as a description of the fallen condition. No theological errors detected.
- *Confessional Alignment* (2/4): The sovereignty material reflects clear Reformed/Calvinistic commitments, well-grounded in multiple texts. The gospel section is too thin to evaluate confessional precision on justification.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel* (2/5): The gospel is named but not proclaimed. "That all starts with the gospel" is a reference to the gospel, not its proclamation. Pascal's Wager is a rationalist argument for belief, not a gospel presentation. The sermon identifies the problem (deceitful heart, doom without God) but does not clearly articulate the solution (Christ's substitutionary death, imputed righteousness, faith and repentance).
- *Necessity of Christ* (3/5): Christ is necessary in the sermon's logic — he is the only alternative to self-trust and doom. "Choose to follow Christ" implies exclusivity. But the necessity is stated rather than demonstrated from the cross.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity* (2/5): The sermon does not move toward the cross. It moves toward a decision at a crossroads. These are not the same thing. The crossroads framing (doom or salvation, which will you choose?) implies the call is primarily volitional. A cross-centered sermon would establish that the cross is the reason the choice is possible at all.

**Synagogue Test**: A devout Jew would not be significantly offended. The sermon argues: don't trust your own heart, trust God. This is entirely available from the Hebrew Scriptures. The specifically Christian offense — Christ's blood is the only ground of acceptance before God — is not proclaimed. **Near-trigger.**

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10/15**

- *Heart Penetration* (4/5): The "where did your heart lead you?" section is genuine and specific — naming individuals (Kurt, Kenny Duffield), asking "Dad, think about that." This reaches the affections, not just the intellect. The list of vices (drugs, sex, lying) drawn from Galatians 5 is appropriately specific.
- *Specificity* (3/5): The named individuals and the "dark paths" language are specific. The mosque analogy is helpful for concrete contextual understanding. The final crossroads call is appropriately direct.
- *Spiritual Differentiation* (3/5): The sermon addresses the unconverted (Perkins #4 and #5 are the primary targets — the humbled-but-not-yet-believing and the general unconverted). Believers are addressed briefly (sovereignty section). But the backslider, the self-righteous, and the religiously confident are not specifically addressed.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5/8**

- *Call to Response* (3/4): The crossroads framing is a genuine call for response: "choose to follow Christ." The praise team transition is a recognized altar-call structure. The congregation is told there is a choice to be made. However, the call is vague about what the response entails — "choose Christ" without specifying repentance, faith, or any concrete action.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness* (2/4): The sovereignty section equips believers to answer the "God can't override free will" objection with Scripture — this is directly useful for witness. The heart-deceitfulness argument equips the congregation to counsel others on self-trust. But there is no sending-out, no missional commissioning.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**

- *Clarity of Outline* (2/4): The outline is: (a) God's sovereignty over the will (John 6 section) → (b) 2 Kings 25 context → (c) It could have been avoided → (d) Where did your heart lead you? → (e) The crossroads. This is traceable but the John 6 opening is a substantial detour. The sermon begins with one argument (God can override the will), shifts to a different argument (don't trust your heart), and converges at a crossroads call. These two arguments are related but not organically unified.
- *Progression* (2/3): The movement from ancient Israel to personal life to crossroads call has genuine progression. The John 6 section creates a false start.
- *Unity* (1/2): Two competing ideas — God's sovereignty over the will, and human self-trust as dangerous — vie for centrality. The sermon would be stronger if one of these governed and the other served.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

- *Relevance* (2/2): The Disney "trust your heart" observation is immediately recognizable and culturally apt. The named individuals (Kurt, Kenny Duffield) ground the application in the congregation's actual experience. The "retired policeman became a pastor" illustration is concrete and compelling.
- *Clarity* (1/2): The illustrations clarify. Pascal's Wager functions as an illustration but muddies the gospel call.
- *Memorability* (1/1): "Your body dying is over in a minute or two, but eternity in hell is much, much worse" is striking and will be remembered.

**8. Sermonic Force — 4/5**

- *Reads as Preaching* (2/2): Strong oral energy throughout. Direct address is sustained and natural. The rhetorical questions ("Can any of you think of a time?", "Where did that path set you on?") are genuine congregational engagement, not decorative.
- *Conviction on the Page* (1/2): Conviction is present in the heart-trust section. The crossroads call has genuine urgency. But the John 6 opening reads more like a doctrinal lecture than preaching, and Pascal's Wager is a cool philosophical argument, not a hot proclamation.
- *Cadence and Breath* (1/1): Sentence variation is good. The "Or we can choose..." section builds appropriately. The closing has a crisp finality.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily myself — the dominant register is self-examination and personal trajectory. Christ is the destination of the crossroads but not the center of the sermon.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Primarily ideas — the sovereignty texts are listed more than preached, and God's character is asserted more than encountered.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Convicted — the "where did your heart lead you?" section genuinely presses. But the person convicted is not given a clear answer in Christ.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Jeremiah 17:9 / 2 Kings 25 connection is the sermon's finest exegetical moment**: Connecting the prophet who delivered God's warning to Zedekiah with the passage describing the fall Zedekiah caused shows genuine canonical awareness. The preacher correctly notes that Jeremiah was present at the events described in 2 Kings 25.

2. **The applicatory specificity is strong**: Named individuals, named vices, the Disney "trust your heart" observation — this sermon does not let the congregation hide behind abstraction. It names the specific lies culture tells about self-trust and gives the congregation real examples of where that path leads.

3. **The sovereignty section is doctrinally rich and equips for witness**: The chain of texts (John 6:44, 65; Acts 13:48; Acts 16:14; Romans 11:34-36) is carefully assembled and constitutes a genuine expository response to the "God can't override free will" claim. This material is independently valuable and equips the congregation for apologetic conversations.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The John 6 sovereignty section is a detour that the sermon never reconnects** (opening section): The opening premise — a preacher wrongly said God *couldn't* override unbelief — is addressed with six or seven texts on divine sovereignty. This is a legitimate point, but it is a *different* point from the sermon's announced text and main application. The sovereignty texts do not organically lead to "don't trust your heart." The sermon jumps from "God can override the will" to "and here's what happens in 2 Kings 25." The two arguments share a family resemblance (human will vs. God's will) but are not structurally unified.

2. **Pascal's Wager as a gospel conclusion** (closing section): Pascal's Wager is a rationalist argument for the reasonableness of belief. It is not a gospel call. "If I am wrong and you are right, I've lived a good life" — this framing makes the gospel sound like a hedge bet on a reasonable proposition, not a surrender to a crucified and risen Lord. The congregation is left with a probability argument where they need a savior. This is the closing theological impression of the sermon, and it does not serve the crossroads call.

3. **2 Kings 25 is never actually preached** (Scripture Reading section): The passage is read and summarized in three paragraphs (temple context, the diaspora, the personal devastation) but none of the passage's actual content is expounded. The sermon announces this text, reads it, explains its historical context, and then largely leaves it. The application comes from Jeremiah 17:9. The announced text is a backdrop, not the source.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see that opening with an extended theological argument (God's sovereignty over the will) and then pivoting to a personal application (don't trust your heart) has the structural effect of treating the congregation as a theological classroom before a gospel call. The sovereignty section is designed to rebuke a bad preacher's bad theology — a legitimate pastoral concern — but it positions the congregation as observers of a theological debate rather than sinners approaching a crossroads. By the time the personal application arrives, the congregation has been in lecture mode for several minutes.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon *feels* strong in the heart-trust section because the illustrations are vivid and the named examples are striking. But the emotional power of the middle section ("Kurt ended up in the motorcycle club, Kenny Duffield ended up becoming an alcoholic") can mask the fact that the gospel, when it arrives, is thin. A congregation moved by a compelling personal account of sin's destruction may feel as though they have received a full sermon when the gospel they most need has only been gestured at.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Jeremiah 17:9 — "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?" — is followed immediately by Jeremiah 17:10: "I the LORD search the heart and test the mind, to give every man according to his ways, according to the fruit of his deeds." And then, just a few verses later, Jeremiah 17:14: "Heal me, O LORD, and I shall be healed; save me, and I shall be saved, for you are my praise." The text's own answer to the problem of the deceitful heart is *divine healing and salvation*. The text moves from diagnosis to cry for rescue. Preaching Jeremiah 17:9 in its context would have given the sermon a gospel center native to the text itself, and a crossroads call with deep emotional and theological roots: "The heart is desperately sick — who can understand it? *God* can. And he is willing to heal it."

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Cut the John 6 sovereignty section or relocate it to a supporting role.** The sovereignty material is valuable but it is not the main argument of this sermon. Either: (a) preach the sovereignty texts separately on another Sunday and begin this sermon at 2 Kings 25, or (b) compress the sovereignty point to one paragraph and use John 6:44 as *evidence* that God can change the heart — directly supporting the "trust God not your heart" thesis. Don't open with a theological debate; open with the burning city.

2. **Replace Pascal's Wager with Jeremiah 17:14.** The cry "Heal me, O LORD, and I shall be healed; save me, and I shall be saved" is the text's own answer to the deceitful heart. This is more powerful than a French philosopher's probability calculus, it is native to the canonical context, and it is a genuine gospel invitation: "The heart is sick — but the Great Physician is here."

3. **Give the crossroads call a gospel center.** "Which way will you choose?" requires an answer to "Choose *what*, exactly?" Articulate the gospel at the crossroads: Christ died for your deceitful heart. He took the punishment your self-trust deserved. He rose. He offers forgiveness and a new heart (Ezekiel 36:26). Repent and believe.

4. **Add a specific address to believers who recognize their own heart-trust.** The self-righteous believer who trusts their spiritual track record, the tired believer who has been trusting their own strength, the backsliding believer who has followed their heart off a cliff — these are Perkins categories left unaddressed. The sermon presses the unconverted; it does not press the converted in the same way.

5. **Choose one primary text and make it the spine.** Either preach 2 Kings 25 with Jeremiah as its prophetic commentary, or preach Jeremiah 17:9-14 with 2 Kings 25 as its historical illustration. The current hybrid treats both as interchangeable, which weakens both.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered — the sermon has pastoral heat and genuine applicatory pressure. It is not cold.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon has genuine eternal urgency — "eternity in hell is much, much worse" is not a decorative phrase, and the crossroads framing carries real eschatological weight — but the weight is carried by fear of judgment rather than by the glory of what Christ has done, and a sermon whose gravity depends entirely on the terror of hell without the wonder of grace is only half of its proper burden.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

The rubric strains slightly at the Berean Gate because this is a narrative text (2 Kings 25) being used didactically — the "main point" of a historical narrative is less straightforward to articulate than a Pauline argument. However, the rubric's Scopus Test is illuminating rather than punitive here: the question "does the sermon's thesis demonstrably arise from the passage's own argument?" correctly identifies that the sermon's thesis comes from Jeremiah 17:9, not from 2 Kings 25, and this reveals the real structural choice the preacher needs to make.
