The term and the theology are from different traditions. Given the Reformed framing evident throughout this sermon series, consider replacing "prevenient grace" with "effectual calling" or simply "the work of God in a soul."

In preparing this week for Sunday school we've had a number of things pop up. We had bugs and vehicle troubles. Basically between Ellisha, Mom and Dad, and me, all of our vehicles have had something break or some kind of work done on them, and the red truck broke twice.

The blue truck had a transmission go out. Ellisha's car blew a head gasket. The Hyundai's brakes went out. My truck had the air worked on — it leaks, but we just can't figure out where. The red truck had a fuel pump go bad, and then maybe the battery, and the solenoid came apart in Dad's hand. His shot wore off mid shelf-installation. And they have bugs — this will be the third time they have had to have the exterminator out.

So much drama, and so much noise. Headaches, and work, and stress. Literal headaches too — with the storms and seasons changing the barometric pressure changes, and sometimes that gives me a headache. Had a pretty bad one on Monday, to the point where I was nauseous.

Every time we turned around, every time we went to do something, something had broken.

To top it all off this week I've been moving my library from one end of my house to the other. Ever moved a library? The best part was my house is so small that in order to move things around, most of the other things had to go outside. It's been a physically tiring week as well. Thousands of books.

In the midst of that, it's hard to slow down and pursue Christ, isn't it.

He talks about a shallowness in the church *(Tozer, paraphrase)*, and the need for simplicity. Sometimes I think we become so busy and caught up in the drama of the week and daily struggles that we do our best to passively resist Christ without trying to. We let the car troubles, and bug troubles, and work, and everything else drown out the voice of God in our life.

I think instead of focusing on ourselves — I'm upset because my air doesn't work and it's going to be hot, or I'm upset because I'm down to one car, or my brakes, or that boss of mine, or this or that circumstance — we need to be doing two things.

Firstly, we need to slow down. Let's read:

---

We serve a God that can make wars cease, that can drop desolations on the earth, that can cast mountains into the sea. Think about what that would be like — a great mountain there one moment, the next being tossed into the sea. The pinnacle of our machines of war can be laid to waste, shattered (v. 9). What do we really have to worry about with such a God watching out for us? He promises to be our refuge and strength.

If you're like me, your brain is already running in the background saying things like, "Refuge and strength… as long as he isn't using these circumstances to grow me. He's my protector, unless I need a lesson taught by being unprotected. He's my strength, unless I need a lesson in humility and weakness."

I think that's exactly the thinking Tozer was talking about when he said the church is all too often wooden *(paraphrase)*.

My favorite verse in there was "Be still, and know that I am God."

Sometimes we have to stop and just take a minute, and re-orient our hearts and minds towards Christ. We need to shift our focus from ourselves and our circumstances onto our Savior who can do all these things and more. We need to be looking for the hand of God at work in the midst of the circumstances.

*[Discussion prompt — invite congregant to share:] Remember the guy that you led to the Lord in Grandpa Ed's nursing home room? Can you tell that story?*

*[Discussion prompt — invite congregant to share:] Or about the time Shannon spent with her dying grandma?*

God is always at work. We just need to be still, and wait, and watch — keep our eyes off ourselves and on Christ, so that we can join in.

I'm convinced selfishness is the pet sin of our generation. We are so consumed with what we want and how we feel and what seems right to our own twisted hearts that we can't or don't do those things.

So sometimes God has to help us. I talked to Dottie about her knee one time. He brought her low, and stripped everything away. You guys lost your business, your retirement, her health, and it seemed like at one point you might even lose her. But on the other side of that, she's closer to the Lord than ever. She may have her doubts some days. But doubts are proof of the pudding, I think. Because of God's effectual work in a soul — without God having done a work that leads to salvation, you don't have any interest in things of the Lord — a Christian who has spent years in the church and sometimes wonders is evidence of salvation. Someone walks in off the street and wonders — that's a whole different story. Proof that God is at work and that they need to have the gospel shared with them right away.

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Devotional Conversation, Not a Sermon — Warm Community, Absent Exposition**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A brief, conversational Sunday school devotional that uses Psalm 46 as a backdrop for an encouragement to slow down and seek God, but does not open the text, present the gospel, or function as a sermon in any meaningful sense of the word.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**FAIL — with pastoral charity noted.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's dominant idea — slow down, stop focusing on yourself, re-orient toward Christ — does not arise from Psalm 46's main point. Psalm 46 is a song of confident trust in God as the fortress of Zion who defeats the nations and commands the earth to cease striving. Its claim is cosmic and its ground is Yahweh's identity as Lord of Hosts and God of Jacob. The sermon domesticates this claim into a personal stress-management lesson. "Be still" (v. 10) is not primarily an invitation to personal quiet time; in context it is a divine command addressed to the nations and the earth — cease your striving against God and know that he is Lord. The main point of the sermon is not the main point of the text.

*Authority Test:* The preacher does not actively edit or soften the text. But neither does he stand under it as a herald — he stands beside it as an encourager, using it as a sympathetic backdrop for pastoral conversation.

### 5. Score Breakdown (Diagnostic — No Formal Score Assigned)

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics**

The psalm is read in full — a genuine strength for a short devotional. But it receives no treatment beyond verse-level summary. The structure of Psalm 46 (three stanzas, each ending in or building toward the refrain of v. 7 and v. 11, with the divine speech at the center in v. 10) is not explored. The crucial interpretive question — who is being told to "be still" and in what context — is not asked. The preacher reads "be still" as an invitation to personal quiet; the text uses it as a command addressed to raging nations. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is identified as selfishness and distraction, which is a real pastoral diagnosis, but it is not what Psalm 46 was written to address.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity**

The handling of effectual calling is the strongest theological moment — the source notes' correction of "prevenient grace" to "effectual work in a soul" reflects good doctrinal instinct, and the distinction between a doubting believer and an unbeliever who needs the gospel is a sound pastoral observation. God's sovereignty and providence are affirmed through the Dottie illustration. But Christ is never named. The Trinitarian shape of salvation is absent. No doctrinal precision beyond general theism is exhibited.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force**

The gospel is not present. Christ's person and work are not mentioned. The Synagogue Test: this sermon would offend no one in a synagogue, mosque, or morally serious secular context. There is no cross-centered gravity. The law/gospel dialectic is absent — there is no hammer, no balm.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force**

The opening is specific to this congregation (vehicle troubles, library move, bugs, headaches) — and this specificity is the sermon's primary pastoral strength. The congregation knows this preacher lives in their world. The Dottie illustration is moving and well-placed. However, the application never advances beyond "slow down and look to God." No heart condition is named; no sinner is cornered; no hearer is addressed distinctly.

**5. Exhortation & Response**

There is no exhortation. The sermon ends: "Let's spend the rest of the time we have in prayer." Prayer is good. It is not a substitute for the "therefore" moment — the imperative edge where the preacher summons the congregation to specific action in response to the Word.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow**

The structure is a loose funnel: personal circumstances → psalm reading → brief observations → Dottie illustration → discussion prompts → prayer. There is no outline, no progression of argument, no single dominant idea that builds to a climax.

**7. Illustration & Imagination**

The discussion prompts (nursing home conversion, Shannon's grandmother) suggest a congregation with real testimonies of God's faithfulness that, if drawn out, would have been more powerful than anything the preacher could construct.

**8. Sermonic Force**

This reads as extemporaneous notes for conversation, not as a sermon manuscript. The oral register is natural and warm. But sermonic force — the sense that God is speaking through a herald — is absent. This is a kind elder speaking to his family, not a preacher standing under the Word and delivering it.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Congregational warmth and genuine pastoral presence.** The preacher is known to these people and speaks from within their world. The vehicle breakdown list is not filler — it establishes that the preacher and congregation share a life, and that the Word being offered comes from someone living in the same pressure they are. This is a real pastoral gift.

2. **The Dottie illustration.** The story of God stripping everything away and drawing a soul closer through suffering is theologically honest, personally shared, and moves in the right direction — toward God's sovereignty and grace in affliction. It is the sermon's best moment and its nearest approach to gospel weight.

3. **Reading the psalm in full.** Many short devotionals skip large portions of their text. Reading all eleven verses gives the congregation the whole psalm and lets the Word do its own work even where the preacher does not open it. The congregation heard "God is our refuge and strength" and "the Lord Almighty is with us" without editorial trimming.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text is not opened.** Psalm 46 is one of the most theologically dense and structurally rich psalms in the Psalter. Its three stanzas move from cosmic chaos (vv. 1–3) to the security of Zion (vv. 4–7) to God's sovereign acts in history (vv. 8–11), with the divine speech in v. 10 as the psalm's theological climax. None of this is surfaced. The congregation heard the psalm but received no help understanding what God is saying through it.

2. **"Be still" is misread.** The sermon treats v. 10 as an invitation to personal quiet and stillness — a devotional application that is not unreasonable but is not the verse's primary sense. In context, God is commanding the nations and the earth to cease their striving against him and acknowledge his lordship. The psalm's pastoral comfort for the believer flows from this cosmic claim, not the other way around. Preaching the personal application without the cosmic ground produces a truncated and ultimately weaker comfort.

3. **Christ and the gospel are absent.** This is the most serious weakness. A Christian congregation gathered on the Lord's Day — even in a Sunday school setting — needs the gospel. The sermon mentions God's effectual calling, God's sovereignty, God's care in affliction — but does not name Christ, does not articulate his work, and does not connect the peace of Psalm 46 to the peace made through Christ's blood (Col. 1:20; Eph. 2:14–17). The congregation is encouraged to trust God, but not shown why the cross makes that trust possible and safe.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see the extent to which the sermon's frame (personal busyness and distraction) has substituted for the psalm's own frame (cosmic sovereignty and the defeat of God's enemies). From the inside, the opening illustrations feel like honest pastoral preparation — sharing real life, building rapport, setting up the need the text will address. From the outside, the psalm is being asked to speak to a problem it was not primarily written to address, and its own problem — the raging nations, the shaking earth, the enemies of Zion — goes unpreached.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The opening vehicle-and-bug litany feels like strength — the congregation laughs, recognizes their week, and feels known. This warmth creates the impression of pastoral depth. But warmth without textual substance is sympathy, not preaching. The congregation leaves feeling accompanied in their circumstances; they do not leave having been met by God in his Word. The intimacy of the small-group context can mask the absence of expository content, because closeness substitutes for clarity.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 10 — "Be still, and know that I am God; I will be exalted among the nations, I will be exalted in the earth" — is a divine self-declaration of coming universal lordship. The God who speaks this verse is not offering a quiet morning devotional; he is announcing his inevitable triumph over every power that has ever challenged him. For a congregation exhausted by broken trucks, broken bodies, and broken weeks, the comfort available here is not "slow down and breathe" — it is "the God who defeats nations and shatters spears is your fortress, and nothing that broke this week was outside his hand." That is a different and far more powerful sermon, and the text offers it freely.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the sermon around the psalm's three-stanza structure.** Stanza 1 (vv. 1–3): God is our refuge even when the cosmos collapses. Stanza 2 (vv. 4–7): God dwells with his people; therefore they are unmoved. Stanza 3 (vv. 8–11): God acts in history; he commands the earth to cease striving. Let the psalm's own movement shape the devotional's movement.

2. **Exegete "be still" correctly — then apply it fully.** First give the congregation the cosmic sense: God is commanding the nations to cease striving. Then draw the application: if the sovereign God has already spoken this word over the nations, how much more can you lay down your anxiety over the truck? The application is more powerful when it flows from the cosmic claim, not instead of it.

3. **Name Christ as the ground of the psalm's promise.** Psalm 46's confidence is covenant confidence — the God of Jacob is our fortress (v. 11). For the New Covenant congregation, this covenant is secured in Christ. The river of v. 4 that makes glad the city of God has its New Testament counterpart (Rev. 22:1–2). The peace of v. 10 is made available through the Prince of Peace. One paragraph that connects the psalm to Christ transforms a devotional into Christian preaching.

4. **Give the congregation one clear response, not prayer requests.** End with a specific call: "This week, when the next thing breaks — and it will — say out loud: 'The Lord of Hosts is my fortress.' Then pray. Then watch for where God is at work." A specific, repeatable act is more equipping than an open prayer time, though prayer time may follow it.

5. **Use the congregational testimonies as textual confirmation, not as content substitutes.** The nursing home conversion and Shannon's time with her dying grandmother are potentially powerful illustrations of the God who acts in the midst of chaos. Frame them explicitly around the psalm: "This is what verse 8 looks like — 'Come and see what the Lord has done.' Come and see what he did in that nursing home room." This makes the testimonies expository, not merely devotional.
