# More Than Love
## Baptist Faith and Message — Article 2: God

**Scripture**: Numbers 23:19; Psalm 31:5-6; Jude 9; John 3:16

---

We looked at the first article of the Baptist Faith and Message last week — the Word of God. This week I want to look at the second article, which discusses God.

There has been so much written about God — not only in Scripture but in our own statements of faith. Every single creed and confession and statement of faith, including our Baptist Faith and Message, has something to say about God. Who God is matters to us because, quite frankly, a word is just a vessel into which we pour meaning. So when the Bible says "God," it contains a rich description of a vibrant being. When other religions say "god," they fill that word with a different meaning than our Scriptures would. That is how you know they serve a different God — by knowing the meaning God's Word pours into the name, and then, when someone says "No, my god is just this or just that," you can know whether you are talking about the one true God of the Bible or not.

I need some people to look up some verses. We're going to be jumping around a little bit today — some of you may need to take two. We'll try to get through all of them. The first is Numbers 23:19, Psalm 31:6, Jude 9, and John 3:16.

---

There are a whole host of groups out there that call themselves Christian, and whether they worship our God or not comes down to how they fill those words with meaning. When they say "God," do they mean our God?

Let's evaluate a couple of statements by groups that claim to worship the same God we do. I want you to raise your hand if you think the statement describes our God, and keep it down if not.

Ready?

*"And they cheated and deceived, and God deceived — because God is the best of the cheaters and deceivers."* — Sura 3:54.

[Note: Translations of Sura 3:54 vary significantly. The Arabic root *makara* carries a semantic range from "planning/scheming" to "deception." Sahih International renders it: "And they planned, and Allah planned. And Allah is the best of planners." Yusuf Ali's margin offers "best of deceivers." The pastor's rendering — "cheaters and deceivers" — reflects the more polemical translation tradition. The theological contrast with Numbers 23:19 is the point being made, and that contrast stands regardless of which English translation is used.]

Does that describe the God of the Bible?

> "God is not man, that he should lie, or a son of man, that he should change his mind. Has he said, and will he not do it? Or has he spoken, and will he not fulfill it?"
> — Numbers 23:19 (ESV)

Good. They get trickier from here.

---

This next one is tricky if you're not paying attention. Ready?

*The ONLY true name for God is Jehovah.*

True or not? In truth, there are scores of names for God — just the ones God gives us in the Old Testament. Many of them include the word Jehovah, but not all of them. Let me give you the name and the translation:

- **El-Elyon** — God Most High
- **El-Kanna** — God the Jealous
- **El-Olam** — Eternal God
- **El-Shaddai** — God Almighty
- **Jehovah** — LORD
- **Jehovah Adon Kol Ha'aretz** — LORD of All the Earth
- **Jehovah Bara** — Creator
- **Jehovah Chereb** — Glorious Sword
- **Elohim** — God of Strength or Power
- **Elohay Elohim** — God of Gods
- **Elohay Emet** — The God of Truth

[Note: The names in the list are a popular teaching device. Most are well-attested. However, "Jehovah Bara" (Creator) is a non-standard composite — the Creator title in Scripture is typically *El Bore* ("Creator of the ends of the earth," Isaiah 40:28), not a compound with Jehovah. "Jehovah Chereb" (Glorious Sword) is likewise not a recognized divine name in the traditional sense; the "sword of the LORD" appears as poetic imagery in Deuteronomy 33:29 and Isaiah 27:1 but not as a formal covenant name. The theological point about God's many revealed names is sound; the specific list reflects popular devotional teaching rather than a formal Hebrew lexicon.]

Read Psalm 31:6 for me.

> "I hate those who pay regard to worthless idols, but I trust in the LORD."
> — Psalm 31:6 (ESV)

See where it says "Lord"? This is one of the areas our English translation of the underlying Hebrew is weak. The name behind "LORD" here is YHWH — the great covenant name. But one verse earlier, in verse 5, God reveals himself as *Elohay Emet* — the God of Truth:

> "Into your hand I commit my spirit; you have redeemed me, O LORD, faithful God."
> — Psalm 31:5 (ESV)

[Note: The pastor directed the congregation to Psalm 31:6 to illustrate the Hebrew name *Elohay Emet*, but the phrase actually appears in Psalm 31:5, where "faithful God" translates the Hebrew *El Emet*. In verse 6, the underlying Hebrew is simply YHWH (LORD). The theological point is correct — Scripture calls God *Elohay Emet* — but the verse reference should be Psalm 31:5.]

The Lord who is, in every conceivable way, truth. English just says "Lord." But the truth is so much deeper than that. God is Truth. He is what Numbers 23:19 says — God is not human that he should lie, he doesn't change his mind. He says things, and they happen. He makes promises and fulfills them.

God, by his nature, is capable of thinking things, and they become reality. Humans just can't do that, can we? I know of a church that teaches you can speak things out into the universe and the universe — I think they mean God — gives them to you. That what you speak over a person becomes truth. But that isn't how it works for humans. That isn't found in Scripture. Only God can do that. Not us. God can answer our prayers — he provides for us. But that is different from speaking something out into the universe and expecting it to happen simply because you spoke it.

---

This next one should be a little easier. *Jesus used to be Michael the archangel.* True or not?

Look at Jude 9.

> "But when the archangel Michael, contending with the devil, was disputing about the body of Moses, he did not presume to pronounce a blasphemous judgment, but said, 'The Lord rebuke you.'"
> — Jude 9 (ESV)

If Jesus and Michael were the same person, why would Jesus have to invoke the Lord's name? Why wouldn't he just rebuke them the way he rebuked the legion of demons inhabiting the Gadarene demoniac? Those demons he simply commanded, and they obeyed in fear and trembling. Why is he somehow weaker now? The only answer is that Michael is a lesser being than Jesus. Michael is merely an angel. Jesus is the Son of God.

---

Next: *Jesus is a created being.* This heresy is as old as the second century.

Does anyone have John 3:16 memorized in the King James Version?

> "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."
> — John 3:16 (ESV)

The King James says "begotten." Jesus was *begotten*, not made. He existed before he came to earth and lived as man, and he exists now that he is no longer a physical bodily inhabitant of earth.

---

What about this one — pay attention because this one is tricky too.

*God exists only in three persons.* Raise your hand if it's true — keep it down if you think it's false.

Tricky, right? You're inclined to say yes: there is God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Spirit. But when the Mormons say that, they mean that these are three *separate entities*. They would argue God the Father is a physical and separate individual from the Son, who is a physical and separate individual from the Father, while the Holy Spirit does not have a body but is a separate individual from both. If you ask them straight out, they will say they only worship one God — but if you ask questions, you'll find out they really believe in three separate, unique, individual, formerly mortal beings of flesh and bones. Explained that way, how many of you would still raise your hands?

This is important stuff.

If there are two kids who share the same name, you have to depend on an individual's characteristics to tell one from the other — facial structure, shape, size, tone of voice, actions. These show one unique individual from another.

If we are going to worship God, we need to know the God we claim to worship.

---

So let's talk about what we know about God — starting with the fact that there is only one real, living, and true God. Our Baptist Faith and Message says:

> "There is one and only one living and true God. He is an intelligent, spiritual, and personal Being, the Creator, Redeemer, Preserver, and Ruler of the universe. God is infinite in holiness and all other perfections. God is all powerful and all knowing; and His perfect knowledge extends to all things, past, present, and future, including the future decisions of His free creatures. To Him we owe the highest love, reverence, and obedience. The eternal triune God reveals Himself to us as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, with distinct personal attributes, but without division of nature, essence, or being."
> — Baptist Faith and Message 2000, Article 2

I counted thirty-seven attributes of God in just that brief paragraph — and thirty-seven is just scratching the surface. I remember one class at Trinity where we were asked to sit down and spend an hour writing out the attributes of God. I was daunted at first. That seemed like a lot. But after a few minutes I began to write, and before I knew it the hour was over. A professor I once studied under wrote a book outlining five hundred attributes of God — and each of the three members of the Trinity had their own section. We could spend all morning naming the attributes of God as laid out in Scripture without fully plumbing the depths of just those things God has chosen to reveal to us in his Word.

What a glorious day it will be when we one day get to heaven and are able to meet him face to face — and spend the rest of eternity getting to know God very God.

---

One more. Raise your hand if it's true, keep it down if it's false.

*God is ONLY love.*

Let me give you a little background. An acquaintance of mine was objecting to something — he said the real God could only be love by his estimation, and that the God of the Bible clearly has self-esteem issues. This was my response to him.

I don't disagree that God is perfect love. But he's a lot of other things too — perfect holiness, perfect grace, perfect mercy, perfect justice. Emphasizing any one at the expense of the others cheapens God to a caricature of what God has said about himself.

If God weren't different from us — if his view of life weren't higher and more comprehensive than ours — if God was just like us, he can't be God.

I think you know that we are incapable of expressing perfect love. Our actions are tainted by sin. And since he brought that up — Adam was there with Eve, he didn't really try to stop her, and then sinned himself.

The breakdown comes in a few key places. The idea that God has a fragile confidence — I wouldn't describe it that way at all. He created everything to be without sin and peaceful, to be a reflection of God himself. Every human being and element of creation was meant to reflect God. One of those elements meant to be reflected was the ability to choose. God gave Adam and Eve that ability. Whatever they ate isn't the point so much as the disobedience and disbelief exhibited when they did the one thing they were told not to do.

As a result, every child was born knowing how to do wrong things. Kids don't need to be taught that if they are sneaky enough they can probably get a cookie even though mom told them to wait until after dinner, or how to lie to get out of trouble. These are things Adam and Eve were born without, but that mankind gained through sin. In the process, the image of God imprinted on us and on nature was shattered, and what's left is a dim reflection of what was. Our best love is still tainted by our own selfishness and other sinful desires that lurk deep in our hearts.

We were given a choice. Much of the rest of the Bible is God working out different arrangements with man, giving us a chance to try on our own to reach God. These all point toward the need for Christ. In the words of the old hymn — we owed a debt we could not pay, and Christ paid a debt he did not owe.

The story of the Bible is not one of a fragile God trying to improve his own self-confidence. The story of Scripture is one of a God who loved us enough to pay the price his perfect justice demanded. That is the epitome of love.

> "Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends."
> — John 15:13 (ESV)

It also shows his perfect mercy — that he would go to such extreme lengths to satisfy his justice and wrath by taking the punishment required by our sin upon himself. What a wonderful, unquenchable sea of grace. If a man killed your child, it would be hard to be in the same room with the killer. Though it was our sin that required Christ's death, God offers to make us his children. Though we murdered his Son through our sin, he makes us heirs to his kingdom.

Yeah — God is love. But God is so much more than love. Sure, God has wrath — who doesn't? God's is perfect, and holy, and justified. Sure, God's justice requires that holy wrath to be appeased. Sure, God in his love and mercy made a way for that to happen through the vehicle of God's unlimited, unmerited grace extended to us.

The only people who end up in hell are those who continue to reject God and his Son until their death. If one spends a lifetime actively rejecting God, it seems to me it wouldn't be very loving of God to force them to live for eternity with the one they hate — so he allows them to have what they want: an eternity apart from him. If God is the epitome of all that is good, where he is not is the epitome of everything God is not.

God allows us to choose to be separate from him. He allows us to choose that eternity if we want to. But he loved us enough to give us a better way forward.

Our Baptist Faith and Message talks about that too:

> "Christ is the eternal Son of God. In His incarnation as Jesus Christ He was conceived of the Holy Spirit and born of the virgin Mary. Jesus perfectly revealed and did the will of God, taking upon Himself human nature with its demands and necessities and identifying Himself completely with mankind yet without sin. He honored the divine law by His personal obedience, and in His substitutionary death on the cross He made provision for the redemption of men from sin. He was raised from the dead with a glorified body and appeared to His disciples as the person who was with them before His crucifixion. He ascended into heaven and is now exalted at the right hand of God where He is the One Mediator, fully God, fully man, in whose Person is effected the reconciliation between God and man. He will return in power and glory to judge the world and to consummate His redemptive mission. He now dwells in all believers as the living and ever present Lord."
> — Baptist Faith and Message 2000, Article 2 (God the Son)

---

All of that starts with a relationship with Christ. Apart from the work of God in our hearts, we're not even capable of having an interest in God.

I'm reminded of a video clip I once saw of a boy — maybe six years old — running around with his shoes untied. His mom no sooner tells him to tie his shoes than he steps on his laces and trips headlong into a mud puddle. He stands up upset, his tiny glasses absolutely covered with mud. He just stands there as the shock turns into tears, holding his arms out to the side. Completely, totally muddy.

But his mom just sighed, walked over, wiped the mud off his glasses, and toweled the mud off him as best she could.

That little boy in that moment of shock didn't know what to do on his own. But his mom did, and his mom loved him enough to go rescue him from the mud.

It's like that for us too. In our own efforts we have turned life into a messy thing — we've face-planted into the mud of sin — and God is the only way out. Jesus' death on the cross is the only way to be clean.

Do you know him today?

*[Gospel]*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title

**A Catechetical Survey With Gospel Gestures — Theologically Honest but Expositionally Homeless**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A well-intentioned doctrinal survey of God's attributes that effectively guards the congregation against common heresies while falling short of exposition, proclamation, and the kind of applicatory force that turns theology into doxology.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**CONDITIONAL PASS — Topical/Catechetical Genre.**

This is not an expository sermon on a biblical text. It is a doctrinal teaching lesson structured around the BFM Article 2, using Numbers 23:19, Psalm 31:5–6, Jude 9, and John 3:16 as proof texts within a comparative theology exercise. The stated main point — "God is more than love; he is holy, gracious, just, and triune, and to worship him rightly we must know him truly" — is scripturally sound and arises from the passages cited. The Berean Gate passes for a catechetical address.

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's thesis is identifiable ("Don't reduce God to love alone; know who God actually is"). This is scripturally grounded, even if not derived from the progressive argument of a single text.

*Authority Test*: The preacher does not stand above the texts. He uses them faithfully as the basis for comparative theological claims.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Score capped at 69.**

**Flag #4 — Application is entirely generic**: The sermon's applications are all theological corrections to false teaching rather than specific calls to the congregation's particular lives, hearts, or sins. "You need to know the God you claim to worship" is true but is not specific application. The closing "[Gospel]" placeholder is not a gospel presentation — it is a bracket in the manuscript indicating the pastor planned to give one. As written, the gospel is never proclaimed; the sermon ends with "Do you know him today?" followed by a placeholder.

**Flag #5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon tells the congregation what to know but never tells them what to do with what they know. There is no "therefore" moment. There is no call to repentance, faith, worship, or obedience. The closing question ("Do you know him today?") is an invitation to reflection, not an exhortation.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 10/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (4/10)*: Individual texts are used accurately — Numbers 23:19 establishes divine truthfulness; Jude 9 refutes the Jesus-is-Michael heresy effectively; John 3:16 is used for "begotten not made." But the texts function as proof texts in a comparative theology exercise, not as passages whose own argument is being followed. The sermon never stays inside any passage's own flow.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5)*: The passages are used without regard to their own contexts. Numbers 23:19 is Balaam's oracle — a remarkable Gentile prophet testifying to God's faithfulness against Israel's enemies. This context enriches the theological claim but is not developed. Jude 9 is from a warning against false teachers — its own context is relevant to the sermon's anti-heresy aim but unacknowledged.
- *Exegetical Clarity (2/5)*: The note on ἐρημος being Elohim versus YHWH in Psalm 31:5 vs. 31:6 is handled — with the end note correction noted. The teaching is generally accurate.
- *Logical Faithfulness (2/5)*: The sermon imports the BFM's theological framework as the governing structure, with Scripture as confirmation. This is confessional teaching, not biblical exposition.
- *FCF*: The fallen condition is implicitly "false knowledge of God leading to false worship." This is a legitimate and important FCF, but it is not named and pressed — it is assumed throughout.

The cross is present but the offense of its necessity is not felt.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** More of God's attributes than either — the sermon is primarily analytical. Christ appears in the BFM quotation and in the closing illustration. Neither encounter is deep enough to produce doxology.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about Him, primarily. The comparative theology format keeps God as the object of analysis rather than the Subject who addresses the hearer.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Informed. The congregation learns to identify heresy and affirm the BFM. They are not convicted of specific sin or comforted with specific gospel promises.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The comparative theology "raise your hand" exercise**: This is a genuinely skillful teaching device. Presenting the Sura 3:54 quote and asking the congregation to evaluate it before offering the biblical corrective is engaging, memorable, and apologetically useful. It models how to recognize counterfeits before explaining the genuine article.
2. **The wrath-justice-love integration**: "Sure, God has wrath — who doesn't? God's is perfect, and holy, and justified." This is a genuinely brave statement in a cultural moment that resists divine wrath, and the sermon's argument that emphasizing love alone cheapens God to a caricature is well-made.
3. **The Trinitarian distinction against Mormonism**: The two-children-with-the-same-name analogy is clear, the Mormon position is accurately represented, and the distinction between unity of essence and distinctness of persons is made accessibly.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The "[Gospel]" placeholder renders the sermon incomplete for evaluation and for preaching**: The sermon ends without a gospel proclamation. "Do you know him today?" is a question, not a gospel. This is not a minor omission — it is the fatal absence. Every sermon must end with Christ offered, not merely gestured at. The preacher knows this; the bracket is evidence of intention. The intention must be fulfilled.
2. **The sermon produces knowledge of God without pressing the question of response**: The congregation learns thirty-seven attributes of God. They are never asked: *What do you do with a God this vast, this holy, this just? Does the existence of his wrath alarm you? Does the existence of his grace undo you?* The theological content is real; the move from knowledge to worship is absent.
3. **The sermon's text selection defeats exposition**: Four texts, each used as proof for one claim, with no passage given sufficient space to do theological work on its own terms. The sermon would benefit from selecting one text — perhaps Psalm 31:5 ("Into your hand I commit my spirit; you have redeemed me, O LORD, faithful God") — and treating God's attributes through the Psalm's own movement of trust, lament, and confidence.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the "raise your hand" exercise, while effective as a teaching tool, structures the congregation as evaluators of theological propositions rather than as sinners standing before a holy God. When the sermon ends with "Do you know him today?", the congregation has been trained to be theological judges, not kneeling worshipers. The sermon's form contradicts its intended pastoral effect.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The doctrinal precision creates an impression of thoroughness. The congregation leaves knowing what Mormons believe, why Jehovah's Witnesses are wrong about Jesus, and how many attributes the BFM attributes to God. This *feels* like deep theological teaching. But theological accuracy without doxological response is not a sermon — it is a class. The gap between "we've learned correct things about God" and "we have been drawn near to God by what we learned" is the gap this sermon does not cross.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The "what if someone said God only is love" section contains the sermon's most preachable theological claim — that God's wrath is satisfied through Christ's sacrifice, making his love possible without abolishing his justice. "Though we murdered his Son through our sin, he makes us heirs to his kingdom" is the sermon's most profound line. It arrives at the end of a paragraph and receives no development. This is the sermon's homiletical center — the moment when "God is more than love" becomes "and yet his love is more than we could have imagined." It should be the climax, not a passing observation.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Complete the "[Gospel]" section before preaching**: State the problem (sin creates the very separation that God's justice demands), the solution (Christ absorbed the wrath that the God-is-only-love crowd wants to pretend doesn't exist), and the response (repentance and faith — not knowing correct doctrine about God, but trusting the God you now know). The sermon's doctrinal content cries out for this landing.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This is a catechetical teaching sermon within a BFM sermon series — a distinct genre that stresses the rubric's exposition and exhortation categories.
