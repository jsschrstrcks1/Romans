Continuing the theme of last week, we're going to be in Psalm 103 today. Psalm 103, if you've brought your Bibles — and I hope you have.

One of the commentators I read, who is much better at the Hebrew here than I am, says it carries with it the sense that David was setting himself aside from people — alone, just him and God. It's as though he's crying out for the sum total of his being to worship God: heart, soul, and mind.

Interestingly, there are places in the New Testament — and I know we're in the Old Testament here — where the English word "heart" or "deep affection" is actually translating a Greek word that means intestines: *splanchna*. Paul uses it that way — in Philippians and Philemon, where "affection" and "hearts" in English are really *splanchna* underneath. John uses it in 1 John 3:17, "closes his heart against him." The ancient world located deep emotion not in the chest but in the gut. And if we're being honest, the heart pumps blood but doesn't have a lot to do with emotions other than moving the blood that carries the neurotransmitters sent out by the body — so they weren't entirely wrong. We say "heartsick" and we mean someone is in a hard time emotionally. The ancient listener would have said the same thing and pointed a few inches lower.

David wanted his heart and soul and mind to worship God. All of his inmost parts. My commentator believed that David had set himself aside, alone with God, and was opening himself up to examination by God — to make sure he was being thankful, to make sure his life reflected "blessing the Lord," or thanksgiving to God.

People think Thanksgiving is about turkey and stuffing, but it's about more than that. It's about being together with those we love and giving thanks to God. About examining ourselves and seeing whether we live up to the biblical standard of thanksgiving, and giving thanks for the fact that though we fail, God's mercies are new every morning. We can never run so far away from God that he can't draw us back.

God is so good to us.

Let's read the whole thing.

> Bless the LORD, O my soul, and all that is within me, bless his holy name! Bless the LORD, O my soul, and forget not all his benefits, who forgives all your iniquity, who heals all your diseases, who redeems your life from the pit, who crowns you with steadfast love and mercy, who satisfies you with good so that your youth is renewed like the eagle's.
> — Psalm 103:1-5 (ESV)

### Living as an Example

It strikes me right away that though in the moment of writing David was alone, examining himself before God, and yet in recording that moment in Scripture he made this very personal and private moment very public. He's encouraging others to follow his example — I think both in private and in public. To lead by example is in view here, and should be how Christians live. In private we should read our Bible and pray. We should live a lifestyle of evangelism and worship — in public and in private. We should be fellowshipping with other believers as well. In so doing, Scripture says:

> Iron sharpens iron, and one man sharpens another.
> — Proverbs 27:17 (ESV)

Living for Christ makes one different. Makes one peculiar in this world. A true follower of Christ stands out from the crowd in the same way one would if they had pressed their face into a red-hot glowing stove burner. The circular scars for the rest of your life would mark you as having pressed your face into the stove. The same is true of followers of Christ. We should live our lives as examples of God's mercy.

That's uncomfortable sometimes. Who wants their failures on display? But as Paul said — *Let the one who boasts, boast in the Lord* (1 Corinthians 1:31).

A commentary called JFB said this: that God is "not only intrinsically worthy of praise, but is specially suited to man's frailty."

### Suited to Our Frailty

I had a situation a few weeks back. You guys know I took in Cooper a while back as a rescue. He did what puppies do and absolutely destroyed my sandals — both pair — and my shoes. All of them. All in about five minutes.

I hate shoe shopping. I really don't enjoy shopping for most things. But there is something about the process of finding shoes that I really don't like. Part of it is that it's hard to find shoes in my size. If a shoe store has one or two pairs in stock in my size, it's because of God's providential grace for sure. I've been told I might have better luck at a boatyard.

It's hard to find shoes. So when I find a pair I like, I go back and buy them over and over and over again. Shoes that are comfortable and fit just right — whether walking, or hiking, or preaching — are hard to find. So when I find them, I stick with them. It's hard to find shoes that feel like they were made just for you.

Yet we have that same thing with God. We were made, and then we broke ourselves in ways that only God can make up for. Or said another way — our frailty is specifically suited to God's power. Where we are weak, God is strong. Where we are broken, God can repair it. Where we hurt, God brings healing. Where we struggle, God supplies us with his strength, his comfort, his protection and security. Where we lack, God is there, and he provides.

And God is great.

### Forgive All Your Iniquity

Verse 2 starts giving thanks for the ways in which God benefits us directly. Some people like to start small and build, but David just goes right straight to the top of the list. Thank you, God, for forgiving our sins.

What sins did David have to be forgiven of? I mean, we know he wasn't an attentive father. He raised kids that raped and murdered and tried to kill even him. Then there was the murder he committed as part of a conspiracy to cover up his sinful relationship with Bathsheba. You guys remember the story.

One of the jobs of a king is to lead the army. So naturally David stays home from the war. He was in the wrong place, and then — perhaps thinking of his men — he wandered up on the roof of the palace and looked out across the city at just the wrong time. In his lust he sent his messengers to "retrieve" Bathsheba. Some commentators make it seem like David and Bathsheba mutually consented. But to me that doesn't seem to have been the case. It's not specifically spelled out, but my best guess is he raped Bathsheba.

She realized shortly thereafter she was pregnant and contacted the king. He tried to make it look like maybe Uriah was the father. He recalled Uriah from the front lines, got him drunk, and sent him home. But Uriah was too honorable — even when drunk — to do that. So he slept in the stables. Unfortunately for David, that wouldn't do at all, so David sent Uriah back to the front lines and ordered Uriah's commander to pull back from Uriah so he would be killed by the enemy.

I think this must have been what was going through David's mind when he said, "Forgive my sins."

God's punishment for David was very public. Their firstborn son died. God's prophet showed up and announced that David's son died out of punishment for David and his sin against Uriah and Bathsheba. David spent a period of time in sackcloth and ashes — very publicly mourning his sin and mourning his son.

It's interesting that public repentance seems to have preceded the major revivals through history also.

### Crowned with Steadfast Love

So he's thankful our sins are forgiven in verse 3, and then he says he's thankful for salvation. We — even David — deserved to spend an eternity apart from God. But God, being rich in mercy and because of his great love for us, has offered us forgiveness. Verse 4 says we are crowned with steadfast love and mercy.

Think about the word "steadfast" for a moment. Who can define that word for me?

The Hebrew word here is *hesed*. Let's look at it through some Bible stories.

**Abraham and Lot.** Remember the story of Abraham and Lot? They end up parting company and Lot chooses to march towards the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah. He goes from living kind of nearby, to living just beside, to living in the midst of these sinful people. And then one night Abraham pleads for the life of Lot. *Hesed* love is ascribed to God for Lot when God spares his family on the eve of the destruction of those cities.

**Rahab.** Or when we read about Joshua and the battle of Jericho. Remember the story of Rahab? She hid the Israelites and helped them spy on the city. She protected them. And so on the seventh day, when the army marched around the city seven times blowing their trumpets and playing their song, and the walls fell down — everyone's home in the walls was destroyed, except that of Rahab and her family. And there again, this is attributed to the *hesed* love of God. Rahab helped the spies. But her family, as far as we know, wasn't involved. Yet God — because of his *hesed* love — spared Rahab's family also.

It's kind of deep stuff. People that don't deserve God's love being the recipients of God's love.

Every time *hesed* is used, there is another dimension to it though. There is an expectation of reciprocity. God showed his *hesed* love to Lot by sparing his family from the hellfire and brimstone that rained down upon the city, and he expected them not to look back and to pursue God. Both Lot, his wife, and his daughters screwed up in different ways, with different results. Most of you remember Lot's wife turning into a pillar of salt. Lot didn't look back, but the next thing that's said of Lot involves him going and getting drunk. His daughters took advantage of that, and they all had their own consequences — though none as dramatic as those Lot's wife encountered.

Rahab is a better example. She is said to have exhibited *hesed* love for the soldiers, and they — and God — in turn expressed *hesed* love for her, in sparing her and her family, in their home still standing after the destruction of Jericho.

**The Good Samaritan.** Another example is the Good Samaritan. You remember the story, of course. An Israelite was walking down the road, was beaten up, robbed, and left for dead. A Levite walks past and did nothing. Then a priest came along, and he scurried on to church rather than help the dying destitute man. Finally a Samaritan walks past, and the Samaritan bandages him up, gets him to an inn, and leaves enough money for them to take care of the Israelite until he's well again. When Jesus asks, "Which of these three proved to be a neighbor?" — the answer points to the one who showed mercy, the Greek word *eleos*. It's the same concept the Old Testament captured in *hesed*: undeserved, covenant-faithful, pursuing love.

It's undeserved, steadfast love from God — and the loving response that engenders in a truly saved person as a result.

### The Billion-Dollar Debt

Let's look at it a different way. You're struggling with something — let's say it's financial. Let's say you've run up a billion dollars in debt. You've gone out and just spent and spent and spent like a crazy person, knowing you could never pay it back. You bought houses and cars and clothes. You gambled and bought liquor for days. And now the fun is over and the creditors are knocking, and you're hiding in the kitchen closet hoping they leave — because there is no way you can afford to pay them.

My grandma had a story like that. When she was married to her first husband, he was an alcoholic. He would get his check and buy rounds of liquor for people at the bar with his income, rather than buy food or medicine for the little kids they had at home. So in order to get milk from the milkman, they would put in the order and pretend to not be home so he would leave it on the stoop. She said she literally would hide in the pantry when he would come around looking for money — knowing she didn't have it, couldn't pay it, and didn't want to face the man knowing these things. So she purposed in her heart she was never going to be that low on money or food again. And to my knowledge, she never was.

But then in our analogy, something happens. Someone comes along, opens up their checkbook, and pays the bill. How would you respond to that? Gratitude? Joy? Love?

Yet we have done the same thing. Scripture says:

> For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.
> — Romans 6:23 (ESV)

We have spent our life sinning and racking up debt. The only way that debt can be satisfied is with our death, followed by an eternity in hell. Pretty grim news. We can't do anything about it. Might as well be a billion-dollar debt. It's overwhelming and insurmountable. It's an unclimbable mountain of debt.

Yet Scripture says:

> But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ — by grace you have been saved.
> — Ephesians 2:4-5 (ESV)

And in another place:

> but God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.
> — Romans 5:8 (ESV)

Or:

> For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, that he might bring us to God, being put to death in the flesh but made alive in the spirit.
> — 1 Peter 3:18 (ESV)

We deserved death and hell. But Christ offers life eternal.

We have so much to be thankful for.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Warm Pastoral Reminiscence Without a Pulpit: Genuine Affection for God's Mercy, Insufficient Proclamation**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and personally honest meditation on Psalm 103's opening verses that demonstrates genuine love for God's mercy and hesed, while falling short of text-driven exposition, conscience-pressing application, and the exhortation required to constitute full preaching.

---

### 3. Berean Gate
**PASS — Conditional**

- **Scopus Test**: Marginally passes. The sermon does circle around the psalm's central movement — blessing God for his benefits, particularly forgiveness and steadfast love. A thesis can be reconstructed: *God's hesed love, demonstrated supremely in forgiving our sins, calls forth gratitude and worship from his people.* That arises from the text.
- **Authority Test**: Passes, but the text is more a launching pad than a governing authority. The sermon departs for significant stretches — the shoe-shopping illustration, the David/Bathsheba narrative, the grandmother's story, the billion-dollar debt analogy — and does not consistently return the hearer to the psalm's own argument. The preacher does not stand *above* the text editing it, but he does not consistently stand *under* it either. He is adjacent to it more than governed by it.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No score cap triggered. No fatal flaw flags formally engaged — but two are close enough to warrant warning:**

- **Flag 4 (Application is entirely generic)** — Near-trigger. The application throughout is warm but non-specific. "We should live our lives as examples of God's mercy." "We should read our Bible and pray." These are accurate but undifferentiated. No specific hearer is addressed. No conscience is cornered.
- **Flag 5 (No actual exhortation)** — Near-trigger. The sermon ends mid-thought ("We have so much to be thankful for.") with no clear summons to a specific response. There is no "therefore" moment. The congregation is informed and warmed, but not summoned.

Neither flag formally triggers, but both are present in weakened form and materially affect the score.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 13/25**

- *Textual Fidelity*: 5/10. The sermon treats only verses 1–5 of a 22-verse psalm, and even within that range, the text is referenced more as occasion than as authority. Significant sections of the sermon (David/Bathsheba backstory, grandmother illustration, Good Samaritan aside) do not arise from the psalm's own argument. The FCF is partially named — human ingratitude, moral failure, debt of sin — but never formally identified as what Psalm 103 was written to address.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 2/5. The comment on Davidic authorship and solitary examination is useful. No canonical context (where 103 sits in the Psalter, its doxological framing, its corporate conclusion in vv. 20–22) is explored.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 3/5. The *splanchna* material is genuinely interesting and honest about its New Testament origin. The *hesed* treatment is the sermon's best exegetical moment — clear, illustrated, and true. However, the Greek etymology of *eleos* in the Good Samaritan section drifts away from the psalm and doesn't return.
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 3/5. No eisegesis is imposed, but the David/Bathsheba narrative, while related to forgiveness, is imported rather than demanded by the psalm. The text is not misread — it is under-read.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness*: 5/7. The gospel presentation in the "Billion-Dollar Debt" section is genuine and Christocentric. ### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The hesed exposition** (the Billion-Dollar Debt section and the three OT illustrations). This is the sermon's best material. Abraham/Lot, Rahab, and the Good Samaritan are used well to give *hesed* concrete biblical texture. The move to the debt analogy and then to Romans 6:23, Ephesians 2:4–5, Romans 5:8, and 1 Peter 3:18 is the most textually grounded and gospel-saturated passage in the sermon.

2. **Pastoral authenticity and voice**. The shoe-shopping story, Cooper the dog, the grandmother's pantry — these are real, unforced, and arise from the preacher's actual life. The congregation trusts this voice because it is not performing. This is a genuine strength that the rubric does not grade away.

3. **The gospel is present and not embarrassed**. The sermon does proclaim the cross. It does not soften the debt of sin or the necessity of Christ's payment. In an era of therapeutic preaching, this instinct is worth affirming.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The sermon never returns to Psalm 103 after verse 4**. Verses 6–22 are entirely absent — including the psalm's majestic catalog of divine attributes (vv. 8–14), its meditation on human frailty and divine permanence (vv. 15–18), and its corporate doxological conclusion (vv. 19–22). The psalm is preached as a launching pad for the debt illustration rather than as a governing text. The congregation leaves knowing about hesed but not knowing Psalm 103.

2. **No exhortation. The sermon stops before it finishes preaching.** The final line — "We have so much to be thankful for" — is a statement, not a summons. There is no "therefore" moment. The congregation is not told: *Bless the Lord today. Bring your whole soul to him this week. Name one benefit and give thanks aloud.* The transition from explanation to application to exhortation was not completed.

3. **The application never differentiates hearers**. "Live as examples of God's mercy" and "read your Bible and pray" are accurate but could be said in any church about any text. No particular sinner is addressed. No particular struggle is named. The self-righteous who knows the doctrine but has never been undone by it; the backslider who once felt the joy of forgiveness but feels nothing now; the new believer who is overwhelmed by remaining sin — none are reached.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that his most powerful material — the debt analogy and the chain of gospel texts — functions almost as a separate mini-sermon inserted into the psalm meditation rather than arising from the psalm itself. The exposition of Psalm 103 and the gospel presentation are adjacent rather than integrated. The psalm *should* generate the gospel urgency, not serve as a warm-up for it.

Additionally: the preacher's natural storytelling instinct (grandmother, Cooper, shoe shopping) is an asset that currently runs on a parallel track from the text rather than being harnessed by it. The illustrations illuminate the preacher's life; they do not yet illuminate the psalm's argument.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The *hesed* section feels like rich exposition. The three OT illustrations (Abraham, Rahab, Good Samaritan) give it the texture of careful biblical theology. But the three illustrations are all the preacher's addition — none of them arise from what Psalm 103 says about hesed. The psalm itself never mentions Lot or Rahab. The sermon is adding texture to the word that the psalm itself does not provide, and that texture may obscure rather than illuminate how the psalmist himself uses *hesed* — as the covenantal ground of divine forgiveness, healing, and redemption in his own specific history.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Psalm 103:10–14** — "He does not deal with us according to our sins, nor repay us according to our iniquities. For as high as the heavens are above the earth, so great is his steadfast love toward those who fear him; as far as the east is from the west, so far does he remove our transgressions from us. As a father shows compassion to his children, so the LORD shows compassion to those who fear him. For he knows our frame; he remembers that we are dust."

This passage is the psalm's theological center and the most exegetically rich material available. It speaks directly to David's own sin and God's non-retributive response. It would have organically generated the debt illustration, the forgiveness theme, and the gospel presentation — with the psalm's own authority behind it rather than the preacher's analogy. This was the heart of the sermon that was never preached.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Anchor the gospel to the psalm's own language**. Before quoting Romans 6:23, spend two minutes in Psalm 103:10–14. Let the psalm generate the gospel need. "He does not deal with us according to our sins" is the psalmist's own way of saying what the debt analogy says — and it has the weight of Scripture.

2. **Add a "therefore" paragraph before the close**. The sermon must end with a summons. Specific options: *"This week, take one benefit from Psalm 103 and write it down. Tell your spouse or a friend why you're grateful for it. Don't let Sunday pass without blessing the Lord in private the way David did in Psalm 103 — alone before God, examining your heart."* Make the exhortation concrete and achievable.

3. **Address at least two distinct hearers**. After the gospel presentation, turn to the congregation and differentiate: *"If you have never trusted Christ — the debt is real. The creditors are coming. But someone has opened his checkbook. That is not metaphor; that is the gospel."* Then turn: *"And for those of you who have trusted Christ but have lost the feeling of that debt being paid — Psalm 103 is written for you. 'Forget not all his benefits.' The psalmist had to preach to himself. So do we."*

4. **Preach verses 19–22 in the close**. The psalm concludes with a cosmic call — angels, hosts, all his works — to bless the Lord. If David's private moment becomes a cosmic choir, the sermon's call to individual gratitude lands with vastly more weight. The ending writes itself from the text.

5. **Cut or reposition the "Living as an Example" section**. In its current location it serves as the sermon's first major point, but it is the weakest connection to the text. Move the example-living material to the exhortation section as its application, not as a standalone point.
