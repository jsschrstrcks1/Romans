## The World Paul Wrote Into

Philippians was written around 61 AD — about twenty years after the death of Christ, and ten years after Paul first preached in this city. [*Acts 16 records this as Paul's second missionary journey, not his first.*]

The Philippians were doing well. Their province had been granted permanent tax exemption as a Roman colony — the city held the *ius Italicum*, the full legal privileges of Italian soil, won after the Battle of Philippi in 42 BC. People moved to Philippi to do business. In some ways, writing to the Philippians was like writing a letter to your friends at the local Rotary Club. They were in a position to be a blessing to others, and they knew it. They were generous. Paul loved them for it.

But things across the empire were heating up.

Caligula was dead. No children, no heir. His uncle Claudius stepped into the power vacuum. There's a story — true or not, it captures something — that Claudius was found hiding behind a curtain in the palace on the day of the assassination, and the Praetorian Guards who found him decided on the spot that he would be emperor. He wasn't supposed to be there.

Claudius had a daughter named Octavia. Agrippina the Younger — a woman of remarkable ambition and very few scruples — arranged a marriage between Octavia and her own son. Her son had a strong claim to the throne. He was a great-great-grandson of Augustus Caesar. His name was Nero.

Agrippina wasn't content to stop there. She poisoned her own husband. She maneuvered Claudius's wife out of the picture. Then she married Claudius himself. She convinced him to adopt Nero and push aside his own son Britannicus. And then — once Nero was positioned to inherit — she had Claudius killed too.

Nero became emperor at sixteen years old. 54 AD.

His father's name, translated from Latin, meant "Brazen Beard."

For a few years, something like adult supervision held. Agrippina, the philosopher Seneca, and the Praetorian Prefect Burrus kept Nero under some measure of restraint. But Seneca and Burrus were eventually removed. And then Nero had his mother killed. The last voice that could say no to him was gone.

What followed was one of the most excessive reigns in Roman history. The story that Nero fiddled while Rome burned — near as we can tell, that is essentially true. He wanted a bigger palace. So much of the existing city stood in the way. According to a number of historians, Nero and his agents went around the city starting fires — fires that killed scores of people — so that he could build himself a bigger house. When the fires spread and people died in the streets, the shadow of that crime followed him everywhere. He couldn't escape the suspicion. So he found a scapegoat. Those evil Christians, he said. They did it.

Tacitus recorded what happened next:

> "He put forward as guilty, and afflicted with the most exquisite punishments, those who were hated for their abominations and called 'Christians' by the populace. … They died by methods of mockery; some were covered with the skins of wild beasts and then torn by dogs, some were crucified, some were burned as torches to light at night. … Commiseration was stirred for them, although guilty of deserving the worse penalties, for men felt that their destruction was not on account of the public welfare but to gratify the cruelty of one."
> — Tacitus, *Annals* 15.44 (c. 117 AD)

That was Nero. Brilliant. Violent. Raised by an evil woman. Given ultimate power while he was still in his stupid years. That was the emperor.

Paul was that man's prisoner.

Not in a dungeon — his Roman citizenship afforded him some measure of dignity. He was under house arrest. He could have visitors. He had to pay rent on the place. There was a soldier with him at all hours. He wasn't going anywhere.

And from that room, guarded by Nero's soldiers, Paul wrote a letter to his friends in Philippi — the Rotary Club crowd, the generous ones, the ones doing well — and he wrote them a letter full of joy.

## Scripture Reading

> And it is my prayer that your love may abound more and more, with knowledge and all discernment, so that you may approve what is excellent, and so be pure and blameless for the day of Christ, filled with the fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ, to the glory and praise of God.
> — Philippians 1:9–11 (ESV)

## Discernment

Paul is praying for these people. He is a prisoner under house arrest, paying rent on his prison, waiting to stand trial before the most dangerous man in the Roman Empire — and he is praying for them.

And what does he pray?

He doesn't pray for their safety. He doesn't pray for their prosperity. He doesn't pray that their businesses do well or that the tax exemption holds. He prays that their love would abound more and more, with knowledge and all discernment — so that they would be able to approve what is excellent, and be pure and blameless for the day of Christ.

Discernment. That's the word he lands on.

The gifts of the Spirit are listed in 1 Corinthians 12. Among them is the ability to distinguish between spirits — what we commonly call discernment. It is the capacity to see from God's perspective. To look at a situation and understand not just what is happening, but what is true. What is right. What honors God and what does not.

Paul prays they would have more of it. Not just a little. Abounding more and more.

Think about that in context. The man writing this letter is sitting in a room under guard, watching Nero's empire from the inside. He knows better than most what it looks like when a whole civilization loses its ability to discern what is excellent. When a nation stops being able to tell the difference between right and wrong — between what endures and what burns.

And so he prays: *let your love abound in knowledge and all discernment.*

Love without discernment is sentiment. It feels good in the moment and wanders off the road. Discernment without love is cold, judgmental, and useless. But love rooted in knowledge, grounded in discernment — that is the thing Paul is asking God to build in his friends.

He says: so that you may *approve what is excellent.* Sift through what is in front of you. Know the difference. Then choose the excellent thing.

And then: *be pure and blameless for the day of Christ.*

There is a day coming. We are working toward something. Every decision, every act of discernment, every choice between the excellent and the merely acceptable — it is all preparation for that day. Paul is not asking them to be discerning for their own comfort or their own reputation. He is asking them to be discerning because there is a day of accounting, and the King they serve deserves their best.

*Filled with the fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ, to the glory and praise of God.*

Not your righteousness. His. Through Jesus Christ. This isn't a moral self-improvement program. Paul isn't telling the Philippians to try harder. He is praying that Christ's righteousness would work itself outward in their lives — that the root would bear fruit — to the glory and praise of God.

That is what Paul prays for from a prison cell. Not comfort. Not rescue. For the people he loves — discernment. Fruitfulness. The glory of God.

What would it look like if that was what we prayed for each other?

**Factual repairs applied:**

1. **"Caligula died, passing the empire to his son Claudius"** — Corrected to *uncle*. Claudius (Tiberius Claudius Caesar) was Caligula's paternal uncle, brother of Germanicus (Caligula's father). Caligula died childless.

2. **"Agrippa (Nero's mother)"** — Corrected to *Agrippina the Younger* throughout. "Agrippa" in a biblical context is Herod Agrippa (Acts 12; Acts 25–26) — a completely different figure. Using "Agrippa" from the pulpit in a Philippians series would create confusion for any congregant who knows their Acts.

3. **Tacitus quote** — Was unattributed in the source notes. Attribution added: Tacitus, *Annals* 15.44 (c. 117 AD). The same quote appears with full attribution in *Grace and Peace* (the previous Philippians sermon).

4. **"Nero became emperor at 17"** — Corrected to *sixteen*. Nero was born December 15, 37 AD and became emperor October 13, 54 AD — sixteen years old.

5. **"Great grandfather Augustus Caesar"** — Corrected to *great-great-grandfather*. Lineage: Augustus → Julia (daughter) → Agrippina the Elder (granddaughter) → Agrippina the Younger (great-granddaughter) → Nero. Four generations.

6. **"Two senators" kept Nero under control** — The source notes said "two senators." Per *Grace and Peace*, the actual figures were Seneca (philosopher/tutor) and Burrus (Praetorian Prefect) — neither was a senator in the primary sense. Corrected accordingly.

7. **"First missionary journey"** — The source notes say "10 years after Paul's first missionary journey through the area." Paul's visit to Philippi is recorded in Acts 16, which is the *second* missionary journey. Already corrected in *Grace and Peace*; corrected here as well.

## 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Historical imagination and accuracy.** The Roman background is narrated with narrative confidence and factual precision (confirmed by the detailed End Notes). The description of Paul writing "a letter full of joy" from a room guarded by Nero's soldiers is one of the finest setup lines in this entire series. It establishes genuine stakes.

2. **Anti-moralistic instinct.** When the preacher reaches the closing clause of Phil. 1:11 ("fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ"), he immediately guards against a moralistic reading: "This isn't a moral self-improvement program." That reflex is theologically sound and shows doctrinal instincts at work even when the gospel is not yet fully developed.

3. **Textual sequencing.** The Discernment section follows Paul's own logical sequence in the passage — love → knowledge → discernment → approval of excellence → purity → day of Christ → fruit of righteousness → glory. This is exposition following the text's own argument, not rearranging it.

---

## 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is absent.** Phil. 1:11 — "the fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ" — is the theological climax of the passage, and it contains a gospel in miniature: righteousness that is not ours, that comes through Christ alone, to the glory of God. The preacher names it but does not open it. Why does the righteousness come through Christ? What did Christ do to make that transfer possible? The Synagogue Test would not be triggered here — but only because the sermon fragment ends before the gospel would need to be proclaimed.

2. **Application is a question, not a summons.** "What would it look like if that was what we prayed for each other?" is a soft question that invites reflection but demands nothing. This is the point where the preacher must press: *This is what you must pray. For your spouse. For the person in your row. For your enemy.* The text supplies the content of the prayer; the preacher must supply the command to pray it.

3. **The Greek is untouched where it matters most.** *Dokimazein* (to approve, to test and find genuine) is the key verb in v.10 — it is the word used for assaying metal, for testing whether a coin is genuine. *Ta diapheronta* (things that are excellent, things that differ) carries real weight. The preacher lands on "discernment" without showing the congregation why that word is the right word for what Paul is doing. One sentence of exegetical transparency here would multiply the applicatory force.

---

## 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that the historical introduction has become the sermon's center of gravity. More words are spent on Nero than on Paul's prayer. The narrative skill that makes the Roman section so compelling may be drawing energy away from the text itself. Vivid context risks becoming the message.

The "day of Christ" (*hemera Christou*, v.10) is mentioned twice but not developed. This is an eschatological horizon that Paul uses to generate both urgency and hope — it is not decoration. The sermon treats it as a future audit rather than as a motivating reality.

---

## 12. Illusion of Strength

The historical introduction feels like strong preaching because it is vivid, accurate, and narratively confident. It reads like a man who knows his material. But historical background is not exposition. The congregation may leave having learned about Nero and felt the drama of Paul's imprisonment without having encountered the text's actual claim on their lives. The confidence of the historical section can mask the thinness of what follows it.

---

## 13. Missed Opportunity

The phrase "pure and blameless for the day of Christ" (*eilikrineis kai aproskopoi eis hemeran Christou*) is the goal of Paul's prayer — not discernment for its own sake, but discernment aimed at eschatological readiness. The preacher touches this but does not let it land. A congregation full of people making daily compromises in what they approve — in entertainment, in relationships, in politics, in ambition — needs to hear that those choices have a day coming. The eschatological weight of Paul's prayer is this sermon's most underdeveloped resource.

## 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Complete the manuscript.** The most important fix is structural: the sermon is explicitly unfinished. No further evaluation is meaningful until the remaining sermon points are written.

2. **Open the verb *dokimazein*.** Add one sentence after "approve what is excellent": "The word Paul uses — it's the word you'd use for testing whether a coin is genuine. Not a vague preference. A test. A standard. An assay." This costs nothing and multiplies the applicatory force of everything that follows.

3. **Develop the eschatological frame.** "For the day of Christ" appears twice and disappears. Write one full paragraph on what that day means: the return of Christ, the evaluation of lives, the exposure of what we have approved and what we have settled for. Then connect it back to discernment: *This is why Paul prays for it. Not for your comfort — for that day.*

4. **Replace the closing question with a summons.** "What would it look like if that was what we prayed for each other?" should become something like: "Pray this for someone this week. Not in general — for one person by name. Pray that their love would abound in knowledge and discernment. That they would be able to tell the difference between what is excellent and what is merely acceptable." The text supplies the content of the prayer; the preacher must supply the command.

5. **Develop the gospel clause.** When the manuscript reaches "fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ," add two sentences: "He earned the righteousness. He gives the fruit. Apart from union with him, there is no fruit — only the performance of righteousness that withers and dies." This is the gospel planted in the text's own soil.

6. **Address differentiated hearers.** Before the closing question (or the summons replacing it), add a brief Perkins-style differentiated address: one sentence to the believer who has been approving the acceptable instead of the excellent; one sentence to the person who has never asked what God's standard for excellence is; one sentence to the person who is exhausted from trying to discern everything alone.
