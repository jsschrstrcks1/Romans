1. **No governing text is identified.** This sermon has no passage. Ephesians 5:4 is quoted but not governed by — it is used to add a practical note, not as the textual foundation. ### 13. Missed Opportunity
Ephesians 5:4 is the most generative moment in the sermon and the one most completely wasted. Paul's argument in Ephesians 4-5 is structured around the "put off / put on" pattern — the old self is being replaced by the new self, and thanksgiving is the Spirit-filled alternative to corrupt speech. This is not a minor behavioral tip — it is a pneumatological argument about what a Spirit-filled community looks like. The preacher quotes it and moves on. Had this been developed — "What is the thankless speech that belongs to your old self? What does the Spirit-filled replacement look like in your marriage, your workplace, your social media?" — this would have been the most specific and most powerful moment in the sermon.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific, Actionable)
1. **Choose a governing text and expound it.** Psalm 107:1-9, Deuteronomy 8:10-18, or Ephesians 5:15-20 are all viable. The best choice for this sermon's existing instincts is Psalm 107 — it provides four categories of delivered people (wanderer, prisoner, sick, storm-tossed) that map onto a real congregation and whose refrain ("Oh give thanks to the Lord, for he is good, for his steadfast love endures forever") is the theological engine for everything the preacher already wants to say.
2. **Move substitutionary atonement from a single sentence to the sermon's climax.** The current structure buries the cross between the fall illustration and the heaven speculation. Restructure: the fall establishes the problem → the cross provides the solution → gratitude is the only fitting response. Everything else (creation's scope, heaven, Ephesians 5:4) becomes illustration or application of this central movement.
3. **Develop a proper exhortation before the prayer invitation.** Before "let us pray," the preacher should name one specific thing the congregation must do: "Cry to the Lord in your trouble today. Do not leave this room with an unspoken prayer. He has promised to hear you. He proved that promise at the cross." This transforms the participatory close from an activity into a response to proclamation.
4. **Apply Ephesians 5:4 specifically.** Name the thankless speech that a Thanksgiving congregation is most likely carrying: complaining about family, cynicism about the year, bitterness about what they lack, social media grievance. Then show that thanksgiving is not the suppression of these — it is what happens when the heart sees itself as "redeemed from trouble" by a God whose love endures forever.
5. **Cut the heaven speculation entirely.** "God has been working on heaven ever since Earth was made — imagine what He could build in ten thousand years" is speculative, scripturally unmoored, and consumes space that the cross should occupy. It moves the congregation's attention from what Christ has done to what heaven might look like. Replace it with one sentence grounded in a text (e.g., John 14:2-3 or Revelation 21:4).
