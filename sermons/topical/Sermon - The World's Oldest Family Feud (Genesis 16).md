# The World's Oldest Family Feud
## Genesis 16:1-16

---

### Catching Up: Genesis 14-15

Before we get started on chapter 16, let me catch you up on what's happened since we last left Abram.

In chapter 14, Abram made a sacrifice through Melchizedek — who some scholars believe may have been Shem, one of Noah's sons. God was pleased, and he appeared to Abram once more in chapter 15. The first thing Abram asked about was his kids. "God, what are you going to give me since I obviously haven't had any children."

Think about that for a second. You're having a conversation with God where he is answering you back, and Abram could have said or asked for anything. But the only thing on his mind right then are his children. He's essentially saying — maybe you can't heal Sarah's womb, maybe it's just too hard, but I still believe you can do things. Since you couldn't follow through on your promise, what will you give me?

That whole line of thinking is kind of impudent, if you ask me. God made all these promises — huge promises. But when it doesn't happen in a time, and in a way that made Abram happy, he decides to put the thumb screws to God.

He's like I was a few months back at a restaurant. Three of us went out and ordered three prime ribs and a shrimp appetizer. The hors d'oeuvres came and the inside was still frozen. We sent it back, and they microwaved it, and it came back out — acceptable, but nowhere near the high expectations we had for a relatively nice restaurant. Then they finally brought out our steaks. We'd asked them to sear the outside on the grill. They said fine. They brought us steaks that were cold clear through. I don't think that steak saw the inside of an oven, much less a grill.

So we sent those back. They put some water on the plate and microwaved them. At home, fine, whatever. But when you are taking people out for their anniversary, at a nice restaurant, and you pay $25 for the best steak in their house — and you don't get what you want, you get it slowly, and it's not what you want, how you want it, when you want it — you get a little cantankerous. The waitress, who was fantastic by the way, kept our drinks full and was constantly apologizing for the back of the house. She explained everything to the manager. He came over and offered to take $2 off the bill for the trouble we had. I took it. But I haven't been back, and I haven't recommended Kally K's to anyone since.

That's more or less what Abram is doing here. Look, I came to your restaurant. You promised good food. What I received was less than that. How are we going to make this right?

God told Abram in chapter 15 that he was going to have an offspring of his own blood — that though there would be points of turmoil, and times where things looked bleak for his people, ultimately they would be as numerous as the stars in the sky.

That brings us to chapter 16.

---

### The Plan

God has just told Abram he's going to have kids of his own. The implication is a child with Sarai. But what ends up happening is that Sarai decides she's going to have to help God along.

Read with me. Genesis 16:1-16.

---

So Sarai hatches this plan. Maybe, like Abram, she thought God was having a hard time fixing her womb — that her damage was so severe he couldn't heal it. But in truth God was just trying to teach them, and us, a lesson in faith. As we've talked about before, God almost never does things in the time we want. He does them in his time, and often for the same reason. I've told you about Haiti before, and how the money and the passport came a lot later than I had wanted — but arrived at the precise time God wanted them to.

Maybe due to impatience, maybe due to lack of faith, Sarai appeals to local custom to try and help God out. While this seems strange to us today, in Abram's day a marriage was often less about companionship and more about producing children. Barrenness would have struck at the very heart of Sarai's identity. Because of the nature of marital arrangements at that point in the Old Testament, it was customary in that part of the ancient world for a wife to offer her slave to her husband if she could not produce children herself. In fact, ancient records suggest there were preferred arrangements for this kind of thing, and it's likely Hagar came from that world — though we know for certain only that she was Egyptian. Because of the nature of the arrangement, and the fact that Hagar was a slave, any child she bore was by rights Sarai's. Normally she would have raised the child as her own.

So while this seems strange to us, back then this was standard operating procedure.

I want you to notice something about Sarai: she didn't pray before she hatched this plan. She didn't pray before she executed it. And Abram didn't pray before he obeyed his wife and went along with it.

Hagar, I think, gets the worst end of this deal. Because Sarai commanded it, she had no real choice. She was probably in her teens — if she'd been older, she likely would have been married off already. What was done to her was, by any fair reading, very close to rape. Not only that, but she would not have been allowed to keep the child once he had finished nursing, at around two years of age.

Then Sarai started mistreating her. And Hagar ran.

---

### God Saw Her

Next time you wonder if you've done things so bad that you can never be saved, I want you to remember this story. God used this man and this woman to be the father and mother of nations. Not only the Israelites, but most of the countries that are now Muslim will trace their ancestry back to Ishmael — Hagar's son — and thus back to Abraham. All the trouble in the Middle East is the world's oldest family feud, and it all comes down to a little girl who was mistreated.

From Hagar's perspective it was terrible. From Sarai and Abram's perspective, this was something that happened all the time. Some of their friends and acquaintances had done the same. So this horrible thing they did to this poor teenage girl — they were numb to it. Their consciences were unpricked by what they were doing. The same was true of Abram. We don't see him objecting.

But God saw her circumstances. Being moved to compassion, being righteous and just, full of mercy and love — God appeared to her. Look at verses 8-12.

---

One of the things I love about the Bible is that what it said thousands of years ago is still descriptive of the world today. Think about the Muslim community worldwide — many are descended from Hagar. Scripture says Ishmael's hand would be against everyone, and everyone's hand against him. With all the terror attacks, with all the unrest, the riots, and all the tension — scripture said it would happen within a couple of centuries of Noah's flood. Four thousand years ago. God said it would happen, it was recorded in scripture, and it has proved true to this very day.

God is amazing like that. And we shouldn't expect anything less from a God capable of speaking the universe into existence.

I've said it before — there are stars so enormous that our minds can barely fit the scale. Stars that dwarf our sun. Stars larger than earth's entire orbit. The heavens, scripture says, are his footstool. People, we serve a big God. When our big God wants to do something, he can and he will. If he wants to make a woman in her nineties have a baby, he absolutely can. And in Sarai's case, as we'll see in the next chapter, he absolutely did.

---

### Sarai

Let's look at Sarai for a minute, and then a little more at Abram, and then we'll close.

Sarai was a strong woman. I think she had to be. In this passage, Abram dropped the mantle of leadership and Sarai picked it up. We don't know for certain, but I suspect that happened fairly frequently in their marriage. After the situation in Egypt — where Abram told everyone she was his sister and she ended up being given to Pharaoh — I think she probably took a more active role in family decisions from that point on.

I also don't think she had the same level of faith that Abram had. When Abram had doubts in the previous chapter, he asked God directly and received a direct answer. When Sarai had doubts about God's promise, she turned not to God but to the worldly wisdom of the day. And in doing so, she had a little girl put in a position that was, from our perspective, not far from rape.

Because of her exposure to this custom, she was numb to what she was doing. Her conscience wasn't sounding any alarms. The same was true of Abram. Every time we see Abram talking in this chapter, he's essentially saying, "Yes dear." Verse 2 says he listened to the voice of his wife instead of the voice of God. And later in the chapter, when Sarai starts mistreating Hagar, Abram tells her to do whatever she pleases.

In this chapter, Sarai has the backbone. Sarai is leading. And Sarai gets mad at Abram for it.

---

### Sin-Tainted Logic

Consider this: whose plan was it? Abram followed her plan. But who did she blame when she didn't like the outcome of her own plan? Did she point the finger at herself? Certainly not. She blamed Abram.

We're going to call this sin-tainted logic.

How often do we do the exact same thing? We screw up and we blame God. We screw up and we point the finger at our spouse, or at anyone else. I heard someone say it was their drug dealer's fault they were addicted. But unless the dealer held a gun to his head and made him do the drugs, that's not the drug dealer's fault. It's his own fault. It's his sin nature. It's his own innate corruption that causes him to do things God hates.

Everyone knows the old saying — you can't get help until you admit you have a problem. That's absolutely true. When we stop pointing the finger at everyone and everything else, then we can find help. There we can find hope, and forgiveness. Scripture says when we confess our sins, God is faithful and just to forgive our sins and cleanse us from all unrighteousness. Salvation is precipitated by realizing that you and your sinful actions are exactly why you need saving in the first place.

---

### Abram

Fortunately — and I'm saying this again because it bears repeating — there is nothing you can do that is so bad that God can't forgive you. Abram is fortunate in this regard as well. He lets his wife be given to another man. He constantly forgets God's promises. He questions God's ability to fulfill them. When he has a chance to stand for what's right, he doesn't. He bows to the will of his wife and the weight of local culture.

If Abram had stood up in this moment, the world would look different.

This is a heavy mantle we carry, men. Scripture says we are to be the leaders of our households. We need to be leading our families in love, according to scripture. We are held accountable for our own failings, and to a point, for the failings of our families. This shouldn't make us controlling — we are to lead in love.

Scripture says the church is the bride of Christ. How did Christ show us he loved us? Greater love has no man than this — he who lays down his life for a friend. That is exactly what Christ did for us, and more. The method of his death is arguably one of the worst ever conceived by mankind.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm Pastoral Story with a Missing Sermon Inside It**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A conversational, culturally accessible opening of Genesis 16 that surfaces real human texture in the narrative while failing to deliver sustained exposition, gospel proclamation, or a traceable theological argument.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: CONDITIONAL PASS (weak)**
A thesis can be loosely reconstructed: *When people act in unbelief and sin, God still sees and acts in mercy — and we must not evade accountability.* This does arise from the passage, so the gate is passed — but narrowly. The sermon's actual emphasis drifts between Sarai's leadership failures, Abram's passivity, Hagar's victimhood, Middle East geopolitics, and sin-tainted logic. The passage's own main movement — the angel's encounter with Hagar at the well, the naming of God as "El Roi" (the God who sees), and the covenantal complication introduced by Ishmael — receives almost no treatment.

**Authority Test: PASS (marginal)**
The preacher submits to the text's claims and does not obviously soften what offends. However, the bracketed placeholder `*[Gospel presentation.]*` is a significant indicator that the gospel section was not written — it was intended but absent. The authority test passes by what is present; the gospel test fails by what is absent.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Score capped at 69**

- **Fatal Flaw #1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The gospel section is explicitly marked as a placeholder: `*[Gospel presentation.]*` Christ's finished work is not proclaimed anywhere in the sermon. The cross is mentioned briefly and obliquely in the Abram section ("The method of his death is arguably one of the worst ever conceived by mankind") but never as atonement, never as the ground of forgiveness, never as the reason God can be both just and the one who forgives. This is a structural failure, not a minor gap.
- **Fatal Flaw #4 — Application is largely generic**: "There is nothing you can do that is so bad that God can't forgive you" is stated twice without being grounded in what makes that true. The application to men as household leaders is the most specific moment in the sermon, but it applies only to one demographic and is not developed with enough texture to be actionable.
- **Fatal Flaw #5 — No actual exhortation**: The congregation is informed and occasionally challenged, but they are never summoned to a specific response. There is no "therefore" moment. The sermon ends with announcements.

Three fatal flaw flags are active. Score is capped at 69.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 12/25**

- *Textual Fidelity* — 4/10: The sermon tracks the narrative broadly but misses the passage's own climax. The theophany in vv. 7-14 — the angel of the LORD finding Hagar, the divine command, and especially the naming of God as "El Roi" — is the theological center of the chapter. The sermon references "God saw her" but does not open the name or the theological weight of that moment. Verses 13-14 (Hagar naming God, Beer-lahai-roi) receive no treatment. The sermon spends more time on the restaurant story than on the text's resolution.
- *Contextual Awareness* — 3/5: The Genesis 15 context is handled, the Abram/Sarai backstory is noted, and the ancient Near Eastern custom of surrogacy is helpfully explained. This is one of the sermon's genuine strengths. The link to the Abraham covenant is present but underdeveloped.
- *Exegetical Clarity* — 2/5: No engagement with the Hebrew. The name "El Roi" — arguably the most theologically significant element of the chapter — is not unpacked. The text is summarized rather than opened.
- *Logical Faithfulness* — 3/5: No serious eisegesis detected. The application points arise from the narrative. However, the "sin-tainted logic" section drifts from the text into general moral teaching with only a loose tether to the passage.

**Fallen Condition Focus**: Partially identified — unbelief leading to manipulation, moral numbness from cultural accommodation, blame-shifting. These are real and present in the text. But they are not named with surgical precision, and the FCF is never bridged to the gospel's answer.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 10/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness* — 3/7: The Christological connection is introduced through the servant-leadership / cross parallel, which is organic to the Abram section and not forced. However, it is brief, underdeveloped, and not returned to. The angel of the LORD (a potential theophanic appearance) receives no theological comment.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* — 2/3: The Abraham covenant is referenced and the sermon correctly notes the complication introduced by Ishmael. The redemptive-historical location of the passage is gestured at but not inhabited.
- *Doctrinal Precision* — 3/4: No significant doctrinal errors detected. The statement "when we confess our sins, God is faithful and just to forgive" (1 John 1:9) is cited accurately. But the basis of that forgiveness — the atonement — is entirely absent.
- *Confessional Alignment* — 2/4: No contradictions to confessional theology. Covenant themes are present but handled at a popular level with no precision.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 3/15**

The gospel section is a placeholder. What exists in the sermon is one oblique reference to Christ's death and one citation of 1 John 1:9 without the theological underpinning that makes it coherent. An unbeliever could not understand how to be saved from this sermon. A believer would not be freshly amazed at grace. The law/gospel dialectic is absent — the sermon moves from moral failure to general encouragement without the hammer of the law or the relief of the gospel.

*Synagogue Test*: A devout non-Christian would not be offended by this sermon. The cross is mentioned as "arguably one of the worst deaths ever conceived" — this is historical, not soteriological. This fails the synagogue test.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15**

- *Heart Penetration* — 2/5: The sermon has genuine pastoral warmth and the Hagar section shows real compassion. But the conscience is not searched in any Puritan sense. The observation about sin-numbed consciences ("their conscience wasn't sounding any alarms") is a strong diagnostic moment — but it is never turned on the congregation with the same force.
- *Specificity* — 3/5: The restaurant illustration is vivid and the Abram leadership application is specific to men. The Haiti reference adds personal texture. But most application is stated in general terms.
- *Spiritual Differentiation* — 2/5: The sermon primarily addresses believers. There is a brief gesture to the unconverted ("Salvation is precipitated by realizing...") but it is undeveloped. Perkins' categories of the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, the backslider, and the knowledgeable-but-unmoved are not addressed. The sermon largely speaks to one kind of person.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 2/8**

- *Call to Response* — 1/4: The call to stop blame-shifting and confess sin is present but is buried in a paragraph rather than proclaimed as a summons. There is no moment where the congregation is directly addressed with "You must now..."
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness* — 1/4: Men are given a brief word on household leadership. The congregation as a body — corporate responsibility, covenant community — is not addressed. The sermon ends with announcements, not a summons.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**

- *Clarity of Outline* — 2/4: The headers provide structure but the sermon's movement is associative rather than progressive. The sections feel like a tour of the passage rather than a building argument.
- *Progression* — 2/3: There is general forward movement, but the restaurant illustration at the top is very long relative to the theological weight it bears, and the pivot to the gospel is never executed.
- *Unity* — 1/2: Multiple competing ideas — Hagar's victimhood, Middle East geopolitics, sin-tainted logic, male leadership, God's power — coexist without a single dominant idea tying them together.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**

- *Relevance* — 2/2: The restaurant story is vivid and accessible. The Haiti passport story is personal and effective. Both land in the congregation's world.
- *Clarity* — 1/2: The restaurant story is overlong for the point it makes. It illustrates Abram's impatience effectively but the return-on-investment for the word count is low.
- *Memorability* — 0/1: The illustrations are engaging in the moment but unlikely to carry the sermon's main idea home. The congregation will remember the bad steak; it is less clear they will remember what it was meant to teach.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching* — 2/2: The manuscript reads conversationally and with direct address. It sounds like it was written to be spoken, not read. This is a genuine strength.
- *Conviction on the Page* — 1/2: The conviction in the room is personal and warm, but not textually grounded. The Scripture-to-general-statement ratio is low.
- *Cadence and Breath* — 0/1: Sentence variation exists but the sermon never reaches a climactic compression point. It does not build to anything. The ending dissipates rather than landing.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* — Primarily myself: my blame-shifting, my passivity, my conscience. Christ is a brief reference, not the destination.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* — Ideas about Him. The "El Roi" moment — God seeing and naming Himself to a frightened slave girl — is the passage's most encounter-laden verse, and it is summarized rather than inhabited.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* — Informed, with warm pastoral tone. The conscience is touched but not searched.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Cultural-contextual grounding of the surrogacy custom** — The ancient Near Eastern background is explained accessibly and accurately, and the observation that Sarai and Abram were culturally numb to what they were doing is exegetically sound and pastorally sharp.
2. **Hagar's dignity is defended** — The sermon gives unusual attention to Hagar's position as a victim with no real choice, explicitly naming what was done to her as "not far from rape." This is honest and compassionate, and it honors the text's own sympathy for her.
3. **Oral manuscript quality** — The sermon reads like preaching. The voice is natural, direct, and unaffected. There is no academic posturing, no filler. This is a real strength that serves the congregation.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a placeholder** — The bracketed `*[Gospel presentation.]*` is not a minor gap; it is the sermon's center of gravity, and it is absent. Every other strength in the sermon is undermined by the fact that the congregation was not told how they may be saved or why God can forgive the sins that were named throughout.
2. **"El Roi" — the theological climax of the chapter — is not opened** — Verse 13 is the most significant verse in the passage: a slave woman, abandoned and afraid, names God. "You are a God of seeing... truly here I have seen him who looks after me." This is the text's own answer to every human in the room who feels unseen, trapped, or forgotten. The sermon gestures at it ("God saw her") but does not stop there, does not open the Hebrew, does not sit in the moment.
3. **The Middle East geopolitics section is the longest sustained theological reflection in the sermon — and it is tangential** — The paragraph on Ishmael's descendants and modern terrorism, while factually interesting, occupies prime real estate that should have gone to the gospel and the "El Roi" moment. It reads as a sidebar that became the main room.

### 11. Blind Spots

- The preacher appears unaware that the sermon's actual climax — the gospel section — was never written. The sermon was delivered (or prepared for delivery) with a structural hole at its center.
- The "El Roi" name of God is the passage's own answer to the application the sermon is driving toward. The sermon correctly identifies God's compassion for the unseen but does not see that the text *names* that compassion in a way that should have governed the entire structure.
- The sin-tainted logic section, while accurate, is the most generic moment in the sermon. The preacher likely feels it is the application section, but it applies the text at the level of general moral truth rather than gospel-specific conscience work.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The restaurant illustration *feels* strong — it is vivid, relatable, and gets a laugh. But it bears a small load (Abram's impatience) at very high word count. The congregation will remember the bad steak. The illustration has displaced the time needed for "El Roi." The sermon feels accessible because of it, but accessibility without depth is not a virtue.

The observation about Sarai and Abram's numbed consciences also *feels* like a searching moment, but it stops short of turning that observation on the congregation. It diagnoses the characters; it does not press the hearer.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Genesis 16:13 — "You are a God of seeing."** This is the only place in the entire Bible where a human being gives God a new name. It is not Abraham. It is not Moses. It is a teenage Egyptian slave girl who was used, discarded, and driven into the wilderness. And she names God. This is one of the most remarkable verses in the Torah, and it is the text's own gospel moment: God sees the lowest, the forgotten, the one with no rights and no recourse. This is where the sermon should have ended. This is where the gospel should have been proclaimed — not as a separate section, but as the passage's own eruption of grace.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Write the gospel section — do not leave it as a placeholder.** Ground it in the text: God's appearing to Hagar is an act of unearned grace. She did not call on Him. He found her. This is the gospel shape — God pursuing the lost. Then name the cross: this is what it cost God to be the God who sees and does not pass by.
2. **Restructure around "El Roi."** Let the entire sermon build toward Genesis 16:13. Every section should be asking: What does it mean that God sees? The Abram section, the Sarai section, the Hagar section — all of it flows toward the moment a forgotten woman names God.
3. **Cut or compress the restaurant illustration by 60%.** One paragraph, not four. The point is Abram's impatience; make it and move.
4. **Cut the Middle East geopolitics paragraph and replace with a direct address to the unseen in the congregation.** "Some of you are Hagar right now. You did not choose the situation you are in. You are running from something someone else did to you. And this text says God found Hagar in the wilderness. He will find you." This is the application the congregation needs, and the text earns it.
5. **Add a direct exhortation at the close.** After the gospel, summon the congregation: "Stop running. Let the God who sees, see you. Come to the cross. Confess. Believe. He found a slave girl in the desert. He will find you."
6. **Apply the sin-tainted logic section to the congregation directly.** After identifying Sarai's blame-shifting, turn it: "Where are you doing this right now? Who are you blaming for a plan you hatched? Name it. Confess it. That's where God can work."

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered** — The sermon is not doctrinally orthodox enough to qualify. Dead orthodoxy requires correct theology functioning without pastoral heat. This sermon has pastoral warmth but insufficient theological precision. The problem is not dead orthodoxy; it is an incomplete sermon.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

There are genuine flickers of eternal gravity — particularly the compassion for Hagar and the awareness that God sees the forgotten — but the sermon does not carry eternal weight because it never arrives at the gospel that makes God's seeing more than sentiment.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Genesis 16 is Old Testament narrative, and the rubric handles this well. The Fallen Condition Focus framework is particularly useful here — the passage is rich with FCF material (unbelief, moral numbness, exploitation of the powerless). The Christ-connection is organic rather than forced: the angel of the LORD, God's covenantal compassion for the outsider, and the naming of El Roi all point toward the Gospel without requiring an artificial leap. The rubric bends slightly in that "exhortation" in narrative sermons often lands differently than in epistolary sermons — but the absence here is not a genre artifact; it is a structural omission. The rubric fits this passage well.
