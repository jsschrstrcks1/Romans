# Power Encounter
## A Sermon on 1 Samuel 28

**Series**: The Night Is Far Gone
**Type**: Evangelism — Reaching Those in Wicca, the Occult, and the Psychic World

## Opening

I had actually planned to talk about atheists today, but God led me in another direction Friday morning when I encountered an ad by a woman calling herself a "woman of God" — and worse, calling herself a pastor — and then advertising her ability to speak to the dead, find information from spirits, and through the manipulation of spirits force former lovers to return to you.

It was infuriating to me that a person would call themselves a woman of God, and a pastor, and yet intentionally engage in activities that violate Scripture. We are going to spend most of our time in 1 Samuel 28 this morning, but before we get to that, I need three people to each look up a verse and read it out for us: Leviticus 19:31, Leviticus 20:6, and Leviticus 20:27. These are all similar in message and purpose. But they inform the backstory of our passage today.

> "Do not turn to mediums or necromancers; do not seek them out, and so make yourselves unclean by them: I am the Lord your God."
> — Leviticus 19:31 (ESV)

> "If a person turns to mediums and necromancers, whoring after them, I will set my face against that person and will cut him off from among his people."
> — Leviticus 20:6 (ESV)

> "A man or a woman who is a medium or a necromancer shall surely be put to death. They shall be stoned with stones; their blood shall be upon them."
> — Leviticus 20:27 (ESV)

## The Backstory

While you are turning there I'll tell you about what's going on in 1 Samuel. At this point in time Saul is King of Israel, David is one of his soldiers. You remember the story about Saul getting bad because the people sang songs about David saying "Saul has killed his thousands, but David his ten thousands." Saul was the king, and the chief military officer, and frequently fought alongside his troops. But he just wasn't as effective of a warrior as David. God's hand was on David, and God had turned his back upon Saul. Saul had been disobeying the commands of God. In so doing, the Philistines had gotten the upper hand in their multi-generational battle for dominance in the region.

Saul is absolutely completely desperate. He's so upset he's sitting there shaking. You ever been so upset that all you could do is sit there and shake? I was at the vet on Wednesday, and the vet tech thought my dog was so afraid she was shaking. But what she couldn't see is I was scratching her itchy chest for her. What she thought was quaking in fear was her foot tapping because she was enjoying a good scratch where she itches. Good news on that front — it turns out she has a food allergy, and not mites! I have to give her food without chicken, beef, or lamb in it going forward.

Anyway, while they were checking out my dog, this older gentleman walked in with a little dachshund. The dog was clearly old, and in pain. I tried to strike up a conversation with the guy and found out his little dog was 17 years old. After telling me that he quit talking — all he could do was sit there and shake. He was trying his hardest not to cry. I sort of connected the dots, that he was there to put his faithful friend down. This was confirmed by the conversation he had with the vet tech. Afterwards I told the man that I had just lost Grace a few months back, and I'd be praying for him. The man hopped up and ran out of there like a scalded dog. If you think about him, keep him in your prayers.

Anyway, Saul was shaking like that man who lost his dog. He felt like he was at the end of his rope, surrounded by enemies, about to lose everything. He was the first king of Israel, and probably thought he was about to be the last king of Israel.

In his desperation, he turns to a medium.

## The Hebrew Word: Oboth

A medium in Hebrew is *Oboth*. *Oboth* is a word with a lot of definitions. Depending on the little dots and lines above and below it, it can mean a lot of things — whore, it can mean, or concubine. But in Scripture it's only used four ways, and the most common way that it is used is of one who seeks out evil spirits for their own ends. In this specific instance, *Oboth* is translated "medium," because Saul is seeking out one who can talk to a dead person.

There are basically two types of mediums out there today. Those that are fake — I think this is probably the bulk of them — and those that talk to demons. As a Christian, do we want to give our time, money, and attention to either?

This woman Saul found seems to be in a third category. Pay attention when we get to verse 12 though. I think this might be the first time her quote-unquote powers actually worked. It startles her and she screams.

## False Spiritual Authority

In the Catholic church, the Pope has a special chair, and when he sits in the chair, anything he says is believed by the Catholics to have come straight from the mouth of God. I can assure you that's not how that works at all. Anyone can talk to God. God has given us the entirety of his revealed word in the Bible. God still talks to us today, but usually through the Bible, and through that still small voice inside of us, and sometimes through the testimony of other believers. Magic chairs have nothing to do with it. In fact, if you look at the things Popes have said from their magic throne over the years, I think it becomes clear that perhaps the voice the Pope hears from the chair is not only NOT the voice of God, but perhaps the voice of our enemy.

Here are some things the Popes have said. Francis, the current Pope, said all atheists will go to heaven if they are good enough. But what about what God says in Ephesians 2:8-9?

> For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast.
> — Ephesians 2:8-9 (ESV)

In addition to things that are against Scripture, the Popes frequently say things from their magic chair that contradict other Popes. The first one says the sky is blue, the second says no, no, it's always purple and green polka dots.

There are two categories of worst possible things they can say. The first one is things that violate Scripture. The second is things that violate children. Pope Boniface said around 1000 AD that "sex with male children is no more a problem than one hand rubbing the other." Little wonder that "church" has wandered so far from the word of God. I could go on. But I think that makes my point that when the Pope speaks on behalf of God, he is not speaking on behalf of God, but on behalf of either himself, or our enemy. He — at that point — is little better than a medium himself.

## The Occult in Our Community

In our area psychics are quite in demand. In fact, there are more people seeking out psychics than seeking out churches according to the Pasco yellow pages website. "Psychic" was the 20th most popular search term; 19th was "metaphysical store." Pagan practices of all types are huge in our community. Wiccan covens are adopting roads, and stepping out of the dark shadows that in other communities they hide in.

The group we are going to be talking about reaching tonight is generally any group that gets its power from demons — whether they realize that's where their power comes from or not.

Ouija boards are often a young person's first foray into this realm. A Ouija board is sold in the children's game aisle, rated for age 8 and up. The game claims you are asking questions of a spirit. There are two kinds of spirits, right? Angels and demons. Angels only deliver messages at the command of God — you can't seek them out and ask them questions generally. You can only ask them questions when God sends them and they appear to you. The Ouija board, then, is not a way God's angels would communicate with you. The Ouija board, then — if you contact anything, you are contacting directly a demon.

Other games like Bloody Mary, or Light as a Feather also have demonic ties. All of these things that we've been discussing — Wicca, Satanism, the occult, Ouija boards, Bloody Mary, Light as a Feather, psychics, séances, mediums, and witchcraft — these all fall under that Hebrew word for medium: *Oboth*.

## Old Testament and New Testament

> "If a person turns to mediums and necromancers, whoring after them, I will set my face against that person and will cut him off from among his people."
> — Leviticus 20:6 (ESV)

That is an Old Testament command, true. But let me remind you of this. God is the same, yesterday, today, and tomorrow. If God hated something yesterday, how does he feel about it today? We are the bride of Christ, are we not? If he felt like one of his children seeking out a medium was whoring themselves out — that's God's word, not mine — then how does God feel about one of his children today seeking out such things?

What's changed is the punishment. In the Old Testament sin economy, punishments were often immediate and severe. Saul, for instance, getting himself and his kids killed almost immediately after seeking the witch of Endor. Likewise Isaiah 2:6 says that God rejected the house of Jacob over witchcraft, sorcery, fortune tellers — calling it idolatry.

Here's the thing though. We know that in the Old Testament Saul and Samuel ended up in the same place. Look at 1 Samuel 28:19 — "tomorrow you and your sons shall be with me."

This was back when the law ruled, and sacrificing an animal covered the sins of a man. In the New Testament, God is more patient, and longsuffering with us. Committing a sin today does not often mean death in a hailstorm of arrows tomorrow. However that does not mean there is no cost, or consequence for dabbling in these things. Ultimately, if you spend your life courting demons, in the end — apart from the saving grace of Jesus Christ — you will spend your life with the demons in hell.

## Matt Slick's Story

The founder of CARM — the Christian Apologetics and Research Ministry — with whom I am acquainted, got involved with these types of things when he was a kid. His brother was a believer in Jesus Christ, and was constantly witnessing to him, but my friend had no interest. He started out in Ouija boards, Wicca, and attempting astral projection. Ultimately they ended up in necromancy, and the occult. He kept progressing deeper and deeper into these things, and he did find power. Anyway, one day, he and his buddy were trying to summon a dead person, and this ball of light appeared to them that slowly turned into a man with the face of a cross. They freaked out, and went to their friends who had been involved in this longer than they, and were told that's a very bad sign, and they need to quit right now. That they had found a power greater than anything they could control or fight. That if they continued it would likely cost their life.

This was the slap in the face my friend Matt needed to get out of this mess. Ultimately he started searching for this greater power, and ended up becoming a Christian.

He wrote:

> "Those in Wicca are unwittingly contacting demonic forces and because of that, they gain a form of power. But, that power has a cost. The cost is darkness. I remember very clearly the anti-Christian attitude that gradually grew in me the further into darkness I went. I also remember the fascination with things of darkness such as skulls, pentagrams, the darkness of night, and a general defiant attitude. The more I got involved with that, the more these things increased. There is definitely a spiritual connection with the author of the power behind the occult. Likewise, there is definitely a power that comes from the true and living God, Jesus Christ. He has far more strength, clarity, and truth than anything Wicca can offer. Though the occult is real, God is, too, and the only safeguard against the enemy is salvation in Jesus Christ."
> — Matt Slick, founder of CARM (Christian Apologetics and Research Ministry)

## The Power Encounter

Any time you are witnessing to anyone, you should have spent time in prayer beforehand. It's best to have someone praying during, and afterwards spend time praying for those who you spoke to.

This is especially true with anyone involved with these things. God is our shield, our fortress, our strong tower against these types of spiritual situations. Any time you engage in evangelism with someone lost in these things, expect a fight on your hands. It would be like dropping a soldier off behind enemy lines. The difference here is God has already won the war, won the battle, and won every fight. But don't be surprised when you see the fighting. Scripture says:

> "For we do not wrestle against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the cosmic powers over this present darkness, against the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly places."
> — Ephesians 6:12 (ESV)

So spend time in prayer asking God to equip and prepare you.

One common story I hear out of those who have had this sort of background — actually there are two themes in their story. The first theme is that they kept descending down until they reached a point that scared them. The second theme is that in their search for power they encountered God, and he stopped them from taking the next step. It's sort of like how when you pray for alcoholics to hate the taste of liquor, they frequently find their favorite booze now tastes like chemicals or lighter fluid.

In Bible college my professor called that a "power encounter" — praying for these people to have an encounter with the true and living God in such a way that their existing system is proved to be bankrupt.

## Denete

I worked with a lady named Denete. She was a practicing witch. Every day when I would get to work she would tell me how she was casting spells on me — for my car to break, for physical illness, for my death, and a hundred other things. I'll tell you there was never a time in my life where I was healthier. I was in a car accident, but the insurance paid more than the cost of the repairs by about a thousand dollars. God was good!

Ultimately the company ended up closing and we all lost our jobs at about the same time.

She never did accept the life-saving truth of the gospel. But she did ask me one day if I knew why her spells didn't work on me. I told her: because of my Lord and Savior Jesus Christ, who is stronger than any demonic force that can be brought to bear on anyone. A child of God is protected, safe and secure. She cursed at me and walked off. But she knows the truth. I think if the company had stayed open that over time she would have accepted Christ.

## How to Reach Them

The best thing you can do, when trying to reach a person stuck in any of the types of Wicca, or the occult, is to keep taking them back to Scripture. Scripture has a washing effect. Don't just read it to them, but have them read it out loud.

You can start right at the beginning.

Creation. The Fall. The Cross. Redemption.

**Target Group**: Those involved in Wicca, the occult, psychics, mediums, Satanism, séances, necromancy, witchcraft, and related practices.

### 3. Berean Gate

**PASS** — with a qualification.

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's thesis — consulting mediums is forbidden by God, brings judgment, and those entrapped in these practices need to be reached with the gospel — demonstrably arises from 1 Samuel 28, supported by the Leviticus prohibitions and the narrative conclusion in chapter 31. Pass.

*Authority Test*: The preacher stands under the text's authority. He does not soften the prohibitions. He quotes Leviticus 20:27 (the death penalty) without flinching. He does not minimize Saul's judgment. Pass.

The qualification: While the text is the stated foundation, the sermon spends significant time away from 1 Samuel 28 — the Pope section, the Pasco County statistics, and the Matt Slick story together comprise more than half the manuscript. The text is the launching pad; it does not remain the orbit. The gate is passed, but the preacher should know how far from the text he travelled.

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Score capped at 69.**

This is not a full double-flag, but it is noted as a critical weakness below.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The vet illustration is sermon-caliber work.** The older gentleman with the dying dachshund — sitting there shaking, unable to speak, just trying not to cry — lands Saul's desperation with emotional precision. "Saul was shaking like that man who lost his dog. He felt like he was at the end of his rope, surrounded by enemies, about to lose everything." This is how illustrations are supposed to work: native to the text's emotional register, drawn from real life, not imported from a sermon illustration database. It will be remembered.

2. **The Denete story is the spiritual heart of the sermon.** Daily curses that produced nothing but health and reimbursed car repairs — and the moment she finally asked why her spells didn't work — is a lived testimony to the sovereignty of God over demonic power. "She cursed at me and walked off. But she knows the truth." That sentence has the weight of genuine pastoral experience. This is the sermon's closest approach to gospel witness, and it lands.

3. **The evangelism equipping section is specific and actionable.** Prayer before, during, and after. Expect warfare. Take them to Scripture and have them read it aloud. Start at Creation, Fall, Cross, Redemption. These are not vague exhortations — they are a deployable methodology. For a congregation being sent into a community with more psychics than churches, this is genuinely useful pastoral equipping.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is named but never proclaimed.** "Saving grace of Jesus Christ" (OT/NT section) and "Creation. The Fall. The Cross. Redemption." (How to Reach Them section) are the sermon's only gospel content — and neither is developed. The congregation is told what to tell others (Creation/Fall/Cross/Redemption) but never told it themselves, freshly and fully, in this sermon. What does the cross actually accomplish? Why must it be Christ's blood and not moral improvement? Who is Christ that his name stops demonic power? These questions are not answered. The congregation hears the framework but not the freight.

2. **The primary text is never preached — only narrated.** The Scripture reading ("1 Samuel 28:6-20 (ESV)") is a placeholder. The passage itself — Samuel's devastating words, the medium's terror at seeing a genuine spirit rather than a fabricated one, Saul's collapse and inability to eat, the medium's act of kindness — receives almost no textual engagement. The sermon knows the story; it does not preach the passage. The text's own dramatic force, its irony (Saul drove out mediums but now seeks one), and its theological weight (God will not answer Saul, even through the very prophet whose death Saul contributed to) are entirely undeveloped.

3. **The Pope section is a costly detour that never returns.** The False Spiritual Authority section makes a valid point — there are counterfeit channels of divine communication, and the ex cathedra chair functions like a medium's table. But the detour runs long, introduces a competing subject (Roman Catholic ecclesiology and papal scandal), and the connection to 1 Samuel 28 is loose. The section occupies space that should belong to the text, to the gospel, or to the conscience of the person in the room who consulted a psychic last Tuesday. The Pope is a useful foil; he should not be a featured character.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

**The preacher does not see the person in the congregation who has dabbled in these things.** The sermon frames the entire congregation as evangelists going *out* to occultists — but in a county where psychic searches outnumber church searches, some of those occultists are in the room. The person who attended a séance out of grief, the teenager who bought a Ouija board with friends, the woman who consulted a psychic after a diagnosis, the believer who has spiritually drifted toward horoscopes or tarot out of boredom or loneliness — none of these people are addressed. The sermon trains missionaries but does not search hearts.

**The preacher does not see the implicit moralism.** The sermon's gravitational center is: *do not consult mediums, and help others out of these things.* Without a fully proclaimed gospel at the center, this is moralistic in structure — good behavior (don't consult mediums, do evangelism, do pray) replaces grace as the organizing principle. The indicatives (what God has done in Christ) are underdeveloped relative to the imperatives (what you must now do). The Denete story nearly becomes a testimony to the preacher's faithfulness rather than to Christ's power.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

**The evangelism equipping section feels like an application section, but it is not a conscience-searching application section.** The sermon ends with a training outline — seven-step methodology for reaching occultists — and this has the *feel* of practical application. It is not. Application distinguishes hearers and searches the heart. This is instruction for a specific task. It equips believers for outreach but does not press any individual sinner in the congregation toward conviction or Christ. The section is genuinely useful; it is just not what preaching's application is supposed to accomplish.

**The Oboth word study feels like exegesis but is thin.** Mentioning the Hebrew word, noting its range of meaning, and observing that v.12 startles the medium — this has the texture of exegetical engagement without the depth. A listener might trust the exegetical foundation more than the work warrants.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**The text's own irony is one of the richest veins in 1 Samuel 28, and it was never mined.** Saul expelled all mediums from Israel (v.3) and then secretly sought one (v.7-8). He is the man who enforced God's law against necromancy, now violating it in desperate darkness. This is the anatomy of spiritual ruin — when God goes silent because of accumulated disobedience, the desperate heart turns to the very thing it once condemned. That is a sermon moment that could search the conscience of every hearer who has drifted, every Christian who has sought comfort from the wrong source, every person who has known the truth and then, in a moment of desperation, reached for something they knew was wrong.

This irony was not named. It was not pressed. It was the text's most powerful gift to the preacher's application, and it went unopened.

**Samuel's words in v.19 — "tomorrow you and your sons shall be with me" — received significant attention for the OT/NT comparison, but the theological weight was left on the table.** Saul, who died under judgment, ended up in the same place as Samuel, who died in faithfulness. The sermon uses this to make a redemptive-historical point about OT animal sacrifice. But the deeper question — how is it that a man under God's judgment ends with a righteous prophet? — opens directly onto the theology of grace and atonement. What covers Saul's sin? What covers anyone's sin? That question was right there in the text and was never asked.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Walk through 1 Samuel 28:6-20 verse by verse in the body of the sermon.** The Scripture reading placeholder is not preaching. At minimum, give the congregation three textual anchors: Saul's desperation and God's silence (v.6), Samuel's verdict and its finality (v.15-19), and Saul's collapse (v.20). Let the text carry its own drama rather than summarizing it in narrative backstory.

2. **Proclaim the gospel fully in the OT/NT section.** The transition from "what's changed is the punishment" to "saving grace of Jesus Christ" is the natural moment to preach it. Expand that single parenthetical into two or three sentences: *Why* is the cross the answer? *What* did Christ accomplish that animal sacrifice only foreshadowed? *How* does a person enter into the protection that Denete could not break? The four-word outline (Creation/Fall/Cross/Redemption) is a skeleton — give it flesh here, not just in the "How to Reach Them" section.

3. **Address the person in the congregation who has dabbled.** Before moving to the evangelism equipping section, add a direct address: "Some of you in this room are not the evangelists I'm describing. You're the person we'd be going after. You've consulted a psychic. You've used a Ouija board. You've been curious about what the other side offers. This text is for you too — and the grace of Jesus Christ is wider than any darkness you've walked into." The congregation needs to know the message is not only *for them to deliver* but *for them to receive*.

4. **Either cut or sharpen the Pope section.** The false spiritual authority point is legitimate, but the section currently functions as a digression. If it stays, compress it to two paragraphs and return directly to 1 Samuel 28: the Pope's chair and Saul's medium are both unauthorized channels — one is a counterfeit of revelation, the other a counterfeit of prophecy. Then move on. Cut the Boniface quote entirely — it introduces scandal that serves the argument only marginally and redirects emotional energy away from the sermon's main purpose.

5. **Name the Fallen Condition Focus explicitly.** The FCF of 1 Samuel 28 is desperation — the human condition of needing guidance, comfort, and connection with something beyond the natural when God feels absent. That longing is not evil; consulting demons to satisfy it is. Name this: "Every person in this room has felt the silence Saul felt — when God seems far and the darkness feels close. The question is not whether you hunger for spiritual reality. The question is where you go when you do." This bridge makes the text speak to every hearer, not only those with explicit occult ties.

6. **Move the climax.** The sermon's structural climax is currently "Creation. The Fall. The Cross. Redemption." — four words that land with rhetorical punch but carry no weight because they were never unpacked. The true climax should be the gospel moment: what the Cross accomplished, why it alone can break demonic bondage and cover the sin of the person who courted demons. That climax should be followed by the Denete story as testimony, not precede it as anticlimax.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon does not score high enough in Exposition or Theology to trigger dead orthodoxy diagnostics. The weakness here is not cold orthodoxy — it is incomplete proclamation. The fire is present in places; the full gospel content is what's missing.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

There is genuine pastoral weight in this sermon — the Denete story, the spiritual warfare framing, and the honest acknowledgment that evangelizing occultists is like dropping behind enemy lines — but it does not culminate in worship, and the congregation is more likely to leave equipped for a task than undone before God.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This passage is narrative, and the rubric bends slightly in one direction: 1 Samuel 28 is not a doctrinal epistle, and the demand for verse-by-verse exegetical development (Exposition subcategory) applies differently here than in, say, Romans 3. Narrative sermons legitimately draw the theological freight from the story rather than parsing individual clauses. The exposition score reflects not that narrative was the wrong genre, but that the sermon did not develop even the narrative's own most powerful moments — the irony, the terror, the verdict, the collapse. The rubric is fit for this text; the sermon simply did not use the text it had.

The evangelistic orientation (series: "The Night Is Far Gone") also shifts the genre slightly toward training and equipping, which the rubric accommodates under Exhortation & Response. The evangelism framework scores appropriately there. What the evangelistic purpose does not excuse is the absence of a gospel proclamation — an evangelism sermon that does not itself evangelize is not a paradox the rubric resolves in the sermon's favor.
