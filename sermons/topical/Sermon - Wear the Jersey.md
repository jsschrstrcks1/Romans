# Wear the Jersey
## A Sermon on 1 Peter 1:1-9

**Series**: The Night Is Far Gone
**Type**: Special Message — Easter

---

## Opening

A friend of mine has a few kids, and he recently was asked to be the coach of the basketball team that one of his sons played for. He said the first night was a struggle. They had to decide on an identity. He took a poll, and the top three choices were the Tigers, the Senators, and "the Basketball Team." He was kind of hoping for the Tigers, and he would have been ok with the Senators, but the team really, really wanted "the Basketball Team." So he relented and now they are the Washington Basketball Team. That is their identity, and their rallying cry as they go forward now. When you have a team it's important to be about that team, and not some other team.

I remember when I was a kid I was on this one soccer team called "United." We were a pretty good team, my friend from school was the goalie, and he and I worked really well together. I was on defense, and we were hard to get around. Anyway, this one particular game, the team we were facing — and I've forgotten the name, I just remember their jerseys were hot pink — anyway, we were doing really good. They weren't even really getting to our side of the field. We were up by like 6 points, and that was when it happened. Our forward had the ball, and he was running towards the goal, and this one kid masterfully slipped in and stole the ball. He dodged and dodged and dodged our players, and by the time he had reached the middle part of their half of the field, he had lost all of our players. He was fast.

He paused, and in the heat of the moment, he wound up and kicked that ball as hard as he could, and scored a goal — on his own team. Soccer teams usually only have 11 players, but in that moment, United had a 12th player supporting our team, and our goals, and ultimately gave us our 7th point. We went on to win that game 8 to 0. The kid that stole the ball was pulled out of the game by his coach at that point, and likely got a stern talking to. He was put back in the game, but he only scored the one goal, and it was for the team opposing him.

My friend continued his story saying that one of his players thought the name was really stupid, and so he refused to wear the jersey. My friend, his coach, said if you don't wear the jersey you can't play any of the games, and will only be riding the bench. If you don't like the jersey, you might want to consider quitting the team. Apparently that kid came to every game and practice anyway, and over the course of the whole season wouldn't wear the jersey and so he didn't get to play in any of the games. The parents came up to the coach at one point, and asked why their son wasn't being allowed to play — sure he's not the best at scoring, and he's kind of short. He may not be the fastest kid, or the strongest kid, and he can't shoot from the 3pt line to save his life. But he comes to every game, and every practice, why can't he play? Hasn't he proven himself? And my friend said to the parents — it's true that he can't throw or block, or score. He's the shortest kid on the team, and the slowest. And it's true that he does come to every game and every practice, and him coming to every game and every practice would be enough to put him in to play. But he refuses to wear the jersey.

Folks, when you're on a team, it's important to wear the jersey. Who and whose we are affects every facet of our life. Not only who we are, but it should also affect our thinking as well — how we think about the things that we do and say, and others do or say. I'd even go so far as to say our struggles mostly come back to forgetting which team we are on.

## Our Team Name: Elect

Our team name is right there in the first verse — "elect exiles." I read a story this week about a basketball player. He was highly sought after but for a variety of non-basketball reasons, he ended up playing for a team in Charlotte, North Carolina. This team is the losingest team in basketball. They had the worst offense in the league, and the worst defense. Hard to come back from that — year after year they lost every game. But this guy chose that team. Things got so bad the team decided to change names, thinking that might help, but they continued to be the worst in basically every metric — this team was so bad they don't even have any players that made it to the hall of fame. A few years go by and someone notices this guy, and offers him a really great contract to go and play for the Celtics. He went from the worst team in the league, to the team that year that had the best offense, the best defense. He went from a team with no players in the hall of fame, to playing in the championship game while hall of famers watched from the stands. He's good enough some people say he will be in the hall of fame one day. All it took was a change of teams.

Our team name is elect exiles. I think I like that better than "the Basketball Team." But let's unpack that a bit. A lot of people bristle at the word elect, but it's right here in scripture, and it applies to us, so we should understand what it means a little, right?

So who can tell me what "elect" means?

The premier Greek-to-English dictionary, *A Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament and Other Early Christian Literature*, says the Greek word *eklektos* means "chosen, elect, picked out, selected, made excellent."

Liddell and Scott in their Greek-English lexicon translate the same word "picked out, selected." Louw-Nida says "chosen." In Moulton and Milligan's *Vocabulary of the Greek New Testament*, they translate the word "choice" or "chosen," and "to become beautiful." NASB translates it as "resident aliens," the newer NIV translates that word as "elect exiles," the older NIV translates it as "God's elect, strangers in the world." The King James Version is missing words at this point. It leaves the Greek word *eklektos* out altogether. So if you're reading from that translation, it's weak here, and instead simply says "strangers." I have a Greek New Testament on my laptop, and I can show you in the Greek where that word should be. But it isn't there in the King James. It's either a poor translation choice, or more likely a result of having access to fewer manuscripts of lesser quality at the time of translation.

Point is the Bible, in the Greek, and in maybe 30 different Bible translations says "elect" right there. Elect meaning chosen of God — and I loved "made beautiful" or "made excellent." Or simply: chosen.

These are people that God has chosen to himself. Or John 6:44 which says:

> "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him. And I will raise him up on the last day."
> — John 6:44 (ESV)

These are the people drawn by God to Jesus. They are the elect. We the elect Gentiles are who Romans 9 talks about when it says:

> "What if God, desiring to show his wrath and to make known his power, has endured with much patience vessels of wrath prepared for destruction, in order to make known the riches of his glory for vessels of mercy, which he has prepared beforehand for glory — even us whom he has called, not from the Jews only but also from the Gentiles? As indeed he says in Hosea, 'Those who were not my people I will call "my people," and her who was not beloved I will call "beloved."'"
> — Romans 9:22-25 (ESV)

This is scripture quoting scripture here — it's a quote from Hosea 1, and in Hosea 1, God tells Hosea to marry a prostitute, and that his children will be named: about to be punished, not my people, and no mercy.

This is a glorious promise for us as Gentiles. You know of course there are three groups of people in scripture: the Jews — God's people, the Gentiles — not God's people, and those in between were called Samaritans. In short what's being said is that God has chosen people that are not Jews to be a part of the promise also — the new covenant consummated in the death of Christ. In fact if you read the New Testament, you'll see there is a Pentecost for the Jews, one for the Gentiles, and one for the Samaritans. No longer is God only pulling for the Jews — he's expanded that to include us. We can all be on the same team now.

But still, it blows my mind to think about this — that we who rejected God, and chose sin, and in doing so shattered creation. That we who rejected his Son, and killed him by crucifixion — one of the most grisly and shameful forms of execution one can endure — should still want anything to do with us after. You might say it wasn't me, I wasn't there, I didn't have anything to do with Christ's execution. People, if you have ever committed a sin, Christ died for that sin. If we as a people had never sinned, then we as a people wouldn't be guilty of Christ's death. But we have as a people, and we have individually, and that sin required Christ's death. We are guilty. Yet in spite of that guilt, God chased us down, and overcame us, and drew us to his Son, so that we could be God's children.

I think if someone murdered my child, I'd be hard pressed to sit in the same room with the murderer, and yet God pursued us, and called us, and made us his children. What a wonderful, marvelous God we serve. Today, on Easter we remember that day. Not the day Christ was murdered but the day he defeated sin and death and hell and Satan and rose again. Easter is a celebration of the resurrection of Jesus Christ.

---

## Our Team Name: Exiles

The second word was exiles. Some of the translations — like the NASB — say "resident aliens." We are here, but this world isn't our home. We're just passing through.

Our citizenship is in heaven, and God brings us onto the new team. We're no longer playing for the losing team. As believers in Christ, now we play for the winning team. We're no longer bound for an eternity in hell. We can spend an eternity in heaven. And yet we are not yet in heaven, are we. We are here, on earth, in a form of exile. We are strangers in a foreign land. We're living here, but this is not our home. This is like living in a hotel for a while — like we are on vacation in a hostile country. Imagine going to bed one night, and waking up in North Korea — a land hostile to Americans, a land where we don't speak the language, or understand the culture. That's not too dissimilar from what this world is to the Christian. These people that Peter was writing to were citizens of the Roman Empire — the dominant world power — and yet he calls them exiles. Because even though they were Romans, their citizenship is no longer of earth, their citizenship is of heaven. That's Peter's point — even though you were born and raised here, this is not your home. You have a different homeland, a higher homeland. You're foreigners here, and your home is heaven.

I love verse 2 of 1 Peter. Elect according to God's foreknowledge. He knew you, he knew what part you would play in the world, however big or small, and he knew what sins you would commit. He knew what good you would do, and what bad you would do. And he chose to love you anyway. In love he adopted us as sons and daughters. In love he chose to allow his Son to die for us. In love he made us alive in Christ. A Puritan, Thomas Watson, said it this way: Satan wanted Christ to prove his deity by turning rocks into bread, but Jesus has worked a far greater miracle by turning hard hearts of stone into flesh. In repentance Christ turns a heart of stone to flesh. What a wonderful thing. If you've ever looked around and said, hey, there is something wrong with this world — it goes right back to sin. Right back to those hearts of stone. It taints everything. It's why there is suffering in this world. It's why we struggle. Anyone have struggles in their life?

---

## A Living Hope

I want to read verses 3-9 here.

> Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! According to his great mercy, he has caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead, to an inheritance that is imperishable, undefiled, and unfading, kept in heaven for you, who by God's power are being guarded through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed in the last time. In this you rejoice, though now for a little while, if necessary, you have been grieved by various trials, so that the tested genuineness of your faith — more precious than gold that perishes though it is tested by fire — may be found to result in praise and glory and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ. Though you have not seen him, you love him. Though you do not now see him, you believe in him and rejoice with joy that is inexpressible and filled with glory, obtaining the outcome of your faith, the salvation of your souls.
> — 1 Peter 1:3-9 (ESV)

I love this part. Because of what Christ did on Easter — because of his death, burial and resurrection — we have hope. A living hope in verse 3, an imperishable inheritance. When you're going through hard times look back at what Christ has done for you, and look ahead to what's coming. Because what's coming is absolutely amazing. No more sadness, no more tears. We lost a loved one this week. But we know that one day soon we're going to see her again in glory. I'll talk more about who in a minute. But for now we can rest on the promise that all those we have lost, who are in Christ, we will be reunited with.

We have a marvelous hope. I was thinking about some of these other religions out there. Specifically Buddhism, because I was talking to a Buddhist this week. Doesn't Buddhism sound awful?

What's their hope? That one day when they die, they get to come back to this? To go through all this again? That's their hope? There is no hope in that. One day when we take our last breath we will have finally finished the test, finished boot camp, and will have eternity to bask in the glory of our Savior. Perfect justice, perfect peace, perfect love, perfect joy for eternity. In a billion to the billionth power years we will just be getting started. If eternity were a book, a billion times a billion years to the billionth power wouldn't even cover the foreword. And then forever — no tears, no death, no sickness, no more cancer. No more wheelchairs. No more infirmity. We will be free from all that. Forever.

## Wear the Jersey

> Do not love the world or the things in the world. If anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him. For all that is in the world — the desires of the flesh and the desires of the eyes and pride in possessions — is not from the Father but is from the world.
> — 1 John 2:15-16 (ESV)

With all that God has done for us — even in spite of our sin, and our guilt and shame in the death of Christ — those that love the world are like that boy who wouldn't wear the jersey. When you choose to live for this world, to live for the flesh, and the pleasures of this world, it's like the boy who wouldn't wear the jersey. It's like you're saying, yeah, God, I know your Son died for me, but I don't need it. I don't want it. I'd rather be something and someone else. It's a form of spitting in the eye of God.

I doubt anyone here or who's watching with us online would intentionally look God in the eye, and spit in his face, and yet through our actions, sometimes that's exactly what we do.

Like that boy from the hot pink soccer team, that scored a goal on his own team. He sort of spit in the eye of his team. I don't think he meant to, but the net result of his actions were bad for his team, and good for his enemy.

It would be like the basketball player that I was telling you about — it would be like him going back to the Charlotte losing team on purpose after getting the good paying job with the Celtics, after having good teammates on the Celtics, after winning the season with the Celtics. It would be like showing up to the Celtics vs. Charlotte game, and putting on the Charlotte jersey and playing like he's on the Charlotte team.

It would be ridiculous. But that's exactly what we do when we sin as Christians.

In this analogy there are only two teams.

Which team do you play for? Consider your actions — do you play for God's team? Or for the team of his enemy?

## In Memoriam

I mentioned a bit ago that we lost someone this week. Do you guys remember Larry and Judy Ayers? Late last week, Judy passed away. She came home from an appointment, laid down, and that was the end. She took her last breath. She knew Jesus as her Savior, and so we who are followers of Christ will see her again one day. We met Judy through my grandmother. Granny used to teach riding lessons, and Judy was her student. The three of them hit it off, and have been fast friends ever since. They came to church, and have been attending here ever since I think their first week in Florida, maybe the second. They watch us online from Ohio even still, and stay in touch. Judy loved animals. She loved horses, and little dogs. I got to meet her new little dogs last time she was in town. She was quite the dog trainer, and I don't know that I had ever seen her happier than when she was loving on animals.

We don't know when the service will be for Judy, but we know there will be one, and it will most likely be in Ohio, so we plan to send some flowers, and have a card to sign, and send up there.

---

## Closing

But all this just drives home the point that you don't know how much time you have left. You don't know when you'll take that last breath, you don't know just exactly when you'll meet God face to face. But I do know that you can know how the big strokes of that conversation will go in advance. Scripture says either God will say "Well done, my good and faithful servant," and welcome you into glory, as he has done this week with Judy, or he'll say "Depart from me, I don't know you."

If you have a relationship with Jesus Christ, then you'll have the former conversation, and if not, then you'll have the latter one. All of that starts with the gospel.

# Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**File**: Sermon - Wear the Jersey.md
**Text**: 1 Peter 1:1–9
**Occasion**: Easter Sunday
**Evaluator**: Thus Says the Lord Rubric
**Date**: 2026-03-22

---

## 1. Verdict Title

**An Easter Sermon With Excellent Election Doctrine and a Resurrection-Shaped Hole at Its Center**

---

## 2. One-Sentence Summary

A Reformed, identity-driven Easter sermon that opens Gentile election with precision, deploys the *eklektos* word study and the basketball-team illustrations effectively, honors the Judy Ayers memorial with pastoral weight, and supplies a pointed "wear the jersey" exhortation — but preaches *about* the resurrection ("born again to a living hope through the resurrection") rather than preaching the resurrection itself, which is the singular theological occasion of Easter.

---

## 3. Berean Gate

**PASS**

**Scopus Test**: "God has sovereignly chosen elect exiles from every nation — including Gentiles who were 'not my people' — and through Christ's death and resurrection given us a living hope, an imperishable inheritance, and a new team identity: the duty to wear the jersey and live as who we are." Arises from 1 Peter 1:1–9. Pass.

**Authority Test**: Election (God chose us, John 6:44) is not softened. Total depravity ("we who rejected God, and chose sin, and in doing so shattered creation") is named. The two possible outcomes at the final conversation ("Well done" or "Depart from me") are named without softening. Pass.

> **Textual accuracy concern — KJV and *eklektos***: The sermon states the KJV "is missing words at this point" and "leaves the Greek word *eklektos* out altogether," attributing this to "fewer manuscripts of lesser quality." This is an overstatement. The Textus Receptus underlying the KJV *does* contain the Greek word *eklektos* in 1 Peter 1:1. The KJV renders the phrase *eklektois parepidemois* as "strangers scattered" — a translation decision, not a manuscript omission. The word study observation is correct and valuable; the textual criticism claim should be revised to: "The KJV's translation at this point renders 'elect exiles' as 'strangers scattered,' partly as a translation choice and partly due to working with fewer manuscripts — but the Greek text itself includes *eklektos*." Do not imply the word is simply absent from the manuscripts.

---

## 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**None triggered.** The gospel is present: Christ's death for sinners, the resurrection, the living hope of 1 Peter 1:3–9, the invitation to faith. The substitutionary element ("Christ died for us") is implied through the "guilty of Christ's death" framing.

---

## 5. Score Breakdown

### 1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17/25

- **Textual Fidelity (7/10)**: The sermon follows 1 Peter 1:1–9 structurally: (1) elect exiles (vv. 1–2), (2) a living hope through resurrection (v. 3), (3) imperishable inheritance (v. 4), (4) guarded through faith (v. 5), (5) trials and tested faith (vv. 6–7), (6) joy inexpressible (vv. 8–9). This is genuine textual fidelity. The *eklektos* word study is exegetically grounded. The exile/resident alien unpacking of *parepidemois* is correct. Minor deduction for the KJV textual claim (overstated) and for the sermon not returning to the text's specific language throughout — the word study section departs into general commentary rather than staying in 1 Peter 1.
- **Contextual Awareness (4/5)**: The Gentile-inclusion material (three Pentecosts, Hosea 1, Romans 9:22–25) supplies rich canonical context for "elect exiles from the nations." The Acts 2/8/10 structure is accurate and illuminating.
- **Exegetical Clarity (3.5/5)**: The *eklektos* definitions from BDAG, Liddell-Scott, Louw-Nida, and Moulton-Milligan are impressive and correctly glossed. The election doctrine arising from the word study is textually connected. The three-Pentecosts structure is a creative but accurate redemptive-historical observation.
- **Logical Faithfulness (2.5/5)**: The "wear the jersey" application arises from "elect exiles" in v. 1 and from "do not love the world" in 1 John 2:15–16. It is thematically connected to 1 Peter 1 without being directly derived from a specific verse.

**FCF**: "Our struggles mostly come back to forgetting which team we are on." Named in the introduction. Well-carried through the basketball illustrations and the final exhortation.

### 2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 14/18

- **Christ-Centeredness (4/7)**: Christ is named throughout — his death, resurrection, the living hope he provides. But the resurrection — the singular theological occasion of Easter — is referenced rather than proclaimed. "We have hope. A living hope in verse 3, an imperishable inheritance" — but what happened on Easter Sunday, what the resurrection means, what it accomplished, what it guarantees — these are gestured at rather than preached. The Thomas Watson quote on stone-to-flesh regeneration is the sermon's most Christ-centered moment.
- **Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3.5/3)**: The three Pentecosts, the Hosea trajectory, the "vessels of mercy" from Romans 9, the Gentile inclusion arc — this is excellent redemptive-historical awareness. One of the corpus's strongest in this category.
- **Doctrinal Precision (3.5/4)**: Unconditional election, effectual calling (God "chased us down"), perseverance ("guarded through faith," 1 Peter 1:5), regeneration (Watson on stone to flesh) — all present and precise.
- **Confessional Alignment (3/4)**: Reformed Baptist through-and-through. John 6:44, the sovereignty of election, the imperishable inheritance by God's power — all consistent with the 1689 Confession.

### 3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 10/15

- **Clarity of the Gospel (3.5/5)**: The gospel elements are present: we are guilty in Christ's death through our sin, God chased us down, Christ died (implied), rose again (stated in v. 3 reference), we have a living hope. But the mechanism of atonement — how Christ's death pays for our sin — is not stated. The sermon says "we are guilty of Christ's death" without explaining how Christ's death removes that guilt.
- **Necessity of Christ (4/5)**: Christ's resurrection is the explicit anchor of the living hope in v. 3. Easter occasion gives necessary weight to Christ's person.
- **Cross-Centered Gravity (2.5/5)**: The resurrection is referenced but not proclaimed. ## 8. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The *eklektos* word study**: Drawing from BDAG, Liddell-Scott, Louw-Nida, and Moulton-Milligan to establish "chosen, picked out, selected, made excellent, made beautiful" — and then connecting that to "God chose us despite our guilt in Christ's death" — is the kind of textual engagement that equips a congregation for a lifetime. The observation that God chose people who were "not his people" (Hosea 1, Romans 9:22–25) is both exegetically grounded and doxologically powerful. The inclusion of Gentiles in the covenant is genuinely gospel.

2. **The Judy Ayers memorial and the urgency it creates**: "You don't know how much time you have left. You don't know when you'll take that last breath, you don't know just exactly when you'll meet God face to face." The sermon earns this urgency because Judy's death is fresh, specific, and real. The closing gospel dichotomy ("well done, good and faithful servant" or "depart from me, I don't know you") lands with full weight because the congregation has just been reminded that a person they knew took her last breath this week. This is pastoral preaching at its most effective.

3. **The Thomas Watson quote on regeneration**: "Satan wanted Christ to prove his deity by turning rocks into bread, but Jesus has worked a far greater miracle by turning hard hearts of stone into flesh." This is the best use of a Puritan quotation in the corpus — it is a theological thunderclap in a sermon about God's initiating love. It does in two sentences what a paragraph of explanation could not. It also connects election doctrine to regeneration doctrine in a way that is both precise and experiential.

## 9. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **An Easter sermon about election that doesn't preach the resurrection**: 1 Peter 1:3 says we were "born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead." The resurrection is not merely the background to the living hope — it *is* the living hope. What does the resurrection mean? That death has been conquered. That the body will be raised. That Christ's vindication is our vindication. That because he lives, we will live also. An Easter congregation expects — and deserves — to hear the resurrection proclaimed, not just referenced. The word "resurrection" in v. 3 is the Easter gift Peter hands the preacher, and it was not fully opened.

2. **The KJV textual criticism claim is overstated**: The statement that the KJV "leaves the Greek word *eklektos* out altogether" and attributes this to "fewer manuscripts of lesser quality" implies the word is *absent* from the manuscripts underlying the KJV. It is not. The Textus Receptus includes *eklektos* in 1 Peter 1:1. The KJV translates the phrase differently — rendering "elect exiles" as "strangers scattered" — but this is a translation decision, not a manuscript lacuna. This should be corrected before the sermon is preached again. The point the sermon is making (that *eklektos* is genuinely in the Greek and carries the weight of election) is true and valuable; the textual argument needs to be restated accurately.

3. **The gospel's mechanism of atonement is absent**: The sermon says "we are guilty" but does not say how Christ removes that guilt. "Christ died for our sins" is implied but not stated. On Easter Sunday, when the congregation includes visitors and seekers, the sermon should at minimum state that Christ's death was the payment for our sin and his resurrection the proof of its sufficiency.

## 10. Surgical Fixes

1. **Add a resurrection proclamation paragraph before the "A Living Hope" section**: "Two thousand years ago this weekend, something happened that changed everything. On the third day, the tomb was empty. The guards who were posted to prevent exactly that reported it to the chief priests, who paid them to lie. An empty tomb with no body. Mary thinking he was the gardener until he said her name. Two disciples on the road to Emmaus whose hearts burned within them. Thomas putting his fingers in the wounds. The same body, glorified, present, real. He is risen. And because he lives, this hope Peter is talking about is not a wish — it is a certainty. His resurrection is the guarantee of ours."

2. **Correct the KJV textual criticism**: Change "the King James Version is missing words at this point — it leaves the Greek word *eklektos* out altogether, either as a poor translation choice or due to fewer manuscripts" to: "The King James Version renders this phrase as 'strangers scattered' — a translation that buries the election language. The Greek word *eklektos* is there in the text; the KJV simply didn't bring it across as fully as later translations have. When you read in the ESV or NIV 'elect exiles' — that's the Greek."

3. **Add the atonement mechanism at the guilt paragraph**: After "we are guilty" add: "That guilt required a payment. Christ made it. The sinless Son of God took our sin onto himself and bore the judgment we deserved. 'He was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities' (Isaiah 53:5). And then — on Easter — God raised him from the dead as the proof that the debt was fully paid. The resurrection is God the Father's receipt."
