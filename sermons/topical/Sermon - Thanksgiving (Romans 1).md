## Opening: The Surprise Text

I'm going to be deliberately brief today — but this is an important topic, and it is relevant to the holiday.

Our scripture today is Romans chapter 1. If you brought your Bible, open there now.

Those of you familiar with Romans chapter 1 might be a little surprised that this is a text being preached at Thanksgiving. It tells us that God's wrath will be poured out against mankind because, although we have knowledge of God, we willfully suppress it. In doing so we embrace idolatry, resulting in moral impurity, dishonorable passions, and finally a total societal breakdown.

Thanksgiving indeed.

But hidden in plain sight is verse 21 — and that is where I want us to spend most of our time today.

Let's read it.

> *"For although they knew God, they did not honor him as God or give thanks to him, but they became futile in their thinking, and their foolish hearts were darkened."*
> — Romans 1:21 (ESV)

---

## The Progression in Verse 21

It is interesting to me that no matter which translation you read, the progression is the same:

1. They **knew** God.
2. They did not **honor** God.
3. They were not **thankful** to God.
4. And so their thinking became **futile**, and their hearts were **darkened**.

In the verses that follow, Scripture says such people turned toward idolatry, then sexual immorality, homosexuality, and so forth. Paul ultimately says that people who refuse to glorify God and give thanks to God are deserving of God's wrath.

## What Is Man Made For?

A preacher I was listening to this week, preaching on this same subject, brought up the Westminster Shorter Catechism at this point.

*What is the chief end of man?*
*To glorify God and enjoy him forever.*

*[Note: Westminster Shorter Catechism, Question 1: "What is the chief end of man? A. Man's chief end is to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever."]*

Knowledge demands a response. Truth demands a response. Who among you could sit there and allow a bad person to do bad things to someone you loved without acting? Who wouldn't try to protect your family? That is a normal, natural response — it is what we do with truth we believe.

All truth demands a response. God's truth demands that we glorify him — as we talked about last week, *Sola Deo Gloria*. We see Adam and Eve breaking this from the start. Obedience to God is part of glorifying God. When they disobeyed, they introduced sin into the world and shattered the image of God in creation.

## Spurgeon on Ingratitude

The famous preacher Charles Spurgeon — the aptly named "Prince of Preachers" — said that the worst thing you can say of a man is that he is not thankful to God.

In his sermon "Inexcusable Irreverence and Ingratitude" (Sermon #2257, preached July 13, 1890, on Romans 1:20–21), Spurgeon said precisely this:

> *"I cannot say anything much worse of a man than that he is not thankful to those who have been his benefactors; and when you say that he is not thankful to God, you have said about the worst thing you can say of him."*

In Spurgeon's day — and in ours — we prove ourselves unthankful to God in some key ways. All of these things count as disobedience. But let me be more specific.

We know from Scripture that God has set aside a day of rest for us, that we should reflect on our Lord on that day. We recognize this on Sundays in our culture. Other traditions observe it on Friday or Saturday, some in the mornings, some in the evenings. The *when* of it matters less than the fact that we take the time to do it. Ignoring the Lord's Day — do you think that makes you thankful or unthankful? It is certainly disobedient.

What about when we disregard God through the day? Thankful or unthankful?

What about when we neglect to read his love letter to us, the Bible? Thankful or unthankful?

What about when the Son of God is rejected? Thankful or unthankful?

What about when we allow circumstances to take our focus off God and place it on the circumstances around us — a moment of faithlessness? Thankful or unthankful?

Spurgeon goes on to speak these hard words to his congregation:

> *"Why, look at some of you! You never missed a meal in your lives. When you went to the table, there was always something on it. You never had to lose a night's rest for need of a bed. Some of you, from your childhood, have had all that heart could wish. If God has treated you so, while many are crushed with poverty, should he not have some gratitude from you? You had a good mother; you had a tender father; you have gone from one form of relationship to another with increasing comfort. You are spared, and your mother is spared; your wife and children are spared.*
>
> *Indeed, God has made your path very smooth. Some of you are getting on in business, while other men are failing; some of you have every comfort at home, while others have been widowed, and their children have fallen, one after the other. Will you never be grateful? Hard, hard heart, wilt thou never break? Will any mercy bend thee? I do appeal to some here, whose path has been so full of mercies, that they ought to think of God, and turn to him with sincere repentance and faith.*
>
> *Someone says, 'I have had good luck.' What can be worse than that? Here is unthankfulness to God indeed, when you ascribe his good gifts to 'good luck.' 'Well, you know, but I have been a very hard-working man.' I know you have, but who gave you this strength for your work? 'I have a good supply of brains while others do not.' Did you make your own brains? Do you not feel that any man who talks about his own wisdom and his own wit writes 'FOOL' across his forehead in capital letters? We owe everything to God! Shall we not give God something? Shall we have no gratitude to him from whom all our blessings have come?"*

*[Note: All quoted material above is from Spurgeon's Sermon #2257, "Inexcusable Irreverence and Ingratitude" (1890), on Romans 1:20–21. The text is in the public domain. The pastor's oral delivery contained minor wording variations — "for want of a bed" vs. CCEL's "for need of a bed," and "shall we not give God nothing" vs. the original "Shall we not give God something?" — suggesting he was quoting from memory or from a secondary source (likely Pritchard's sermon). The exact text above is drawn from CCEL's edition of Spurgeon's Sermons, Vol. 38.]*

## Two Ways We Express Ungratefulness

### 1. We Take Abundance for Granted

We are so blessed in this country. Someone once said: "America is the only country in the world where the poorest inhabitants can grow fat."

*[Note: The origin of this quote is uncertain. The pastor did not attribute it to a source. It is in circulation but appears to be of unclear provenance. Flagged for verification.]*

I know someone who visits two food pantries and is given enough food to fill the fridge and the freezer. They don't even have to go every week because the free food from charities is so plentiful.

Our country is relatively peaceful. How many wars have been fought on our soil? Not many. We don't have to worry much about being invaded. In some countries, you aren't even allowed to own your home. We have been given the opportunity to do that.

Is it perfect? Certainly not. But there is so much to be thankful for. This is why we give thanks before we eat.

*[Note: The pastor mentioned that "only 20% or so of the world's population has the luxury of not having to eat bugs to survive." The pastor appears to have confused "80% of the world's nations" (countries where entomophagy occurs) with "80% of people." The stat should not be repeated in future preaching without correction.]*

## Do We Thank God for Our Paycheck?

Let me ask you this — those of you with any kind of income. Have you ever stopped to thank God for that provision?

I have even heard pastors confess that they felt this way about their weekly check: *"This is my money. I earned it. I worked hard for it. It is what is owed me for my labor."*

Ray Pritchard tells a story from his own life. He had been in pastoral ministry for some sixteen years in the Chicago area — senior pastor of Calvary Memorial Church in Oak Park, Illinois — and every week the check came in, every week it cleared, and every week he quietly thought of it as his due.

*[Note: "Sixteen years" refers to his tenure specifically in Chicago (Oak Park, IL). Pritchard's total pastoral ministry before this transition was approximately 26 years. The pastor's "16 years" figure is accurate for the Chicago period specifically.]*

Then the Lord moved him and his family to Tupelo, Mississippi. He had no salary lined up — he simply knew he needed to be there, and that God would provide. So he went, like Abraham stepping into the unknown, and took up full-time leadership of Keep Believing Ministries, his existing online ministry.

*[Note: The pastor described Pritchard as having "decided to start a church in Mississippi." This is not accurate. Pritchard moved to Tupelo to lead Keep Believing Ministries full-time — a parachurch/internet ministry he had founded — not to plant a congregation. His brother owned property in the area, which is part of what drew him there.]*

That first year, there was no salary. If Pritchard was invited somewhere to preach and received a small honorarium, he was grateful in a way he had never been before.

In his own words:

> *"So during that year without a salary, if I went to preach somewhere and they gave me a small gift for preaching a sermon, I was profoundly grateful to God. Thus does God even the scales. I learned to say thank you to the Lord for his provision for what I had once taken for granted.*
>
> *A few years ago if you had asked me, 'Are you a grateful person?' I suppose I would have said that I am about as grateful as the next person. And from the standpoint of our text, that would be a true statement. We are all unthankful by nature because we all take for granted the goodness of the Lord. We think God owes us something when in fact he owes us nothing at all.*
>
> *Every good gift comes down from the Father of lights above.*"

*[Note: "Every good gift comes down from the Father of lights above" — this is a paraphrase of James 1:17 (ESV): "Every good gift and every perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of lights, with whom there is no variation or shadow due to change."]*

> *"What do you have that you did not receive?"*

*[Note: 1 Corinthians 4:7 (ESV): "What do you have that you did not receive? If then you received it, why do you boast as if you did not receive it?"]*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title

**Pastoral Warmth Over a Gospel Placeholder: A Thanksgiving Meditation That Stops Short of Preaching**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A brief, accessible opening of Romans 1:21 that rightly identifies ingratitude as the root of human fallenness and marshals Spurgeon effectively, but fails to preach Christ from the text — substituting a bracketed placeholder for the gospel the sermon most needed to deliver.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**PASS — with qualification.**

The Scopus Test passes: the sermon's thesis ("we are unthankful by nature, and ingratitude is the root of idolatry and God's judgment") arises directly from Romans 1:21 and its surrounding argument. The Authority Test passes at the level of diagnosis — the preacher does not soften the text's indictment. However, the sermon passes the gate by a narrow margin. The text functions more as a topical launch point than a sustained exegetical anchor; the progression of v. 21 is noted but not deeply unpacked, and the bulk of the sermon moves away from the passage to illustrations and quotes rather than returning to it. This is a gate pass, not a gate commendation.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag triggered: Fatal Flaw #1 — No clear gospel presentation.**

The section titled "The Greatest Gift" contains exactly this: `*[Gospel]*` — a bracketed stage note. The gospel is not present in this manuscript. It is anticipated, gestured toward, and then absent. The sermon tells the congregation that salvation is what they have most to be thankful for, asks "what did it cost him?" — and then stops. There is no proclamation of Christ's finished work, no penal substitution, no justification by faith, no invitation to faith or repentance. The gospel is not assumed here; it is structurally absent.

**Score capped at 69.**

**No additional flags triggered**, though the application section (while showing some specificity) is largely generic enough to approach Flag #4, and the closing exhortation is pastoral but not summoning enough to fully escape Flag #5.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a bracket, not a proclamation.** "The Greatest Gift" section — the structural climax of the sermon — contains `*[Gospel]*` and nothing else. This is not a minor gap. The entire sermon is building to the claim that salvation is the greatest gift, and then that gift is not given. This is the fatal flaw and the defining failure of this manuscript as a preached document.

2. **The exposition is announced but not sustained.** The four-step progression of v. 21 is correctly identified, but the sermon does not return to the text after noting it. The bulk of the message is illustration and quotation layered onto a text that has been outlined but not genuinely opened. The congregation leaves knowing about Romans 1:21 but not having been inside it.

3. **The sermon does not address the congregation asymmetrically.** The application is aimed at the comfortable and the taking-for-granted. It does not speak to those who are genuinely suffering, who find it hard to thank God, or who are grieving loss. The one-note address (you have much to be grateful for; be more grateful) misses Perkins' differentiation entirely — particularly the humbled hearer, the doubter, and the person for whom life genuinely has not been smooth.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's thesis — that ingratitude is the root of human sin — is not obviously good news for the congregation. The sermon diagnoses the disease but withholds the cure. The pastoral instinct toward warmth and corporate prayer at the close is genuine, but it substitutes a devotional atmosphere for a gospel moment. The preacher may believe the congregation already knows the gospel and does not need it repeated. This is a dangerous assumption in a mixed congregation at a Thanksgiving service that likely includes nominally Christian attendees, guests, and the spiritually indifferent. A holiday service is one of the highest-risk environments for assumed gospel.

There is also a blind spot around inaccurate statistics. The insect-consumption statistic is incorrect (flagged in the manuscript notes), and if preached without correction, it would erode credibility for attentive listeners. The sermon relies on factual illustration; the facts must be right.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Spurgeon citation creates an illusion of expository and applicatory depth. The extended quote genuinely corners hearers — but it is Spurgeon's conscience work, not the sermon's. The preacher is borrowing force he has not generated. The section reads as strong because Spurgeon is strong. This masks the fact that the sermon's own applicatory architecture does not achieve what Spurgeon achieves in the quoted passage. If the Spurgeon quote were removed, the sermon's applicatory weakness would be immediately apparent.

Similarly, the precision of the manuscript notes (flagging statistical errors, correcting quote attributions, noting inaccuracies in Pritchard's story) gives an impression of careful scholarship, but those notes are editorial apparatus, not sermon. A sermon that requires footnotes to be honest has not yet resolved its honesty issues into the manuscript itself.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Romans 1:21 sits inside one of the most theologically loaded passages in the New Testament. The text itself culminates in a condemnation that Paul will spend the next two chapters deepening — all in service of the great reversal in Romans 3:21: "But now the righteousness of God has been manifested apart from the law." The sermon identifies the diagnosis (ingratitude as the root of fallenness) but does not complete the argument Paul is making. Romans 1:18–3:20 is Paul's extended prosecution of all humanity — the very ground on which Romans 3:21–26 becomes gospel thunder. A Thanksgiving sermon from Romans 1:21, faithfully preached, has every right to say: "This is our condition. And *this* — Christ crucified, justifying the ungodly — is why we give thanks." That movement is entirely native to the text. It was not taken.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Fill the gospel placeholder.** The `*[Gospel]*` bracket must become a paragraph. Minimally: name what Christ did (bore the wrath described in Romans 1:18, died for those who suppressed God's truth, rose to justify the ungodly), how it is received (by faith alone), and why it is the ground of all true thanksgiving. This single addition would lift the sermon from fundamentally flawed to solid. Consider a direct address: "You came here today with much to be thankful for. But the deepest thanksgiving you will ever know is this: that while you were unthankful, ungrateful, suppressing the truth — God sent his Son to take the wrath you deserved."

2. **Add a direct address to those for whom gratitude is hard.** After the Spurgeon quote (or before the closing prayer), add a pastoral word to the congregation member who is not experiencing smooth provision — who has lost a spouse, a job, a child. The sermon currently assumes everyone is comfortable. A single paragraph acknowledging that gratitude in suffering is a different and harder thing — and that the gospel makes it possible — would address Perkins' humbled and grieving hearers and deepen the sermon's pastoral credibility.

3. **Sharpen the "therefore" moment at the close.** The closing prayer invitation is warm but is not an exhortation. After the gospel is proclaimed (Fix 1), the sermon needs a direct summons: "So here is what I am calling you to today: repent of taking God's gifts for granted. Receive the greatest gift — Christ crucified. And let that be the ground of your thanksgiving, not just today, but every day." The prayer then becomes the corporate expression of that response, not its substitute.

4. **Correct or remove the insect-consumption statistic before preaching.** The statistic as stated is inverted and inaccurate. Either replace it with a verified figure or drop the comparative point entirely. The illustration of American abundance does not depend on a global entomophagy statistic.

5. **Return to the text after the progression.** After noting the four-step progression in v. 21, spend thirty additional seconds on "futile in their thinking" and "their foolish hearts were darkened." These phrases do real theological work — they describe the cognitive and affective consequences of ingratitude, not just the moral ones. The congregation would benefit from seeing that ingratitude is not merely an attitude problem but a darkening of the mind.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered in the traditional sense**, but a related flag applies: this sermon exhibits *incomplete orthodoxy* — the diagnostic is Reformed in framework, the application is pastoral and specific, but the gospel is literally absent from the manuscript.
