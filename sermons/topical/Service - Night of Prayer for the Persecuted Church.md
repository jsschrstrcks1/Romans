## Opening Remarks

Tonight we are going to do things a little differently than normal.

When I was at Trinity finishing my bachelor's degree, one of my favorite professors retired — and instead of going somewhere quiet, he went to the mission field. Before he left, he held a day of prayer in chapel for the persecuted church. He challenged each of us to keep those believers in our prayers. Privately. And publicly. And he asked us — if we ever found ourselves pastoring a church — to commit to bringing the persecuted church before our congregation at least once a year.

We touched on this last year. But I recently read a book, and then came across some videos, and it all came back. Not as a thought, exactly. More like a weight.

*[Note: if the pre-screen copy of the film becomes available through your seminary contacts before service, insert the appropriate title and a one-sentence description here.]*

The plan tonight is simple. We are going to watch six short videos — five or six minutes each. After each one, I am going to ask someone to pray. Not a prepared prayer. Just an honest one.

---

## Call for Volunteers

Before we start, I need six people willing to pray out loud tonight. You don't need to be eloquent. You don't need to have the right words. You just need to be willing to open your mouth in front of God and this room on behalf of someone who is suffering for the name of Jesus.

*[Pause. Wait for hands. Assign volunteers to videos 1–6 by name.]*

---

## Framing: What You Are About to See

I want to say something before we press play.

What you are about to see is not a dramatization. These are real people. Some of them have had their identities protected — names changed, faces obscured — because the danger to them and their families is that immediate.

The world they live in looks nothing like ours. People parking down the street from a believer's house and slipping through backyards so the secret police don't see them arrive. Songs of worship mouthed together in the dark — no sound, no instruments — because sound carries. Children who are not safe to ride the bus. Not safe in their own neighborhoods. Not safe to be seen carrying a Bible.

This is what it costs to follow Jesus for roughly two thirds of the Christians alive on earth today. *[See end notes on this figure.]*

When we are done tonight, you are going to go home to a house that is yours. You will not be followed. You will sleep in a country where you can walk through those church doors next Sunday morning without anyone tracking your car, without anyone calling your employer, without anyone threatening your children.

They don't have that.

So when we watch, watch slowly. When we pray, mean it.

---

## Video 1 — Sarah

*[Introduce before playing.]*

We will start with Sarah.

Sarah is a follower of Jesus Christ who was arrested for her faith. She was put in prison. She was tortured.

This is her story.

And her story is not unusual. It is the story of hundreds of millions of people in her country alone.

*[Play video.]*

*[After video — pause. Do not fill the silence immediately.]*

*[Volunteer 1 — pray for Sarah, her family, believers like her in that country.]*

## Closing

*[After the sixth prayer:]*

I am not going to preach at you tonight. You've heard enough.

But I want to ask you one thing before you go.

My professor — the one who held that day of prayer at Trinity — made us all commit to something. Not a big thing. One day a year. One service. One moment where the persecuted church stops being an abstraction and becomes a person with a name and a face and a story.

I'm asking you to make the same commitment. Not just to come back next year when we do this again. But to carry these people in your private prayers. To mention them by name when you pray with your families. To not go home tonight and forget Sarah by the time you fall asleep.

Because she hasn't forgotten you. She prays for the church that is free. She prays that you will use your freedom well.

Let's not waste it.

Would you bow your heads.

*[Pastoral closing prayer — for the persecuted church worldwide, for boldness in the room, for gratitude and faithfulness.]*

## End Notes

**"Two thirds of the world's Christians":**
- The raw notes say "around 2/3s of the rest of the world to follow Christ" — likely meaning that roughly two thirds of the world's Christians live under some form of persecution or restriction. This figure should be verified before preaching. Open Doors' 2020 World Watch List reported approximately 260 million Christians facing high levels of persecution — roughly one in eight Christians worldwide. The "two thirds" figure may reflect a broader definition that includes countries with significant societal hostility or legal restriction, not only state-sponsored persecution. Verify against a current Open Doors or Voice of the Martyrs report for the year of the service and use their exact language, which will be defensible.

**The book and pre-screen copy:**
- The book the pastor read is not named in the notes. The film adaptation mentioned ("I'm trying to get a pre-screen copy") is not named either. A bracketed placeholder is included in the Opening Remarks. Fill in the title once confirmed — do not guess.

**"Trinity":**
- Referenced as the pastor's bachelor's institution. No additional detail needed; the congregation will know.

**The professor:**
- Not named in the notes. Retained as anonymous throughout — the congregation may or may not know who he is.

**"Sarah":**
- The name is from the raw notes. It is almost certainly a pseudonym used by the producing organization to protect the subject's identity. Do not add biographical detail (country, denomination, specific charges) beyond what the video itself discloses. The brief introduction before the video should remain thin on specifics — let the video speak.

**Videos 2–6:**
- The other five video subjects are not named in the raw notes. Structure is provided but content must be filled in by the pastor once the specific videos are selected. Each introduction should follow the same pattern as Sarah's: two sentences maximum, who this person is, what they faced.

**Silence after videos:**
- The script notes "do not fill the silence immediately" after each video. This is intentional. The instinct to move quickly to the next thing should be resisted. Let the room sit with what it just saw before the volunteer begins to pray.

*Theological Weight*: The service does not develop theology discursively, but the theology embedded in the framing remarks is correct: the persecuted church suffers *for the name of Jesus*, the congregation is asked to pray *on behalf of someone who is suffering for the name of Jesus*, and the final call reminds them that their freedom is a stewardship, not an entitlement. This is theologically grounded without being preachy.

*Applicatory Force*: The closing exhortation is the strongest pastoral moment in the document. "She hasn't forgotten you. She prays for the church that is free. *Gospel Centrality*: The gospel is present structurally (the persecuted believers suffer *for Christ*, which implies Christ is worth suffering for), but it is not proclaimed. The service is not an evangelistic service — it is a church mobilization service. The gospel can be assumed here without penalty.

*Sermonic Force*: The framing remarks and closing are written with genuine oral authority. "Not as a thought, exactly. More like a weight." — this is preaching cadence applied to prose. "Watch slowly. When we pray, mean it." — compressed, directive, effective.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The framing remarks are pastorally masterful.** The description of believers parking down the street, mouthing songs in the dark, children not safe on the bus — this is specific, unhurried, and refuses to let the congregation hold the persecuted church at arm's length. The contrast with the congregation's own security ("You will not be followed. You will sleep in a country...") is not manipulative — it is honest. It names the gap without shaming.

2. **"I am not going to preach at you tonight. You've heard enough."** This is a display of pastoral wisdom that many preachers lack. The instinct after six emotionally laden videos would be to reinforce, summarize, and expand. The decision to stand down and let the content do its work — and then close with a single, quiet exhortation — shows the pastor knows when to speak and when silence is the sermon.

3. **The closing exhortation is theologically grounded and personally directed.** "She hasn't forgotten you. She prays for the church that is free." This reverses the expected direction of compassion — the suffering believer is not only the object of the comfortable church's pity; she is also interceding for them. This is theologically true and pastorally disarming. It positions the congregation not as benefactors but as recipients of the persecuted church's faithfulness.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **There is no biblical text anchoring the service.** The entire evening proceeds without a passage being opened, read, or applied. This is not fatal — prayer services, vigils, and testimonial evenings need not be exposition-centered — but for a Reformed Baptist congregation accustomed to the Word as the center of gathered worship, one brief passage would ground the service in Scripture rather than in sentiment and video. Hebrews 11:35b–38, Revelation 6:9–11, or Romans 8:17–18 would each serve without requiring a sermon.

2. **The gospel is structurally present but never verbally stated.** A visitor — or a nominally churched family member who came because they were invited — could sit through this entire service and never hear the gospel articulated. The service presupposes that everyone in the room knows why Christians are persecuted and who they are suffering for. That assumption may not hold. One sentence in the framing remarks — "These believers have staked everything on the truth that Jesus Christ is the Son of God, died for sin, and rose from the dead" — would anchor the gospel without turning the service into an evangelistic meeting.

3. **Videos 2–6 are entirely blank.** This is a practical weakness rather than a theological one, but the service cannot be fully evaluated — or fully executed — without knowing what testimony is being shown. The structural template is sound, but the evaluator cannot assess whether the selected testimonies will carry the theological and emotional weight the service design requires. The pastor should curate these videos with care: theological fidelity of the subjects (are they clearly evangelical?), emotional register variation across the six (not all six should be equally devastating), and attention to geographic and demographic diversity.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The service is built entirely around what the congregation will *feel* after seeing the videos — and the closing call assumes that emotional engagement will translate into ongoing intercessory practice. This may not hold. Emotional conviction after a powerful evening rarely produces sustained behavioral change without a concrete, structured mechanism. The closing asks for private prayer and family prayer by name — but gives the congregation no tool to do this. A printed prayer card with the names of the subjects and a commitment space, or a single-page guide to praying for the persecuted church (Voice of the Martyrs, Open Doors), would give the emotional conviction a concrete landing place.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The service's greatest apparent strength — its emotional restraint, its refusal to preach — is also its most significant structural risk. "I am not going to preach at you tonight" is wise if the videos have done the theological and emotional work adequately. It is a missed opportunity if the congregation leaves moved by human suffering rather than theologically equipped to understand their solidarity with the body of Christ under persecution. The restraint is genuine pastoral wisdom — but it could mask an under-developed theological framework if the framing remarks are the only explicitly theological content the congregation receives.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**One biblical text, read without comment, before the first video.** Revelation 6:9–11 — the souls under the altar, crying "How long, O Lord?" and told to wait a little longer — is the New Testament's most direct engagement with the persecuted church waiting for vindication. Reading it aloud before the first video, without explanation, would establish that the evening is not a humanitarian awareness event but a response to a biblical reality. The passage would frame the congregational prayers as participation in something cosmic, not merely sympathetic.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Add one biblical passage — read, not preached — before the first video.** Revelation 6:9–11 or Hebrews 11:35b–38 ("Others were tortured, not accepting release...") would ground the service theologically without requiring exposition. Two minutes. No comment necessary.

2. **Add one sentence in the framing remarks that states the gospel explicitly.** Something like: "Every person you are about to meet has staked their life on a specific claim: that Jesus Christ is Lord, that he died for sin and rose from the dead, and that no government on earth has authority over that allegiance." This states what the persecution is about without turning the framing into a sermon.

3. **Provide a tangible take-home resource.** A printed half-sheet with: the names of the video subjects (or pseudonyms), a URL for Voice of the Martyrs or Open Doors prayer resources, and a blank space for the congregation to write down one commitment. This converts emotional engagement into ongoing practice.

4. **Curate video 2–6 subjects for theological variety and emotional pacing.** Not all six should be maximum-weight testimonies. Consider a sequence: grief → endurance → joy → community → cost → hope. The emotional arc of the service should build, not plateau.

5. **Consider a brief congregational Scripture reading in unison before the closing prayer.** Psalm 46:1 or Romans 8:35–39, read together, would give the congregation a shared verbal act before the pastoral prayer and dismissal — anchoring the closing in corporate worship rather than individual response alone.
