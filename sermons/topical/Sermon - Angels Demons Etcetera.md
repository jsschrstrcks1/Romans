## Pseudepigrapha and the Roots of Demon-Binding

This garbage about mumbling nonsense words to bind demons has its roots in non-canonical literature. Anyone remember what the pseudepigrapha was? We have talked about it before — there are three groups of ancient books that are, or are thought to be, or call themselves Scripture.

The sixty-six books of the Bible are called the Canon of Scripture.

Then there is a group of books called the Apocrypha — books that our Catholic brothers and sisters include in their Bible, and only then with a caveat that these aren't quite the same in substance as the books of the Canon.

Then we have this whole huge third group of writings. The Pseudepigrapha. These are books that claim to be Scripture, claim to be written by an apostle, but are verifiably false.

Let me ask you this — who remembers when the word "groovy" came into common use? Can you tell me within a decade or so? What about the word "bad" — when was it used to mean something good? Likewise the 90s saw a rise in the usage of words like "booyah, word, psych, fly" and so forth. Walking around the street in the 2000s you would have heard things like "you go girl," or "cool beans." In our current decade you'll hear things like "bae," or "lit," or "snowflake," or "deplorables."

Point is language dates itself. So if you have a document that says "I was written by Paul the Apostle" yet talks about how "lit bae is," you know that Paul who lived 2,000 years ago did not write the document, because the language is wrong. Make sense? So we have this whole huge third category of literature that claims to have been written by various people, but scholars can prove through language that those books were not written by who they claim. The book's handshake, right out of the gate, is a lie. If it contains lies, is it the same as Scripture? Certainly not.

Most of the time when you're watching Discovery Channel, or National Geographic and they talk about lost gospels, or gnostic gospels, or suppressed gospels, these are the books they are talking about.

## Two Examples from Non-Canonical Literature

The Book of Tobit is in the Apocrypha — Catholics include it; Protestants don't. In it, there's a woman named Sarah who had been married seven times, and seven times her husband had been killed on their wedding night by a demon named Asmodeus. So along comes a man named Tobias and an angel named Raphael, and Raphael tells the man to marry Sarah, and explains if you burn a fish liver and heart it will scare the demon away.

So the story goes the father-in-law had already dug grave number eight, but the man obeyed the angel, burned a fish liver and heart, and the demon fled.

Now — it's a story. But it is not Scripture. And yet that idea — that you can drive away demonic influence through specific ritual acts using specific substances — that's the root of a whole tradition of demon-binding and spiritual formulas.

The Testament of Solomon is a pseudepigraphal text — no one in the church has ever accepted it as Scripture. In it, a demon was sucking on the finger of one of Solomon's children, and as time went by the child began to fail to thrive. The demon was literally sucking the life out of the child. So in this lie of a story, Solomon prayed to God and received a ring that could bind a demon and make them do what Solomon wanted. So Solomon captured and interrogated demons, and learned the demons' names and what words forced them to be obedient. For instance this lie of a book reported that there was a female demon named Onoskelis — she is responsible for giving people sore throats, but she flees if the angel Raphael approaches. Solomon kept interrogating the demons until one day his dalliances with foreign women irritated God, and God broke the ring, and so ends that particular pseudepigraphal book.

Point is there is a lot of weirdness out there believed by a lot of people. People want to pierce the veil, and find out this secret knowledge that God didn't see fit to give us.

## What Scripture Actually Teaches

So what does Scripture teach us about angels and demons?

Firstly, we have all seen the movies of people's heads spinning in circles, and all the craziness Hollywood associates with demonic possession. You're probably wondering if things like that can happen to a believer in Jesus Christ. The short answer is no. The long answer is that never does such a thing happen in Scripture, and while we are warned about a number of things related to demons, we are not warned about the possibility that they could possess a true follower of Christ.

Most importantly though we know what Scripture does say —

> He who is in you is greater than he who is in the world.
> — 1 John 4:4 (ESV) ✓

Or in Colossians 1:13-14 —

> He has delivered us from the domain of darkness and transferred us to the kingdom of his beloved Son, in whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins.
> — Colossians 1:13-14 (ESV) ✓

Or as our passage for today said — when you became a believer in Jesus Christ, you were sealed with the Holy Spirit. Greater is he that is in us than he that is in the world.

## The Riches of God

Stop right there. I read an article a few years back that said winning the lottery was the worst thing that had ever happened to most people. Sure they had an influx of cash. But they also had an influx of spending, and needy family members, and shysters, and trying to do good things with the money, they ultimately spent most of it right away, and then spent the rest of it trying to win a second time. In the end they frequently die alone, deep in debt, depressed, and isolated from their families.

If you think winning the lottery is riches, you have no idea the width and breadth of riches my Jesus has for us. Simply talking about material possessions for a moment — is there anything in this world that doesn't belong to him? Every single euro belongs to God. Every single Great British pound. Every single Japanese yen. Every single US dollar has George Washington's face on it, but the dollars don't belong to Washington. They don't belong to Trump, and they don't belong to you or I — they belong to God. Your house — hah, not your house. God's house that he lets you live in. That he enabled you to work for, that he enabled you to pay for. Your house according to the state, but if God had opposed you having the house, you wouldn't have it, plain and simple. God's house that he lets us live in.

But if we stopped there the riches of God would be a shallow thing indeed. God is the fullness of perfect goodness. I'd say that's a treasure. God drawing us towards goodness ourselves is a treasure. God is the perfect fullness of grace, and drawing us towards being people that offer grace is a hard road. The prize is valuable. But the cost is high. Likewise God is the perfect fullness of patience. But you know as well as I do that patience is one of the more difficult lessons to learn. In fact there is a joke that goes around from time to time that says basically you only want to ask for patience if you're ready to encounter situations that make you impatient so you can learn to be more patient. Patience is a treasure, and part of the riches of God, but praise God he lavishes patience on us in accordance with his wisdom, amen?

## Guardian Angels and the Cargo Cult Error

Which brings me to guardian angels. I feel like there are times when people in the church elevate angels to a place they should never occupy. The hero of the story is God — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. Angels are usually messengers. Sometimes we elevate the messengers beyond what is healthy. I think people fall into this trap because they maybe see God as being busy and distant, but angels — there are lots of angels, and aren't some of them guardians?

It's sort of like what happened in the Pacific Islands after World War II. American military forces flew in supplies by cargo plane — food, medicine, equipment — to remote islands that had never seen anything like it. After the war, American forces left. And some of those island peoples, trying to get the cargo to come back, built replica airstrips out of straw and bamboo. They carved wooden headphones. They stood on mock airstrips waving homemade signal paddles. They were worshipping the plane instead of the people who sent it.

Anthropologists call these cargo cults.

Angels are sentient tools in God's hands. But the hero of the story is God. Scripture does talk about angels protecting people. But it is by the command of God that they do so or don't. We have to be careful that we don't begin to worship the boat.

## The Dualism Heresy

There is an ancient heresy that has become popular once more in the last few decades. We call it dualism. There are elements of it that sound good. But it falls far, far short of the scriptural picture.

Dualism says everything in existence and the world is a galactic fight between angels and demons. Dualism fails because it fails to factor in God. God is sovereign. God is in control. God has already won the battle. Dualism sees man as collateral damage in a battle of titans. Dualism forgets the battle is already won. That we who are saved have a protector, that we are sealed with and protected by the Holy Spirit. God himself is standing guard.

Likewise dualism is one of the ways people seek to remove their individual responsibility for sin. Today a man named Flip Wilson is credited with popularizing the phrase "the devil made me do it," but in truth, as with all the other heresies we've looked at today, its origins are ancient. Right at the beginning in the Garden.

God asks them why they disobeyed him. Rather than being honest, Adam says:

> The woman whom you gave to be with me, she gave me fruit of the tree, and I ate.
> — Genesis 3:12 (ESV) ✓

Eve's response was basically "the devil made me do it":

> The serpent deceived me, and I ate.
> — Genesis 3:13 (ESV) ✓

Eve didn't have a gun to Adam's head. She didn't force Adam's mouth open, and stick the fruit down his throat, nor did the devil do any of that to Eve. She tried the fruit because she wanted to, and so did Adam. They didn't believe God's promise of death, they thought they knew better, and a dozen other things when they put that fruit in their own mouths. They chose to do that. They chose to chew. They chose to swallow. They chose to rebel against God. They are responsible for that. It was their choices that brought about the curse. They chose to anger God, and accepted the consequences.

## Personal Responsibility

The devil and his demons didn't break your lawn mower. They didn't break the blue truck, the red truck, or the Buick. The devil doesn't make my AC leak. The devil didn't make Dad's back hurt. The devil didn't break the red truck a second time, or make the Hyundai brakes go out, and his minions didn't either. Neither the devil nor his demons gave you gas or indigestion or nausea. They didn't make you sign up for that credit card that you weren't responsible with. And they didn't make you disobey. They didn't make you embrace a life of sin. You chose that. You are responsible for that.

Angels and demons do play a role in what goes on. Scripture says we fight not against flesh and blood alone but also against the evil things of this world.

Ultimately though, when we take that last breath, it is our own thoughts and actions that we will be held accountable for. Face to face with God, he will know why you did it. You won't be able to get away with saying "the devil made me do it." Demons can't make you do anything. God protects us from that. But God only protects those who are Christians from that.

If you are here today and you have never surrendered your life to Christ — if you have never repented of your sin and placed your trust in Jesus as Lord and Savior — then you are still in the domain of darkness. You have not been transferred. You have not been sealed. The riches Paul describes in Ephesians 1 are not yours yet. But they can be. Today. Right now. The same God who chose us before the foundation of the world is offering you redemption through the blood of his Son. That's the gospel. And it's for you.

### 8. Core Override Questions

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The cargo cult illustration** — Theologically precise, culturally memorable, and native to the text's concern about misplaced worship. "Angels are sentient tools in God's hands. But the hero of the story is God." This is the sermon's best line and its best-constructed illustration.
2. **Personal responsibility section** — The application is concrete, specific, and conscience-pressing. Naming the actual broken vehicles and lawn equipment while making the doctrinal point ("The devil didn't break your lawn mower... you are responsible for that") is Puritan application in plain clothes. This section earns its place.
3. **The Badhai / carpenter connection** — The move from Badhai carpenters to "The Son of God spent thirty years working with his hands in a carpenter's shop. I don't think that's an accident" is organic, warm, and genuinely Christological without being forced.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Ephesians 1:3-14 is never expounded.** The passage is read aloud and mined for two supporting concepts (the sealing of the Spirit; the riches of grace), but its own argument — the Trinitarian doxology of sovereign grace, the movement from election to adoption to redemption to sealing to inheritance, all "to the praise of his glory" — is never opened. The congregation heard the text read but did not hear it preached. A listener could not reconstruct what Ephesians 1:3-14 is actually about from this sermon.
2. **The cross is a late arrival, not the gravitational center.** The sermon builds to personal responsibility rather than to the cross. The gospel call at the close ("redemption through the blood of his Son") is clear but comes as an addendum rather than as the climax of everything that preceded it. The law (you are accountable; you cannot blame the devil) is pressed effectively but the gospel relief is compressed into one paragraph. The hammer swings; the balm barely touches.
3. **Spiritual differentiation is underdeveloped.** The sermon speaks to the uninstructed believer and, briefly, the unconverted. But it does not address the person who uses demonology as a perpetual excuse for sin-patterns (who is not merely uninstructed but self-deceived), the fearful believer who needs the sealing of the Spirit preached as assurance rather than correction, or the intellectually orthodox churchgoer who has never moved from knowing these truths to resting in them.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that his opening personal illustration (the week of broken things) and the personal-responsibility section function together to make the sermon autobiographically coherent — but they also subtly shift the center of gravity from God's sovereign grace in Ephesians 1 to the preacher's week and the congregation's daily frustrations. The passage the preacher says he loves is the passage that receives the least sustained attention. The sections on pseudepigrapha, which are historically interesting, are doing work that could be done in half the space — freeing room to actually open the text.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The pseudepigrapha sections feel like expositional depth — the preacher is clearly informed, the historical content is accurate, and the congregation is learning. But this is background research *about* the topic of demons, not exposition *of* Ephesians 1:3-14. The sermon's most "impressive" middle section (Tobit, Testament of Solomon, cargo cults) is its most distant from the stated text. It reads strong because it is confident and well-researched, but it is not what the Berean Gate requires.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Ephesians 1:3-14 contains one of the most direct rebuttals of dualism in the entire New Testament: "a plan for the fullness of time, to unite all things in him, things in heaven and things on earth" (v.10). The cosmic scope of Christ's lordship — things in heaven and on earth, not a dualist battle between equals — is the text's own answer to the dualism heresy the sermon rightly addresses. The preacher got to the right destination by a detour. The text itself was the road. Verse 10's statement of Christ's cosmic unification is the most powerful anti-dualism statement available, and it sits in the passage that was read aloud and then largely bypassed.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild the expositional spine around the text's own argument.** Track Ephesians 1:3-14 movement by movement: election (v.4) → adoption (v.5) → redemption through blood (v.7) → the mystery of cosmic unification in Christ (v.10) → sealing of the Spirit as guarantee (v.13-14). Let each point of the sermon hang on one of these movements, rather than importing topics and returning to the text as needed.

2. **Use Ephesians 1:10 as the direct refutation of dualism.** "To unite all things in him, things in heaven and things on earth" — this is the text's own anti-dualist declaration. Replace or supplement the philosophical anti-dualism argument with the text's own counter-claim. This keeps the sermon in orbit around the passage.

3. **Move the cross from the closing paragraph to the structural center.** The law (personal responsibility, accountability before God) is laid down in the second half. The gospel relief should arrive in proportion. Expand the closing gospel call from one paragraph to a full section — and let redemption through blood (Eph. 1:7, already in the text) be the pivot from accountability to rescue.

4. **Compress the pseudepigrapha section.** One example (either Tobit or Testament of Solomon, not both) is sufficient to make the point. The saved space should go to expounding the passage.

5. **Add a direct address to the self-deceived sinner** after the personal-responsibility section: "Some of you have been in church long enough to know these things and have been using your knowledge of demonology as a theological cover for patterns of life you have chosen and could choose otherwise." This would press the "knowledgeable but unhumbled" hearer that the current draft doesn't reach.

6. **The conclusion does triple-duty and needs one clear landing note.** It moves from personal responsibility → warning to unbelievers → gospel invitation. These three belong together but need a single climactic sentence that ties them: something that declares *because God sealed his own through Christ's blood, the unsealed are offered that same blood today.*

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This rubric is designed primarily for textual-expository preaching. This sermon is a topical message that uses Ephesians 1:3-14 as its anchor text rather than expounding it as its primary source. The rubric applies, but the Exposition category will naturally score lower for any topical sermon regardless of quality — that is an inherent genre difference, not merely a craft failure. The preacher and evaluator should note: if this sermon were assessed as a *topical message* (which it functionally is) rather than an *expository message* (which the stated text implies), the genre expectations shift. The sermon is a stronger topical message than it is an expository one. Rebuilding it as a genuine exposition of Ephesians 1:3-14 — using the passage's own argument as the anti-dualism and anti-spirit-binding case — would significantly raise the score and deepen the sermon's force.
