# Beauty for Ashes
## Matthew 9:9–13 — The Calling of Matthew

---

Good morning. We're going to be in Matthew chapter 9 this morning — Matthew chapter 9, if you've brought your Bibles and I hope you have.

## Tax Collectors in the Ancient World

While you're turning there — one of the topics that has come up a lot in this political season is taxes. I don't want to get into politics, or even modern taxes. Taxes are a thing no one really likes and everyone has to pay, and that was true in ancient times as well. In fact, it was an even bigger racket back then.

Rome was a conquering and occupying power. Imagine Rome was the superpower today and they came in and conquered Florida. They would levy taxes equivalent to their own expenses — so you lose the war, and then you have to pay the costs and upkeep associated with your own oppression. Rome wasn't worried about being fair. They would auction off the *right to collect taxes* to the highest bidder. Rome got their money upfront and didn't particularly care what the tax collectors did after that.

The tax collector then had to recoup his investment, pay his own wages, and hire the biggest, toughest men he could find to enforce his collections — plus whatever additional profit he thought he could squeeze out. So Matthew, before Christ, would most likely have operated essentially like the mafia: going around, forcing people to pay up at the point of a spear. Not just enough to cover Rome's share, but enough to fund a lavish lifestyle at the expense of his countrymen.

Tax collectors were among the most hated people in Israel. Scripture references them almost like a curse word — *tax collectors and sinners*, *even the tax collectors do this or that*. They are frequently equated with dirty Gentiles or even prostitutes. These people were on the fringes of society, despised because they collaborated with Rome to take advantage of their own people. There was nothing respectable about the work. You just had to know enough thugs to keep people in line — to extort every possible penny from an already suffering population.

Think of it this way: imagine a hurricane levels your neighborhood — Moon Lake, Hudson, Bolton Heights — and your belongings are scattered everywhere. How would you feel toward people from Wesley Chapel or Tampa or Clearwater who then came in and began looting? Taking your grandmother's bed, your mother's silverware, your children's artwork off the refrigerator, their toys, your food. Now imagine they did all of this with the full backing of law enforcement — and that if you tried to stop them, the police would step in and end your resistance with the most advanced weapon of their era, the spear.

That is the world Matthew inhabited. Tax collectors had earned their reputation as monsters. If ancient Israelites sat around campfires telling horror stories, they were telling tax collector stories. We have the Italian mafia, gangs, organized crime. They had tax collectors — backed by the full power of Rome.

## What Matthew Doesn't Say

It's interesting that despite the Gospel of Matthew being written *by* Matthew, this is the only place Matthew speaks of himself. That says a great deal about the man.

It would have been easy to tell the stories that made him look good — spiritual, generous, kind. He doesn't. The one story he shares about himself is of Jesus taking his great shame and absolving him of it. There is no drama here, no hand-wringing. Jesus approaches Matthew and says "Follow me," and Matthew gets up and follows.

What Matthew *doesn't* say are the full implications of that moment. Right there in verse 9 — where it says Matthew got up and followed Jesus — Matthew quietly said goodbye to his wealth. He had already paid Rome's taxes for the year on behalf of thousands of people — potentially the modern equivalent of millions of dollars — and would not have collected it all back yet. Whatever remained uncollected, he walked away from when he stood up.

He left his job. He left his career. There was no turning back — not for Matthew. He said goodbye to his life of luxury and any prospect of earning more.

The fishermen — Peter, James, John — when they left their work, they still owned their boats and their nets. They were even able, and at one point *did*, return to fishing for a time (John 21). Not Matthew. For Matthew, following Jesus cost him everything.

One commentator suggested it was Jesus' *weight of personality* that made the difference. But that seems too small. What we see is what John 6:44 describes:

> "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him. And I will raise him up on the last day."
> — John 6:44, ESV

[Note: the sermon renders this as "draws *them*"; the ESV says "draws *him*."]

God had been working in Matthew's heart before Jesus ever walked past that tax booth. The weight of God pressing against Matthew's heart was what made the difference.

## God's Sovereignty

Some people believe God is not more powerful than man's free will. And in a world that feels out of control, it is easy to wonder whether God is in control at all.

A pastor described being strapped down before heart surgery to have a pacemaker installed. He was trembling involuntarily with fear. He lay there recalling every verse he could about God's sovereignty — and when he reached Colossians 1:16–17, God's peace washed over him like being wrapped in a warm blanket:

> "For by him all things were created, in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or dominions or rulers or authorities—all things were created through him and for him. And he is before all things, and in him all things hold together."
> — Colossians 1:16–17, ESV

We exist for God, by God, because of God, and are maintained by God. He was here before us, and as believers we will exist in eternity with him.

What does *sovereign* mean? Sovereignty means unlimited power. Ultimate authority. The authority of a king — with the power to ensure that his will is accomplished. Or to say it plainly:

> "When we say that God is sovereign, we are declaring that He has absolute authority over all His creation, that His rule extends to every nook and cranny of what He has made."
> — Dan Mastrapa [Note: unable to verify this attribution against a published source; pastor should confirm]

> "God's sovereignty is the pillow on which we rest our weary heads and our anxious hearts. It is the governing and central truth that guarantees He is able to do everything He determines to do, that He will fulfill all His promises."
> — Dan Mastrapa [Note: same — unverified attribution]

It is because of his sovereignty — his righteous rule over all things — that he *can*, and sometimes *does*, draw people like Matthew toward himself. This is the wonderful attribute of God that makes salvation possible for any of us. If it were not for God's sovereignty, we would have no interest in the things of the Lord.

This is something of what Matthew experienced in that moment — and it is what he traded his wealth and position and authority for. Jim Elliot understood it:

> "He is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain that which he cannot lose."
> — Jim Elliot, journal entry, October 28, 1949

[Note: the sermon renders this as "gain what he **cannot earn**" — the correct Elliot quotation is "gain that which he **cannot lose**." The original wording is the more theologically precise form and the one Elliot actually wrote.]

Could Matthew have ever *earned* heaven? Could he have *deserved* it? Of course not:

> "But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ—by grace you have been saved— and raised us up with him and seated us with him in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus, so that in the coming ages he might show the immeasurable riches of his grace in kindness toward us in Christ Jesus."
> — Ephesians 2:4–7, ESV

[Note: the sermon begins this quotation with "God, being rich in mercy"; the ESV text opens with "**But** God, being rich in mercy." The "but" is the hinge of the passage — worth retaining.]

## God Came for the Lost

My last point before the gospel: God came to save the lost. Matthew was as low as anyone could be in Israel at this time — reviled, a collaborator, a profiteer off his own people's pain. He was the chief of sinners, and he knew it. It calls to mind Isaiah 57:15:

> "For thus says the One who is high and lifted up, who inhabits eternity, whose name is Holy: 'I dwell in the high and holy place, and also with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit, to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite.'"
> — Isaiah 57:15, ESV

[Note: the sermon quotes this as "revive the spirit of the **broken**" — the ESV reads "revive the spirit of the **lowly**." The passage describes the same condition; "lowly" is the accurate ESV rendering. Also, the sermon's quotation contained cross-reference markers ("q", "r") from a Bible app — removed here.]

I think Matthew was broken. He barely includes himself in the stories he tells. This passage is the only place he quotes himself — and God took that brokenness and contrition and made something beautiful out of it. That is what God does. He takes the worst things we walk through and shapes us, molds us through them. He takes our brokenness and gives us beauty for ashes (Isaiah 61:3). He takes the broken mess we have made and lovingly puts things back together and points us toward Christ.

God is a careful, patient, and loving craftsman. He shapes us — patiently, carefully — into something useful, something that makes a difference for his kingdom. He gives us a place at his table. He gives us the title son or daughter, prince or princess. He gives us an eternity with him.

All he asks for is our mess. In exchange he gives us everything.

Beauty for ashes. But that starts with the gospel.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm and Winsome Opening with a Gospel Placeholder Where the Climax Should Be**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A textually honest and pastorally accessible opening of Matthew 9:9–13 that traces divine sovereignty convincingly but ends on the threshold of the gospel rather than through its door, leaving the sermon's entire weight resting on a bracketed note that says "*[Gospel]*."

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with reservation**

*Scopus Test*: The main point — that Christ sovereignly calls the worst of sinners, transforming their shame into belonging — does arise from the passage's own argument. The sermon tracks Matthew's calling, Christ's table fellowship with sinners, and Jesus' self-declared mission to call the sick and not the well. The thesis is traceable and text-governed.

*Authority Test*: The preacher submits to the text's claims. The sovereign draw of John 6:44 is not softened. The ugliness of Matthew's pre-conversion life is not sanitized. The Jesus who eats with sinners is not made polite.

*Reservation*: The sermon passes the gate but the entire gospel application is deferred to a bracketed placeholder. The passage itself contains an explicit statement of Christ's mission ("I came not to call the righteous, but sinners" — v.13), and the sermon does not land on that declaration with the full weight it deserves. The hermeneutical work is done; the proclamatory work is unfinished.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**FLAG TRIGGERED — Score capped at 69**

**Flag #1: No clear gospel presentation.** The sermon ends with "*[Gospel]*" — a placeholder. Christ's finished work is not proclaimed; it is anticipated. Ephesians 2:4–7 is quoted but not applied soteriologically. The mechanism of salvation — the cross, atonement, justification by faith — is absent from the manuscript. The phrase "that starts with the gospel" in the final line is an acknowledgment of the gap, not a filling of it.

**Flag #5: No actual exhortation.** The sermon explains and applies but never calls for response. There is no moment where the congregation is summoned — to believe, to repent, to come to Christ, to walk away from what Matthew walked away from. The closest the sermon gets is "All he asks for is our mess." That is a comfort; it is not an exhortation.

*These two flags are structurally related: the sermon is a runway without a landing strip. The approach is excellent. The touchdown does not happen.*

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17/25**
- *Textual Fidelity*: 7/10 — The sermon faithfully handles the calling narrative, the table fellowship scene, and Jesus' physician saying. The sovereign draw from John 6:44 is an appropriate and organic cross-reference. However, verse 13's "Go and learn what this means" — Jesus quoting Hosea 6:6 — receives no exegetical treatment. This is the hinge of the passage. Jesus is not just explaining himself; he is correcting the Pharisees' whole hermeneutic. That moment is skipped.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 4/5 — The historical-cultural context of tax collectors is developed with unusual depth and genuine accessibility. The Florida hurricane illustration is original and local. The redemptive-historical trajectory (Christ as the one who comes to sinners) is noted if not fully developed.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 3/5 — The meaning is clear, but the Hosea 6:6 quotation is not unpacked. Jesus' use of Hosea is not accidental — it is a direct rebuke of the Pharisees' sacrificial externalism. The sermon misses the exegetical nerve of the passage.
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 3/5 — The move from Matthew's calling → sovereignty → "God came for the lost" is logically sound. The sermon does not impose ideas. The Fallen Condition Focus (the lost condition of sinners like Matthew, and the self-righteous condition of the Pharisees) is present for Matthew but absent for the Pharisees — the text addresses both.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The hurricane illustration is exceptional.** The localization — specific neighborhoods, specific images (grandmother's bed, children's artwork, spears vs. law enforcement) — makes the first-century tax collector's world tangible and morally outrageous in a way that directly serves the text. This is illustration at its best: native to the passage, rooted in the congregation's actual geography, and impossible to forget.

2. **The treatment of Matthew's cost is exegetically sharp and underpreached.** The observation that Matthew, unlike the fishermen, could not return — that he walked away from uncollected revenue with no way back — is a genuine exegetical and pastoral insight. It is the kind of specific textual observation that rewards careful reading and makes the grace of the calling land harder.

3. **The doctrine of sovereignty is stated plainly without apology.** The sermon does not hedge on John 6:44 or the claim that divine drawing precedes human response. The statement "If it were not for God's sovereignty, we would have no interest in the things of the Lord" is confessionally precise and preached without embarrassment. This is a courage point.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a placeholder, not a proclamation.** The entire sermon drives toward "*[Gospel]*" — and that bracket contains everything that gives the sermon its reason for existing. Beauty for ashes is a true and beautiful image. But the preacher has not yet shown *how* the ashes become beauty — the mechanism is the cross, and the cross is absent from the manuscript. Ephesians 2:4–7 is quoted but not expounded. The word "justify" does not appear. "Atonement" does not appear. The sermon ends at the threshold of salvation and does not step through.

2. **Hosea 6:6 and the Pharisees receive no treatment.** This is the exegetical nerve of the passage. When Jesus says "Go and learn what this means: 'I desire mercy, and not sacrifice,'" he is not offering a polite self-explanation — he is dismantling the Pharisees' entire theological system. He is saying their religion produces the wrong kind of righteousness. This is the text's direct address to the self-righteous, and it is the most pastorally dangerous point in the pew. The sermon narrates past it entirely.

3. **No exhortation is ever issued.** The sermon ends with "Beauty for ashes. But that starts with the gospel." This is a transitional sentence to a gospel presentation that does not come. The congregation was never called to do anything — not to examine whether they are Matthew or a Pharisee, not to come to the physician, not to bring their mess, not to follow. The call to discipleship that cost Matthew everything is described but never extended.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears not to see the Pharisees as a live pastoral category in this sermon. The text gives equal structural weight to the Pharisees' question ("Why does your teacher eat with tax collectors and sinners?") and Jesus' response to it. The Pharisees are the sermon's second audience — and they represent the self-righteous, the morally respectable, the religiously active members of every congregation who would never be caught at the tax booth but who would also never recline with sinners. The sermon addresses the Matthew-types (the lost, the broken) without ever turning to face the Pharisee-types (the morally self-sufficient, the religiously proud). In a Baptist congregation, the Pharisee-type is statistically more common than the Matthew-type — and the text speaks to them directly. The preacher has a blind spot on the right side of the aisle.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The cultural context section is thorough, vivid, and well-structured — and it may create the impression that exegetical depth has been achieved before the passage is even read. The depth of the introduction produces a confidence that the exegetical work is done when in fact the hardest verse (v.13, Jesus quoting Hosea) has not been touched. The sermon *feels* like it has earned its theology before it begins, but the actual exegetical engagement with the text is shallower than the introduction suggests. The first fifteen minutes of preparation are not the same as exposition.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 13 is the sermon's missed gold. "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners." This is a declaration of Christ's entire mission — his self-stated purpose, the reason he was in that room, the reason Matthew was at that table, the reason the disciples were confused and the Pharisees were offended. The preacher touches the phrase in the passage reading but never expounds it. This is the verse that contains the direct answer to every question the sermon raises: Why did Jesus call Matthew? Why did he eat with sinners? Why does it matter? Because this is what he *came to do.* A full exegetical and applicatory landing on verse 13 — including the Hosea 6:6 citation and its rebuke of external religion — would have given the sermon the climax it is reaching for. The beauty-for-ashes image is drawn from Isaiah 61; Jesus' own words in verse 13 are the Matthew 9 equivalent of that same declaration. The preacher chose the cross-reference and missed the verse.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach verse 13 as the sermon's climax, not its background.** "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners" is the scopus of the passage. Rebuild the sermon's final movement around this declaration. Explain Hosea 6:6 in plain terms — Jesus is not softening the Pharisees; he is exposing their category error. Then apply it in both directions: to the Matthew-types (Christ came for you) and to the Pharisee-types (your respectability may be the thing most keeping you from him).

2. **Fill the gospel placeholder with a penal substitutionary proclamation.** The sermon's final line gestures toward the gospel — now preach it. Beauty for ashes becomes concrete at the cross: the ashes are Christ bearing the judgment we earned; the beauty is the righteousness he gives in exchange (2 Corinthians 5:21). The bracket must become a declaration. State who Jesus is, what he did, why his death accomplishes what no sacrifice could, and what faith in him receives.

3. **Issue a direct exhortation before the close.** After the gospel is proclaimed, summon the congregation. Distinguish: "If you are like Matthew — you know the weight of your own shame — Christ is calling you today. Rise and follow. If you are like the Pharisees — you have been religious and respectable and quietly confident in your own decency — Jesus is saying to you: your sacrifice is not what I want. Your mercy toward the broken is. Come to the physician. You are sick, too."

4. **Integrate the sovereignty material rather than parking it.** The John 6:44 / sovereignty section is theologically sound but structurally isolated. Consider folding the sovereignty point into the exposition of the calling narrative rather than treating it as a separate theological module. The sequence "Jesus called → Matthew followed → how?" is already asking for the sovereignty answer. Move it there and remove the standalone section.

5. **Retain the Hosea 6:6 address to the Pharisees and apply it to the congregation's religious respectability.** The self-righteous Pharisee is not just a first-century villain — he is the man who has been in church every Sunday, tithes faithfully, volunteers consistently, and has never seriously considered that his religiosity might be the thing Christ finds most offensive. The text addresses him. The sermon should.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Matthew 9:9–13 is a narrative passage with a pronouncement at its center (v.13). The rubric's exposition categories apply well — the passage has a clear scopus, a clear FCF (the human condition of being spiritually sick and either knowing it or refusing to), and a clear gospel movement. The illustration and sermonic force categories also apply straightforwardly to a narrative text. No rubric strain detected. The passage is well-served by this framework. The sermon's gaps are not artifacts of genre — they are genuine absences of gospel proclamation and exhortation that the narrative's own shape was calling for.
