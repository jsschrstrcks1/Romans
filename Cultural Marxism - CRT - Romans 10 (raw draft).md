**Dhund Abbasi — Pakistan**

The Dhund Abbasi people are said to have come to ancient Pakistan from Egypt around 850 AD, as traders and merchants in commodities. It is likely that they are descended from the Abbasid dynasty. This dynasty governed for 500 years from Baghdad, Iraq. The rule of the Abbasi extended eastwards across Afghanistan into the South Asian subcontinent, covering the eastern part of modern-day Pakistan. A conflicting account is that the Dhund Abbasi are descendants of Abbas, an uncle of their prophet Mohammad. Either way, there are nearly a million of these people, and to the best of our knowledge not a single one is a believer in Jesus Christ. *(Verify current numbers against Joshua Project/IMB data before reuse.)* Muslims who can trace their lineage back to Mohammad tend to be especially proud of this and more devoted as a result. This makes ministering in their midst especially challenging. Who will pray for the Dhund of Pakistan?

There is so much more to talk about when it comes to the church and current issues facing our denomination. One of the leaders of our denomination — the president of The Southern Baptist Theological Seminary in Kentucky — is calling what we are seeing the invasion of cultural Marxism. That sounds like a complicated term, but it's really not. Anyone know what Marxism is? It's a political system where everything you own is taken away by a group of people at the top, to be redistributed by them to whomever they want for whatever reasons they deem important.

Can anyone define culture? Culture is a reflection of a people's achievements, aspirations, art, and intellect.

Cultural Marxism, then, seeks to suppress any cultural thing with which it disagrees, and support any cultural thing with which it agrees. Think in terms of what's been happening with southern culture recently — people have been tearing down statues, burning flags, encouraging and discouraging elements of our collective heritage. Have you picked up on this at all? I'm sure we're all aware of this happening to some extent even here in our own area — there was a statue pulled down in Brooksville because it didn't agree with a certain cultural narrative. It isn't just the radicals on the left that are guilty of this. If you're familiar with the alt-right, they use these same tactics to oppress cultural things produced by ethnic groups they dislike. Suppressing anyone or anything simply because it was thought of, painted by, or written by a person of a different group than you are part of is racism. Full stop.

The way this is manifesting in our denomination right now — and especially through some of the seminaries — is that... well, let's do it this way.

Let's say Dottie is from Albania, and let's say Greg is a white Protestant American male.

Let's say Dottie writes a book on philosophy. It's good overall. It's not a subject she's passionate about, and it shows through in her work. However, she is an excellent writer, and that makes up a lot of the difference — making it a worthwhile book to read. Then let's say Greg writes a book on the same topic. Except let's say for Greg this subject is his passion. He's studied it for his whole life. He wakes up thinking about it; it's the last thing he thinks about before he goes to bed. He eats this subject, he drinks this subject. He's read every book on the subject that has been published since the invention of the printing press. Not only that, but he has the same writing ability as Dottie — and he has a depth and consideration on this topic that few in the world could match. So he produces a book that is groundbreaking and completely shakes up the world of philosophy, redefining the subject for everyone. He basically writes THE book that everyone is going to be studying for the next century whenever this topic comes up.

If you decided to teach a class on the subject, which book would you choose to teach from? Greg's book or Dottie's? Based on those descriptions, we would all choose Greg's book.

The form of cultural Marxism we are facing ignores the merit of Greg's book and says that because Dottie is Albanian — even though her book isn't as good, or as groundbreaking, or as important to the subject — we should eliminate Greg's book and only allow everyone to read from Dottie's book. Primarily because Dottie was born with certain genes.

The definition of racism is prejudice, discrimination, or antagonism directed against someone of a different race. You don't have to believe you're better than someone to be a racist. Hitler believed blonde-haired, blue-eyed white people were superior. He was not blond, he did not have blue eyes, and he did not fit his own ideal — yet he hated people based on their ethnicity. Hitler was no less a racist for not thinking he himself was superior.

People today who are prejudicial, discriminatory, or antagonistic based simply on race are no less racists than Hitler was.

It isn't Albanians that are the focus of this invasion. And we love everyone that walks through those doors regardless of race, or politics, or gender.

The practice of cultural Marxism can best be expressed by a guy I went to school with. He wrote: "White people need to quit their jobs, and stop publishing their books, and let people that are not white have them, and write them. Schools need to fire their white professors and give the jobs to people that are not white. Schools need to purge their libraries of books that were written by white professors. Professors need to purge their syllabi of any books that were written by white people. Even if those professors, or books, are objectively better than the books written by any person of color." Let me ask you this: does this discriminate based on race? Does it antagonize based on race? Is it prejudicial based on race?

This most recent convention of the Southern Baptists had a resolution that talked about critical race theory and intersectionality. The idea is that... well, let me quote David Brooks *(⚠️ source unverified — see flags at bottom)*, who writes: "Two great belief systems are clashing here. The older liberals tend to be individualistic and meritocratic. A citizen's job is to be activist, compassionate, and egalitarian. Boomers generally think they earned their success through effort and talent." But "the younger militants tend to have been influenced by the cultural Marxism that is now the lingua franca in the elite academy. Group identity is what matters. Society is a clash of oppressed and oppressor groups. People who are successful got that way through some form of group privilege and a legacy of oppression."

If you are comfortable, they seem to argue, you are comfortable because you took advantage of people to get to that point. If you are uncomfortable, it is because the world, your boss, some other race, etc., is taking advantage of you. Coupling this with the theory of intersectionality — the idea that the more ways in which you are a minority (gender, race, sexual orientation), the greater degree to which you have been taken advantage of — if you are a white male, you are at the top of the heap. White female, you're a close second. If you're a male of color, you're below that depending on the color you happen to be. And if you're a female of color, you're lower still. A base assumption, then, is that all men are always taking advantage of all women, all the time. All white men are taking advantage of all people of color, all the time. And every ethnicity takes advantage of people of less dominant ethnicities.

This is nonsense. Think about your own actions and this idea of intersectionality: are you actively trying to take advantage of everyone lower than you on the list, always? Knowing most of you pretty well, I don't think any of you are the type to be actively trying to take advantage.

It goes at least one step further when it starts talking about truth. When you and I evaluate the truth of something, what do we compare it to? Scripture. Under this worldview, experience is the arbiter of truth — especially the experience of those who have been the victims of white privilege. The more subjugated you are, the more truth you have experienced; the more truth you have experienced, the more valuable your opinions are. This is similar to the error many Catholics make when they elevate the words of the Pope or tradition above Scripture. What happens when people do that? Nothing but trouble. The Jehovah's Witnesses do this, and they are a cult. The Mormons do this, and they are also a cult. If a person finds ultimate truth outside of Scripture, they are walking down a dangerous path.

What does the Bible say about all of this? Like we read earlier:

> ...for in Christ Jesus you are all sons of God, through faith. For as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ. There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus. And if you are Christ's, then you are Abraham's offspring, heirs according to promise.
> — Galatians 3:26–29 (ESV)

> — Acts 10:34–35 (ESV)

*(Someone open to Colossians 3:11)*

> Here there is not Greek and Jew, circumcised and uncircumcised, barbarian, Scythian, slave, free; but Christ is all, and in all.
> — Colossians 3:11 (ESV)

*(Someone open to Acts 10:28)*

*(Someone open to Revelation 5:9–10)*

> For there is no distinction between Jew and Greek; for the same Lord is Lord of all, bestowing his riches on all who call on him. For "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved."
> — Romans 10:12–13 (ESV)

This is the clear and consistent testimony of Scripture:

> For the LORD your God is God of gods and Lord of lords, the great, the mighty, and the awesome God, who is not partial and takes no bribe.
> — Deuteronomy 10:17 (ESV)

Those who adhere to this worldview forget that our citizenship is not on earth. We are citizens of heaven, here just for a short while. This whole worldview forgets that we are just passing through, and that what matters most isn't the things of earth, but the things of heaven. Is your citizenship in heaven? Then a wonderful eternity awaits. If your citizenship is of earth, then you have no hope.

One day we are all going to be standing in front of God in heaven. What race we are doesn't matter. What culture we are doesn't matter. What color shoes, or what watch, or what car we drive isn't going to matter. The most important thing is going to be whether or not we have a relationship with Jesus Christ.

In Christ we are all equal. Slaves bought back off the auction block. Sinners condemned and unclean — except for the work of Christ. None of us are better or more deserving. But for the grace of God, we would all be spending eternity in hell apart from Christ.

As the praise team comes up and sings a final song, I want you to ask yourselves: where is your citizenship? Are you a citizen of heaven? Or does your heart belong to the things of earth? If you aren't a citizen of heaven, come talk to me after the service, and I'll show you in the Bible how you can have eternal life.
