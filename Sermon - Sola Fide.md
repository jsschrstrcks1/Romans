## The Danger of Adding to Salvation

A few weeks back, studying grace alone, we learned that the Catholics were adding things to salvation — telling people that if only they gave enough money to the church, their dead loved one could get out of hell; the selling of indulgences, where one could commit adultery, murder, rape, or steal, "approved quote-unquote by God."

Most of the things they added to salvation are not innately sinful — but adding things to Scripture is sinful. These additions to salvation persist to this day. Scripture says in various places that adding to or taking away from Scripture leads to trouble. Galatians talks specifically about adding to or taking away from the gospel.

*[Note: Galatians 1:8–9 says those who preach a different gospel are to be "anathema" — that is, accursed or under God's curse. The pastor describes this as "cause for breaking off relationships," which significantly understates the force of the Greek. The text pronounces a curse, not merely a relational break.]*

### What Were They Adding?

**Baptism.** Baptism isn't a bad thing — we are Baptist, we put a strong emphasis on baptism. But baptism according to Scripture is not a prerequisite for salvation. It is the first act of obedience a believer takes after being saved. Encouraging believers to be baptized is good. Adding it as a requirement for salvation is wrong.

**Confession.** Confession of sins to a priest — Scripture commands us to confess our sins to one another (James 5:16), but not specifically to a priest. The Catholic Church adds requirements for penance on top of that. Not one word in Scripture requires a priest; not one word about rosary beads or repeating Hail Marys.

*[Note: James 5:16 — "Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed." ✓]*

Our enemy the devil is tricky. He takes things that are either good, or at least not bad, and twists them into something that is not good.

- Baptism is good — but saying it is a necessary piece of the salvation puzzle is a perversion of Scripture.
- Confessing our sins is good — but saying that failure to confess to a priest causes sin to overcome Christ's work, and sends us to hell, is a twisting of Scripture.
- Prayer is good — but praying to saints and to Mary is wrong.
- Memorizing Scripture is wise — but memorizing only one passage as a mechanical formula to escape trouble is a twisting of Scripture.
- Marriage is a good thing — but requiring it as a sacrament for salvation goes too far. Paul writes the opposite: it is better to remain single if you can, because the single person only has to concern himself with pleasing God. If you are married, stay married — you preach the gospel in a good marriage. But if not, do not worry about it; God has purposes in that too.

This is how the Catholic Church was taking good things and twisting them — in most cases just a little, in others grossly.

## Always Be Reforming

As Christians, we must always be on guard. I believe it was Karl Barth who said *semper reformanda* — Latin for "always be reforming."

*[Note: The full phrase is "Ecclesia semper reformanda est" — the Church must always be reformed. The phrase's earliest well-documented source is Jodocus van Lodenstein, a Dutch Reformed pietist, writing in 1674. Karl Barth greatly popularized it in modern usage, particularly after 1947. The phrase may trace to earlier sources still. Attributing it to Barth is not wrong given his prominent role in its modern transmission, though Van Lodenstein has the stronger historical claim to origination.]*

Always be examining yourself, your actions, your beliefs — comparing them to Scripture. Always be taking steps to be more aligned with God's Word, each and every day.

## Luther on Sola Fide

With that backdrop, the reformers looked at Scripture and compared it to what they saw around them. Luther then wrote:

> *"Formerly, when our hearts were blinded by errors in teachings from Rome, we imagined that God was a salesman who sold his grace in exchange for our works and achievements. Now, however, since the light of the gospel has dawned, we know that we are credited as righteous only by faith in Christ."*
> — Martin Luther

*[Note: Confirmed authentic, cited in Luther's Works (American Edition), 1965. The specific volume and page were not listed in the source found, but the attribution to LW is well-documented.]*

Sola Fide — faith alone.

If salvation can only be granted by grace alone, it can only be ours through faith alone. And this faith, Ephesians 2 says, is not of ourselves; we cannot earn it or buy it with money. We can only receive it through faith.

Apart from Christ, all we could do over all the years of our lives has left us wretched, blind, and poor — beggars. Even the best of us, merely beggars. Martin Luther, who stood firm against the full weight of the Catholic Church with the help of God and prevailed, called himself a mere beggar.

*[Note: Luther's last written words are reported as "Wir sind Bettler, das ist wahr" — "We are all beggars, that is true." This is historically documented. ✓]*

Paul, who wrote half of the New Testament, also considered himself the chief of sinners (1 Timothy 1:15). If anyone could brag or earn their way, it would be Paul. But no — we are beggars. Sinners. Lost. Apart from Christ.

## Text: Psalm 46 — What Faith Is Placed In

It strikes me that it is hard to have faith in someone we do not know. So let us look at Psalm 46.

*[Psalm 46 — paraphrased in the sermon:]*

- God is our Refuge
- God is our Strength
- God is Ever-Present
- God is a help in and out of trouble
- God's presence chases away fear — even in the midst of great catastrophe, even when it looks like you are going to drown, even when the very foundations of your world are shaken
- God makes his people glad; his presence is near
- God and his purposes cannot be defeated
- God always shows up on time
- When God speaks, nothing can stand
- God is with us

This is the God of Jacob — the God of our fathers and their fathers. The God who led Israel out of Egypt with all the plagues. The God who led his people by day and by night as a pillar of smoke or fire. The God who helped Gideon, Samson, Elijah. The God who defeated the prophets of Baal at Mount Carmel. The God who protected us from the storm.

He can bring ruin and peace. Weapons of war are like toothpicks to him.

This is the God who says "Be still — know that I am God."

That commands us to exalt him.
That promises to be with us.
That is our fortress who will protect us.

What a mighty God we serve.

## The Scale of God's Creation

Another Psalm says the heavens are God's footstool. Consider what that means.

People, space is huge. If we wanted to travel from Earth to Saturn, it would take us the better part of a decade in a straight line.

One of the well-known large stars — often referred to as Betelgeuse, though the pastor pronounces it "Beatlejuice" — is a red supergiant in the constellation Orion.

*[Note: Betelgeuse is the correct spelling and standard pronunciation is typically "BET-el-jooze" or "BEE-tel-jooze." The name also gives us the word "Betelgeuse" in pop culture.]*

> **Size comparison:** If placed at the center of our solar system, Betelgeuse would engulf Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, and possibly Jupiter. Current best estimates put its radius at approximately 700–760 solar radii, which puts its surface near or just short of Jupiter's orbit. The pastor's comparison to "Saturn's orbit" is a slight overstatement — most current estimates put it closer to Jupiter's orbit — though some older, larger estimates did approach Saturn. The comparison conveys Betelgeuse's staggering scale accurately enough for the point being made. ✓ in spirit.

*[Note: The pastor says Betelgeuse is "one of the next closest star systems." This is incorrect. Betelgeuse is approximately 400–700 light-years from Earth (current best estimates cluster around 548 light-years). The closest star system to Earth is Proxima Centauri at 4.24 light-years. Betelgeuse is not among the nearest stars by any measure — it is roughly 100 times more distant than the nearest star.]*

If you wanted to get to Betelgeuse today, using the technology we have, it would take an enormous span of time.

*[Note: The pastor says "about 80 billion years" at 57,000 km/h. The actual calculation for 548 light-years (lower-end estimate) at 57,000 km/h:*
*548 light-years × 9.461 × 10¹² km/ly ÷ 57,000 km/h ÷ 8,760 h/year ≈ 13 million years.*
*Even at 700 light-years, the figure is still approximately 16–17 million years — not 80 billion. The "3.2 billion generations" figure is similarly off; at 25 years per generation and 13 million years, the calculation yields approximately 520,000 generations. The pastor's numbers overstate the travel time by a factor of roughly 5,000–6,000. The conceptual point — that the distance is incomprehensibly vast — is absolutely correct. The specific figures are not.]*

Our tiny galaxy — the Milky Way — is approximately 100,000 to 200,000 light-years across.

*[Note: The pastor says "200,000 light years." The traditional figure is ~100,000–120,000 light-years for the visible disc; more recent research suggests the outer disc may extend the total to ~180,000–200,000 light-years. The higher figure is defensible.]*

When I was a kid and we would travel, I always liked to get a map from each state we went through — a free souvenir that gave me something to do. Some of those towns on a state map are just a tiny flea-speck of a thing. You know the kind — one traffic light blinking yellow, you might have to slow down for the chickens. The smallest town in Florida by population is Marineland, near Flagler. If the universe were a state map, our galaxy is Marineland.

Our galaxy is part of a grouping we call the Local Group.

*[Note: The pastor uses the term "local cluster" and says it "contains a hundred thousand or so galaxies." The Local Group — the standard term for our galactic neighborhood — contains approximately 54 known galaxies, not 100,000. If the pastor means a larger supercluster (such as the Virgo Supercluster), it contains several thousand galaxy groups and clusters, but still not 100,000 individual galaxies at comparable size. This figure appears to be a significant overstatement. The conceptual point — that our galaxy is one among many — is accurate.]*

The Local Group is part of the Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex.

*[Note: The Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex is a real proposed large-scale structure, one of the largest known in the observable universe, spanning approximately one billion light-years. To traverse it at the speed of light would take on the order of a billion years — far longer than the "hundreds of billions of years" the pastor cites, though the point about the incomprehensible scale is valid. Exact dimensions of such large-scale structures are still subject to ongoing research.]*

To traverse the sky — from one end of such a structure to the other — would take unimaginable spans of time at the speed of light. We cannot go that fast. And that is just one structure among many.

*[Note: The "400 billion stars" reference — the Milky Way is estimated to contain 100–400 billion stars. 400 billion is on the high end of the accepted range but not outside it.]*

All of this — every atom of it — God calls his footstool. We serve a mighty God. A BIG God.

It should boggle our minds that such a being would stop to listen to the cares of us tiny, tiny creatures. We seem like the least significant things in all of creation, and yet he loves us. He chose us. He called us. He extended grace to beggars.

We lacked the faith — and yet Romans 12 says God gives each of us a measure of faith.

*[Note: Romans 12:3 — "...as God has apportioned to each one a measure of faith." ✓]*

All of that is on top of what Psalm 46 tells us:

God is our Refuge. Our Strength. Our Ever-Present help. He chases away fear. He makes his people glad. His purposes cannot be defeated. He always shows up on time. When he speaks, nothing can stand. God is with us. He can bring ruin and peace. Weapons of war are like toothpicks. He is our fortress.

What calamity is too big for our God? Is there any? Is cancer too big? AIDS? Addiction? What problem is so big our God cannot handle it?

We know he is omniscient — he knows everything, past, present, and future. Nothing catches him off guard.

We know he is omnipresent — here, there, everywhere. There is nowhere he is not. He is at Betelgeuse now and at the other side of the Pisces–Cetus complex now and between and around every atom now.

And he is omnipotent — all powerful. All of these things that boggle our brightest minds — all of these beautiful complexities we have spent thousands of years trying to understand — were spoken into existence with a single word from God.

*"Let there be."*

And it was.

This incomparable, wonderful God wants to have a relationship with you. He supplies the grace. He supplies the faith. He has done the hard work.

These glorious truths — Sola Gratia, Sola Fide, Solus Christus — are our inheritance from men like Martin Luther. That is far better than dressing up like a goof and getting candy. This has lasting meaning.
