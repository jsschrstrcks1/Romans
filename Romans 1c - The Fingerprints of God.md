## People Group: The Balija of India

Before I do, let me give you a number. Seventeen thousand people groups in the world today. More than 17,000. How many of those do you figure are unreached? The Joshua Project defines unreached as less than 2% evangelical Christian. More than 7,000 people groups are either completely unengaged, or have less than 2% evangelical Christians in their population.

Our people group this week falls into the unreached category. Only a fraction of one percent of them are believers in Jesus Christ.

They are called the **Balija of India**.

They are largely traders — bangles, salt, pearls. Those who aren't in trade are in agriculture. Nearly all are vegetarian, which tracks with their Hinduism — though some aren't, which is the surprise. They marry within their caste. And unlike many castes, the Balija are permitted to drink alcohol. In India, alcohol consumption by a caste is usually associated with lower caste status. The Balija are not a low caste. That's an unusual combination.

They are hard to reach. Hindus in India generally want to add Jesus to their collection of idols — not exchange the collection for him. And that's exactly the problem. The Balija who consider Christ often believe that accepting him means rejecting their culture, their history, their traditions, their family.

That's a real cost. We shouldn't minimize it.

But what does God say? He says he is a jealous God. He does not share his throne. He does not accept second place in the heart or the household. He is Lord, or he is not Lord at all. And nothing is too great for God to overcome.

Who will pray for the Balija?

Let's pray.

## I. Handle with Care — What General Revelation Does and Doesn't Do

This is one of those areas of Scripture where you have to be careful, or you end up becoming a heretic.

People have tried to use these verses to say something Paul didn't intend. Some have read verses 19-20 and concluded: anyone can be saved apart from Christ. If God has shown himself through creation, maybe creation is enough.

That is not what Paul is saying. You have to twist this passage to make it say that.

How is someone saved? We know we are not capable of faith or belief on our own — those can only come from God. So when someone draws a conclusion from this passage that contradicts that, we know the interpretation has gone sideways.

What Paul says is that they *knew* God. They could see his fingerprints across all of creation. But they didn't acknowledge him. Didn't honor him. Didn't give thanks to him as God.

Here's the distinction we must not miss: general revelation — what God shows through creation — is sufficient to *condemn*. It is not sufficient to *save.*

Creation tells you God is real. It tells you he is powerful. It tells you he is eternal. But it does not tell you his name. It does not tell you about the cross. It does not tell you how to be reconciled to the God whose fingerprints are on everything.

For that, you need the gospel. You need the Word spoken. You need a person to go.

This is why the Balija matter. They can look up at the stars and know that someone made them. But unless someone goes, they will die knowing God exists without ever knowing how to reach him. Creation condemns. Only the gospel saves.

And the gospel must be carried by human feet to human ears.

## II. The Fingerprints of God

So what does it mean that we can see God in his creation?

This is called the anthropic principle. Most of us are familiar with the content even if we don't know the term. You've heard it before: if we were much closer to the sun, the earth would burn — oceans evaporate, life impossible. Much farther away, the earth freezes. That narrow band we inhabit is not an accident.

William Lane Craig often talks about nuclear resonance — the speed at which atomic nuclei vibrate. I'm no nuclear physicist, but here's what I've gathered.

You've had an MRI, or know someone who has. Part of the electromagnetic spectrum is fired into your body, which makes the nucleus of every cell it encounters get excited — vibrate, essentially dance. Those nuclei reflect a signal back, and the machine takes its picture from that signal.

Now. The speed at which nuclei resonate seems like a fairly unimportant detail. But if they were to resonate just a little slower — at .8 instead of 1 — you could throw away the periodic table. Every element except helium would be impossible to form. Just that small a shift. And if they vibrated just a little faster — 1.2 instead of 1 — everything after the first four elements (hydrogen, helium, lithium, beryllium) would be impossible.

If the resonance were sped up by a full one percent, every atom of carbon in the universe would become oxygen. Think about what that means. We are carbon-based life — nearly 20% of the human body is carbon. Carbon bonds to other molecules in four directions; that's what makes life's chemistry possible. One percent shift. All of it gone. Life as we know it ceases.

[*Careful: The specific thresholds — .8, 1.2, and the 1% carbon-to-oxygen conversion — are presented here as heard from Craig's teaching. The underlying fine-tuning argument is established (Hoyle state / triple-alpha process); the precise numbers should be verified against Craig's published work before preaching.*]

Some things in the universe are constant. Electromagnetism is one of them. If it grew stronger by one part in a thousand, our sun would rip itself apart and send whatever earth-shrapnel remained hurtling at ballistic speeds into deep space.

The atmosphere we breathe is the result of factors so complicated we can't fully account for them: the composition of the earth, our distance from the sun, liquid water, the interaction of electromagnetism and gravity. Even a slight change in sunlight, distance, gravity, or electromagnetism triggers a cascade that ends in life as we know it becoming impossible. Slightly less gravity, and the atmosphere bleeds away. Our planet, unshielded from the sun's radiation, becomes a desert. The plants die first. The animals follow within a month. Planet-wide death.

They say there are no straight lines in nature — that's not quite true. You know the classic picture of an atom: one ball in the center, orbited by a smaller ball. That smaller ball is an electron. If electrons didn't follow the precisely same path on every single atom, all the time, without exception — life as we know it couldn't exist. An atom with an unstable electron path produces two things: radiation, and heat. If even one electron could choose between two possible paths, everything would generate too much heat for life to continue. If electrons broke free, radiation would be produced. What happens when organic life is exposed to too much radiation? Ask the Japanese. Hiroshima. Nagasaki. Now imagine radiation being emitted by every atom, all at once. Our planet ignites.

There is a particle called a baryon — smaller than the atom. Named from the Greek word for *heavy*, because scientists at the time thought it was one of the heavier subatomic particles. The discovery of the baryon opened the study of entropy per baryon — the ratio of accessible to inaccessible energy in the cosmos. That ratio is precisely 1 to 10⁹. If it shifted too high or too low, galaxies cannot form. No galaxies, no solar systems. No solar systems, no life.

This is all stuff that God has revealed to us through science. One of my sister's professors told her class that the more she studies science, the more she believes there must be an all-powerful God who designed everything. She's right. And Paul says they didn't even need the science. "Ever since the creation of the world," the evidence has been "clearly perceived." The first humans who looked up at the sky knew.

### We Are Positioned

We have a unique spot in the Milky Way.

If we were deeper in the galaxy, we couldn't use telescopes to study the universe beyond us. The Magellanic Cloud would block us — in the southern sky, away from light pollution, there's a smear of pink and blue and brownish-yellow. That's the Small Magellanic Cloud. We have no technology that can see through it. Deeper in the Milky Way, we'd be blind.

But if we were farther out, we'd be largely unprotected. Rogue planets — worlds that have broken free of their stars — pass through our galactic neighborhood regularly. Where we sit, they'd have to navigate past hundreds of other stars, possibly black holes, before reaching our solar system. And if one did come our way, it would still have to avoid thousands of objects in the Kuiper Belt, then Mars, the asteroid belt, then Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Pluto, and thousands of other objects orbiting our sun, before reaching Earth.

It's almost like someone planned it.

### The Philosophers at the Shore

Humans have always looked up and wondered.

There was a philosopher named Empedocles — he's the one who gave us the four elements: earth, wind, fire, water. Another, Heraclitus, took it further. He believed fire was the primary element, and that the universe was made up of a great wheel; as the wheel rotated, fire burned through it, and the gaps were what we saw — the sun, the stars, the moon. All of it fire peeking through holes.

[*Careful: The fiery-ring cosmology with holes for stars is more precisely attributed to Anaximander. Heraclitus's contribution was the primacy of fire as element and logos, but his cosmology was somewhat different. This may be a conflation of two pre-Socratics. Worth verifying the attribution before preaching.*]

It's in our nature — in the DNA of who we are — to sit and marvel at things bigger than us, bigger than what we could build. These old Greek philosophers would do what the tourists do on our beaches: go out, sit down, look up at the sun, and try to make sense of it. *What is it? Why is it? How is it?*

These same men who couldn't explain the universe without God — if they had been walking along a beach and found a watch lying in the sand, they would have said immediately: a watchmaker made this. Someone bought it. Someone lost it. They would never say the watch assembled itself. Yet when they looked at the stars, they said: impossible for it to have been made. Must have just happened.

Or think about the first humans to encounter the Grand Canyon. I am looking forward to getting to see it in a few weeks — thank you for making that possible. But what did they think?

Scientists today say it was carved by the Colorado River. The Grand Canyon is so large it could hold all the water in all the world and only be half full. Maybe it was caused by water. I have my doubts about the little Colorado. Maybe it was the flood. When I was a kid I would fill a water bottle and squirt a path in the dirt — imagine that on God's scale. I read once about a discovered underground reservoir that holds more than three times the amount of water on the surface of the earth, and that it would take three times all known water on earth to cover the earth completely. That's a lot of water that had to go somewhere. It's something I'll have to ask God about one day — but it makes sense to me.

Point of all this: there is enough information in the world around us to know that the world was created by an all-powerful God.

## III. The Exchange

And yet a devastating number of people choose to reject him.

Verses 21-22: they *knew* God. They saw his fingerprints. But they didn't honor him, didn't give thanks, and their thinking became futile. Their hearts went dark. Claiming to be wise, they became fools.

John Calvin wrote that "the human heart is a perpetual factory for idols" (*Institutes* I.xi.8). We may not carve them from wood. But we worship the idol reflected in a calm stream — or in a mirror. We feel we are enough. We can figure it out. We are smart enough, and strong enough, and powerful enough to do anything we set our minds to.

I love our nation. And for a lot of things, that's accurate. Want to put men in space? We can figure that out. We're even working on a space elevator — I bet they don't fix the elevator music though. Want to travel to the bottom of the ocean? There are survey teams descending to the deepest trenches in the world right now. You can watch them live.

Want to reach God on your own? That is something the combined military, scientific, and financial genius of the entire planet cannot buy or take. There is only one way to God, and that is through Jesus Christ.

## IV. The Mirror

Because apart from Christ, mankind resembles verses 28 through the end.

We are described as having "all manner of unrighteousness, evil, covetousness, malice... envy, murder, strife, deceit, maliciousness... gossips, slanderers, haters of God, insolent, haughty, boastful, inventors of evil, disobedient to parents, foolish, faithless, heartless, ruthless."

We like to think people are basically good. But when you look at yourself in the mirror of this passage — apart from Christ — how do you look?

Gossiped lately? Boasted? Been foolish? Faithless? Heartless? Ruthless? Coveted something? Harbored anger?

Jesus said in Matthew 5:21-22: "You have heard that it was said to those of old, 'You shall not murder; and whoever murders will be liable to judgment.' But I say to you that everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment" — and he goes on to say, in danger of hell fire.

I daresay there is not a single person in this room who hasn't been angry. The seed is in all of us. This list is not a description of exceptional sinners. It is a description of humanity apart from the restraining grace of God. It is a description of all of us.

Yet God has a plan. And in that plan he has already factored in the entire sum of our stupidity and failure.

## V. The God Who Planned the Cross

Before we close with the gospel, I want to share something. A friend of mine wrote a meditation that was inspired by a quote — let me share both.

The original comes from Octavius Winslow:

> "So completely was Jesus bent upon saving sinners by the sacrifice of Himself, He created the tree upon which He was to die, and nurtured from infancy the men who were to nail Him to the accursed wood."

[*Careful: Verify exact wording and source before preaching. In the spoken delivery the pastor added "(God Through)" before "Jesus" as a theological clarification; that parenthetical is his own addition, not Winslow's. The quote is presented here without the parenthetical, preserving the apparent original. Confirm against a primary Winslow source.*]

He planned it all — precisely. He arranged every element of his life and orchestrated every element of his death in such a way that it would defeat sin, and death, and hell, and Satan.

My pastor friend Michael Shultz wrote this:

> The men who would grow up to be the Roman soldiers who crucified Christ were once little boys. When they were little, God took care of them to make sure they safely grew up. For 30 years, God had planned and prepared for those little boys to be the men they needed to be in that moment.
>
> The tree which would be cut down to provide Christ's cross weathered storms and winds because God made sure it grew into maturity and strength. For a hundred or more years, God had planned and prepared for the tree upon which his Son would die for that moment.
>
> The nails in his hands and feet were forged of ore that God had overseen the formation of at the foundation of the world. For thousands of years, God had planned and prepared for that moment.
>
> Christ died on that cross — not only at the hand of the Romans or Jews, not just at the hands of you and I, but at the hand of his Father. God the Father had planned and prepared from before the foundation of the world for that moment.
>
> Christ's sacrifice was not a Plan B or a spur-of-the-moment event. God planned for that moment from eternity past. And he planned the moment of your conversion — and the moment of your death — with equal precision and care.

Nothing catches him off guard. Nothing is a surprise. It all fits into the grand scope of his plan. If he attends to something as small as the resonance frequency of an atomic nucleus — if the baryon entropy ratio is precisely calibrated to one part in ten billion — do you think there is anything in your life that escapes his notice?

Rest in the providence of our God.

## VI. Why Would He Choose Any of Us?

Considering the lengths God went to in order to secure our pardon — it is amazing to me that any of us were chosen at all.

We talked about this Thursday night before dinner. People ask: how is it fair or just for God to choose this person and not that one?

Honestly — considering what we put God's Son through, what you and I put Jesus through — it is more amazing to me that he would choose any of us at all. It was *my* sin that held Jesus there on the cross until it was finished.

I love all of you. But let me make it personal for a moment. Imagine someone came to me and said: "I've been disobedient. You said the cost was my life. But I'm hoping you'll let your child die in my place instead." If someone asked me to hook Wyley up to an electric chair and throw the switch to save Dottie, or Willie, or Richard, or Greg, or John, or Bob, or any of you — my instinct would be no. Wyley didn't do anything to deserve your death. He's innocent. Why would I do that?

Yet God, in his mercy and grace, not only chose not to spare Jesus in your place and mine — he nurtured the tree, and the soldiers, and the iron he would use to spill the innocent blood of his own Son. So that we could be with him in glory.

Time after time God gave us chances. He set up the sacrificial system. We failed it. He sent judges. We failed that too. He led his people directly in the early days of Israel. And how did that work out? They told God he wasn't enough, and demanded not just a king, not even God's choice of king — they demanded an ungodly man, Saul, to take God's place in their lives. Time after time God gave us chances, and we spit in his face. We worshiped idols. We rejected him. He even wiped the world clean and kept one believing family — and within a generation the world was a mess again. Not because God failed. Because we couldn't keep a good thing going for one generation.

So God made a new covenant. He paid our ransom with the one most precious to him — his Son. Scripture says his only Son, whom he loved. Jesus' life was freely poured out for those who believe — for those whom he had chosen before the foundation of the world. Those whose belief he himself enabled them to have.
