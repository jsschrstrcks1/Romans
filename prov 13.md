## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Date Evaluated**: 2026-03-21
**Passage**: Proverbs 13
**Evaluator**: Thus Says the Lord Rubric v1.0

---

### 1. Verdict Title
A Culturally Engaged Topical Talk That Uses Proverbs 13 as a Frame for a Generational-Gap Lament

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A passionate, culturally observant address built around the decline of biblical wisdom across generations that engages the congregation's real world honestly but derives its governing argument from Paul Washer and a cultural diagnosis rather than from Proverbs 13's own argument, leaving the text as backdrop rather than authority.

### 3. Berean Gate
**FAIL**

- **Scopus Test**: The sermon spends the majority of its length on: (1) the Paul Washer anecdote about Puritan children's education, (2) the public school system and its connection to secularism, (3) a chemistry classroom explosion story, and (4) a Live PD story about a teenage motorcyclist. The passage from Proverbs 13 is read late in the sermon and only two verses are actually discussed: v. 1 (a wise son hears his father's instruction) and v. 20 (walk with the wise and become wise). Proverbs 13 has 25 verses covering speech, diligence, lying, hope, discipline, poverty, and righteousness. The sermon's main point — raise your children to love God; return to biblical wisdom — is *related to* themes in Proverbs 13 but is not derived from the passage's own argument.
- **Authority Test**: The text is read and then partially cited. The preacher's governing authority is the cultural diagnosis (we've declined from Puritan heights), not the text's own structure and movement.

> "This sermon is not text-driven. The main point of the sermon does not arise from the main point of the passage. No score is assigned under the strict gate. A diagnostic score is provided to identify what is present and what is missing."

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Neither clearly. Primarily: my cultural moment and my parenting.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about cultural decline and the need to return to biblical education. God as the one who speaks in Proverbs — the God of wisdom who is wisdom — does not appear.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Culturally convicted. The sermon succeeds as a diagnosis of a problem more than as a proclamation of a cure.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Cultural engagement is honest and sharp** — The Puritan-education decline argument, the video game hours vs. Bible hours comparison, and the generational gap analysis are all grounded in real observations and will land in this congregation's real world.
2. **The Live PD illustration** — The teenager who built a motorcycle from scrap and smoked pot in his sock is a precise, congregation-native portrait of the sermon's subject: a young person who thinks he knows better than every adult and therefore does not listen. This is Proverbs 13:1 in contemporary dress.
3. **The pastor's genuine pastoral burden** — The closing appeal to parents, grandparents, aunts, and uncles to be the change is warmly delivered and reflects real pastoral care for the next generation.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **Proverbs 13 is not preached.** Twenty-five verses are read; two are briefly mentioned. The text's own argument (the contrast between the wise and the fool played out across speech, work, money, hope, discipline, and death) is never opened.
2. **Christ is absent until the final note.** A wisdom sermon that does not ground wisdom in Christ (as Wisdom himself — 1 Cor 1:30, Col 2:3) will produce moralism, not transformation. The congregation is told to be like the Puritans without being told why the Puritans were who they were: because they were saturated with the Christ of the Scriptures.
3. **No gospel proclamation.** "Gospel" as a terminal note is a reminder to preach the gospel — not the preaching of it. The unbeliever in the room was not addressed until the very end, and then only by a notation.

---

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher appears genuinely passionate about biblical wisdom and the Puritan legacy — but the enthusiasm for the cultural argument has displaced the text. Proverbs 13 is a rich chapter that covers exactly the themes the preacher cares about (the wise child, the destructive fool, the fruit of righteousness, the discipline of a loving parent) but in far more specific and textually grounded form than the cultural diagnosis provides. The text would have said the same thing and said it with more authority.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The Puritan-education anecdote and the Paul Washer reference *feel* like theological seriousness because Washer is a Reformed voice and Puritan theology is substantive. But referencing Reformed preachers about the Puritans is not the same as opening Proverbs 13 and letting the Wisdom of God speak. The appearance of theological seriousness does not substitute for textual engagement.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Proverbs 13:24 — "Whoever spares the rod hates his son, but he who loves him is diligent to discipline him." The preacher mentions this verse explicitly ("I thought about talking about the verse that says spare the rod, spoil the child") and then passes by it. This verse, preached in full, would have grounded the entire parenting-and-generational-wisdom argument in the text itself — and it would have opened the door to the gospel: God's discipline of His children is itself a sign of love, and Christ bore the ultimate discipline so that His children could be sons, not orphans.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes
- **Rebuild the outline from Proverbs 13**: Use the chapter's own contrasts as the three-point structure. Suggested: (1) v. 1 — The wise son receives instruction; the fool rejects it (the generational gap argument *is* in the text); (2) v. 24 — Love disciplines; hate indulges (grounds the parenting burden in the text); (3) v. 20 — Walk with the wise and become wise; companion of fools suffers harm (grounds the cultural-influence argument in the text). Every cultural illustration can remain — but it now supports the text rather than replacing it.
- **Ground wisdom in Christ**: One paragraph connecting Proverbs 13 to Christ as the Wisdom of God (1 Cor 1:30; Col 2:3) will prevent the sermon from becoming a moralism dressed in Puritan clothes.
- **Write and deliver the gospel call**: Do not leave it as a note. A single paragraph: "The Puritans loved the Scriptures because they loved the Christ the Scriptures reveal. Every wise son and daughter in Proverbs 13 points forward to the one Son who heard the Father's instruction perfectly, who walked in wisdom without flaw, and who died so that foolish children like us could be brought into the family of God."
- **Shorten the pre-text material**: The Washer anecdote and science story occupy more than half the manuscript. Move the text earlier and let the illustrations serve it.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
Not triggered in the traditional sense. The sermon has pastoral warmth and cultural fire. The issue is *displaced authority* — the preacher's cultural diagnosis drives the sermon where the text should.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The sermon has the weight of pastoral urgency for the next generation — the preacher clearly loves his people and fears for their children. But the Weight of Glory that comes from God speaking through His Word is absent because the Word is not the governing authority of the sermon.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Proverbs 13 is wisdom literature — proverbial in form, not narrative or argumentative in the way Pauline epistles are. The rubric notes (Section G.17) that wisdom literature "may stress categories differently." Fair. However, wisdom literature still has a textual argument — the contrast between the wise path and the foolish path, with their respective ends — and that argument can be preached. The rubric's Berean Gate applies: does the sermon's main point arise from the passage? Here, it does not. The rubric bends only slightly for wisdom literature — it does not dissolve. The genre stress here is not severe; the real problem is the sermon's departure from the text, not the genre's resistance to exposition.
