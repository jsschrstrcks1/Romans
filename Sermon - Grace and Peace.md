## Background: The Book of Philippians

- Written approximately **61 AD**
- About ten years after Paul's second missionary journey to Macedonia [Note: the sermon says "first missionary journey," but it was Paul's *second* missionary journey (Acts 15:36–18:22) that reached Philippi and Macedonia — Acts 16. Paul's first journey did not reach Macedonia.]
- Philippi is a city in Macedonia — modern-day northeastern **Greece**, near the modern city of Kavala [Note: the sermon notes say "modern-day Italy," which is incorrect. Macedonia was in the northern Aegean region, not Italy.]
- The church at Philippi was founded primarily by Paul and Silas, with Timothy also present at the time (Acts 16:1–3, 12–40) [Note: the sermon credits Timothy as co-founder, but Silas is the more prominent named companion throughout Acts 16; Timothy had just joined the party before they reached Philippi]
- Philippi had been granted **permanent tax exemption** as a Roman colony (*colonia*) following the Battle of Philippi (42 BC) — the city had the *ius Italicum*, granting it privileges equivalent to Italian soil including freedom from provincial property taxes. Wealthy Romans therefore sought to live there.
- Philippi may have functioned something like a modern Rotary Club — wealthy businesspeople and philanthropists in a position to give generously to those doing the Lord's work. They gave to Paul and his ministry actively.

**Nero — Emperor in 61 AD (approximately seven years into his reign):**

- Nero became emperor in **54 AD**, so by 61 AD he had reigned approximately seven years ✓
- Prior to this period, Nero had been guided by a council that included his mother Agrippina and two key advisors — the philosopher Seneca and Praetorian Prefect Burrus [Note: the sermon says "two senators," but Seneca was a philosopher/tutor and Burrus was Praetorian Prefect; neither held senatorial authority in the primary sense]
- Nero orchestrated the removal of these advisors. His mother Agrippina was murdered in 59 AD on Nero's orders. Burrus died in 62 AD (possibly poisoned); Seneca was later forced to commit suicide in 65 AD.
- Agrippina had maneuvered politically — and at times murderously — to secure Nero's path to the throne
- Nero's lineage: he was the son of Agrippina (daughter of Germanicus) and adoptive son of Emperor Claudius. Through the Julian-Claudian dynasty, he was a distant descendant of Augustus and carried Julio-Claudian blood. [Note: the sermon calls Nero "great-great grandson to Julius Caesar" — this is a simplification; the connection is indirect and runs through Augustus, who was Caesar's adopted heir. The sermon also calls Nero "nephew of the reigning Monarch" — technically Claudius was Nero's great-uncle by blood (Claudius was Germanicus's brother; Germanicus was Nero's maternal grandfather) and also became his stepfather and adoptive father. "Nephew" alone is misleading.]

**Nero and the burning of Rome (64 AD):**

Most ancient historians — and many modern ones — suspected Nero of ordering or facilitating the Great Fire of Rome in 64 AD, then using Christians as scapegoats. [Note: the sermon says Nero was 24 at the time; he was born December 37 AD and the fire occurred in 64 AD, making him approximately 26–27 years old, not 24.]

Tacitus records what happened to Christians in the aftermath (*Annals* 15.44): [Note: the sermon reproduces this quote without attribution; source is Tacitus, *Annals* 15.44, written c. 117 AD. The translation used appears to be scholarly with bracket interpolations for the Latin terms.]

> "He put forward as guilty [*subdidit reos*], and afflicted with the most exquisite punishments, those who were hated for their abominations [*flagitia*] and called 'Christians' by the populace. Christus, from whom the name was derived, was punished by the procurator Pontius Pilate in the reign of Tiberius. The noxious form of religion [*exitiabilis superstitio*], checked for a time, broke out again not only in Judea, its original home, but also throughout the city [Rome], where all the abominations meet and find devotees. Therefore first of all those who confessed [i.e., to being Christians] were arrested, and then as a result of their information a large number were implicated, not so much on the charge of incendiarism as for hatred of the human race. They died by methods of mockery; some were covered with the skins of wild beasts and then torn by dogs, some were crucified, some were burned as torches to light at night. … Whence [after scenes of extreme cruelty] commiseration was stirred for them, although guilty of deserving the worse penalties, for men felt that their destruction was not on account of the public welfare but to gratify the cruelty of one [Nero]."
> — Tacitus, *Annals* 15.44 (c. 117 AD)

*[Pastoral aside on Nero's character — his wild years of excess, abuse of power, and eventual descent into paranoia and cruelty.]*

**Pax Romana context:** The letter was written approximately three years before the fire of Rome. Roman society believed it had achieved peace. The Christians of the era understood true peace differently.

**Paul was Nero's prisoner when he wrote this letter.**

## Textual Observations — Philippians 1:1–3

> "Paul and Timothy, servants of Christ Jesus, to all the saints in Christ Jesus who are at Philippi, with the overseers and deacons: Grace to you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ. I thank my God in all my remembrance of you."
> — Philippians 1:1–3, ESV

**Timothy (v. 1):**
- Paul mentions Timothy immediately — but the remainder of the letter is written in the first person singular. Grammatically, Timothy is named but Paul is the author. Their relationship was that of mentor and beloved son in the faith.

**"Grace and peace" (v. 2):**
- "Grace" (*charis*) echoes the standard Greek greeting (*chairein*) — meeting the Greek-speaking Philippians where they are
- "Peace" (*eirene* / *shalom*) — the Jewish greeting, carrying the full weight of Hebrew *shalom*, a greeting meaningful to the Jewish diaspora present in Philippi
- Pastoral observation: in a city proud of its *Pax Romana* — Roman peace — Paul redefines peace as something Rome cannot give

**"Servants" — doulos (v. 1):**
- Greek: *doulos* — slave, bond-servant; one who is owned completely by his master, with no rights of his own
- Some translations render this "bond-servant" [Note: the sermon interprets *doulos* as "one who waits tables, specifically a servant that brings food." This is a creative homiletical application, but lexically the table-server is *diakonos*, not *doulos*. The pastoral image is rich — Paul who feeds people spiritually calls himself a servant — but it should be noted that *doulos* primarily carries the meaning of slave/property, and the "brings food" nuance belongs to a different word. The application is still beautiful; the etymology needs care.]
- What is notable: Paul omits his apostolic title (*apostolos*) — which he uses in nearly every other letter. In Philippians he is simply "Paul... servant." This indicates a relationship of deep mutual affection — the formality of title is unnecessary between friends who love each other. Most probably he deeply loved these people, and they loved him back, in part demonstrated through their giving.

**"I thank my God in all my remembrance of you" (v. 3):**
- Sometimes you just click with people — some congregations and some relationships are marked by a deep, mutual joy

- Being a church that Jesus himself could say that about — one marked by:
  - **Fellowship**

  - **Exhortation / Discipleship**
  - **Evangelism**
