### 13. Missed Opportunity
The central action of Ezra 6 — the finding of Cyrus's decree in the Persian archives at Ecbatana (v.2) — is theologically stunning: God had sovereignly moved a pagan king to issue a decree decades earlier, preserved the record of it through regime change, and then brought it to light at precisely the moment it was needed to silence opposition and fund the temple's completion. This is one of the most concrete illustrations of providential sovereignty in all of Ezra-Nehemiah. The sermon never names it. The preacher's instincts about God's sovereignty over earthly powers are entirely correct — but the best evidence for that thesis is sitting in the text he listed at the bottom of the page.

The temple completion itself (6:14-15: "they finished their building by decree of the God of Israel and by decree of Cyrus, Darius, and Artaxerxes") is a single verse that encapsulates the sermon's entire thesis — God's sovereignty working through human kings — but it is never quoted or opened.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)
1. **Build the sermon from Ezra 6:2 outward**: The discovery of Cyrus's decree in the archives is the narrative's hinge. Start there. Let that moment define the thesis: God's purposes are not derailed by opposition — He has already arranged for their completion. Everything else in the sermon (sovereignty over nations, no one too far gone) flows from this narrative center.
2. **Deliver the exhortation at Romans 10:9-10, don't just cite it**: If Romans 10:9-10 is the gospel call, preach it. Read the verses aloud. 3. **Trim the Nebuchadnezzar section and redirect to Ezra**: The Daniel 4 material (Neb grazing in a loin cloth, the pedophilia detail, the salt law) occupies proportionally more sermon space than Ezra 4 and 6 combined. Cut to two minutes of background, then get into the text.
4. **Name the second point with more precision**: "If it keeps you from obedience to the Lord, it's not a blessing" is a strong pastoral point with good illustrative support. But it is underdeveloped theologically and is not connected to anything in Ezra's narrative. Either connect it to the opposition in Ezra 4 (the adversaries' "help" as a blessing that would have corrupted the work), or cut it and develop the material that is textually grounded.
5. **Address the slight tension in the phrase "Their choice to follow God"**: In the context of the surrounding affirmations of sovereign regeneration, clarify that the choice is real and required, but that it is itself God's gift — the renewed will turning freely toward what God has sovereignly ordained. A single sentence would prevent confusion without disrupting the pastoral flow.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This rubric is well-suited to Ezra's narrative and historical genre with one note: narrative texts reward a slightly different expositor's movement — the preacher should trace the narrative's own theological argument (providence, opposition, perseverance, completion) rather than importing a Pauline thesis and using the narrative as illustration. The rubric's categories all apply, but the exposition category must account for how narrative preaching works: the FCF and the main point arise from the story's conflict and resolution, not from a didactic argument. This sermon's structural failure is not genre-related — it would apply to expository preaching of any genre. The rubric fits.
