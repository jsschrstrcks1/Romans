Psalm 78:1–8 — Don't Forget What the Lord Has Done


If you've brought your Bibles, and I hope you have, open with me to Psalm 78. While you're turning there, let's talk about our people group of the week.

Now I want to tell you about a documentary I watched this week.

I saw one recently about JFK Jr., specifically about the night he died. He was a pilot — he, his wife, and his sister-in-law were heading to Martha's Vineyard. He had about 310 total flight hours, with relatively few of those in his six-seat Piper Saratoga. That sounds like a lot, but if you compare it to driving a car, it's roughly the equivalent of a sixteen-year-old who just traded their restricted permit for a full license. Kennedy was new enough to the plane that he wasn't certified for instrument flying. That meant he could only fly safely as long as the conditions were good — which they were when he took off.

Most of the flight was uneventful. The takeoff was fine, and the skies were clear. Flying toward Martha's Vineyard you're over land for most of the route, which matters. In small planes when you're flying by sight over water, it is easy to confuse sky for sea and panic — you have to be able to fly by instrument to travel over open water safely.

Flying by sight over water at night is even more dangerous. When Kennedy approached Rhode Island's Buzzards Bay, he had two choices. The safe choice was to follow the coastline — he was flying at night, by sight, without instrument certification. The second choice was faster, a straight line, but it multiplied the risk enormously. He chose the straight line, flying directly over the bay and losing sight of land. Accounts suggest there had been tension between Kennedy and his wife that evening, and he may have been emotionally compromised. A few minutes after he lost sight of shore, the fog came in.

If he had completed his instrument training, the story ends differently. His friend and flight instructor had advised against the flight and then offered to co-pilot for additional safety — Kennedy declined that too.

For 95 percent of the flight, his path was exactly as expected. But right at the end he began turning erratically. Stephen Davey notes that he came dangerously close to other air traffic. The tower tried to raise him, but he had his radios set to the wrong channel. Investigators concluded he had lost his sense of which way was up. In his compromised state he panicked. He could have activated the autopilot, which would have leveled the aircraft — but he didn't. Instead he made a very sharp right turn and began losing altitude. By 700 feet above the water it looked as though he had corrected. Then he pushed the nose down and went in nearly straight.

If JFK Jr. had trusted his instruments when his eyes couldn't be trusted, he never would have crashed. If he had taken his friend and instructor with him, they never would have crashed. If he had followed the coastline and stayed with his guideposts, he would still be here.

Let's read our passage. Psalm 78:1–8.


Do you see the superscription right before verse 1? "A Maskil of Asaph."

We don't talk about superscriptions often, but the authors gave them to us. They help us place the Hebrew hymn with the events surrounding it, or with some other important piece of information about the song. In this case: a Maskil of Asaph.

Maskil carries more than one meaning. A theological journal I read described it as referring to a complex musical arrangement in which multiple groups respond to each other, passing the stories of God's provision back and forth. But it also carries the sense of instruction that must be repeated until it is remembered and fully believed. It's Asaph's way of saying: stop, pay attention, this is important.

And the very next thing he says is: don't forget what the Lord has done for you. Remind each other. Tell your children and your grandchildren. Pass on the good things God has done.

I was thinking about 9/11 this week. Everyone who was alive that day remembers exactly where they were when that first plane hit. I was in a Greek class, about to fail a test. A group of us had arrived early to study together, trying to nail down things you have no choice but to memorize cold.

A friend burst in and announced that a small Piper Cub had hit the World Trade Center. We hurried him out so we could get back to work. Class started. Our professor, as he always did, went around the room for prayer requests, and we had just finished the opening prayer when the same friend came back. A second plane had hit the second tower. The nation was under attack.

We were confused — a second plane hit the second tower? So three planes had struck two buildings? Another small private plane? No. A hijacked passenger jet.

The professor told us to go find a television. We gathered in the commons around the corner. I found a cart with a TV, plugged it in, someone tuned to a local station. A terror attack, they said. Another plane hijacked — where is it going? The White House? A nuclear plant?

The Pentagon was hit. People were jumping from the top of the World Trade Center — an estimated 200. Can you imagine the desperation it takes to see jumping from 100 stories as preferable to staying inside? Fire on one side, a ten-second fall to certain death on the other.

The South Tower fell. The North Tower fell.

The president of the school sent us home. We prayed and left. It was a day to be with your family.

But do you remember what God did in this country in the aftermath?

I don't think churches have been fuller in my lifetime. People were seeking God everywhere — spending time in prayer, buying Bibles, filling sanctuaries. Thousands were converted. Thousands who were heading for hell were reached by the grace of God. People talk about wanting to see miracles. A dead man made alive in Christ is the best miracle there is.

Verses 4–6.

It is so important that we share these stories with our children. Not just the things we ourselves have been through, but the things we see God do in Scripture. Fathers — this falls on you first. You need to be telling your children and grandchildren about the Lord. Teaching them the Bible. Modeling a life shaped by Scripture. Teaching them to love God, to spend time in his Word, to do better than we have done.

How many of you have kids or grandkids?

Don't raise your hands. How many of you spend time intentionally teaching them about the Lord? Scripture tells us to.

Let me ask you: if your father told you to do something and you didn't do it — if you told your child to do something and they didn't do it, over and over — what happened?


The second story I want to share is Hurricane Irma — just a few years ago this week, a massive storm hit the state hard. The storm surge was enough that we needed to be further inland, in something stronger. Our dear friend Kenny Duffield opened his brick home to us for a few days, and we were safe.

I remember the night the storm came through. It was ugly. The eye wall made an unearthly, mournful howl. I sat there thinking: my house is gone. I'm probably homeless. I grieved for a moment. Then it occurred to me — God gave me that house. I gave it back to him. If he chose to take it, it was his to take. He would provide something else. I had peace.

I still fully expected to find a debris field when I drove home the next day. There was my house, standing. The storm that had leveled entire islands — made them uninhabitable — had registered on my weather station as 30-mile-per-hour gusts. A tree limb that by all rights should have fallen on my roof instead somehow fell into the wind and landed behind the house.

God is so good to us.

We could talk about God healing my mother of arthritis. We could talk about a lot of things — and I think there are a lot of stories in this room. Healing from addiction. Healing from all sorts of things. But none of those miracles is as staggering as God taking a dead man, a sinner, and making him alive in Christ.

You and I are responsible — because of our sin — for Christ's death. It was your sin, and my sin, and the sin of everyone like us across time that made it necessary for Christ to die. We hold some of the blame. We were rebels who killed the Son of God. And God's response was to bring us in, to make us part of the family, to give us eternal life with him in glory.

God is so much better to us than we deserve.

God's Word is the instrument that keeps us on course. If JFK Jr. had trusted his instruments when his eyes failed him, he never would have crashed. Scripture is that instrument — it is true when our senses and emotions are lying to us.

God gave us the church to help keep us on course. If Kennedy had taken his friend and instructor that night, he never would have gone off course. "As iron sharpens iron, so one person sharpens another." Staying in the body of believers is not optional — it keeps us from going into a spiral we can't recover from.

And if Kennedy had followed the coastline — kept his landmarks in sight — he would still be here. We have landmarks: the stories of God's provision in our lives, in our parents' lives, in some of our grandparents' lives. We have the writings of men who walked before us — R. C. Sproul, Charles Spurgeon, scores of godly men who are still present in their work. We have centuries of that kind of witness to learn from. Spurgeon said: visit many good books, but live in the Bible. Not only for your own sake, but for the sake of your children.

We have the Word. We have the church. We have the stories.

Don't forget what the Lord has done.

Maybe you're here today and you don't yet have a relationship with God.
