I read a story this week. Actually if you follow me on facebook, you may have seen it. But its worth sharing again. The story is about this lumberjack chopping trees down in the forest as usual, until he comes across this one particular tree that has an especially beautiful bird just starting to build a nest in it. The lumber jack grabs a heavy hammer and starts striking the tree. Needless to say it doesn’t take much disruption for the bird to fly to a different tree. The lumber jack watched the bird, and chased it from a second tree. Five or six trees later, the bird makes its nest in the safety of the cleft of a rock on a hillside, where there are no trees marked for cutting. 

I imagine the bird thought the lumberjack was mean, or perhaps cruel. Chasing it away from every good spot it found for a long time. But the lumberjack was motivated by compassion. He knew what trees were going to be cut down, and he knew where the bird would be safe. He wanted the bird to build its next in a place where it would be safe from his axe.

Read 2 tim 4:1-5

We should know a little background here. I recently wrote a paper on this and came up with two full pages of background info. Were not going to dive that deep. But suffice it to say a lot was going on. Paul is in prison for the second time. If you skip ahead a few verses you’ll see he thinks his death is imminent. So this is his last chance to write to timothy, and indeed, it’s the last book of the bible chronologically that he wrote. He’s about to be on trial in front of Nero, who was crazy, and murderous, and even had his own mother killed. In the midst of this nearly all of his friends have abandoned him. No one spoke up for him at the hearing. Paul writes that they cared more for the world than for Paul. Paul is isolated, and alone, and facing his own mortality. 

Timothy is like a son to him, and Paul calls him a son several times. They traveled on several missionary journeys together. Timothy runs his letters from here to there and back again. Timothy is loyal above all else, and Paul spends  a lot of time talking about Timothy’s loyalty. Paul wants to squeeze everything he can into this letter to Timothy, because he probably wont get another chance to write. So its with this poignant backdrop that Paul writes. Verse 1 says I charge you in the presence of God. This is no mere suggestion. If you were to look up the Greek word you would find its more like a command and demand than request. The English translation I charge you just falls short of everything the Greek is trying to communicate. In fact the root of the word for “charge” here is the same word we get our word Martyr from. Pauls tone is deadly serious. He chose to write in some of the most serious words he could. To add the most weight to it that he could. 

I have two little nieces that are social butterflies. They love people, and are outgoing, sweet, and cute. When they were little they would just run up to strangers, and treat them like family. Which is sweet of them. But terrifying at the same time. So we started telling them stranger danger stories. Some came from a book, some were psychology and counseling case studies. All were true as far as I know. But to add weight to the idea that some strangers are not OK, we spared no gory detail about kids being kidnapped, kept in bad circumstances, and sometimes killed. Nothing carnal. Just the scary gory details. It worked. After a half dozen stories, they learned to take their cue about strangers from a trusted adult. 

Just like we needed to add as much weight as we could to the stories so they would make an impact on the girls, Paul is trying hard to make this really hit home with Timothy, so he even brings God and Jesus into it. He commands, he charges them in the presence of God and Jesus. In verse 2 he drives home the urgency. Jesus will be judging the quick and the dead. Judgement is something the world doesn’t like to hear about these days isn’t it. I think mat 7 is probably the chapter of the bible that is most taken out of context. Two words: Judge not, are plucked out of the rest of the chapter. Jesus talks about Dogs, and bad builders, and pigs, and so forth. How can you tell a good builder from a bad builder without making a judgement? Its not about not judging, its about judging rightly, righteously, fairly, with love, and most importantly in accordance with scripture, and in so doing, know that we are going to be judged in return. How we handle that will reflect directly on Christians as a whole, and most importantly on God. 

Right now the situation outside our homes calls for a lot of judgement. We have to decide if its worth the risk to go to the store, and how much toilet paper to buy, we have to make judgement calls on which stores are going to be the safest, and when. We have to make judgement calls even about the holiday today – do we go see our loved ones on easter, or is it perhaps more loving to stay home? its hard to say. Judgements must be made about who to listen to, and who to trust. Do we trust WHO? The white house covid team? The media? Hah. Politicians? Ultimately their voices are not as important as whose voice? Be safe, be smart. Stay home when you can. But if God gives you an open door for the gospel, you need to walk through it. 

That’s what Paul means in verse three where he orders us to preach. This command starts at a point in the past, and doesn’t stop until its fully completed. Maybe you’re thinking pastor, I don’t preach, I don’t get up there every week like you do and preach. But in truth we all preach. Every day. Every action, and every word that comes out of our mouth is a sermon. If your life was the only sermon someone ever heard, would that be enough for them to know the gospel?  We must lead lives that bring glory to God, not shame and dishonor. Do you think Jesus would be proud of every action you took this past week? DO you think he would be proud of every word that came out of your mouth? For most of us the answer is embarrassing. Most of us have said, or done something that would bring shame to our savior. Were all still human afterall, thank the Lord we are forgiven. But still we should be striving for better next week than last right? Better tomorrow than today, and when we fall down, we get back up and keep pressing towards the goal. 

Vs 3 also talks about being ready in season and out. Most of the time when I’ve heard these verses preached, they said you gotta be ready to preach when you’re ready, and when you’re not. But once again the Greek is deeper than that. In season could also be translated when its comfortable. That one is pretty straight forward. If someone walks up to you and says please, tell me about Jesus! Most of us would be able to stand there and share at least the outline of the gospel. You’d be able to tell them that Jesus was born, lived a perfect life and was killed for our sins. He took the punishment we deserved on good Friday, and rose again on easter Sunday. He is risen! He will save us, if we ask. I think most of you could get that much out pretty easily at least. That would be fairly comfortable for most. Though I know a few of you are shy, and would feel uncomfortable even with that much. 

Yet Paul takes it a step further. We have to be ready in season and out. Out of season in Greek could also be translated as whether it be welcome or not, or lacks opportunity. Paul commands us to preach, with our mouth, and with ourlifestyle whether its comfortable or not, whether its welcome or not, and if there is not an opportunity to preach, you make one. God’s command doesn’t give a lot of room for equivocation. You either obey or not. You either preach, or you disobey. Why is this so important? Because peoples eternity is on the line. Jesus will judge the quick and the dead. This is talking about the end times, about revelation. Its talking about the end of things when Jesus stands there and judges people for their deeds. One day when you’re standing before Jesus, will the decisions you’ve made make Jesus proud? In the words of the old hymn, we owed a debt we could not pay, he paid a debt he did not owe. He paid in his blood. He allowed his life to be poured out for you. Were you a wise investment? 

Paul gives us a series of commands at the end of that verse. He says “reprove, rebuke, and exhort”. Who can tell me what reprove means? Oh. Right, most of you guys are out there on facebook. I feel like most people have a feeling what the word reprove means, but would have a hard time nailing down the definition. The Greek word here means to shine a light on, or to expose darkness. Rebuke likewise means to say something forcefully enough that it prevents someone from making a bad choice, or stops them from continuing to make that same choice. Basically Paul’s calling for judgement. Paul s asking us to take scripture (the light) and shine the light of scripture into the darkest recesses. Sort of like hand sanitizer. I saw a video a while back of just what you have to do to make sure your hands are really clean. They put two white gloves on, and covered one side of each of their gloves with black ink, and rubbed their hands around like one might when washing. It was amazing the extent to which people have to go to actually really clean their whole hands. 

Likewise there are corners and pieces of our soul we try to hide, and some we are blind to. In order to grow we have to be willing to take rebuke from our brothers and sisters sometimes. We have to accept their biblical reproof. Now this doesn’t mean you should call everyone up right away and be like hey, you’re feet stink, and you’re a bad person. No, you need to speak the truth, but speak the truth in love. We are called to compare things to scripture, but its really scripture that does most of the judging. Exhorting involves calling someone to come up along side you. You have to be in a good place in order to do that. You have to actively work at it to be in a good place. So the biblical model is this. Lets say we have a friend, and he’s strayed into having sex out side of marriage. Or maybe course and vulgar talk with the boys? Or a hundred things. He’s done something that scripture condemns. In doing so around the lost, he’s giving Christ a black eye. He’s making Christ look bad. That’s not his goal. But when he’s violating God’s commands about sex, he’s not thinking about Christ is he? So what are we to do when our brother or sister stumbles in this way? We are to point them to the bible – and let the light of scripture have its sanitizing effects. Then we are to rebuke them. We are to speak forcefully and convincingly to them with the goal of helping them change their behavior, and finally we are to exhort them. Which starts with being in a healthy place on that issue to start with. If you are sleeping around, you won’t be able to speak with authority into the life of someone whose struggling in that way. So always be growing closer to Jesus, so you are able to be obedient to God’s command given through Paul here. Balance all of this with both Love, and sober-minded-ness vs 5.
