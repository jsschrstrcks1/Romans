Psalm 63:1–8 — Thirsting for God in the Wilderness


We're going to be in the Psalms tonight. It's one of my favorite psalms, and I think it gives us a glimpse into why God loved David so much.

He was being hunted. Maybe by Saul, but probably by his very own son Absalom.

David had been a great king, but kind of a poor father. He warred and ruled, and his people loved him — but his children were a mess. His family life had been messy right from the start. To win his first wife, you'll recall, he had a loyal soldier murdered to cover the fact that he had slept with this man's wife and gotten her pregnant. Their first child died. By the time we get here he has had several wives and a growing number of children. And then the unthinkable happens. Put yourself in David's place for a moment. Picture your children doing this to each other.

It started with a rape. One of his sons raped one of his daughters. Half siblings — same father, different mothers. The son went through schemes and machinations to get his half-sister alone in his rooms. It worked. He raped her, then blamed her for it. She told her full brother Absalom what had happened, and he grew angry. I can't imagine getting that news. Absalom set in motion a plan of his own: he hunted down and killed his half-brother, then fled to live with his grandparents.

What a pile of wreckage. David is grieving that his son raped his daughter. Then another son murdered the rapist. Tragedy piled on tragedy.

A few years back, a Christian musician named Steven Curtis Chapman was home on a Saturday. He was inside with some of his kids. Two others were playing outside. His son Will — eighteen years old — was on his way out to visit friends. Stephen said he'll never forget the sound Will made: a sickening, otherworldly howl, almost animal. He dropped what he was doing and ran outside.

What he found forever shaped the course of his life.

Will had been backing out the driveway when Maria, their youngest, ran behind the SUV. He backed over her. When Will saw his father's face, he laid Maria down, got back in the car, and drove away sobbing. One child was gone — they wouldn't know that until they flew her to the hospital — and another was driving away alone with what he had done. It's recorded that Stephen ran after the car screaming "Will — I love you" while his wife attended to Maria and called 911.

There are some roads I pray the Lord never asks us to walk down.

At Maria's funeral, her sister Emily spoke and shared the story of her engagement. Her fiancé had proposed, and she came home to break the news. The family was thrilled. Maria, in the way of little kids, kept asking "What did you say? Emily, what did you say? What did you say?!" Finally Emily told her she'd said yes. Then Emily turned to the room and asked: "When Jesus proposed to pay the price for your sins, what did you say to Jesus?"

After Absalom had been with his grandparents for a few years he came back home with a plan to overthrow the kingdom. He spent four years quietly subverting David's authority, building his base, so that when the moment came he'd have the force to back it up. The day came. Absalom declared himself king, seized the weapons, called up his bannermen, and took Jerusalem. David went into hiding.

That is where we meet him tonight. In Psalm 63. One thing I want you to listen for as we read: David complaining. Every time he complains, make a note. Ready? Verses 1–8.

I love that even at one of the lowest points in his life — being hunted by the son who murdered the son who raped his daughter — he is focused on worship, not on questioning God. There is room for questions. But David jumps straight to worshipping.


A few things to point out.

The first verse jumped out at me. "In a dry and thirsty land where there is no water, my soul thirsts for you, my flesh" — and you can read "flesh" here as the sum total of his being — "cries out for you." Have you ever wanted something so badly you could feel it physically? Double that. I think that is where David was.

It reminds me of The Insanity of God. Some of you have seen it, some haven't yet — I've got another copy on the way. There's a scene not shown in the film where a box of Bibles is opened and people are weeping with joy. Then they start tearing the Bibles apart to share them. This one gets a section of Genesis; that one gets three chapters from Revelation. We may have felt like we were in Genesis forever — but could you imagine if that handful of chapters was all you had? You would know those chapters cold. You would be an expert in them. The point is: they wept because they were so happy to have any part of Scripture at all. Like people dying of thirst in a desert.

We Americans are so blessed. We can own full copies of Scripture. We can meet in peace. Almost every city has a Christian bookstore. And we frequently don't even realize it. It's like people who live in cities and after a while stop looking up at the buildings. Or people in the mountains who stop noticing the peaks. Or people on the ocean who never go out back to watch it. Prosperity breeds contempt. The things of the Lord are all around us — in print, on the radio, sometimes on TV — and we tune them out for something entertaining that brings no glory to God.

Hebrews 12:2 says what our approach ought to be: "looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith, who for the joy that was set before him endured the cross, despising the shame, and is seated at the right hand of the throne of God."

Are we keeping our eyes on Jesus? Are we fixing them on him in triumph and in struggle?


Now look at what happened to David as a result of that focus. Verses 5–7.

There's a pattern here. Verse 5: his soul will be satisfied. What had he been doing to find that satisfaction? He had been yearning after God — engaging the spiritual disciplines. Scripture, prayer, worship, and today we would add evangelism. And what does he do once he finds that satisfaction? He doubles down. If a little is good, more is better. Verse 6: he spends the night watches in meditation on God. At that point in history there were three watches in the night. When he's sleepless he focuses on God — prays, meditates on Scripture, doesn't circle his own problem but fixes his eyes on God, who he knows will provide the solution.

And then he praises God for covering him in "the shadow of your wings."

Scholars have talked about that phrase at length. Some argue that in our weakest moments God protects us the way a mother bird covers her chicks. When we picture birds we picture doves, robins, hummingbirds — small, gentle. But some birds are fierce. We have guinea hens. I saw a video of guinea hens in Africa — three mothers circling a black mamba. Whichever one the snake wasn't watching would attack it. One bite at a time, working together, they killed that snake and saved their babies. These same birds will sacrifice themselves for their young against dogs, cats, even the big cats of Africa. That ferocious protectiveness is what comes to mind when people argue for the mother-bird image.

Others argue for a different kind of wing entirely. Ezekiel describes one such type — I'd recommend you read it later, because it's a lot of verses and we're close to done for the night. You'll find it in Ezekiel 1 and again in Ezekiel 10. Each of these beings had four faces: a lion, an eagle, a man, and an ox. They had the stature of a man with the legs of a cow. Four wings each, and under the wings arms like a man's. Every wing was completely covered with eyes. Surrounding them was cloud, lightning, and blazing light. Other passages say buildings shook when they spoke. These are the beings who guarded the garden of Eden after Adam and Eve were cast out. The only other place we see them in Scripture is around the throne of God.

If this is what David meant by "the shadow of your wings" — and it makes sense to me that it is — then David was being covered by God's personal attendants. Not too different from the president assigning you Secret Service protection.

These beings could see in every direction at once, with perfect clarity. Cameras are starting to model this to a lesser extent. Cameras and phones used to have one sensor. They took decent pictures, but pictures always fell flat compared to the actual memory — we see in three dimensions, pictures only show two. So engineers started adding sensors. New iPhones have two, which lets you capture more information. A camera I read about had hundreds of sensors. Each one focused on a different element in the scene, and later on your computer you could change the focus, zoom in or out, or have everything in focus all at once.

These beings would have that — except perfect, and in every direction simultaneously, with no blind spots.

In motorcycle school they taught us that the average person truly sees only about three degrees directly in front of them. Everything outside that, you detect motion and that's about it. With thousands of eyes in every direction, there is nothing out of focus, nothing missed, nothing approaching from behind.

And this being has characteristics of four creatures at once. The sentience of a man — or more. The ferocity of a lion. The strength of an ox. The cunning and aerial ability of an eagle. I would be thrilled with a protective lion. Combine all four and you have unquestionably the most capable guardian in existence. David seems to be saying that at his lowest, God sends the best he has — to cover him with their wings. And with eyes on the inside of their wings as well, they can watch the one they're protecting just as well as anything approaching.

When we are at our weakest, God sends his best. Either protecting us personally like a mother bird — fierce and self-sacrificing — or sending his personal attendants to shelter us. Those wings shelter, hide, and protect all at once. It is rest that only God can provide.

One caveat. We only see them protecting followers of God. David related to God through the covenant available to him — sacrifice, the priesthood, the blood of bulls and goats. His sin was covered, not yet completely removed. It wasn't until Christ came that sin could be fully wiped away. Christ died for us. Because of the things we've done, we deserve to be separated from God for eternity — we deserve death. But Jesus took that punishment. He said, "I am the way, I am the truth, I am the life. No one comes to the Father but by me."

As the praise team comes up — what say you to Jesus? He died for you. Will you follow him?
