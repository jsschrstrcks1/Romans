John 12:1-8 — Mary and Judas: What Does Your Life Resemble?


We're going to be in John chapter 12 today.

While you're turning there, I want to share a story I brought on video tonight. It's one that the director of the International Mission Board shared recently at a conference I was watching. David Platt is a graduate of the same school I'm attending, and was pastor of a small church that started out meeting on Saturdays. The Church at Brook Hills went from a group of 10–20 people to a church of thousands across several locations in Birmingham, Alabama. He was about 40 years old, and had been pastor there for roughly 15 years before being called to lead the IMB. Anyway, the point of his story is that sometimes love makes you do crazy things.

That's a good introduction to our scripture tonight — a woman who loved Christ so much she did something the people around her felt was crazy.

Before we read, let me ask you: Is your heart full of adoration for the Lord? Full of affection and longing? Does your heart cry out with love for him? If it doesn't resound with a loud yes, then at the end of the service, during the final song, just slip your hand up and I'll spend a few minutes talking with you one on one about all that our Savior has done for us.

In the meantime, let's read. John 12.


Mary gave everything she had for Jesus.

Financially — this perfume was worth a year's wages. A laborer's full annual income, poured out in a single act.

Socially — if the disciples didn't understand it, imagine how the world would have responded. Think of Noah. His neighbors thought he was out of his mind. But he poured everything he had into what God called him to do. Mary did the same.

Emotionally — she gave herself entirely. She was contemplative by nature. When Lazarus died, Martha ran to Jesus with demands. Mary stayed home with her thoughts and tears. But here she breaks open everything she has and gives it.

Mary had no doubts and no confusion about who Jesus was. She knew she owed more to Christ than she could ever repay. And the scent of her gift filled the whole house.


Statistically, we are more like Judas than Mary.

Sixty percent of Americans say they have doubts about the God of the Bible. Seventy-three percent claim to be Christians — but of that seventy-three percent, forty-one percent say they have no need to read their Bible, attend church, or spend time in prayer unless they need something. No need for worship, no need for fellowship with other believers. Only thirty-one percent are genuinely involved in church life and consider the Bible, prayer, worship, and fellowship necessary.

Ninety percent of Americans say Jesus was a real historical person. Of those, sixty-three percent say they have made a commitment to Jesus Christ that still matters to them. Of those, forty-three percent say Jesus was both God and man. Of that forty-three percent, only thirty-one percent believe God had a purpose in sending Jesus to the world. Nearly twenty percent say he was simply the best possible man — a great teacher, but not God himself.

And fifty-four percent of that shrinking thirty-one percent say the Christian has no business telling people about Jesus, because — fifty-five percent of them believe — good works will send you to heaven.

When it came right down to it, the survey found that forty-eight percent of people in this country do not believe in God or identify as agnostic, have not prayed in the last year, have never made a profession of faith in Jesus, believe the Bible is not accurate, have never read it, have not attended a church in the last year, and believe Jesus was just as sinful as any other fallen man.

Half. Half of our country. In our area, statistically less than two percent attend a Bible-believing church. We are living in a spiritual vacuum — a time and place where most people in our community are lost and heading for hell. How can we stand by and watch?

Judas lived in a time and place where most people around him were lost too.

Judas may have had doubts of his own. He seems to have been there for the earthly benefits of being near Christ — he was stealing from the money box. He probably enjoyed the social elevation. Feed five thousand people and five thousand people likely know your name. He may have had misconceptions about what Jesus came to do. Most of the disciples thought Jesus was going to be an earthly king until Jesus sorted them out. Judas may have believed that having Christ arrested would trigger the revolution and position him as one of the king's right-hand men.


Have any of you heard of the Wesley brothers? They more or less started the Methodist church. They wrote hundreds of hymns — some still in our hymnals today.

Charles Wesley wrote this of Mary:

"Selfishness seeks a gift,
Love loves to give;
Love gives itself away,
Love loves to live.
Love's grand munificence
Counts not the cost;
Feeling, tho' nought is left,
Nothing is lost."

His brother John Wesley did everything he could for the Lord. He founded numerous orphanages, regularly visited large numbers of prison inmates, was a fixture at the local hospital meeting with the infirm and dying. He traveled and preached as much as any itinerant preacher of his day. One article I read compared him to George Whitefield — and when you add up all the miles Whitefield traveled on horseback preaching the gospel, the distance was staggering. John Wesley matched that pace.

But one of the most striking things about John Wesley is that a number of years into his ministry, he realized from reading the Bible that he was lost and bound for hell. At that moment, in tears, he knelt down and accepted Christ as his Lord and Savior.

He had been living more like Judas than like Mary.

Whose life does yours most resemble?

It struck me this week, reading people's reactions to the death of Hugh Hefner, that we have lost sight of what a hero looks like. People were calling this man of sin a hero. He exploited women, kept them essentially as property, humiliated them, and coerced them into things they did not choose freely. One woman I know said he bought her brother's home and threatened to throw him and his children out if she stepped out of line. She didn't go into detail, but said it was the darkest chapter of her life to this point.

Beyond what he did to individuals, Hugh took a form of depravity that had been resigned to dark alleys and brought it into the mainstream. Before him, people who indulged in that material had to go somewhere that declared by their presence that they too were part of it. He worked to remove the stigma and make it accessible to anyone. He has damaged countless marriages, caused countless young women the anguish of believing they could never measure up to his manufactured standard of beauty — even girls in the church. One young woman who used to attend here said this week that it had been a life goal of hers to be one of his bunnies.

One article I read noted that Hugh was proud of having started his empire with $600 and a camera. Yet when he died, by his own account, he was as hollow and miserable as anyone infatuated with the world. Now — short of the grace of God and a deathbed confession, and I pray he did accept Christ — but apart from that, Hugh is spending an eternity in hell. As will anyone who dies apart from Christ.


Looking at your life, who does it most resemble?

Deep down, do you come to church for the social benefits? Are you here for the blessings you can gain from being around God's people? Or are you here for God?

Judas ultimately committed suicide out of shame and regret. We don't know whether he had a change of heart before he died. It's possible. I wish none would spend an eternity in hell. But apart from Christ, that is where Judas is.

Let me ask you again.

Is your heart full of adoration for the Lord? Full of affection and longing? Does your heart cry out with love for him? If it doesn't resound with a loud yes, then maybe you need to spend time talking to the Lord tonight. Because one day each of us will take that last breath.

I normally have the praise team come up, but tonight I have a song I want to play — "Give Me Jesus" by Steve Camp. While the song plays: if you're a Christian and you have something against a brother, don't take communion — go make it right first. The same is true with God. If there is unconfessed sin in your life, now is the time to spend this song in prayer and make things right.

If you aren't a Christian, or you aren't sure, while everyone else has their eyes closed, slip your hand up so I can see you, and after the service we will talk about what Jesus has done for you. If you're ready to join the church, or if you've accepted Christ but never taken the next step of believer's baptism, talk to me after the service.
