Psalm 23 — We Need a Shepherd


It's easy to tell from the world around us that something isn't quite right. You notice that?

I'm reminded of a phrase repeated over and over throughout Judges: the people of Israel kept doing what was right in their own eyes. What do you remember of Judges? We've spent some time there over the last couple of years. The Israelites would spot something shiny and run away from God. Cycle after cycle of sin and rebellion, followed by God allowing them to be consumed by the consequences, followed by rescue — and then repeat. Even Gideon got mixed up in idolatry himself.

Are we any better? It's easy to look at the Israelites and point. It's a little harder to do the soul-searching and admit where we've missed it too.

It strikes me that during the American period of slavery, the church was the only institution that ever tried to construct a theology justifying it. That is something our denomination has had to repent of more than once, and rightly so. The point is: every generation has its own pet sin it's blind to.

Young people today have no shortage of obvious ones — from the hours consumed by video games at the expense of time with the Lord, to what I'll call plurosexuality. That's a word I made up to describe what the LGBTQ+ movement has become: the full embrace of homosexuality, bisexuality, trans-sexuality, open relationships. What one generation hid in shame, the next generation celebrates publicly. It's heartbreaking. These are the things people in my generation are engaging in openly. These are the things your kids and grandkids are navigating.

In previous generations, even among mature believers, there's a different cluster of sins held more strongly than Scripture sometimes warrants. I've been to nearly thirty countries. Of all the nations I've visited in the West and elsewhere, in the first world and the third, we live in the best country — not a perfect one, but a remarkable one. But sometimes previous generations mistake the president for Christ. They see politics as the path back to God rather than evangelism. I vote for the person who best represents me morally first, fiscally second. I want to see a nation that reflects Christ in its laws. But I also know that any real and lasting change will have to come from the bottom up. When enough people in this nation have accepted Christ, our laws will begin to reflect that.

I saw a meme recently that stopped me cold. In it, a girl in pink is walking toward the camera, oblivious. A guy is staring at her inappropriately, while the girl next to him — clearly his girlfriend — looks disgusted and angry. The girl in pink was labeled "nationalism." The guy was labeled "American evangelicalism." And the girlfriend was labeled "the way of Jesus."

In other words: has the church become so enamored with nationalism and politics that we've become an offense to the way of Jesus? Just something to search our hearts over. We can't stop caring about our country, the same way we can't stop eating — both are necessary. But there's a difference between nourishment and obsession.

Which brings me to Joy. She's a purebred Catahoula leopard hound — kind of like Grace, for those of you that knew her, but not a cross. Unbeknownst to me when we brought her home, she had an abscess on top of her head. Nearly flat the day we got her, but by Thursday it looked like a golf ball. Tender to the touch. Every time we tried to lance it with an X-Acto knife, she'd cry and wiggle and cry and wiggle. We finally gave up Friday and applied hot compresses until it started to drain.

But draining alone wasn't enough. We had to poke at it, squeeze it, give her antibiotic injections — all necessary, all things she hated. Especially the shot. Ultimately we took her to the vet and had it cut out entirely. She had a tooth lodged in her skull. Being a good shepherd to that dog meant treating something painful. The loving thing was cutting it open.

OK. We need a shepherd, because we are about as smart as sheep — and I don't mean that as a compliment. We are quick to forsake Christ, and so God through David gave us a message. And I want you to notice something about it right away.

It's personal.

My shepherd. I shall not want. He makes me rest. He leads me. He restores me. I walk. I will not fear. He comforts me. He prepares a table for me. He anoints my head. Goodness and mercy follow me, all the days of my life, and I will dwell with the Lord. Fourteen times in six verses the author refers to himself. This was something deeply personal for David. Many commentators believe he wrote it during one of the hardest seasons of his life — probably while Saul was hunting him.

You might remember that you sent me to Europe to share the gospel with Muslims back in 2009. I spent time mostly in the UK, working with Muslim communities that had emigrated a generation or two prior — mostly from Pakistan and Afghanistan. In order to meet with my team I had to carry names, addresses, and phone numbers. Enough information to get a missionary hurt or killed if my laptop fell into the wrong hands. I had to use heavy-duty encryption software that locked every bit of data behind a 128-character password. They warned me: if you ever end up in a country run by an Islamic state rather than a Western government, even that isn't enough. What the state wants, the state gets.

There was a TV show a couple years back that offered $250,000 to anyone who could elude a team of elite trackers for 30 days. Some were cops. Some were from Interpol, the FBI, CIA, MI6, KGB, SAS, and Israeli intelligence. They studied their targets, manipulated friends and family, preyed on weaknesses. By the end of the month, almost everyone had been caught — because when an entire country's resources are pointed at one person, it's nearly impossible to disappear.

Saul didn't have the internet, but he had an army, likely had informants, and desperately wanted David dead. And Israel was not a large territory. The whole thing was only about 6,600 square miles — slightly bigger than the Bahamas. Haiti is nearly twice the size. Finding one man in that space was not theoretically difficult.

Yet God cupped his hands around a single man and kept him alive. Not comfortable. Not spared from hard times. Alive. And looking at the later psalms you can see what that season produced in David — a thirst for God, a longing that reads like someone dying in a desert. He wasn't spared from the valley of the shadow of death. He wasn't spared from needing his soul restored. He wasn't spared from enemies. But listen to what God whispered into his heart.

The whole psalm is promises. And descriptions of what God does for us when we stay close to him.

I love the line about making us lie down in green pastures. We are sheep. Left to our own devices we so frequently run as hard as we can in exactly the wrong direction — just like the Israelites in Judges. "In those days there was no king in Israel; everyone did what was right in his own eyes." And you know how that worked out. Cycles of sin, punishment, repentance, deliverance, and repeat. We left to ourselves stray into trouble. We work too hard and rest too little. I think as Americans especially we need to be made to lie down in green pastures.

Those of us who've been saved a while tend to get wrapped up in being busy. There is so much need. After Irma — or Helene, or Idalia — many of you opened your homes, stayed with people who had opened theirs, spent weeks cutting limbs and hauling debris, helping neighbors. We evacuated to Kenny Duffield's house after Irma, which was a great blessing, and we were able to bless him afterward by helping him move. But you can get so busy with life — or even so busy helping people for God — that you forget to spend time with God himself. Helping people can become an idol. Life can become an idol.

Even believers sometimes need to be made to lie down. Dottie has shared her testimony about her leg before — how God used that season to deepen and strengthen her faith, and how it became the starting point for her using her musical gifts intentionally for his glory. I'm sure it wasn't fun. But God has used it in a major way. Before, she was riding horses. Now she's a musician writing books that tell people about Jesus. I honestly don't think anything less would have moved her to set aside horses and step into the ministry God had in store. She needed both the deepening and the equipping that year brought.

I see the green pasture as an equipping time. Sheep eat there, drink there, rest there. But some sheep wander. And when that happened, you know what shepherds did? They'd go find the lamb, bring it back, and break its leg. That sounds awful — and I imagine PETA would have opinions. But for the next couple of weeks while it healed, that shepherd carried the lamb everywhere he went. Carried it to water. Carried it to good grass. Met every need personally. And for the rest of that sheep's life, it never left the shepherd's side. It had learned — in its body — that there is safety with the shepherd. Comfort there. Love there. It learned the shepherd would lay down his life for it. That's something a sheep can't see from the far side of a field, where lions prowl and bears are looking for something to drag back to their cubs.

The predator I like least is the coyote — not because it's the most dangerous, but because it doesn't always kill to eat. Sometimes it kills for sport. Kills and walks away.

God doesn't often break our legs literally. But hard seasons do the same work. Spurgeon put it plainly: "The Christian is made strong and firmly rooted by all the trials of life." He promises to equip us when we need equipping, rest us when we need rest, lead us toward righteousness, restore our soul, walk with us when things are dark. His very presence is a comfort. Preparing a table in the presence of enemies, anointing, goodness and mercy, dwelling in God's presence forever — these are not small promises.

On this earth, I think we're more likely to learn through the valley of the shadow than through the green pasture. Through the hurricanes, the con men, the heartaches. That's true of almost everything God teaches us — we learn through experience. That's why it's dangerous to pray for patience; God gives you situations that require it. Pray for discernment, and he gives you situations where discernment must be exercised. Pray for faith and trust, and he allows circumstances that demand them. Like maybe a 185-mile-per-hour storm bearing down on everything you own.

But this is a training ground. We're being prepared for eternity. The green pastures and still waters we know now are a taste. We won't know perfect rest until we're in glory. We won't know perfect comfort until we're in the presence of God. We won't know the fullest expression of security until we are face to face with Jesus. People think they know joy now. Just wait. When we meet our God face to face, the things of earth will grow strangely dim in the light of his glory and grace.

I was reading in Romans 12 this week — we almost had a sermon on it, presenting our bodies as living sacrifices as our reasonable act of worship. But it struck me while I was reading that so often our worship is exactly where we struggle through the week. Not Sunday worship here — I think we're doing our best to be close to God on Sunday morning. I mean through the week.

A friend of mine grew up in a Christian home where cursing was not allowed. At school it felt risky, forbidden, and she thought it would help her fit in. For her, cursing and belonging had become synonymous. But Scripture is plain:

"Do not let any unwholesome talk come out of your mouths, but only what is helpful for building others up according to their needs, that it may benefit those who listen." — Ephesians 4:29

"Bless and do not curse." — Romans 12:14

"Whoever would love life and see good days must keep his tongue from evil and his lips from deceitful speech." — 1 Peter 3:10

James says this in chapter 3: with the tongue we praise the Lord, and with it we curse people made in God's image. Out of the same mouth come praise and cursing. My brothers and sisters, this should not be. A spring cannot produce fresh water and salt water at the same time. A fig tree cannot bear olives, or a grapevine bear figs. Our speech should reflect what's in us.

Jesus himself says it in Matthew 15: "What comes out of the mouth proceeds from the heart, and this defiles a person. For out of the heart come evil thoughts, murder, adultery, sexual immorality, theft, false witness, slander." The language we choose is a window into what we're carrying.

People are a lot like Joy with her abscess. There is a nasty infection on top of their head, and it hurts to treat it. We all have — or have had — different idols. For some it was drinking, or drugs, or lies, or gang life, or politics, or nationalism. Whatever yours is: think of it as an abscess on your heart. Treatment is painful, and you might need a shot. Maybe you need to go to rehab. But you know on some level that without treatment the infection spreads — and apart from Christ, sin over the long term puts your very soul at risk.

As the praise team comes up — search your heart. If there are abscesses of sin or idolatry you've been leaving untreated, the altar is right here. Come talk to God and get clean. If you don't yet have a relationship with Jesus, that's where this starts. Come find me during the song and I'll show you in the Bible how you can have eternal life.

Let's close in prayer.
