That phrase carries a deep and powerful truth — and it’s worth slowing down to see it. The Hebrew word here translated “want” is חָסֵר (ḥāsēr). It means to lack, to be in need, to be diminished. It’s used elsewhere in the Old Testament — for instance, in Deuteronomy 2:7, where it says, “These forty years the Lord your God has been with you. You have lacked nothing.” Same word — ḥāsēr. That’s the picture David is painting here. Not a life without difficulty, but a life where nothing essential is missing because the Lord is your Shepherd.

Now here’s where it hits home: ḥāsēr isn’t just about physical needs. It can refer to any area of deficiency — emotional, spiritual, moral. One theological dictionary puts it like this: “It describes the condition of being emptied out — of living in the aftermath of folly, sin, and separation from God.” In other words, the kind of want that stems not just from hard times, but from disordered hearts.

In fact, one commentator pointed out that want — this kind of deep soul-lack — often arises from four things: hastiness, greed, sloth, and debauchery. That sounds a lot like Romans 1, doesn’t it? Where people exchange the truth about God for a lie and are handed over to their own desires. When we abandon the Shepherd, we end up shepherding ourselves — and the result is want.

Hastiness stands out on that list. That tends to be a young mans sin, but I’ve known old men who were hasty as well… Hastiness is often confused for having faith. I spoke to someone once who was considering buying a building. He had around 3000$ a month extra in the budget, no plan for the building, and committed himself to paying 5000$ a month for the building. He argued that it was stepping out in faith. That he was trying to honor God. But I argue unless God is specifically telling you to do something like that, then you’re acting in haste, you’re being hasty. Ultimately, he ended up losing the building. Its true that God own the cattle on a thousand hills, and If he wanted that man to have that building he’d only need to sell the cattle off one of them. But at the same time, without a plan for the building, what was his purpose in the purchase? Trying to expand what he owns? Trying to invest? Perhaps. But God usually asks people to do things that expand the kingdom – not our wallets – though sometimes he does choose to bless financially. I met a man last year at a gas station. I was willing to let him in, but the cashier told him he must wait his turn. In his hasty treatment of the man behind the counter, how do you think that shaped the mans thinking about Christians? Haste has an effect not only on you, but on your witness to the world around you. When you are hasty while driving, and cut someone off, or tailgate with your Jesus fish on the car. Do you think that makes it easier or harder for you to tell that person you were rude to about Christ?

Hastiness masquerades as faith. Faith listens, and waits. Haste runs ahead. Proverbs 19:2 warns, “Desire without knowledge is not good, and whoever makes haste with his feet misses his way.” And when we act hastily — in finances, relationships, decisions — we often reap the pain of want.

Second was Greed. I think often greed and haste going hand in hand. One leads to the other. I saw a few minutes of a movie a few years back about an American man traveling across Europe by cheating people out of their money. Most of the time, he would get them thinking about the possible rewards, and that would loosen their hold on their wallet, and before you know it he had milked them dry. They could taste all the rich foods they would buy with that money. They could feel the water of the pool they would soon own. They could smell the sea from the water front property they would buy if only they would invest with this guy. Greed makes us believe we’re always one more purchase, one more experience away from happiness. But Ecclesiastes 5:10 says, “He who loves money will not be satisfied with money.” Why? Because greed is a hunger that feeds itself. You feed it — and it only grows. Leaving you empty and wanting. 

Third was Sloth. Some people think sloth is the opposite of haste. If doing something in haste is doing something quickly without fully thinking it through, caution is the opposite of haste then and not sloth. Because sloth is basically not doing anything at all. Not just laziness, but a kind of spiritual lethargy. A refusal to respond to God’s voice. Proverbs 13:4 says, “The soul of the sluggard craves and gets nothing, while the soul of the diligent is richly supplied.” Sloth leads to want not because the world is unfair — but because sloth refuses to get up and receive what God is offering.

He said well I guess you probably oughta keep that money since you did walk all the way over there and pick it up. Sloth will bring you to ruin just as quickly as haste.

And here’s where we really start to see the beauty of the Shepherd.
David doesn’t just say “The Lord is my Shepherd; I shall not want” and then move on. He tells us why. He says, “He makes me lie down in green pastures. He leads me beside still waters. He restores my soul.” That’s not just a poetic line — that’s the very heart of our God. That’s a Shepherd who provides. A Shepherd who knows what you need better than you do. Green pastures aren’t about comfort for comfort’s sake — they’re about nourishment. Still waters aren’t just quiet places — they’re places of peace, where your soul can breathe. And when David says “He restores my soul,” he’s talking about healing that goes all the way down to the core — to the places that drugs, and sex, and money, and success can’t ever reach.
Some of y’all have tried to shepherd your own lives — and you know it doesn’t work. It leads to stress, and exhaustion, and pain. Wanting more and more but never satisfied. But the Lord leads with purpose. Jesus says in John 10, “I am the good Shepherd. The good Shepherd lays down His life for the sheep.” That’s not a distant leader. That’s a Savior who bled for you. That’s provision in blood, sweat, and tears.
And He doesn’t just provide stuff — He provides Himself. Jesus doesn’t say, “I’ll give you peace.” He says, “I am your peace.” He doesn’t say, “I’ll send you bread.” He says, “I am the Bread of Life.” He is the provision. So when David says “I shall not want,” he’s not saying life is easy — he’s saying Jesus is enough.
Even when the path winds through dark valleys — and it will — the Shepherd doesn’t vanish. He walks with us. He prepares a table in the presence of our enemies. That’s not scraping by — that’s abundance even in battle. He anoints your head with oil, your cup runs over — not because of your effort, but because of His grace.
And that last verse? David says, “Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life.” Not some days. Not just Sundays. All the days — even the ones where you’re limping, doubting, or falling short. That’s our Shepherd. He doesn’t just lead from the front — His goodness and mercy follow close behind, picking us up, patching us up, and pulling us forward.


Your walk with the Lord MUST begin with a relationship with him. When you have a relationship with him, you will have no want – a want that is caused by greed, hastiness, debauchery and sloth. You’ll find fulfillment, and have no room for greed. You’ll be concerned with living right and being holy as He is Holy, and you’ll have no room for debauchery. You wont be slothful, nor hasty, but you’ll move in time with the Lord as though it were a great dance and Christ was leading us every step of the way.
