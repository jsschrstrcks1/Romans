Leadership is a weighty task in any context. Doubly so for those of us who endeavor to do so in obedience to Christ in a Christian context. Specifically for those of us who are pastors. The author of Hebrews writes in 13:17 (ESV) “Obey your leaders and submit to them, for they are keeping watch over your souls, as those who will have to give an account. Let them do this with joy and not with groaning, for that would be of no advantage to you.”

The imagery evoked is that of Moses traveling around in circles in a desert listening to the people whine “Oh Moses, you should have left us with our slave masters. You should have left us in Egypt at least there we had food and water regularly. Every decision made was second guessed six different ways. Every direction taken was questioned. Moses was it seemed from their complaints, the cause of all their discomfort and misery. The truth was different of course, but what thick skin Moses must have developed. 

Secondly, and more importantly is the first part of Hebrews 13:17 which said essentially that leaders keep watch over their follower’s souls, and are going to be held accountable for their follower’s actions. In our culture today we don’t always know where our people are in all things. We are not in a culture that discloses things like hidden addictions to pornography. Yet God sees, knows, and will hold us accountable for the steps we took in those areas. We don’t always know that Sally is a speeder and wantonly breaks traffic laws and yet the leader is going to be accountable one day before God for the actions of his people. In the words of the old Hymn, “It was my sins that held Him there until it was accomplished, His dying breath has brought me life, I know that it is finished”. Except in the case of a pastor, it was my sin, and also Sally’s and Bobs, and Jim – and the rest of the people whose souls I must keep watch over. 

This second element is much more difficult than the first. Scripture says a nagging wife (and in Moses case a nagging nation) is like a cancer to a mans bones. Yet what and whom should we fear? Flesh and blood human beings? Or the infinite God that uses the heavens as his footstool. That in under a week spoke everything we’ve spent the next 5-10,000 years trying to understand! The answer is obvious! Fear not the one who can take your life, fear the one that has made a determination regarding your soul before the foundation of the earth(Jer 1:5)! 

As undesirable of a job as that sounds, its still a position ordained by God. Some folks are tasked with that burden by God (Romans 13:1) Anyone who is in authority is only in authority of any form because God has placed him there, and established him as the leader. Some translations say ordained by God. If God has put us in a position then we know that God will give us what we NEED to accomplish His end goals. Sometimes, or perhaps usually that means God putting us in positions that require us to be stretched, and molded to rise to the challenge. Moses again comes to mind. What was his response to Gods leadership task? Sorry God but no. I’m not enough (Exodus 4:10). 

Even though God does supply our needs in the context of leadership, even though he does stretch us to fit, and help us to get to the point we need to be. The point is for us to reach a place of deeper submission to God. A place where we realize I am insufficient, but God is always sufficient. We m,ust reach a point where we can say “I’m not enough, but God is, and if God has asked me to accomplish this task, AND God wants me to do it, God is going to have to supply the lack and if all of this is a lesson in how to fail, then thank you Lord for teaching me”. 

That is a hard place to be. But God has advice for those who are in the midst of such struggles – Galatians 6:9 (ESV) as an example. Paul writes “Let us not become weary in doing good, for at the proper time we will reap a harvest if we do not give up”. God through Paul is saying, hey, I know it’s hard. But the reward is worth the struggle. 

In Isaiah 41:10 (ESV) God promises to strengthen us, and uphold us with His right hand. And in Philippians that we “Can do all things through Christ who gives me strength”. 

Is there any better place that we can be than in the palm of the right hand of God? Is there any place or way we can have more strength than when God is supplying the requisite strength? Certainly not! God will not bring us to a task he won’t help us get through. The best and scariest part of God’s plan is that he chooses us not because we are necessarily the best man for the job. Moses was another perfect example of that – Moses felt inadequate when it comes to public speaking. Yet do we read of him speaking poorly in front of pharaoh? Certainly not, he spoke in a powerful way that in conjunction with the actions of God cause God’s people to be let loose, and not loosed as paupers and slaves they were sent out with wealth beyond their imaginings when they were slaves!. Moses may not have been the strongest public speaker, but God chose Moses for a specific reason, for a specific time, and the results dramatically altered the course of history not only for the mighty Egypt but also for the Israelites, and ultimately the world through Christ. 

So far we have established The scale of leadership – its hard! We’ve established that we have help in God. Also that it is something we have been ordained by God to do before the foundations of the world. So in light of that what does a Christian leader look like? To answer this question we need to go straight back to scripture. There are two passages that come to mind – first lets look at 1 Timothy 3. Listed there are the following qualifications (ESV): above reproach, sober-minded, self controled, Husband of only one wife, respectable, hospitable, able to teach, not a drunkard, not violent, gentle, not quarrelsome, not a lover of money, manage his own household well, maintain dignity, keep his children submissive, not a young believer, well thought of by outsiders, not greedy, not double tongued, hold the mystery of the faith with clear conscience, tested, proved blameless, and if married there are other requirements for the wife as well. As I am not married I’m not diving into those. 

Titus 1 likewise has a list, and it adds (ESV): Children believers in Jesus Christ, subordinate, not debauched, not arrogant, not quick tempered, not violent, upright, holy, disciplined, Hold firm to the word of God as taught (presumably by Paul and the disciples), able to give instruction, has sound doctrine, and able to rebuke where needed – Paul goes on to explain that a person fit for leading as a Christian is essentially the opposite of a Cretan – which he describes as liars, evil beasts, lazy, gluttons, not sound in faith/doctrine, defiled, and approach the world through a lens of defilement, defiled conscience, deny God through their actions, detestable, disobedience, and unfit for any good work. 

With a list like this it’s a wonder anyone is ever selected as leader. One of the words that stood out was upright. Initially it seems upright would seem to indicate a state of unfailing morality. If down is bad, always up means never down. Never doing bad things. The Greek however explains the word differently.  The word in greek is δίκαιον, which according to A Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament and other Early Christian Literature (BDAG) conveys the idea of “being in accordance with high standards of rectitude, upright, just, fair”. So maintaining a high standard of rectitude (righteousness), of fairness and justice. That one little word packs in a lot more meaning than one might expect. Maintaining a high standard though, is different than never failing. Its endeavoring to never fail, its making constant and consistent effort to never fail (in terms of falling short of high standards of righteousness). It doesn’t mean you will never have bad days – even Paul had is moments of failing to maintain a high standard – Romans 7:15 “For I do not understand my own actions. For I do not do what I want, but I do the very thing I hate”. If even the earthly author of the command to remain upright did things that he hates – and so things that presumably were less than perfectly righteous – then our intial assessment of the definition of righteousness can’t stand. A good leader – an upright leader – then is one who works hard at being righteous, fair, and just. 

In Paul’s letter to Timothy, he used another phrase – sober-minded. That word has a depth of meaning. At first it may seem that one means simply to not be drunk with alcohol. But as is frequently the case there is more depth in the greek word used, than in the English word that is used for translation. Additionally Paul calls out addiction to alcohol by name so it is not simply a prohibition against being a drunkard (which is repeated in Titus). Its talking about being temperate, or sober in terms of ones thinking. It is a common thing in our day to hear about people engaging in road rage. Such behavior would be the anti-example. One engaging in road rage is not being sober in their thinking, or behavior. Point of fact, there is a common thread in all of the prohibitions and requirements listed in both of these two passages. 

When one desires liquor above everything, what is on the throne of his heart? An alcoholic might argue that they are believers in truth, and that God is still in control of their heart and mind, and that they are obedient only to God. But from the perspective of an outside observer; a person who is a slave to alcohol is daily, multiple times, is polluting their ability to rationally think with a substance taken liberally we should only have in moderation. It is at least sinful in that it disobeys God’s directives about alcohol, but further it reeks of idolatry. Perhaps the idol is the feeling one has when drunk, perhaps it’s about not feeling a certain way or drinking to forget. Regardless what you end up with is an entire bouquet of idols. The alcohol becomes something you lust after more than God. If you’re drinking to be drunk then your own feelings are an idol. If you’re drinking to forget, then not only are your own feelings an idol, not only is alcohol and idol, but you are sinning in that you’re not depending upon God to help you handle your emotions, your memories, or whatever the root cause of the drinking is. A quote oft attributed to John Calvin, that the heart of man is a factory of idols. The more deeply we look at the context of a sin, the more clearly this proves to be the case. 

The other negative behaviors likewise circle this same Idolatry of I. Divorce comes down to this: “I choose to break God’s commands because I am uncomfortable”. The merits of divorce in a murderously abusive situation can be argued, but it doesn’t change the fact that someone chose to go against God’s commands. Likewise when it comes to self control. If someone is lacking self-control it’s because they desire things more than they respect God’s command regarding moderation. Over eating? Sex before marriage? Adultery? All of these things fit that same overarching paradigm. If he’s conceited and excessively proud of himself, then he’s not focused enough on God. Glory is not for man, but for God. A famous catechism reprinted in my study bible says it this way “Man’s chief end is to glorify God, and to enjoy Him forever”. Some of the prohibitions in our passage are a little more difficult to link to idolatry, but in the end, even things like speaking with a “double tongue” all go back to idolatry. The phrase double tongued, lets define as saying one thing while meaning another. The CSB translation gets it right here by translating the greek word as “hypocritical”. If a person is living their life one way but trying to make people think they are a different way that is hypocrisy, or in this case being double tongued. 

The things scripture commands us as leaders to be, follow the obverse of the same pattern. That is to say, if everything prohibited follows one pattern, everything in these passages that scripture commands follows a similar but opposite pattern. The common denominator in all the positive commands in these passages is that in order to achieve them (uprightness for instance) one must put ones self aside, and be obedient to Christ regardless of the personal cost. Consider with me the initial clause – being “upright”. One of the words that is in the semantic domain of the Greek word translated upright is the word “fair”. 

Every society has an unspoken set of societal contracts. These are generally lumped in together along with many many other things all under the term “culture”. Things like which side of the sidewalk do you walk down in America are all included in that. Additionally, the idea of what is fair to a point a cultural construct. In the cultural construction of the idea of fair, I think its safe to say the extent to which a culture is objectively biblically fair is relative to the general level of evangelism and discipleship country wide. Regardless its safe to say that fair is the opposite of unfair. Unfair is choosing a side based on prejudice or otherwise having broken some rules of the societal contract in a way that is prejudicial. As a fallen people we are all prone to bias no matter how we might fight it. This bias, or prejudice makes our assessments of things by nature of the assessment being a product of a fallen mind prejudiced. We are prejudiced because of our fallen sin nature, because of our tendency towards selfishness, and because of our conformity to the sinful culture around us. Selfishness is a sin. Prejudice at the expense of another is a sin. Unfairness is a sin. While as believers we have victory over sin, there is an element of “already not yet” that factors in so while victory in the war is ours, there are still battles that are being waged. The result of this is greater or lesser degrees of an individual’s fairness. We could talk about sanctification at this point but the long and short of it is this: If an individual is able to put themselves aside and put Christ on the worship alter of their heart, as demonstrated by obedience to Christ, then one is going to be as fair as is humanly possible. 

If one is able to do those things, one will be as upright as is humanly possible. If one is able to cast aside their earthly desires and adopt God’s desires as their own, then they are going to be as upright as is humanly possible. 

When we are acting in selfish ways we are putting our selves as the object of worship in our lives. When we are acting in obedience to Christ we are putting Christ as the object of worship in our lives.
