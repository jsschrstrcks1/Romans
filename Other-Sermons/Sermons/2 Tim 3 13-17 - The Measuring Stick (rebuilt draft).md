# The Measuring Stick
## 2 Timothy 3:13–17 — Scripture, Salvation, and the Christ Who Holds It All Together

Preached as part of a Five Solas series (Sola Scriptura). Communion Sunday.

---

### Scripture Reading: 2 Timothy 3:13–17 (ESV)

> **13** But evil people and impostors will go on from bad to worse, deceiving and being deceived. **14** But as for you, continue in what you have learned and have firmly believed, knowing from whom you learned it **15** and how from childhood you have been acquainted with the sacred writings, which are able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus. **16** All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, **17** that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work.

---

### I. Deceiving and Being Deceived (v. 13)

Paul doesn't sugarcoat it. Evil people and impostors will go on from bad to worse. Not *might*. Will. And notice what they're doing — deceiving *and being deceived*. That's how it works. The man who sells the lie eventually buys it himself.

A few years back I met a pair of women going door to door in my neighborhood — Jehovah's Witnesses. One of the neighbors called me over, and we started talking. Come to find out the younger of the two was a new recruit. And as the conversation went on, I learned she had been a Southern Baptist. Born to Baptist parents. On the cradle roll. Involved with GA's, part of a Baptist youth group as a teenager. And yet, according to her, she had never taken the Bible seriously — not until she became a Jehovah's Witness.

Now the JW's use a twisted version of the Bible. In their translation some places sound very, very familiar, but in others it's been perverted — adding to or taking away from the text. I wish I could have talked to her longer because she began to waver. She knew what the truth was in her heart. And when the older woman with her saw that waver, she pulled the plug and stormed out.

That young woman was being deceived. And the woman who recruited her? Deceiving *and* being deceived — both at the same time. Just like Paul said.

This is the world Timothy lived in. The people he was pastoring in Ephesus were surrounded by false teachers who took things from the Old Testament out of context, produced a distorted view of Christ, and made a mess of sound doctrine. It wasn't a new problem then, and it's not a new problem now.

So what does Paul tell Timothy to do about it?

---

### II. But as for You — Continue (v. 14)

"But as for you." Two of the most important words in the passage. Paul draws a hard line. *They* will go from bad to worse. But *you* — you do something different.

Continue in what you have learned and have firmly believed.

The verb there — "continue" — is present active imperative in the Greek. That's a command. Not a suggestion. Not a helpful tip. A command: start now, keep going, don't stop. Continue acting in accordance with what you were taught.

And then Paul adds this: "knowing from whom you learned it."

That matters. Timothy didn't learn the faith from a stranger on the internet. He learned it from his grandmother Lois and his mother Eunice — 2 Timothy 1:5 tells us that. He learned it from Paul himself. There was a chain of faithful teaching, person to person, generation to generation. And Paul is telling Timothy: you know the people who taught you. You know their character. You watched them live it. Trust that.

We have that same chain. It runs through the centuries — through the Apostles, through the early church, through Augustine, through the Reformers. In 1611 Thomas Helwys wrote in the first Baptist confession that Scripture "set down an absolute, and perfect rule of direction, for all persons, at all times, to be observed; Which no Prince, nor any whosoever, may add to, or diminish from." In 1689 the Second London Baptist Confession declared Scripture "the only sufficient, certain and infallible rule for saving knowledge, faith, and obedience." Our own Baptist Faith and Message says the same thing today.

The chorus has never changed. Continue in what you have learned. Know from whom you learned it. The chain of faithful witnesses stretches back to the Apostles, and every link says the same thing: this Book is God's Word to us.

But Paul doesn't stop there. Because the chain of teaching is not the point. It's the road. The point is where the road leads.

---

### III. Wise for Salvation Through Faith in Christ Jesus (v. 15)

Here's the hinge of the whole passage. Don't miss this.

"And how from childhood you have been acquainted with the sacred writings, which are able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus."

Read that last phrase again. *Through faith in Christ Jesus.*

Paul does not say the sacred writings make you smart. He does not say they make you moral. He does not say they make you a better citizen or a better debater. He says they make you *wise for salvation* — and that salvation comes *through faith in Christ Jesus*.

Christ is not the appendix to the Bible. He is the point of the Bible.

Every sacrifice in Leviticus pointed to him. Every promise to Abraham found its yes in him. Every psalm of lament cried out for him. Every prophet who said "thus says the Lord" was preparing the way for the Word made flesh. The Baptist Faith and Message says it plainly: "All Scripture is a testimony to Christ, who is Himself the focus of divine revelation."

You can read your Bible cover to cover and miss the whole thing if you miss Jesus. The Pharisees did. They memorized the Torah, debated it, built hedges around it, wore it on their foreheads — and when the Author of it stood in front of them, they didn't recognize him. Jesus told them to their faces: "You search the Scriptures because you think that in them you have eternal life; and it is they that bear witness about me, yet you refuse to come to me that you may have life" — John 5:39–40.

The Scriptures bear witness about *him*. That's what they're for.

So hear me — if you have read your Bible faithfully, if you have memorized verses and attended services and served on committees, but you have never placed your faith in the Jesus that the Bible is actually about — you have the road but you've never arrived. The sacred writings are *able* to make you wise for salvation. But the salvation comes through faith in Christ.

And if you have never begun that relationship — if you have been going through the motions, or if you walked in today carrying guilt or shame or the weight of a life that's fallen apart — the gospel is not complicated. You are a sinner. So am I. So is every person in this room. We have all fallen short of the glory of God. But God, being rich in mercy, sent his Son to live the life we couldn't live, die the death we deserved to die, and rise again on the third day. And everyone who turns from their sin and trusts in Christ is forgiven. Not improved. Not upgraded. *Forgiven.* Declared righteous. Adopted into the family of God.

That's what the sacred writings are pointing at. That's the salvation Paul is talking about. Christ is not optional. He is the whole point.

---

### IV. All Scripture Is God-Breathed (v. 16)

Now — if verse 15 tells us what Scripture is *for*, verse 16 tells us what Scripture *is*.

"All Scripture is breathed out by God."

The Greek word is *theopneustos* — God-breathed. Not man-dreamed. Not committee-authored. *God-breathed.* We believe that God inspired each of the authors in a special way — through their vocabulary, their personality, their circumstances — and through them expressed eternal truths in a way that would be translatable into every language, understandable by every person, and inexhaustible no matter how many times you read it.

Think about what that means. The Bible is 66 books, written over roughly 1,500 years, by dozens of authors who had little or no contact with one another. Shepherds, kings, fishermen, tax collectors, a doctor, a tentmaker. And yet — no internal contradictions. One story. One thread. One Christ, from Genesis to Revelation.

One of my pet peeves is continuity errors in fiction. I love a good fantasy novel, and nothing pulls me out of a story faster than a character who's in two places at once. Brandon Sanderson — and if you haven't read Sanderson you should — he writes prolifically, but he employs an army of beta readers and gamma readers, and his books go through draft after draft to catch exactly those kinds of errors.

Paul didn't have beta readers. He wrote letters — personal correspondence, sent once, no revisions. And yet what he wrote in Romans doesn't contradict what he wrote in Galatians, or Ephesians, or this very letter to Timothy. Across 66 books and dozens of authors and centuries of writing — one voice. Because behind every human pen was one divine Author.

And God didn't just inspire it — he preserved it.

The Masoretes were a group of Jewish scribes whose entire mission was the faithful copying of the Old Testament. They counted every word. They knew the middle word of each book. They tracked the last word of each line on each page. When they came to one of the divine names, they stopped, underwent a purification ritual, and only then continued writing. When a manuscript was finished, a separate scholar read every word of every page. One error — a single letter — and the entire manuscript was destroyed. The copyist started again from scratch. One copy of the Old Testament took roughly twenty years to complete.

And when the Dead Sea Scrolls were discovered — manuscripts roughly a thousand years older than our best existing copies — the text was remarkably, astonishingly faithful. A thousand years of hand-copying, and the message didn't change. Because the God who breathed it out also kept it.

If he wanted us to have his Word, he would preserve it. He did.

And what is that preserved, God-breathed Scripture profitable for? Paul gives four things: teaching, reproof, correction, and training in righteousness.

Teaching — it shows us what is true.
Reproof — it shows us where we've gone wrong.
Correction — it shows us how to get back on track.
Training in righteousness — it shows us how to stay there.

Luther understood this. He wrote: "Nothing helps more powerfully against the devil, the world, the flesh, and all evil thoughts than occupying oneself with God's Word, having conversations about it, and contemplating it."

When life gets hard — when you are weary, sad, discouraged, tempted, under attack, carrying a diagnosis, watching a marriage crumble — don't reach for the self-help shelf. Open your Bible. The God who breathed it out will meet you in it.

---

### V. Complete, Equipped for Every Good Work (v. 17)

"That the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work."

That word "complete" — *artios* in the Greek — carries the sense of being fitted, capable, ready for the task. And "equipped" — *exērtismenos* — is even stronger. Thoroughly furnished. Everything you need.

The Bible is sometimes called the canon of Scripture. Not *cannon* — like the weapon that fires iron balls at wooden boats. *Canon* — from the word for reed. They used to use reeds of a certain length as measuring sticks. Scripture is the measuring stick for the Christian life. It contains both the standard we should live up to and the means to measure our progress.

My father has a door that he has the grandkids back up against, and he takes their measure every so often. That door used to be my grandmother's, and so my measurements are on there too. Most of my cousins — basically our whole family — has had their measure taken on the back of that door.

One day we are going to stand face to face with God, and he will take our measure. His measuring stick will be Scripture.

But here's what I want you to see — Paul doesn't end with judgment. He ends with *equipment*. "Equipped for every good work." The purpose of Scripture isn't just to show you where you fall short. It's to make you complete. To fit you for the work God saved you to do.

Verse 15 said the sacred writings make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus. Verse 17 says that same Scripture makes you complete, equipped for every good work. Salvation and service. Christ and calling. The Bible does both — because the Bible is about Christ, and Christ saves people for a purpose.

---

### So What Do We Do?

Read it. Every day. Not to check a box. Not because you're a better Christian if you read five chapters instead of one. Read it because the God who breathed it out is meeting you in it. Read it because every page points to Christ. Read it because it will wash you, comfort you, reprove you, correct you, train you, and equip you.

Some of you committed to reading through the Bible in a year with us. I won't ask for a show of hands, but some of you are behind. That's fine. Pick it back up. If you didn't make it last year, try again. If you did — you're in for a double blessing, because the second time through you find things you didn't see the first time. Your soul gets more deeply anchored. You have quicker access to the things the Prince of Peace has told us about himself.

But all of that starts with verse 15. It starts with faith in Christ Jesus. Without him, the Bible is just a book. With him, it is the living Word of God — breathed out, preserved, profitable, and sufficient to make you everything God intends you to be.

If you have never begun a relationship with Christ, I would love to talk with you after the service.
