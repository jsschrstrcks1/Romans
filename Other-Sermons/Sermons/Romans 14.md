# Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Sermon**: Am I Fully Convinced (Romans 14:1-21)
**Evaluated**: 2026-03-22

---

## Verdict Title

**A Pastoral Heart With an Unfinished Sermon — Warmth Without Structure, Application Without Exposition**

---

## One-Sentence Summary

A warm, personal, and evangelistically motivated treatment of Romans 14 that demonstrates genuine pastoral care for the congregation but does not yet constitute text-driven exposition — the outline floats above the passage rather than arising from it.

---

## A. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test**: Marginal pass — the sermon’s organizing questions ("Am I fully convinced? Am I doing this unto the Lord? Will it stand before God? Will I cause others to stumble?") are related to Romans 14’s concerns, but they are imposed on the passage as a grid rather than derived from the passage’s own argument. The text’s central burden — the unity of weak and strong believers in accepting one another as God has accepted them — is not the sermon’s main point.

**Authority Test**: Conditional pass — the preacher submits to the passage’s general direction, but the hard edges of Paul’s argument are rounded. The theological grounding of Paul’s case (Christ died and rose to be Lord of the living and the dead, v. 9; the *bēma*, v. 10) receives almost no development.

**Verdict**: The gate opens, but narrowly. The sermon is better understood as a pastoral meditation using Romans 14 as a framework than as an exposition of it.

---

## B. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 4 — Application is largely generic**: The application questions ("Am I doing this for the Lord? Will it stand before God?") are real and biblical but remain at the level of principle throughout. The sermon does not press into the specific fallen conditions these questions are meant to address or differentiate among hearers with genuine specificity.

**Flag 5 — No clear exhortation**: The sermon closes with a gospel appeal (which is good) but never issues a clear call to specific action for believers regarding the Romans 14 situation. The congregation is told to consider their actions but is never summoned to a concrete response.

**Score cap applies: maximum 69.**

---

## C. Score Breakdown

### 1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 13/25

**Textual Fidelity (5/10)**: The four-part outline traces thematic territory covered in Romans 14, but the sermon does not work through the passage’s actual argument. Several key verses receive no treatment. The *bēma* (vv. 10-12), the strong obligation to bear the failings of the weak, and the text’s ground in Christ’s lordship over the living and the dead (v. 9) are absent or underdeveloped. The dog story is genuinely engaging but does not arise from the text — it is imported and then pressed to carry theological weight it cannot bear.

**Contextual Awareness (3/5)**: The reference to food sacrificed to idols and the weak/strong dynamic shows awareness of the passage’s setting. The connection to the broader Romans narrative is not drawn.

**Exegetical Clarity (2/5)**: No exegetical work is present. No Greek terms, no engagement with Paul’s logic, no explanation of *why* Paul structures the argument as he does. The preacher’s conclusions are sound but their derivation from the text is opaque.

**Logical Faithfulness (3/5)**: No overt eisegesis, but the sermon’s questions are imported rather than discovered in the passage.

**FCF (Fallen Condition Focus)**: Not named explicitly. The fallen condition lurking in this text — our tendency to usurp God’s judicial authority and subject our brothers to our own conscience rather than releasing them to their Lord — is not surfaced as such.

---

### 2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18

**Christ-Centeredness (4/7)**: Christ appears at the closing gospel appeal and in the Spurgeon quote’s implied urgency, but He is not the center of the sermon’s argument. The theological freight of Romans 14:9 — that Christ died and rose precisely to be Lord of the living and the dead, and that this is the ground of the command not to judge — is entirely absent.

**Redemptive-Historical Awareness (1/3)**: No placement of this text in the redemptive-historical story.

**Doctrinal Precision (4/4)**: What doctrine is explicitly handled is handled fairly — the closed gospel appeal at the end ("believe in Jesus, put your faith in Jesus") is sound, if brief. No errors introduced.

**Confessional Alignment (2/4)**: The sermon does not contradict confessional theology. But the distinctive soteriological and ecclesiological concerns of this passage (Christ’s lordship as the ground of brotherly acceptance) are not engaged with confessional depth.

---

### 3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 8/15

**Clarity of the Gospel (3/5)**: The closing gospel appeal is present and real. An unbeliever could understand they need to trust Christ for salvation. However, the mechanics of the gospel — what Christ actually did, why it works, what it cost — are absent. The appeal is more of an invitation than a proclamation.

**Necessity of Christ (2/5)**: Christ is necessary to the sermon’s close but largely decorative in the body. The Spurgeon quote carries more theological weight than the sermon itself. A moralistic reading of the four outline questions could survive without Christ.

**Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5)**: The sermon moves toward a gospel appeal, which is good. But the law is not allowed to land meaningfully before the gospel relieves it. The "will it stand before God" section gestures at accountability but does not press hard enough to make the gospel’s relief genuinely necessary.

**Synagogue Test**: This sermon could be heard in a moralistic synagogue without major offense. The questions ("Am I doing this for the Lord? Will it stand before God?") sound in principle. The cross as penal substitution, as the only ground of acceptance before that judgment seat, is not proclaimed in the body of the sermon.

---

### 4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15

**Heart Penetration (3/5)**: The story of Rami and the hat and Holly rubbing her sister’s hand are genuinely powerful pastoral illustrations. They corner the person who uses "doing it for the Lord" as cover for controlling others. That is real conscience work.

**Specificity (3/5)**: The alcoholism illustration (the cousin Larry who struggled) is specific and grounded. The political argument examples are relevant to this congregation. But the application does not press into differentiated heart territory — why do we judge? What does it reveal about our trust in God? What specific repentance looks like is not named.

**Spiritual Differentiation (2/5)**: The sermon addresses the congregation largely as a single undifferentiated group. The weak and the strong of Romans 14 are named but not addressed distinctly. Perkins’ categories are not approached — the sermon does not speak separately to the person who is judging, the one who is despising, the one who is struggling with a genuine conscience issue, or the unbeliever.

---

### 5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8

**Call to Response (2/4)**: The gospel call at the end is a genuine call for unbelievers. For believers, the call is implied ("consider your actions") but never crisp. What does repentance look like for the person who has been acting as a stumbling block? The sermon does not say.

**Equipping for Holiness and Witness (1/4)**: The Nik Ripken "two teams" section pivots to evangelism in a way that feels disconnected from the Romans 14 argument. The transition is abrupt, and while the concern for evangelism is admirable, the application to the Romans 14 situation is lost. The congregation is not sent out with specific equipment for handling disputable matters differently.

---

### 6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9

**Clarity of Outline (2/4)**: The four questions work as a memory structure. But the sermon’s outline is not the passage’s structure — the four questions do not trace Romans 14’s argument. The outline is imposed, not discovered.

**Progression (2/3)**: The sermon moves forward but the pivot from "will I cause others to stumble" to the Nik Ripken "two teams" section and then to the church-size note is jarring. The closing feel is miscellaneous rather than climactic.

**Unity (1/2)**: There is a unifying theme, but the sermon’s final third wanders — Nik Ripken, church growth concerns, and the Spurgeon quote (quoted twice) compete with the passage’s conclusion.

---

### 7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5

**Relevance (2/2)**: The Grace the dog story is memorable and completely native to this congregation. The cousin Larry illustration is personal and specific. Rami and Holly land as real pastoral situations. These illustrations are the sermon’s greatest asset.

**Clarity (1/2)**: The Grace illustration is used three different times in the outline, which risks overuse. By the third mention, the illustration is doing work it wasn’t originally set up to do (Grace leading Opie astray as an illustration of causing others to stumble). It stretches.

**Memorability (1/1)**: A congregation will remember Grace the dog. That is both a strength and a warning — the illustration may be more memorable than the text.

---

### 8. Sermonic Force — 4/5

**Reads as Preaching (2/2)**: This is clearly written for oral delivery — short sentences, direct address, personal stories. It breathes. There is no academic throat-clearing.

**Conviction on the Page (1/2)**: The conviction is pastoral and personal, but not textually grounded. The sentences that carry the most force are the ones furthest from the passage ("I don’t care how big we are, I care that we tell people about Jesus").

**Cadence and Breath (1/1)**: The rhythm is natural and accessible.

---

### D. Cognitive Load Modifier: 0

The sermon is highly accessible. An average church member, a new believer, a high schooler can follow this without difficulty. No cognitive overload.

## F. Weight of Glory Bonus

**+1/5**

The closing gospel appeal and the Spurgeon quote carry genuine eternal weight. The concern that someone in the room might not be saved is real pastoral gravity. But the sermon does not sustain that gravity throughout — it appears at the end rather than pervading the whole.

## G. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ or myself?** — Primarily myself and my motivations. Christ appears at the close.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** — Ideas about how to live. The encounter with God is gestured toward but not achieved.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** — The Rami and Holly stories may convict someone who has done similar things. The closing appeal may comfort the uncertain. The majority of the sermon informs.

---

## H. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered — this sermon is not dead orthodoxy. The preacher has pastoral warmth and genuine concern for his people. The deficit is not bloodlessness but depth.

---

## I. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The pastoral illustrations are genuinely strong.** The Grace story, the cousin Larry example, and the Rami/Holly vignettes are all specific, native to the congregation, and not interchangeable with another sermon. The illustration of watching Grace in the window’s reflection is particularly sharp.

2. **The evangelistic concern is authentic.** The closing gospel appeal is not a formula — it reads like a preacher who actually believes people in the room may not be saved. That urgency is worth preserving.

3. **The stumbling block section lands well.** The connection between Grace leading Opie astray and the weaker brother/stronger brother dynamic is the most text-connected moment in the sermon, and the application (don’t drink in front of struggling alcoholics) is specific and real.

---

## J. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The sermon does not exposit the text.** Romans 14:1-21 has a driving argument — Paul is calling the church to receive one another as God has received them, grounded in Christ’s lordship over the living and the dead. That argument is not preached. The outline questions are true principles but they are not Paul’s argument.

2. **The theological center of the passage is absent.** Verse 9 ("For to this end Christ died and lived again, that he might be Lord both of the dead and of the living") is the foundation of everything Paul says in 14:10-21. The reason we don’t judge our brother is that Christ is his Lord, not ours. This is never stated.

3. **The sermon ends in multiple directions.** The Nik Ripken material, the church-size concern, the doubled Spurgeon quote, and the gospel appeal feel like four different conclusions. The final impression is miscellaneous, not climactic.

---

## K. Blind Spots

The preacher is comfortable with pastoral application but may not yet see the difference between applying a text and expositing it. The four outline questions are sound sermon questions — they just aren’t this text’s questions. Paul is not primarily asking "are you doing this for the Lord?" He is asking "why are you judging the servant of another?" The shift in framing is not cosmetic — it changes what the sermon actually does to a congregation.

---

## L. Illusion of Strength

The Grace illustration feels strong because it is warm, personal, and memorable. But it is doing the work that the text should be doing. The congregation may leave thinking about Grace the dog and about cousin Larry rather than about Christ as Lord of the living and the dead. A vivid illustration can mask underdeveloped exposition — and here it does.

---

## M. Missed Opportunity

Romans 14:7-9 is the theological engine of the entire chapter: "For none of us lives to himself, and none of us dies to himself. For if we live, we live to the Lord, and if we die, we die to the Lord. So then, whether we live or whether we die, we are the Lord’s. For to this end Christ died and lived again, that he might be Lord both of the dead and of the living." This passage offers a stunning christological ground for the entire "doing this unto the Lord" section of the sermon. It is the passage’s own answer to the preacher’s second question. It was not used.

---

## N. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild Point II ("Am I Doing This Unto the Lord?") around Romans 14:7-9.** The text answers the question the preacher is asking. Use Paul’s own Christological ground — Christ died and rose to be Lord — as the foundation for why we evaluate our motives.

2. **State the passage’s main point before the first point.** "Paul’s argument in Romans 14 is this: receive one another as God has received you in Christ, because Christ is Lord of you both." The congregation needs the target before the arrows.

3. **Add distinct address to the judging believer and the despising believer.** These are Paul’s two audiences in the passage. The sermon does not speak to them differently. A brief moment of direct address to each — "Some of you are the judge in this room. You have a mental list..." and "Some of you are the despiser. You roll your eyes..." — would do more conscience work than the four outline questions combined.

4. **Cut the church-size paragraph.** It is a good pastoral word but it belongs in a different sermon. Here it dilutes the conclusion.

5. **Use the Spurgeon quote once, at the climax, not twice.** A quote that appears twice loses half its force both times.

6. **Develop the gospel appeal with one sentence of theological substance.** "Believe in Jesus" is real, but why? What did He do? Adding one sentence — "He took the judgment you deserved, died in your place, and rose to be Lord of all who trust in Him" — grounds the appeal in the text’s own *bēma* imagery.
