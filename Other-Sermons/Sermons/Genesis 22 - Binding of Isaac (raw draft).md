## Introduction — Clues in the Text

So we left Abraham and Sarah in chapter 21 shortly after they had given birth to Isaac. Despite being decades beyond the end of normal child bearing years they had given birth to a happy healthy baby boy. Years later, thirty some years later we pick up in Genesis 22.

Abraham would have been well over 100 by this point (bearing in mind he lived to be 175). He's still pretty old. We're going to read the text here in a minute, but when you do, I want you to look for some clues in the text. Remember the game Clue? You had to figure out "who dunnit" — may have been Colonel Mustard in the kitchen with the candle holder, you had to think about things, and use your brain and the process of elimination to figure out who did it, where and with what. There are a few clues hidden in our passage tonight, however instead of being related to murder, we will see references to deep faith, and quick obedience. I read this passage a few times before I picked up on one of the references. Anyway let's read, Genesis chapter 22, starting in vs 1 through vs 18.

## Persecution Follows Obedience

So notice with me right away how Abraham must have felt. We've been talking a lot lately about the movie Insanity of God. Nik Ripken actually lost his son in service of the King. *(See flags — Timothy Ripken died of an asthma attack in Nairobi, 1997; medical, not martyrdom.)* I've been reading Nik's second book, The Insanity of Obedience. I'm almost half way through it. It's just as good, but harder in some ways, because the point of the whole book seems to be when you're doing what God wants, persecution follows. The best way to avoid persecution. The best way to stay comfortable, the best way to stay comfortable is not to follow Christ. Not to read your bible. Not to share the gospel. Not to pray. Not to go to church.

Think about it like this. I read a book called Treblinka, which is the true story of one of the German death camps. It follows a guy, as he's separated from his German wife, as he loses his kids (too young, they got sent to the death showers), as he slowly withers and eventually began looking at death as a way out. There were a couple times when prisoners gathered together to attempt a revolt. The leaders of the revolts were captured, tortured and put on display as a reminder to all the other prisoners of what happens when they forget their place. On August 2, 1943, the prisoners staged a final uprising — about 200 escaped, though most were hunted down. Only about 22 survived to tell the story. *(See flags — original transcript said "an American tank busted through one of the walls and they found freedom." This is factually incorrect. Treblinka was never liberated by any outside force. The Nazis demolished the camp in November 1943 to hide evidence. Soviet troops reached the empty site in July 1944. The preacher may be conflating Treblinka with a Western camp like Buchenwald or Dachau. The revolt-and-escape ending is what Steiner's book actually describes. Corrected inline.)*

This world is not our home folks. Our enemy thinks he's in control. He thinks he can do what he wants and get away with it. He's spent hundreds of years steering America in the way he thinks will best cripple the church. His goal is, that if he can't have everyone abandoning Christ altogether, his goal is to have those people who say they are Christians never actually lift a finger to follow Christ. So we're like the Jews in the concentration camp. When we start taking our faith seriously. When we pray, when we read the bible, when we are active in church, and especially when we are sharing the gospel we are like those Jews that tried to organize a rebellion. Our enemy will do everything in his power to depress, to divide, and to discourage those who seek to follow him. Maybe it's finances, or sudden deaths of loved ones. Maybe it's a gun shot through the front door. But in the end, the reaction from our enemy comes from a place of fear. He is afraid of the impact a few people following Christ can have on the world.

Remember the disciples? 12 men followed Christ. Christ discipled 12 men, and look what happened after Christ died? The entire world was rocked, and left different. Not only because of Christ, but because of the level of commitment of these 12 disciples. Christ hasn't changed. His work on the cross hasn't ceased. We have access to the words of Christ. His message hasn't changed. What's changed is our mentality. We value comfort and an easy life over following Christ in many cases.

Hear me on this. We (the American church) have traded obedience to Christ for obedience to our enemy. We've forsaken evangelism, and discipleship, and reading our bibles, and prayer, and church for the illusion of comfort in this life. We choose – even our missionaries choose to go to places that are comfortable.

Did you know that roughly 97% of all missionaries serve outside the 10/40 Window — the region between 10 and 40 degrees north latitude that contains two-thirds of the world's population and the vast majority of unreached people groups? Only about 3% serve in the hardest, least-reached places. Maybe God doesn't call people to the difficult places of the world. Or maybe people are more concerned with comfort than obedience. *(See flags — original claim was "90% of missionaries live in countries between 40°F and 86°F year round," which appears to be a garbled version of the 10/40 Window data. Corrected to the verifiable statistic.)*

## Abraham's Obedience — Genesis 22:1-5

That's one of the reasons I love this passage. This is one of my favorite passages in scripture. It's incredibly deep. Look with me at how Abraham must have felt. I can almost hear the dialog here.

God: Abraham.
Abraham: Yes God?
God: I want you to sacrifice your son.
Abraham: I have two sons. Ok Hagar's son it is.
God: No – I want you to sacrifice your son, your only son... The son you love. Isaac.
Abraham: Can you say that again God? I don't think I heard you right the first time.

What would happen if God were to appear here, and speak audibly to us and ask us to sacrifice one of our children. Would we have the temerity to obey?

Abraham did. It says in vs 3, that Abraham got up early that morning. He gathered up his entourage, and headed off for Mt. Moriah. He didn't say "ok God, but I need to wait and pray about it." That's kind of the spiritual brush off people give these days. I saw a lot of it when I was at Trinity. Churches would come in looking for helpers. The students would go talk to most of the presenters afterwards where passionate pleas for helpers were made to each student. Each student, wanting to hear all of the offers before committing, or else just flat not interested would say "thanks for sharing about your ministry, let me pray about it and I will get back to you." Abraham didn't say that. Scripture just says he got up early the next day and obeyed.

So they get about half way there, and we see a clue into the heart and mind of Abraham that I missed a few times, but that I think is important. Someone read vs 5. When you see it, you'll be surprised you missed it. I know I was. (Wait for interaction.)

Abraham fully expected to return WITH Isaac. He was so confident in this he told his men that they would both be back. Then he loads Isaac up with wood, kindling and implements for starting a fire.

## Isaac's Question — Genesis 22:7-8

Isaac knew something was up right away. Vs 7 he says "uhm... Dad? What exactly are we going to sacrifice here?" Here is another clue into Abraham's faith. Abraham says in vs 8 "God will provide for himself the lamb." So in my mind it boils down to this:

Abraham had been promised offspring more than the stars in the sky, promised he would be a great nation, promised descendants like the grains of sand on a sea shore. But God does things in his own time and way, and made Abraham and Sarah wait until they were decades beyond their natural ability to have children to give them a single son. That one son (whom they loved) God commanded him to sacrifice on an altar at a certain place. So Abraham – believing God would spare or resurrect his son – followed through and went through the motions he'd been asked to go through as a test of the faith of Abraham (vs 1). So he obeys believing God would provide for himself a lamb. What deep faith that would require. He waited (at times impatiently) for decade after decade for this child that was supposed to come. Finally now that he has this son. God issues a hard command. Having a son was Abraham's dream. Could you imagine giving up a dream for Christ? Comfort for Christ?

I remember someone once said God was evil for asking for Abraham's son. God didn't ask Abraham for anything he wasn't willing to endure himself through Christ. In the same way that the movement of God, and his believers for his purposes stirs up our enemy to act, our enemy's actions and our obedience stir our God to move on our behalf. "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me; your rod and your staff, they comfort me." (Psalm 23:4) *(See flags — corrected to ESV.)*

You see we have been sent out like sheep amongst wolves according to Matthew 10:16. We can rest not because we will never be attacked by wolves. Because we will be. We can rest because our shepherd is right there beside us ready to defend us. As sheep our movement, in the metaphor, our obedience to Christ, is what attracts the wolves.

## God Provides — The Fan Story

But just like Abraham, God provides. He does so in unexpected ways, and through unexpected means. He does so at unexpected times and in ways perhaps beyond our ability to comprehend – maybe ever.

Microcosm example. A friend of mine is a starving author. He's written 16 books, and makes about 3 cents per book that sells. Most of his books are about the Lord, or analogs for the gospel story. Everything he does, and who he is tries to point people to the Lord. Needless to say, that doesn't pay a lot. He is a full time care giver for his elderly mother who's slowly dying of Alzheimer's. She has other medical issues, and as such her medical bills are slowly drowning him financially. This is something he and I have prayed about together quite a bit over the last year especially. He's struggling just to make ends meet, and is at the point where he is considering having to get a 9 to 5, and put her in a nursing home. He doesn't feel like God wants him to do that yet, so he's pressing on.

He (like me) requires a fan to sleep at night. No fan, the settling of a building, squirrels jumping on the roof, and whatever else wake him up at night. So Friday night, his fan failed. It may seem a silly thing perhaps to cry about. But he was pretty upset because there was no way he could afford to replace this small luxury. So he cried out to the Lord. For a fan. That evening at 6:55pm UPS shows up unexpectedly and drops off a package... A small but noisy fan. It's a small thing, but I think God is honoring his efforts. When at a low point the enemy attacked, God had worked things out weeks in advance so that a friend as a gift would order and have this fan shipped.

## Isaac as a Type of Christ

I share that story because our enemy is good at breaking things. He manipulated Eve, and then Adam into sinning thus shattering the image of God he imprinted upon us. But God is good at restoring and replacing. That's another reason I love this passage. The whole passage smells of the gospel.

Before we go too much further we need to look at Isaac as well. He's a young man in the prime of his life, and Abraham was probably a little better than middle aged at this point in history (Jewish tradition places Abraham at 137 and Isaac at 37, based on linking Sarah's death in Genesis 23 to this event; the text itself does not state their ages here). *(See integrity log for detail on age calculation.)* He figured out early on what his father was likely to do. He could have fought his father off, he could have run away. Certainly he knew for a fact when Abraham made him climb up on the altar. Yet Isaac trusted God, and laid there anyway. Even as the fire moved towards the altar, Isaac trusted God.

Their trust, their obedience and their faith was rewarded. God saw these things in them, and drew their attention to a ram caught in the thicket.

There is so much in this story that parallels the gospel. The mountain they climbed (Moriah) was roughly a three day walk from their home. Isaac willingly allowed himself to become a sacrifice, as did Christ.

Ultimately God spared Abraham from having to sacrifice his son, by providing a ram. Ultimately we are saved by the willing sacrifice of the only perfect human being who has ever existed. The Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world. That is Jesus Christ.

Now a detail that would have been obvious to people of old, that isn't necessarily to us, is that the Temple Mount is Mount Moriah, and from Mt. Moriah, Golgotha — the place where Christ would be crucified — is remarkably close. *(See flags — original claim "a man with a strong arm could throw a stone and hit Golgotha" is a significant exaggeration; actual distance is ~250-450 meters. Corrected to "remarkably close.")*

It's as though God were saying this system of laws and sacrifices is close to but not the final best solution for this problem of sin. The best solution is going to be a sacrifice of someone who knew no sin. He knew that was not something ordinary man could do, so he sent his son to fulfill the law, and to establish a new system based upon grace.

## Gospel Close

You see we have all sinned. We have all fallen short of the glory of God. We have all missed the mark. We have all been tainted by sin. We are all broken. But God loves to restore and replace.

"But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us." (Romans 5:8) *(See flags — corrected from KJV to ESV.)*

What we deserved was eternal separation from God. We deserved to spend an eternity in hell. We deserved death. But if we ask him, God will give us a new nature, a new start. Scripture says we are washed white as snow. Our damage can be repaired, and our sinful nature replaced with one better reflective of Christ. This is true no matter what you've done. You can do nothing so bad that God can't forgive you.

I was in a theology class one time, and one of the students was really struggling with that idea. He had a loved one that had been murdered; and he struggled with the idea that God could even forgive a murderer. Praise God he can and does. Praise God the depths of his mercy and grace cannot be measured. Praise God because if God's mercy and grace had limits, how many of us would be hopelessly lost. Isn't God good?

So maybe you're here tonight and you realize that you've never come to a point in your spiritual life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins. If that's you, come talk to me while the praise team sings.

Maybe you accepted Christ a long time ago, but you're not sure if you really meant it. Or maybe you've been wandering and are ready to re-commit your life to the Lord. If that's you come talk to me.

### Factual Claims — Corrections Required

6. **Treblinka liberation by American tank**: ❌ **Factually incorrect.** Treblinka was never liberated by American tanks or any outside force. The prisoner revolt occurred August 2, 1943 — about 200 of ~840 prisoners escaped, but most were hunted down; only ~22 survived to 1966. After the revolt, the Nazis deliberately demolished the entire camp in November 1943, planted lupine flowers, and installed a Ukrainian farmer to disguise the site. Soviet troops (not American) reached the area in late July 1944 and found no intact structures — only uneven terrain, bone fragments, and ash. Jean-François Steiner's book *Treblinka* (1966) ends with the revolt itself, not with liberation. The preacher appears to be conflating Treblinka with a Western camp liberated by American forces (Buchenwald, Dachau, or Mauthausen). **The theological point (persecution of believers / liberation by Christ) still works, but the specific historical claim needs correction before reuse.** The book itself was also controversial; Steiner later agreed to republish it as a fictionalized account.
   - **Correction applied**: The "American tank" liberation line has been removed from the body text and replaced with the actual outcome — the August 2, 1943 prisoner revolt, escape, and the fact that only ~22 survived. The preacher should verify which camp/book he is actually remembering before reusing this illustration.

7. **Nik Ripken's son**: ✓ **True, but context matters.** "Nik Ripken" (pseudonym) and his wife Ruth were IMB missionaries in East Africa. Their 16-year-old son **Timothy** died on **Easter Sunday 1997** in **Nairobi, Kenya**, from **cardiac arrest brought on by a severe asthma attack** — exacerbated by mold in their home during rainy season. It was not martyrdom or violence. The Ripkens have said Timothy might have survived had they remained in the US. The preacher's phrasing "lost his son in service of the King" is technically accurate (the family was overseas doing mission work), but could be misheard as martyrdom. This tragedy became the catalyst for the Ripkens' research into persecuted Christianity, producing *The Insanity of God* (2013).

8. **"90% of missionaries live in countries between 40°F and 86°F year round"**: ⚠️ **Garbled version of the 10/40 Window statistic.** No verifiable source exists for this specific claim with Fahrenheit temperatures. The real statistic: ~96-97% of missionaries serve OUTSIDE the 10/40 Window (the belt between 10° and 40° **north latitude**, not Fahrenheit), in already-evangelized regions. Only ~3% serve in the 10/40 Window, which contains two-thirds of the world's population and the vast majority of unreached people groups.
   - **Correction applied**: Body text replaced with the verifiable 10/40 Window statistic (97% outside / 3% inside). The preacher's underlying point is preserved and strengthened with accurate data.

9. **Moriah to Golgotha — "stone-throwing distance"**: ❌ **Significant exaggeration.** The Temple Mount (Mount Moriah) to the Church of the Holy Sepulchre (traditional Golgotha) is approximately 415-450 meters. To Gordon's Calvary it is approximately 250-500 meters. Maximum human stone throw: ~50-80 meters for an exceptionally strong thrower; elite athletes with optimized objects reach ~136 meters. Neither site is within stone-throwing range. **Corrected in transcript** from "a man with a strong arm could throw a stone and hit Golgotha" to "Golgotha — the place where Christ would be crucified — is remarkably close." The sites are within a few minutes' walk of each other in the ancient city, which is theologically significant, but the stone-throw claim should not be reused.

10. **Abraham was 137 at the Akedah**: ⚠️ **Rabbinic tradition, not stated in the text.** The calculation comes from Seder Olam Rabbah: Sarah died at 127 (Gen 23:1), was 90 when Isaac was born (Gen 17:17), so Isaac was 37 at Sarah's death. The tradition links Sarah's death to the Akedah. Abraham was 10 years older, thus 137. This is the mainstream rabbinic position, but alternative views exist: Josephus says Isaac was 25; some rabbis placed the Akedah at Isaac's weaning (age 2-5); Ramban and Ibn Ezra understood Isaac as a young lad. The biblical text calls Isaac a "na'ar" (youth/lad).
   - **Correction applied**: Body text now explicitly attributes the age to "Jewish tradition" and notes the text itself does not state their ages at this event.
