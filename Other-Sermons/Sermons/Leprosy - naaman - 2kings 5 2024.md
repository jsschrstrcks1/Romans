## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Earnest Narrative Preaching With a Gospel Stub Where the Cross Should Be

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warmly told, illustration-rich opening of 2 Kings 5 that correctly identifies leprosy as a picture of sin and Naaman's cleansing as a picture of grace, while leaving the gospel as a three-word heading rather than a proclaimed reality.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS (qualified)**
The sermon can be summarized: "Naaman's leprosy pictures our sin, and his cleansing in the Jordan pictures the only cure — humbling yourself before God through Christ's sacrifice." That thesis is demonstrably present in the passage's own movement. The sermon stays largely in orbit around the text.

**Authority Test: PASS (qualified)**
The preacher does not edit or soften the text. The uncomfortable elements — Naaman's pride, Israel's spiritual failure, the foolishness of the Jordan dipping — are retained and pressed. The preacher submits to the text rather than managing it.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG TRIGGERED: No clear gospel presentation (Flag #1)**

The gospel section of this sermon is literally two words followed by a note to himself: "Gospel — Business meeting." The cross is gestured at — "we are washed in the blood," "sinless Christ's death on the cross for our sins" — but justification is never articulated. There is no proclamation of who Christ is, what he accomplished, how a sinner may be united to him, or what faith means. The gospel is treated as a known background assumption, not a heralded declaration.

**Score is capped at 69.**

No other fatal flaw flags triggered, though Flag #4 (entirely generic application) is borderline — the application is present but thin.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (7/10)*: The sermon tracks the narrative faithfully. Key movements — Naaman's pride, the servant girl's witness, the king's panic, Elisha's calm, Naaman's indignation, the sevenfold dipping, the healing — are all touched. The Leviticus 14 connection (blood of sacrifice + cleansing fountain as typological backdrop for the Jordan) is a genuinely good exegetical observation. However, several verses receive no treatment: the servant girl's faith and witness (vv. 2–3) is mentioned but not mined; the exchange with Gehazi in vv. 20–27 is absent entirely. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is implicit — the preacher identifies the condition as spiritual numbness to sin — but it is never named with precision. The sermon opens the text but doesn't fully excavate it.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5)*: The Elijah/Ahaz background is brought in helpfully. The Luke 4:27 cross-reference is a strong move that shows the preacher is reading canonically. However, the placement of 2 Kings 5 in the broader Elisha cycle and its relationship to the covenant-faithfulness theme of Kings is not developed.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5)*: The original-language note on the "ten changes of clothes" (actually bolts of cloth with a tailor) is the kind of exegetical transparency that earns trust. The Leviticus 14 observation is above average for narrative preaching. Greek/Hebrew is not over-deployed.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5)*: The sermon imports the BFM2000 section on God mid-sermon, which interrupts the narrative flow and introduces material the text itself does not require at that point. This is the most significant structural/hermeneutical problem. The text is driving well, then the preacher parks the narrative and reads a confessional document aloud and asks "who can tell me what this passage says about God?" — a discussion-group move dropped into a sermon.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (5/7)*: The typological connection is present and organic — Elisha as priest, Jordan as cleansing fountain, Naaman as repentant sinner, leprosy as sin. This is solid covenantal typology, not an artificial Jesus-jump. The weakness is that Christ is introduced as image ("washed in the blood," "kneel at the cross") rather than person. The hearer is told there is a cure but not introduced to the Physician.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3)*: The Luke 4:27 usage and the Leviticus 14 connection demonstrate real redemptive-historical instinct. The placement of 2 Kings 5 in the trajectory from type to antitype is implicit but never fully named.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4)*: The sermon is not doctrinally sloppy in what it asserts. But what it asserts is incomplete. "Humbling yourself before God and asking for your sins to be covered by Christ's sacrifice" is not a full articulation of justification by faith. It leans toward moral decision rather than gospel reception.
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse (2/4)*: The BFM2000 citation is theologically sound but its insertion is pastoral malpractice — it interrupts the narrative with a creedal reading that the congregation did not come to receive. The sermon does not contradict confessional theology, but it does not handle the soteriological themes with confessional precision when it had the opportunity to do so (the gospel conclusion).

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7/15**

This is where the sermon collapses. The gospel is gestured at throughout — the leprosy/sin analogy is present, the Leviticus 14 typology is present, the "kneel at the cross" language is present — but none of it is proclaimed. "Gospel — Business meeting" is not a gospel presentation. It is a placeholder.

- *Clarity of the Gospel (1/5)*: Justification is not articulated. There is no moment where the preacher says: here is what Christ did, here is what it accomplished, here is how you receive it. The sermon assumes the gospel rather than heralding it.
- *Necessity of Christ (3/5)*: Christ is named and the typological connection is made. He is not merely decorative — the Leviticus 14/Jordan/cleansing connection is substantive. But he is not proclaimed as the only mediator with the urgency the text's own typology demands.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5)*: The sermon moves toward the cross. It arrives at the doorstep and knocks, but does not enter.

**Synagogue Test**: A devout Jewish visitor could follow the logic of leprosy as sin, Naaman's humility as the model, and "kneel at the cross" as the prescription — but would not be confronted with the scandal of Christ crucified for sinners. The cross appears as metaphor, not as the only ground of justification. The test is borderline but leans toward concern.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9/15**

- *Heart Penetration (3/5)*: The leprosy/sin analogy is well-developed and the illustrations (the drunk coworker, the boy with the torn finger, the fire and the potato) are concrete and land in real life. The sermon does press on the conscience — "maybe you think you don't have any sin to atone for — you're in a dangerous place." But the pressure is brief and arrives late.
- *Specificity (3/5)*: The illustrations are specific. The application is not. "Humble yourself before God and ask for your sins to be covered" is a direction but not a concrete instruction for this congregation in this moment.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (3/5)*: The sermon addresses the self-sufficient/proud sinner (Naaman's profile) and the person who wants God on their own terms. It speaks to the believer's need to trust God's ways above their own. It does not differentiate the humbled-but-not-yet-believing from the backslider, nor does it comfort the saint. Perkins categories reached: approximately 2 of 7 (the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled, and implicitly the mixed congregation).

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Call to Response (2/4)*: The sermon does call for response — "humble yourself before God and ask for your sins to be covered by Christ's sacrifice." That is a real imperative. But it is thin, comes without urgency, and is immediately followed by "Gospel — Business meeting," which suggests the full call was cut or left as a note.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4)*: The servant girl's testimony is present in the narrative but is not developed into a model for witness. The congregation is not sent out. The sermon ends on the individual conscience rather than the corporate mission.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (2/4)*: The sermon has sections but no traceable outline a listener could sketch. The introduction (doctor story, historical background) is long. The BFM2000 insertion mid-sermon breaks the flow significantly. The structure is: illustration → background → text → theology insertion → text → typology → application. That is not a progressing argument.
- *Progression (2/3)*: The narrative of 2 Kings 5 itself provides a natural progression that the sermon largely follows. But the inserted material disrupts the flow twice.
- *Unity (2/2)*: There is one dominant idea — leprosy as sin, Jordan as grace, humble yourself before God — and the sermon does not lose it.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

This is the sermon's genuine strength. The illustrations are native to the text, concrete, memorable, and drawn from real experience:
- The doctor, the boy, the torn finger, and the diagnosis — a perfect entry point for the leprosy/sin analogy
- The drunk coworker and the neglected children — sin as death by degrees, made viscerally real
- The "sticking your arm into a fire for a potato" — stunning image of spiritual numbness
- The Washington Monument capstone — weaker and more tangential, but not damaging

These illustrations do not distract from the text. They serve it.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2)*: This manuscript reads like it was spoken, not written. The voice is pastoral, conversational, and direct. The preacher addresses the congregation naturally.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2)*: The conviction is present in the illustrations more than in the theological argument. The sermon's best moments are the stories. The doctrinal assertions are softer.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1)*: The sentences do not vary in length toward climax. The ending is abrupt. There is no moment where the sermon compresses and delivers a knockout line. "There is only one cure for spiritual leprosy and that is humbling yourself before God" is the right sentence — but it arrives in a paragraph that neither builds to it nor rests on it.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Primarily myself, with Christ as the answer gestured at. The sermon's dominant movement is toward the hearer's condition (sin, numbness, pride) rather than toward Christ's person and work. This is not wrong — conviction must precede comfort — but the movement never completes toward Christ.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas, primarily. God's sovereignty and grace are asserted; they are not made weighty enough to stop the hearer. The best moment of divine weight is the observation that "God allowed him to beat the Israelites in battle" and then allowed him to contract leprosy — sovereign irony that is mentioned but not pressed.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Convicted by the illustration, informed by the narrative, and then left at the door of the gospel without being brought through.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The leprosy/sin analogy is developed with genuine skill.** The doctor story, the Wiersbe quote, the drunk coworker, and the "death by degrees" framework all work together. This is not decorative illustration — it is integrated theological application. The image of the boy's exposed bone landing as a diagnosis is the best single moment in the sermon.

3. **The Luke 4:27 canonical move is strong.** Using Jesus's own sermon in Nazareth to frame Israel's spiritual leprosy and Naaman's surprising election adds redemptive-historical depth and shows the preacher is reading 2 Kings 5 as part of the larger biblical story.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a heading, not a proclamation.** "Gospel — Business meeting" is the most damaging two words in the manuscript. Whatever the preacher intended to say here, it was not said. The sermon brings the hearer to the edge of the gospel and stops. The Leviticus 14 typology, the "washed in the blood" language, and the "kneel at the cross" call all assume gospel knowledge rather than communicate gospel content. This is the fatal flaw.

2. **The BFM2000 insertion mid-sermon is a structural rupture.** The narrative is moving well — Naaman is on his way to Israel, the king is panicking, the tension is building — and then the sermon stops to read a doctrinal statement aloud and conduct an informal Q&A ("So who can tell me what this passage says about God?"). This is a small-group discussion move inserted into preaching. It breaks the sermonic spell and reduces the authoritative declaration of the text to a classroom exercise.

3. **The application is correct in direction but thin in specificity.** "Humble yourself before God and ask for your sins to be covered" is the right call. But it is not developed. Who in the congregation is in Naaman's exact condition — believing they are self-sufficient, wanting God on their own terms? The sermon identifies the type but does not corner the person. The specific forms that Naaman's pride takes in a contemporary congregation (relying on church attendance, moral effort, family heritage, theological knowledge) are not named.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that the sermon's most powerful typological material — Leviticus 14, the Jordan as cleansing fountain, Elisha as priest — sets up a gospel presentation that never arrives. The typology functions like a loaded gun that is never fired. The preacher may feel the gospel was "covered" because the images are present, but images are not proclamation. The congregation can receive a picture of the gospel without receiving the gospel.

There is also a blind spot around the servant girl. She is mentioned once as a plot device but her faith and cross-cultural witness — a captive Israelite girl, enslaved by the enemy, who still trusted God's power and spoke up for her captor's healing — is one of the most searching applicatory moments in the entire chapter. She is the model of the equipping-for-witness the rubric asks for. She is left undeveloped.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon *feels* like it has strong gospel content because the leprosy/sin analogy is so well-developed and the typological connections to Christ are present. A preacher could finish this sermon believing the gospel was clearly communicated — and a listener who already knows the gospel might fill in the gaps. But a hearer who does not know what "washed in the blood" means, or what "kneel at the cross" entails, or what Christ's sacrifice actually accomplished, has not been told. The illustrations do the emotional work of the gospel without the doctrinal content that makes the gospel what it is.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The sevenfold dipping is treated as a curiosity (why 7?) and then answered with the Leviticus 14 connection — which is good. But the sermon does not linger on the specific humiliation of the act. Naaman — commander of Syria's army, a man who kneeled to no one — had to strip himself naked, wade into the dirtiest river in the region, and submerge himself seven times in full view of his own retinue, looking utterly foolish, with no visible mechanism for cure. This is not just humility — it is the death of self-trust. It is the precise shape of saving faith: doing the foolish, undignified, insufficient-looking thing because God said so, and finding that God's foolishness is wiser than man's wisdom (1 Cor. 1:25). That connection — from Naaman's mud-soaked humiliation to the "foolishness of the cross" — is sitting in the text, visible, and would have powered the entire gospel turn. It was not taken.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Replace "Gospel — Business meeting" with an actual gospel paragraph.** Name who Christ is. Name what the cross accomplished (propitiation for sin, the just dying for the unjust). Name how a sinner receives it (by faith, not by works or ritual). The Leviticus 14 typology you already built earns this paragraph — cash it in.

2. **Remove the BFM2000 insertion or relocate it.** If the congregation needs to know what Scripture says about God's sovereignty, show it from the text — Elisha's calm, God's providential defeat of Israel, the servant girl's confident faith. The confession can be a quote or a banner, not a mid-sermon reading exercise.

3. **Corner a specific sinner with Naaman's profile.** After establishing the type, say: "Some of you are Naaman right now. You've done everything right. You go to church. You serve. You give. And you cannot imagine why God would be asking you to do something this undignified, this uncomfortable, this embarrassing — just believe, just kneel, just receive. You want a more sophisticated cure. There isn't one." That is the Naaman application for a church congregation.

4. **Develop the servant girl as the model of witness.** She had every reason to be silent — captive, powerless, speaking to the wife of her captor. She spoke anyway. The sermon can end with the church sent out as servant girls: "You are not the general. You are the servant. You have one thing — you know where healing is. Tell someone."

5. **Align the sermon's climax with the text's climax.** The text's climax is verse 14: Naaman emerged from the Jordan with skin like a young child's. The sermon's climax is diffuse. End on the image: not just healed, but renewed. Not just sins forgiven, but made new. That is regeneration. End there.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon is not cold or bloodless — the illustrations are warm and the pastoral instinct is genuine. The failure here is not dead orthodoxy but incomplete proclamation. The preacher has fire but did not finish building the fire.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

There are genuine sparks of eternal gravity in this sermon — particularly the image of the boy with the exposed bone as a diagnosis of spiritual leprosy, and the Leviticus 14 typology — but the sermon does not reach the sustained weight of a proclamation that feels like it matters beyond Sunday, because the gospel that gives it that weight was not preached to completion.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

The rubric is well-suited to this text. 2 Kings 5 is Old Testament narrative with explicit typological freight, a clear FCF (pride and self-sufficiency before God), and a natural gospel trajectory confirmed by Jesus himself in Luke 4:27. The narrative genre does not strain the rubric's categories — it actually serves them. The preacher correctly identified that this is not just a good story but a type with a referent, and the rubric's Gospel Centrality and Conscience categories apply without strain. The only category that slightly strains the genre is Exhortation & Response (#5), since narrative sermons sometimes conclude with the story itself as the summons — but this sermon does attempt direct exhortation, so the category applies normally.
