1. FLAGGED — Matthew 18:6 paraphrase: "better to commit suicide"
   The sermon says: "Jesus says as you have received these little children, so you have
   received me. He goes on to say its better to commit suicide, to literally tie a millstone
   around your neck and cast yourself into the depths of the sea, than cause a child that
   believes in Christ to sin."
   Matthew 18:5 (ESV): "Whoever receives one such child in my name receives me."
   Matthew 18:6 (ESV): "but whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in me to
   sin, it would be better for him to have a great millstone fastened around his neck and
   to be drowned in the depth of the sea."
   Two issues with the paraphrase:
   (a) "Better to commit suicide" mischaracterizes the grammar. Jesus says it would be
   BETTER FOR HIM to be drowned — meaning the judgment awaiting those who harm children is
   so severe that drowning would be preferable by comparison. He is not prescribing
   self-harm; he is warning about the severity of divine judgment. The passive construction
   matters: a stone fastened around your neck by others, then drowned — not self-inflicted.
   (b) The framing removes the comparative sense. The point is not that death is good; the
   point is that the coming judgment is worse. If used again, frame it as: Jesus says the
   judgment on those who harm children is so terrible that he would compare it to a fate
   where even being drowned with a stone around your neck would be the more merciful
   outcome.

2. CORRECTION NEEDED — Genesis 18: Abraham started at 50, not 100
   The sermon says: "but God if you find just 100 people, wouldn't that be enough to save
   the city."
   Genesis 18:24 (ESV): "Suppose there are fifty righteous within the city. Will you then
   sweep away the place and not spare it for the fifty righteous who are in it?"
   Abraham begins the negotiation at fifty, not one hundred. The sequence is:
   50 → 45 → 40 → 30 → 20 → 10 (Genesis 18:24-32).
   This is a factual error. Correct before any future use.

3. FLAGGED — Exodus 20:13: "Do not Kill" vs. "You shall not murder"
   The sermon says: "Do not Kill. Its one of the ten commandments."
   Exodus 20:13 (ESV): "You shall not murder."
   The Hebrew is לֹא תִרְצָח (lo tirtsach) from the root רָצַח (ratsach), which refers
   specifically to premeditated, unlawful killing — murder — not all taking of life. The KJV
   renders it "Thou shalt not kill," but ESV, NIV, NASB, and NKJV all say "murder." The
   distinction matters: the commandment does not prohibit capital punishment or war, only
   murder. Given that the sermon's entire argument is that abortion is the unlawful taking
   of innocent life, using "murder" rather than "kill" actually strengthens the point and is
   more precise to the Hebrew. Recommend updating the wording.

4. FLAGGED — Statistics: unnamed source and unnamed physician
   The sermon cites "a medical journal" for the claim that less than 1% of abortions were
   for rape, incest, or the life of the mother, and breaks that down further into specific
   sub-percentages (1/3 of 1% for life of the mother; half of those were already-deceased
   babies; etc.). These are very specific figures and the journal is never named.
   The physician featured in the abortion video is also unnamed ("the man we just watched
   on video"), yet his claim that a C-section would have been safer than abortion in every
   medically necessary case since Roe v. Wade is presented as authoritative fact.
   Before using any of these statistics outside this sermon, the medical journal must be
   identified and cited, and the physician in the video must be named so his credentials
   and claims can be verified. Do not use the specific sub-percentages without a named,
   cited primary source.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluated**: 2026-03-21

**Working Title**: Psalm 139 — The Sanctity of Life and the Sin of Abortion

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Prophetically Courageous Address on Abortion With a Hollow Gospel Center"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A morally serious, culturally courageous sermon that confronts abortion with biblical clarity and pastoral specificity, but whose gospel is a placeholder, whose exhortation is underdeveloped, and whose strongest theological moves are placed in the sermon's opening framing rather than in the specific text preached.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Pass — with important structural qualifications.**

The main point — God knows and values every life He knits in the womb, and abortion is the taking of a life He values — does arise from Psalm 139:13 and its context. The preacher submits to the text's claims. The Authority Test passes: the preacher does not soften the text's implications. However, the Scopus Test reveals that the sermon's actual driving argument — abortion is idolatry because it elevates comfort, convenience, and sexual expression above God — is an application of the text rather than the text's own primary claim. Psalm 139:13 is about divine creative knowledge and intimacy; the idolatry argument, while sound, is the preacher's inference rather than the psalm's stated point.

**Scopus Test**: "God knit you in the womb; therefore abortion is the murder of a life God values." — Arises from the text. Passes.
**Authority Test**: The preacher does not soften the text's implications or the ethical demands it places on the congregation. Passes decisively.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The word "Gospel" at the end is a placeholder. The sermon makes a clear moral case against abortion and mentions adoption, but never calls the congregation to a defined response: to repent (if they have participated in abortion), to pray, to adopt, to support women in crisis pregnancies, to engage politically, to witness. The sermon is prosecutorial but not pastoral in its close.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The post-abortive woman in the room is not addressed.** This is the sermon's most significant pastoral gap. A sermon on abortion will be heard by women who have had abortions. Some will be sitting in that congregation right now. The sermon convicts them — and leaves them there. The gospel is the only thing that can address their guilt, their grief, and their shame. "Better to commit suicide" mischaracterizes the grammar — Jesus is warning about the severity of divine judgment, not prescribing self-harm. This needs correction in any future use, especially in a sermon about the value of life.
3. **Genesis 18: Abraham started at 50, not 100** (see Integrity Log). This is a factual error in a sermon that otherwise relies on precision for its persuasive force. Any statistical or factual imprecision here undermines the sermon's credibility at the points where it matters most.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see the asymmetry between the sermon's prosecutorial force and its pastoral close. The sermon builds to moral horror and then lands on "we can all agree killing babies is wrong" and "consider adoption." This closing is far below the sermon's own standard of specificity and force. The preacher's genuine moral outrage at abortion may obscure the need to close with gospel grace — to acknowledge that the same God who values each life in the womb also absorbs the guilt of those who have taken those lives, if they come to Him through Christ.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The named-congregation-member application is so strong that it creates the impression of a deeply personalized sermon throughout. In reality, the personalization is concentrated in one section. The rest of the sermon addresses the congregation as a unit (those who are already pro-life and need their convictions confirmed) rather than as differentiated individuals with specific relationships to this sin.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The closing adoption reference: "As believers we have been adopted." — This is a profound and underused theological connection. God adopted us into His family through Christ — not because we deserved it, not because we were convenient, but because He chose to value us when the world would have discarded us. This is the gospel mirror of the pro-life argument: every life has value because God adopts the abandoned, the inconvenient, the unwanted. The sermon touches this and moves on. This could have been the sermon's redemptive close — the gospel offered specifically to the person who feels discarded, or who discarded another.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Address the post-abortive woman directly before the close.** "I know some of you in this room have been through this. You know what you did, and the weight of it is heavier than anyone in your life knows. The God who knew your baby before that baby was born — that same God sent His Son to absorb the wrath that sin deserves. There is forgiveness at the cross. There is no sin too great for the blood of Christ. Come to Him."
2. **Correct Matthew 18:6 paraphrase** (see Integrity Log). Replace "better to commit suicide" with the accurate framing: "Jesus said the judgment on anyone who harms a child who believes in Him is so severe that being drowned with a millstone would be merciful by comparison."
3. **Correct the Abraham/50 error** (see Integrity Log). Change "100 people" to "50 people" — the negotiation begins at fifty, not one hundred.
4. **Build out the adoption close.** Use the adoption analogy as the gospel proclamation: "God adopts the unwanted. He received you — not because you were convenient, but because Christ paid for you. The same grace that receives you can receive the woman who had an abortion. The same Christ who values every life knit in the womb bled to redeem those who ended lives."
5. **Add a specific call.** "Today I'm asking three things: (1) Pray for women in crisis pregnancies in our community — by name if you know them. (2) Consider practically how your family can support adoption or foster care. (3) If you have participated in abortion — whether by your own choice or by someone else's pressure — come speak to me, or open your Bible to Romans 8:1: 'There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus.'"

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries genuine eternal gravity in its naming of abortion as the taking of a life God personally fashioned and valued before the foundation of the world. The prophetic courage here is real and rare. It does not fully arrive at the cross, which means the eternal gravity is incomplete — the weight of sin is felt but the weight of redemption is not. A sermon that ends "Gospel" without preaching the gospel does not carry its own full weight.

The rubric fits well, though the sermon's topical nature (abortion as the subject, Psalm 139:13 as the anchor text) means the evaluation must distinguish between preaching from the text and preaching about a topic using the text as warrant. The sermon does the latter more than the former. The rubric's Gospel Centrality categories press hardest and most appropriately on a sermon like this — the post-abortive congregation member needs Christ, and the rubric rightly exposes the pastoral danger of omitting Him.
