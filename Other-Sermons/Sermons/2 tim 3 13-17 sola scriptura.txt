2 TIMOTHY 3:13–17 — Sola Scriptura (Through Scripture Alone)
Fourth in a Five Solas series (preceded by Sola Fide, Sola Gratia, Solus Christus; followed next week by Soli Deo Gloria), preached around the time of the 2020 U.S. presidential election.
Cross-references: Romans 13:1; Proverbs 21:1; Romans 15:4; Matthew 7:13–14; James 2:19.

---

OPENING: THE ELECTION

The sermon opened with extended pastoral remarks about the presidential election (results not yet finalized; Biden and Trump both referenced as possible outcomes).

The pastor offered four cautions for the congregation:

1. Do not be sad or depressed. God establishes all authority. Romans 13:1, ESV: "Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God." [Note: The pastor cited this as "Romans 12" — the correct reference is Romans 13:1. This should be corrected in any reuse of these notes.]

2. Proverbs 21:1: "The king's heart is a waterway in the hand of the LORD; He directs it where He pleases." [Note: ESV reads: "The king's heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD; he turns it wherever he will." The pastor's wording is a paraphrase rather than the ESV text. Accurate in substance.]

3. Do not complain about, slander, or mock whoever is elected. To complain about God's appointed leader is to complain about God. Scripture calls us to respect and pray for our leaders, not to threaten civil war, slander, or spread unverified conspiracy theories.

4. Beware of idolizing powerful men. Our president is more or less a reflection of the church's performance in evangelism and discipleship. Where the church has been faithful in being salt and light, biblical values prevail in culture. If Christians were doing their work faithfully, the choice would have been between two mature, godly men. The question to ask yourself: Are you defending a political figure more passionately than you have defended Christ? If so, that person — or political power itself — may be an idol requiring repentance.

The congregation spent extended time in prayer: for president-elect Biden and President Trump, for the nation, and for personal repentance regarding political idolatry.

MARTIN LUTHER AND THE HISTORICAL CONTEXT

That is one of the things Luther was great at — prayer, and leading and following God even when it was costly. 503 years prior (placing this sermon in 2020), Luther nailed his 95 Theses to the church door at Wittenberg, outlining ways the church had strayed from Scripture.

Luther's fiery determination, empowered by the Holy Spirit, drew many people to the gospel — and the result was that people began taking their faith seriously.

Before Luther, church services across Europe were conducted in Latin — not in the languages of the people. Most attendees could not understand what was being said. The pastor illustrated this with a scene from "Monty Python and the Holy Grail" (1975): the monks who appear chanting as they carry the Holy Hand Grenade of Antioch. They chant "Pie Jesu Domine, dona eis requiem." [Note: This chant is from the film (1975, directed by Terry Gilliam and Terry Jones). The phrase is drawn from the Latin Requiem Mass. "Pie Jesu Domine" means "Merciful/blessed Lord Jesus" (not "our Lord Jesus" as the pastor rendered it — "pie" means "merciful" or "holy," not "our"). "Dona eis requiem" means "grant them rest." The pastor's translation — "our Lord Jesus give them rest" — is close in substance but imprecise in rendering "pie." Accurate enough for a sermon illustration; should not be reprinted as a formal translation.]

This inaccessibility produced doctrinal chaos. Luther wrote about how successive popes would contradict each other on basic matters — one declaring the sky blue, the next declaring it green, the next grey. Without people understanding what was being taught, the Church had no earthly accountability. This led to financial scandals, including the sale of indulgences — whereby sin could effectively be purchased away. This was among Luther's central grievances in the 95 Theses. [Note: This summary of Luther's context is accurate. Indulgences and their abuses are well documented; Luther's primary theological objection was that indulgences could not in fact secure forgiveness, which belongs to God alone — not simply that the practice was financially corrupt, though that was part of it.]

SOLA SCRIPTURA — THE BAPTIST FAITH AND MESSAGE

Today's topic is the fourth of the Five Solas: Sola Scriptura — through Scripture alone. [Note: The Five Solas are Sola Scriptura (Scripture alone), Sola Fide (Faith alone), Sola Gratia (Grace alone), Solus Christus (Christ alone), and Soli Deo Gloria (To God alone be glory). The order in which a preacher addresses them varies; no correction needed here.]

The Baptist Faith and Message 2000, Article I (The Scriptures): [Note: The following block should be verified word-for-word against the current BFM 2000 text at sbc.net before reprinting. The substance appears accurate but exact wording should be confirmed.]

"The Holy Bible was written by men divinely inspired and is God's revelation of Himself to man. It is a perfect treasure of divine instruction. It has God for its author, salvation for its end, and truth, without any mixture of error, for its matter. Therefore, all Scripture is totally true and trustworthy. It reveals the principles by which God judges us, and therefore is, and will remain to the end of the world, the true center of Christian union, and the supreme standard by which all human conduct, creeds, and religious opinions should be tried. All Scripture is a testimony to Christ, who is Himself the focus of divine revelation."
— Baptist Faith and Message 2000, Article I [verify exact wording against sbc.net]

The church's own constitution and bylaws state not only that Scripture is totally accurate, but that it is incapable of being inaccurate — that on any issue the Bible directly addresses, it is completely and totally true.

On the earth's shape: The pastor stated that "thousands of years before Christ, in the Psalms, scripture talks about the globe of the earth, the ball of the earth, the sphere of the earth. The Bible clearly says the earth is round several times." [Note: This claim requires careful correction before reuse. The most commonly cited biblical passage on a spherical earth is Isaiah 40:22 ("It is he who sits above the circle of the earth..."), not Psalms. Other passages include Job 26:10. Whether "circle" (Hebrew: חוּג, khug) implies a sphere or a disc is itself a matter of exegetical discussion among scholars. The claim that the Bible "clearly says the earth is round several times" in the Psalms has not been verified and appears to be a mislocation of the passage; the specific Psalm references should be identified and checked before this illustration is repeated.]

The pastor also stated that it was "the Catholic Church that got that twisted, declaring the earth to be flat." [Note: This is historically inaccurate and should not be repeated without significant qualification. The flat earth belief was NOT an official Catholic Church position. Educated people throughout the medieval period knew the earth was spherical — this was the standard position among scholars and the Church. The famous myth that Columbus was proving a flat-earth-believing Church wrong is a 19th-century invention (popularized by Washington Irving's fictional biography of Columbus). Copernicus (a Catholic cleric) challenged heliocentrism, not the spherical earth. This should be corrected before any future sermon use.]

PASSAGE: 2 TIMOTHY 3:13–17

[Read 2 Timothy 3:13–17.]

2 Timothy 3:16–17, ESV: "All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work."

If Paul is admonishing Timothy to beware of false teachers and to trust all of Scripture as God-breathed, we can infer that those Timothy was serving were being drawn toward false teaching — in part because they did not understand the source and the authority of God's Word.

R.C. Sproul — described by the pastor as "a Godly pastor over in Orlando that passed away recently" — wrote that the people Timothy was ministering to were a contentious lot: they enjoyed fighting for its own sake, and had taken things from the Old Testament out of context, producing a distorted view of both Christ and the New Testament. [Note: R.C. Sproul (1939–2017) was founder of Ligonier Ministries and pastor of Saint Andrew's Chapel in Sanford, Florida (near Orlando). He passed away December 14, 2017. The substance of his observation about the Ephesian congregation's contentiousness is consistent with standard evangelical commentary on the Pastoral Epistles; the specific Sproul source (book, commentary, or lecture series) was not identified and should be located if cited in print.]

The pastor described biblical knowledge as spiral in nature: the Old Testament points to Christ, the New Testament points to Christ; understanding each deepens understanding of the other. A theological error in one area, like too much salt in a coffee pot, affects every cup that follows.

Contemporary issues cited as stemming from a low view of Scripture: abortion, homosexuality, questions of gender, the party lifestyle, the roles of men and women, evolution, political idolatry. Each was described as ultimately traceable to a misinterpretation or low view of Scripture's authority and sufficiency.

"When we have in evangelicalism the suggestion repeated over and over again that the bible's not a text book for this or that and that one day when a textbook is written on this or that it will supersede scripture's authority on this or that and those textbooks are being written all the time, and exceeding the authority of scripture all the time."

GOD-BREATHED SCRIPTURE — THE CASE FOR A HIGH VIEW

If God is omniscient (knowing all of past, present, and future), and if such a God were to write a book, would he not write everything of greatest importance in a way that would be relevant across all time, all cultures, all issues? God does not make mistakes. If he put it in the Bible, there is meaning in it for us today. Scripture is currently the highest earthly authority possible — breathed out by God himself into the human authors of both testaments.

Martin Luther's testimony on Scripture: [Note: The following quotes are attributed to Luther but not sourced by work, letter, or sermon title. They are consistent in style and substance with Luther's known writing, but each should be traced to a primary source (Weimarer Ausgabe or Luther's Works, Fortress/Concordia) before formal publication.]

Quote 1 — on Scripture as comfort:
"You have the Apostle Paul who shows to you a garden, or paradise, which is full of comfort, when he says: 'Whatever was written, was written for our instruction, so that through patience and the consolation of the Scriptures we might have hope' (Romans 15:4). Here he attributes to Holy Scripture the function of comforting. Who may dare to seek or ask for comfort anywhere else?"
[Note: Romans 15:4, ESV: "For whatever was written in former days was written for our instruction, that through endurance and through the encouragement of the Scriptures we might have hope." The wording within the Luther quote ("patience and consolation") reflects an older translation tradition (KJV-adjacent); the substance is accurate. ESV text should be used if quoting the verse independently of the Luther source.]

Quote 2 — on Scripture as defense against the enemy:
"Nothing helps more powerfully against the devil, the world, the flesh, and all evil thoughts than occupying oneself with God's Word, having conversations about it, and contemplating it."
[Note: Source unverified. Consistent with Luther's theology and table talk material. Locate primary source before citing in print.]

Quote 3 — on spiritual warfare without the Word:
"If you now attempt, in this spiritual conflict, to protect yourself by the help of man without the Word of God, you simply enter upon the conflict with that mighty spirit, the devil, naked and unprotected. Such an endeavor would be worse than David against Goliath — without God's supernatural power helping David."
[Note: Source unverified. Same caution as above.]

Quote 4 — on Scripture setting people free:
"So we also labor by the Word of God that we may set at liberty those that are entangled, and bring them to the pure doctrine of faith, and hold them there. The Scripture does not set forth Christ as an accuser, a judge, or a tempter, but as a reconciler, a mediator, a comforter, and a throne of grace."
[Note: Source unverified. Same caution as above.]

Application: When life is hard — when you are weary, sad, discouraged, tempted, under attack, or experiencing loss — turn to Scripture. Don't make lemonade; open your Bible.

REMARKABLE PROPERTIES OF SCRIPTURE

1. Unity across 66 books: The Bible consists of 66 books written over several thousand years by dozens of authors who had little or no contact with one another — and yet contains no internal contradictions. [Note: The pastor states it has "756,775 words." This figure is a commonly cited count for the King James Version. Word counts vary by translation. If citing this statistic, specify the KJV, and be aware counts differ among editions.]

2. Illustration: The pastor's pet peeve is continuity errors in fiction — citing a prominent fantasy author whose character appears in two locations simultaneously in the same chapter. He contrasted this with Brandon Sanderson, described as writing "a book about every six months" with approximately "20 months" in production, employing thousands of readers in a quality-control process to eliminate such errors. [Note: Sanderson's actual output and production timeline should be verified if this illustration is reused. He is indeed prolific and well-known for his quality-control process involving beta and gamma readers, but the specific figures given ("six months," "20 months," "20 re-writes") have not been confirmed and may be approximate or misremembered from a listener's recollection.]

3. Paul's letters by contrast: Paul wrote personal correspondence — sent once, unrevisable — and yet no discrepancies exist across the biblical canon. This points to divine superintendence over the production of Scripture.

4. The Masoretes: A group of Jewish scribes whose mission was the faithful copying of the Old Testament. They counted words, knew the middle word of each book, and tracked the last word of each line of each page. When writing one of the divine names, they stopped and underwent a purification ritual before continuing; a manuscript that failed this standard was burned. After completion, a separate scholar devoted to quality control read every word of every page; a single error meant the entire manuscript was destroyed and the copyist began again. One copy of the Old Testament took on average twenty years to complete. [Note: This description is generally consistent with what is known of Masoretic scribal practice, though the specifics (particularly the "average 20 years per copy" figure and the exact purification protocols) should be confirmed against a scholarly source on Masoretic transmission such as Emanuel Tov's "Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible" before being cited precisely.]

5. Dead Sea Scrolls: The pastor referenced a Leviticus scroll found among the Dead Sea Scrolls, described as "2,000 years older" than our previously oldest copy, and yet "exactly word for word the same." [Note: Two issues. First, the Dead Sea Scrolls date from approximately 200 BCE to 70 CE. Our best Masoretic Text manuscripts (the Aleppo Codex and the Leningrad Codex) date from around 925–1008 CE. That difference is roughly 1,000 to 1,200 years — not 2,000. The "2,000 years older" figure appears to be an overstatement, perhaps by a factor of two. Second, the Dead Sea Scrolls do contain Leviticus fragments that confirm the extraordinary fidelity of textual transmission — but the manuscripts are not entirely "word for word the same" in every detail; minor scribal variants exist (though none affect doctrine). The broader point — that the transmission of Scripture has been remarkably faithful — is accurate and well-established. The specific figure of "2,000 years" should be corrected to approximately "1,000 years" before reuse.]

God's preservation of his Word: If he wanted us to have it, he would preserve it. He did.

APPLICATION

What should Christians do with God's Word?

- Read it daily. It washes, comforts, helps fight temptation, draws us closer to Jesus, and deepens our relationship with him.
- Recognize that not reading Scripture daily is a spiritual vulnerability.

For the non-Christian: Believing in God's existence is not sufficient. James 2:19, ESV: "You believe that God is one; you do well. Even the demons believe — and shudder!" [The pastor paraphrased: "Even the demons believe in God, and they tremble." Accurate in substance.] Christianity is not mere assent to God's existence; it is a relationship with God — one that involves prayer and receiving God's Word.

The pastor described the friend who comes for advice but never listens — dumping problems and leaving without receiving counsel. When we fail to read Scripture and heed the Holy Spirit, we treat God the same way.

Gospel invitation: Matthew 7:13–14, ESV: "Enter by the narrow gate. For the gate is wide and the way is easy that leads to destruction, and those who enter by it are many. For the gate is narrow and the way is hard that leads to life, and those who find it are few." [The pastor paraphrased: "Wide is the gate that leads to destruction and many there are that find it; narrow is the way that leads to salvation and few there are that find it." Accurate in substance; not the ESV wording.]

Those who have never begun a relationship with Jesus Christ were invited to speak with the pastor after the service.
