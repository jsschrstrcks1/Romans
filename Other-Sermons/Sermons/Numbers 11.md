### Opening — Mark Dever and the Book of Numbers

I was listening to Mark Dever preach this week. He was preaching through the entire book of Numbers in a single sitting. Don't worry. We are not doing that tonight.

But we are going to spend some time in Numbers. And Dever had some insights that got me thinking, and you'll hear the gleanings of that tonight.

His point was this: as you read through Numbers again and again, you see the Israelites locked in a cycle they can't break out of. Over and over and over. They start out lacking contentment. That lack of contentment leads them to sin. Leads them to grumbling. Leads them to idolatry. And it even leads Moses — Moses — to sin.

At one point Moses says to God: *these people you've saddled me with make me wish I were dead.*

Think about that for a moment. Dealing with discontentment — other people's discontentment, his own — to the point where death seems like the better option. Some of you have been there. If you're there now, or someone you love is there, please get help. I know a counselor. She's excellent. Don't try to carry that alone.

But that's where Moses lands in tonight's passage. He is struggling under the weight of his people's whining. And before we judge him for it — let's be honest about what that whining looks like.

### Illustration — The Stuffed Animal

I love my nieces and nephews. I do. And I just got back from a wonderful week with them — thank you for letting me go. We spent the week by the pool, reconnecting, catching up on some much-needed rest. It was a great week.

But there was one moment.

One of the boys wanted a little stuffed animal from a gift shop. A five-dollar item. You can get it at Rural King for three. It was honestly a little on the girly side, and this boy just had to have it at all costs. He said the thing every kid says when they've locked in on something: *I'll never ask for anything again. I just want this stuffed toy. Please get me the toy.*

I didn't have my wallet with me. If I had, I probably would have bought it for him just to make the whining stop. Five dollars is sometimes a small price to pay for peace. His parents were there, though, and they made the right call — they told him to wait and see what else he might come across that he'd like better. It took a half hour, but it was the better decision.

Because here's the thing about that toy. He would have played with it for five minutes and never touched it again. No lasting value. No lasting joy. It would have satisfied for a moment and then sat in the bottom of a toy box until someone threw it away.

It's wonderful being young, because the thing your heart most desperately wants only costs five dollars.

As adults, what we covet tends to be far more costly. Houses. Status. Relationships. Authority. Power. What's yours? We all have something.

Pastor Zitt shared last week that for him, for a season, it was illegal drugs. It doesn't matter what the object is. The lesson is the same. Discontentment, left unchecked, leads to sin.

### Part 1 — What Discontentment Really Is

I want to walk through several things discontentment actually is. This isn't an exhaustive list. But each one is worth sitting with.

**Discontentment is a failure of trust.**

Contentment is trusting God. Completely. Discontentment is the opposite of that — which means it's the opposite of faith. When you're discontent, what you're actually saying is: *God, I know what I need better than you do.*

Say that out loud and it sounds absurd. We would never say that to God's face. But when we nurse discontentment in our hearts, when we let it grow, that's exactly what we're doing. We're declaring ourselves the better judge of our own situation.

**Discontentment is a complaint against God's plan.**

Paul Tripp puts it sharply: *I love me and have a wonderful plan for my life.* There's a lot of truth in that. When we're discontent, we're not just unhappy about our circumstances. We're insisting that our plan is better than His. That we should be the sovereign one.

**Discontentment covets what God has chosen not to give.**

Romans 8:32 asks: *He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things?*

If He isn't giving it to you right now, He has a reason. And that reason is grounded in a God who already gave His Son for you. Whatever He's withholding, He is not withholding out of indifference. He knows what you need. He proved it on the cross.

**Discontentment tells God He's made a mistake.**

My circumstances are wrong. They should be otherwise. I will not be at peace until they change to match what I want.

That's what discontentment says. And notice what it assumes: that your desires are trustworthy. But your desires, apart from God, are shaped by a sin nature. By selfishness. Why would you trust that over the wisdom of God?

**Discontentment denies the wisdom of God.**

Raise your hand if you think you know more than God. Anyone? If you think you're smarter? More wise?

Nobody. Of course nobody.

And yet — isn't this exactly what Eve did in the garden? She didn't raise her hand either. She just acted on it. *Has God really said?* That question is at the heart of every sin ever committed. And it is at the heart of discontentment. We may not say it, but we're asking it. *Has God really said He knows best? Has God really said this is good for me? Has God really said I can trust Him here?*

### Illustration — The Lottery Winner

Let me tell you a story I heard recently.

A woman up in the northeast won one of those multi-state jackpots. One of the enormous ones. She'd been buying the same ticket every week for years. This particular week she bought her ticket, went home, and didn't check it for several days. When she finally looked, she had won something in the range of three hundred million dollars.

She called her boss and quit before she ever cashed the ticket. Bought a new car. A boat. An RV. Then she cashed in, made the rounds on the news programs, and spent like she had an endless well. She started buying houses for family members. Paid off her best friend's mortgage. Paid off her best friend's son's student loans. Before she knew it she had burned through six months of income.

So she called one of those companies that buys out lottery winnings — you get all the cash now, minus their cut, and they collect your payments for the next thirty years. She took the deal.

Within a year, she had spent every dollar. She had never finished paying off the things she bought. She had nothing left. No job. No retirement. No savings. And people were still calling her asking for more.

She ended her own life. What she believed was the best thing that had ever happened to her destroyed her.

The things we think will finally satisfy — the thing we're convinced we need — are not always what we think. Sometimes what we're most desperate to have would wreck us. We don't always know what's good for us. God does.

### Part 2 — How God Feels About Discontentment

I've heard people say over the years, *Wouldn't it be great if God spoke as clearly today as He did with Israel?* No. That would not be great.

When God needed to communicate with particular force, people's houses caught fire. The ground opened up and swallowed people whole. Plagues came and killed tens of thousands. God's anger is a terrible thing, and we should have a very healthy fear of it.

Look at verse 1 again: *His anger was kindled, and the fire of the LORD burned among them and consumed some outlying parts of the camp.* God got so angry at Israel's complaining that the perimeter of the camp caught fire. Over three million people — some estimates run even higher — and fire started breaking out around the edges of their camp because of their whining.

That's how God feels about discontentment.

I've read about a plant called Stapelia. It's a large flower — I saw a picture of one that dwarfed the man standing next to it. Its color has been described as "the color of three days of rotted meat." A scientist who tried to study it in a greenhouse described the smell as *worse than an entire herd of rotting elephants* — and reported that the stench was so severe his team couldn't enter the building.

In Leviticus we read about sacrifices and offerings that were a *pleasing aroma* to the Lord. There are things that please God. And discontentment is not one of them. If a pleasing aroma is what faithfulness looks like before God, then discontentment is the Stapelia — rotten and repulsive.

Thank God for the cross. Because Christ satisfied the wrath of God — He took the full weight of it — so that you and I are not washed away by it.

### Part 3 — What to Do About It

The Puritan Jeremiah Burroughs wrote extensively about discontentment and its connection to sin. He wrote:

> *Have good thoughts of God and make good interpretations of his dealings toward you. It is very hard to live comfortably and cheerfully among friends when one makes harsh interpretations of the words and actions of another. The only way to keep sweet contentment and comfort in Christian societies is to make the best interpretations of things we can. Likewise, a primary way to help keep comfort and contentment in our hearts is to make good interpretations of God's dealings with us.*

*(Contentment, Prosperity, and God's Glory)*

We know God is good. We know He has good plans for His children. We know He is working all things for our good. We can take that to the bank. But when we grow discontent, we're challenging all of that. We're assuming He's doing something wrong. That we know better.

Burroughs says this is making bad interpretations of what God is doing.

I took a counseling course some years ago, and the professor described what he called the four horsemen of the marital apocalypse — four patterns that, when they take root, tend to end marriages. One of them is always assuming your partner has evil motives. If you say to your wife, *You look nice today,* and she responds in complete seriousness, *What — you think I look bad every other day?* — you might be living with that horseman.

What he meant was a compliment. She received it as a criticism. When you consistently interpret your partner's motives with suspicion and doubt, you have a serious problem.

The same thing applies to your relationship with God. If you're discontent with what He's allowing — if you're questioning His motives, His methods, His wisdom — then you have constructed a version of God who is working against you. And that version is not real.

Philip Holmes wrote: *If we truly believed what the Bible says about how God sees us, it would transform the way we interpret all His actions as mercies. I know that in the midst of my battles with discontentment and besetting sins, it's hard to view what is happening in my life as anything but a condemnation and punishment.*

Most of us know this from the inside. In the middle of hard seasons, it is difficult to see what is happening as anything but punishment. The mind slides there naturally. But that is a bad interpretation of a good God. Burroughs' prescription applies directly: make good interpretations of His dealings. The God who did not spare His own Son is not withholding good from you out of indifference or cruelty.

After pride, discontentment may be the thing we struggle with most on this side of heaven.

### Tell Yourself the Truth

One of my nieces, when she was smaller, had a habit of turning her hopes into certainties. *I hope it rains today* would slowly, over the course of an afternoon, become *It's going to rain today.* It wasn't necessarily true. But she hoped it would be, and the hope and the fact got twisted together until she couldn't tell the difference.

We had to regularly remind her: tell yourself the truth.

That's the first move when discontentment sets in. Tell yourself the truth about who God is and what He has done.

The work of the cross is infinitely more than we ever deserved. If God had only done that — died for rebellious sinners who contributed nothing to their own salvation — He would already have been incomprehensibly merciful. And yet He does far more than that. You have stories. Every one of you has a story of how He has worked in your life for your good. Sometimes toward your comfort. Sometimes toward your joy. Always for your good. Hold onto that when things are hard.

### Keep Your Eyes on the Prize

Paul writes:

> *Indeed, I count everything as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord. For his sake I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as rubbish, in order that I may gain Christ.* (Philippians 3:8)

The best possible things this world can offer — the best — are rubbish measured against knowing Christ. That stuffed animal the boy wanted? Rubbish. The new house? Rubbish. The relationship you've been chasing? Rubbish. The shiny thing, whatever it is — rubbish. None of it compares to the surpassing worth of knowing Jesus.

Now remember who wrote that. Here's what preceded it:

> *…with far greater labors, far more imprisonments, with countless beatings, and often near death. Five times I received at the hands of the Jews the forty lashes less one. Three times I was beaten with rods. Once I was stoned. Three times I was shipwrecked; a night and a day I was adrift at sea; on frequent journeys, in danger from rivers, danger from robbers, danger from my own people, danger from Gentiles, danger in the city, danger in the wilderness, danger at sea, danger from false brothers; in toil and hardship, through many a sleepless night, in hunger and thirst, often without food, in cold and exposure. And apart from other things, there is the daily pressure on me of my anxiety for all the churches.* (2 Corinthians 11:23-28)

That man — with those marks on his body, with that list behind him — says the best things this world has to offer are garbage in comparison to knowing Jesus.

James says it the same way: *Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds.* (James 1:2)

In Romans he says: *For I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us.* (Romans 8:18)

And Jesus himself said it this way:

> *The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field, which a man found and covered up. Then in his joy he goes and sells all that he has and buys that field.* (Matthew 13:44)

We may never know, on this side of heaven, the why behind the things that make us discontent. But we can hold onto the Who — the one who is the source of every lasting joy.

When discontentment rises, tell yourself the truth. Then fix your eyes on the treasure.

He is worth more than anything you are grieving. He is worth more than anything you have wanted and not received. He is worth more than whatever you have lost.

---

### Gospel

Maybe you don't know Him today. Maybe you've been polite about Jesus for a long time — nodding at the right moments, keeping a respectful distance. Maybe you've been chasing the thing that was going to finally satisfy, and you keep finding out it doesn't.

There is only one source of lasting joy. And He is not a thing to be acquired. He is a person who gave everything to make a way for you.

You are not here by accident. The God who provided bread from the sky for three million people in a desert has not lost track of you. And He is not your enemy. Whatever He is withholding, whatever He is allowing, whatever road He has you on — He is not your enemy.

He is the treasure in the field.

Do you know Him today?
