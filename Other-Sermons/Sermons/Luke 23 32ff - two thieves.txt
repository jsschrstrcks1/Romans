Luke 23:32–43 — The Two Thieves


One of the things that stood out to me right away is the mocking of Christ. Not just in this passage but throughout the preceding chapters — he's mocked by the Romans with a purple garment, by the priests, and even by the other criminals on the cross. Mark records that several criminals mocked him. Here in Luke only one is mentioned. That's likely for simplicity's sake. There could have been as many as 42 criminals crucified on Golgotha that day — the highest number ever recorded at once. But even if there were 42, thirty-nine of them were not relevant to the story. Only these two, and Jesus.

Pay close attention to these two thieves. One day you'll spend eternity with one of them.

They're never mentioned anywhere else in Scripture. Catholic tradition gives the name Dismas to the repentant thief — but honestly, even their names are immaterial at this point. What Luke tells us is that he calls them criminals. Matthew and Mark use the Greek word lēstai — generally translated "thief," but the word carries a range of meanings. The historian Josephus uses it for insurrectionists, people trying to seize power by force. Scripture also applies it to Barabbas, who was a murderer — one who steals life itself. So it's possible these men were what we would call terrorists today: people using murder and fear to gain political influence. In other words, they were not good men. A commentary I read suggested the Romans often crucified people convicted of the same type of crime together. Christ was accused of what? Trying to usurp Roman authority. So it's likely these criminals were guilty of the same charge — attempting to overthrow Roman rule in Israel.

These men didn't end up on the cross by accident. It was the calculated consequence of a capital crime.

They are broken people. They carry inside themselves the desperation of brokenness. Broken by their sin, now their bodies are being broken for their crimes. In their brokenness, at the end of their lives, there are two responses to Christ. Pay attention — because you're going to spend eternity with one of them.


Look at their first responses. They both mocked him. Verse 39: "Are you not the Christ? Save yourself and us!" The people of Israel had been groaning for a military deliverer — someone like Gideon who would rally the people and drive off the enemy, or Samson walking out with the jawbone of a donkey and slaying their oppressors, or Joshua marching around Jericho with nothing but musical instruments and watching the walls fall. They wanted a warrior who would end the yoke of Roman oppression. But none of them could see past their own immediate wants. They were so fixed on what they desired in that moment that they could not see what God was doing right in front of them. You can't see the forest for the trees.

Any of you seen Braveheart? Near the end, the character Mel Gibson plays is being tortured before his execution, and he rears back and screams "Freedom." Ultimately, the Scottish people did find freedom. I half think that's what the mocking thief wanted Jesus to do — call down angels, scatter the Romans, let's fight. What he couldn't see was that the real battle was being waged unseen all around him.

Every moment Christ resisted the temptation to crush those who put him there was another victory over sin. Every instant of obedience was another battle won. For six hours Christ hung on that cross. For six hours he had to pull his battered and broken body upward just to draw a breath. For six hours his back slid against wood that had likely been struck with swords to splinter it into his flesh — tearing away whatever meat remained after the scourging. Every breath a struggle. Every second a victory. John Owen put it plainly: "There is no death of sin without the death of Christ."

At some point Dismas, the second thief, had a change of heart and stopped mocking him. As a Jew he would have grown up hearing the Old Testament read — passages that pointed forward to the Messiah. He would have known from Isaiah that they would gamble for the Christ's clothes. He would have known that the Messiah would be beaten beyond recognition. There are something like 65 prophecies in Isaiah alone that point to Christ in ways Dismas would have recognized. All of those Old Testament signposts may have started running through his mind as he hung there. However it happened, he appears to be the only person on that hill who recognized Jesus for who he truly was.

Not only did he stop mocking him — Dismas is the only one in the entire scene focused on the resurrection.

Think about that. Where were the disciples? You'd think Peter would be up there preaching to anyone who would hold still. But he wasn't. Mary, Jesus' mother, and Joseph of Arimathea were making burial arrangements. The soldiers were focused entirely on making sure the condemned died. A prisoner who came off the cross alive meant a death sentence for the soldier responsible. They were not interested in mistakes.

Some people — mostly Muslim apologists — claim Jesus merely lost consciousness, that the Roman soldiers couldn't have known he was truly dead since they weren't medical doctors. But they were professional executioners. Their lives depended on doing their job. They would never have allowed someone to survive. And even if they had, when a soldier drove a spear up through his side into his heart, blood and water flowed out — a documented medical condition caused by death by asphyxia. It cannot be faked. The Journal of the American Medical Association concluded it is more likely that Christ was resurrected than that he was removed from the cross still living. These were secular doctors — most of them not Christians — and they found no possible way he could have survived.

Others argue Jesus was a liar, that everything he taught was fabricated right to the end. But does that make sense? Why would a sane person lie repeatedly knowing those lies would cost him his life? A simple retraction would have saved him. The only reason he would walk knowingly to the cross is if he believed everything he had taught to be true.

Some say he was mentally ill. Have you ever spent time with someone in the grip of a severe mental illness? I once saw a video of a woman who talked about how "the Ford" would provide for her and bring her money. It turned out her husband had worked at Ford and a friend from his old job checked in on her out of respect for her late husband. Her mind couldn't process what was happening, and it came out as a word salad that made sense to no one but her. If Jesus had been like that, how would he have ever attracted a single follower? He wouldn't have. It would have been impossible.

After the crucifixion there were more than 500 witnesses to Christ's resurrection — some believers, but not all. Some were actively involved in persecuting the church, and they recorded what they witnessed anyway. Skeptics say it must have been mass delusion, that all 500 people happened to have the same hallucination. Even atheist and agnostic psychologists say that is impossible.

What's left? That the resurrection was true.

This must have been the conclusion Dismas reached on the cross. Jesus was truly the Christ, and he would be resurrected into paradise.

I love what Dismas's request reveals about the weight he felt. He doesn't ask Jesus to save him from the cross. He doesn't ask to be teleported to safety. He doesn't ask him to lead the rebellion. He simply says: "Remember me when you come into your kingdom." There is a quiet admission of guilt in those words. He knows he is wrong. He knows he deserves the punishment — earthly and eternal — he's receiving as he speaks. There is humility in it, and brokenness, and a recognition that everything he had worked toward in life may have been the wrong thing. Paul says in Romans 6:23 that the wages of sin is death. Dismas understood he was about to receive what his life had earned.

And then Christ's response: "Today you will be with me in paradise." Grace to this man who in that moment would have called himself the chief of sinners. Grace, grace — God's grace — grace that is greater than all our sin.


We are no better than either of these two thieves. Think about it. Dad talked last week about the throne in our hearts that belongs to Christ — and yet we try to sit there anyway. Are we not then guilty of lēstai — trying to steal authority from Christ over our own lives? God is sovereign and ultimately we can't. But we are guilty of trying. Each of us has ignored the still small voice of God and done what we wanted, what felt good in the moment. Many have had sex outside of marriage, or lied, or stolen, or committed adultery. I'm confident that between those, each of us has broken at least one — and we've barely gotten through the first three commandments. We are guilty.

What we do now, where we go from here, determines which thief you'll spend eternity with. Dismas went to paradise. There are only two options after death — heaven, which Christ called paradise, and hell. The unrepentant thief went to one. Dismas went to the other. It's one or the other for each of us.

As the praise team comes up, one more story.

George Vandeman, in Planet in Rebellion, writes:

"It was May 21, 1946. The place: Los Alamos. A young and daring scientist was carrying out a necessary experiment in preparation for the atomic test to be conducted in the waters of the South Pacific atoll at Bikini. He had successfully performed such an experiment many times before. In his effort to determine the amount of U-235 necessary for a chain reaction — scientists call it the critical mass — he would push two hemispheres of uranium together. Then, just as the mass became critical, he would push them apart with his screwdriver, thus instantly stopping the chain reaction.

But that day, just as the material became critical, the screwdriver slipped. The hemispheres of uranium came too close together. Instantly the room was filled with a dazzling bluish haze. Young Louis Slotin, instead of ducking and thereby possibly saving himself, tore the two hemispheres apart with his hands and thus interrupted the chain reaction.

By this instant, self-forgetful daring, he saved the lives of the seven other persons in the room. As he waited for the car that was to take them to the hospital, he said quietly to his companion, 'You'll come through all right. But I haven't the faintest chance myself.' It was only too true. Nine days later he died in agony."

Nineteen centuries ago the Son of the living God walked directly into sin's most concentrated radiation, allowed himself to be touched by its curse, and let it take his life. But by that act he broke the chain reaction. He broke the power of sin. And unlike Louis Slotin, three days later Christ rose again. Five hundred witnesses. That empty tomb is the reason we celebrate Easter.

That's what Easter is all about.
