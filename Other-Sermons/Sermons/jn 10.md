## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Pastoral Heart with a Wandering Text: Sincere Gospel Warmth Undermined by Absent Exposition

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A well-meaning sermon on thankfulness that deploys genuine gospel content and pastoral instinct but fails to anchor its main point in John 10’s own argument, functioning more as a thematic devotional using the text as a launching pad than a proclamation arising from the passage.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**FAIL — Conditional Pass on Gospel, Hard Fail on Scopus Test**

**Scopus Test: FAIL.** The sermon’s announced thesis is “be thankful” and “stop complaining.” This thesis does not arise from John 10. John 10 is the Good Shepherd discourse — a declaration of Christ’s sovereign love, his voluntary self-sacrifice, the nature of his sheep, his unique authority, and his unity with the Father. The passage’s main point is Christological and covenantal: Jesus is the true Shepherd who voluntarily lays down his life and takes it up again by his own divine authority. “Be thankful” is not the text’s main point. It is at best a downstream implication — and a weakly connected one at that.

**Authority Test: CONDITIONAL PASS.** The preacher does not soften or edit the text’s claims about Christ. He handles John 10:11 and 10:17–18 honestly and with genuine force. He submits to what the text says about Christ’s voluntary sacrifice. The problem is not that he stands above the text as editor — it is that he largely ignores the text’s own argument in favor of a topical agenda the text was not written to carry.

**Gate Ruling:** The sermon does not pass the Berean Gate in its current form. The main point of the sermon does not arise from the main point of the passage. John 10 is used as a proof-text for a topical sermon on gratitude, not expounded as a self-contained argument about the Good Shepherd. The evaluation will proceed — because the gospel content in the appendix section is real and the Christ-centered material is genuine — but this structural failure will be reflected throughout the scoring.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag #2 — Main point does not equal text’s point: TRIGGERED.**
The text (John 10) is about the Good Shepherd’s sovereign, voluntary self-sacrifice and his unique authority. The sermon’s main point is “be thankful / stop complaining.” These are not the same point. The text is used illustratively, not expositionally.

**Flag #4 — Application is partly generic: PARTIAL TRIGGER.**
Much of the application is directional (“be thankful,” “stop complaining,” “deny yourself”) without distinguishing among hearers or pressing specific circumstances with textual force. The application does improve somewhat in the gospel section. The flag is noted but not at full severity.

**Score cap: 69 applies due to Flag #2.**

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 10/25**
- Textual Fidelity: 3/10. John 10:11 and 10:17–18 are quoted accurately but used as illustrations for a pre-set thesis rather than expounded on their own terms. The passage’s argument — who the Good Shepherd is, what distinguishes him from the hired hand, what it means that his sheep hear his voice — receives no treatment. The surrounding verses (10:1–16, 10:19–30) are entirely absent.
- Contextual Awareness: 2/5. There is no engagement with the immediate context of John 10 (the healing of the blind man in chapter 9, the Feast of Dedication, the Pharisees as hired hands). The redemptive-historical location of John 10 as the fulfillment of Ezekiel 34 is untouched.
- Exegetical Clarity: 2/5. The preacher does look up the Greek for “deny” in Matthew 16:24 (which is not even the text) and explains it helpfully. But no Greek work is done on John 10 itself.
- Logical Faithfulness: 3/5. The connections between the illustrations and the application are often asserted rather than argued. The move from John 10:18 to “be thankful” requires logical steps the sermon does not supply.
- **Fallen Condition Focus:** Partially identified — the FCF is ingratitude and self-worship. But it is named without being traced to the text John 10 was written to address.

**Category 2 — Theology & Doctrinal Integrity: 13/18**
- Christ-Centeredness: 5/7. Christ is genuinely central — his voluntary sacrifice, his love, his cross. The A.W. Pink quotation is excellent and used well. The Christology is not superficial.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3. There is no placement of John 10 within redemptive history. The Ezekiel 34 background (God himself coming as Shepherd because Israel’s shepherds failed) would have massively strengthened both the theological and applicatory force. It is entirely absent.
- Doctrinal Precision: 4/4. Penal substitution is clear (“he who knew no sin became sin for us”), wrath is present, hell is named, the resurrection is affirmed with historical evidence. This is the sermon’s strongest doctrinal section.
- Confessional Alignment: 3/4. The doctrines of grace appear implicitly — “his elect people” in the Pink quote is retained and not softened. No contradictions of confessional theology are present.

**Category 3 — Gospel Centrality & Force: 10/15**
- Clarity of the Gospel: 4/5. The gospel appendix is clear, sequential, and honest. Romans 3:23, 6:23, Ephesians 2:8, John 3:16, Romans 10:9 are used correctly. Justification by faith is present.
- Necessity of Christ: 4/5. Christ is essential — the sermon would collapse without him. His voluntary death is not incidental.
- Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5. The cross is mentioned but the sermon does not build toward it with cumulative force. The gospel section feels appended rather than integrated. The cross arrives as an add-on after the moral application has already concluded, rather than as the ground and power of the application.
- **Synagogue Test:** Borderline. The main body of the sermon (be thankful, stop complaining, deny yourself) could survive in a theologically-inclined synagogue. The gospel appendix would not — but the structural separation weakens the overall offense of the cross.

**Category 4 — Conscience & Applicatory Force: 8/15**
- Heart Penetration: 3/5. There are moments of genuine conscience-searching: “If a stranger were to put you into one of those two categories based on just the thoughts and words of the past week, would they say you are grateful?” and “Sometimes I love myself so much that I won’t tell someone what they need to hear.” These land.
- Specificity: 3/5. The illustrations are concrete — the noisy person, the dog dying, the truck breaking. But the differentiation is thin. The application addresses mostly the already-converted and already-convicted.
- Spiritual Differentiation (Perkins): 2/5. The sermon speaks primarily to Category 5 (believers) and Category 3 (knowledgeable but unhumbled). The self-righteous are addressed but not cornered. The humbled-but-not-yet-believing, the backslider, and the hardened are not distinctly reached.

**Category 5 — Exhortation & Response: 5/8**

- Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 2/4. The application gestures toward holiness (deny yourself, take up your cross) but does not send the church out equipped. There is no corporate dimension — no address to the church as a covenant community. The sermon speaks entirely to individuals.

**Category 6 — Structure & Logical Flow: 5/9**
- Clarity of Outline: 2/4. There is no discernible structure. The sermon moves: story → historical note on Thanksgiving → thankfulness in Scripture → grateful vs. ungrateful audit → Christ’s example → complaining as self-worship → MT 16:24 → closing application → gospel appendix. This is a series of related moves, not a structure. It could not be sketched on a napkin.
- Progression: 2/3. There is forward movement — it does build toward the cross — but it wanders considerably before arriving.
- Unity: 1/2. There is one dominant idea (be thankful / don’t complain because of Christ’s sacrifice) but the route to it is cluttered.

**Category 7 — Illustration & Imagination: 3/5**
- Relevance: 2/2. The illustrations are drawn from real life — the pastor’s wife and daughter, Bob Clark and son Rob, the niece with the attitude, the dog dying, the broken truck. These are native to the congregation’s world.
- Clarity: 1/2. The opening leukemia story is long (nearly 600 words) relative to the exegetical weight it carries. It illuminates the point but consumes disproportionate space.
- Memorability: 0/1. The Pink quotation used twice is memorable, but the repeated use dilutes rather than doubles its impact. The illustrations themselves are less memorable than the Christ-content they illustrate.

**Category 8 — Sermonic Force: 3/5**
- Reads as Preaching: 2/2. This is clearly oral manuscript. Direct address is present (“I want you guys to name some things”), questions are asked of the congregation, the cadence is conversational and natural.
- Conviction on the Page: 1/2. There is genuine conviction in the Christ-section, but the opening and structural sections are meandering. The ratio of Scripture-grounded assertions to general statements is imbalanced in the first half.
- Cadence and Breath: 0/1. Sentence variation is low. The climactic moments (Christ loved us “to death and back”) are present but not earned by the manuscript’s architecture. The sermon ends three times.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Partly Christ. The A.W. Pink quotation and the John 10:17–18 material are genuinely Christ-exalting. But the extended self-examination sequence (“audit your thoughts and words,” “sometimes I worship myself”) pushes toward self-focus as the sermon’s primary concern.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas about him, with occasional warmth. The Pink quotation comes closest to God-encounter. The leukemia story produces emotional weight that is attributed to God but does not arise from exposition of who God is.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Some conviction (the self-worship diagnosis is sharp). Some comfort in the gospel appendix. But the primary mode is informational-exhortational, not deeply searching.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Genuine gospel content, clearly stated.** The gospel appendix (Romans 3:23, 6:23, Ephesians 2:8, John 3:16, Romans 10:9) is honest, sequential, and theologically accurate. An unbeliever sitting in the room would understand the gospel. This is not nothing — many Reformed sermons assume the gospel without proclaiming it. This one proclaims it.

2. **The self-worship diagnosis is sharp and memorable.** “Complaining happens when you worship yourself instead of God” is a genuine insight, well-stated, and pressed with concrete illustrations (the noise, the truck, the cross-street). This is the sermon’s best applicatory moment, and it arises from a real theological instinct.

3. **The A.W. Pink quotation and John 10:18 together.** The combination of “I lay it down of my own accord” with Pink’s exposition of Christ’s voluntary death is the theological heart of the sermon, and it lands with genuine force. The preacher has good instincts about what to do with this material.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The sermon has no text.** It is announced as a John 10 sermon. John 10 is essentially unpreached. Two verses are quoted (10:11, 10:17–18) as illustrations for a topical point the passage was not designed to make. The surrounding argument of the chapter — who the Good Shepherd is, what distinguishes him from the hired hand, the sheep hearing his voice, the Pharisees as false shepherds, the unity of Father and Son — receives zero treatment. A congregation cannot reconstruct John 10’s argument from this sermon because the preacher never preached it.

2. **Structure is absent, and the gospel is structurally quarantined.** The formal “Gospel:” heading at the end creates a two-sermon problem: a moral exhortation sermon followed by an evangelistic gospel outline. The cross should not appear as an appendix after the application has concluded. This structural choice undercuts the gospel’s power — it is placed where it cannot drive the application, only follow it. The gospel needs to be the ground of the exhortation, not its afterthought.

3. **The opening story is disproportionately long for what it delivers.** Nearly 600 words on the leukemia story, followed by 150 words on Thanksgiving history, means the sermon is already 750 words deep before the biblical text is seriously engaged. This is a significant structural problem in a manuscript of approximately 1,400 words before the gospel section. More than half the sermon’s real estate is occupied by material that could be reduced to two sentences.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher does not appear to see the difference between a topical sermon that uses a text and an expositional sermon that preaches a text. He opens to John 10, quotes two verses, and proceeds to preach a sermon on thankfulness that John 10 does not primarily teach. This is likely unconscious — the preacher clearly loves the text — but the discipline of asking “What is the main point of this passage, and is that my sermon’s main point?” appears absent from the preparation process.

There is also a blind spot regarding the Ezekiel 34 background, which would have made the sermon simultaneously more expositional, more Christ-centered, and more directly applicable: God himself came as Shepherd because every human shepherd — every pastor, every leader, every parent — fails. That failure is the fallen condition this text addresses. The sermon preaches the solution (Christ’s voluntary sacrifice) without naming the problem the text was written to solve.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon feels pastorally warm and gospel-centered because it quotes A.W. Pink, uses John 10:18, proclaims penal substitution, and includes a clear Romans Road gospel presentation. These elements create the impression of solid Reformed evangelical preaching. But the warmth is not generated by the text — it is imported from good sources and placed onto a topical frame. The exposition has not done the work that makes the application earn its power. A listener who felt this was strong Reformed preaching would be partly right about the content and entirely wrong about the method.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

John 10:17–18 is one of the most stunning declarations of Christ’s divine authority in the Gospels: “I have authority to lay it down, and I have authority to take it up again.” This verse, properly expounded, would have produced exactly the thankfulness the sermon wants to call for — and would have grounded it in Christ’s identity, not merely his example. The sermon quotes the verse but does not preach it. The word “authority” (Greek: *exousia*) deserved direct attention: this is not merely a good man dying nobly — this is the God-Man exercising sovereign dominion over death itself. That exposition would have made the call to thankfulness not just morally urgent but cosmologically grounded.

Additionally, the Ezekiel 34 background is a complete miss. The Good Shepherd passage is the fulfillment of God’s promise to come himself and gather his scattered sheep when every human shepherd had failed. Preaching this context would have spoken directly to anyone in the congregation who has been failed by leaders, pastors, or fathers — making the application both more searching and more comforting.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the sermon around John 10’s main point.** The text’s main point is not “be thankful” — it is “Christ is the Good Shepherd who voluntarily lays down his life and takes it up again by divine authority.” Make that the thesis. Thankfulness and the rejection of complaining can still be the applicatory destination — but they must arise from what the passage actually establishes about who Christ is and what he has done. The application follows from the exposition; the exposition cannot follow from a pre-set application.

2. **Move the gospel from appendix to engine.** The gospel section currently appears after the application has concluded, under its own header. This must be restructured. Christ’s voluntary death (10:18) should be the center of the sermon’s body, from which application radiates. The Romans Road content is fine — but it should be woven into the sermon’s movement, not bolted on at the end. “This is the Christ who laid down his life — and here is what that means for the person who has never trusted him” should arise naturally from the exposition, not arrive separately.

3. **Cut or compress the opening story and Thanksgiving history to under 150 words combined.** The leukemia story is moving but consumes real estate that the text needs. A single paragraph capturing the pastor’s testimony — “fifteen years later, they return to the room where they received their death sentence and give thanks” — does the same work in 40 words. The Thanksgiving history can be cut entirely without loss.

4. **Add direct address to the self-righteous and the backslider.** The sermon speaks primarily to convinced believers who already agree that complaining is wrong. It does not corner the person who is convinced they have a right to their grievance, nor does it reach the backslider who has stopped giving thanks entirely because faith has grown cold. After the self-worship diagnosis, a turn like: “Some of you have not given thanks to God in weeks — not because you’ve forgotten how, but because you’re not sure he’s still good. This text speaks to you too...” would extend the sermon’s reach significantly.

5. **Introduce the Ezekiel 34 background in one paragraph.** “When Jesus says ‘I am the Good Shepherd,’ he is making a claim his Jewish hearers would have heard immediately: God himself promised in Ezekiel 34 that he would come and shepherd his people, because every human shepherd had abandoned them. Jesus is saying: I am that shepherd. I am God come to do what no man could do.” This one paragraph would triple the theological weight of John 10:18 without adding complexity — it would make it clearer.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon is not dead orthodoxy — it has genuine warmth, pastoral instinct, and real gospel content. The problem is not bloodlessness but groundlessness. The fire is present; the textual foundation under it is weak. Dead orthodoxy is correct and cold. This sermon is warm but untethered. A different diagnosis is needed: *pastoral sincerity in search of expositional discipline.*

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries genuine evangelical warmth and a real love for Christ’s sacrifice, but it does not yet carry the weight that comes when a congregation is made to stand inside the text and see God — the Good Shepherd who lays down his life by divine authority — rather than merely hearing a preacher’s enthusiasm about him; +2 of 5 bonus points awarded.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This rubric is well-suited to this sermon’s genre (expository/applicatory). John 10 is a dominical discourse — not narrative, not apocalyptic, not wisdom literature — and the rubric’s categories apply without strain. The Fallen Condition Focus lens (Category 1) is especially relevant here: the text was written to address Israel’s experience of failed shepherds and the disciples’ need to understand who Jesus truly is. The FCF would have strengthened both the exposition and the application considerably. No category bends under the genre pressure of this passage.
