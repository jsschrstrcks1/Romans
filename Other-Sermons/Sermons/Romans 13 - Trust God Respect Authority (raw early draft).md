## Integrity Log

### Scripture Errors
- **"Romans 12:6"** → Should be **Hebrews 12:6** ("For the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and chastises every son whom he receives." ESV). Romans 12:6 is about spiritual gifts, not discipline.
- **Proverbs 21:1** — Quoted in NKJV, not ESV. ESV: "The king's heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD; he turns it wherever he will."

### Historical Errors
- **"in 55 Nero murdered his mother"** — Nero murdered Agrippina in **AD 59**. In AD 55, Nero poisoned his stepbrother Britannicus. Two different events.
- **"Molinism… in the 1100s"** — Luis de Molina lived 1535–1600 (16th century). Off by ~400 years.
- **FDR "shipping American military vehicles and men covertly"** — Lend-Lease Act (March 1941) shipped materiel publicly, not troops. US troops were not deployed before Pearl Harbor (December 1941).

### Theological Notes
- **Molinism label** is imprecise. The pastor rejects most of Molinism but affirms that God in His sovereignty knows all possible worlds, not merely the one that exists. The underlying conviction is sound; the label and the Molina dating are the errors, not the theology itself.
- **"String Theory"** — loose illustration, not a theological commitment; conflates physics multiverse with philosophical possible worlds.

### Factual Claims (Acceptable Range)
- Billy Graham "215 million people" — widely cited figure from BGEA; acceptable as delivered
- Paul writing Romans AD 56-58 from Greece — standard scholarly consensus
- "Hundreds of cops killed" — within range for mid-2010s line-of-duty deaths (all causes)
- Nero biographical details (aside from the AD 55/59 date confusion) are accurate

### Cross-References to Other Sermons

- "Stephen last week" — references a previous sermon on Stephen's martyrdom (Acts 7)

- "Stephen last week" suggests this was part of an ongoing preaching series (possibly Acts or topical)

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Raw Pastoral Instinct with an Unfinished Gospel Center and a Politically Exposed Conscience

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A raw early draft of Romans 13:1–7 that demonstrates genuine pastoral engagement with a difficult text and real-world urgency, while stopping short of a complete gospel presentation and displaying the doctrinal and structural roughness of a sermon not yet ready for the page.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with qualification.**

Scopus Test: The main point — submit to God-ordained authority because God is sovereign even over wicked rulers, therefore trust God — does arise from Romans 13:1–7. The sermon's thesis is traceable to the text.

Authority Test: The preacher does not stand above the text; he submits to it, including its uncomfortable demands (Nero, Obama, Pelosi, Schumer are named as legitimate authorities). He does not soften what offends. The gate passes — but the passage deserves more than it receives, and several significant sections drift away from the text entirely into political commentary.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Flag 5 — No sustained exhortation**: The sermon gestures toward exhortation ("trust God and respect our leaders") but never summons the congregation to a concrete response with pastoral weight. The "if you've never asked Jesus to pay the price" close is tacked on without development.

**Flag 4 — Application is partially generic**: Application stays at the level of "respect authority" without pressing the specific conscience of the civic-rebel, the grieving family member of a police victim, the bitter partisan, or the anxious saint watching chaos. Different hearers are named but not differentiated with pastoral specificity.

**Score cap: 69 maximum.**

*Note: Flag 6 (inaccessibility) was considered but not applied. The sermon is accessible — arguably too colloquial at points. The theological detour through Molinism may have confused hearers, but the main argument is followable.*

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity: 6/10* — The sermon correctly identifies that God ordains all authority (v.1), that rebellion is rebellion against God (v.2), and that rulers are God's servants for good (v.4). But the text's own argument — the table of duties in vv.6–7 (taxes, revenue, respect, honor) — receives almost no treatment. The passage's careful delineation of specific obligations is bypassed in favor of political illustration. Vv.3–7 are either skipped or only gestured at.
- *Contextual Awareness: 2/5* — No engagement with the broader context of Romans 12–13 as a sustained ethics of the new age. No connection to the "night is far gone" eschatological frame (13:11–14). No awareness that this follows Paul's "bless those who persecute you" (12:14) or "do not be overcome by evil" (12:21) — the continuity of the passage's logic is absent.
- *Exegetical Clarity: 3/5* — The Nero backdrop is genuinely illuminating and well-deployed. The Proverbs 21:1 parallel is apt. But the "Molinism/possible worlds" detour is exegetically disconnected — Paul is not arguing from divine middle knowledge in Romans 13; he is arguing from divine appointment and created order.
- *Logical Faithfulness: 3/5* — The main argument is sound. The Molinism label and the "String Theory" aside import categories foreign to the text. The FCF (fallen condition focus) is present but unnamed: the congregation's resistance to God-ordained authority they dislike is the condition the text addresses, and the preacher identifies it accurately, if informally.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness: 4/7* — The cop-as-Christ analogy is evocative but imprecise and could mislead. The eventual gospel close ("ask Jesus to pay the price for your sins") is present but thin. Christ is not unnecessary to the sermon, but he is not organically arising from the text either — Paul's argument in Romans 13:1–7 does not directly invoke Christ, so organic Christological connection requires more care than the sermon provides.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3* — No placement of this passage within Paul's broader argument in Romans, within the new covenant ethic, or within the eschatological tension of Romans 13:11–14. Stephen's martyrdom (referenced) is a missed opportunity to connect submission to authority with the cross's own pattern of unjust suffering.
- *Doctrinal Precision: 4/4* — The limits-of-obedience examples (murder, adultery, worship of false gods) are scripturally accurate and reflect the patristic/Reformed tradition on civil disobedience (Acts 5:29). The underlying conviction on God's sovereignty is sound despite the Molinism mislabeling.
- *Confessional Alignment: 2/4* — No contradiction of confessional theology. But the possible-worlds detour, while theologically defensible when labeled correctly as Reformed natural knowledge, is introduced under a wrong label (Molinism) and without pastoral guardrails. No engagement with covenant categories.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7 / 15**

- *Necessity of Christ: 3/5* — Christ is present, and the cop-as-Christ analogy attempts a christological move. But the sermon could largely survive without the cross. The argument from divine sovereignty to trusting authority does not require the gospel to function.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5* — The sermon moves toward trust in God's sovereignty rather than toward the cross. The cross is decorative, not gravitational. The Synagogue Test: a devout non-Christian who believes in a sovereign God could receive most of this sermon without offense at the necessity of Christ's blood.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8 / 15**

- *Specificity: 3/5* — The political examples are specific to the congregation and moment. But the application does not differentiate: the man whose brother was killed by a cop and the man who watches Fox News with reflexive anger are addressed identically.
- *Spiritual Differentiation: 2/5* — Only two categories are addressed with any distinction: the partisan-resistant believer and the not-yet-Christian. The doubter ("how can a good God ordain Nero?") is engaged briefly. The grieving, the fearful, the cynical, the civic moralist who already respects authority — unaddressed. - *Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 2/4* — The limits-of-obedience framework does equip the church practically. The Shannon's grandma / Judy illustrations hint at bearing suffering for others' salvation. But the sermon never sends the congregation out with a clear charge.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* — Largely of God's sovereignty and political circumstance. Christ is present at the margins but does not occupy the center. **Partial no.**
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* — The sovereignty section ("Proverbs 21:1 — God turns the king's heart like a river") approaches encounter. The political name-dropping pulls back from it. **Partial yes.**
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* — The naming of political enemies as God-ordained authorities does convict. The comfort is real but underdeveloped. **Convicted, but not yet comforted with gospel weight.**

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Pastoral nerve in naming names.** The sermon does not flinch. Naming Obama, Pelosi, Reid, Schumer, and Ginsburg as God-ordained authorities to a congregation that dislikes them — and then pressing "God even ordained Obama to be our leader" — is exactly what Romans 13 demands. This is textual submission, not political safety. The preacher stands under the Word here.

2. **The Nero backdrop is genuinely illuminating.** "Paul had the confidence to pen these words while Nero was plotting to kill his mother" — this is the kind of historical grounding that makes a passage come alive. The congregation understands that Paul was not writing from comfort when he wrote Romans 13, and that changes everything about how the command lands.

3. **The limits-of-obedience framework is scripturally sound and practically applied.** The list of examples (murder, adultery, false worship, abortion) is not arbitrary — it reflects the patristic and Reformed tradition on civil disobedience accurately. This is not cultural instinct; this is biblical reasoning applied to real civic situations. It equips the congregation.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel close is a tack-on, not a culmination.** "If you've never asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins, tonight is the night!" — placed at the end of a sermon about civic authority — has no preparation, no connection to the text, and no pastoral weight. An unbeliever in the room would not know what they were being invited into. This is the gospel as punctuation, not as the sermon's beating heart.

2. **Romans 13:3–7 (the actual table of duties) goes almost entirely unpreached.** The passage carefully enumerates taxes, revenue, respect, and honor as specific obligations. The sermon skips them almost entirely, substituting political commentary for exegesis. The text's own structure — the movement from theological grounding (vv.1–2) to practical application (vv.3–7) — is exactly the pattern the sermon should have followed but didn't.

3. **The Molinism section imports foreign categories without textual support.** Paul's argument in Romans 13 is not from divine middle knowledge — it is from divine appointment ("there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God," v.1). The providence argument belongs in the sermon, but it should be grounded in Paul's own logic (and in Romans itself — cf. 8:28, 9:17, 11:36), not in a mislabeled and misdated philosophical framework.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how politically located this sermon is in ways that will age it. The sermon is almost entirely calibrated to the grievances of right-leaning Americans in the Obama era. The passage itself is far more scandalous and symmetric: Paul wrote it under Nero, and it applies equally to the congregation member who despises the politician the preacher admires. The sermon addresses political discomfort from one angle only. A congregation member who did not share the preacher's political instincts would hear the sermon as tribal, not biblical.

There is also a blind spot around the grief dimension of this text. Some hearers may be resistant to authority not out of partisan anger but out of personal trauma — the family of someone killed by police, the immigrant afraid of government, the person ground down by unjust institutions. The sermon does not see them.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The political specificity — naming Obama, Pelosi, Reid — feels bold and feels like courage. It reads as pastoral nerve. But it also does the sermon's hardest work without actually doing it: the congregation agrees that they are supposed to respect authorities they don't like; what they need is to be searched for the ways they fail to do so, differentiated by the particular shape of their resistance, and sent away with a specific charge. The name-dropping creates the feeling of confrontation without the surgery of it.

Similarly, the Nero background feels like scholarship. It is good historical context. But the sermon spends considerable time on Nero's biography and relatively little on the text itself — the historical material displaces exposition rather than serving it.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The passage closes with vv.6–7: "For because of this you also pay taxes, for the authorities are ministers of God, attending to this very thing. Pay to all what is owed to them: taxes to whom taxes are owed, revenue to whom revenue is owed, respect to whom respect is owed, honor to whom honor is owed." This is remarkably specific. Paul is not merely making a philosophical point about sovereignty — he is telling the Roman church to pay their taxes, honor the emperor, and do so as an act of theological conviction. This is the application built into the text itself. The sermon misses it entirely and substitutes external illustrations for the text's own applied demands.

The Stephen's martyrdom reference also points toward a missed christological connection: Jesus himself submitted to unjust governmental authority (Pilate), and his submission purchased our redemption. The pattern of Romans 13 is not just pragmatic — it echoes the cross. This is never developed.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach vv.3–7.** The second half of the passage — taxes, revenue, respect, honor — must be opened, not skipped. These specific obligations are the text's own application. The congregation needs to hear Paul name what submission looks like concretely, not hear the preacher substitute his own list of political illustrations.

2. **Ground the gospel in the text's own logic before the close.** Christ submitted to governing authorities who crucified him (1 Peter 2:13–23 is the parallel passage). His submission was not defeated — it was the instrument of salvation. Connect the congregation's call to submission with Christ's own pattern: "This is what it looks like when the Son of God lives under Romans 13. And it saved the world." Then the gospel invitation arises from the sermon's argument rather than appearing as a separate conclusion.

3. **Replace the Molinism section with Paul's own argument.** Romans 9:17 ("For this purpose I have raised you up, that I might show my power in you") and Proverbs 21:1 are already present. Ground the sovereignty argument there — in Scripture — rather than in a mislabeled philosophical framework. The point is sound; the vehicle is wrong.

4. **Differentiate the application.** Address at least three types of hearer with distinct pastoral specificity: (a) the partisan resister who hates the current administration — press the idolatry of political identity; (b) the grieving or traumatized person who fears authority — offer the comfort of God's sovereignty over those who have harmed them; (c) the comfortably compliant person who needs to be told that submission to God-ordained authority is not the same as silence before injustice — Dietrich Bonhoeffer's category.

5. **Build a real exhortation.** The sermon must move from "trust God" (summary) to "therefore, this week, you will do this specific thing." Options: pay your taxes without bitterness and let that act be a small act of faith; pray for the named leaders by name this week — not for their policies, but for their souls; refuse to speak of governing authorities with contempt in front of your children. These are specific, weekly, and text-grounded.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** This sermon does not have the bloodless correct-doctrine problem — it has the opposite problem: pastoral heat and instinct without adequate doctrinal scaffolding. The fire is present; the structure needs building.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries real pastoral weight in its central conviction — that God ordains even those who offend us, even those who would harm us, and that trust in God's sovereignty is the only solid ground for civic submission — but it does not yet press that weight into the eternal register; it remains political and circumstantial where it should become eschatological and worshipful.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Romans 13:1–7 is a hortatory-didactic passage within a larger paraenetic section, and it does not center on Christ in the way that Romans 3–5 or 8 does. The rubric's Gospel Centrality scoring appropriately penalizes a missing gospel center, but evaluators should note that organic Christological integration in this passage requires the kind of redemptive-historical bridge-building (Christ's own submission to Pilate; the cross as the paradigm of submitting to unjust authority) that is present in 1 Peter 2:13–23. The rubric is well-suited to this passage; the challenge is that the passage itself requires more christological work from the preacher than a text like Romans 8:1. The raw draft's thin gospel center is a genuine weakness, not a rubric artifact.
