INTRODUCTION: THE PURPOSE OF THE BIBLE

I'm not going to be starting a series on Revelation, but if you've brought your Bible — and I
hope you have — we're going to be in the book of Revelation, chapter 2, today.

It strikes me that most people read the book of Revelation trying to predict the future, and
trying to understand exactly where we are in the grand scope of world history or future history.
But at the end of the day, what is the purpose of the Bible?

Let's back it up a step: what is the purpose of the Old Testament? Who can tell me? The Old
Testament tells us about God and Jesus. It points forward to Christ. It has an emphasis on
God's holiness and the consequences of sin. It tells us about God's deliverance for his people,
too, doesn't it? How many times do the Israelites forget about God and do their own thing? How
many times do they forget about God, worship this idol or that idol, this dead stick or that
dead stick — and how many times does that activity get them in trouble, cause them to end up as
slaves or captives here or there? And how many times does God fight for them and free them? It
happens over and over and over. It's this continual cycle of failures and God's rescue —
ultimately pointing to a permanent solution to come, ultimately pointing to our Savior Jesus
Christ.

Likewise the New Testament looks back towards Christ. It points out the foibles of people where
it needs to, but mostly it's telling us about Christ — what Christ has done, and will do. It's a
promise of Jesus' already-and-not-yet victory over sin, and death, and hell, and Satan. It's
pointing to the ultimate rescue — for the Jews first, and also the Gentiles — from sin and
this world.

Revelation, being part of the New Testament, is part of that too. People always want to use the
New Testament as a predictive roadmap, and they all have these complicated graphics and charts.
And while the book of Revelation does show what will happen, it's more meant to be a book of
hope for a persecuted people — pointing to what is to come, pointing to Jesus' victory over the
enemy — even more than being a road map. The purpose of Revelation is to give us hope. Not a
step-by-step diagram — it does that also, I think. But its purpose is hope for the future. Not
confusion, or division. Just hope. I think when we focus only on the predictive, we miss out on
the richness of Scripture and the hope we are supposed to find here.

PEOPLE GROUP OF THE WEEK: CHAMAR OF INDIA

The Chamar people have been imprisoned by the reputation of their ancestors. Chamar means
"leather worker." They tanned cattle leather and made leather products to sell. They were
reputed as poisoning animals to use for their craft. Since cattle were worshiped, Chamars were
considered untouchables and were disallowed education, medical help, and many other benefits.
Today they benefit from India's "reservation" system for underprivileged communities.

Ministry Obstacles: The Chamar are Hindu; they recite mythological tales and sing songs from
their religious epics, often at their festivals. The religious system is a key part of their
identity, leaving no room for Jesus Christ.

FOCUS: VERSE 4 — FORGOTTEN FIRST LOVE

I really want to drill down on verse 4.

I read a number of things this week, and one of them was this long list of signs that you've
forgotten your first love. I've kept some and tossed others out — some were simply repetitive.
But here is what I kept:

You can go hours to days without thinking about God. You lack a desire to spend time with God —
this includes putting other things before God. Appearance, work, an inner coldness towards God,
or his word, or the church. Christianity is more of a checklist. Comparing yourself to others
rather than to God's word. More concerned about the opinions of others than the opinions of
God. You can talk with strangers about your or their kids, but not about things of the Lord. You
prefer the company of people who don't love Christ over those who do. You justify small areas
of disobedience or compromise against God's word. Little things that once pricked the
conscience no longer do. You have a sin you are unwilling to give up for Christ. More concerned
about others' sin than your own. Dealing harshly with those with whom you disagree.

It's a hard list, isn't it? If you answered even one of those with a yes, you might have
forgotten your first love.

GREG MORSE ON FORGOTTEN FIRST LOVE

Greg Morse, a preacher with Desiring God, talked about his own experiences here. He writes:

"During these times, I've wanted to blame God for where I ended up and double down on my
rebellion. But the haunting question God asked his spiritually callous people shoots down all my
excuses, 'What wrong did you find in me that you went far from me?' (see Jeremiah 2:5). When we
find ourselves far from God, he is never the one to blame.

And this makes the darkness darker. I know that spiritual dullness often results from my
treating God like a pigeon in the park to whom I leisurely throw the crumbs of my leftover
devotion after a long day of caring about other things. In such seasons, God allows my
joylessness to snap me out of treating him as a hobby, to learn afresh to seek his face as if
he was, well, God.

I have even dressed up my desertion in religious robes. I might refuse discipline by calling it
legalism; refuse God's presence, calling it freedom; refuse to commune with him, calling it
salvation by grace. Christ's blood becomes that which was shed so that I might safely ignore
him. Even though I love Jesus, my love threatens to grow cold when the familiar becomes taken
for granted and neglected."

[SOURCE VERIFIED: Greg Morse, "Have You Left Your First Love?" Desiring God, April 10, 2025.
https://www.desiringgod.org/articles/have-you-left-your-first-love
See correction log items 1-2 for notes on the Jeremiah 2:5 paraphrase.]

This is exactly what's being talked about here in this passage. The Ephesians had emphasized
doctrinal accuracy at the expense of having a sluggish love for Jesus. The Corinthians had
emphasized the emotional experience at the expense of loving Jesus enough to maintain moral
purity.

How often do we toss our treasure into the trash in just the same way?

ILLUSTRATION: THE GIRL IN THE AMAZON RIVER

I heard a story last week about a situation that happened in Peru. I'm not going to share any
names — just some of the details. You guys remember Peru — you sent me and some others from
church there back about ten years ago. We were up in the north western part of Peru called
Piura. This story takes place in the northern part of the Amazon — the north eastern part.

In the Amazonian rainforest, along the river, there are a lot of little towns, and big ones.
There are a couple of common things that all the little cities do. For one, they almost all dump
their garbage into the Amazon River. Google it sometime — it's awful. Huge, stinking, nasty
piles of garbage leaning out over the river, the river running with trash, giant flotillas of
garbage working their way downstream.

But it's not the worst thing they have in common. Apparently, in situations of sexual
trafficking or sexual promiscuity, if a woman has a child and doesn't want it — or if a woman
has a child and her new boyfriend doesn't want it — they toss the kids in the Amazon River and
hope the gators eat it. The story I heard about was this little girl tossed into the river who
wound up on one of the trash piles and was rescued and adopted by an American couple.

[NOTE: This is an anecdotal/heard report. See correction log item 8. Do not present as a
documented case or statistic.]

Horrifying to think about anyone tossing their child in the Amazon River to die, isn't it?

It's heartbreaking to hear about such irreplaceable treasures being tossed into the river like
that and left for dead. Reminds me of last week's sermon, where Moses' mother had to put him in
the river — but for other reasons. Here in Peru it's often out of convenience. They prefer
other things to their child. They prioritize their comfort, or their relationships, or their job
over their child.

At the end of the day, it's not a lot different than what we do when we forget our first love —
when we forget about Jesus. We take something incredibly precious and of immeasurable worth and
toss it in the trash.

We all do. Church members, pastors — this is a risk for everyone, and most people, if they're
honest, have been through times like this in their life. And if they haven't, give them time.
They will. Looking back, I think of all the kids that went through youth group at Jasmine. I've
looked a lot of them up on Facebook. So many mired in drugs and alcohol. Sex outside of
marriage. Some of them live in a nudist camp with their children. So many, it seems, have
wandered away from their faith — a thing they picked up and put down so easily. My friends,
they are only a few steps further down the road of having forgotten their first love.

THE CURE: VERSE 5 — REMEMBER, REPENT, RETURN

I think verse 5 begins the talk of the cure. Someone read it.

REMEMBER

Pastor Greg writes:

"Do you remember the excitement you had when he plucked you out of darkness? How joyful did
you dance, from an orphan to a son, a dead sinner to a resurrected saint, an enemy with God to
his beloved? Do you remember?"

[SOURCE VERIFIED: Greg Morse, "Have You Left Your First Love?" Desiring God, April 10, 2025.
See correction log item 1, Note on Quote 2: the full published paragraph opens with the
additional sentence "Do you remember when God first awakened your soul?" — omitted in dictation.
Restore for print use.]

Remember how excited you were to spend time in prayer — remember time just flying by. Prayer
wasn't a dose of ill-tasting medicine; it was an opportunity to talk to your Savior! Remember
the vibrancy of worship, that expectation of meeting God in church and the thrill when he showed
up every week. Remember how easy it was to talk to people about the Lord, and the excitement
you had for your Bible — God's love letter to you?

I love that it starts with remembering. So many things start with remembering. When you feel
discouraged and alone, remember what God has brought you through. When you feel cold and the
excitement has worn off, remember.

REPENT

The second part of verse 5 is: repent.

Repent means to turn away from by 180 degrees. So if that back door is a sin and you're walking
towards it, to repent means to turn around and walk up here towards the pulpit, towards the
altar. It's a word picture that reminds me of the Israelites fleeing Egypt. They are leaving
slavery and oppression for freedom and a land flowing with milk and honey. I wonder how many
of them turned around and started walking back to Egypt? Because when we forget our first love,
that is exactly what we are doing.

When we turn for Egypt, when we walk towards our old way of life, we are no better than those
who throw their baby in the Amazon River to die from exposure and alligators. For that we must
repent, and follow Christ.

Don't just say in your heart you want to be better. Actually do the work and be better. Go to
God. Because you want to know a secret? You can't do it in your own power. You need help. I
need help. Go to God and repent. Tell him you've grown cold and hard-hearted. Tell him you've
forgotten your first love. Tell him you haven't loved him as he deserves.

Hebrews 4:16 says: "Let us then with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may
receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need."
[VERIFIED: Hebrews 4:16, ESV. Quoted accurately.]

Boldly go before the throne of grace. One author this week said you must undress your soul in
front of your Savior every night, and rise up early every day to dress yourself with the
heavenly attire of Scripture. I think there is truth in that — an intimacy with God that comes
from that.
[NOTE: "One author this week" — unattributed. Source not identified. See correction log item 6.]

RETURN

Thirdly, we are to return. If repentance is turning away from things, returning is then walking
away — taking steps towards the goal, towards the prize, towards the lover of our souls.

Returning would be: even though we tossed the baby in the river, returning would be jumping in
after him and swimming him back home. Returning is giving the baby the love and attention he
deserves. Returning is lived out by never tossing the baby in the river again.

We are still human. Chances are good that one day you'll wander these dark roads again. They
say the Christian is at his darkest when his soul isn't satisfied in God — when Scripture is
bland, and sermons go unheard, when prayers seem to land on deaf ears, when distractions are
allowed to stop our time with God, and maybe at that point we wonder if we are really even his.
At that point we are at our darkest.

I'm reminded of the old hymn — Dottie knows what number. It says:

"Prone to wander, Lord I feel it,
Prone to leave the God I love."

[VERIFIED: "Come Thou Fount of Every Blessing," verse 3. Robert Robinson, 1758. Lines quoted
accurately.]

If that's where you're at today: Remember. Repent. Return.

CLOSING APPEAL

Maybe you're here today, or watching on Facebook or YouTube, and you've not yet become a
believer in Jesus Christ. If that's you, I want you to hear a little more of the story of the
little girl tossed into the Amazon River. That little girl — she couldn't swim. She was too
little. She couldn't do it on her own. But God sent a rescuer for her. An orphanage found her
and scooped her up. They brought her home, dressed her in nice clothing, gave her food and a
bed and shelter. She was headed for death but was given life.

Apart from Christ we are all that little girl in the river. We can't swim. We're headed for
death. Scripture says we have all sinned and fallen short of the glory of God (Romans 3:23),
and that the wages of sin is death (Romans 6:23). That's what we have coming. Death.
[VERIFIED: Romans 3:23, ESV. Usage appropriate. Romans 6:23a referenced without citation —
accurate.]
