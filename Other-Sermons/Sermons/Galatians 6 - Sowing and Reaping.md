## Main Illustration — David and Svea Flood

*This is a documented historical account. Primary published source: Aggie Hurst, "Aggie: The Inspiring Story of a Girl Without a Country" (1995).*

- Young Swedish couple (David and Svea Flood) called to a single village tribe in the Belgian Congo, 1920s (~650-700 people)
- Tribal chief was suspicious; only permitted one boy to sell them eggs and chicken twice weekly
- After approximately five years, Svea discovers she is pregnant — four months of travel to reach Sweden; they decide to stay
- Svea determines to reach that one boy even if no one else in the village
- One month before her due date: the boy is overcome by grace and prays to receive Christ — the first and only believer in the village
- The chief permits a midwife for the birth; after a week of labor, the baby is born alive; Svea dies hours later, age 27
- David digs the grave himself and places a white cross marked "Svea Flood"
- David declares "God has ruined my life," gives the baby away, and returns to Sweden
- The baby (Aggie) is raised by the Ericksons (who later die of malaria), then by an American couple
- After high school, a Swedish magazine arrives with a photo of the white cross marked "Svea Flood"
- A college professor translates the article: the boy had returned to his village, told his parents, they believed — then friends, neighbors, then the chief — the entire village became Christians
- Aggie tracks down her father, who had remarried; siblings warn her never to mention God
- Aggie tells David the whole story — over 600 in the village came to faith because of Svea's witness to one boy
- David re-dedicates his life to the Lord; he dies the following week, surrounded by family
- Aggie is sent to Africa; she meets the boy, now an old man — he leads a national church of 110,000 people
- Aggie kneels at her mother's grave, fingers in the soil, and thanks God

## Two Responses to Hardship

**David** — focused on his circumstances; tried in his own strength; burned out and grew bitter.

**Svea / Aggie** — life centered on the Lord: *"It's OK, Lord, whatever you want from me."*

> "For someone whose life is centered around Christ, death is a promotion."

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is missing from the manuscript.** The single most significant weakness: there is no moment in the surviving text where Christ's atoning work is named, where justification is proclaimed, or where a sinner is given the terms of salvation. The free-will quotes presuppose the gospel; the Svea Flood story lives in the gospel's world. But the gospel itself is never stated. A congregation that does not already know the gospel would not learn it from this sermon.

2. **The Galatians context is unexplored.** "Flesh" in Galatians 5-6 is not merely a moral category — it is covenantal. The "sowing to the flesh" that Paul warns against is specifically the flesh-based religion of the circumcision party: trying to be righteous through external law-keeping. The sermon treats "flesh" as generic self-centeredness and "Spirit" as generic God-dependence, missing the text's precise argument. This is a significant exegetical loss.

3. **Application lacks differentiation and specificity.** The sermon addresses the discouraged and the weary — a real pastoral need — but does not distinguish among hearers or give specific texture to "sowing to the Spirit." What does sowing to the Spirit look like on a Tuesday morning in this congregation's actual life — in work, marriage, finances, evangelism to the Sindhi Samas mentioned in the header? The Svea Flood narrative suggests missions, but this is not pressed into the congregation's hands as a live commission.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the Svea Flood illustration, while powerful, risks becoming the sermon's primary content rather than its illustration. A congregation may leave with "I need to be faithful like Svea" rather than "I need to trust the God who raised Christ, who is the source of every harvest." The story is so compelling that it can carry the room without ever arriving at the explicit gospel. This is the danger of excellent illustrations: they can do the emotional work of the gospel without the theological work of the gospel.

Additionally, the People Group note (Sindhi Samas of Pakistan) in the header suggests a specific missiological context, but this context does not appear to shape the sermon's application in any surviving section. If this sermon was preached in a context related to unreached peoples or missionary sending, that context is a massive missed connection — the Svea Flood story is itself a missions story that could have been used to name and call the congregation to specific engagement with unreached peoples.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 10 is almost entirely absent: *"as we have opportunity, let us do good to everyone, and especially to those who are of the household of faith."* This verse introduces the concentric circles of Christian obligation — the church first, then the world — and it is directly relevant to the People Group context noted in the header. The Svea Flood story is a story about doing good to one boy who had no access to the gospel. Connecting verse 10 explicitly to the unreached, to the Sindhi Samas, to the congregation's specific calling in mission — this was the sermon's natural landing point and it was left unclaimed.

Also missed: the explicit connection between Svea's suffering and the sufferings of Christ. Svea's death in the Congo is not merely a moral example of perseverance — it is the pattern of the grain of wheat falling into the ground and dying (John 12:24). This redemptive-historical connection would have rooted the entire illustration in the gospel rather than leaving it as an inspiring biography.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3-5 Actionable)

1. **Add an explicit gospel paragraph before the exhortation.** After the Svea Flood story has done its work and the congregation feels the weight of sowing and waiting, name clearly: "But here is the ground of every harvest — Christ himself sowed in blood at Calvary, and the harvest of that sowing is the forgiveness of every sinner who trusts him. Svea could sow because she was already the fruit of Christ's sowing. So can you." This resolves the fatal flaw flag and roots the entire narrative in the cross.

2. **Open the Galatians context.** Add 2-3 sentences explaining what "flesh" means in this letter: "Paul isn't only talking about moral failure. In Galatians, the flesh is every attempt to be right before God on our own terms — through our own performance, our own religion, our own willpower. That is what Paul says will produce corruption." This connects the exegetical weight of the text to the free-will quotes that follow.

3. **Apply verse 10 to the Sindhi Samas (or to your congregation's specific mission field).** "Paul says: as we have opportunity, do good to everyone — *everyone*, including people like the Sindhi Samas of Pakistan who have almost no access to the gospel. Svea's one opportunity was one boy. What is yours?" This converts the illustration from inspiration to commission.

4. **Press the free-will quotes into a specific temptation rather than a theological position.** Instead of presenting them as a sequence, lead with the temptation: "Some of you are trying to sow to the Spirit through sheer determination. You think if you just try harder, give more, read more, pray more — *in your own strength* — you will produce the harvest. Here is what the wisest men in church history say about that strategy..." This makes the quotes applicatory rather than decorative.

5. **Add a differentiated closing exhortation.** Address at minimum three groups: (a) the weary believer who has been sowing for years without seeing a harvest — "Don't give up. In due season. Trust the God of Svea."; (b) the nominal Christian who knows the truth but is sowing to the flesh — "You know this text. You have heard it. But where are you sowing this week?"; (c) the unbeliever who has never trusted Christ — "Before you can sow to the Spirit, you must be born of the Spirit. That happens only one way: by faith in Christ crucified and risen."
