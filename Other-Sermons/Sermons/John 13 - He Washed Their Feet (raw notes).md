# John 13 — He Washed Their Feet

*Raw notes — continuation of John 13 series. Previous week covered the setting and the condition of the disciples' feet.*

---

## Scripture

John 13:1–17 (ESV)

---

## I. He Washed Us Clean at Salvation

We talked last week about what those feet must have looked like — cracked, calloused, caked with the dust of Judean roads. That's not background color. That's the point.

Because that's us. Apart from Christ, that's what we looked like on the inside.

Paul puts it plainly in 2 Corinthians 5:17 — *"Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come."*

And a few verses later, he gets even more specific about how that happened:

*"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."* — 2 Corinthians 5:21

Think about what that means. Before Christ, our career was sinning. We worked our whole lives at it — incapable of doing the right things for the right reasons. Romans 6:23 tells us what we earned: *"the wages of sin is death."* That's the pension plan. That's the retirement package. An eternity separated from God — and we earned every bit of it.

But God, through Jesus, at the moment of salvation — washed us clean. Just like Christ got down on His knees and washed those feet.

The feet that looked cracked and unclean. The heart that was worse. He washed both.

---

## II. The Word Keeps Us Clean

Now here's where it gets practical.

You remember the children's sermon a while back — the glass of water with drops of color going in, one after another, until the water was dark. And then one drop — one drop of the blood of Jesus — and the water ran clear.

That's what Christ did at salvation.

But we don't stay home after we're saved. We go back out into the world. We get dust on our feet again. Not the dust of the unregenerate heart — that's gone, the old has passed away — but the daily grime of living in a fallen world.

And this is what the Word does. Hebrews 4:12 — *"For the word of God is living and active, sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing to the division of soul and of spirit, of joints and of marrow, and discerning the thoughts and intentions of the heart."*

The Word gets to the places we can't reach ourselves.

There's a tradition in Jewish practice — the high priests who ministered in the Temple had a set of cleansing rituals for whenever they had to leave the inner sanctuary and go back out among the people. The further they went out, the more washing was required before they could return. Step out briefly and come right back — wash the hands and feet. Go all the way out to the courtyard and spend time with the crowds — full immersion before re-entering the presence of God.

We're not under those rituals. We've been hidden in Christ with God. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come. But the picture holds — the further we wander from time with the Lord, the more we need to be brought back.

So we come back through the Word. Through prayer. Through fellowship. Through worship.

Not to earn our standing — that was settled at the cross. But because Christ washed those feet, and we are people who live near the basin.

---

## III. Not All of You Are Clean

Now here's where Jesus says something that changes the temperature in the room.

Not all of you are clean.

He knew Judas was in that circle. He washed Judas's feet anyway. And then He said it plainly — *one of you is not clean.*

That's not just a historical footnote about a first-century betrayer. That's a word for tonight.

Consider the numbers for a moment. The statistics vary depending on the survey, but most estimates put the percentage of Americans who have genuinely trusted Christ somewhere around 13 percent. Which means that out of every ten people you run into on any given day — the cashier at the grocery store, the kid who bags your groceries, the attendant at the gas station, the housekeeper, the neighbors on either side of you, your co-workers, the mechanic, your nieces, your nephews, your brothers and sisters and cousins — almost nine of those ten people are heading somewhere they were not designed to go.

Not all of them are clean.

And Jesus knew it. He always knows it. He knew it when He washed Judas's feet. He knows it now.

Which raises a question about us.

---

### Pride Won't Go There. Humility Will.

Jesus got down on His knees and washed feet He knew were going to walk Him to Gethsemane. That's not just humility as a character trait — that's humility as a *mission*.

We've been given the same mission. And the same obstacle stands in our way that has always stood there.

Pride.

Pride worries about saying the wrong thing. Pride worries about feeling foolish. Pride worries about accidentally telling the same person twice. Pride keeps score on outcomes and decides it's not worth the risk.

Humility cares about following Christ even when you feel unqualified. Humility gets down on its knees even when it's uncomfortable.

I heard a testimony on the radio some time back — a woman who came to faith in Christ through a sermon on women's roles in the church. Of all the sermons, in all the churches, on all the Sundays — that was the one God used to open her eyes. The very passage she had raised as an objection became the door. If God can do that, He can use you too.

Moses stood in front of the burning bush and told God he couldn't speak. God said — *"Now therefore go, and I will be with your mouth and teach you what you shall speak."* (Exodus 4:12)

Jonah ran the other direction and preached the shortest, worst-delivered sermon in the history of prophecy — and God used it to bring an entire city to its knees.

If He can do that with Moses the stutterer and Jonah the fugitive, He can do something with the rest of us when we stumble and bumble our way through the conversation.

There is probably someone in this room tonight who has never come to genuine faith in Jesus Christ. If that's you — you are not here by accident. God has a purpose for you that did not begin tonight, and it does not end tonight. But it begins with Him. And the most important thing you could ever do is come talk to me after this service.

---

## IV. He Knew Judas. He Knows You.

Jesus knew exactly who was in that upper room. He knew Peter, who was about to argue with Him about the foot-washing and then deny He ever knew Him. He knew John, who would be the only disciple standing near the cross. He knew Thomas, who would need to touch the wounds before he believed. And He knew Judas — who had been walking with Christ, talking with Christ, present for every miracle, and was there that night to line his pockets.

Judas wasn't there for Jesus. He was there for what Jesus could do for him.

I think that still happens. People come to church because they were raised to. People come because they can make connections, build relationships, find a network. I've watched people choose a church the way they choose a country club — based on who attends, what it can do for them, what doors it might open. Those people exist. And they know how to look the part.

But Christ knew Judas. And He knows us.

You can make the people around you think you've got it together. You might be able to maintain that for years. But Jesus looked across that table at Judas, took the bread, and knew exactly what was in his heart. He knows what's in yours.

So if you are here tonight for reasons other than drawing closer to the Lord — hear me say this gently: you are not here by mistake. God is not surprised by your presence. He put you in that seat on purpose. And He loves you enough not to leave you the way you walked in.

There is only one thing that changes the question of where you spend eternity — and it is not church attendance, not good behavior, not sincere effort. It is Christ. His death on the cross. His resurrection three days later. His righteousness counted as yours.

*"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."* — John 3:16

If you were to stand before God tonight and He asked you why He should let you in — and your answer is anything other than "because of what Jesus did on the cross" — we need to talk.

And if you can't say with certainty that you know that you know that you know — come find me.

---

## Closing

The old hymn says it plainly:

*Here I raise my Ebenezer, hither by thy help I've come,*
*And I hope by thy good pleasure safely to arrive at home.*
*Jesus sought me when a stranger, wandering from the fold of God,*
*He to rescue me from danger, interposed His precious blood.*

"Come Thou Fount of Every Blessing" — Robert Robinson, 1758.

We didn't deserve it. Not one of us. But God loved us so much He sent His Son — that whosoever believes in Him shall not perish, but have everlasting life.

That's still the offer. It's still open.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Warmth with a Shallow Exegetical Root — Gospel Present, Text Thinly Worked**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A pastorally warm opening of John 13:1–17 that moves with genuine gospel instinct and reaches the conscience at moments, while leaving the passage's own argument largely undeveloped and its structural center (the foot-washing as enacted parable of servanthood and love) functionally replaced by imported cross-references.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**Conditionally Pass — with significant reservation.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon does arise from John 13, but the main point it develops — salvation washes clean, the Word maintains cleanliness, and we must reach the unwashed around us — is a synthesis the preacher has assembled *from* the passage rather than the passage's own dominant claim. John 13:1–17's chief burden (as signaled by Jesus' own interpretation in vv. 12–17) is servanthood and humility as the pattern of discipleship: "as I have done to you, you also ought to do." The sermon touches this but does not make it the governing thesis. The gospel insertion in Section III–IV, while pastorally valuable, is not the text's own primary move.

*Authority Test:* The preacher does not stand above the text as editor in a dismissive sense — he does not soften hard material. But he does select lightly from the passage, allowing the text to serve a pre-formed outline rather than being governed by the text's own argument. This is a structural concern, not an integrity concern.

**Result: The sermon passes the gate loosely, but the main point of the sermon is not fully the main point of the passage. This is flagged but not treated as a hard failure — the gospel is present and the text is genuinely in view. However, the score reflects the drift.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No fatal flaw flags triggered.** The gospel is clearly present (Section IV includes explicit substitutionary language, John 3:16, and a direct call). Christ is essential to the sermon. Application is present, though it skews toward evangelism and personal cleanliness more than the text's corporate discipleship thrust. Exhortation is present. The sermon is accessible.

*Near-miss noted:* The exhortation in Sections III–IV is directed primarily at the unconverted. The sermon's call to the already-converted regarding foot-washing as a pattern of humble service — the text's actual imperative in vv. 14–15 — is underdeveloped. This approaches but does not trigger Fatal Flaw #5 (no actual exhortation) for the believing hearer.

### 5. Score Breakdown

The text's christological depth — "you are not yet fully clean" (v. 10), the "one of you" that hangs in the air — is worked. But the governing argument is imported rather than discovered.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Primarily of Christ — the gospel presentation is Christ-centered, and the Judas section directs the hearer toward Christ's omniscience and grace rather than self-improvement. Partial yes.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
The Judas section produces a genuine encounter — "He knows what's in yours" is not merely informational. Sections I and II are more instructional than confrontational. Partial yes.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
The unconverted hearer is convicted. The believing hearer is primarily comforted (Section II) and moderately encouraged (Sections III–IV). The deep conviction that the text offers — the foot-washing as a dismantling of pride, the call to costly servanthood — is not fully pressed on the believer.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is genuinely present and clearly stated.** Section IV contains precise substitutionary language, a direct call to the unconverted, and a specific invitation ("come find me"). This is not assumed — it is proclaimed. The line "His righteousness counted as yours" is theologically exact.

2. **The Judas application is sharp and searching.** "You can make the people around you think you've got it together... but Jesus looked across that table at Judas... and He knows what's in yours." This is conscience-level preaching. It corners the self-deceived nominal churchgoer without alienating the genuine believer.

3. **Illustration quality is above average.** The water-drop children's sermon callback, the country club image, and "we are people who live near the basin" are all native to the congregation's experience, clear in their function, and genuinely memorable. The Moses/Jonah pairing effectively dismantles the pride-of-inadequacy excuse.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text's own governing argument is never preached.** John 13:12–17 is the passage's interpretive key: Jesus himself explains the foot-washing as a model his disciples must follow. "As I have done to you, you also ought to do." The sermon mentions this obliquely in the pride/humility section but never makes it the sermon's central imperative. The text is used as a springboard for a salvation/sanctification/evangelism framework that the passage does not itself generate.

2. **The believing hearer is not cornered by the foot-washing's demand.** The application of the text to the church's corporate life — specific, costly, humiliating service to one another, to enemies, to those who will betray — is absent. The foot-washing is applied metaphorically to salvation and the Word, but never pressed as what disciples must actually do. This is the text's primary burden, and the believer sitting in the pew leaves largely uninstructed about it.

3. **Section II (The Word Keeps Us Clean) is the weakest exegetical move.** The connection between foot-washing and Scripture's sanctifying function relies on the priestly washing tradition (flagged as unverified) and a general association rather than anything in the text itself. John 13:10 does not teach that the Word functions like the foot-washing — the word "clean" in v. 10 refers to those who have "bathed" (positional cleansing, already granted by Jesus' word in John 15:3), not to a sanctification-by-Word dynamic. This point may be true theologically, but it is not what this passage teaches.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears not to see that the text's primary imperative — "you also ought to wash one another's feet" (v. 14) — is a direct, concrete call to humility and mutual service within the community of disciples. The sermon's evangelical instinct (reaching the lost around us) is admirable, but it has effectively displaced the text's own discipleship imperative. The outward mission (Section III) has crowded out the inward formation the text demands. This blind spot likely reflects the preacher's evangelistic burden more than any theological error — but it produces a sermon on John 13 that doesn't primarily call the church to wash one another's feet.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

Section II feels like strong, nuanced pastoral theology — the distinction between initial cleansing and ongoing sanctification is carefully handled, and the language is warm and accessible. But this section is built on a connection the text does not make. John 13:10 is not a sanctification text in the sense the sermon uses it. The priestly washing illustration is evocative but unverified. The result is a section that sounds like careful exegesis but is actually careful pastoral synthesis — true in what it affirms, false in attributing that truth to this passage. The confidence of the writing masks the thinness of the textual foundation.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The foot-washing in John's Gospel is unmistakably proleptic — it anticipates the cross. Jesus "laid aside his outer garments" (v. 4) — the same verb used in John 10:17–18 for laying down his life. The basin and towel are the cross in miniature. This is John's own typological logic. If the preacher had surfaced this, the foot-washing would have become a living picture of the atonement — not a metaphor imported from outside the passage, but the passage's own gospel. The congregation would have seen the cross in the upper room. This connection is fully available in the text and was not taken.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild Section II from the text, not from the priestly tradition.** Remove or heavily qualify the priestly washing illustration (as the preaching notes already flag). Replace with John 15:3 — "Already you are clean because of the word that I have spoken to you" — which is the text's own teaching on cleansing through the Word. This makes the connection exegetical rather than associative.

2. **Add a direct application of vv. 14–15 to the believing hearer.** After or within Section III, press the question: "Who do you need to wash feet for this week?" Name specific categories — the difficult brother, the draining family member, the person whose betrayal you already sense. The text makes this the primary call to the disciple. It must become the primary call in the sermon.

3. **Surface the proleptic/typological connection between the foot-washing and the cross.** Point to "laid aside his outer garments" (v. 4) and connect to John 10:17–18. "What Jesus did in that upper room with a basin and towel, He was about to do on a cross with nails and a crown." This does not require lengthy exegesis — two or three sentences. It would transform the sermon's christological depth without restructuring it.

4. **Sharpen the exhortation to the believer.** "We are people who live near the basin" is a beautiful line but is descriptive, not imperative. Convert it to a call: "Go live near the basin. Go get low. Go find the person whose feet no one wants to wash and wash them." The sermon needs a "therefore" that lands on the church as a community of servants, not only as a collection of individuals with evangelistic obligations.

5. **Consider reordering the structure to follow the text's argument.** The text moves: (a) Jesus washes feet — an act of incomprehensible condescension; (b) Peter protests — pride confronted; (c) "not all of you are clean" — the conscience pressed; (d) "as I have done, you also ought to do" — the imperative. A sermon structured around this movement would be tighter, more text-driven, and would carry the same pastoral concerns while being governed by the passage rather than imported to it.

The rubric fits well here because the text provides its own interpretive key (vv. 12–17), making the Berean Gate and Scopus Test clear and fair. The FCF criterion is especially productive for this passage — the specific fallen condition John 13 addresses (pride that refuses to kneel, religious proximity that substitutes for genuine union with Christ) is rich and largely untouched in this sermon. No genre strain on the rubric. The passage rewards the full framework.
