## "On His Terms"

### Opening — On Your Terms

We are a society of instant everything.

Fast food. Redbox. Movies on demand at the click of a button. We can gratify ourselves with a remote control or a mouse in virtually any direction we choose, at any hour, with almost no waiting. Most of the things we want, we can have before dinner.

And so we have quietly built a way of thinking about the world that says: things should come to me on my terms. I decide what I want, when I want it, and how I want it delivered. That's the air we breathe.

And somewhere along the way — and this is the uncomfortable thing — we let that thinking into the way we present the gospel.

We talk about the Romans Road. The four spiritual laws. Come forward during the invitation, raise your hand, say this prayer, and you're in. The emphasis lands heavily on you. Your decision. Your moment. The way Christianity gets packaged in much of American church culture, it sounds like Jesus is waiting for you to invite Him into your life — like He's standing at the door hoping you'll find time for Him in your schedule.

I want to step back for a moment and ask: did you set aside your deity to come to earth? Did you go from the unimaginable glory of the Father's presence to abject poverty in a Roman province? Did you allow yourself to be humiliated, beaten, and killed for things you were not responsible for? When you have done those things — when salvation cost you what it cost Christ — then perhaps salvation can center on you. Until then, salvation is about Christ. His work. His blood. His terms.

That shift — from Jesus on your terms to Jesus on His terms — is exactly what Luke 14:25-35 is about. And it is going to make us uncomfortable.

---

### Context

Look at verse 25 when we get there. It says *great crowds accompanied him.* Jesus is not talking to a skeptical crowd. He's talking to followers — people who are walking with Him, presumably committed, presumably enthusiastic. They think they're in.

And what does Jesus do? He turns around and tries to thin the crowd. He doesn't seize the moment to get more people on board. He doesn't launch a campaign. He turns and tells them what this is going to cost, and He does it in terms designed to stop people in their tracks.

That detail alone tells us something. Jesus wasn't interested in padding His numbers with people who hadn't counted the cost.

### Part 1 — Superior Love

The first thing that would have hit the crowd is verse 26. *If anyone comes to me and does not hate his own father and mother and wife and children and brothers and sisters, yes, and even his own life, he cannot be my disciple.*

That is a hard sentence.

And if you think it sounds hard to us, imagine how it landed in a first-century near-eastern context — a world built on honor, family loyalty, and ancestral obligation. You didn't just have a family. Your family was your identity, your financial network, your social standing, your entire support structure. Insulting a man's mother was an invitation to serious violence. Honor was the currency of daily life, and family was where you stored it.

I have a friend who spent time as a missionary in Asia Minor. He told me it was nearly impossible to find people willing to seriously hear the gospel — not because they were hostile to the idea of God, but because they couldn't get past what it would mean for their family relationships. He said they seemed willing to go to hell with their parents rather than go to heaven without them. I heard similar things from a missionary working with Asian college students in New York — Chinese, Korean, Japanese students who told him plainly: they wouldn't convert without their parents. Their parents wouldn't convert without the grandparents. If the grandparents had already died, the conversation was over.

Jesus walked right into that world and said: if you love your family more than you love me, you cannot follow me.

Now — before we get lost in the word "hate," we should note that Jesus uses this kind of language in other places where the meaning is clearly about priority, not emotion. In Matthew 10:37, the parallel passage, He says *whoever loves father or mother more than me is not worthy of me.* So what He's calling for is not contempt for your family. He's calling for a love so supreme that, by comparison, every other love looks like hatred.

That doesn't soften the demand. It just clarifies it.

---

Here is what that looks like in practice. John Bunyan was a poor preacher in 17th-century England — a Puritan at a time when being a Puritan could land you in prison. And it did. The government told him to stop preaching, and he kept preaching, and in 1660 they locked him up.

He sat in the Bedford jail for twelve years. And while he was there, he wrote a book — *The Pilgrim's Progress* — which became one of the most widely read books in the English language. C.H. Spurgeon, who became one of the most notable preachers in modern history, wrote that next to the Bible the book he valued most was *Pilgrim's Progress*, and that he had read it at least a hundred times.

But the book wasn't all Bunyan wrote in prison. He also wrote *Grace Abounding to the Chief of Sinners*, and in it he described what it cost him to stay in that jail. He had a blind daughter — Mary, his eldest — and she and the rest of his family were left without provision every day he sat in his cell. He wrote:

> *The parting with my wife and poor children hath oft been to me in this place as the pulling the flesh from my bones; and that not only because I am somewhat too fond of these great mercies, but also because I should have often brought to my mind the many hardships, miseries, and wants that my poor family was like to meet with, should I be taken from them, especially my poor blind child, who lay nearer my heart than all I had besides.*

And then:

> *O I saw in this condition I was a man who was pulling down his house upon the head of his wife and children; yet thought I, I must do it, I must do it.*

*(John Bunyan, Grace Abounding to the Chief of Sinners)*

I must do it.

What kept him there? Not stubbornness. Not pride. He loved his family deeply — he says so plainly. But he loved Christ more. His Savior, as Bunyan put it in that same season, required the superior love. And Bunyan gave it.

That is what verse 26 looks like when it becomes a life.

### Part 2 — Heart, Not Obligation

Now I want to spend a moment on something that sits underneath all of this, because there is a wrong way to hear what Jesus is demanding.

A pastor I have read and listened to — David Platt — described it this way in one of his sermons. He said Christianity is not begrudging obedience to Christ. And he put it in terms of marriage. He said: if he came home and greeted his wife Heather with a big kiss, and she stepped back and asked *what was that for*, the wrong answer would be *the marriage book told me to.* At that point the book would be going somewhere unpleasant, because she doesn't want to be loved out of compulsion. She wants to be loved because he loves her. What he does for her should come from his heart, not from a checklist.

The same question faces us in the room tonight. Do you obey Christ because a book told you to? Or because you want Christ — because you love Him with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength?

That's not the same question as: do you teach Sunday school, or raise your kids in church, or show up on Sunday morning? Cut through all of that and ask yourself the thing underneath: *Do you want Christ?*

And if you're sitting there thinking *I want to want him* — you know in your own heart whether that's the honest answer or whether it's the safe answer. Do you want Christ more than your kids? More than your parents? More than your house and your car and your hobbies? If He asked you to give it up today, would you?

Bunyan knew the answer to that question. It cost him twelve years and the daily anguish of knowing his blind daughter was going without.

---

### Part 3 — Count the Cost

Now Jesus does something interesting. He doesn't stop at the demand. He follows the demand with two parables — the tower builder and the king going to war — and they both make the same point: sit down and think before you start.

The tower builder who doesn't count the cost lays a foundation and can't finish. Everyone who walks by sees it. *This man began to build and was not able to finish.* The incomplete tower becomes a monument to his poor judgment. The king who doesn't count the cost marches out against twenty thousand men with ten thousand, and either gets slaughtered or has to send for terms of peace with his army already in the field — humiliated.

Jesus is saying: don't follow me without thinking about what it is you're signing up for.

That is not what the crowd expected to hear.

What does counting the cost look like? It's not a complicated calculation.

Your money. A professor I had used to say: show me your checkbook and I'll show you your priorities. He was right. Does your spending reflect a wartime faith — the faith of someone who believes the eternal souls of people around them are at stake — or a peacetime faith that keeps Jesus in one comfortable corner of a comfortable life? Many of you already give sacrificially, and I'm not here to hammer on that. But money is one measure.

Your time. If you logged your hours for a week — time on television, on hobbies, on entertainment — and set that against time in the Word, time in prayer, time in active ministry, what would the ledger show? Some of you cannot serve physically. You are old or sick or housebound. Then *pray.* Pray for this church. Pray for our country. Pray for the young families here and the kids coming up in them. Pray for Christians dying for their faith in places where the church is under open assault — people whose suffering puts our inconveniences in proper proportion. You don't need to be able to stand or speak to do the most powerful work available to us. You need to be able to pray.

---

### Part 4 — Saltless Salt

The passage ends with a warning, and it's one of the stranger images in the Gospels. *Salt is good, but if salt has lost its taste, how shall its saltiness be restored? It is of no use either for the soil or for the manure pile. It is thrown away.*

Jesus is talking about the disciple who never counted the cost — who started building and couldn't finish, who began following and drifted away. A disciple who never really counted the cost becomes like salt that's lost its savor. Useless. Not even fit for the compost heap. Thrown out.

That is a sobering image. And Jesus puts it at the end of this passage on purpose. He's warning the crowd: don't start something you haven't counted. The half-hearted follower does no one any good — not the kingdom, not the world around them, not themselves.

---

There are people in Pasco County right now who need the real thing. Not saltless Christianity — the kind that blends in with the culture, costs nothing, and transforms no one. A while back I saw a video on Facebook from a rapper who had passed through this area and remembered it vividly for the wrong reasons. He called Pasco County the capital of pill country in America, and the video showed exactly what he meant — people passed out under old drive-through canopies, footage of Hudson and Shady Hills looking like a war zone of addiction.

Those people need to know that Christ can free them. That there is hope. That the same God who is sovereign over every wave and every storm — the God who redirected a prophet with a fish, who held back rain for three and a half years, who fed a widow on the last of her oil — that God is sovereign over addiction, over despair, over the wreckage of a life spent chasing the wrong things. There is no mountain high enough, no addiction strong enough, no city far enough to keep God from reaching someone He is drawing to Himself.

But it takes people whose salt has not lost its savor. People who have counted the cost, decided to follow, and actually followed.

That's what this world needs from the church.

---

### Gospel

*Choose you this day whom you will serve.*

That line from Joshua is exactly what Jesus is pressing in this passage. Elijah drew the same line on Mount Carmel — not between good people and bad people, but between those who worshipped the Lord and those who didn't. The line is the same tonight.

Are you going to spend your life in pursuit of comfort, pleasure, and the things that feel good for a season and leave you empty? Or are you going to take up your cross and follow Christ?

If you have never come to that point — if you have been in the building for years without really counting the cost, or if tonight is the first time you're hearing any of this — I want to say clearly: following Jesus starts with receiving what He did for you. He went to the cross voluntarily, in your place, for sins He did not commit, so that the debt you owe could be paid. He rose from the dead. And He is offering you life — not on your terms, but on His.

And His terms are this: come to me. Trust me. Love me above everything else. Take up your cross and follow.

One day this world will pass away — the infirmities, the sickness, the addiction, the sorrow. Those who are His will walk in glorified bodies with the loved ones who've gone before, worshipping the Savior for eternity. But it starts tonight with counting the cost and deciding.

As the music begins: if you have never made that decision, come find me.

*He who has ears to hear, let him hear.*
