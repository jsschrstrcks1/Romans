# Old vs New — Hebrews 1:1-14
### Raw transcript (~2014–2016, Obama era)

---

Old word from God, new Word from God.

The Old Testament showed God's superiority over idols — Elijah and the prophets of Baal at Mt. Carmel. Showed God's supremacy over our own stubborn will, over the weather, over animals — Jonah. Showed God's sovereignty over enemy nations — Gideon, Samson. God is sovereign over every giant in our life — David and Goliath.

The Old Testament shows God's sovereignty over so many aspects of life.

The New Testament is like the crescendo of the orchestra and proves through Christ God is sovereign over death! Christ is sovereign over sin. Addiction. Sexual immorality. Idolatry of every kind. Over the weather. Over demons. Everything God showed his sovereignty over in the Old Testament, Christ shows his sovereignty over in the New!

Christ's victory is complete and total. His sovereignty is total.

If God is sovereign over all these things, he can handle your troubles too. If he can overcome an army of thousands with an army of hundreds, he can help you meet your bills. He can help you deal with unruly neighbors, and family stresses. He can give you the courage to share the gospel with your kids. He can break the cycles of addiction. He heals the lame, and sets captives free! The sovereignty of God is complete and total over everything through Christ Jesus.

---

Jesus demonstrated God's righteousness. Vs 8–9.

Jesus was still new to the ministry, and so the Devil himself tried to push Christ to sin three times.

First was the bread. Jesus was hungry. I've never fasted for forty days, but at that point I imagine you would be near death. The body needs food, and likely no more than on that 40th day. 40 is significant: Moses fasted in the desert for 40 days, Elijah fasted in the desert for 40 days and was tested by the brook, the Israelites wandered through the desert for 40 days.

Jesus could have turned a stone to bread. It was within the scope of his power. Would have demonstrated a lack of obedience, and lack of faith in God and God's sovereignty to take care of Christ's physical needs.

God always comes through with our needs, even if not in the time frame we would have liked.

Christ succeeded where the Israelites failed. The desert was a time of testing for them, to see if they would follow God. The Israelites failed, Adam failed, Moses failed, Abraham failed, and I would have failed. Only Jesus had a chance at succeeding and praise God he did!

The second temptation according to Matthew — and I won't get into it, but in the Greek, in Matthew the way he forms the sentence seems to indicate that he was repeating the temptations in order, unlike Luke, who in this case ordered them according to his own interests. Luke had a strong interest in the temple. Anyway, the second temptation: Satan tries to get Jesus to toss himself off the top of the temple saying that surely God wouldn't allow him to die.

This is in part a mocking of Christ. Just a moment ago, Christ in essence told Satan that God could be trusted to provide for him physically, that God wasn't going to let him die. Satan more or less comes back saying "maybe so, why don't you prove that. If God will protect you, then he will surely save you if you jump off the temple."

Interesting to note that the Devil changed Jesus' physical location. Whether he picked Christ up and flew him, or did something more instant like the teleporter on Star Trek, the Devil "took" Christ from one place to another. I'm happy this doesn't regularly happen to servants of God!

Jesus in his response alludes to what happened at Massah in Exodus 17:1-7. The Israelites were thirsty. That was to be expected after such a long walk in the desert. Instead of depending on God to meet their needs, they grumbled against Moses. Instead of depending on God to meet their needs they wished for the slavery of their old life in Egypt.

How often do we do this? Scripture says when we do this we are like a dog returning to its vomit.

We have victory over sin, freedom from its bondage and yet so many times we return to it over and over and over.

Sin will keep you from the word, or the word will keep you from sin.

Rejoice in the Lord and again I say rejoice. Whatever is true, whatever is noble, whatever is right, whatever is pure, if it is lovely, admirable, excellent or praiseworthy think on these things.

Keep your focus off yourself, keep your focus on God, and everything else fades away into the background.

When you feel temptation: Flee if possible, like Joseph. Focus on God always — Jesus memorized scripture for just that reason. Rejoice in the Lord. Think about the attributes of God. Spend some time in prayer. Get your mind off yourself!

Finally Satan tried to tempt Christ by offering him the kingdom, without paying the price for it on the cross. It's like every cartoon bad guy: just when he thinks he has the hero in his trap, he offers the hero a place at his side; or death.

Christ chose death, and in his death he (Col 2:14) nailed the record of our wrongdoing to the cross. Heb 1:3 says he made purification for us on the cross.

Perhaps the most difficult point prior to the crucifixion and God turning his back on his son, was the night before. His closest friends let him down. One of his friends betrayed him. Cried tears of blood knowing what would come, and still he was obedient unto death.

Christ took on the sins of the world, took their punishment, defeated death and Satan and set us free!

David Platt says Jesus took the cup of God's wrath, drank down every drop, flipped it upside down at the cross and said it is finished.

Vs 12 — if the 80 or 90 years that a person can reasonably expect to live is to God as the changing of clothes might be to us, then who we elect to be our president must be like a breath of air. Air is necessary, air is important. From the perspective of eternity, who leads America is a small thing. Politics are a small thing. To the sovereign triune God nuclear weapons are a small thing. Barack Obama (or any other president) might think he's hot stuff, but where was he when God hung the stars in the sky? God who is sovereign over everything, can't be usurped by any man. Our God is sovereign even over our leaders.

So as the election cycle warms up — yes, vote. No, don't let fear, and fear mongering by the media make you anxious.

Philippians 4:6: "Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."

If this one wins, or that one wins — so what? Whoever will be our leader will be our leader not by our vote, but by God's sovereignty. Yes, vote. But don't be surprised when God allows a leader to be raised up for our punishment. Maybe a true believer will be elected, or maybe we'll get another punishment. But in the end, be anxious for nothing. Let God's peace guard your heart in Christ Jesus.

---

Maybe you don't yet know Christ. If that's the case, come talk to me. Maybe you have been looking for a church, if that's the case come talk to me. Maybe you've been too focused on yourself and not focused enough on Christ. If that's the case, and you can come up, then come on up and talk to God. Make things right! Set your eyes upon Christ, the author and perfector of our faith! As the worship team comes up and sings a song, be thinking about these things. There is no judgment to be found here, just Jesus.

## Corrections Log

**IMPORTANT: Review before reuse. Items marked ⚠️ need correction or clarification.**

### Scripture — Verified Accurate
1. **Hebrews 1:8-9 (ESV) — confirmed.** "Your throne, O God, is forever and ever, the scepter of uprightness is the scepter of your kingdom. You have loved righteousness and hated wickedness…" Quoting Psalm 45:6-7. Using this for Christ's righteousness is appropriate.
2. **Hebrews 1:12 (ESV) — confirmed.** "Like a robe you will roll them up, like a garment they will be changed. But you are the same, and your years will have no end." The clothing/changing metaphor is genuinely in the text (quoting Psalm 102:25-27).
3. **Philippians 4:6-7 (ESV) — exact match.** The sermon's quotation matches the ESV word-for-word.
4. **Matthew 4 / Luke 4 — temptation order confirmed.** Matthew uses temporal adverbs (*tote*, *palin*) indicating chronological sequence; Luke uses generic conjunctions (*kai*, *de*). Scholarly consensus: Matthew's order is chronological, Luke's is thematic (climaxing at the temple, fitting Luke's Jerusalem emphasis).
5. **Exodus 17:1-7 — Massah reference confirmed.** v.7: "And he called the name of the place Massah and Meribah." Jesus' response to the second temptation (Deut 6:16) does allude to this incident.
6. **Moses fasted 40 days — confirmed.** Exodus 24:18, 34:28; Deuteronomy 9:9, 9:18. On Mount Sinai (technically a mountain in the wilderness, not "the desert" per se, but close enough).
7. **Elijah fasted/traveled 40 days — confirmed.** 1 Kings 19:1-8. Sustained by angelic food for a 40-day journey to Horeb.
8. **1 Kings 18 — Elijah and the prophets of Baal at Mt. Carmel — confirmed.** Appropriate reference for God's superiority over idols.

### Scripture — Needs Correction
9. ⚠️ **Hebrews 1:3 — "purification for us on the cross."** ESV actually reads: "After making purification **for sins**, he sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high." Two issues: (a) "for sins" not "for us"; (b) "the cross" is not in the verse — that is interpretive addition. The theology is sound, but if quoting directly, use the actual wording.
10. ⚠️ **Colossians 2:14 — "record of our wrongdoing."** ESV actually reads: "by canceling the **record of debt** that stood against us with its legal demands. This he set aside, nailing it to the cross." The sermon says "record of our wrongdoing" — close in concept but not the ESV wording. The ESV uses legal/financial imagery ("record of debt," "legal demands").
11. ⚠️ **"80 or 90 years" — wrong numbers.** The lifespan reference is drawn from Psalm 90:10, which reads: "The years of our life are **seventy**, or even by reason of strength **eighty**." The sermon inflates this to "80 or 90." Correct to 70 or 80 if reusing.
12. ⚠️ **Israelites wandered "40 days" — should be 40 YEARS.** Numbers 14:33-34: "Your children shall be shepherds in the wilderness **forty years**." The 40-year sentence was based on the 40 *days* the spies explored the land (one year per day), but the actual wandering was 40 years. Significant factual error.

### Factual Claims — Needs Correction
13. ⚠️ **Elijah "tested by the brook" during 40-day fast — conflation of two events.** The brook Cherith episode (1 Kings 17:1-7, fed by ravens during drought) is a completely separate and earlier event from the 40-day journey to Horeb (1 Kings 19:1-8, sustained by angelic food after fleeing Jezebel). These are years apart. Do not combine them.
14. ⚠️ **Massah — "wished for the slavery of their old life in Egypt" — conflation.** In Exodus 17:3 the Israelites accuse Moses of bringing them out to die of thirst, but they do not explicitly long for Egypt's slavery. That language appears in Exodus 14:12 ("it would have been better for us to serve the Egyptians"), Exodus 16:3 (meat pots of Egypt), and Numbers 11:5 (fish, cucumbers, melons). The grumbling at Massah is about water, not nostalgia for Egypt.

### Attributions
15. **David Platt quote — substantially correct.** Platt has used very similar imagery in his Radical.net teaching on Jeremiah 25:15-16: Jesus taking "the full cup of infinite divine judgment and wrath" and "he drank down every drop. He turned it over and cried out, 'It is finished.'" The sermon's wording ("flipped it upside down") appears to be a paraphrase. The core imagery and theology match Platt's published material.

### Theological Notes
16. **"Satan physically moved Jesus"** — The Gospels say the devil "took him" (Mt 4:5, 8). Whether this was physical transport, a vision, or something else is debated. The sermon presents physical transport as one possibility ("whether he picked Christ up and flew him, or did something more instant like the teleporter on Star Trek"), which is fair — just noting the text doesn't specify the mechanism.
17. **Philippians 4:4, 8 — paraphrased from memory.** "Rejoice in the Lord and again I say rejoice" (Phil 4:4) and "whatever is true, whatever is noble…" (Phil 4:8) are quoted from memory without verse citation. The wording is close to ESV but not exact (ESV 4:8: "whatever is **honorable**" not "noble"; "**if there is any** excellence, **if there is anything** worthy of praise, **think about** these things"). Minor differences; the sense is preserved.

### Dating and Context
18. **Obama-era sermon (~2014–2016).** References to Barack Obama, election cycle warming up, and "another punishment" suggest this was preached during 2015–2016 primary season. Consistent with other Obama-era sermons in the collection (Romans 13).
19. **Proverbs 26:11 / 2 Peter 2:22** — "like a dog returning to its vomit." Not cited by verse but the reference is correct.
20. **Joseph fleeing temptation** — Genesis 39 (Potiphar's wife). Correct reference for fleeing temptation.
21. **Gospel close with altar call.** "Come talk to me… there is no judgment to be found here, just Jesus."
