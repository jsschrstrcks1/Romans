Missions and Hell — Why It Matters


We've been talking a lot about missions lately, and about our brothers and sisters in other countries enduring persecution. One sermon I listened to was by the director of our denomination's foreign mission board — the IMB.

He told a story about serving in Indonesia — one of the black countries on our wall. He was on the run, trying to make contact with an underground church. They were camping in the forest waiting for word, and he was sitting there after a long and fruitful day of ministry, reading a book by A. W. Tozer. And he thought to himself: "I bet God is really glad to have me on his team."

Let the arrogance of that sink in for a moment.

God. The one who spoke the universe into existence. The one who hung the stars in the sky, who has storehouses of snow, who in six days made a universe so complex we have spent the next five thousand years trying to figure it out and have only scratched the surface. We are talking about the God who holds our molecules together. A supreme and sovereign, omnipotent, omniscient, omnipresent God like that — he doesn't need us to accomplish his will.

When I was little, my dad used to say: "I brought you into this world, and I can take you out and make another one that looks just like you." My dad's threat was empty. God could actually do it. With a word. He could wipe us out and start over. We serve a mighty and powerful God.

So my friend sat there thinking that, and then opened his book and read the following. It's a long quote — but a good one. The book was The Knowledge of the Holy by A. W. Tozer:

"So, were every man on earth to become atheist, it could not affect God in any way. He is what He is in Himself without regard to any other. To believe in Him adds nothing to His perfections; to doubt Him takes nothing away.

Almighty God, just because He is almighty, needs no support. The picture of a nervous, ingratiating God fawning over men to win their favor is not a pleasant one; yet if we look at the popular conception of God, that is precisely what we see. Twentieth century Christianity has put God on charity. So lofty is our opinion of ourselves that we find it quite easy, not to say enjoyable, to believe that we are necessary to God. But the truth is that God is not greater for our being, nor would He be less if we did not exist. That we do exist is altogether of God's free determination, not by our desert nor by divine necessity.

Probably the hardest thought of all for our natural egotism to entertain is that God does not need our help. We commonly represent Him as a busy, eager, somewhat frustrated Father hurrying about seeking help to carry out His benevolent plan to bring peace and salvation to the world; but, as said the Lady Julian, 'I saw truly that God doeth all-thing, be it never so little.' The God who worketh all things surely needs no help and no helpers.

I fear that thousands of young persons enter Christian service from no higher motive than to help deliver God from the embarrassing situation His love has gotten Him into and His limited abilities seem unable to get Him out of."

We are included in God's mission not because he needs us — but because he loves us enough to include us in his plan.

Does the God who hung the stars in the sky truly need us? No.

Have you ever taken a child fishing? You don't take a kid fishing because you need them to help catch more fish. You take them because you love the child and want to do something you enjoy together. You don't take a child horseback riding because you need them to hold the horse down. You take them because you love your kid and want to draw them deeper into your world.

Right now there are two billion people in the world who are utterly and completely unreached. Two billion. With a B. That's 6,000 people groups. 6,000 unique languages.

What does it mean to be unreached?

Unreached means being like Yemen. You've heard of Yemen — it's one of the black countries on our map. The entire country has around 30 million people. Yet every Christian in Yemen could fit in this room and we would still have seats to spare.

To be an unreached people group means you live in a place where you could spend your entire life and — short of the direct intervention of God — never hear the gospel. Never have a chance to respond to it. Two billion people. A quarter of the entire world. Places where short of an act of God you will never see, much less possess, a Bible.

An unreached people group is a desert. The missionary is an oasis of God's truth.

John Piper says that missions exists because worship doesn't. If we loved God the way we should, if we worshiped God the way we should, there would be no lost people around us. He says one of God's purposes for the world is that the knowledge of him spread across the world like a flooding ocean of truth.

And yet even global missions are neglected by the average Christian. Even national missions, state missions, are neglected by the average Christian. Look in our own backyard.

The Orlando metro area recently topped 2.5 million people. Only about 3% are believers in Jesus Christ — roughly 75,000 Christians in an area of several million people. Millions lost and heading to hell in our backyard. Millions dying without the gospel. I know people in Orlando. I imagine most of you do too.

And it's a massive opportunity, because Orlando draws people from the whole world. Disney alone drew nearly 70 million people last year — from Yemen, from China, from Indonesia, from Russia and Germany and Paris and London and India. From Iraq and Afghanistan. From every country colored black on our map.

Think about that for a moment. What do you think the average person in Afghanistan earns in a year?

Take a guess.

$410 a year. So it isn't the average Afghan who comes to Disney for a week. It's the leaders. The wealthy. The people with power and influence and the authority to affect real change back home.

I heard this from Stephen Davey: Ashraf Ghani is the president of Afghanistan. He has two children — a daughter named Mariam who lives in Brooklyn, and a son named Tariq who was born and raised in Maryland. And Tariq spent a year at Valencia College. You know where that is? Orlando. Near Kissimmee.

Imagine what could happen if God got hold of Tariq's heart here in the states. Every door back in Afghanistan would be open to him. He speaks the language. People want to hear what he has to say. The doors are flung wide.

Orlando is also an epicenter of Muslim activity — something like 30 mosques spread through the city and its suburbs. What is going to happen to all of these people if no one shares the gospel with them?

I spoke with a pastor a while back who told me Islam is the biggest problem facing our country today — and that he would not share the gospel with a Muslim because they all deserve to go to hell. That is the wrong attitude. The best way to address Islam is to tell Muslims about Jesus. Meet one. Learn a name. Ask one to lunch. They are very hospitable people. Generally, women should reach women and men should reach men — the mixing of company is frowned upon, and an American man approaching a Muslim woman in particular will not go well. But don't miss this opportunity. We live in a time and place where the people from the countries hardest to reach are coming to our back door.

54,000 people a day fly in and out of Tampa International Airport. We pass these people in the grocery store, at the gas station, at the local college, at Weeki Wachee, basically everywhere we go — people who are lost and bound for hell. Will you stand by as they march toward an eternity without God?

Don't raise your hand. But how many of you have shared the gospel with someone this week? Statistically, in a group this size, other than the pastor, fewer than a fraction of one percent of you have.

I'm convinced the American church either doesn't truly believe in the horror of hell — or hasn't truly tasted the majesty of God.

So let's look at hell for a few minutes. I want you to see what Scripture actually says about what awaits the lost people we know and love.

Mark 9:48: *"...where their worm does not die and the fire is not quenched."*

Mark 9:49: *"For everyone will be salted with fire."*

Salt is a preservative. It seals a covenant. Salt in a wound burns. And sulfur — which commonly forms in salt domes — burns well, with a blue flame, sometimes in the form of solid rock. Brimstone is the old English word for it: "the stone that burns." People argue that hell probably smells like burning sulfur.

The worm is a reference our next passage picks up. Isaiah 66:24: *"They will go out and look on the dead bodies of those who rebelled against me; the worms that eat them will not die, the fire that burns them will not be quenched, and they will be loathsome to all mankind."*

The word for worm in Hebrew can be translated grub, worm, or maggot. Maggot seems most natural when the context is flesh. It is a horrific picture. Are the worms literal? Are they a metaphor for horrors we can't yet name? We can't know with certainty. What we do know is that this state — burning, being consumed, never ending — is eternal. It never stops.

Do we really want to find out if the worms are literal? Do you want your loved ones to find out?

Second Peter 2:17 adds another element: *"For them the gloom of utter darkness has been reserved."*

Hell is dark. Have you ever been in a cave and had the lights turned off? You cannot see your hand in front of your face. Your eyes never adjust. It is an oppressive, total, palpable blackness. That is the picture here — and it has been stored up and reserved for eternity.

So we have the smell. The sight. Something of what it will feel like. What will you hear?

Matthew 24:51 says there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. The only sound in hell is the screaming of agony and billions of people grinding their teeth. Matthew 13:49–50 adds that it will be like being inside a fiery furnace — and there too, weeping and gnashing of teeth.

And finally, Isaiah 59:2 tells us why anyone is there at all: *"But your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear."*

Our sin separates us from God. That is the gospel's starting point.

The question is whether we believe it. Whether we believe it enough to walk across the room, cross the street, pick up the phone. The world is coming to our doorstep — from Yemen, from Afghanistan, from the countries in black on that wall. God doesn't need us. But he has invited us in. Will we go?
