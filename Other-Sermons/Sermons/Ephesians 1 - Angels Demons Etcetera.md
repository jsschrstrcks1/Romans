## Opening — The Week from Hell

## I. Cultural Obsession with Angels

Chuck Hastings quote: angels on coffee mugs, t-shirts, checkbooks, license plates, soap dispensers, Bubble Angel Barbie. Our culture has made angels into decorations.

TV preacher with mountain of credit card statements claiming to bind "demon of debt" with made-up words.

## II. Pseudepigrapha and False Teaching

Three groups of ancient books:
1. **Canon** — the 66 books of Scripture
2. **Apocrypha** — accepted by Catholics, rejected by Protestants
3. **Pseudepigrapha** — claim to be scripture but are verifiably false

**How we know they're fake**: Language dates documents. "Groovy" = 1960s. "Bad" meaning good = 1980s. "Booyah," "psych," "fly" = 1990s. "Bae," "lit," "snowflake" = 2010s. If a document claims Paul wrote it but uses language from centuries later — it's a forgery.

**Pseudepigraphal examples**:
- Sarah married seven times, each husband killed by a demon on the wedding night. A blind man and the angel Raphael burn fish liver and heart to scare the demon away. This is the root of the practice of mumbling nonsense to "bind" demons.
- Solomon's ring to bind demons. Interrogating demons for their names and weakness words — Orniskelos causes sore throats, flees from Raphael; Hellorath causes gas, flees if you mutter "aiarhea." The ring was broken when Solomon dallied with female demons.

This is where the modern charismatic obsession with "binding" demons and learning their names comes from — not from Scripture, but from pseudepigraphal folklore.

## III. What Scripture Actually Teaches

> "Little children, you are from God and have overcome them, for he who is in you is greater than he who is in the world." — 1 John 4:4 (ESV)

> "He has delivered us from the domain of darkness and transferred us to the kingdom of his beloved Son, in whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins." — Colossians 1:13-14 (ESV)

Ephesians 1 teaches that believers are **sealed with the Holy Spirit** — believers cannot be demon-possessed. Angels are sentient tools in God's hands — the hero is God, not the angels.

**Cargo cult analogy**: A tribe worships cargo ships instead of the God who sent them. Don't worship the boat.

## IV. The Dualism Heresy

Treating everything as a galactic fight between angels and demons fails by leaving out the sovereign God. The battle is already won. We are sealed and protected by the Holy Spirit.

This worldview is also used to remove individual responsibility for sin. "The devil made me do it" — this excuse has its origins in the Garden:
- Eve: "The serpent deceived me"
- Adam: "The woman *you* gave me"

**Personal application**: The devil didn't break the lawn mower, the trucks, the Buick, the AC, or the Hyundai brakes. He didn't give you gas or make you sign up for an irresponsible credit card. He didn't make you embrace a life of sin. "You chose that. You are responsible for that."

## V. The Riches of God (from Ephesians 1)

Article on lottery winners: winning was the worst thing that happened to most of them — spending, shysters, debt, dying alone.

God's riches are different. Every euro, pound, yen, dollar belongs to God. Your house is "God's house He lets you live in."

Beyond material riches: perfect goodness, grace, patience — all treasures, all costly to learn. "You only want to ask for patience if you're ready to encounter situations that make you impatient."

God lavishes on us "in accordance with His wisdom."

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**Status: CONDITIONAL PASS — with significant notation**

- **Scopus Test**: The sermon's thesis is, in effect: "Christian superstition about angels and demons is wrong because God is sovereign, believers are sealed, and individual responsibility cannot be delegated to demonic forces." This thesis is not the main point of Ephesians 1:3-14. The passage's main point is doxological and soteriological: God has blessed his people with every spiritual blessing in Christ — election, adoption, redemption, forgiveness, sealing — to the praise of his glorious grace. The sermon uses the passage's conclusion (the sealing of the Spirit, vv.13-14) as a corrective argument while ignoring the passage's argument as a whole. This is a borderline gate situation. The passage is not dishonored, but it is not preached.
- **Authority Test**: The preacher does not stand above the text as an editor in a deceptive sense — the corrections he makes are not foreign to the passage's logic. However, he imports a corrective agenda as the primary frame and uses the text as ammunition for it rather than standing under the text's own proclamation. Soft pass.

**Berean Gate: Soft Pass.** The sermon is not text-driven in the full sense — it is correction-driven and uses the text to support a prior agenda. This does not fail the gate outright but is a structural weakness that shapes every category below.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears not to see the structural irony: he is correcting people for treating angels as intermediaries between themselves and God, while inadvertently treating Ephesians 1 as an intermediary between his corrective agenda and the congregation — rather than letting the passage speak for itself. The corrective instinct is pastoral and good; the problem is that it has colonized the hermeneutical task. The preacher sees what the congregation has wrong but may not see that the passage's own solution is more gloriously positive than any corrective could provide.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The sermon's corrective thrust — "don't look to angels and demons; look to the sovereign God" — sets up perfectly for an exposition of Ephesians 1:3-12. The passage answers the question the sermon raises: *What has God done for his people that makes angel-worship and demon-fear so unnecessary?* He elected them before the foundation of the world (v.4). He predestined them for adoption (v.5). He lavished grace on them (v.8). He revealed the mystery of his will — to sum up all things in Christ (vv.9-10). He gave them an inheritance (v.11). This is the answer to the corrective question, and it is lying in the passage, untouched. The sermon builds the burning building and then does not enter it.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3-5)

1. **State the thesis before the outline begins.** One sentence: "Ephesians 1:3-14 teaches that God has already given every spiritual blessing to his people in Christ — which makes angelic superstition not just wrong, but unnecessary and insulting to what we already have." This anchors every corrective section in the positive proclamation.

2. **Rebuild Section III as an exposition of Ephesians 1:3-14.** Walk verse by verse through the passage's argument: What does it mean that we are "blessed with every spiritual blessing in Christ" (v.3)? What does election before the foundation of the world (v.4) do to the fear of demonic powers? What does redemption through blood, the forgiveness of trespasses (v.7) mean for the person who just heard they are responsible for their sin? This is the passage's own answer to the corrective sections.

3. **Add the gospel pivot after the blame-shifting section.** After "You chose that. You are responsible for that." — pause and turn: "And here is where Ephesians 1:7 becomes the most important verse in this sermon: 'In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of his grace.' You are responsible. And Christ is the answer to that responsibility. He took it. He bore it. He was crushed under it so that you could be forgiven."

4. **Issue a differentiated exhortation.** Address the unbeliever: "If you are not in Christ today, you are not sealed. You are in the domain of darkness Colossians 1:13 describes. The transfer from that kingdom to Christ's kingdom happens not through ritual but through repentance and faith in Christ alone." Address the believer: "You are sealed. That means no demon, no accusation, no week of breakdowns can undo what God determined before the foundation of the world. Live from that security."

5. **Close with the passage's own doxology.** Ephesians 1 ends its three stanzas with the same refrain: "to the praise of his glory" (vv. 6, 12, 14). End the sermon there — not on information, not on correction, but on worship. The congregation was corrected; now they must be sent to their knees.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Ephesians 1:3-14 is an epistolary passage structured as a berakah (blessing formula), not as a propositional argument — meaning its natural register is doxological and its natural response is worship. The rubric applies well to this genre, but the Sermonic Force and Weight of Glory categories are especially load-bearing here: the passage was written to make believers marvel at what God has done, and a sermon that does not produce marvel has not yet done what the text demands. The rubric's Exhortation & Response category should be read with this in mind — the primary response called for by this passage is not behavioral action but worship and renewed confidence in God's sovereign grace.
