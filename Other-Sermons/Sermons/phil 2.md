# Philippians 1:27–2:16 — Manner of Life Worthy of the Gospel

**Primary Text**: Philippians 1:27–2:16
**Series**: Philippians

---

Our passage today is Philippians chapter 2. While you're turning there, let's talk about our people group of the week: the Gujjars.

The Gujjars are an equestrian, nomadic, pastoral people who largely raise herds of cattle and wander the forested regions of India, living in tent communities. There are approximately one million Gujjars, and to the best of our knowledge none of them are believers in Jesus Christ. They are a Muslim people group, which makes evangelism difficult to begin with. The regions they inhabit are also largely Hindu, which brings additional challenges. Conversion to Christianity is against the law in several of the states where Gujjars are commonly found, making it even harder to reach them.

In some areas, Gujjars are viewed by outsiders as a community of criminals — cattle thieves, horse thieves, and looters — something like the Indian Muslim equivalent of a Romani traveler. This is largely a misconception. Some Gujjars are very prosperous landlords. Generally speaking, they are tall, strong, and physically imposing people with fair complexions and sharp features. In some regions they are considered natural wrestlers, and their other beloved pastime is lifting enormous stones. They are also accomplished horsemen.

They are in desperate need of people who will come and teach them modern animal husbandry techniques — techniques that could allow them to put down roots and build stable communities. They need education, so that practices like child marriage can be set aside. Most of all, they need to hear about our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ. Who will pray for the Gujjars?

---

I heard a funny story this week. Stephen Davey tells it well. A friend of his was a junior high school principal in North Carolina. The sixth-grade girls at his school had developed a daily ritual — they would go into the bathroom, put on their makeup, and on their way out, kiss the mirror, leaving little lipstick outlines of their lips on the glass. In a large public school, this quickly meant the janitor was spending an enormous amount of time on the mirrors. There were discussions, threats, school-wide assemblies — nothing stopped it.

Finally, the principal came up with a plan. He briefed the janitor, then called all the sixth-grade girls into the ladies' room. He told them clearly that the problem had continued, and that he wanted them to watch — right now — exactly how long it took the janitor to fix their little kiss marks.

He began his lecture about how disrespectful it was to the school and to the janitor — how it was costing money the school could have spent on better things. And at the conclusion of his remarks, he told the girls to sit there and watch.

The janitor pulled his longest sponge on a stick out of the closet, opened one of the stall doors, dipped the sponge in the toilet — and began scrubbing the mirror.

Needless to say, the kissing problem stopped that day. Let's read our passage.

---

*Philippians 1:27–2:1. We will read more in a moment, but let's start there.*

The situation Paul is addressing is his imprisonment. You may have heard of the "prison epistles." Philippians is one of four letters Paul wrote while imprisoned in Rome — part of that grouping along with Ephesians, Colossians, and Philemon. When he writes back in verse 12, "I want you to know, brothers, that what has happened to me has really served to advance the gospel," what he means by *what has happened* is his imprisonment.

Let's look at verse 27: "Only let your manner of life be worthy of the gospel of Christ."

There is a Greek word there that is unusual: *politeuomai*. From what I can gather through my commentaries, this word carries a double meaning — a kind of double entendre. The first and primary meaning is translated "manner of life." The second meaning is harder to convey, because we no longer know exactly what it meant to live as a citizen of Philippi. What we do know is that Paul spent a considerable amount of time there, and the church at Philippi held a special place in his heart. The closest illustration I can think of is the way a pastor might speak of a particularly Spirit-filled gathering — a Bible study where God was palpably present, where people were striving together toward God every single day, nearly holy ground. Paul poured his life into those people. They may have been his gold standard. That, I believe, is the second layer of meaning: *live like Philippians* — in the finest sense that Paul would have meant it.

Verse 29 is important too. Someone read verse 29 for me: "For it has been granted to you that for the sake of Christ you should not only believe in him but also suffer for his sake."

So many voices in the church today preach that if you become a Christian and obey God, you are going to be comfortable, healthy, wealthy, and wise. We naturally think that way. We raise our children that way — do your homework, eat your dinner, and maybe you get dessert. Do your chores, you get an allowance. We train animals that way. Do the trick, get the treat. Conversely, misbehave and you get punished — grounded, isolated, deprived. This is our carrot-and-stick philosophy, and it is only natural that we assume God operates the same way: obey and get what we want, disobey and get the rod.

But all of that fails to reckon with several important things. For starters, the idea that we could ever do enough good to earn God's blessing contradicts Scripture outright. How does Scripture describe us? Corpses. Children of wrath. Isaiah wrote:

> *"We are all infected and impure with sin. When we display our righteous deeds, they are nothing but filthy rags. Like autumn leaves, we wither and fall, and our sins sweep us away like the wind."* — Isaiah 64:6 (NLT)

Paul wrote in Romans 3:23, "for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." We have all fallen short of God's standard. If we were able to be good enough to earn Christ's favor, Christ never would have needed to die. It was our sin that held Him on that cross until it was finished — as the old hymn says. If there had been any other way, wouldn't God have spared His Son? But we couldn't live up to God's standard on our own. The things of the Lord are foolishness to the natural mind, apart from God's intervening work in our hearts.

Could you imagine those sixth-grade girls coming back to school the next day and kissing the mirror again — pressing their painted lips against a mirror that had been scrubbed with toilet water? Certainly not. I can maybe picture a very small child touching something disgusting without fully understanding what it is. But when you have the mental capacity to comprehend what that sponge has been through — most people would never go back.

Yet how many of us adults willingly return to the toilet water of the sins of our old lives, again and again and again?

The lost person doesn't yet know better. They follow the corrupt desires of their heart, living in rebellion against God, until God does a work in them and brings them to a place where they can truly see who He is. Those who cannot see, who don't understand, who cannot smell sewage — they are not repulsed by it. Is a corpse repulsed by sewage? Certainly not. A corpse isn't thinking about anything. It's just decaying. If a corpse encounters sewage, it fits right in.

But a Christian — one in whom God has begun the work of regeneration — *knows* what sewage is, and is repulsed by it. They know the cost of taking what they want at any price. And when they choose to sin anyway, they are no better than those sixth-grade girls pressing their lips into the toilet-washed mirror.

Christian, is your manner of life worthy of the gospel? If Paul were here today, would he say, "That Paula — she reminds me of the wonderful brothers and sisters in Philippi"? Or "That Willie Burdette — he reminds me of some of the finest deacons I knew back in Philippi"? Would he say that of you, Greg? Of you, Kathy? Would he say it of me?

Or would he say what he said to the Corinthians — "When you come together it is not for the better but for the worse"?

That is harsh criticism. He said something similar about Peter. In Acts 10, God gave Peter a vision and commanded him not to call anything impure that God had made clean. Food was no longer to be a source of division; Peter had been shown that the Gentiles were to be welcomed fully into the fellowship of the church.

Not long before this, Paul had come to faith on the road to Damascus — struck down by a blinding light, carried blind into the city, and there discipled by a believer named Ananias before his sight was restored. When Paul came to Jerusalem, the disciples were afraid of him — they didn't believe he was truly a disciple. But Barnabas came alongside him, brought him to the apostles, and vouched for him (Acts 9:27). That act of grace opened the door for Paul to serve.

And then, years later, Paul had to rebuke Peter publicly. Galatians 2 records the incident at Antioch: when no Jewish believers were watching, Peter ate and drank freely with the Gentiles — he taught among them, broke bread with them. But the moment a group of Jewish believers arrived from Jerusalem, Peter withdrew. He pulled back from the Gentiles so he wouldn't be seen in their company. For this hypocrisy, Paul says Peter "stood condemned" (Galatians 2:11). He had some stern words from Paul for Peter. The charge was serious: Peter was, by his behavior, compelling Gentile believers to live like Jews.

It isn't being good that saves us — we can never be good enough. It isn't following the Old Testament law that saves us. Even the Jews failed to keep the law adequately, and in so doing came to worship the law rather than the Lawgiver. So Christ had to come and fulfill the law on our behalf, that we could enter a new covenant in grace — free from the condemnation of the law, all of it through Christ alone.

*Now let's read the rest of our passage through verse 16.*

---

Charles Spurgeon wrote:

> *"If Christ is not all to you He is nothing to you. He will never go into partnership as a part Saviour of men. If He be something He must be everything, and if He be not everything He is nothing to you."*

His words still ring true today. Either you love Christ enough to be at war with sin in your life — or maybe you have never truly come to love Him at all. Only you and God know the answer to that.

John Calvin wrote that the human heart is a perpetual forge of idols. We must always be fighting the darkness in our own twisted hearts to keep God as the object of our worship. Jonathan Edwards, in the spirit of his famous Resolutions, captured something similar — a determination to live for Christ without reservation, regardless of whether anyone else would join him. John Owen, the Puritan, put it in unflinching terms: *"Be killing sin, or it will be killing you."*

To that end, Paul calls us to do nothing from selfish ambition or conceit — to put others first, and to "have this mind among yourselves." The Greek verb form here indicates continuous, ongoing action: we are to start now, and never stop. How can we be obedient to God in this if we are not sharing the gospel with the people around us?

I have read this C.T. Studd quote to you so many times over the past few years that your spouses probably hear it in their sleep:

> *"Only one life, 'twill soon be past; only what's done for Christ will last."*

Looking back on this past week — did you do anything of lasting value? I know — work, family, keeping up the house, some of you have been dealing with hard things, making funeral arrangements. I understand all of that.

But listen. In a thousand years, when this earth has passed away and we are standing in the presence of God — will it matter if your roof leaked in 2019? I know the Rays are in the playoffs and the Bucs are playing the Saints today. In a thousand years, standing before Christ, will it matter whether your team won or lost? Will it matter that your house was neat and tidy — a house that no longer exists?

Or will it matter that your children knew about God? That they were raised to love the Lord? That they have a saving relationship with Jesus Christ? That your neighbor heard the gospel? What matters more — what happens on *Survivor*, or your neighbor's eternal destination?

Fix your roof. Watch your ball games. But do so to the glory of God, and use those things as vehicles to share the life-saving truth of the gospel with the people around you who are lost and bound for an eternity in hell. Because only one life will soon be past, and only what's done for Christ will last.

What we know with certainty is this: one day, we will be standing face to face with Christ — the One who endured a horrific death on the cross for our sins. On that day, at the very name of Jesus, every knee shall bow and every tongue will confess that Jesus is Lord (Philippians 2:10–11).

For some of us, there will be no greater joy than to kneel at His feet in that moment. I can picture Him reaching down and lifting us up, saying to some: *"Well done, my good and faithful servant."*

But to others He will say: *"Depart from me. I never knew you."*

Both groups will kneel before Him. Both groups will declare Him Lord — one in glory, the other in judgment.

Which group are you in today?

*Gospel.*
