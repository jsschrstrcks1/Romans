PHILIPPIANS 3:1-12 — Sola Gratia (By Grace Alone)
First in a Five Solas series, preached in October 2020 (503rd anniversary of the Reformation).
Cross-references: Ephesians 2:1-12; John 6:44, 65; Ephesians 2:8-9.

PEOPLE GROUP OF THE WEEK: THE DAR HAMID OF SUDAN

The Dar Hamid is a group of 19 tribes representing approximately 750,000 people. [Note: Population figures are drawn from missions databases such as Joshua Project and are periodically updated; the figure given here should be verified against current sources.] They are a nominally Muslim people — most are Muslim only because someone once told them they were. People go through all the time selling charms and trinkets promised to ward off spirits or encourage good health. They put more stock in the charms than they do in Islam. They are a nomadic people with few possessions who trade in wool and leather products.

They are difficult to reach because Sudan as a country is hostile to Christians, and nomadic people groups are incredibly suspicious of outsiders. Please pray for opportunities for the gospel.

INTRODUCTION: OCTOBER AND THE REFORMATION

October is an important month in church history. This week is the first in a series on the Five Solas of the Reformation, which started 503 years ago at the end of this month. A man named Martin Luther put hammer to nail and began what became the Protestant Reformation — the hammer strike heard around the world.

One reason Luther nailed his theses to the door was that the Catholic Church had been preaching that you could buy your way out of trouble. Not all the way into heaven perhaps, but you could buy a better place in purgatory. [Note: Before going further — there is no purgatory mentioned in the Bible. If it is not in the Bible it is not God's word and should not carry the same weight as Scripture.]

As it happens, 90 of the 95 theses were about this issue — adding things to the gospel, adding a way for people to pay to be crooked. [Note: The 95 Theses (1517) are almost entirely concerned with indulgences and the related corruptions of the indulgence system. "90 of 95" is an informal approximation rather than a verified count; in substance the characterization is accurate, as the vast majority of the theses address the indulgence system and its abuses.]

Want to have an affair? Just fine in the eyes of the pope, as long as you paid the assessed sum. Rome had to build those cathedrals somewhere. In fact, if you ever make it to Europe and wander through those old cathedrals — many of them were paid for through indulgences. People's sin paid for those cathedrals. [Note: This is accurate. Revenue from the indulgence campaign promoted by Johann Tetzel (commissioned under Archbishop Albert of Brandenburg) was partly designated for the construction of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome. This was a direct trigger for Luther's 95 Theses.]

The pastor also described the possibility of buying a deceased loved one out of hell. [Note: This conflates two related but distinct Catholic doctrines. In Catholic theology, indulgences applied specifically to purgatory — a place of temporal purification for those already saved — not to hell. Those condemned to hell were considered beyond any intervention in Catholic teaching. The famous saying attributed to Tetzel was "As soon as a coin in the coffer rings / the soul from purgatory springs." The pastor's general point — that money was exchanged for spiritual favor for the deceased — is correct; the specific claim that one could buy someone out of hell overstates the Catholic doctrine.]

Just in case it is not clear: giving money to the church does not save you. Giving your time does not save you. A pastor cannot put you in better or worse standing with God. If anyone says they can, they are lying. The reformers' fight against these false doctrines became known as Sola Gratia — by grace alone.

PHILIPPIANS 3:1-12 — PAUL'S RÉSUMÉ AS REFUSE

It can only be grace alone. Turn to Philippians 3:1-12.

Right away in my Bible the heading here says "No Confidence in the Flesh." The subject headings were not written by the apostles or necessarily by God, but they are a good indicator of what is coming next. This is also Paul's résumé. Considering all he has done and been through — including things not even listed in the text, like writing roughly half the New Testament [Note: Paul is traditionally credited with 13 epistles (14 if Hebrews is included), out of 27 books in the New Testament; "roughly half" is a fair rhetorical approximation.] — wouldn't you expect Paul, if anyone, to have the best shot at earning his way to heaven? And yet what does he say? All those things I did? Garbage. Refuse. Worthless.

His list of "garbage" accomplishments:

- Circumcised on the eighth day (Philippians 3:5)
- Of the people of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin [Note: Philippians 3:5, ESV — accurate.]
- A Hebrew of Hebrews — meaning fluent in Hebrew culture and language, in addition to Greek and Aramaic. [Note: The pastor adds Latin as a "lingua franca of Rome" Paul would have known. It is worth noting that Greek, not Latin, was the lingua franca of the eastern Mediterranean where Paul ministered, and all of Paul's letters are in Greek. Whether Paul spoke or wrote Latin is uncertain. This is a minor historical imprecision.]
- Educated under Gamaliel — one of the leading thinkers of the day [Note: Acts 22:3, ESV: "I am a Jew, born in Tarsus in Cilicia, but brought up in this city, educated at the feet of Gamaliel according to the strict manner of the law of our fathers." Accurate.]
- As to the law, a Pharisee — a Pharisee's Pharisee, meaning other Pharisees looked to him as a model

If anyone had a chance of living up to God's standard on his own, it was Paul.

But Paul says it is garbage. He has no confidence in his flesh. All his accomplishments are worthless — one might say "as filthy rags" compared to knowing Christ.

EPHESIANS 2 — WHAT WE ARE WITHOUT CHRIST

He explains why in Ephesians 2 [we are not turning there, but here is the summary]:

We are dead in sin. Followers of the world. Followers of the devil. Sons of disobedience. Subservient to the passions of our flesh. Carrying out the desires of the flesh. By nature we are children of wrath. Separated from Christ due to our sin. Alienated. Hostile. Slaves to the law.

What can a dead body do on its own? Absolutely nothing. Our culture is fascinated with the walking dead — horror films, zombies, the dead eating brains and walking the streets. Millions of dollars are made scaring people with this concept. And yet is this not exactly how we are apart from Christ? Jesus said as much:

    "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him."
    — John 6:44, ESV

And again:

    "This is why I told you that no one can come to me unless it is granted him by the Father."
    — John 6:65, ESV

Apart from God, what do we have to offer? Zilch. Zero. Nothing. We are a dead body walking around. The famous preacher of the Great Awakening said it this way:

"You contribute nothing to your salvation except the sin that made it necessary!"
— Jonathan Edwards [Note: This quote is widely attributed to Jonathan Edwards (1703-1758), but the specific source within his writings has not been verified. It circulates broadly in Reformed preaching contexts. Before citing it formally, the original source in Edwards's works should be confirmed.]

THE PASTORAL APPLICATION: EXAMINE YOURSELF

Is your relationship with God marked by obedience rooted in faith? A famous preacher once said that being a Christian in the Bible Belt is dangerous — because going to church is easy. Bringing a dish to a potluck is easy. Staying to vacuum is easy. Going to Sunday school and Bible study is easy. And it is easy to look real good and still be as lost as a tare among the wheat.

If there are no marks of obedience to God in your life, you are not a Christian — consider yourself lost and in danger of damnation. You might think that is hard or mean-spirited. But it is more cruel to allow someone to choose hell without warning.

Instead we should follow the example of Billy Sunday. [Note: Billy Sunday (1862-1935) was one of the most famous evangelists in American history — not a locally famous figure. He was born in Iowa, played professional baseball, and became a nationally prominent revivalist who preached to millions across the United States. He held a significant revival campaign in Tampa (including Ybor City) in February-March 1917, which drew enormous crowds and was a major event in Tampa's history. Describing him as "locally famous" significantly understates his national prominence. The pastor's framing likely refers to Sunday's Tampa revival rather than his origins.]

This is what Billy Sunday said:

"I'm against sin. I'll kick it as long as I have a foot. I'll fight it as long as I have a fist. I'll butt it as long as I have a head. I'll bite it as long as I've got a tooth. And when I'm old and fistless and footless and toothless, I'll gum it till I go home to Glory and it goes home to perdition."
— Billy Sunday [Note: This is an authentic Billy Sunday quote, well-attested in multiple sources.]

Does God expect us to be perfect? No. He provided the cross for that. He expects us to try to meet his standard — to push through, and even though we trip and fall, to keep getting back up and pressing forward. If we were all perfect, God would not have had to send his Son to die. But we are anything but perfect, and God loved us anyway.

THE BOYCE QUOTE ON SOLA GRATIA

Finally, the Baptist preacher of the last century James Petigrew Boyce wrote: [Note: Two issues here. First, the name is spelled "James Petigru Boyce" (1827-1888), not "Petigrew." He was the founder and first president of Southern Baptist Theological Seminary and wrote "Abstract of Systematic Theology" (1887). Second, the quote that follows does not match Boyce's known style or the vocabulary of his era, and may come from a different source. The language and framing ("human methods, techniques, or strategies," "releasing us from our bondage") reads like 20th-century Reformed writing. This may be from James Montgomery Boice (1938-2000), a Reformed Presbyterian pastor and prolific author on the Reformation — a different person entirely, sometimes confused with J.P. Boyce due to the similar surname. The quote should be sourced before further use.]

"Grace alone. The words sola gratia mean that human beings have no claim upon God. That is, God owes us nothing except just punishment for our many and very willful sins. Therefore, if he does save sinners, which he does in the case of some but not all, it is only because it pleases him to do it. Indeed, apart from this grace and the regenerating work of the Holy Spirit that flows from it, no one would be saved, since in our lost condition, human beings are not capable of winning, seeking out, or even cooperating with God's grace. By insisting on 'grace alone' the Reformers were denying that human methods, techniques, or strategies in themselves could ever bring anyone to faith. It is grace alone expressed through the supernatural work of the Holy Spirit that brings us to Christ, releasing us from our bondage to sin and raising us from death to spiritual life."

GOSPEL CLOSE

God owes us nothing. Absolutely nothing. What we deserve is eternity separated from him. But those who are believers in Jesus Christ, he has chosen to save. As Ephesians 2:8-9 says:

"For by grace you have been saved through faith, and this not of yourselves — it is the gift of God, lest any man should boast."
— Ephesians 2:8-9 [Note: The pastor's wording blends KJV ("lest any man should boast") with a paraphrase of the middle clause. ESV reads: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast." The substance is accurate; the wording is from memory rather than a single translation.]

When God intercedes on our behalf it is an undeserved blessing. If you ask him and mean it, you will be saved, Scripture says.

[Gospel.]

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Date Evaluated:** 2026-03-21
**Text:** Philippians 3:1–12
**Series:** Five Solas (Sola Gratia — first in series, October 2020)

---

### 1. Verdict Title
*A Better-Organized Version of the Same Problem: Paul's Résumé as Refuse, Undone by an Unexpounded Cross and an Absent Exhortation*

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A clearly structured opening of Philippians 3:1–12 that rightly established total depravity and the worthlessness of human religious credentials, while failing to expound Christ's righteousness as the substance behind Paul's "surpassing worth of knowing him" and leaving the congregation with a warning rather than a proclamation.

---

### 3. Berean Gate
**Result: Pass — with noted reservations.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — that all human accomplishment is refuse compared to knowing Christ, because salvation is by grace alone, not human merit — arises directly from Philippians 3:7–9. Pass.

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text's claims about human inability and the worthlessness of religious credentials. The Reformation history material in the introduction is proportionally shorter here than in the John 6 version, and the pivot to Philippians 3 is made. The text governs the doctrinal content. Pass, with the reservation that vv. 10–12 (the resurrection, the fellowship of sufferings, pressing on toward the goal) receive no treatment.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score cap: 69 — one fatal flaw confirmed.**

- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation:** The sermon warns, explains, and illustrates. It does not summon. "If you ask him and mean it, you will be saved" is not an exhortation — it is an assertion. The congregation is not told specifically what to believe about Christ, what to turn from, or what pursuing the knowledge of Christ looks like in practice. The "[Gospel.]" placeholder at the close confirms this is unfinished.
- **Flag 3 — Christ functionally absent from the substance (partial):** The sermon correctly states that Paul counts everything as loss "for the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord" (v. 8), but what does Paul mean by "knowing Christ"? Verse 9 answers: "found in him, not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God that depends on faith." This verse — the sermon's theological climax — is mentioned but not expounded. The imputed righteousness of Christ, which is the explicit content of Paul's grace-theology here, goes unnamed. The cross is gestured at ("He provided the cross for that") rather than proclaimed. Christ is present as a concept but not as the living ground of the sinner's standing before God.

*Score is capped at 69.*

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (6/10):* Paul's résumé (vv. 4–6) is handled well — circumcision, tribe of Benjamin, Hebrew of Hebrews, Pharisee, zeal in persecution are all cited. The "garbage/refuse" (σκύβαλα) language is captured in spirit even without Greek engagement. But vv. 10–12 — the resurrection, the fellowship of suffering, the straining toward the goal — receive no treatment. The sermon uses approximately half the passage.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5):* The Reformation context is used appropriately as a framing device. The polemical edge of Philippians 3 — Paul writing against the Judaizers — is not identified, which would have strengthened the connection to indulgences and earned-merit considerably.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5):* The key term σκύβαλα (refuse/dung) is not examined, though the concept is captured. Philippians 3:9's imputed righteousness — the most exegetically significant verse in the passage — is not opened. "That which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God that depends on faith" is the doctrinal center of this text, and it is passed over.
- *Logical Faithfulness (2/5):* The use of Ephesians 2 as a supplementary text is appropriate. The Boyce/Boice quotation (note the attribution concern) and BFM citation are inserted as supporting material rather than as textual development. No eisegesis is present, but the passage's own argument is not followed to its conclusion.
- *FCF:* The fallen condition — human religious pride and the impossibility of self-righteousness — is clearly identified and named. This is well done.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7):* Christ is genuinely central to the sermon's argument in that Paul's credentials are weighed against knowing Christ. But what "knowing Christ" means theologically — union with Christ, imputed righteousness, resurrection life — is not expounded. Christ is the standard the sermon measures human accomplishment against, but not the substance of what the sermon proclaims.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* The Reformation context provides a redemptive-historical frame — though not from the text's own trajectory. The connection between Judaizers in Philippians and Catholic indulgences is implicitly drawn and is a legitimate parallel.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4/4):* Total depravity, irresistible grace, perseverance of the saints — all present and accurately stated. Election is treated in BFM terms. The doctrinal floor is solid.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4):* Consistent with 1689 soteriology. The perseverance of the saints is explicitly included. The attribution concern over "Boyce" vs. "Boice" is flagged — this is a factual/sourcing matter, not a theological drift.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel (3/5):* Ephesians 2:8–9 is quoted. Salvation as gift, not merit, is clearly stated. But justification — the crediting of Christ's righteousness to the sinner's account — is not proclaimed. The "how" of grace saving a dead sinner is left at the level of assertion rather than proclamation.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5):* The sermon would not survive without Christ in the sense that the entire argument depends on Christ being the one thing worth knowing above all human accomplishment. This is genuine.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5):* The cross appears twice: "He provided the cross for that" and "he sent his Son to die on the cross for us." Neither occurrence is expounded. The atonement is presumed, not proclaimed. The cross is background music, not the melody.
- *Synagogue Test:* Partially fails. The doctrinal content of the sermon — that earned merit is insufficient, that grace alone saves — would not deeply offend a Jewish hearer who agrees that God owes nothing to sinners. The specific offense of the cross — that Christ's blood is the only sufficient payment, that righteousness comes exclusively through faith in him — is not clearly enough planted to constitute the scandal it must be.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15**
- *Heart Penetration (3/5):* The "lost as a tare" warning and the examination question have genuine pastoral heat. The résumé-as-refuse illustration connects well with the text.
- *Specificity (2/5):* The application is general — examining one's life for marks of obedience. What does obedience rooted in faith look like? What specific things does the Philippians passage demand? The text's own application — pressing forward, not resting on past accomplishments, knowing Christ increasingly — is not drawn into the application section.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (3/5):* The nominal Christian and the genuine believer are distinguished. The backslider and the knowledgeable-but-unmoved are not addressed. Three of Perkins' seven categories are reached at best.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8**
- *Call to Response (1/4):* "[Gospel.]" at the close of the manuscript is a placeholder, not a proclamation. The actual exhortation is absent. The congregation is told what not to trust (their own merit) but not told specifically what to trust or how to trust it.
- *Equipping for Holiness and Witness (2/4):* The Billy Sunday quotation provides a witness orientation. The call to fight sin is present. But the positive program — what does pressing on toward the goal (v. 12) look like for this congregation this week? — is absent.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**
- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* This sermon is noticeably better structured than the John 6 version. The movement is: Reformation context → Philippians 3 résumé → Ephesians 2 on total depravity → pastoral application → BFM/Boice → gospel close. The sections are identifiable and traceable.
- *Progression (2/3):* The first two-thirds progress logically. The back half (pastoral application → BFM → Boice → close) loses momentum and feels assembled rather than built.
- *Unity (1/2):* One dominant idea governs: human accomplishment is worthless; grace alone saves. However the sermon's closing sections introduce the sanctification theme (obedience, fighting sin, trying) in tension with the sola gratia argument without reconciling the two.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**
- *Relevance (2/2):* The children-on-ankles illustration is personal and vivid. The cathedrals-built-on-indulgences image is historically striking.
- *Clarity (1/2):* The ankles illustration illustrates the entangling weight of sin but does not map cleanly onto sola gratia — it illustrates sin's bondage more than grace's sufficiency.
- *Memorability (0/1):* No single image will carry home with concentrated force.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**
- *Reads as Preaching (2/2):* Oral quality is genuine. The manuscript was clearly spoken before it was written (or written to be spoken). Direct address and rhetorical questions are present.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The résumé-as-refuse section has real force. The close deflates it.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1):* The sermon does not build to a climax. It accumulates and then ends.

**Subtotal: 59/100** (before cognitive load modifier)

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Paul's résumé as refuse is well-preached** — The list of Paul's credentials (vv. 4–6) is effectively narrated. "If anyone had a chance of living up to God's standard on his own, it was Paul. And yet what does he say? All those things I did? Garbage." This is strong proclamatory movement that arises naturally from the text.
2. **The Reformation introduction is proportionate and purposeful here** — Unlike the John 6 version, the Philippians sermon's introduction is briefer and connects more directly to the text. The parallel between Judaizers/indulgences and earned merit is legitimate.
3. **Factual annotation discipline is admirable** — The embedded notes represent a genuine commitment to accuracy, which is the kind of self-correction that makes for trustworthy preaching over time. The flagging of the Boyce/Boice confusion is especially important.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Philippians 3:9 is the sermon's text and the sermon skips it** — "Not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God that depends on faith" is the explicit doctrinal statement Paul makes. This is imputed righteousness — Reformation theology's beating heart — stated plainly by the apostle. The sermon preaches Sola Gratia without expounding the verse that most precisely defines what grace alone means in this passage.
2. **Vv. 10–12 are entirely absent** — "That I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his fellowship of his sufferings... I press on toward the goal." This is Paul's vision of the Christian life — which is not striving to earn but pressing toward knowing Christ more fully. This resolves the tension the sermon creates between "you can't earn it" and "God expects you to try." Paul's answer is: yes, press on — but press toward *knowing Christ*, not toward meeting a standard. This is the pastoral heart of the passage, and it is unpreached.
3. **The "[Gospel.]" placeholder confirms the sermon is unfinished** — An actual gospel proclamation has not been written. The sermon ends in assertion ("if you ask him and mean it, you will be saved") without the cross being explained, the sinner's position being clarified, or a genuine call to specific faith being issued.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the tension between "total depravity — you can do nothing" and "God expects you to try / marks of obedience" creates a pastoral problem that the text itself resolves in vv. 10–12. The sermon creates the problem (you can't earn it) and the warning (look for obedience) without providing the bridge (knowing Christ produces the life that shows the evidence). The text has the solution. It was not used.

Additionally, the Boyce/Boice attribution confusion — flagged admirably in the notes — represents a broader pattern: the sermon relies heavily on external quotations to carry its theological weight. Paul's own words in vv. 7–9 are rich enough to carry the sermon without BFM articles and patristic quotes. The external sourcing, while not wrong, substitutes for deeper textual engagement.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Reformation introduction feels like it earns the Sola Gratia claim — it establishes the historical stakes, the Protestant identity, the Reformation context. But this is the scaffolding, not the building. The actual theological argument for Sola Gratia is Paul's own résumé in Philippians 3, and the sermon's engagement with that argument is thinner than the introduction suggests. The preacher has set up a ten-story building and built three floors.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Philippians 3:10 — "that I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his fellowship of sufferings" — is the passage's most devotionally potent verse and the one that distinguishes Paul's Christian life from mere doctrinal correctness. Paul is not just saying "grace saves me." He is saying "I want to *know* him — personally, increasingly, in suffering as in triumph." This is the answer to the nominal Christianity problem. The cultural Christian knows about Christ but does not *know* him. Paul's ambition is to know Christ ever more deeply. That is the gospel's fruit and the text's own pastoral application. It was entirely missed.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach Philippians 3:9 as the sermon's climactic verse.** "Not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God that depends on faith." Spend three minutes here: What is this righteousness? Where does it come from? How is it received? This is Paul's explicit statement of Sola Gratia, and it points directly to the cross.
2. **Preach vv. 10–12 as the practical answer to nominal Christianity.** The marks-of-obedience application is legitimate — but root it here. "The mark of genuine faith is not just church attendance. It is the hunger to know Christ more, to share his sufferings, to press toward him. That is what Paul describes. Is that your ambition? Or are you satisfied with the social benefits of Christianity?"
3. **Write the actual gospel close.** The "[Gospel.]" placeholder must become a real proclamation: "Here is what you must believe: That Christ, the perfectly righteous Son of God, bore the penalty for your sin on the cross, satisfying God's just wrath, and that his righteousness is credited to all who trust in him alone. Not your morality. Not your church attendance. Not your baptism. His righteousness. Received by faith. That is grace alone."
4. **Verify the Boyce/Boice attribution before the next use.** The quote is theologically sound, but if it is James Montgomery Boice rather than J.P. Boyce, the attribution is incorrect and should be corrected.
5. **Resolve the "tries to meet God's standard" language.** Replace with: "God does not demand perfection from us — he demands perfection *for* us, and Christ provided it. What he produces in those he regenerates is a desire to press toward knowing him. Not earning, but following. Not performing, but being transformed."

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Philippians 3:1–12 is Pauline epistle — didactic, confessional, personal narrative. The rubric fits well. The exposition and gospel centrality categories apply directly and without strain. The one area where the rubric pressed hardest on this sermon is the Conscience & Applicatory Force category — not because the rubric is wrong, but because the text's own application (pressing toward knowing Christ, vv. 10–12) was not used, making the application section dependent on generic diagnostic questions rather than text-specific calls. This is a preacher problem, not a rubric problem.
