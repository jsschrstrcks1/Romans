Prodigal Son
Luke 15:11ff & 15:1ff
Western Perspective: (read scripture first, then ask who sinned and how in the story)



The parables in this chapter were given in response to the criticism of the scribes and Pharisees that Jesus had received sinners and even ate with them. These "sinners" were Jews who were not obeying the Law or the traditions of the elders and were therefore "outcasts" in Israel. Jesus had already made it clear that He came to save sinners and not self-righteous people like the scribes and Pharisees (5:27–32; 14:21–24). Jesus saw what these "sinners" really were: lost sheep who needed a shepherd; lost coins that had value; lost sons who needed to be in fellowship with the Father.


I. Searching (15:1–10)

A shepherd is responsible for each of the sheep; and if one is lost or killed, he must make up for it himself. Sheep are lost because of their own stupidity; they wander away and fail to see the danger they are in. Jesus came to "seek and to save them that are lost" (19:10). Note the emphasis on rejoicing: the shepherd rejoices, the neighbors rejoice, and heaven rejoices.
The necklace of ten coins was a headband that signified that a woman was married. To lose one of the coins would be to ruin the necklace and embarrass the woman. Like that coin, sinners bear the imprint of the image of God and are valuable (20:24–25); but they are lost and "out of circulation." When found, sinners are once again useful and able to serve the Lord. Again, there is joy in the family because the lost has been found.
What does it mean to be lost? It means, like the sheep, to be away from safety and in a place of danger; or like the coin, to be useless and out of circulation. In the case of the younger son, it means to be out of fellowship with the Father and away from the joys of the family.


II. Waiting and Welcoming (15:11–24)

It is significant that the father did not go searching for his son, but waited at home for the boy to come back. When the boy did come back, the father ran to meet him. Like sheep gone astray, some sinners are lost through their own stupidity; and, like coins, some are lost by the carelessness of others. But the son was lost because of his own willfulness, and the father had to wait until that will was broken and submissive.
For the younger son to ask for an early inheritance was like asking his father to die! It must have broken the father's heart, but he gave the boy his share of the wealth! God likewise has shared His wealth with a world of lost sinners, and they have wasted it (Acts 14:15–17; 17:24–28). It was not the badness of his life that brought the boy to his senses but the goodness of his father (v. 17; Rom. 2:4).
In the East, it is unusual for older men to run; but the Father had to run because of his compassion for the boy. Also, the son had disgraced his family and his village and could have been stoned to death (Deut. 21:18–21). If they threw any stones, they would have to hit the father! The best robe would be the father's expensive festal robe; the shoes indicated that the son was not a servant (in spite of his request); and the ring was the proof of sonship. Again, there is joy, for the lost has been found!


III. Pleading (15:25–32)

The elder brother is the forgotten person in this parable, and yet he is the key to the story. If the prodigal son symbolizes the "publicans and sinners," then the elder brother represents the scribes and Pharisees. There are sins of the spirit as well as sins of the flesh (2 Cor. 7:1). The religious leaders may not have been guilty of the gross things that the younger son did, but they were still sinners, guilty of a critical and unloving spirit, pride, and an unwillingness to forgive.
Because the younger son had received his inheritance, the estate belonged to the elder brother; but it was run by the father, who benefited from the profits. If the younger brother came back home, it would confuse the inheritance even more, so the elder brother did not want him back, nor was he looking for him.
Now we discover that the elder brother had a "hidden agenda" of his own, a longing to have a big party for his friends. He was angry with his brother for coming home and with his father for welcoming him and forgiving him. Like the scribes and Pharisees, he stayed outside the joy and fellowship of those who had been forgiven.
By staying outside the house, the elder brother humiliated his father and his brother. The father could have commanded him to come in, but he preferred to go out and plead with him. That is what Jesus did with the Jewish religious leaders, but they would not be persuaded. They thought they were saved because of their exemplary conduct, but they were out of fellowship with the Father and needed to repent and seek forgiveness.


Warren W. Wiersbe, Wiersbe's Expository Outlines on the New Testament (Wheaton, IL: Victor Books, 1992), 182–184.
Focus groups in various parts of the world read and then interpreted this well-known story. In the U.S., 94% of the focus group missed the detail in the text that the young man was a famine victim. For we Western capitalists, his sin was squandering his money.

Near East: Vs:12 is interpreted as the son saying to his father
With seminarians in St. Petersburg, Russia, 84% mentioned the famine while only 34% mentioned him squandering his money. The Eastern readers from a socialist background saw that his sin was putting a price tag on his family. He was less wicked than he was foolish. The Eastern Europeans acknowledged that he was stupid in squandering his money, but the really dumb thing was leaving home. Their idea was that famines and other disasters are bound to come up, and you need your family and your village. It's stupid to leave that behind.
A Bible school in Tanzania came up with another result. The Tanzanian readers noticed something in the text that neither the Americans nor the Russians had: No one gave him anything to eat. They just couldn't get over the inhospitality of the people where the prodigal ended up. Tanzanians know famine. But they also know about having to immigrate somewhere to find work. They note that a lot of people who leave home seeking their fortune end up losing their money because they don't understand what they will need when they arrive or how the local economy works. For those readers what is determinative is that the locals where the boy ended up were cruel enough to let him starve.
What accounts for these variant interpretations? Culture. Life experience. Those things lead us to pick up on different cues in the text.
Western readers tended to read the Prodigal Son as a story of reform. The climax is when the boy "comes to himself" in the pig pen. They read the story in light of the elder brother's charge that the prodigal wasted his money on harlots.
Eastern readers on the other hand, tend to emphasize the Prodigal Son as a story of recovery. They are reading it in light of the parables that come before: the lost sheep and the lost coin. Did the sheep leave the fold out of greed? Did the lost coin see itself as less shiny than its sisters, and so took off? No. So for Eastern readers, the climax of the tale is the father's joy that "what is lost has been found."
Tanzanians see the key to interpretation as the fact that Jesus is using these parables to answer the Pharisees who censure him for eating with sinners and outcasts. For them, the main thrust of the story is that the Pharisees are like the inhospitable people who will not feed the prodigal. The Father's house represents the kingdom of God Jesus preaches, where even the sinners have a place at the table and are fed. So for the Tanzanians the Prodigal Son is a story of contrast.
--Mark Allan Powell, What Do They Hear?: Bridging the Gap Between Pulpit and Pew (Nashville: Abingdon Press, 2007)

Two things are striking. First, in the obvious analogy to the search for the sheep, Jesus takes the initiative in seeking out lost people—a major theme in Luke (cf. 19:10). In contrast were some rabbis in the early centuries who hesitated to seek Gentile converts. But that does not invalidate Jesus' comment in Matthew 23:15 about Pharisees who were proselytizing aggressively. They were apparently trying to gain adherents to their sect, rather than compassionately seeking the lost. Second, the climax of the story is not only the return of the sheep but the triumphant rejoicing in its rescue (v. 6). Jesus is stressing, both by parable and direct statement (v. 7), that his seeking and receiving sinners pleases God.

CORRECTIONS LOG (added by Claude Code)
Verified against primary sources, BDAG, and Powell's actual text. Do not preach
or publish without resolving flagged items below.

1. POWELL BOOK TITLE WAS WRONG — CORRECTED IN THIS FILE
   The notes cited "Do They Hear: Bridging the Gap Between Pulpit and Pew."
   The correct title is "What Do They Hear?: Bridging the Gap Between Pulpit and Pew"
   (Nashville: Abingdon Press, 2007). The "What" at the front is part of the title.
   The citation in the body of this file has been corrected.

2. CAROB TREE "LIFE TREE" CLAIM IS NOT SUPPORTED — DO NOT USE
   The notes claim: "The name of the carob tree in Hebrew means 'life tree' when
   translated into English." This is false. The Hebrew word for carob is חָרוּב (kharub).
   The root is associated with dryness/aridity or the sword-shape of the pod — not life.
   No scholarly source was found supporting the "life tree" meaning. Do not use this
   illustration. Remove it from preparation notes before preaching.

3. "NEAR EAST: VS:12 IS INTERPRETED AS THE SON SAYING TO HIS FATHER" — INCOMPLETE
   This sentence is unfinished in the notes. The Near Eastern interpretive point needs
   to be completed before the sermon is preached. Powell's book covers this; verify
   and complete the thought.

4. DEUT. 21:18–21 — STONING REQUIRED PROCESS, NOT SPONTANEOUS MOB ACTION
   Wiersbe says the son "could have been stoned to death (Deut. 21:18–21)" and that
   the father ran so stones would hit him first. This is technically accurate but should
   be clarified in delivery: Deut. 21:18-21 required the parents to formally accuse the
   son before the city elders, who would then pronounce judgment before the community
   acted. It was not automatic mob action. The father's running as a protective/shame-
   absorbing act is a well-established interpretation (Kenneth Bailey, "Poet & Peasant"
   and "Jacob & the Prodigal"), but the stoning scenario required legal procedure.
   Clarifying this strengthens rather than weakens the point.
