Matthew 28:18–20
No Exceptions: The King’s Command and the Christian’s Commission Introduction — The King Who Sends
If you have your Bible—and I hope you do—please open it with me to Matthew 28, verses 18 through 20.
These are the final recorded words of Christ in the Gospel according to Matthew.
They are not a suggestion; they are a summons.
Not a private call to a select few, but a royal decree to the whole church of Jesus Christ.
Here we stand on holy ground—the risen Christ commissioning His redeemed people.
As I read this passage, I want you to look for loopholes.
Find every possible excuse to avoid it.
“And Jesus came and said to them, ‘All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the Name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age.’”
— Matthew 28:18–20
Now—what part of that command excuses us from sharing the gospel?
Raise your hand if you found one you’d like to discuss.

(Don’t everyone raise your hand at once.)
If God had wanted to excuse children, He could have said, “Adults, go therefore.” But He didn’t.
He could have said, “Go until your knees need replacing,” or, “Go until your body gives out.” But He didn’t.
He could have said, “Go once you have your life together,” or, “Go when you feel ready.” But He didn’t.
He commands us right where we are—now—with all our giftings and all our weaknesses, every flaw and every limitation.
As the Lord said to Gideon, “Go in this might of yours… do not I send you?” (Judg. 6:14).
Perhaps it is because of our lack that He calls us, for “God chose what is weak in the world to shame the strong.” (1 Cor. 1:27).
We all stand lacking in the teeth of this command, and yet He tells us to go.
He sends us just as we are—in the full beauty of our weakness—to share the gospel.
He knows we are not enough.
He made us.
He knows we will sometimes say the wrong thing, yet He planned around that anyway.
“Who has made man’s mouth?” He said to Moses. “Now therefore go, and I will be with your mouth and teach you what you shall speak.” (Ex. 4:11–12).
He knows that when we are put on the spot, our words may flee, but He assures us, “The Holy Spirit will teach you in that very hour what you ought to say.” (Luke 12:12).
Not because we are sufficient, but because He is.
“Not that we are sufficient in ourselves… but our sufficiency is from God.” (2 Cor. 3:5).
He overcomes our lack, our brokenness, our fear.
“My grace is sufficient for you, for My power is made perfect in weakness.” (2 Cor. 12:9).
He tells us to go because He is our strength and our portion forever. (Ps. 73:26).
The command to “Go” begins right where we are—but it never ends there.
A church that loves the Bible must love the nations, because the Bible itself reveals the God of all nations.
The same Word that builds disciples in our pews compels us toward those who have never heard it.
And some today have begun to say that the Great Commission is not the gospel—that the command to make disciples somehow distracts from grace, or even that it fuels oppression.
But beloved, the gospel that saves is the gospel that sends.
The Great Commission is not the enemy of grace; it is grace in motion.
The same Christ who said, “It is finished,” now says, “Go.”
Far from creating prejudice, this is the antidote to it—because only a global gospel destroys every wall of race, pride, and self.
“There is neither Jew nor Greek… for you are all one in Christ Jesus.” (Gal. 3:28).
And yet, how many sermons have we heard on the Great Commission while we still linger in silence?
How often have we nodded in agreement and then lived as though Christ never said these words?
Beloved, delay in obedience is not humility—it is quiet rebellion.

These verses are often called The Great Commission.

But the Puritans sometimes called it The Great Commandment of Christ’s Kingdom.
They understood something we have too easily forgotten—that this is not an optional calling for the energetic, the young, or the ordained.
This is the King’s command to every citizen of His realm.
He says we are to Go. Right now. Today.
The Greek verb πορευθέντες (poreuthentes) carries the sense of “as you are going.”
It implies a life in motion—mission is not a department of the church; it is the direction of the believer’s life.
Let me paint the scene.
Christ has risen.
The wounds in His hands and feet are still visible.
The One who was humiliated now stands exalted.
The disciples—ordinary, trembling men—stand before the Lord of glory.
And what does He do?
He sends them.
He commissions the weak.
He entrusts His gospel to those who, by every earthly measure, should have been unqualified.
Fishermen, tax collectors, doubters—these are the first ambassadors of a global gospel.
That is the wonder of this passage: the King of all creation gives His royal authority to the most ordinary of servants—and He still does.

Think of John, exiled to the prison island of Patmos in his eighties, sleeping on rocks in a damp cave.
Yet there, God used him to pen Revelation—a vision that would shape the hope of the church for millennia.
Be thankful God has placed you in the mission field He has.
And before you start thinking that missions are for someone else, remember the words of Charles Spurgeon:
“Every Christian is either a missionary or an imposter.”
There is no neutral ground.
There is no category for spectators in the kingdom of God.
Some will say, “But I’m too old.”
Others, “I’m too young.”
Some, “I don’t know enough.”
Others, “I don’t have the courage.”
Yet the command remains: “Go therefore.”
The One who gives the command also gives the power—and He does not grant us the right to decline what He has decreed.
Richard Baxter wrote, “If you have the hearts of Christians, you will have the tongues of Christians.”
If Christ has truly saved you, He has also sent you.
And if He has sent you, He will fill your mouth with the words you need when the moment comes.
From Genesis to Revelation, the heartbeat of God is that His glory would fill the earth as the waters cover the sea (Hab. 2:14).
The nations—those like us and those unlike us—are meant to hear, believe, and worship the Lamb who was slain (Rev. 5:9).
From Abraham’s call—“In you all the families of the earth shall be blessed” (Gen. 12:3)—to the song of heaven—“A great multitude from every nation and tribe and tongue” (Rev. 7:9)—this mission has never changed.
Evangelism is not an extracurricular activity; it is evidence of a regenerate heart.
It is not something we do when convenient; it is the command of Christ for every believer.
The Great Commission is not reserved for the missionary on a far shore;
it is for the mother in her home, the student in his class, the retiree in her neighborhood, and the believer whose prayers reach the nations he may never see.
There is no rank, no restriction, no retirement—no excuses.
So today we will look at the Great Commission under three headings:
The Authority That Commands the Mission — Christ reigns.
The Command That Compels the Mission — Christ sends.
The Presence That Sustains the Mission — Christ remains.
My prayer is that by the end, you will not merely know this Commission but feel its weight and joy—that you would walk out convinced that obedience to this command is not for “someone else,” but for you.
Point 1 — The Authority That Commands the Mission
“All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to Me.” — Matthew 28:18
Before He sends, He claims.
Before He commands, He crowns Himself King.
Daniel 7:13–14 foresaw this moment:
“Behold, with the clouds of heaven there came one like a Son of Man… and to Him was given dominion and glory and a kingdom, that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve Him.”
When Jesus declared that all authority had been given to Him, He was announcing that prophecy fulfilled.
The Crucified One now reigns as the Crowned One.
His pierced hands hold the scepter of the universe (Heb. 1:3).
Every molecule, monarch, and missionary moves under His sovereignty (Ps. 103:19).
John Calvin wrote, “When Christ bids us go, He shows that He reigns.”
Our evangelism is not grounded in our initiative but in His enthronement.
Mission begins not with the zeal of man but with the sovereignty of God.
And when the Sovereign speaks and His church stays still, that is not delay—it is quiet rebellion.

Every time we treat His authority as optional, we whisper that His crown can wait.
Yet He reigns now.
How much authority? All authority.
Where? In heaven and on earth.
That leaves no corner unclaimed.
Psalm 2 declares, “Ask of Me, and I will make the nations Your inheritance.”
Isaiah 49:6 adds, “I will make You a light for the nations, that My salvation may reach to the end of the earth.”
From the throne of heaven Christ looks upon Mecca and Manhattan, Kathmandu and Kansas, and says, “Mine.”
His authority spans the angels of glory and the dust beneath our feet.
There is no competing empire.
We could travel a thousand times a thousand years at the speed of light and still not reach the edge of His dominion.
When we go with the gospel, we do not trespass—we deliver a royal decree to a rebel world.
Disobedience to the Great Commission, then, is not mere neglect; it is defiance against the Great King (Luke 6:46).
Because Christ reigns, His people must obey.
Because His throne is above every throne, His command outranks every excuse.
Paul reminds us, “He died for all, that those who live might no longer live for themselves but for Him who for their sake died and was raised.” (2 Cor. 5:15)
Our lives are no longer our own.
Our mission is no longer optional.
If He rules all, then He rules you.
If He possesses all power, then He possesses your witness.
Spurgeon said, “The King’s business requires haste.”
To delay obedience is to doubt His dominion.
To obey His command is to display His kingship to the watching world.

Transition to Point 2
Before we move on, look at what that authority means globally.
The same Christ who claims “all authority” over heaven and earth now calls His church to proclaim that reign where it is least recognized.
(Display the 10/40 Window map.)
In that vast stretch between 10° and 40° north latitude lies the greatest concentration of unreached souls on earth—nations under Christ’s claim but not yet under Christ’s name.
His authority extends there; our obedience must follow there.
And yet, one of the beautiful things about where God has placed us is that we can reach the nations simply by crossing the street.
Look again at that map—India, Pakistan, the Middle East—and realize: there’s a young couple at the gas station up the street from India.
You can literally share the gospel with people from the 10/40 Window right here in our own backyard.
There are countless Russians and Ukrainians in Tampa.
Want to reach Muslims? There are three mosques within ten miles of this church’s front door.
Should we go to the nations? Absolutely—we are commanded to.
Some of you may never fly to West Africa, but every one of us can drive to the corner gas station, build bridges of friendship, and reach our neighbors with the gospel.

Point 2 — The Command That Compels the Mission
“Go therefore and make disciples of all nations…” — Matthew 28:19
The Great Commission is not advice—it is a royal command.
The Greek word μαθητεύσατε (mathēteusate) means make disciples—lifelong learners who love, obey, and resemble Jesus.
Evangelism and discipleship are one motion: proclaim, baptize, teach.
When Jesus said “Go,” He used πορευθέντες (poreuthentes)—“as you are going.” The call is not to an occasional trip but to a continual lifestyle of witness.
Hudson Taylor once said, “The Great Commission is not an option to be considered; it is a command to be obeyed.”
Discipleship, then, is more than information—it’s formation.
We study the Word to be shaped by it, and we use faithful tools that help us see it clearly.
God has given “pastors and teachers, to equip the saints for the work of ministry” (Eph. 4:11–12).
A good commentary, a trusted book, a careful teacher is not a distraction from Scripture but a servant to it—helping us obey the very text we cherish.
“Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly, teaching and admonishing one another in all wisdom.” (Col. 3:16).
The phrase πάντα τὰ ἔθνη (panta ta ethnē) means every people group, every tribe and tongue.
From the very beginning, God’s promise was global:
“In you all the families of the earth shall be blessed.” (Gen. 12:3)
“Declare His glory among the nations.” (Ps. 96:3)
“I will make You a light for the nations.” (Isa. 49:6)
When Jesus said “all nations,” He erased the notion of a chosen race monopoly.
The gospel is not Western; it’s universal.
Christ did not come to build a cultural enclave but to redeem a kingdom of every color and language.
“There is neither Jew nor Greek… for you are all one in Christ Jesus.” (Gal. 3:28)
The accusation that this command breeds superiority could not be further from truth.
It does not elevate one people; it levels them all.
The Great Commission calls sinners—every color, every culture—to bow before one King.
Racism withers at the foot of this mountain, because grace leaves no room for pride.
(Display the Islam Prevalence Map.)
Much of this same 10/40 Window remains unreached—lands where the name of Jesus is seldom heard.
Each shaded region represents millions still waiting for a witness.
Yet the Shepherd says, “I have other sheep that are not of this fold; I must bring them also.” (John 10:16)
To love the nations is not to neglect our own—it is to reflect the heart of God, who “so loved the world.” (John 3:16)
There are no exemptions.
Moses claimed he couldn’t speak (Ex. 4:10–12).
Jeremiah said he was too young (Jer. 1:6–8).
The Samaritan woman testified within hours of believing (John 4:28–30).
Paul preached from prison (Phil. 1:12–14).
The pattern is clear: God delights to use the weak.
Richard Baxter said, “If you have the hearts of Christians, you will have the tongues of Christians.”
And what is the first mark of a discipled heart?
A burden for the lost.
To follow Christ is to join Him in seeking the lost sheep (Luke 19:10).
A disciple without compassion for the perishing is a student who has missed the heart of his Teacher.
The danger facing the modern church is not persecution but passivity.
The gospel’s greatest enemy is not hatred—it is indifference.
When we withhold the message entrusted to us, our silence becomes rebellion wearing the mask of reverence.
(Display the Persecution Map.)
Eighty-six percent of the nations facing severe persecution lie within or near the 10/40 Window.
And yet, the Word of God runs swiftly there—house churches in China, gatherings in Iran, believers whispering hymns in North Africa.
This is not the triumph of courage but of promise: “I will build My church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it.” (Matt. 16:18)
Some of us will cross oceans; all of us can cross streets.
The nations have moved into our neighborhoods.
We can meet the world at a gas station, in a classroom, at a café.
We can love through friendship, prayer, generosity, and truth.
Should we go abroad? Absolutely—we are commanded to.
But obedience begins where our feet already stand.
If we will not go across the street, we will not go across the sea.
True discipleship always bends outward.
And when disciples stop going, when churches grow content to sit, we fall into what the prophets called “hearing but not doing.”
That is not humility—it is silent rebellion against the command of Christ.

Point 3 — The Presence That Sustains the Mission
“And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age.” — Matthew 28:20
The same Lord who sends also stays.
Mission without the presence of Christ would be unbearable.
But the One who rules heaven and earth walks with us into every conversation, every rejection, every act of obedience.
When Moses trembled to face Pharaoh, God promised, “My presence will go with you.” (Ex. 33:14)
When Paul preached alone in Corinth, Christ appeared to him saying, “Do not be afraid, for I am with you.” (Acts 18:9-10)
When the early church scattered in persecution, Luke records, “The hand of the Lord was with them, and a great number believed.” (Acts 11:21)
He does not send us where He will not be found.
He meets us in the mud, the market, the mission field, and the mundane.
The Great Commission ends where it began—with Jesus Himself.
The promise of His presence turns command into comfort, labor into worship, and fear into faith.
The Lord who says “Go” also says “I am with you.”
And that promise has never expired.
The same Christ who stood on that Galilean hill stands beside every witness today.
Conclusion — Until All Have Heard
The Great Commission began with a handful of frightened men on a hillside and ends with a multitude no one can number before the throne.
Between that mountain and that multitude stands us.
The King who reigns has spoken.
The command has gone out.
The promise still stands.
He says: “All authority is Mine.”
He says: “Go therefore.”
He says: “I am with you always.”
Those three sentences are the spine of history.
They are enough to carry the church until the sky splits and the trumpet sounds.
Forty percent of the world has yet to hear the gospel even once.
Do not say, “Someone else will go.”
Do not say, “When I am ready.”
Do not say, “My part is too small.”
If Christ is your King, you are part of His campaign.
You are a messenger of a conquering Savior.
You are sent into your street, your city, and your century with news that can raise the dead.
Let the nations rage; the King has already been crowned.
Let the world resist; the Lamb has already overcome.
And let the church arise, because the world will not hear if we do not speak.
Forty percent of the world—whole families, villages, tribes, and tongues—remain bound for eternity without the gospel.
We are commanded to go. Not when we are ready—now.
I love Spurgeon’s heart for the lost when he said,
“If sinners be damned, at least let them leap to hell over our bodies.
If they perish, let them perish with our arms about their knees.”
Let it be said of us that we made it hard for people to go to hell.
That is the heartbeat of heaven—the burden of the Great Commission.
So go—
not in your strength, but in His.
Go—
not because you are worthy, but because He is.
Go— until the last person from the last tribe hears,
until the last sinner repents,
until the last voice joins the song:
“Worthy is the Lamb who was slain,
to receive power and wealth and wisdom and might
and honor and glory and blessing!” (Rev. 5:12)
And when that song fills the earth, our mission will be complete—
and our King will return.
The command is clear, the King is near, and the time is now.

If you only hear one thing I say all day, hear this: 

The question is not if we are sent, but how soon we will obey.
Not whether we should take the gospel to the nations, but how long we will linger in silent rebellion against God.

Closing Prayer
O Sovereign King,
we confess that too often we have treated Your command as suggestion.
We have loved comfort more than obedience,
and silence more than witness.
Forgive us for our quiet rebellion—
For speaking when it costs nothing,
and falling silent when it costs much.
You are the Lord of all nations, the Savior of all who believe.
You have bought us with Your blood and placed Your commission in our hands.
We repent of every excuse that has kept our mouths closed and our hearts cold.
So send us again, Lord—
send us across the street and across the sea,
into our neighborhoods and into the nations.
Teach us to love the lost as You have loved us.
Fill our weakness with Your strength,
our fear with Your Spirit,
our hesitation with holy fire.
May it be said of this church that we went,
that we spoke,
that we obeyed the voice of our King. That we made it hard for the nations to end up in Hell. All the nations.
For Yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory,
forever and ever.
In the name of Jesus Christ we pray. Amen.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Commanding Missional Charge with Gospel Assumed Rather Than Proclaimed**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A textually grounded, rhetorically powerful call to Great Commission obedience that marshals Christ's authority, the global scope of the nations, and the promise of His presence with genuine force, while leaving the gospel itself declared but not fully explicated — assumed by the congregation rather than pressed open for the unconverted.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS**

- **Scopus Test**: Pass. The sermon's thesis — "The risen and enthroned Christ commands every believer, without exception, to make disciples of all nations, equipped by His authority and sustained by His presence" — arises directly and demonstrably from Matthew 28:18–20. The three-point structure maps cleanly to the three clauses of the passage (authority / command / presence).
- **Authority Test**: Pass. The preacher does not soften the passage's imperative force. He presses on conscience, confronts passivity directly, and names silent non-obedience as "quiet rebellion." He stands under the text as herald.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Flag Present — Score Capped at 69**

- **Flag #1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The sermon assumes the gospel rather than proclaims it. The cross is referenced briefly ("It is finished," "bought us with His blood," the Spurgeon quote), and Christ's resurrection is mentioned. However, justification by faith is never articulated. What it means to "believe" is never opened. An unbeliever in the room would understand they are commanded to go share the gospel — but would not hear what the gospel *is* from this sermon. The Synagogue Test is not entirely failed (the necessity of Christ's blood is present), but only barely — the mechanics of salvation are absent. The sermon sends people to share a gospel it never presents.
- **Flag #4 — Application is largely undifferentiated for hearers**: Application is specific geographically (gas station, mosques, Tampa) but not spiritually differentiated. The sermon addresses primarily already-convinced believers. The backslider, the nominal Christian, the unconverted, and the doubter are not meaningfully distinguished.

Score cap: **69**

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 19/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (8/10)*: The three-point structure arises from the text's own three clauses. The Greek terms (πορευθέντες, μαθητεύσατε, πάντα τὰ ἔθνη) are deployed appropriately and illuminate real meaning rather than serving as decorative scholarship. The Daniel 7 connection to "all authority" is exegetically responsible.
- *Contextual Awareness (4/5)*: The redemptive-historical sweep (Gen. 12:3 → Ps. 96 → Isa. 49 → Rev. 5, 7) is well-handled and genuinely illuminating. The sermon locates Matthew 28 within the biblical storyline.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5)*: Greek terms explained accessibly without condescension. Non-technical hearers can follow.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5)*: The passage's primary imperative — *mathēteusate*, "make disciples" — receives less development than the sub-elements of baptizing and teaching. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is partially named (passivity, fear, comfort-love) but not pressed to the root. The sermon does not ask *why* the church is so passive — what misbelief or idolatry drives the silence. This is a genuine exegetical gap.

**Category 2 — Theology & Doctrinal Integrity: 13/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness (5/7)*: Christ is genuinely central as the commanding King and present companion. The resurrection is noted. But Christ's mediatorial work — *why* His authority was "given" to Him, what the resurrection accomplished soteriologically — is underdeveloped. The sermon highlights the enthroned Christ more than the crucified and risen Christ.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3/3)*: Strong. The Abraham-to-Revelation arc is traced with care and is organically connected to Matthew 28.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4)*: No errors. The sovereignty of God in mission is rightly emphasized. However, justification and regeneration as the foundation of obedience are absent. Evangelism is called "evidence of a regenerate heart" — a good instinct — but regeneration itself is not explained.
- *Confessional Alignment (2/4)*: No contradiction of confessional theology. However, where the text naturally surfaces the covenant people of God, the nature of saving faith, and the gospel's content, the sermon does not engage with confessional precision. The treatment of "all nations" is solid but the soteriological underpinning of the mission is thin.

**Category 3 — Gospel Centrality & Force: 8/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel (1/5)*: The gospel is assumed, not proclaimed. "It is finished" appears once. "Bought us with His blood" appears in the prayer. But what Christ accomplished, for whom, how it is received — none of this is opened. An unbeliever would not know how to be saved from this sermon.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5)*: The sermon would not survive without Christ — He is the commanding, sustaining, authorizing King throughout. This is genuine.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5)*: The sermon does not orbit morality, but it also does not orbit the cross. It orbits mission and obedience. The cross appears as motivation rather than the sermon's gravitational center. The law/gospel dialectic is partially present — "quiet rebellion" creates conviction, but the relief of the gospel is thin and does not arrive with full weight before the imperative is pressed again.

**Category 4 — Conscience & Applicatory Force: 9/15**
- *Heart Penetration (3/5)*: The sermon does press on conscience — "quiet rebellion" is a sharp phrase, and the Spurgeon quote at the close lands with pastoral weight. But the heart-level diagnosis stops at behavior (not going) without diagnosing the idol beneath (comfort, fear of man, unbelief in the sufficiency of Christ).
- *Specificity (4/5)*: The local illustrations are genuinely specific — Tampa Russians and Ukrainians, mosques within ten miles, gas stations. This is a real strength. The congregation's actual geography is used.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5)*: The sermon primarily addresses the already-believing-but-passive church member. The nominal Christian, the unconverted visitor, the doubter, the exhausted saint, and the backslider are not addressed distinctly. Perkins' categories are largely collapsed into one: "believer who needs to obey."

**Category 5 — Exhortation & Response: 6/8**
- *Call to Response (3/4)*: The closing is genuinely exhortatory. "Go — not in your strength, but in His" has real force. The prayer of confession models corporate repentance. However, the exhortation is primarily directional (go share) rather than comprehensive (what must I believe, repent of, obey today — beyond evangelism?).
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (3/4)*: The sermon sends the church outward, which is its strength. The "cross the street" concreteness is excellent. It stops short of equipping the saint to give an account of the gospel hope within them — you are sent, but not trained in the content of what to say.

**Category 6 — Structure & Logical Flow: 7/9**
- *Clarity of Outline (3/4)*: The three-point outline is memorable and maps cleanly to the text. A listener could sketch it: Authority → Command → Presence.
- *Progression (3/3)*: The sermon builds well. The conclusion genuinely lands on the sermon's main thrust. The transition to Point 2 with the 10/40 Window is an effective pivot.
- *Unity (1/2)*: The Richard Baxter quote appears twice (verbatim). Some ideas circle back rather than advancing. The sermon occasionally repeats "quiet rebellion" as a refrain that, while effective the first time, becomes slightly less sharp with repetition.

**Category 7 — Illustration & Imagination: 4/5**
- *Relevance (2/2)*: The gas station illustration, the Tampa demographics, the mosques within ten miles — these are genuinely from the congregation's world. John on Patmos is an effective image.
- *Clarity (1/2)*: The map displays (10/40 Window, Islam Prevalence, Persecution) are referenced in the manuscript but are visual aids — their effectiveness depends on delivery. On the page, they read as instruction rather than illustration.
- *Memorability (1/1)*: The Spurgeon "leap to hell over our bodies" quote is a memorable closer that will be carried home.

**Category 8 — Sermonic Force: 4/5**
- *Reads as Preaching (2/2)*: The manuscript reads as oral proclamation. Breath-length sentences, direct address ("Some of you will say…"), rhetorical repetition ("Go — not in your strength... Go — not because you are worthy"). This is written to be preached.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2)*: The conviction is present but slightly uneven — some sections feel like they are building toward something that doesn't fully arrive. The gospel relief that should come after the "quiet rebellion" hammer is thin, which leaves the conviction without its proper resolution.
- *Cadence and Breath (1/1)*: The closing cadence is genuinely excellent. Sentence compression at the climax is well-executed.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Redemptive-historical sweep is genuinely excellent.** The sermon traces the mission of God from Abraham through the Psalms and Isaiah to Revelation with organic coherence (lines 85, 162–165). This is not proof-texting — it demonstrates that the Great Commission is not a new idea but the culmination of the entire biblical storyline. This is a real expositional strength.

2. **Local application is specific and congregationally grounded.** The 10/40 Window is brought inside the congregation's own neighborhood — Tampa's Russian and Ukrainian populations, mosques within ten miles, the gas station up the street (lines 141–147). This grounds the "all nations" command in the actual geography of the congregation rather than leaving it abstract.

3. **The sermonic close is powerful and well-constructed.** The three-sentence spine — "All authority is Mine / Go therefore / I am with you always" (lines 229–233) — is a genuinely memorable compression of the text's argument. The Spurgeon quote and the cascading imperatives ("Go — not in your strength... Go — not because you are worthy") are written with real preacherly force.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is assumed, never proclaimed.** This is the sermon's most serious structural failure. The commission is to "make disciples" — but the sermon never opens the content of the message those disciples are to carry. Justification, the nature of saving faith, repentance — these are absent. A visitor who has never heard the gospel would leave this sermon knowing they are commanded to share it but not knowing what it is. If the sermon sends people to evangelize, it must itself model evangelism. This is not a minor gap — it is the central irony of the sermon. (See lines 37–41, 86–88: mission is addressed; the message's content is not.)

2. **"Quiet rebellion" is a hammer without a balm.** The phrase "quiet rebellion" is used three times as the diagnostic of missional failure (lines 45, 109, 192, 268). This is a faithful and sharp charge. But the law has time to land with real weight without sufficient gospel relief arriving before the next imperative. The sermon does not give the convicted hearer Christ's finished work as the ground from which they can now obey, not as rebels but as beloved children. This creates a motivational structure more akin to duty under threat than love under grace.

3. **Spiritual differentiation is nearly absent.** The sermon speaks to one type of hearer: the already-believing but passive church member. It does not address the nominal Christian who has heard this sermon a hundred times and is numb to it, the doubter wondering if they are truly saved and therefore qualified to witness, the brand-new believer who is eager but terrified, or the unbeliever in the room. The congregation is addressed as a uniform body of passive disciples who need activating. This flattens the pastoral range of the text. (Lines 74–79 gesture toward differentiation — "I'm too old / too young / don't know enough / don't have courage" — but these are addressed as excuses to be swept aside rather than real spiritual conditions to be pastored through.)

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears not to see that the Great Commission sermon that lacks a gospel presentation is self-undermining. The entire burden of the sermon is that silence about the gospel is rebellion — and yet the sermon itself is largely silent about the gospel's content. This is not hypocrisy; it likely reflects a preaching context where the gospel is assumed to be known. But the rubric asks whether the gospel is *proclaimed*, not merely assumed. A congregant who has never heard the gospel explained — or who has heard it so many times the categories have grown stale — needed this sermon to break open the treasure it is commanding them to carry.

There is also a potential blind spot regarding the difference between *mission* and *evangelism*. The sermon uses these terms somewhat interchangeably. "Making disciples" involves proclamation, baptism, and teaching — the sermon handles baptism and teaching very lightly. The full scope of the disciple-making commission is somewhat collapsed into evangelistic witness.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The biblical scope of the sermon creates an illusion of gospel richness. The Abraham-to-Revelation arc, the references to Isaiah 49 and Revelation 5, the Daniel 7 connection — these feel theologically substantial, and they are genuinely present. But they serve the argument that the mission is globally intended, not that the gospel itself is glorious. The theological richness is in the *scope* of the mission, not in the *content* of the message. A listener could walk away feeling they have heard a theologically weighty sermon — and in terms of biblical-theological breadth, they have — without being able to articulate what they are to tell the nations. This is the sermon's central illusion.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The phrase "All authority has been given to Me" (v. 18) is the text's own foundation for the commission — and it is the most profound missed opportunity. The sermon develops this as Christ's enthronement and sovereignty (rightly), but it misses the *gospel* embedded in this claim: authority was *given* to Christ precisely because He earned it through the humiliation of the cross and the vindication of the resurrection. Philippians 2:8–11 is the commentary here — "He humbled Himself... therefore God has highly exalted Him." The authority that grounds the commission is resurrection-authority, purchased-authority, suffering-authority. This is where the gospel most naturally enters the passage — and it is the point at which it was passed over. Developing this would have anchored the entire commission in the finished work of Christ and given the sermon its missing gospel center.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Open the gospel at Point 1, where the text demands it.** When expounding "all authority has been given to Me," insert a brief but explicit statement of what that authority cost — the cross, the resurrection, justification by faith. A paragraph connecting Matthew 28:18 to Philippians 2:8–11 would give the sermon its missing gospel center without disrupting the structure. The authority that commands the mission is resurrection-authority — and resurrection authority is purchased authority. That purchase is the gospel.

2. **Add a paragraph addressing the unconverted hearer.** After the strong "quiet rebellion" charge, insert a section that distinguishes hearers: for the unconverted visitor — "Before you can carry this gospel, you must receive it. Here is what it is..."; for the doubting believer — "If you wonder whether you are truly sent, the question is whether you are truly His — and here is how you may know." This pastoral differentiation would both strengthen application and model the very evangelism the sermon commands.

3. **Resolve the law/hammer with fuller gospel balm before the final imperatives.** The closing exhortation is strong, but it arrives before sufficient relief has been given. After "quiet rebellion," the sermon should give full weight to the gospel promise: "You are not sent as rebels performing penance — you are sent as beloved children of a victorious King whose work is finished. The mission does not save you; the Savior does. Go, therefore, from the table — not toward the throne." This reordering of indicative and imperative would make the final "Go" feel like liberation rather than obligation.

4. **Eliminate the duplicate Baxter quote.** Richard Baxter's "If you have the hearts of Christians, you will have the tongues of Christians" appears on lines 80 and 185. The second instance weakens the first. Remove the second; let it land once with full weight.

5. **Develop the "baptizing and teaching" elements of the commission.** The sermon correctly centers on *mathēteusate* but leaves the two participles (baptizing, teaching) largely undeveloped. A brief treatment of these would complete the disciple-making picture and prevent the commission from being reduced to a simple evangelistic call. What does it mean to bring someone all the way to baptism and doctrinal formation? That is a fuller vision of mission than what the sermon currently presents.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** This sermon is not bloodless or pastorally inert. The preacher's burden for the lost is evident. The closing prayer carries genuine confession and heat. The sermonic force is real. The weakness is not dead orthodoxy — it is a specific structural absence (gospel proclamation) in an otherwise energetic and warm sermon.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries genuine eternal gravity in its final movement — the vision of a multitude from every tribe and tongue, the Spurgeon quote, and the cascading imperatives toward the conclusion earn moments of real Weight of Glory, though the absence of the gospel's full content prevents the bonus from reaching its full potential. **Bonus: +2/5**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Matthew 28:18–20 is a commission/command text — not primarily a doctrinal exposition or a narrative passage. The rubric's heaviest categories (Exposition, Theology, Gospel Centrality) apply fully and appropriately. The one place the rubric may slightly strain the genre: this is a commission text, not a gospel text — it does not itself present the gospel but assumes it. A fair evaluation must therefore acknowledge that the preacher is not expounding Romans 3 or John 3; he is expounding a command to go with a gospel that is elsewhere presented. However, the rubric's insistence that every sermon present the gospel clearly is not abrogated by the genre. A Great Commission sermon that commands people to carry a gospel it never presents is precisely the kind of sermon that must be flagged — because the congregation cannot carry what they have not been shown.
