# Fear Is the Evidence — Hebrews 11:1 / 1 John 4:18

**Primary Text**: Hebrews 11:1
**Supporting Texts**: 1 John 4:18, Romans 13:1
**Themes**: Fear vs. faith, fear vs. love, trusting God in suffering, God's sovereignty over politics, testimony service
**Illustrations**: Grandmother's dark room (childhood fear), Hurricane flooding (March '93), murdered child, singleness, political fear (Trump/Hillary era)
**People Group**: None
**Communion**: No
**Format**: Short sermon followed by open testimony time

---

I was asked to open up the service to those who would like to share a little bit of what God is doing in their lives. I love hearing stories of God at work, and so I agreed to it. We will get to that here in a few minutes, but you guys know me, I gotta talk for a few minutes at least.
So turn with me to Heb 11:1.

It seems to me a lot of us have things in our lives that we are afraid of no? I remember being 5 or 6 and about to stay the night at my grandmothers. My grandmother loved to listen to her TV LOUD. Her TV had I think 100 volume settings, and she'd keep her tv at around 99. I think if 120 were an option, she'd have pushed it there. So my dad tucked me in, turned out the lights, and closed the door. I don't remember why, probably some weird shadow, or sound, but something spooked me, and I went running back out after him. He walked me back in, and turned the lights back on, and sat there with me for several minutes re-assuring me that there is nothing there to be afraid of. Told me I had to face my fears or they would cripple me. I trusted my dad, and before long, he left and went after whatever he and moms plans were. I slept through the night, and I don't think I've ever had an issue with the dark after that.

Lets read.

We have a lot of fears don't we, financial fears, social fears, relational fears. Fears about our stuff. Fears about our kids. We all have serious and significant situations going on in our life that can cause us to feel fear.

One day I'll tell you more about some situations in our life that we just have had to trust God about. We've had a good number of them that I can talk about though. We have talked about Irma a fair bit, Irma required a lot of faith. My dad has shared about his father. My grandfather. Did he die a Christian? Is he in heaven or isn't he? We will never know before we get to heaven. Or the night the waters just kept coming up, up over the planks of the dock, up across the yard to the steps, and one at a time up and over our steps until the water began coming into our house. Up into our cars. Up into our dresser. Up over the dresser, up over the freezers, up over the tools, the books, the toys. Up over everything we owned one inch at a time until pretty much everything we owned was ruined, soaked in sewage, and eventually covered in mold. Where do you live? By the end we didn't even have cleaning supplies to begin putting things back together. Doesn't take much to ruin a mobile home. You don't get much from your insurance for one either.

I remember a little girl that had lived with us being murdered by her babysitter. Little wonder I wasn't allowed to ride my bicycle out of the yard until I had my drivers license. Can you imagine that parents? A little child, an infant in arms that had lived under your roof being murdered by an acquaintance. What fears would that stir in you? I was young enough, or self assured enough, or protected by God enough that I wasn't really phased by that part of it. I was sad she was gone. But it never occurred to me to be afraid.

Time after time after time we as a family ran head long into a situation where we were under water, or out of our depth, or in a place where we just couldn't.

But God could. A year or two after the flood, God provided for us a beautiful home. We had just enough money to pay to have it moved in, lifted up onto the safety of stilts, and brought up to code. I think we had 100$ left over. It's a much better house. In the mobile my sister lived in a room smaller than some closets I've had. Dresser built into the wall, enough space between the bed and the wall that if you walked in sideways you could fit. In the new house, her bedroom was as big or bigger than the master bedroom in mobile. We went from a scary propane heater that didn't always work, to central heat and air. We went from 800 sq feet to 2000 or more. We loved the mobile. We were sad to see it Go, but its as though God was saying that night in march of 93 – just trust me. I have something better around the corner.

With the little girl that was murdered, God protected my sister and I. He didn't let that trauma shape the perspective on the world that was still being shaped in us. He kept us from being afraid of everyone, and that sort of thing happens to kids in these situations.

Point is God is active and working and moving in every situation we are involved with. It comes back to a question of trust I think. A quote I came across this week that I love is that "Fear is the result of distrust, of taking the eye off God, of being unduly occupied with difficulties and troubles." (A.W. Pink, *An Exposition of Hebrews*, p. 808). That kinda stings sometimes. When we have these bouts of fear, our fear says to God "I don't trust you to handle this".

Open with me to 1 John 4:18. The ESV says "There is no fear in love, but perfect love casts out fear." So love is the opposite of fear, and so when we are gripped with fear, we aren't loving God like we should. When we love God properly we are focused on him and not the scary waves. "Fear is the result of distrust, of taking the eye off God, of being unduly occupied with difficulties and troubles." He could have as easily said fear is the result of not loving God fully, of taking the eye off God, or of being occupied with our difficulties and troubles.

The long and short of it is this: Fear is the evidence of things in our heart that we love as much or more than God.

Think about it. When we fear for our physical needs, we are telling God we don't trust him to follow through on his promises to do so. We are demonstrating our love for our own comfort over our Love for God. We are saying to God we don't think he's taking good enough care of us. Imagine for a minute you were visiting a friend out of state, lets say pastor Bob and Sherry, you're visiting them out in texas. How mortified would you be if your kid or grand kid said to them that they weren't meeting your families needs well enough? Bob and Sherry go out of their way to love people, and give freely. Can you imagine how mortified you would be in that situation? Yet that's exactly what we do to God when we fear for our physical needs. We tell God his provision is insufficient. What he's done, and is doing isn't good enough. Shameful stuff isn't it. We all do it. Not just physical needs. Financial needs, or basically any need you have where you become entrenched in fear. I know a young lady that struggles with her singleness. For her it's a fear that she'll never meet the right guy, never settle down. Never start a family. She has to be careful, or she can stray into distrust of God.

Heres another one that stings. Politics. Everyone has a political view, and everyone is pretty passionate about it. Everyone wants to see their candidate win. Everyone has fear of the opposing candidate. Fear. Fear of what Hillary, or Joe Biden will do to our country. Fear that the country won't be able to survive. Fear fear fear fear fear. But what does God say? Turn with me to Romans 13:1. "Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God." *(See flags — corrected from NLT to ESV.)*

If someone is in authority, they have been placed there by God. That means when a republican is in charge of something, its because God put them there. Before you Amen that, the otherside of that coin is that if a democrat, or independent is in charge of something God has placed that person there as well. If Hillary had won, it would have been because God had ordained it, because God had placed her in the highest office of the land. I am personally pretty grateful that God didn't do that. Trump has done better than I expected, but I expected a dumpster fire knowing full well that dumpster fire would have been far far better than anything Hillary managed to do. But the point is, God promises he has our politics under control. We should absolutely vote. But God has the results in his hand. So what do we have to fear? When we do fear what is it we are telling God? I don't love you enough to trust you even in the midst of a political candidate I disagree with. I don't love you enough to trust you with my singleness, I don't love you enough to trust you with my Kids… Is that what we want to say to God?

God has it all under control. I think back on that little baby, a toddler really that was murdered. God had a purpose even in the midst of that horror, of that absolute worst nightmare for a parent. God had a plan. We don't know what would have happened to this little girl had she lived. But you know who does? God. God whom we love. God who we can trust. The God that safeguards our eternity can certainly safeguard our short fragile lives. That God that sent his son to earth in humility to live a sinless life, to pay our sin debt for us through a horrible death on the cross is certainly deserving of our love.

Hate isn't the opposite of love. Fear is. Lets go ahead and pray, and after that we will open it up for some testimonies.

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A warm, autobiographically grounded sermon that arrives at a sharp and memorable pastoral thesis — "Fear is the evidence of things in our heart that we love as much or more than God" — while leaving Hebrews 11:1 nearly unexplored and the gospel only gestured at.

### 3. Berean Gate

**PASS — with significant qualification.**

The sermon's thesis is: "Fear is the evidence of things in our heart that we love as much or more than God." This connects to both primary texts (Hebrews 11:1 on faith, 1 John 4:18 on perfect love casting out fear) but does not arise from either text's own main argument.

**Hebrews 11:1**: The text's main point is a definition of faith as the substance of things hoped for and the conviction of things not seen — as the introduction to a long gallery of Old Testament examples of faith. The sermon does not preach this chapter's argument; it uses v. 1 as a springboard for a talk on fear.

**1 John 4:18**: The text's main point is that God's love perfects and expels fear — placed in the context of John's argument about abiding in love and love's perfection. The sermon quotes the verse but does not preach its argument.

The gate passes because the thesis is genuinely supported by the texts and does not contradict them. But the "main point of the sermon = main point of the text" test is not clearly met for either text. This is a soft pass.

**Authority Test**: The preacher does not edit the texts. Pass.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Hebrews 11:1 is not preached**: The sermon title references Hebrews 11:1, but the verse is referenced rather than exposited. The sermon's argument does not arise from the text's own argument (the definition of faith as substance and conviction — as the introduction to a gallery of faithful suffering). If the text is Hebrews 11:1, the sermon should preach what that verse means in context, not only riff on its vocabulary.
2. **The gospel is incomplete**: An unbeliever attending this service would understand that God is trustworthy and that Christ died — but not why Christ's death is necessary for *them specifically*, or what it means for their standing before God, or what they must do. The gospel is present but not proclaimed. This is a meaningful gap for any sermon, but especially one followed by a testimony service open to all.
3. **Abrupt closing**: "Hate isn't the opposite of love. Fear is. Let's go ahead and pray." The thesis is restated and then prayer begins immediately. The sermon deserves a closing sentence that allows the thesis to land — one breath of silence between the statement and the prayer prompt.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher does not appear to see that the testimony service following the sermon is itself an act of pastoral theology that the sermon could explicitly frame. Testimonies are the congregation's answer to the question "What has God done for you when you were afraid?" The sermon builds toward this perfectly — personal narratives of fear met by God — but does not name the testimony service as the extension of the sermon's argument. "And now I want to hear from you — what is your flood story? What fear did God meet?" would unify the sermon and the service.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal narratives are so well told and so emotionally resonant that the sermon *feels* more exegetically grounded than it is. The congregation will experience this as Bible-based preaching because the Pink quote is in there and the texts are cited — but the texts are doing less work than the preacher's testimony. That testimony is valuable and genuine, but it is not a substitute for exposition.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

First John 4:18 reads: "There is no fear in love, but perfect love casts out fear. For fear has to do with punishment, and whoever fears has not been perfected in love." The sermon quotes only the first sentence. The second sentence — "fear has to do with punishment" — is the diagnostic key: fear at its root is the terror of a God who will punish us for who we are. That is why the gospel is the cure for fear. When we know that Christ has taken our punishment (penal substitution), the fear of punishment is gone. The sermon reaches toward this but does not name it. One sentence connecting 1 John 4:18b to penal substitution would have completed the theological argument and anchored the gospel presentation.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Quote and briefly exposit 1 John 4:18 in full**: Include v. 18b ("fear has to do with punishment") and connect it to penal substitution — Christ took our punishment, so the fear of God's judgment is replaced by love. This completes the gospel without lengthening the sermon significantly.
2. **Frame the testimony service explicitly**: Transition from the sermon to the testimonies with a sentence that connects them: "Now I want to hear from you — when has God met you in the thing you feared most?" This makes the testimony service part of the sermon's argument.
3. **Preach Hebrews 11:1 in its own context for one paragraph**: What does it mean that faith is the "substance" of things hoped for? What were the Hebrews afraid of? This verse was written to people who were being pressured to abandon Christ and return to the synagogue — their fear was real and the stakes were eternal. One paragraph establishing this would give the text its own weight.
4. **Write a closing sentence**: Replace the immediate prayer prompt with one sentence that allows the thesis to resonate. "God has your fear. He has already met it at the cross. Trust him with the rest."
