# Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

## 1. Verdict Title
**A Warmhearted Hope Sermon That Glances at the Text and Gazes at Illustrations**

## 2. One-Sentence Summary
A gospel-affectionate message on Christian hope anchored in Titus 2 that offers genuine warmth and doxological sweep while failing to exposit the passage's argument, press the conscience with specificity, or exhort a concrete response.

## 3. Berean Gate
**PASS (with warning)**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's thesis — Christians have hope grounded in grace, gospel, and the second coming of Christ — is a legitimate theme present in Titus 2:11–14. The passage does link the two advents to grace and sanctification.

*Authority Test*: The preacher does not edit or soften the text. He submits to its claims. However, the sermon treats the passage as a launching pad rather than as the governing argument. Titus 2:11–14 has a specific logical structure (grace appeared → trains us → we await glory → Christ gave himself → to purify a people for good works), and the sermon does not follow that structure. The passage passes the gate, but just barely.

## 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Flag 4 — Application is Entirely Generic**: The sermon's applications ("Are you eagerly waiting for Christ's return?" "You might have a heart issue") are general exhortations that do not differentiate hearers with specificity, do not name concrete sins or situations, and do not press distinct categories of the congregation.

**No score cap applied** — the flag is present but borderline. The gospel is clearly proclaimed, Christ is essential, and ordinary hearers can follow it. The flag is noted and weighted in the scoring.

## 5. Score Breakdown

### 1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 13/25
- **Textual Fidelity (6/10)**: The sermon touches Titus 2:11–14 but does not exposit it. Verses 11 and 13 are quoted; verse 14 is read; the passage's governing logic (grace trains → awaiting glory → Christ purifies → zealous for good works) is gestured at ("grace sandwich") but not worked through. Romans 5:8–10 and Hebrews 9:27–28 do significant heavy lifting in place of the home passage.
- **Contextual Awareness (2/5)**: No discussion of Titus as a letter, Paul's purpose, Crete's context, or why this doxological section sits between ethical instructions.
- **Exegetical Clarity (2/5)**: No engagement with the Greek, no explanation of *epiphaneia*, *makarios*, or the grammatical link between sanctification and eschatology. The "grace sandwich" metaphor is creative but imprecise — it flattens the text's argument.
- **Logical Faithfulness (3/5)**: The preacher does not import alien ideas, but the Fallen Condition Focus is never named. Why did Paul write these verses? What failure in the Cretan church (or in us) required this word? That question is never asked.

### 2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18
- **Christ-Centeredness (6/7)**: Christ's death, resurrection, and return are clearly central. The kinsman-redeemer illustration from Ruth is a genuine typological connection, not an artificial Jesus jump.
- **Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3)**: The first and second advents are linked, which is organically true to the passage. But no redemptive-historical framing of where this fits in the covenant story.
- **Doctrinal Precision (3/4)**: The gospel is stated clearly. "He destroyed the empire our enemy has been building since his fall" is vivid and substantially accurate. However, the atonement is described in Christus Victor terms almost exclusively, with no articulation of penal substitution beyond "died for our sins." Justification is not named; imputation is absent.
- **Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse (2/4)**: No contradiction of confessional theology, but significant positive soteriological content (justification, the covenant of grace, the church as the redeemed people) is left undeveloped.

### 3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 10/15
- **Clarity of the Gospel (3/5)**: The gospel facts are present (death, burial, resurrection). But the logic of salvation — how Christ's death actually secures the sinner — is not articulated. "He died for our sins" is stated but not explained. Substitution, propitiation, justification are absent as categories.
- **Necessity of Christ (5/5)**: Christ is thoroughly essential. The sermon cannot survive without him.
- **Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5)**: The sermon moves toward the second coming and heaven, not the cross. The cross is mentioned but not dwelt upon. The movement is eschatological rather than cross-centered. The law has no time to land before grace relieves it — conviction is essentially absent.

*Synagogue Test*: The gospel is present enough that a devout non-Christian could not sit through this entirely undisturbed — Christ's atoning death is named. But the cross is not a scandal here; it is a foundation piece in a larger hope argument. Borderline.

*Law/Gospel*: The hammer is absent. The gospel is offered without prior law-work stripping the hearer of self-trust. This produces warmth without wound.

### 4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15
- **Heart Penetration (2/5)**: The $20 bill illustration and the nature imagery are evocative. But these illustrations move toward comfort, not conviction. No sinner is cornered. No self-righteous hearer is exposed. No doubter's specific fear is named.
- **Specificity (2/5)**: "If you're not eagerly waiting for Christ's return, you might have a heart issue" — this is the sermon's most pointed moment. It is not specific enough. What does a cold heart look like in this congregation? What idols displace the longing for Christ?
- **Spiritual Differentiation (3/5)**: The sermon briefly addresses the believer who has "grown scales" over their heart. But only believers are addressed; the unbeliever is not confronted, the doubter is not engaged, the moralist is not exposed. Two or three of Perkins' categories are touched, the rest are untouched.

### 5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8
- **Call to Response (1/4)**: "Are you eagerly waiting for Christ's return?" is a diagnostic question, not an exhortation. The sermon ends on doxology ("What a mighty God we serve. Amen?") without a clear imperative call — what must the congregation do, repent of, pursue, or obey today?
- **Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4)**: The second-coming orientation has some equipping force. Titus 2:14's "zealous for good works" is mentioned but not unpacked — what does that zeal look like in this congregation's week?

### 6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9
- **Clarity of Outline (2/4)**: The structure is: people-group intro → hope defined → threat to hope (liberalism) → hope grounded in grace/gospel → $20 illustration → attentiveness of God → glory/heaven. This is traceable but not tightly unified.
- **Progression (3/3)**: The sermon does build — from threat to foundation to doxology. The movement is genuine even if the logical connections are sometimes loose.
- **Unity (1/2)**: The Bultmann/Neil/Best section on liberalism takes significant space and feels like a detour. It is relevant but competes with the primary argument.

### 7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5
- **Relevance (2/2)**: The $20 bill, the sparrows and flowers, the mountain in the Alps, the Friday sunset — these are drawn from accessible, concrete life.
- **Clarity (1/2)**: The $20 bill illustration is memorable and clear but is used to make a point about God's attentiveness (our value), not directly about hope. The connection is made, but the illustration does more comfort work than gospel work.
- **Memorability (1/1)**: The sunset description and the mountain at the window are images that will stay.

### 8. Sermonic Force — 3/5
- **Reads as Preaching (2/2)**: Yes — oral cadence, conversational asides, direct questions. This reads as spoken word, not essay.
- **Conviction on the Page (1/2)**: Scripture density is lower than ideal. Long stretches go without returning to the text. The sermon sometimes feels like a series of John Piper quotes linked by transition.
- **Cadence and Breath (0/1)**: Sentence variation is present, but there is no moment where the manuscript roars — no climactic compression, no pivot point where the truth lands with full weight.

## 6. Cognitive Load Modifier — 0
Well-paced. The sermon is accessible. An average church member, a high schooler, and a new believer can all follow it. No cognitive load penalty.

## 7. Total Score
13 + 13 + 10 + 7 + 3 + 6 + 4 + 3 = **59/100**

**Interpretation: Below 60 — Fundamentally flawed in application and exhortation. Rebuild the conscience-searching section and the call to response from the text.**

*(Note: This sits at 59 — just below the 60-threshold. The sermon is not without merit, but the near-absence of conscience-searching, the lack of a clear exhortation, and the thin exposition prevent it from crossing into "Concerning Gaps" territory. It is one focused revision from reaching 70+.)*

## 8. Core Override Questions
1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily Christ. The sermon is genuinely God-ward and Christ-exalting. Pass.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Mostly ideas — warmly stated ideas, but God is described more than felt. The doxological moments (sunset, mountains, heaven) approach encounter. Partial.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Comforted and informed. Not convicted. No hammer fell before the balm was offered.

## 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Gospel clarity is genuine**: The death, burial, resurrection, and return of Christ are all explicitly named. The kinsman-redeemer connection to Ruth is exegetically legitimate and pastorally warm (paragraph beginning "In essence, Christ came to conquer Sin and death...").
2. **Accessible oral voice**: The manuscript reads as it was preached — conversational, warm, unaffected. The people-group framing and congregational interaction markers ("Anyone?" "Hah.") signal authentic pastoral relationship.
3. **Doxological imagination**: The illustration of the sunset and the mountains and the projection of heaven from creation is theologically grounded (1 Cor 2:9) and emotionally effective. This is the sermon's emotional peak and it earns it.

## 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The passage is not exposited — it is illustrated**: The sermon reads Titus 2:11–14, calls it a "grace sandwich," then leaves it. The text's own argument — that the same grace that appeared in the first advent is training us in holiness in preparation for the second advent — is never traced through. The sermon uses the passage as a theme anchor, not as a governing text.
2. **No conscience work is done**: There is not a single moment where a specific sinner is cornered, a specific idolatry is named, or a specific category of hearer is addressed with surgical precision. "You might have a heart issue" (paragraph near the end, on maranatha) is the closest the sermon comes to searching — and it retreats immediately into comfort.
3. **No exhortation**: The sermon ends ("What a mighty God we serve. Amen?") having never issued a clear imperative. What must the congregation do this week? Titus 2:14 says Christ purified a people "zealous for good works" — what specific works? The text itself offers the exhortation, but the sermon does not pick it up.

## 11. Blind Spots
The preacher appears unaware that the section on theological liberalism (Bultmann, Neil, Best) functions as intellectual scaffolding for a congregation likely already convinced of biblical inerrancy. It addresses an external threat but does not turn the lens inward — the more pressing question for a gathered evangelical congregation is not "Do you believe in the second coming?" but "Do you live as though you believe in it?" The apologetic section uses valuable sermonic real estate that could have been spent pressing the conscience.

## 12. Illusion of Strength
The Piper quotations feel like theological weight — and Piper is cited six or seven times. But the quotations are doing the sermon's exposition and argumentation work. This creates the impression of theological density while the preacher's own exegetical engagement with the text is thin. The sermon feels more substantive in the reading than it is in the working.

## 13. Missed Opportunity
Titus 2:14 — "who gave himself for us to redeem us from all lawlessness and to purify for himself a people for his own possession who are zealous for good works" — is read aloud and briefly summarized, but its force is never unleashed. "A people for his own possession" is an Old Testament echo (Exodus 19:5; Deuteronomy 7:6; 1 Peter 2:9) with enormous weight: God is reconstituting his covenant people through the cross. The redemptive-historical depth here is the sermon's greatest untouched vein. And "zealous for good works" is the text's own concrete application — but the sermon does not press it into specific obedience.

## 14. Surgical Fixes
1. **Trace the text's own argument in three moves**: Grace appeared (v.11) → grace trains (v.12) → glory is coming (v.13) → Christ gave himself to purify a people zealous for good works (v.14). Let these be the sermon's structure rather than an external hope outline.
2. **Add the hammer before the balm**: Before offering comfort about God's attentiveness and heaven, name the specific failure the text addresses. Why do Cretans (and we) need this word? Probably because worldly passions and ungodliness compete with the blessed hope. Name those idols specifically — comfort, distraction, trivial pleasures, an unexamined life — before offering the grace that defeats them.
3. **Replace or reduce the liberalism section**: The three liberal scholars (Neil, Bultmann, Best) address an external threat. Replace or abbreviate with an internal diagnostic: "What does it look like for a Christian to functionally deny the second coming — not in creed but in calendar, in anxiety, in the things they cannot give up?"
4. **Issue a specific exhortation from v.14**: "This week — where are you not zealous for good works? Name it. Repent of it. And pursue one concrete act of obedience as someone who belongs to God's own possession."
5. **Apply the Perkins grid**: Add one sentence each for the doubter ("If you're not sure Christ will return for you — hear this: he gave himself for sinners, not the righteous"), the backslider ("If you've grown cold — not a little tired, but genuinely cold — the text says grace trains. Submit to that training again"), and the self-righteous ("If you assume the second coming is for your vindication over your enemies — hear that Christ is coming to be glorified, not to validate your politics").

## 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon is not bloodless. The preacher's warmth and genuine delight in Christ are evident. The problem is not coldness but underdevelopment — comfort without conviction, warmth without wound.

## 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The sermon's closing movement — from creation's beauty to the unimaginable glory of heaven — carries genuine doxological weight and leaves the congregation wanting to worship, which partially redeems the sermon's applicatory thinness.

## 17. Rubric Fit Note
This rubric fits doxological-eschatological passages reasonably well, though the Weight of Glory category is where this sermon most naturally excels. The Conscience & Applicatory Force category strains the passage less than it strains this particular sermon — Titus 2:11–14 is rich with applicatory material (renounce ungodliness, live self-controlled, be zealous for good works) that the rubric's categories would reward if the sermon had developed it.
