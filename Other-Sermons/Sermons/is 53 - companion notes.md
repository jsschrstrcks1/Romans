## IS 53:1-3 — Rejection and Humiliation of Christ

Obscurity, poverty, humiliation, misery, and shame. He took on our nature. He set aside the independent exercise of many of the divine prerogatives that belong to him as God — Philippians 2:7: "but emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men" (ESV). It was like trying to store the Gulf of Mexico in a teeny tiny vessel that is trying to hold the entirety of God. God by his nature can't fit into Tupperware. But he can limit himself so that he fits. There are whole libraries of books out there that talk about the communicable and incommunicable attributes of God — those attributes we can obtain, and those that we can't. Jesus voluntarily set aside the independent exercise of certain divine attributes — which he could and would use, and which he wouldn't.

Well let me draw a picture for you. Imagine a production room on the set of some big TV series. They have dozens and dozens of TV screens, scores of people with laptops linked to said screens, all of their outputs linked to a DVD player recording what the final picture would be like. I see Christ's attributes — kind of — like this. All of the play buttons are still there. He could have pushed his big green omnipresence button — but to what end? He set aside the independent use of parts of himself for our sake. Like giving up an arm, or an eye, or any of your senses.

That's just the start. Where was Christ born? Lived obediently to his parents. He was a lowly carpenter. He didn't have a home of his own, and lived in poverty. That's not all either — we could do a whole service just on the humiliation of Christ. Ultimately this culminated with the events of John 19 where Christ is scourged, and mocked, and crucified.

Think about that — the God who uses the earth as a footstool (Isaiah 66:1: "Thus says the LORD: 'Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool'" ESV), the one who spoke life and everything into existence, the one who took five days to create something so complex and wonderful that we've spent the next 5,000 or so years trying to fully understand and yet have not fully been able to grasp everything. Einstein spent much of his life looking for a unified theory of everything, and failed. There are still a number of things science can't explain! But God can! Praise God for it. He set a lot of himself aside, so he could be born in a lowly manger, live the life of a pauper, so he would be beaten, mocked, scourged, crucified and killed. All the while Jesus traveled around healing folks, he worked selflessly to the end preaching the truth, pouring his life into those around him.

It takes a lot of integrity, love and commitment to our Lord to stand there and listen to unjust and false accusations. It took lots of patience and love to stand there while they mocked him, more so I think when they scourged him, and nailed him to the cross.

Think about this too: Remember the words of Stuart Townend's hymn "How Deep the Father's Love for Us" (1995)? "It was my sin that held him there, until it was accomplished. His dying breath has brought me life. I know that it is finished." In a very real way our actions were the reason Christ had to die. How would you feel if someone murdered your son basically in cold blood after lying to him, and about him?

## IS 53:4-6 — Redemption of Christ

One of my favorite commentators stops for a moment and asks a question that I think is important. He asks "why? Why should an innocent man such as Jesus die such a terrible death?" The answer of course is that he was taking the place of sinners and bearing their final judgment.

1 Peter 2:24: "He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, that we might die to sin and live to righteousness. By his wounds you have been healed." (ESV)

2 Corinthians 5:21: "For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God." (ESV)

1. He was wounded, pierced.
2. Bruised or crushed as under a heavy burden — the weight of sin that was laid upon him.
3. He was chastised or punished as though he had broken the law, and in this case that meant the scourging and crucifixion.

All of these pale though in comparison to the spiritual suffering on the cross. When he took on the depths of our depravity, all of our rebelliousness, all of our hate, crookedness, iniquities, our griefs, and sorrows. We humans spend our life committing sin, and earning a wage, and Christ took that wage for each and every one of us who believed across time. He drank down the cup of God's wrath, and from the cross said *tetelestai* — "It is finished" (John 19:30 ESV: "When Jesus had received the sour wine, he said, 'It is finished,' and he bowed his head and gave up his spirit.").

Two weeks ago when he said that word he meant his teaching of the disciples, his earthly physical ministry was done. This week when he says it, it means something a lot bigger. Our God is big folks. The heavens are larger than we can fully comprehend, and yet the earth is his footstool — Isaiah 66:1 tells us "Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool" (ESV). That's one of those things that is easy to say but much more difficult to fully understand. The observable universe stretches some 93 billion light-years across. Man to this point has never come close to traveling anywhere near the speed of light. So if that's the footstool — how big is our God? If that is his footstool — what is his wrath like?

In that moment in John 19 when Christ says "It is finished" it means the cumulative total of all of his work including the satisfaction of the great and just wrath of our mighty God. Wrath he did not deserve, but wrath he chose to satisfy anyway. This is the very heart of the Gospel, that Christ chose to die for all our sins.

### Scripture Corrections Applied
1. **Isaiah 66:1 — "heavens are his footstool"**: Original said "the God that uses the heavens as a footstool." This is reversed. Isaiah 66:1 ESV: "Heaven is my throne, and the **earth** is my footstool." Heaven is the throne; earth is the footstool. Corrected throughout.
2. **Philippians 2:7 — kenosis phrasing**: Original said Christ "emptied himself of many of the attributes that make him God." This borders on heterodox kenotic theology (suggesting Christ divested divine attributes). Orthodox Chalcedonian Christology teaches Christ voluntarily set aside the *independent exercise* of certain divine prerogatives — he never ceased to possess them. Philippians 2:7 ESV says he "emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant" — the emptying is *by addition* (taking servant form), not by subtraction of deity. Corrected to "set aside the independent exercise of" throughout.
3. **John 19:30**: Verified. ESV: "When Jesus had received the sour wine, he said, 'It is finished,' and he bowed his head and gave up his spirit." *Tetelestai* (τετέλεσται) confirmed as the Greek word — perfect passive indicative of *teleō*, meaning "it has been completed/finished."
4. **1 Peter 2:24**: Verified. Full ESV quotation added.
5. **2 Corinthians 5:21**: Verified. Full ESV quotation added.

### Factual Corrections Applied
6. **Pulsar claim**: Original said "a pulsar can get up to 58 billion light years across" and used this as the scale of God's footstool. This is wrong by a factor of ~10²¹. A pulsar is a rotating neutron star, typically ~20 km in diameter. The preacher appears to have been reaching for a cosmically large scale. Replaced with "the observable universe stretches some 93 billion light-years across" — which is the actual scale of the observable universe and serves the sermon's rhetorical point about God's immensity.
7. **"Old psalm" / "old hymn" attribution**: Original called the lyrics "the words of the old psalm" (first reference) and "the old hymn" (second reference). These lyrics — "It was my sin that held him there / until it was accomplished / His dying breath has brought me life / I know that it is finished" — are from **"How Deep the Father's Love for Us" by Stuart Townend (1995)**, not a psalm and not ancient. This is the same hymn already referenced in the companion file `is 53.md`. Corrected attribution.
8. **Einstein unified field theory**: Verified — Einstein spent the last ~30 years of his life (1925-1955) pursuing a unified field theory and did not succeed. Left as-is.
