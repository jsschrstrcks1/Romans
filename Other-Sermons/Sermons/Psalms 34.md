## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluated**: 2026-03-21
**Passage**: Psalm 34:11–22 (with focus on vv. 11–14, 19–20, 22)
**Working Title**: Psalm 34 — The Secret to Long Life

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Christ-Pointing Acrostic Exposition That Earns Its Typology and Almost Earns Its Gospel"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A structurally creative and christologically rewarding sermon on Psalm 34 that correctly identifies the acrostic's structural emphasis, preaches verse 20's typological fulfillment in Christ with genuine textual grounding, and builds toward a clear gospel appeal — but whose gospel remains a placeholder and whose applicatory force is distributed unevenly.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Pass.**

The main point — "The secret to long life is fearing the Lord, and the ultimate fulfillment of this psalm's promise is found in Christ, in whom no bone was broken" — arises from the psalm's own argument. The preacher rightly identifies the acrostic's displaced letter as a structural signal of emphasis on the final verse, and the textual grounding of the typological reading (Psalm 34:20 → John 19:36 → Exodus 12 → Isaiah) is responsible exegesis rather than arbitrary Christology.

**Scopus Test**: "The fear of the LORD is the secret to long life, and the protection promised to the righteous finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ whose bones were not broken." — Arises from the text. Passes.
**Authority Test**: The preacher does not soften or edit the text's demands. Passes.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** "Gospel." is a placeholder. "Have you taken refuge in Jesus?" is a genuine evangelistic question but not an exhortation — it asks rather than summons. The sermon builds well toward the gospel but does not complete the call. The congregation is pointed toward the door; they are not ushered through it.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 18/25**
- Textual Fidelity (10): 8/10. The sermon engages its stated section (vv. 11–22) with genuine textual attention. The acrostic structure and its displaced letter are explained clearly and concretely. Verses 11–14 (the invitation to learn the fear of the LORD) are developed through the tongue-from-evil application and the John 14:15 cross-reference. Verse 19 ("many are the afflictions of the righteous") is quoted. Verse 20 (no bones broken) receives the most exegetical attention and is the sermon's strongest move. Verse 22 ("none who take refuge in him will be condemned") is the climax. The gap: verses 15–18 (the LORD's eyes and ears; the LORD is near to the brokenhearted) receive no treatment, which is a significant pastoral omission given the congregation's stated experience of pain and loss.
- Contextual Awareness (5): 4/5. The acrostic discussion is the best contextual analysis in this batch. The displaced-letter structural observation is legitimate and well-explained. The English acrostic analogy (MERCY) and the misspelled-names illustration are memorable. The historical background (David feigning madness before Abimelech — the occasion for Psalm 34) is noted.
- Exegetical Clarity (5): 3/5. No word studies on the key terms (יָרֵא, yare — "fear"; טוֹב, tov — "good"; נֶפֶשׁ, nephesh — "soul"). The no-broken-bones connection to John 19:36 is the most exegetically transparent move in the sermon. The typological chain (Psalm 34:20 → Exodus 12 → John 19 → Isaiah) is responsibly handled.
- Logical Faithfulness (5): 3/5. The sermon begins at v. 11 and ends at v. 22, but vv. 1–10 (David's personal testimony of deliverance, the angel of the LORD, "taste and see") are absent. **Fallen Condition Focus**: Named and appropriate: human fixation on self-preservation and longevity (the singularity, space colonization, healthcare spending) contrasted with David's revealed path to life through fear of the LORD. 1. **The acrostic displaced-letter observation** — This is the best structural exegetical move in the batch. Identifying the displaced letter as a device to draw attention to the last verse, and then demonstrating its theological significance ("this is what's most important here"), gives the congregation a hermeneutical skill they can use with the Psalter going forward. ### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Psalm 34:15–18 is entirely absent.** "The eyes of the LORD are toward the righteous and his ears toward their cry. The face of the LORD is against those who do evil... The LORD is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit." This is the psalm's most pastorally direct section and it receives no treatment. Given the sermon's opening description of a congregation experiencing pain, accidents, broken families, and grief — these verses are the direct answer to the congregation's named condition, and they are skipped.
2. **The gospel is a placeholder.** The typological development of the bones is brilliant, but it stops short of proclaiming why the unbroken bones matter soteriologically. The Passover Lamb's bones were not broken so that the blood would cover Israel's door and the angel of death would pass over. Christ's bones were not broken — He was the Lamb — so that God's judgment passes over those covered by His blood. This must be stated explicitly, not gestured at.
3. **"Taste and see that the LORD is good" (v. 8) is absent.** Psalm 34:8 is one of the most famous lines in the Psalter — an invitation to experiential encounter with God. It is the psalm's warmest and most direct pastoral invitation. A sermon that begins at v. 11 misses the psalm's most inviting line, and with it the natural on-ramp for the gospel offer at the close.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher is clearly excited by the structural and typological discoveries — and rightly so, since they are genuinely excellent. But this enthusiasm may be slightly obscuring the fact that the psalm's most pastorally immediate material (the brokenhearted of v. 18, the cry that the LORD hears of v. 17, the "taste and see" of v. 8) is being skipped in favor of the structural and Christological material. The congregation needs both. The typology answers the theological question; the brokenhearted verses answer the pastoral one.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The verse 20 development is so strong that it creates the impression of a complete sermon. The preacher arrives at a genuinely compelling Christological conclusion — "this verse was written 900 years before Christ was born" — and the congregation may feel the sermon is finished and satisfying. But the call has not been completed, the gospel has not been proclaimed, and the brokenhearted in the room have not been specifically addressed. The typology illuminates; it does not by itself comfort, call, or send.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Psalm 34:18 — "The LORD is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit." — Given the sermon's opening description of a congregation experiencing specific, named griefs (car crash, hurricane families, broken relationships, kids at risk), this verse is the most direct answer the text offers to the congregation's actual condition. A preacher who names the griefs in his opening paragraph and then does not return to the verse that speaks most directly to them has left the most needy members of his congregation without the specific word they came to hear. This is the sermon's most significant missed pastoral opportunity.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Start at v. 8 or include it.** "Taste and see that the LORD is good; blessed is the man who takes refuge in him!" — This is the invitation that the whole sermon builds toward. Use it to introduce the text, and echo it in the gospel close.
2. **Preach vv. 15–18.** Give at least two minutes to the LORD's eyes, ears, and nearness to the brokenhearted: "I described some of what's been happening in our community this week. The LORD knows. His eyes are on you. His ears are toward your cry. He is near to the brokenhearted. Are you brokenhearted this morning? He is near to you right now."
3. **Complete the gospel proclamation.** Replace "Gospel." with: "The Passover Lamb's bones were not broken. Christ's bones were not broken. He was the Lamb, slain so that God's judgment passes over those who are covered by His blood. That is why verse 22 says 'none of those who take refuge in Him will be condemned.' Refuge in Jesus means His blood covers you. That is the gospel. You don't manage your way to long life — you receive it, by taking refuge in the one whose bones were not broken for you."
4. **Add a concrete closing call.** "Three things: (1) Fear the LORD — which means trust Him, obey Him, look to His face when the steak is in front of you. (2) Take refuge in Him — not in politicians, not in healthcare, not in the singularity. (3) If you've never taken refuge in Christ — today is your day. He is near to the brokenhearted. Taste and see."
5. **Compress the singularity / healthcare / Elon Musk introduction by 40%.** The contemporary-longevity-fixation material is well-chosen, but it is proportionally too long relative to the psalm's own content. Two minutes of that material would do the work that five minutes currently does.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 34 is a wisdom psalm with an acrostic structure and an explicit teaching intent (v. 11 — "Come, O children, listen to me; I will teach you the fear of the LORD"). The rubric fits well — the Exposition and Theology categories are especially apt for a psalm with explicit doctrinal content. The rubric's Redemptive-Historical Awareness subcategory is particularly rewarding for this sermon, which achieves the best typological development in the batch. The pastoral Conscience & Applicatory Force categories appropriately press on the sermon's gap: a teaching psalm should not merely teach — it should press the congregation to live what it teaches.
