We are gathered here today to celebrate the memory of Jean. She was one of my grandmothers dearest friends, and like a second mother to my father. Shes been a part of my life for nearly my whole life, and like all of you, I loved her dearly.

Did you know, as long as she was living on her own, she had never forgotten about my birthday? It was near hers, and getting her card reminded me to call her and wish her a happy birthday also.

She was my co-worker for a time. As my grandmother aged, and needed more help, she was part of the team that took care of her and my grandfather in their twilight years.

After my grandmother passed, we were talking about my grandmothers funeral arrangements, and she mentioned that one of her favorite verses was the same as my grandmothers.

Proverbs 3:5-6 – Trust in the Lord with all your heart and lean not on your own understanding. In all your ways acknowledge him and he will make straight your path.

That's hard to do, when you're grieving the loss of a loved one.

Some people think they shouldn't cry, or grieve at all. But honestly, EVEN Jesus Grieved. In fact I want to spend a moment talking about that. If you've brought your bibles, and I hope you have, please open with me to John 11:30-44

 While you're turning there I want to point out our savior Jesus knows every aspect of what its like to be human. He ate, and slept, and was tired. He even grieved like us.

I read a story about an author who had spent her career writing a series of stories about this one man in particular, and she came to love that man, and wanted to see him properly taken care of. So she wrote herself into the story, so that the man she loved could be properly taken care of.

Its true though, the only way that MacBeth could ever know Shakespear is for Shakespear to write himself into the story. It true of all created things. Unless the creator writes himself in, how can the creation know their creator.

That's exactly what God did for us. He sent a part of himself – Jesus – to live among us, to teach us about him. To show us the way forward. To show us the way to heaven.

Alright, lets read.

Seems like a lot of people feel as though God is a distant, unknowable, unknowing being. Yet the bible tells us Jesus was both fully God, and fully man. I heard someone say if God knew what life is like here, things would be different.

And yet, our God wrote himself into the story, sent his son to live and model for us a perfect life, and then when we killed him, used the death of his son to pay the price for our sins.

It's a gift beyond measure. But you can clearly see his humanity on display here. Mary was quite upset. Here Jesus had been going around healng people all over the place. His God given ability to heal had been on display all over the ancient world. She KNEW on a deep level that Jesus absolutely could have kept Lazarus from death. I think that's why shes a little heated in vs 32. What we didn't read was that Jesus had gotten the message a while back and he waited several days before heading to see Lazarus. Mary is saying – accusing – really of allowing her brother whom she loved dearly to die.

I think that's a position we can relate to. No one likes it when some one dies. Even Jesus weeps – vs 35. I've heard preachers say it must have just been a little tear drop running down the face of Jesus. But I think it is something more than that. He grieved in the way that was appropriate for the time. Maybe he went down on his knees and tore his clothes. Maybe he wept till his nose ran. All we know is the jews around him said based on how Jesus grieved that "wow, he must have really loved Lazarus" vs 36.

I'm not going to dogmatically say this was a full on nose running ugly cry, but it might have been. Jesus felt the loss of Lazarus deeply. Even KNOWING that it wouldn't take long for Lazarus to be restored to life, still he wept. Jesus knows us in our grieving. He knows what it feels like for us to lose someone we love. Even when we have the firm hope, the firm knowledge that we who are believers in Jesus Christ like Jean was will one day spend eternity in heaven with both Jesus, and our lost loved ones like Jean.

I think sometimes especially as men we need permission to grieve. If the one who spoke the universe into existence needed to cry, than I think we can too.

This passage also shows Jesus divinity. Who else but God has command over life and death. We see this power in vs 41 and following. ## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date:** 2026-03-21
**Passage:** John 11:30–44 (with Proverbs 3:5–6)
**Occasion:** Memorial / Funeral sermon

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**A Tender Pastoral Word That Stops Inches Short of the Gospel**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A warm and pastorally sensitive funeral homily that opens the humanity and divinity of Christ from John 11 with genuine emotional intelligence, but ends mid-air — the gospel section is absent, the manuscript literally trailing off at "Gospel."

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**PASS (conditional).** The sermon's main point — that Jesus grieves with us and has power over death — arises legitimately from John 11. The Scopus Test is satisfied: *Jesus, who wept at Lazarus' tomb and raised him from the dead, knows our grief and holds authority over death.* The Authority Test is satisfied; the text is not softened. The conditional flag is that the sermon is visibly incomplete — the gospel section is noted but never written.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG 1 TRIGGERED — No clear gospel presentation.** The manuscript ends with the notations "That starts with Sin." and "Gospel." These are placeholders, not proclamation. Christ's finished work is announced as a topic but never articulated. A congregation hearing this sermon would not know how to be saved.

**FLAG 5 TRIGGERED — No actual exhortation.** The manuscript ends before any call to belief, repentance, or response is made. There is no "therefore" moment, no invitation, no summoning of the hearers to any action.

**Score capped at 69 due to fatal flaw flags.**

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 15/25**
- Textual Fidelity (6/10): The sermon derives its point legitimately from John 11 — Jesus' weeping and the resurrection of Lazarus are handled with fidelity. The preacher does not impose alien ideas. However, the sermon treats the passage selectively: vv. 40 ("Did I not tell you that if you believed you would see the glory of God?") and the explicit purpose statement in v. 42 ("that they may believe that you sent me") receive no treatment. The theological center of the sign — that it points to Jesus as the resurrection and the life (v. 25, deliberately omitted) — is absent.
- Contextual Awareness (2/5): No attention to book-level purpose (John 20:31), no placement in the seven signs structure, no mention of the larger resurrection theme.
- Exegetical Clarity (4/5): The handling of "Jesus wept" is fresh and honest — refusing to minimize it and allowing for genuine grief. The incarnation illustration (Shakespeare writing himself into MacBeth) is hermeneutically sound.
- Logical Faithfulness (3/5): The sermon does not eisegete, but it does skip the most important theological freight in the passage.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18**
- Christ-Centeredness (5/7): Christ's humanity and divinity are both affirmed. The incarnation is handled with warmth. However, the atonement — mentioned only as "pay the price for our sins" in one sentence — is not developed.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness (1/3): None. The resurrection of Lazarus as a sign pointing forward to Christ's own resurrection, and the broader resurrection hope of John 11, is not developed.
- Doctrinal Precision (3/4): What is stated is sound. The problem is what is not stated.
- Confessional Alignment (2/4): No contradiction, but the doctrinal content is thin.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 4/15**
The gospel is gestured at ("paid the price for our sins") but never proclaimed. The Synagogue Test is not technically passed — a Muslim or devout Jew would not be deeply offended by this sermon as preached, because the atonement is not explained with enough specificity to give offense. The cross-centered gravity is missing. The manuscript ends before the gospel section.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15**
- Heart Penetration (3/5): There is genuine pastoral warmth. The "permission to grieve" section is well-targeted for a funeral congregation, particularly for men who feel pressure to suppress grief. This is real pastoral care.
- Specificity (3/5): The congregation is specific — mourners at Jean's funeral. The application to grief is appropriately focused.
- Spiritual Differentiation (1/5): The sermon speaks primarily to grieving believers. The unbeliever is acknowledged ("how to get to where she's at") but never addressed. The backslider, the doubter, and the hardened are not differentiated.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 0/8**
Absent. The sermon ends with the word "Gospel." as a heading for content that was never written. No exhortation, no invitation, no call.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**
- Clarity of Outline (3/4): The structure is traceable: personal remembrance → permission to grieve (John 11) → incarnation → divinity → gesture toward gospel. It is simple and followable.
- Progression (2/3): The sermon builds until it stops.
- Unity (1/2): The Proverbs 3:5–6 opening is not integrated with the John 11 text. It introduces a second theme (trust in grief) that is never developed alongside the main passage.

The Shakespeare/MacBeth illustration is excellent — theologically apt, imaginatively vivid, accessible to ordinary hearers, and native to the sermon's main concern. The "ugly cry" discussion of Jesus weeping is disarming in a funeral context and well-deployed.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- Conviction on the Page (1/2): The emotion is present, but the theological density is low.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ or myself?** Christ — the Christology section is the strongest part.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas. The warmth is pastoral, but the encounter is not catalyzed.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Comforted, partially — but not through the gospel. The comfort offered is emotional solidarity (Jesus grieves with you), which is true but insufficient standing alone without resurrection hope grounded in the atonement.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Permission to grieve — genuine pastoral care.** "I think sometimes especially as men we need permission to grieve. If the one who spoke the universe into existence needed to cry, than I think we can too." This is precisely the right word for a funeral congregation, and it arises organically from the text.
2. **The incarnation illustration.** The Shakespeare/MacBeth analogy is theologically serious, imaginatively effective, and memorable. It earns its place.
3. **Honest about Mary's accusation.** The reading of v. 32 as "a little heated" — Mary accusing Jesus of letting Lazarus die — is honest exegesis that most preachers soften. This willingness to sit with the text's emotional complexity is a strength.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The manuscript is incomplete.** The gospel section — the thing the sermon explicitly promises ("One thing I know beyond a shadow of a doubt that Jean would want from me today is to do my best to tell you guys how to get to where she's at") — is never delivered. This is not a minor gap; it is the point the sermon itself identifies as its purpose.
2. **John 11:25 is absent.** "I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live." This is the theological center of the entire chapter and the most pastorally critical verse for a funeral. Its omission is the most significant exegetical failure in the sermon.
3. **The atonement is asserted but not proclaimed.** "Used the death of his son to pay the price for our sins" appears in a single clause and receives no development. At a funeral — where the congregation will include unbelievers who need to hear the gospel — this is insufficient.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears to assume that the congregation will receive the "you can get to where Jean is" invitation warmly and follow it with minimal articulation. In a funeral context, the preacher may feel that explicit gospel proclamation is intrusive or uncomfortable — but this text (John 11) and this occasion (death of a believer) are precisely the moment where the gospel must be most fully stated, not most gently gestured at.

The sermon also misses that the Lazarus narrative is not primarily about grief — it is primarily about the resurrection. The Fallen Condition Focus (why does this text exist?) is answered in v. 25: human beings die, and apart from Christ have no hope of resurrection. The sermon's FCF is misidentified as "people feel they shouldn't grieve" rather than "people die and need resurrection."

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Shakespeare/MacBeth illustration creates a feeling that the incarnation is being explained with unusual depth. But in context, it is doing only the work of saying "God became man." The illustration is excellent as an illustration, but it is not doing exegetical work — it is decorating a theological assertion that is never connected to the specific claims of John 11 (the "I am the resurrection" declaration, the tears that demonstrate genuine humanity, the raising that demonstrates genuine divinity).

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

John 11:40 — "Did I not tell you that if you believed you would see the glory of God?" — is the hinge of the entire passage. It connects belief to resurrection, and resurrection to the glory of God. For a funeral congregation, this is the word they most need: grief is real, and God does not dismiss it — but He calls the grieving to believe and thereby see His glory, even in death. This verse received no treatment whatsoever.

The "I am the resurrection and the life" declaration (v. 25), omitted entirely, is the single most pastorally powerful statement in the entire passage for a funeral context.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Complete the gospel section.** The manuscript explicitly promises it. State clearly: what sin is, what it deserves, what Christ did on the cross (penal substitution), and what must be done to receive it (repentance and faith). In a funeral context, this is not optional — it is mercy.
2. **Preach John 11:25.** "I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live, and everyone who lives and believes in me shall never die." This verse is the key to the whole passage and the whole occasion. It should be the sermon's climax.
3. **Integrate Proverbs 3:5–6.** Either connect it to the John 11 passage (trusting God's understanding when He delays, as Jesus delayed before coming to Lazarus) or remove it. As it stands, it floats unconnected.
4. **Add an explicit exhortation.** "Jean trusted Jesus, and she is with Him now. The question for each of you today is: have you?" — or something in that register. The congregation must be summoned, not just comforted.
5. **Address the unbeliever directly.** Some in the room will not know Christ. They must hear that death is real, sin is real, and the resurrection of Christ is the only answer — not as background theology, but as an urgent appeal.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon's weakness is incompleteness, not bloodless correctness. What is present is warm and pastoral. The problem is what is missing.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The warmth is real and the pastoral instinct is sound, but the sermon ends before the eternal weight is deposited — the manuscript itself acknowledges it still needs to say the thing that matters most.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This is a funeral homily, not an expository sermon in the traditional sense — the genre genuinely strains some rubric categories (structural outline, cognitive load, sermonic force). The rubric bends appropriately here: the occasion legitimately calls for pastoral warmth, personal remembrance, and accessibility over formal structure. The fatal flaws triggered, however, are not genre-dependent — an incomplete gospel and absent exhortation fail the funeral homily by its own standards, not only the rubric's.
