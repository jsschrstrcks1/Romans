STRUCTURED SERMON MANUSCRIPT — WEEK 1
Romans 12:1–2 — A Life on the Altar
(Structure C: 5 major movements; 45–60 minutes, single-spaced)
INTRODUCTION — THE MOUNTAIN THAT FRAMES THE CHAPTER
If you’ve brought your Bible—and I hope you have—please open with me to Romans 12:1–2. After eleven chapters of the deepest doctrine in Scripture, Paul now pivots. You can feel the hinge turn. Everything God has done now becomes everything we are called to do.
Before we read, let’s take our usual moment for the People Group of the Week. We do this because the grace that saves us is the grace that sends us. Today’s group is the ____________, with fewer than _____ percent believing. Many have never heard the gospel once. Would two of you pray for them?
(Prayer.)
Let’s read the text:
“I appeal to you therefore, brothers, by the mercies of God, to present your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and acceptable to God, which is your spiritual worship. Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind…”
This is the Word of the Lord.
We’ve read the Book—now let’s talk to the Author.
(Father… Amen.)
Before we walk into the text, I want to take you up a mountain in Genesis: Abraham and Isaac climbing Mount Moriah. Isaac carrying the wood, Abraham carrying the knife. Isaac trusting his father. Isaac laying down willingly. That picture — a living, breathing, conscious son offering himself to the will of his father — is the living illustration behind Romans 12:1–2.
That mountain becomes the spine of this entire passage.

POINT 1 — THE MOUNTAIN OF MERCY (ROMANS 1–11)
Paul begins with a thunderclap: “Therefore.”
This is not decorative — it is doctrinal.
You cannot obey Romans 12 until you’ve understood Romans 1–11.
Think of everything Paul has already shown us:
Romans 1–3 — mankind guilty, deserving wrath.
Romans 4 — Abraham justified by faith alone.
Romans 5 — peace with God through the blood of Christ.
Romans 6 — dead to sin, alive to God.
Romans 7 — the agony of remaining sin.
Romans 8 — no condemnation; adoption; the Spirit’s power.
Romans 9–11 — sovereign mercy choosing and saving a people.
After eleven chapters of mercy, Paul says:
“Therefore… by the mercies of God…”
Mercy is the fuel of obedience.
Mercy is what gets you up the mountain.
Just like Isaac climbed Moriah because he trusted his father, we climb the mountain of obedience because we trust ours.

POINT 2 — THE CALL TO THE ALTAR (PRESENT YOUR BODIES)
“Present your bodies.”
Not your intentions.
Not your spiritual feelings.
Not your Sunday version of yourself.
Your bodies — the whole embodied life:
Your habits.
Your eyes.
Your tongue.
Your schedule.
Your private world.
Your online world.
Your money.
Your speech.
Your reactions.
Everything.
And this is where Moriah comes back into view.
Isaac was not dragged up the mountain — he climbed it.
He carried the wood — the very wood he would lie upon.
He surrendered — willingly, consciously, trustingly.
He was a living sacrifice.
But Isaac did not die.
Why?
Because God provided a substitute — the ram in the thicket.
And here is the difference between Isaac and us:
Isaac walked off the mountain because God provided a substitute.
You walk onto the mountain because God has already provided yours — Christ.
So when Paul says your sacrifice is “holy and acceptable,” he means this:
You are accepted because Christ is accepted.
As Colossians 3 says — your life is hidden with Christ in God.
Covered in His righteousness.
Wrapped in His perfection.

POINT 3 — THE NATURE OF WORSHIP (A LIFE SURRENDERED)
“And this is your spiritual worship.”
Not emotional worship.
Not temporary worship.
Not a feeling — pizza can give you feelings.
Not a one-hour-a-week religious moment.
Not singing songs and reading Scripture and then living however we please.
Worship in Romans 12 is a posture of life — a lifestyle of surrender to our King.
A life bowed before God, not just a heart stirred for a moment.
Worship looks like submission in all seasons:
Submission when life is stable.
Submission when life is uncertain.
Submission when plans work.
Submission when plans fall apart.
Submission in strength.
Submission in weakness.
Submission in the quiet, mundane, unglamorous grind of everyday faithfulness.
Worship is not a mood.
Worship is not a moment.
Worship is surrender.

POINT 4 — THE WAR FOR YOUR MIND (CONFORMED VS. TRANSFORMED)
Paul continues:
“Do not be conformed to this world.”
The Greek word pictures being pressed into a mold.
And here’s the frightening truth:
Conformity requires zero effort.
It happens automatically if you do nothing.
If you turn off your mind, the world will disciple you.
Entertainment disciples you.
Politics disciples you.
Your friends disciple you.
Your desires disciple you.
Your phone disciples you.
Your fears disciple you.
So when someone says, “Well, everyone around me cusses, so I do too”—
that’s not an excuse.
That’s a confession.
You’ve allowed the world to disciple your mouth.
And not just your mouth:
Tell little white lies?
Conformity.
Avoid evangelism because your circle has shrunk to only Christians?
Conformity.

It feels safe, but it’s disobedience.
A silent Christian is a conformed Christian.
A comfortable Christian is often a conformed Christian.
Want to see worldly conformity in the wild?
Just look at this week’s Epstein headlines.

The documents show what Scripture already taught us:
People will excuse anything if it protects their access, influence, or power.
Deals in shadows.
Compromises made for advantage.
Sin defended because “our side” benefits.

And the church is not immune.
Many today trust in political maneuvering more than holiness,
in winning more than witnessing,
in strategy more than surrender.

But Psalm 20:7 cuts through the fog:
Some trust in chariots and some in horses,
but we trust in the name of the LORD our God.
John Flavel said,
“To preach the truth without offending carnal men is to attempt what Christ Himself could not do.”
Christ never bartered holiness for access.
He never traded truth for influence.
He needs no running mate and no political machinery.
When Paul says, “Do not be conformed,”
he is calling you to reject every idol,
every compromise,
every worldly strategy masquerading as wisdom.
Then comes the positive command:
“But be transformed by the renewal of your mind.”
Conformity is external pressure.
Transformation is internal power.
This is the Spirit’s work — metamorphosis.
And think of Moriah again:
Transformation happens on the mountain,
not before the climb.
Abraham walked down different than he walked up.
Isaac walked down changed forever.
The altar shapes the worshiper.
Obedience renews the mind.
Surrender reshapes the soul.

You don’t renew your mind so you can surrender.
You surrender so God can renew your mind.

POINT 5 — THE CLARITY THAT COMES AFTER SURRENDER (DISCERNING GOD’S WILL)
“…that by testing you may discern what is the will of God.”
Notice the order:
Surrender → Renewal → Discernment
Not:
Discernment → Then surrender if we like it.
Abraham did not see the ram at the bottom of the mountain.
He saw it with the knife raised in obedience.

Most Christians never discern God’s will
because they never go far enough up the mountain to see it.

They want revelation without obedience.
Clarity without surrender.
Guidance without sacrifice.
But Romans 12 tells us:
Obedience precedes understanding.
Worship precedes wisdom.
Surrender precedes discernment.
And when God renews your mind,
His will becomes “good and acceptable and perfect.”
Sin becomes bitter.
Obedience becomes sweet.
As John Owen wrote:
“He who has slight thoughts of sin never had great thoughts of God.”
CONCLUSION — THE TRUE SON ON THE TRUE MOUNTAIN
Back to Moriah.
Isaac carried the wood.
Isaac surrendered.
Isaac lay down.
But Isaac did not die.

Because God provided a substitute.

But centuries later, on a mountain not far from Moriah,
another Son carried the wood.
Another Son climbed the hill.
Another Son lay down on the altar.

But this time —
there was no voice from heaven saying, “Stop.”
There was no ram in the thicket.
No substitute.

Because He was the substitute.

Christ became the dying sacrifice
so that you could become the living one.

Christ stayed on His altar
so you could climb onto yours.

Christ surrendered His life
so you could surrender yours without fear.

So how do we respond?

To the struggling saint:
God is not asking for flawless climbing.
Just faithful climbing.
Your sacrifice is accepted because Christ’s was perfect.

To the self-assured sinner:
If there is no surrender,
no sacrifice,
no transformation,
no renewal—
then the mercy you claim may not be the mercy you possess.

To the seeking skeptic:
You don’t clean yourself up before coming.
You come as you are,
lay yourself down,
and God does the renewing.

Romans 12 is the call to climb the mountain.
To carry the wood.
To surrender your life.
Not to die for your sins—Christ has done that.
But to live for His glory.

A living sacrifice.
A renewed mind.
A life on the altar.

Because the King who carried His cross up His mountain
now calls you to carry your life up yours.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Structurally Confident Exposition with Christological Power and an Underdeployed Conscience**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A faithful and structurally compelling opening of Romans 12:1–2 that establishes the Moriah typology with genuine expository force and gospel clarity, while leaving the conscience less searched than the text demands and the application more principle-level than surgical.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — solid.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — "present your bodies as a living sacrifice; obedience flows from mercy and precedes discernment" — arises directly from Romans 12:1–2's own argument. The "therefore" as doctrinal hinge, the three-part structure of Surrender → Renewal → Discernment: all are native to the text's own logic.

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text's claims. He does not soften the imperatives or flatten the indicatives. The hard edge — "if there is no surrender... the mercy you claim may not be the mercy you possess" — is submitted to, not edited.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No fatal flaw flags triggered.** The gospel is clearly articulated, Christ is essential to the argument, application is differentiating, and exhortation is present. The sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers.

**Score cap is not in effect.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 20/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (8/10):* The sermon derives its argument from the text faithfully. The "therefore" as doctrinal load-bearing connector, the word study on *suschematizo* (conformed/pressed into a mold), and the Surrender → Renewal → Discernment sequence all arise from the passage's own grammar. The text is never left behind for long.
- *Contextual Awareness (4/5):* The placement of Romans 12 as the hinge after Romans 1–11 is named, explained, and grounded with a brief but accurate survey of the doctrinal argument preceding it. "Mercy is the fuel of obedience" is not decoration — it is an accurate summary of the "therefore." The covenantal-historical context of Moriah adds depth.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5):* The *suschematizo* word picture ("pressed into a mold") and the *metamorphoo* contrast are clearly explained and congregation-accessible. The Greek functions as an unlock, not as credential display.
- *Logical Faithfulness (4/5):* No eisegesis. The Moriah typology is not imported arbitrarily — it is introduced as the living illustration of the concept Paul is naming, and it is consistently sustained and developed rather than dropped.
- *FCF:* The fallen condition is clearly named: self-protective resistance to total surrender, the tendency to seek discernment without obedience, and conformity to the world's pattern through passivity. This is specific and textually grounded. The FCF bridges exposition and application effectively.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 15/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness (6/7):* The Moriah typology builds to an explicitly Christological conclusion: "He was the substitute." The movement from Isaac's preserved life to Christ's unreserved death is organic and earned, not bolted on. The cross is not decoration — it is the structural keystone. Minor deduction: the Christology is concentrated most heavily in the conclusion; it could surface more throughout.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3/3):* The Abraham/Isaac → Christ typological movement is handled with covenantal awareness. The placement of the Moriah narrative within the larger story of redemption — "not far from Moriah, another Son carried the wood" — reflects organic redemptive-historical reasoning.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4/4):* No doctrinal errors. The acceptance of the sacrifice grounded in Christ's acceptance ("You are accepted because Christ is accepted") is a clean articulation of imputed righteousness. The Colossians 3 reference ("your life is hidden with Christ in God") reinforces this.
- *Confessional Alignment (2/4):* No contradictions. The sermon does not explicitly engage Doctrines of Grace, but neither does it contradict them. The "if there is no surrender... the mercy you claim may not be the mercy you possess" line is carefully calibrated — it is not decisionist but is also not fully articulating regeneration as the ground of surrender.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 12/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel (4/5):* The gospel is present and clearly articulated in the conclusion: Christ is the substitute, His sacrifice enables ours. "Christ became the dying sacrifice so that you could become the living one" is clear and correct.
- *Necessity of Christ (5/5):* Christ is not peripheral or merely illustrative — He is structurally necessary to the sermon's entire argument. Without the Moriah typology's Christological resolution, the sermon has no doctrinal foundation. Christ is essential, not decorative.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5):* The sermon moves toward the cross and arrives there with force. The law/gospel dialectic is partially present: the conformity section functions somewhat as law (exposing the failure), and the Moriah conclusion functions as gospel (providing the ground of acceptance). However, the transition could be sharper — the law is applied to behavior (tongue, habits, politics) without a sufficient moment of crushing before the gospel relieves it. The hammer does not fully land before the balm.

*Synagogue Test:* This sermon would be deeply offensive to a devout non-Christian. The argument that Isaac's preserved life points to a Son who was not preserved — who was the substitute — is explicitly Christological and scandalous. The cross is not decoration. Pass.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10/15**
- *Heart Penetration (4/5):* The conformity section searches the conscience effectively — "Tell little white lies? Conformity. A silent Christian is a conformed Christian. A comfortable Christian is often a conformed Christian." These are pointed. The Epstein illustration grounds the abstract in the concrete and culturally recognizable.
- *Specificity (4/5):* The body enumeration (habits, eyes, tongue, schedule, private world, online world) is specific and embodied. The conformity applications (cussing because everyone does, shrinking evangelism circle) are congregation-specific and honest.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The conclusion addresses three categories — the struggling saint, the self-assured sinner, and the seeking skeptic — and this is the sermon's most Perkins-aware moment. However, the categories feel slightly schematic. The backslider, the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled, and the humbled-but-not-yet-believing are not distinctly addressed during the body of the sermon. The differentiation happens only in the conclusion rather than being woven throughout.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 6/8**
- *Call to Response (3/4):* The three-part conclusion (struggling saint / self-assured sinner / seeking skeptic) is the clearest exhortation in the sermon. The word to the self-assured sinner in particular has real edge — "the mercy you claim may not be the mercy you possess." That is a summons, not a suggestion.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (3/4):* "A silent Christian is a conformed Christian" — this equips for witness. The Point 4 material on conformity gives the congregation categories for diagnosing their own discipleship. The sermon sends them out rather than only comforting them. Minor gap: the corporate/body dimension of living sacrifice is underdeveloped — Romans 12 has both individual and community dimensions that the sermon addresses individually but not corporately.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 8/9**
- *Clarity of Outline (4/4):* The five-point structure is traceable and memorable. The Moriah frame introduced in the introduction and sustained through every point and the conclusion is elegant structural architecture. A listener could sketch it on a napkin: mountain of mercy → altar call → nature of worship → war for the mind → clarity after surrender.
- *Progression (3/3):* The sermon builds. Each point advances the argument. The conclusion earns its landing because the whole sermon has prepared for it. Surrender → Renewal → Discernment is not just stated — it is demonstrated by the sermon's own movement.
- *Unity (1/2):* The Epstein section, while not wrong, is the one moment where the sermon's unity strains. It is used to illustrate worldly conformity effectively, but it introduces a culturally specific example that draws significant attention and requires careful landing. It is recoverable — and the sermon does recover — but it briefly pulls focus from the text's own argument.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**
- *Relevance (2/2):* Moriah is native to the text's argument. The Epstein reference is culturally immediate. The embodied list (habits, eyes, tongue, schedule) lands in the congregation's actual world.
- *Clarity (1/2):* Moriah is consistently clear. The Epstein section is clear in its application but risks becoming more about current events than about the text. One illustration that was wholly the preacher's own — native to the congregation's specific world — is missing.
- *Memorability (1/1):* "Christ stayed on His altar so you could climb onto yours." That line will be carried home.

**8. Sermonic Force — 4/5**
- *Reads as Preaching (2/2):* The manuscript reads as preaching, not essay. The staccato lists, the direct address, the short declarative sentences at climax — this was written to be spoken aloud. The oral cadence is strong.
- *Conviction on the Page (2/2):* The Scripture-grounded assertion ratio is high. Very little filler. The text drives the assertions, not the assertions the text.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1):* The manuscript has strong staccato compression in the conformity section and the Moriah conclusion. However, the very consistent use of short parallel lines throughout means that the climactic compression moments do not punch as hard as they could — there is limited contrast between the ordinary pace and the climactic moments.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
More of Christ. The Moriah typology consistently points away from self-effort and toward the substitution that grounds all Christian obedience. The conclusion is Christ-ward in its movement and landing.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Partially encounter. The text is opened faithfully. The conclusion has genuine weight. The conformity section convicts. God is not absent. But the sermon has moments — particularly in the point-by-point enumeration sections — that feel more like organized theological content than the presence of the living God.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Convicted and comforted — in that order. The conformity diagnostic convicts. The Moriah Christology comforts. The conclusion differentiates appropriately between the struggling saint (comfort) and the self-assured sinner (warning). This is the sermon's most Puritan-shaped moment.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Moriah typology is structurally earned, not decorative.** The Abraham/Isaac frame is introduced in the introduction, woven through each of the five points, and resolved Christologically in the conclusion. This is not a "hook" that gets dropped — it is the sermon's vertebral column. "Christ stayed on His altar so you could climb onto yours" is a line with genuine theological freight, not sermonic flair.

2. **The "therefore" as doctrinal hinge is handled with precision.** The sermon correctly identifies Romans 12:1's "therefore" as the load-bearing connector between eleven chapters of doctrine and the ethical argument that follows. "Mercy is the fuel of obedience" — stated early and demonstrated throughout — is both exegetically accurate and pastorally powerful.

3. **The conclusion's three-part differentiation (struggling saint / self-assured sinner / seeking skeptic) is the sermon's most Perkins-shaped moment.** The word to the self-assured sinner — "the mercy you claim may not be the mercy you possess" — has genuine edge and is pastorally necessary. The word to the seeking skeptic — "you don't clean yourself up before coming" — is gospel grace without sentimentality.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The law/gospel sequence is compressed too quickly in the conformity section.** Point 4 does real diagnostic work — "a silent Christian is a conformed Christian, a comfortable Christian is often a conformed Christian" — but moves from conviction to the positive command (transformation) too fast. The hammer needs more time to land before the balm relieves it. The hearer needs to feel the weight of conformity's grip before being handed the promise of metamorphosis. The sequence is correct but the timing is rushed.

2. **Spiritual differentiation is concentrated in the conclusion rather than distributed throughout.** The three-category address in the conclusion is excellent, but the Perkins taxonomy is almost entirely absent during the body of the sermon. The struggling saint, the self-righteous, the backslider, and the seeker are all present in the room during Points 1–4 and receive undifferentiated address. The congregation's consciences are pressed most specifically only in the conclusion.

3. **The Epstein reference is the sermon's most unstable moment.** It is used to illustrate worldly conformity, and the application — "the church is not immune; many trust in political maneuvering more than holiness" — is legitimate and pastorally necessary. But the reference introduces current-events currency that requires careful footing. The illustration would be stronger if the point about political maneuvering and compromised witness were derived more directly from Scripture — the Psalm 20:7 quote actually does this better. The Epstein reference risks making this section feel like cultural commentary illustrated by the Bible rather than the Bible applied to culture.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the consistent use of short parallel list structures throughout the sermon creates a kind of rhythmic uniformity that slightly flattens the climactic moments. When the Moriah conclusion arrives — which is the strongest moment of the sermon — it is delivered in the same staccato rhythm as the body enumerations in Point 2. The change of gear is emotional and theological but not syntactic. A shift to longer, more gathered sentences at the climax would make the emotional weight more distinctive and more felt.

Additionally, the sermon addresses "bodies" in Point 2 with a helpful enumeration of embodied life (habits, eyes, tongue, schedule), but the corporate dimension of Romans 12:1 — that this is Paul's address to a *community* of bodies forming one body — is not developed. The sermon reads Romans 12:1 as primarily an individual command. The communal dimension would deepen the application and connect naturally to the later verses of the chapter.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Moriah typology feels like the sermon's greatest strength — and it largely is. But because it is so well-developed and so consistently sustained, it risks becoming the sermon's primary message rather than the text's primary message. A hearer might leave knowing the Moriah story and its Christological resolution better than they know Romans 12:1–2's own argument. The typology illuminates the text; it must not replace it. When the sermon's most memorable line ("Christ stayed on His altar so you could climb onto yours") is drawn from the illustration rather than from the text, the illustration has briefly outshined the passage.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The phrase "holy and acceptable to God" — *hagia kai euarestos* — is the sermon's most underexplored theological phrase. The sermon notes that "you are accepted because Christ is accepted" (a clean doctrinal statement), but the word *euarestos* (well-pleasing, delightful to) carries the full weight of the gospel's affectional dimension: God does not merely tolerate the surrendered life, He *delights* in it. This phrase, developed, would have grounded the call to surrender not only in duty (mercy demands obedience) but in joy — the Father's delight in the surrendered son/daughter, just as the Father's voice declared Christ "well-pleasing" at His baptism. This would have deepened the gospel's emotional resonance and connected the individual surrender to the Father's pleasure in a way that motivates from love rather than only from obligation.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Slow down the law/gospel transition in Point 4.** Between the conformity diagnosis and the transformation promise, add a beat of conviction. Name the specific weight: "Some of you know exactly where you are conformed. You've known it for years. You've made peace with it. You've named it something other than disobedience. Let the text name it for you." Then arrive at the metamorphosis promise as genuine relief.

2. **Distribute Perkins' differentiation into the body of the sermon, not only the conclusion.** At least in Point 2 (the altar call) and Point 4 (the war for the mind), insert brief targeted addresses to distinct categories. After the conformity examples, speak briefly to the person who is convinced but not yet surrendering: "You know this is true. You've known it for a long time. You are not ignorant — you are unhumbled. The knowledge is there; the surrender is not." This corners a specific hearer rather than letting them apply the sermon generically.

3. **Develop "holy and acceptable" into the joy of the Father's delight.** In Point 3 or the conclusion, add one sentence: "The text does not say your sacrifice is merely tolerated — it says *euarestos*, well-pleasing, delightful. The Father does not grimace when you climb the mountain. He delights in you, because He delights in His Son, and you are hidden in Him." This moves the motivation from duty to wonder.

4. **Either anchor the Epstein reference more firmly to the text or replace it.** If retained, make the transition to Scripture faster — lead with Psalm 20:7 and let Epstein illustrate the psalm rather than vice versa. If the cultural moment has passed in the congregation's awareness, consider a more timeless illustration of political conformity masquerading as wisdom.

5. **Add one moment of corporate address.** "Paul is not writing to individuals shuffling forward alone — he is writing to a *body*. Your body on the altar matters. But so does ours. When this church is conformed — when we together drift toward comfort, silence, and accommodation — it is not only your sacrifice that is withheld. It is ours." This opens the communal dimension of the text and equips the congregation to think ecclesially, not only individually.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon is not correct and bloodless. The Moriah typology produces genuine wonder. The self-assured sinner address has pastoral edge. The conclusion earns its landing. The fire is present. The primary weakness is applicatory distribution, not doctrinal coldness.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries genuine eternal weight — the Moriah typology is handled with reverence, the cross is presented as structurally necessary and not decorative, and "Christ stayed on His altar so you could climb onto yours" carries the kind of compressive theological gravity that can haunt a hearer for a week — though the sermon's weight would deepen further if the Father's delight in the surrendered life were named alongside the duty of surrender.

This passage and rubric are well-matched.
