# There and Back Again: Isaac's Journey to the Brink of Death to Save the World

**Passage:** Genesis 22:1-24
**Type:** Raw transcript / study notes
**Date:** Unknown

---

## Main Idea

God wanted Abraham to demonstrate his faith in a practical way, and through that to depict what he would allow his own one and only Son to go through.

## Theme

Talking in narrative about events in Abraham's life that caused him to exhibit faith to the brink of sacrificing his one and only son, while at the same time modeling events that would happen in the life of Christ.

---

## Seams

### 22:1-2 — God asks Abraham for a sacrifice

God asks Abraham for a sacrifice, his son, his only son, which mirrors God's own sacrifice for our sins (John 3:16). Rabbi Schwarz quotes the midrash saying that this is a dig from God at Abraham. Abraham has two sons, but he favors one over the other. In fact, at his wife's request, he ran Hagar, and his other son Ishmael off. The Hebrew word *veha'alehu* has at least two meanings. Firstly it can be translated "to bring, or raise up," and the second definition can mean to sacrifice (more specifically as I understand it, a burnt offering).

### 22:3-6 — Abraham obeys without hesitation

Abraham does as he is commanded seemingly without hesitation (contrary to what he did to save Lot, and his pleading with God for Ishmael). Together with some servants they walk for three days (which mirrors Christ's three days in the tomb) to the mountain ridge Christ would later be sacrificed upon. Isaac carries his own wood, as our Lord carried the cross, Abraham carried the implements of his son's death, even as God's sovereignty makes him responsible for Christ's death. The fire might be symbolic of judgment (hell). The two servants left behind could mirror our relationship with Christ's sacrifice. All of the details are not known of what happened between and within the Godhead. Parts of it are private — between God and Christ, so it was with Abraham and Isaac. Parts of it the servants were allowed to observe, and participate in, but they did not have full-fledged integral, critical parts in this part of the story.

### 22:7-8 — Isaac could have fought back

Isaac could have fought back (as could have Christ), several scholars (J. Vernon McGee, and rabbinic tradition) place his age in his 30s — he was born when Sarah was 90, and in the following chapter (23) she dies at 127. Isaac calls out to his father, as Christ called out to his Heavenly Father from the cross. Abraham says something that predicts the immediate and distant futures (whether he realizes it or not) regarding atonement — God will provide for himself the lamb.

### 22:9-14 — Abraham built the altar

Abraham built the altar upon which he was to kill his son, which mirrors God's sovereignty through the crucifixion of Christ (Isaiah 53 ESV). The ram was caught in the thicket, Christ wore a crown of thorns. The ram represented life for Isaac, without the ram, Isaac would have been dead — the same should be said for believers — without Christ's death, we would receive what we deserve (hell). God provided salvation (in the physical sense) for Isaac through the ram, but through his Son — the Lamb — he provided salvation in the eternal sense in the future. "The LORD will provide" is meant to remind us of God's provision for his people.

### 22:15-19 — Abrahamic Covenant

He would be blessed, he would have a multitude of descendants, some of whom would rise to positions of great earthly power, and through his "seed" the world would be blessed. Jesus is a descendant of Abraham (and blessed the world with salvation). "It is worth noting that the root of the Hebrew word *vayashav* is the same as the word for 'repentance,' *teshuva*" (Schwarz pg 3). *Vayashav* also carries within its semantic range the idea of restoration, of human relations, spiritual relations, and in repetition (Strong's #07725).

### 22:20-24 — News of Abraham's family

Abraham is told that he has nephews and a grand-niece back in Haran.

### Notes for Future Use
- **Isaac's age**: Calculates to exactly 37 (Sarah 127 minus 90), not vaguely "in his 30s." The calculation itself assumes the Aqedah occurred near Sarah's death — this is midrashic tradition (Seder Olam Rabbah), not explicit Scripture. Genesis 22 does not state Isaac's age.
- **J. Vernon McGee** placing Isaac in his 30s is from *Thru the Bible* commentary, widely cited but speculative.
- **Moriah = Golgotha**: 2 Chronicles 3:1 identifies Moriah as the Temple Mount, not Golgotha. The "same mountain ridge" claim is common sermonic tradition but not textually verified — Golgotha's location is debated, and neither traditional site (Church of the Holy Sepulchre, Gordon's Calvary) is on the Temple Mount itself. Present as typological tradition, not textual fact.
- **Crown of thorns / thicket parallel**: Traditional Christian typology, widely used in sermonic literature. Not a direct textual claim.
- **"Rabbi Schwarz"**: Identified as Rabbi Sid Schwarz (CLAL). The "dig at Abraham" language regarding "your son, your only son" draws on Talmud Sanhedrin 89b and Genesis Rabbah, where God and Abraham engage in back-and-forth dialogue. Could not confirm Schwarz specifically used the word "dig."

A dense study-notes transcript of Genesis 22 that identifies legitimate typological parallels between the Aqedah and Christ's sacrifice but never coalesces into proclamation, never presses the conscience, and never summons the congregation to respond.

The following fatal flaws are present. **Score is capped at 69.**

The gospel is traced (the ram as substitute, the Lamb as provision) but never *proclaimed*. There is no moment where a sinner is told what Christ's death accomplishes for them or how they receive it.

- **Flag #6 — Inaccessible in its current form**: As study notes and a raw transcript, this material is not currently accessible to the ordinary hearer. The single mother, the high schooler, the new believer cannot track a fragmented section-by-section outline of typological observations.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17/25**

However, the text is mined primarily for typological parallels rather than for the passage's own argument. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is never named — what broken human condition does this text exist to address? The Aqedah speaks to the condition of a people who cannot provide their own atonement and cannot know whether God will spare them. That condition goes unnamed.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5)*: The redemptive-historical placement is present but reduced to typological observation. The Abrahamic covenant and its connection to the larger biblical story is touched at vv.15-19 but not developed. - *Exegetical Clarity (4/5)*: The Hebrew observations (*veha'alehu*, *vayashav*/*teshuvah*, the dual meanings) are handled with care and transparency. The corrections log is a strength. The Moriah/Golgotha caveat is honest and necessary.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5)*: The typological moves are generally organic, with appropriate hedging on the speculative ones. The three-day parallel is legitimate. ### 11. Blind Spots

- **The Fallen Condition Focus is invisible**: The preacher has identified what the text points to (Christ) but has not named what condition in the hearer the text was written to address. Genesis 22 confronts us with our inability to provide atonement for ourselves — the lamb cannot come from us. That gap — the terror of owing everything to a God from whom you have nothing to give — is the text's pastoral engine, and it never appears in this manuscript.

- **Abraham's faith as a subject for exhortation**: The stated main idea ("God wanted Abraham to demonstrate his faith") points directly toward an exhortation about obedient faith in the face of impossible demands. But the preacher never returns to Abraham's faith as a word for the congregation. The typological focus crowds out the pastoral use of Abraham as an example.

- **The ram is the sermon's climax, but the sermon doesn't know it**: The most theologically loaded moment in the passage is v.13-14 — the ram provided, Isaac spared, the LORD sees and provides. This is the text's own evangelical center. The sermon acknowledges it but does not dwell, develop, or exhort from it.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The typological density of this manuscript *feels* like theological substance. The parallel between Isaac's three-day journey and Christ's three days in the tomb, the wood/cross parallel, the thicket/crown parallel — these observations are rich and legitimate, and their presence gives the sermon a sense of theological weight. But typological mapping is not the same as gospel proclamation. A sermon can identify every type and shadow in Genesis 22 and still leave the congregation without a saving knowledge of what those shadows point to. The richness of the typology *masks* the absence of application and proclamation. The scaffold looks like a building.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**The cry "God will provide himself the lamb" (v.8)** is the theological heart of the passage and one of the most remarkable statements in the Old Testament. Abraham speaks it as a faith assertion in the midst of anguish — not yet knowing the ram would appear, possibly meaning it in a darker sense (God will provide Isaac himself as the lamb). The double meaning, the irony, the faith, the fulfillment — and then the way it is fulfilled first by the ram and ultimately by Christ — this moment deserved extended development. It is the Aqedah's *scopus*, its most concentrated expression. The sermon notes it but does not stop here, does not expand it, does not press it into the conscience of the hearer. This is the sermon's one golden opportunity to land the gospel, name the substitute, and press the hearer to receive the Lamb — and it passes without landing.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Name the Fallen Condition explicitly**: Before the typological material begins, state the human problem Genesis 22 is written to address: *We owe a debt we cannot pay. We have no lamb. We cannot provide our own atonement. The question "Where is the lamb?" is the question of every conscience before God.* Ground the typology in that condition, and the parallels become pastorally urgent rather than academically interesting.

2. **Proclaim the gospel at v.13-14**: Stop here. Do not merely note the ram. Declare it. "The ram was caught in the thicket so Isaac could walk free. And Jesus Christ — the Lamb of God — was caught in our place, bore the thorns and the fire that we deserved, so that everyone who trusts in him walks free. This is not a metaphor. This is the gospel." This is the sermon's natural gospel declaration moment.

3. **Add differentiated address at least twice**: After the gospel declaration, address at least two categories of hearer directly: (a) the person who has never received this Lamb — "Some of you have appreciated this story for years and have never once trusted the Savior it points to. Today, God is providing himself the Lamb for you. Will you receive him?" (b) the believer whose faith has grown cold — "Some of you came here today doubting whether God sees you, whether he provides, whether he is trustworthy. Look at Mount Moriah. The God who provided for Abraham has not changed."

4. **Restructure the ending**: Remove the vv.20-24 section from the climactic position or subordinate it clearly. The sermon must end at the covenant renewal (vv.15-19) or at the ram (vv.13-14). The family genealogy is a cold landing. If vv.20-24 must be addressed, address them briefly as a narrative transition note and then return to the main idea for the close.

5. **Clarify penal substitution**: The statement "without Christ's death, we would receive what we deserve (hell)" is directionally correct but insufficient. Add: Christ received what *we* deserve so that we receive what *he* deserves — the favor of God, reconciliation, eternal life. This is penal substitution stated plainly enough for a hearer to grasp.

6. **Add an exhortation in the imperative**: Somewhere before the close, the congregation must be summoned. "Therefore — trust this God. He did not spare his own Son. He will not withhold anything you need. Trust him today." Without the imperative, the sermon explains but never preaches.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Genesis 22 is Old Testament narrative, and the rubric bends appropriately here. The heavy weighting on Exposition (25 pts) and Gospel Centrality (15 pts) is well-suited to this passage, which is among the most typologically rich texts in the Old Testament and demands both careful exegetical work and transparent gospel proclamation. The Conscience & Applicatory Force categories (15 pts) bend slightly — Old Testament narrative sermons often press application through the theological import of narrative events rather than through direct command. However, the absence of application here is not a genre accommodation; it is a structural omission. The rubric serves this text well. The sermon has not yet served the rubric.
