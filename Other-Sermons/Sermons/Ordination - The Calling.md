### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **No primary text and no exegesis** — The sermon is constructed around a theme (God's calling and preparation) and illustrated with biblical characters, but it never *opens* a passage. John 6:44 is stated as a fact; the potter texts are listed as references; Eph 4:1 appears as a slogan. A sermon without a text is a talk, not a sermon.
2. **The gospel is absent from a public worship address** — Opening with "reaching a point where you are willing to set aside your pride and accept the gift" is not a gospel presentation. If an unconverted visitor attended this ordination service, they would hear that God uses flawed people, that preparation is hard, and that ministry is often obscure — but they would not hear how to be saved.
3. **The congregation is invisible** — This is almost entirely a private charge to Kenny and an address about Kenny's journey. The congregation is occasionally gestured toward ("The lesson here isn't just for Kenny, but for each one of us") but never directly addressed with their own call.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how heavily the sermon leans on the ordinand's personal biography (years at Trinity, Southeastern, the doctor Baker reference, Loise's health, his broken bones) as structural content. In a published or archivally-preserved sermon, this material is private and inaccessible; it also competes with theological development. The assumption is that the relationship and the occasion carry the weight — which in a room full of people who know Kenny, they likely did. But the text is not doing any lifting.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Kimball → Moody → Graham chain *feels* like it will be the sermon's strongest moment — and in delivery it likely was. But as a planned illustration, it is being used to make the point "your faithfulness may have invisible fruit" — a thoroughly true and important point — without asking whether that comfort is grounded in the gospel. A faithful minister does not persevere because obscure seeds sometimes yield famous fruit. He perseveres because he is a steward of God's Word and accountable to God alone. The Kimball/Graham chain can accidentally teach ministerial success-theology through the backdoor.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The ordination of a minister is one of the rare occasions when the church publicly proclaims its theology of vocation, eldership, and the preached Word. No text from the Pastoral Epistles (1 Timothy 3, 2 Timothy 4:1-5, Titus 1) is present. Paul's charge to Timothy — "preach the word; be ready in season and out of season; reprove, rebuke, and exhort, with complete patience and teaching" (2 Tim 4:2) — is not only the perfect ordination text, it is *what an ordination charge is for*. Its absence is a significant missed opportunity.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Establish a primary text.** Choose one passage and open it — 2 Timothy 4:1-5 or 1 Timothy 4:12-16 would be natural ordination texts. The entire sermon structure (calling, preparation, service) maps cleanly onto Paul's charge to Timothy. Let the text provide the structure, not impose the structure on the text.
2. **Add an explicit gospel presentation.** Even in an ordination sermon, the cross must be proclaimed. The ordinand is being set apart to preach the gospel — preach it. Show the congregation what the man is being ordained to do by doing it.
3. **Address the congregation directly in each section.** Each of the three sections should have a moment that speaks to the gathered church: what does *their* calling look like? What does *their* preparation mean? What does *their* service look like in the pew, the home, the workplace?
4. **Develop or remove the Kimball chain.** If told, tell it fully. If cut for time, remove the names entirely. As a list of surnames without narrative, it communicates nothing to anyone who does not already know the chain.
5. **The "cracked pot" — decide.** The document says "Talk about the cracked pot." If the cracked pot parable is being used, it needs to be recognized as a widely circulated inspirational story (attributed variously to Chinese folk wisdom) — not scripture, and frequently misread as if imperfection is the *qualification* for ministry rather than the grace of God that works *through* imperfection. Flag the distinction from the pulpit or find a better illustration.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

The rubric strains here in an unusual way: this is not a congregation-facing expository sermon but an ordination address — a hybrid of personal charge, congregational witness, and occasional sermon. Ordination addresses have their own homiletical conventions (apostolic model in Acts 20, Paul's charges to Timothy) that do not always conform to the expository preaching rubric. The low score is not a pastoral criticism of the occasion or the man; it reflects a set of notes evaluated against the standard of a completed expository manuscript. The rubric fits best when a sermon is finished; here it measures the distance between notes and sermon.
