## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### Verdict Title
A Warm Gospel Heart with an Unfinished Frame — Promising Pastoral Instincts Undermined by Structural Incompletion and a Missing Sermon Body

### One-Sentence Summary
A genuinely gospel-warmed opening of Matthew 9:9–13 that magnified God's sovereignty and grace toward the outcast while ending mid-sentence before completing its application, leaving the conscience searched only in glimpses and the exhortation entirely undelivered.

### Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with significant qualification.**

Scopus Test: The main point of the passage — Jesus calling a despised sinner to follow him as an act of sovereign grace, and defending his ministry to sinners against the self-righteous — is present in the sermon. The thesis is traceable: "God calls the broken and the outcast to himself through Christ, exchanging their shame for grace." This arises from the text's own argument.

Authority Test: The preacher does not edit around the hard parts. The sinfulness of Matthew is not softened. The Pharisees' objection (vv. 11–13) receives passing reference ("tax collectors and sinners") but is not developed as the text's own confrontation. The Authority Test passes narrowly.

Qualification: The sermon is explicitly incomplete. It ends with "But that starts with the gospel," — a sentence that is not completed. The sermon manuscript as it stands is a fragment. The evaluation proceeds on the material present, but every score must be read in light of the fact that the sermon was not preached to completion, and the most important section — the gospel declaration and exhortation — is missing.

### Fatal Flaw Flags
The following flags are triggered:

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation.** The sermon ends with "But that starts with the gospel," and then stops. The gospel is gestured at throughout (references to grace, to Christ's call, to Ephesians 2:4–7, to Matthew's undeserved salvation) but is never proclaimed as a complete act. An unbeliever sitting in the room hears that Matthew needed the gospel and that the congregation does too — but is never told what the gospel is, what Christ did, or how to receive it. The gospel is promised and then withheld.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The sermon contains no "therefore" moment. There is no call to repent, to follow, to believe, to act. The sermon describes Matthew following Jesus but never calls the congregation to do the same. The manuscript ends before arriving at its own declared destination.

**Score cap applies: maximum score is 69.**

### Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**
- Textual Fidelity: 5/10. The sermon spends the majority of its time on Matthew 9:9 (the call) and the social context of tax collectors. Verses 10–13 — the meal with sinners, the Pharisees' objection, and Jesus' self-declaration ("I came not to call the righteous, but sinners") — receive almost no exegetical treatment. The most theologically freighted verse in the pericope (v.13: "I desire mercy, not sacrifice") is entirely absent. The sermon is built on the foundation of the passage but neglects its explicit climax.
- Contextual Awareness: 3/5. The historical-cultural background on tax collectors is the sermon's strongest section — thorough, vivid, and genuinely illuminating. The hurricane-looting analogy is creative and effective. The redemptive-historical location of this call (its place in Matthew's larger narrative of Jesus gathering outcasts into his kingdom) is not developed.
- Exegetical Clarity: 3/5. John 6:44 is rightly used to ground Matthew's response in divine drawing. Ephesians 2:4–7 is quoted in full. Colossians 1:16–17 is included but functions more as a comfort passage than as exegesis of Matthew 9. Isaiah 57:15 is meaningfully applied. The connections are sound but asserted rather than demonstrated from the passage's own logic.
- Logical Faithfulness: 3/5. No significant eisegesis. The sovereignty of God section (John 6:44, the pastor's pacemaker story, Dan Mastrapa quotations) is a significant digression from the text — theologically sound but structurally dislocating. The sermon leaves Matthew 9 for several paragraphs to develop a theology of sovereignty that could have been grounded directly in the text.
- Fallen Condition Focus: Partially present. The sermon names Matthew's brokenness and social shame, and implies the human condition as lostness and unworthiness. The specific condition the text addresses — self-righteous exclusion of the outcast (the Pharisees' response) as much as the outcast's own lostness — is only half-acknowledged.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**
- Christ-Centeredness: 5/7. Christ is genuinely at the center of this sermon — his call to Matthew, his table fellowship with sinners, his sovereignty in drawing people to himself. This is the sermon's strongest theological feature. Christ is not decorative here.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3. The placement of Matthew 9 in redemptive history — Jesus inaugurating his ministry by gathering the rejected, fulfilling the prophetic pattern of God seeking the lost — is not developed. The Old Testament background (Hosea 6:6, which Jesus quotes in v.13) is absent entirely.
- Doctrinal Precision: 3/4. Sovereign grace is handled well and without apology. The Ephesians 2 quotation is precise. The Jim Elliot line is apt. No significant errors are present. Justification is not articulated as a category — grace is described as God's kindness and exchange ("all he wants is our mess, in exchange he gives us everything") without the legal ground being named.
- Confessional Alignment: 3/4. The doctrine of effectual calling ("God had been working in Mathew's heart before Jesus ever talked to him"; John 6:44) is explicitly affirmed and well-applied. No contradictions of confessional theology are present.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 5/15**
- Clarity of the Gospel: 0/5. The gospel is explicitly deferred: "But that starts with the gospel." The sermon promises the gospel and does not deliver it. An unbeliever cannot be saved from this sermon as it stands.
- Necessity of Christ: 3/5. Christ's necessity is clearly present in what is preached — Matthew cannot come without being drawn, cannot be saved without Christ, cannot be forgiven without grace. This is genuinely embedded in the sermon. What is absent is the announcement of what Christ actually did: his death, atonement, resurrection, and the terms of the gospel offer.
- Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5. The sermon moves toward the cross — the "beauty for ashes" language, the Ephesians 2 passage, the exchange of mess for grace — but never arrives. The cross is the sermon's implicit destination. It is never reached.
- Synagogue Test: Not passed. A devout non-Christian could appreciate most of this sermon as a story of divine grace and mercy without being confronted with the blood of Christ as the ground of forgiveness. The gospel scandal — that Christ died for sinners — is absent from the preached portion.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15**
- Heart Penetration: 3/5. The tax collector background section is viscerally effective and creates genuine conscience-searching: "What are the monsters of our society?" The application to Matthew's courageous abandonment of wealth is felt. The image of God as a "careful and patient and loving craftsman" who gives beauty for ashes has genuine comfort for the broken. These are real moments of pastoral warmth.
- Specificity: 2/5. The hurricane-looting analogy is the most specific moment in the sermon and is well-suited to a Florida congregation. The sovereignty section is less specific — the pacemaker story is illustrative but not applicatory. The closing paragraphs are warm but speak in generalities ("he takes the broken mess we make of things...").
- Spiritual Differentiation: 2/5. The sermon primarily addresses one hearer: the person who feels like an outcast, the broken, the one who has made a mess of things. This is appropriate to the text. But the Pharisees in the pericope represent a second hearer — the self-righteous, the one who would never eat with Matthew — who is entirely unaddressed. Perkins' taxonomy is largely absent. The self-righteous hearer who would have been sitting in the original congregation goes home unchallenged.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 1/8**
- Call to Response: 0/4. There is no call to response. The sermon ends before the exhortation. There is no "follow Jesus" moment, no invitation, no "therefore." The absence of exhortation is the most acute structural failure of the manuscript as it stands.
- Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 1/4. The Jim Elliot line ("He is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain that he cannot lose") is the closest the sermon comes to equipping the hearer for response. It is not developed. There is no sending.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**
- Clarity of Outline: 2/4. The structure is discernible in retrospect: (1) Context of tax collectors, (2) Matthew's call and its cost, (3) God's sovereignty in drawing Matthew, (4) God's grace to the broken. But these movements are not clearly signposted, and the sovereignty section (John 6:44, pacemaker story, Dan Mastrapa) sits somewhat uncomfortably as a digression within the call narrative.
- Progression: 2/3. The sermon moves forward and builds toward something — but the building stops. The progression is genuine but incomplete.
- Unity: 1/2. Two ideas compete: the story of Matthew's call and the theology of God's sovereignty. Both are valid, but the sovereignty section is long enough to become a second sermon rather than support for the first.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**
- Relevance: 2/2. The hurricane-looting analogy is native to the congregation (Florida, local geography named — Wesley Chapel, Hudson, Bolton Heights, Moon Lake). This is excellent contextual illustration that places the hearer inside the tax collector's world.
- Clarity: 1/2. The tax collector background section is vivid and clear. The pacemaker story illuminates sovereignty effectively. The ghost-story campfire comparison ("if Israelites had the same tradition, they would have been telling tax collector stories") is memorable.
- Memorability: 1/1. The hurricane-looting analogy and the "beauty for ashes" closing image will be carried home. This sermon has more illustrative strength than many.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**
- Reads as Preaching: 2/2. This reads like it was spoken, not written. The oral cadence is real — sentence fragments, direct address, conversational rhythm, rhetorical questions. "What are the monsters of our society?" and "they had tax collectors. We have the Italian mafia" — this is preaching voice.
- Conviction on the Page: 1/2. The conviction is present in the illustration sections and the closing "beauty for ashes" passage. The sovereignty section loses some sermonic force — it becomes instructional and loses the direct address.
- Cadence and Breath: 0/1. The sermon does not vary its sentence length at climax. The closing movement is warm but does not compress toward a moment of climactic force. The manuscript ends before the intended climax.

### Core Override Questions
1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** More of Christ — this is one of the sermon's genuine strengths. Matthew's story keeps Christ at the center: his call, his table fellowship, his drawing of the outcast.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Partially. The "beauty for ashes" section and the tax collector background create genuine moments of encounter. The sovereignty section drifts toward ideas.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Primarily comforted and informed. The broken hearer is genuinely comforted. The self-righteous hearer is never convicted. The unbeliever is informed that the gospel exists but is not offered it.

### Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The tax collector background section.** The hurricane-looting analogy — specific to the congregation's geography, viscerally concrete, historically grounded — is one of the best contextual illustrations in the repository. It puts the hearer inside the text's world rather than describing it from outside. The preacher has genuine gift here.

2. **Oral voice and congregation-facing cadence.** This manuscript sounds like preaching. The sentence fragments, the direct address, the rhetorical questions ("Could Mathew have ever earned heaven apart from Jesus?") — these are instincts that many seminary-trained preachers work years to recover. The voice is natural and authentic.

3. **The "beauty for ashes" conclusion and Ephesians 2 application.** The closing movement — God as patient craftsman, Isaiah 57:15, the exchange of mess for grace — is genuinely warm and text-connected. The Isaiah and Ephesians quotations are well-chosen and land with pastoral force. "All he wants is our mess, and in exchange he gives us everything" is a line that comforts the broken.

### Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The sermon is incomplete.** It ends mid-sentence before the gospel declaration. This is the most serious practical weakness. A congregation was presumably not dismissed without the gospel being preached — but the manuscript as preserved does not contain it. The most important thing the sermon was going to say is missing.

2. **Verses 10–13 are effectively absent.** The Pharisees' objection, Jesus' self-declaration as a physician to the sick, and his citation of Hosea 6:6 ("I desire mercy, not sacrifice") are the theological climax of the pericope. The sermon preaches v.9 and gestures at the rest. The text's own answer to the question "why does Jesus eat with sinners?" — because he came to call sinners, not the righteous — is never preached. This is the passage's most confrontational and most glorious claim, and it is absent.

3. **The self-righteous hearer is never addressed.** Matthew 9:9–13 is as much a confrontation of the Pharisees' self-righteousness as it is a comfort to sinners. The tax collector background section builds empathy for Matthew but never turns to apply the Pharisees' role to the congregation. Who in the room is the Pharisee? Who is excluding the modern equivalent of Matthew from fellowship, from grace, from the table? This hearer goes home unsearched.

### Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see the Pharisees as the sermon's second protagonist. The sermon is entirely focused on Matthew as the outcast — which is right and good — but the passage equally exists as a rebuke to religious self-righteousness. A congregation in a comfortable church setting is statistically more likely to need the Pharisee sermon than the Matthew sermon, and the text supplies both. The preacher has embraced the comfort of the passage without engaging its confrontation.

### Illusion of Strength

The sovereignty section feels theologically robust — John 6:44, the pacemaker story, the Dan Mastrapa quotations, Colossians 1:16–17. It is not wrong. But it functions as a theological insert rather than an exposition of Matthew 9. The passage does not require a developed theology of sovereignty — it shows sovereignty in action. The sermon would have been stronger and more text-centered if it had trusted the passage's own narrative to carry the point, using John 6:44 as a one-sentence commentary rather than a theological launching pad. The quotations from Dan Mastrapa are also flagged as unverified (see the corrections log above); using unverified quotations in a sermon is a credibility risk.

### Missed Opportunity

Jesus' citation of Hosea 6:6 — "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" — is one of the most searching lines in all of Matthew's Gospel. It indicts the entire religious performance economy. It exposes the heart that would rather maintain ritual purity than sit at the table with Matthew. It is the text's sharpest edge and its deepest grace: God is not looking for religious achievement, he is looking for the mercy-shaped heart that knows it needs him. This line opens a direct path to the gospel, to justification, to the ground of all Christian ethics. The sermon walks past it without stopping.

### Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach verses 10–13.** The sermon's third movement must develop Jesus' answer to the Pharisees: "Those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are sick. Go and learn what this means: 'I desire mercy, not sacrifice.' For I came not to call the righteous, but sinners." This is the passage's own gospel declaration. Everything in the sermon — the tax collector background, Matthew's call, the sovereignty section — was building to this. Let the text preach its own climax.

2. **Complete the gospel presentation.** The sermon's intended destination is the gospel. The preacher knew it ("But that starts with the gospel"). Write that section. Who is Christ? What did he do? Why did he die? How does Matthew's story — and the hearer's story — end at the cross? Make the gospel the explicit, announced, clear-to-an-unbeliever content of the sermon's final movement.

3. **Add the Pharisee application.** After comforting the broken-Matthew hearer, turn to the self-righteous hearer: "But some of you are not Matthew in this story. Some of you are the Pharisee — not because you are bad people, but because you have spent so long being good people that you have forgotten you are also sick. You also need the physician. And there is a Matthew in your life you have been avoiding." This is the text's other half.

4. **Anchor the sovereignty section to the text.** Reduce the Dan Mastrapa quotations and the extended pacemaker story to supporting material. The point — God was working in Matthew before Jesus spoke — can be made in two sentences from John 6:44, then the sermon returns to Matthew 9. The sovereignty section currently runs four substantial paragraphs; it can do its work in one.

5. **Issue the exhortation.** End the sermon with a clear call: "Follow him. Matthew left everything. You may not have everything to leave. But you have yourself. And that is what he is asking for. Leave the tax collector's booth — whatever form yours takes. Get up and follow Jesus." The sermon describes Matthew's response but never calls the congregation to make the same one.

### Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon is not cold orthodoxy — it is warm and genuinely gospel-hearted. The failure is not bloodlessness but incompletion. The pastoral affections are present and genuine. The sermon simply ended before it became a sermon.

### Weight of Glory Statement
This sermon carries genuine warmth and pastoral love for the outcast — it knows that God gives beauty for ashes — but the eternal gravity of the moment (the dying man's need, the cross as the only exchange, the urgency of the call) is present in the illustrations but does not yet weigh down the whole.

### Rubric Fit Note
Matthew 9:9–13 is a narrative pericope, and the rubric bends slightly on the exposition categories — narrative preaching legitimately develops character and scene before arriving at theological proposition. The preacher's instinct to immerse the congregation in the world of the tax collector before introducing Jesus is sound homiletics for narrative texts, not a structural flaw. The Fallen Condition Focus and the exegetical categories apply well to this text. The greatest strain on the rubric is the sermon's incompletion — several categories (gospel clarity, exhortation, conscience) cannot be fairly scored when the relevant sections of the sermon were never written down. The scores given reflect the manuscript as preserved, which is almost certainly not the full sermon as preached.
