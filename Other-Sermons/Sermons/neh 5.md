### 1. Verdict Title
**A Faithful Ethical Confrontation Without a Gospel Foundation**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A perceptive and practically grounded sermon on Nehemiah 5's confrontation of predatory economic practice that handles the passage's narrative with pastoral honesty and real conscience-pressing specificity, but fails to proclaim the gospel, treats Nehemiah primarily as a behavioral model, and allows the slavery introduction and the SBC racial controversy detour to displace the passage's theological argument.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: MARGINAL PASS (with drift)**
The main point of Nehemiah 5 is that God's people must practice economic justice among themselves because they fear God and the nations are watching — and Nehemiah models both anger at injustice and Spirit-governed confrontation. The sermon's main point is approximately: "Your actions as God's people affect the world's view of Christ; handle conflict and economic dealings in a way that points others to God." This arises from the text's argument and is defensible. However, the sermon spends more time on the preacher's personal conflict story, the SBC alt-right controversy, and the Britney Spears / Muslim testimony illustration than on the passage itself. The text's own argument is present but partially displaced.

**Authority Test: PASS**
The preacher submits to the text. The confrontation of economic sin, the public witness argument, and the call to repentance at the close all honor the passage's demands.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation (TRIGGERED):**
The sermon contains no gospel proclamation. The altar call at the close ("if you realize you have areas in your life that need work, won't you come?") is a sanctification call, not a gospel call. The person in the room who does not know Christ would not know from this sermon what Christ did for sin or how to be saved. The Nehemiah 5 text does not naturally require a full gospel proclamation — it is a narrative passage about ethical conduct within the covenant community — but the sermon's close implies a mixed congregation and the unconverted are not served.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Christ is absent and the sermon could be preached in a synagogue.** The ethical argument — treat others fairly because the nations are watching and you represent God — is present in Nehemiah 5 as an OT narrative. A sermon on this text that does not bring Christ into the frame can be preached by any moral monotheist. The Synagogue Test fires: no offense is given to the devout non-Christian.
2. **The gospel is not proclaimed.** The altar call invites repentance for areas that "need work" but does not ground that repentance in Christ's substitutionary work. Repentance without the gospel is moralism — the call to do better without the power to do it.
3. **The SBC/alt-right section is a pastoral detour.** Spending approximately 15% of the sermon's body on a denominational controversy that the preacher himself says "is not something I had ever heard of prior to the meeting" and "not something we see in the news regularly" diverts the sermon from its textual argument. The "nations watching" point could be made more directly from the passage.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see that Nehemiah himself is a type. His purchasing of enslaved Jews from their enemies (v.8) at personal cost is a redemptive-historical pattern that points to Christ's ransom of sinners from the bondage of sin. This connection is organic to the text and would not only strengthen the Christology of the sermon but ground the ethical argument in the gospel: "Nehemiah bought back his people at personal cost. Christ bought back his people at the cost of his own blood. Because you have been purchased, live as one who has been bought." That is the gospel-grounded motivation for the ethical demands of the passage.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The conscience-pressing specificity of the personal failure story and the Britney Spears illustration creates an impression of pastoral depth. But application without the gospel indicative is, at bottom, moral coaching. The congregation is told to behave better; they are not told why Christ's work makes a new kind of behavior possible.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Nehemiah 5:8 — "We, as far as we are able, have bought back our Jewish brothers who have been sold to the nations" — is a redemptive-historical gem. Nehemiah is functioning as a kinsman-redeemer figure, purchasing his people from bondage at personal cost. This is the text's own gospel-connective tissue: Christ as the ultimate kinsman-redeemer who bought back his people not with silver and gold but with his own blood (1 Peter 1:18–19). The sermon never arrives there, and in missing it, misses both the gospel and the text's deepest logic.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Develop the kinsman-redeemer / Christological connection from v.8.** "Nehemiah bought his brothers back from bondage. This is what Jesus did for you — at a far greater cost. He was sold for thirty pieces of silver, but he purchased you with his own blood. Because you have been redeemed, live as the redeemed."
2. **Add a full gospel proclamation at the close.** Before the altar call, proclaim the gospel: what is the sin that makes us treat each other exploitatively? What did Christ do about that sin? How does his redemption transform our dealings with one another?
3. **Remove or compress the SBC section.** If the "nations watching" point is the goal, the Britney Spears illustration makes it more vividly and efficiently. The denominational controversy adds length without proportional clarity.
4. **Develop Nehemiah's "fear of God" argument (v.9) as the theological center.** "Ought you not to walk in the fear of our God?" is Nehemiah's own theological grounding for the ethical demand. This deserves 5–7 minutes of development: what is the fear of God? How does it change how we handle money, neighbors, conflict?
5. **Address the unconverted specifically.** The "nations watching" argument implies unbelievers in the congregation's world. Before the altar call, turn to them directly: "If you are not yet a Christian but you are watching — you need to know that what you see in us is not Christianity at its best. Christianity at its best is a man who died for his enemies. Let me tell you about him."

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered — the sermon has genuine pastoral fire. But the absence of Christ and the gospel means the fire burns without fuel. The ethical urgency is real; its theological foundation is missing.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries genuine pastoral courage — the preacher includes himself in the failure, presses real economic and relational accountability, and calls the congregation to a life that points others to God — but without Christ and the gospel, the eternal stakes of the text (souls going to hell for lack of a witness) are felt as moral pressure rather than as the weight of eternity.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* Primarily of myself — what I am doing to my neighbor, whether my conduct obscures Christ. Christ himself is absent as the subject of the sermon.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Ideas about conduct and witness. The fear-of-God argument in v.9 is touched but not developed into an encounter with the living God.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Convicted — specifically around financial dealings and public witness. Not comforted. The gospel comfort that enables genuine repentance is not supplied.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The personal contractor failure story is the sermon's most powerful moment.** The preacher's willingness to include himself in the failure — specifically, on the day of his father's heart surgery, when the stakes of his own suffering made a gracious response harder — is pastorally honest and earns the right to press the congregation on similar failures. The connection to Nehemiah's controlled response (v.6) is direct and effective.
2. **The economic dynamic of the passage is accurately and clearly explained.** The summary of who was exploiting whom, how the predatory loan cycle worked, and what it cost Nehemiah to counteract it gives the congregation a clear window into the text's situation. This is good expository groundwork.
3. **The Britney Spears / Muslim testimony illustration is unconventional and memorable.** The argument that our conduct is the primary message non-Christians use to evaluate Christianity is made with unusual specificity. Citing the research on Muslim obstacles to conversion grounds the application in real-world missionary observation, not homiletical speculation.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Christ is absent and the sermon passes the Synagogue Test.** The ethical argument — treat your neighbors fairly because the nations are watching and you represent God — is present in Nehemiah 5 as an OT covenant argument. A devout non-Christian could sit through this sermon without being offended by the necessity of Christ's blood. The sermon's moral argument is sound; its gospel offensiveness is zero. This is a structural failure at the level of gospel clarity.
2. **The gospel is not proclaimed anywhere in the sermon.** The altar call invites repentance for "areas that need work" but does not ground that repentance in Christ's substitutionary work. Repentance without the gospel is moral self-improvement. The congregation is told to do better; they are not given the One who makes doing better possible.
3. **The SBC/alt-right section is a pastoral detour that costs more than it returns.** The preacher says himself that this is "not something I had ever heard of prior to the meeting" and "not something we see in the news regularly." It occupies 15% of the sermon's body to make a point (the nations are watching) that the Britney Spears illustration makes more vividly in half the space. This section should be cut or compressed to one sentence.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not recognize that Nehemiah's purchasing of enslaved Jews from their enemies (v.8) is a kinsman-redeemer pattern — the same theological category as Boaz redeeming Ruth, and pointing forward to Christ as the ultimate redeemer who purchases his people from bondage at personal cost. This connection is not an artificial "Jesus jump" — it arises from the text's own vocabulary and the redemptive-historical pattern the biblical authors themselves developed. Landing there would give the sermon both its missing Christology and its missing gospel motivation for the ethical demands: "Nehemiah bought back his brothers because they were his people. Christ bought back his people because they were his — at the cost of his own life. You have been redeemed. Live as the redeemed."

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon's personal honesty creates an impression of pastoral depth that temporarily masks the gospel deficit. When the preacher confesses his own failure with the contractor, the congregation experiences something real — but the authenticity of the personal disclosure should not be mistaken for the presence of the gospel. A sermon can be fully honest, self-implicating, and practically useful while still being moralistic at its theological core. This sermon is all three.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Nehemiah 5:8 — "We, as far as we are able, have bought back our Jewish brothers who have been sold to the nations" — is a redemptive-historical hinge point. Nehemiah functions here as a kinsman-redeemer, purchasing his people from bondage at personal cost. This is the text's own gospel-connective tissue: Christ as the ultimate kinsman-redeemer who purchased his people not with money but with his blood (1 Peter 1:18–19). The sermon never arrives there. In missing it, the preacher misses both the deepest logic of the passage and the only ground on which his ethical demands can bear the weight he places on them.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Build the kinsman-redeemer / Christological connection from v.8.** "Nehemiah bought back his brothers at personal cost. That is exactly what Jesus did — at infinitely greater cost. He was sold for thirty pieces of silver, and he purchased you with his own blood. Because you have been redeemed, live as the redeemed. This is not a call to be better. It is a call to be what you already are in Christ."
2. **Proclaim the gospel before the altar call.** Before inviting response, articulate clearly: what sin makes us treat each other exploitatively? What did Christ accomplish at the cross for that sin? How does his finished work — not our moral resolve — change how we treat our neighbors? The call to repentance must be grounded in the gospel indicative before it can function as an authentic imperative.
3. **Cut or compress the SBC section to one sentence.** Replace the extended denominational commentary with the direct application: "The nations are watching us right now. Not just a denomination — this church, this week, in this town." The Britney Spears illustration does the same work more effectively.
4. **Develop the fear-of-God argument of v.9 as the theological center.** "Ought you not to walk in the fear of our God?" deserves 5–7 minutes: What is the fear of God? How does it differ from fear of consequences? How does it change the way we handle money, conflict, and neighbors? This is Nehemiah's own theological argument and the text's governing idea.
5. **Address the unconverted directly.** Before the altar call: "If you are not a Christian and you are watching us — you need to know that what you sometimes see in us is not Christianity at its best. Christianity at its best is a man who died for his enemies. Let me tell you about him before you leave today."

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
