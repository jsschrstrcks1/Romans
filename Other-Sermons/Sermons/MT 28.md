## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**"The Great Commission Used as a Guilt Lever — Faithful Command, Shallow Gospel, Incomplete Manuscript"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A well-intentioned but structurally incomplete sermon on Matthew 28:16–20 that catalogues evangelical excuses for disobedience with pastoral energy, but fails to ground the command to evangelize in the finished work of Christ or the indicatives of the gospel, and breaks off before a conclusion is reached.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with concern.**

- **Scopus Test**: Marginally passes. The sermon's thesis — that Christians are commanded to go and make disciples and must overcome the excuses that prevent them — does arise from the Great Commission text. The main point is directionally aligned with the imperative of Matthew 28:19–20.
- **Authority Test**: Passes. The preacher submits to the text's command without softening it. He does not edit out the uncomfortable obedience demand.

*Concern*: The sermon treats Matthew 28:16–20 almost entirely as a motivational command passage, neglecting the weight of verses 16–18 (the worship of the risen Christ, the doubt of some disciples, and the cosmic authority declaration in v.18 — "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me"). The sermon's thesis lives in v.19, but the text's own logic begins with who Jesus is before it gets to what disciples must do. This imbalance doesn't fail the gate but weakens the exposition considerably.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score cap at 69 is triggered.**

- **Flag #4 — Application is largely generic**: While the sermon does mention specific groups (democrats, republicans, the girl with rainbow hair), the application remains at the motivational-category level. There is no differentiated address to different spiritual states in the congregation. The sermon does not press the conscience of the self-righteous church member, the lukewarm attender, the grieving, or the new believer distinctly. It addresses everyone as if they share the same barrier to evangelism.
- **Flag #5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon categorizes excuses and offers Piper's four remedies, but it never arrives at a direct, summoning "therefore" moment. The sermon breaks off mid-sentence before completion. There is no clear call to a specific response. The congregation was informed and prodded but never summoned.
- **Flag #6 — Manuscript is incomplete**: The sermon ends in mid-sentence ("For those of you that are here for the first time, and"). This is a structural disqualification for evaluation of exhortation and conclusion. The fatal flaw for incomplete exhortation is triggered by what is missing, not merely by what exists.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Matthew 28:18 is the engine and it is never turned on** — "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me — *therefore* go." The entire force of the Great Commission hangs on the resurrected Christ's cosmic authority. The sermon lifts the imperative ("Go") from its power source and then wonders why the congregation feels powerless. 2. **The sermon ends mid-sentence — no conclusion, no exhortation, no summoning** — The final sentence is incomplete: "For those of you that are here for the first time, and." This is not a minor editorial issue. The entire evaluative category of Exhortation & Response collapses because the sermon never calls for a response. 3. **The imported framework runs the sermon, not the text** — The four excuses (ignorance, fear, apathy, lostness) and Piper's four remedies are legitimate pastoral categories, but they do not arise from the passage. The text is read early and then set aside. A listener reconstructing the sermon afterward would remember Piper's categories, not Matthew 28. This reverses the priority of text and framework.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that the sermon's motivational structure — cataloguing failures, offering remedies — can actually reinforce the very apathy it diagnoses. When congregants hear "here are the reasons you aren't evangelizing" without hearing "here is the power by which you now can," the sermon produces guilt without fuel. The sermon diagnoses the disease better than it delivers the cure. The Bridges quote gestures toward the cure, but it appears as the second of four practical remedies rather than as the theological foundation for everything that precedes it.

The preacher also appears unaware that the astrophysics section (Jupiter as guardian), while genuinely interesting, occupies structural space that should belong to Christ's resurrection authority. The creation-sovereignty argument for overcoming fear is real but weaker than the resurrection-authority argument that Matthew 28:18 provides. The text has the answer; the sermon reaches past it.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The conversational warmth and relatable illustration density give the sermon the *feel* of pastoral connection and practical application. The congregation likely walked away thinking "that was relatable and honest." But the relational accessibility masked a significant theological absence: nowhere does the sermon tell the congregation *why the gospel is powerful enough to save* — only that they should believe it is. The Bridges quote is handed to the congregation but not explained or applied. The warmth of the delivery style creates a false impression that the gospel has been preached when it has largely been referenced.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Matthew 28:17 — "and when they saw him they worshipped him, but some doubted" — is one of the most pastorally rich verses in the resurrection narrative. The disciples who worshipped and doubted simultaneously are the exact congregation the preacher is addressing: people who believe enough to gather, but whose fear and apathy reveal a faith that still doubts. The preacher held the key to the entire sermon in v.17 and walked past it. An exegetical moment on "some doubted" — and then Christ's commissioning of doubting worshippers — would have addressed the congregation's fear and apathy with far more pastoral force than the four-excuse taxonomy.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (4)

1. **Build the sermon's spine on v.18 before v.19.** The sermon needs a structural reset: begin with "all authority has been given to me" as the theological foundation. The command to go is not primarily a guilt-producing imperative — it is an authority-backed deployment. A king sends his servants not because they are adequate but because he is sovereign. This changes the entire emotional register of the sermon from shame-based to authority-grounded.

2. **Use Matthew 28:17 ("some doubted") as the pastoral bridge.** Develop this verse to address the fearful and apathetic directly: these are the people Jesus commissions. He does not wait for perfect faith before he sends. This is both exegetically responsible and pastorally precise — it addresses the doubters in the room without letting them off the hook.

3. **Complete and sharpen the conclusion.** The sermon needs an actual final section. The Piper remedies should culminate in a direct address to the congregation: "Here is what I am asking you to do this week." Name one or two specific, concrete actions. Then close with the weight of the passage — Christ's authority and promise ("I am with you always, to the end of the age," v.20, which the sermon also entirely omits).

4. **Relocate and develop the Bridges quote as theological foundation, not practical step.** The gospel content of the Bridges quote is the most important material in the sermon. It should not be buried as Remedy #2. It should be the bridge between the exposition of Christ's authority (indicative) and the call to evangelize (imperative). The congregation needs to understand that the gospel is powerful *before* they are told to share it.

5. **Trim the Jupiter/astrophysics section to two sentences.** The God-is-sovereign-in-creation argument for overcoming fear is legitimate. But the detail level (lap times for planetary orbits, Newsweek article, solar system positioning) overwhelms the sermon and dissipates pressure at the worst moment. Two sentences maximum; then return to the resurrection.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** This sermon is not cold orthodoxy — it has pastoral warmth and genuine concern for lost people. The problem is not dead orthodoxy but incomplete preaching: the gospel is gestured at rather than proclaimed, and the sermon does not arrive at its own conclusion.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
There are moments of genuine pastoral burden in this sermon — particularly in the apathy section — but the manuscript does not carry the eternal gravity the text demands; the risen Christ, holding all authority, commissioning doubting worshippers to reach all nations, deserves a sermon that trembles under that weight, and this one is still learning to stand up under it.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Matthew 28:16–20 is a command-and-promise passage with a strong Christological foundation. The rubric fits it well: Exposition (was the authority declaration handled?), Gospel Centrality (was the risen Christ proclaimed as sufficient?), Conscience and Exhortation (were the doubters addressed and the obedient summoned?). No genre strain. The rubric does not bend here — it simply reveals that the sermon addressed the passage's surface imperative while bypassing its theological roots and its pastoral promise ("I am with you always"). The evaluative categories work as designed.
