## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Raw and Warm "But God" Moment — Homiletically Unfinished but Pastorally Honest**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A brief, unpolished post-VBS address on Ephesians 2 that succeeds in naming the human condition with striking biblical comprehensiveness and in lifting the grace of God with genuine warmth, while remaining structurally incomplete, gospel-thin at the point of explicit articulation, and more conversational summary than fully developed preaching.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with qualification**

- **Scopus Test**: Pass, loosely. The sermon's thrust — human sinfulness and depravity addressed by God's grace and mercy in Christ — arises from Ephesians 2, particularly verses 1–5. The "But God" pivot is the text's own turn. However, the sermon is more a survey of the chapter's anthropological content than an exposition of a single main point from a defined passage.
- **Authority Test**: Pass. The preacher does not soften the text's assessment of human sin or the unilateral nature of God's grace. The list of human depravity from Ephesians is delivered with candor, not apology.

*Rubric Fit Note applies here — see item 17 below.*

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Multiple Flags Present — Score Capped at 69**

- **Flag #1 — No clear gospel presentation (partial)**: The sermon gestures toward the gospel ("God made us alive together with Christ," "the faith we needed for salvation") but never explicitly articulates justification, the atonement, the necessity of repentance and faith, or the finished work of Christ on the cross. The address ends with "All of that starts with the gospel though" — which reads as an acknowledgment that the gospel still needs to be presented, not as its presentation. This is a significant gap, particularly given the VBS context where newly-interested children and parents may be present.
- **Flag #5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon explains the text's anthropology and celebrates God's grace, but it never calls the congregation to a specific response. No "therefore" moment. No summons to believe, repent, trust, or act. The Billy Sunday quote is energizing, but it is motivational rather than exhortatory — the congregation is amused and encouraged, not summoned. The sermon concludes mid-thought.
- **Flag #4 — Application is largely generic**: The anthropological content from Ephesians is assembled but not applied to specific heart-conditions in the room. The "ever felt like no matter what you did you just couldn't live up to what was expected?" line is the closest the sermon comes to conscience-level application — and it is brief.

Score cap: **69**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 11/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (5/10)*: The sermon draws genuinely from Ephesians 2, and the catena of sinful conditions is a legitimate (if impressionistic) survey of what Paul says about humanity apart from Christ. However, the sermon does not track Paul's argument — it surveys his vocabulary. The logic of Ephesians 2:1–10 (the movement from death to life, the demonstration of God's grace to coming ages) is not traced. Verses 6–10 (seated with Christ, saved by grace through faith, not of works) receive only a glancing treatment.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5)*: The BF&M framework provides a doctrinal grid, but Ephesians 2's place within the letter (following the prayer of Ephesians 1 and preceding the Jew-Gentile unification of Ephesians 2:11–22) is not developed. The sermon does not locate the passage within its book or redemptive-historical context.
- *Exegetical Clarity (2/5)*: The list-based approach — drawing phrases from across the whole of Ephesians — is accessible but imprecise. The method is more impressionistic than exegetically rigorous. The "filthy rags" reference is from Isaiah 64:6, not Ephesians — a citation error that conflates texts without acknowledgment.
- *Logical Faithfulness (2/5)*: The FCF is partially identified (we are unable to meet God's standard due to our sin nature). The logic of grace is stated but not demonstrated from the text itself.

**Category 3 — Gospel Centrality & Force: 6/15**

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**

Convicted by the list (genuinely). Warmed by the grace turn (genuinely). But not comforted in the full sense — because the comfort that comes from the gospel's specific promises (justification, adoption, union with Christ) is not presented. The hearer is moved without being fully anchored.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The catena of depravity from Ephesians is a genuine pastoral strength.** Reading an accumulated list of what Paul says about humanity apart from Christ — drawn from across the letter — creates a cumulative weight that is convicting without being haranguing. The congregation feels the gravity of the human condition before the grace arrives. This is the right rhetorical order, and the execution is more effective than it may appear on the page.

2. **The "But God" pivot is instinctively well-constructed.** The repetition — "We are licentious, But God. We are deceitful, But God..." — is a natural homiletical anaphora that builds toward grace. This is the sermon's best preaching moment and the most memorable passage in the manuscript. It demonstrates genuine instinct for how to make truth land.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The sermon is incomplete — it ends mid-promise.** "All of that starts with the gospel though" is the sermon's last line. This is not a conclusion; it is a transition that leads nowhere. The sermon names the disease (human depravity), begins to name the physician (God's grace), and then stops before prescribing the cure (the gospel). A sermon that ends by acknowledging the gospel has not yet been presented has not finished. (Final line.)

2. **The gospel is not proclaimed.** The atonement, justification, repentance, faith — none of these are present. "God made us alive together with Christ" is stated but not explained. "The faith we needed for salvation" is mentioned but not opened. Given the VBS context — where children and families may be present who have been introduced to the idea of salvation for the first time this week — this is a critical omission. This congregation needed to hear what the gospel is, not just that grace exists.

3. **The exhortation is absent.** There is no "therefore" moment. The congregation is told about grace and encouraged to fight sin with the Billy Sunday quote — but they are never summoned to a specific response. What should they do, believe, or pursue as they walk out? The sermon leaves this unanswered.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not recognize how much the VBS context shapes the need of this message. The congregation present — parents of VBS children, volunteers who have been immersed in gospel content all week, possibly children themselves who raised their hands at an invitation on Wednesday night — needs something specific: the gospel explained clearly, assurance addressed, next steps named. The address is calibrated for a general Sunday morning audience rather than for this specific post-VBS gathering, which had a particular pastoral opportunity that was not fully seized.

There is also a possible blind spot about the difference between celebrating grace and proclaiming the gospel. Ephesians 2:4–6 is grace celebrated. But the gospel — the content of what Christ did, why, for whom, and how it is received — extends through verses 7–10 and beyond. The sermon stops at the emotional warmth of "But God" without pressing through to the doctrinal substance of how God accomplished what "But God" describes.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sheer length and accumulation of the depravity list creates an impression of expository thoroughness. Because so many Pauline categories are named, the sermon feels like it has done substantial work with the text. But the list is a survey of vocabulary, not an exposition of Paul's argument. Ephesians 2's logical movement — from death in sin (1–3) to spiritual resurrection in Christ (4–6) to seated with Christ in the heavenlies (6) to the display of grace to future ages (7) to salvation by grace through faith not works (8–10) — is largely absent. The sermon reaches for the emotional impact of the list without working through the text's own logic.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Ephesians 2:8–10 — "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast" — is the clearest gospel proclamation in the entire chapter, and it does not appear in this sermon. These verses would have provided the explicit gospel articulation the sermon needs, grounded the "faith we needed for salvation" line in the text itself, and given the congregation the specific promise to hold. These three verses are the natural conclusion of the "But God" movement, and their absence is the sermon's most consequential missed opportunity.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Trim the opening acknowledgments significantly.** The extended thanks for VBS volunteers (approximately one-third of the sermon) delays the preaching and sets a tone of informality that is hard to recover from homiletically. One sentence of thanks, then to the text. Honor the volunteers — but serve the congregation by getting to the Word.

2. **Develop Ephesians 2:8–10 as the sermon's explicit gospel declaration.** After the "But God" turn, do not stop at verse 6. Move to verses 8–10: "For by grace you have been saved through faith — and this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God." This is the text's own gospel articulation. Let it speak. Explain "grace," explain "faith," explain "not of yourselves." This completes the promise made by the last line.

3. **Add a direct exhortation distinguishing hearers.** For the newly-converted child: "If you prayed this week and meant it, here is what that means going forward." For the unconvinced parent: "If you have watched your child respond and wondered whether it is real — here is the question for you." For the volunteer who has given 60 hours: "The same grace that sent you to serve is the grace that will sustain you." Name specific people in the room and speak to them distinctly.

4. **Replace the Billy Sunday quote with an explicit exhortatory call.** The Billy Sunday quote functions as a punchline, which is a missed opportunity at the sermon's climactic moment. Replace or supplement it with a direct call: "You have seen this week what it looks like when someone comes to faith. You have seen three children step from death to life. Now I ask you: have you? And if you have, are you living like it — or have you made peace with the things Paul lists here?" That is an exhortation. The Billy Sunday quote is entertainment.

5. **Define "the gospel" in the closing line or remove the line.** "All of that starts with the gospel though" must either be followed by the gospel, or removed. As a final line it is a promissory note the sermon does not pay. If time is short, simply close with Ephesians 2:8–10 read slowly, and let the text speak. The congregation will leave with the gospel ringing in their ears.
