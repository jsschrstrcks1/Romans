# The Posture of a Disciple

**Passage:** Mark 10:32-45 (with Mark 8:34; Luke 9:24-25; Ephesians 2:1-5; 2 Timothy 2:15)
**Type:** Raw transcript / study notes
**Date:** Late 2019 (based on Benny Hinn reference, Sept 2019)

---

## Main Idea

The posture of a follower of Christ is one of submission to God — even to the point of death. Following Christ is about a relationship with Christ, putting Christ's priorities above our own — not about status, wealth, or comfort.

## Theme

Countering the prosperity gospel mindset by examining James and John's misguided request for status immediately after Jesus predicts his death. The call to take up your cross and follow Christ is costly, but the reward is matchless.

---

## Opener

Heard this passage preached in chapel at seminary this week. Conversations this week with people whose concern is about their earthly status, wealth, power, and prestige — either in this life or the next. That is part of what makes the prosperity gospel so intoxicating.

Benny Hinn — one of the greatest supporters of the prosperity gospel — came out and repudiated some of his own teachings (September 2019). He's publicly repented. He's trying to move towards a more biblically centric theology.

Personal illustration: Ephesians 2 — apart from Christ we are dead in our trespasses and sins. It took a couple of years to think through the implications. If we are dead can we follow Christ? If we are dead what choices do we really have?

The prosperity gospel shifts focus away from Christ and onto my comfort, my security, my wealth, my station, my finances. God as a cosmic vending machine — put your quarters in and out pops the sugary sweet treat of your choice. But is that who God is? If your kids treated you as an ATM machine, how would you feel?

## Seams

### Mark 10:32-34 — Jesus foretells his death a third time

Jesus really lays it out in plain terms. He's going to be delivered over to the chief priests and the scribes, condemned to death, delivered to the Gentiles, mocked, spit upon, flogged, and killed. And after three days he will rise.

Note on "chief priests" plural: Under the Torah, the high priesthood was a lifetime hereditary office. Under Roman rule, Roman governors appointed and removed high priests at will, creating multiple living former high priests who retained influence and the title. Both Annas and Caiaphas held the title during Jesus's ministry.

### Mark 10:35-37 — James and John scramble for status

Jesus just said he's going to die, and James and John are scrambling for status. They want to sit at his right and left hand in his glory.

Illustration: You've been to the doctor, found out you have cancer and are going to die in a week. You have a family meeting about it. But the kids don't react with shock or horror or sorrow. They just look at each other and ask who's going to get the house.

These two disciples were focused on status and position. They wanted the prestige. Not too different from the prosperity gospel — which loses sight of Jesus and focuses on what I want. "I" is always in the middle of sin. Anything that elevates what I want over what God tells us is sin.

For God to really be God, his ways have to be above our ways, his thoughts have to be above our thoughts. If they aren't, then the God you are worshipping isn't God. The God you are worshipping is you.

### DL Moody quote

"Moses spent forty years thinking he was somebody, 40 years learning he was nobody, and forty years discovering what God can do with a nobody."

### AW Tozer — *The Pursuit of God*

In Sunday school class going over the book. One of his consistent points: it's not about you, it's about God. Until we can take our eyes off ourselves and look to Christ, we're not really pursuing God, we're pursuing ourselves.

### Mark 10:38 — "You do not know what you are asking"

Jesus's response: "You do not know what you are asking. Are you able to drink the cup that I drink, or to be baptized with the baptism with which I am baptized?"

Jesus says, really? Didn't you just hear what I said about being arrested, flogged, mocked, humiliated, and put to death? Is that what you want? The disciples still didn't get it. "We are able," they say.

### Mark 8:34 — Take up your cross

What becoming a Christian can actually mean — mockery, rejection, danger.

Personal illustration: In Michigan, going door to door with teenagers on a youth missions trip sharing the gospel. Knocked on a guy's door, he pulled a gun and told us to get off his yard — he didn't "need no Jesus junk."

You have to count the cost. Friend's father who was lost — shared the gospel every chance until his dad said "if you say the name of Jesus around me one more time I'll never speak to you again." Friend shared the gospel again the next day. Not sure if they reconciled before his dad's death.

Mark 8:34 (ESV): "And calling the crowd to him with his disciples, he said to them, 'If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me.'"

Notice: Jesus said this both to those who were following him and to those who might — the crowd and his disciples. In the day and age when Jesus said this it would have been extremely shocking. The closest modern parallel: the beheadings of a few years ago — Americans being kidnapped and beheaded on video overseas. Now imagine being told to do that to yourself daily.

Double entendre — Jesus is foreshadowing his own death (he just said for the third time he was about to be betrayed, tried, and put to death). But also a warning that his followers would go through things for Christ's sake. Some were beheaded (Paul, James son of Zebedee), some crucified (Peter — upside down, by tradition), one died in exile on a prison island (John, on Patmos).

### Application — What is our cross today?

Crucifixion isn't a threat anymore. Our cross in our context is different:

- Being the light of the gospel to a neighbor that hates you and goes out of his way to damage you
- Being diligent when we don't feel like it — those kids don't have anyone else that cares, that shares the gospel, that teaches them the Bible
- Bringing grandkids to church even when we don't feel like making the drive
- Being diligent in going to church, giving, bringing food, cleaning — "I'll tell you I forgot how much weight Carol lifted off our shoulders in keeping things clean. Her diligence in cleaning and teaching is a blessing!"
- Running the sound board, singing and practicing, leading worship on Sundays
- Caring for an ill spouse
- Studying the word of God daily, prayer life, sharing the gospel

Woman who stepped up to teach — plagued with sickness, takes chemo to feel a little better — and where was she Thursday night? Teaching kids at church.

Guarding our tongues. "I can't tell you how many times I've wanted to say unchristian things to someone driving in front of me, or tailgating me, or that has stolen from and slandered me." What's more important — my motorcycle that the neighbors broke, or the gospel? "If my motorcycle being broken for two years buys me a chance to tell them or their 4 kids about Christ, then it's a small price to pay to guard my tongue."

Maybe following Christ means being a little late and missing the kickoff for a football game, or missing a game one night a week. If football is more important than hearing the word of God, what message does that send your kids? The lost people around you? Then football is your little-g god.

### GotQuestions.org quote (presented without attribution in sermon)

"'Take up your cross and follow Me' means being willing to die in order to follow Jesus. This is called 'dying to self.' It's a call to absolute surrender. After each time Jesus commanded cross bearing, He said, 'For whoever wants to save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for me will save it. What good is it for a man to gain the whole world, and yet lose or forfeit his very self?' (Luke 9:24-25). Although the call is tough, the reward is matchless."

### John Piper — Christian Hedonism

Finding your joy in the Lord. Everything else in this world can be stolen by moths, rust, or hurricanes. Find your joy in the things that last — in our Lord and Savior.

### Closing — 2 Timothy 2:15

The AWANA verse. At a teacher's request, spent time trying to translate the Greek directly: "Diligently work HARD to serve as one watched, tested and approved by THE God as a workman unashamed."

Altar call. Christians — would God approve of what he saw in you today? For everyone else — today is the day. It's just a matter of turning from your old ways and turning towards Christ. Christ has already done the hardest parts.

### Notes for Future Use
- **Benny Hinn "repentance"** (Sept 2019): Based on a real event, but the sincerity is widely contested. He made similar statements before and returned to prosperity teaching within days. As of 2024, critics (including his nephew Costi Hinn) consider it insincere. Present cautiously if reusing this illustration — do not present as settled fact.
- **"6 different times in the first four books of the Bible"**: The cross-bearing command appears 5 times clearly (Matt 10:38, Matt 16:24, Mark 8:34, Luke 9:23, Luke 14:27). A 6th (Mark 10:21) appears only in KJV tradition — modern translations based on earlier manuscripts omit it. Also: "first four books of the Bible" is an odd phrasing (that would be Genesis–Numbers); the preacher means "the four Gospels." None appear in John.
- **Dhobi people group**: Real caste community in India. However, the total Dhobi population across sub-groups is approximately 12 million (Joshua Project), not "about a million." The "not a single one" claim overstates it — Joshua Project lists 0.02% evangelical (~2,400 people). They are classified as unreached, but "zero" is inaccurate.
- **"Corpse" / "literally dead"** (Eph 2): The ESV says "dead in the trespasses and sins." The word "corpse" does not appear. "Literally" is the preacher's theological emphasis. The Greek *nekros* means "dead" and can imply a corpse, but the ESV simply renders "dead."

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Congregationally specific cross-bearing applications.** The list in "What is our cross today?" — the cancer patient teaching through chemo, the broken motorcycle and guarded tongue, the football game as false god — is the sermon's genuine strength. These are real, costly, and grounded in actual community life. This is Puritan applicatory specificity in practice.
2. **Prosperity gospel critique is exegetically valid and well-placed.** The contrast between James and John's request and Jesus's passion prediction is the text's own irony. The ATM/vending-machine-God illustration is sharp and memorable.
3. **Total depravity grounded in Ephesians 2.** The use of Eph 2:1, 5 to undercut the self-focused framework of prosperity theology is theologically sound and well-integrated as the opener's doctrinal foundation.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Mark 10:45 is entirely absent.** "The Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many" — the passage's own climax, its most explicitly soteriological statement — receives zero treatment. The sermon builds a cross-shaped discipleship call without anchoring it in Christ's own cross. This is the text's heartbeat. Its absence is not a minor gap; it is the sermon's structural wound.
2. **The gospel is a placeholder, not a proclamation.** The altar call ("Christ has already done the hardest parts") presupposes the gospel without presenting it. An unbeliever attending this service would not know what Christ did, why he did it, what their problem is, or how to respond with genuine faith. This triggers Fatal Flaw Flag 1.
3. **The sermon has two frames that never fully integrate.** The prosperity gospel critique (opener through Mark 10:38) and the cross-bearing application (Mark 8:34 through close) are both valid, but they are parallel tracks rather than a unified argument. A clear thesis sentence stated early would allow both to serve one master point.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that by skipping Mark 10:45, he has removed the very ground on which cross-bearing discipleship stands. The call to self-denial is not self-generating motivation — it is a response to Christ's prior self-giving. Without "a ransom for many," the call to take up the cross can read as moral challenge rather than gospel response. The sermon calls people to the shape of the cross without first showing them what happened on it.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The application section feels strong — and the specificity is genuinely good. But the strength of the applications masks the absence of the gospel foundation. A congregation can leave feeling challenged, encouraged, and even convicted about their cross-bearing without having heard the gospel that makes cross-bearing possible and meaningful. Feeling pastorally warm is not the same as being gospel-fed.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Mark 10:45 is the passage's own answer to every question the sermon raises. Why should we not seek status? Because the Son of Man didn't. Why can we deny ourselves? Because he gave himself as a ransom for many. The word "ransom" (Greek: *lytron*) is the most explicit ransom/substitution language in the Synoptic Gospels. The sermon preaches the shadow of the cross from verse 38 while missing the substance of the cross in verse 45. A five-minute treatment of that verse would have transformed this sermon from an application lecture into a gospel proclamation.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach Mark 10:45.** Anchor the entire cross-bearing call in the "ransom for many." The disciples wanted to sit at his right and left — in Mark 15:27, two criminals hang at his right and left at Golgotha. The sermon could pivot: the glory James and John sought, Christ won through exactly the death he predicted. Make that turn.
2. **Replace the altar call placeholder with actual gospel content.** Before the call to respond, state clearly: (a) the problem — sin separates us from God and incurs his just wrath; (b) the solution — Christ's death as a ransom, bearing what we deserved; (c) the resurrection as vindication; (d) the call — repent and trust Christ alone. "Christ has already done the hardest parts" is a comma, not a proclamation.
3. **State the thesis once, early, and let both frames serve it.** Something like: "Mark 10 exposes our instinct for status and comfort — and then shows us the only one who has the right to demand everything from us, precisely because he gave everything for us." This integrates the prosperity gospel critique and the cross-bearing application under one roof.
4. **Develop the "cup" language in Mark 10:38.** Jesus's cup is Gethsemane's cup — the cup of divine wrath. James and John said they could drink it; they could not. Jesus drank it. That connection, made in one minute, would illuminate both the seriousness of discipleship and the uniqueness of Christ's sacrifice.
5. **Remove or reframe the Benny Hinn opener.** The corrections log is right — this is contested and dated. It makes the sermon reactive rather than expository. Open with the text's own drama (Jesus predicting his death for the third time; disciples immediately asking about positions of honor) — that irony is more gripping than a TV preacher's contested repentance.
