## Verification Flags

- [x] **"the battle is yours, but prepare your horses any way"** — Original transcript conflated Proverbs 21:31 ("The horse is made ready for the day of battle, but the victory belongs to the LORD") with 2 Chronicles 20:15 ("the battle is not yours but God's"). **FIXED** — corrected to reference Proverbs 21:31 with proper wording.
- [x] **John Owen quote** — "until you have prayed you can do no more" was attributed to John Owen in the original transcript. This quote is more commonly attributed to **John Bunyan**: "You can do more than pray after you have prayed, but you cannot do more than pray until you have prayed." **FIXED** — changed attribution to John Bunyan with fuller quote. [FLAG: Even the Bunyan attribution is difficult to trace to a specific published work. Verify before citing from pulpit, or present as "attributed to Bunyan."]
- [ ] **Stephen Davey definition of worry** — "lack of faith in God." Davey's sermon "Killing Anxiety" (Philippians 4:6-7) from Wisdom for the Heart teaches a closely related idea (worry strangles faith), but the exact phrasing "lack of faith in God" may be the preacher's summary rather than a direct Davey quote. [FLAG: Consider saying "as Stephen Davey has taught" rather than "as defined by" to avoid implying a direct quote.]
- [x] **Tertullian quote** — "the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church" — Traditional attribution, broadly accepted. ## Sermon

Goodmorning and welcome to lakeview fellowship church. I hope you've brought your bibles this morning, if you have please open with us to philippians chapter 4:6-7.

While youre turning there I want to talk about worry. I want to make a distinction up front. Worry as defined by Stephen Davey is lack of faith in God. So that's an internal thing. Right? If we are worried its up here in our head, and down here in our heart. Anxiety is our bodies physical expression of the worry in our mind. Of a lack of faith on a given issue. When our heart, or head says "God can't take care of this" we are worrying, and that causes us anxiety.

I'm a worrier. I try not to let my worries out too much in the middle of things. Gotta lead with confidence, but in my head its sort of like in the locker room after a football game. "Evans man, you needed to be a half step faster", "Brady, if you had just looked a little east you'd have seen Gronk open, and that would have been a touch down". You know what I mean? I revisit things play by play to try and prevent issues from re-occuring to the extent that I'm able, and to try and make things better. Good football teams, even when they win get scolded by the coach, and lectures on how to improve. So I worry. I heard someone say one time that worry is necessary. We must worry or we repeat the failures of the past. We create the same problems for ourselves over and over and over. If we don't worry we are doomed before we start. But the problem with that is that worry is lack of faith in God.

Any time we try and say its important to worry about things, and I know you people, I know someone of you do just this. Men and women both. Moms especially. I'm going to tell a story on my mom real quick. I don't think she will mind, because its typical of mothers, and its typical of how we as people fail in the area of worry.

This one time my sister called her on her way home from work. Mom asked her what she had planned, and Ellisha said, oh you know the normal things. Then the phone cut out. Ellisha continued, grocery shopping, so I can get laundry soap, then laundry, and then after that I'll come by with some hors d'oeuvres before dinner. I'll see you around 7.

Mom didn't hear all that. She just knew Ellisha got of work at 5 and if everything else goes ok, and she didn't make any stops, then Ellisha could be home as early as 6. By 6:15 mom was convinced Ellisha was dead on the side of the road, having been burned to death in a huge fireball of a car accident, and the vultures were picking her bones clean. Ten, twenty, fifty calls later, and moms in the car by 6:30 driving over to Ellisha's house looking for her. Ellisha was still of course at the grocery store, and oblivious to all this.

We worry about the things and people we love. We do! But in that moment our worry says God is not capable of taking care of this thing, this person, this situation in my life. And that right there is where it becomes sinful. I can see on my dads face he's thinking about the scripture that says the horse is made ready for battle, but the victory belongs to the Lord (Proverbs 21:31). Yes! When you face a situation that could cause you to worry, what should you do? Where should you turn with your worry? If you keep things in your own hands, you end up with anxiety because realistically you and I will never be enough on our own to over come every situation we encounter. But with God you and God are the majority. John Bunyan said it this way "You can do more than pray after you have prayed, but you cannot do more than pray until you have prayed." To do less than to give that worry to God, to let it consume you says "I know better, or I think I can do better than God can in this situation". Or more succinctly worry says: "I don't trust you, not with this God". I can only trust my self with this.

Now we know we are all broken people right? Is there anyone here that has it all together? If so raise your hand? Anyone? Of course not. No one, if we are honest has it all together. We can barely manage the things that exist in space and time right around us. In fact were probably better at being the Monday morning quarterback in our own lives – that is after the game, saying we "coulda shoulda woulda and maybe we'd have won" – than we are in the moment of the situation.

Hah. We had a situation the other night. You guys know we have sheep, and we have dogs. Prey animals, and predators. Dogs can eventually be trained to do about anything you can imagine. But puppies don't come with the full set of instructions. Puppies, and sometimes if you take in an adult rescue, it takes them a while to understand you don't want them eating your other pets right? So Wednesday night I'm at dinner with some of the guys, and I get a text message "all the dogs are loose and gone, all the sheep are in the canal, I'm leaving now. Gregs in a tent". Turned out to be full of mistakes thanks to voice to text. You know how well that works. Any way. I hop up, and get my bill, grab my leftovers and take off. I get part way home, and I finally get a hold of one of the kids who tells me everything is fine, only two sheep are out, and one of them is already on the kayak. Only one dog was loose, and shes tied up now. No worries. So under those terms, because of my understanding of the situation which was based on the limitations of a ten year old child I turned around and went back to dinner.

Come to find out, two of the three dogs were, out, one got out twice I think, half the sheep were in the canal at two different points, and they really needed me. But I am finite, and operating under an imperfect perspective with an incomplete amount of information. Like the child in my story.

Any way, lets read God's word for this morning. Phil 4:4-7

I love that it starts with the command to rejoice. In Greek like in many languages repeating a word adds weight. Kids do it in our language too. If your child says "Momma I'm Really really really really hungry" you know that they see it as being vitally important that they eat RIGHT now! Pauls doing that. He's repeating a command twice in a row to add weight. He doesn't make it a request, in the Greek it's a command. Rejoice. Rejoice. But not just in the things of the earth. Earthly joys are fleeting and easily ruined. Your new truck can break down, your puppy get run over by a truck, your husband or wife can let you down. These things happen don't they! We've all seen things happen. Our joy here is incomplete. Its fragile, and is a dim picture of the joy to come in eternity. So when we are feeling anxious, we need to refocus on the Lord. Refocus on the source of our joy. The eternal fountain of joy with whom we will spend eternity. The one that can't be run over, break down or disappoint.

I've said it before, but I've been having to remind myself of it again here lately, we need to guard our hearts with thanksgiving. It leads to gladness. This isn't a request or suggestion.

Paul, in his letters, recounts the list of things he's been through – shipwrecked, snake bit, attacked with stones, and left for dead, had to escape over the side of the city walls in a basket at night (2 Corinthians 11:24-33, Acts 28:3-5), and yet he still finds ways to rejoice, and so when he commands us to rejoice, he knows how hard it can be, and has indeed had to rejoice as an innocent man held in prison. He knows how to rejoice when his health is bad, when all of the circumstances in his life conspire to steal his joy, he rejoices. He rejoices when things go south interpersonally with some of his co-laborers. He rejoices when people die, and he rejoices when things are hard.

It strikes me that when we get in trouble is when we turn our focus inward. I heard someone call it navel-gazing. When we look at our self, and our circumstances, when we are focused on "What I want, what I think, especially what I feel". I had this conversation with my cousin this week. This cousin had said something to the effect of you think you have made things hard for me, but just you wait God's going to make it right and then you'll see. You'll see. But here is the thing. There are times when God allows injustices to go on, and for us to experience them for our whole life! Look at the reprehensible issue of slavery for a moment. Many slaves were born slaves, lived their life as a slave, and died a slave. That injustice was never solved in their life time. But God was doing something in them, and in those around them. God was using their tragedy for good. Tertullian said the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church. I fully believe the tears of the Christian help water that seed. So even when we can't understand, even when we can't see what God's doing, we know God is doing something and that we can trust him. We just need to lift our eyes up, and take them off ourselves, and put them back on Christ. The founder and perfecter of our faith (Hebrews 12:2). Its only then that we can really rejoice in adversity.

I think back over some of the songs that were sung by the slaves as they worked in the field. Horrible conditions, and yet they could sing with joy of the day they would leave this earth and spend eternity with Christ. That's what the song "Swing Low, Sweet Chariot" is all about.

This life is not all there is. This life isn't what we live for. This life is a boot camp for eternity. And God commands us to rejoice, and not to be anxious about anything. If something threatens to make us anxious, rejoice, and then take your concerns before God vs 5 and 6.

What do we do after that. Do we sit there, in our own heads and fret and fuss. There is a lot to be concerned about in our world today. The corona virus, the vaccine, the direction of our nation away from God, financial concerns, health concerns, concerns about our families, and loved ones.

So when you're afraid because of covid, what should you do? Rejoice. Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

So when you're afraid of the covid vaccine. What should you do? Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

So when you're worried about your kid, whose struggling, and hurting? Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

What about if you get the diagnosis of cancer? That's a horrible diagnosis. But what should you do? Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

What about if it seems like the world is winning in the government, and immoral things are becoming law? Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

What if it seems like your finances are in trouble, and you're not sure where your money's going to come from? Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

What if you are around someone who doesn't wear a mask? Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

What if you actually catch covid? Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

What about all this sabre rattling with Iran and other middle eastern countries. Is it ok to worry then? What should we do? Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God Through prayer with supplication and thanksgiving. Basically vs 6.

There is no part of scripture that says be anxious about nothing, except you know, when you might get sick. Its not an excuse to worry, to be anxious. Its just not. Scripture says so.

And best of all there are some specific promises for those who do manage to rejoice in times of hardship. Someone read vs 7 for me, nice and loud for facebook.

God will give you peace. He will guard your heart. You will be set free from the bondage of worry through the gospel. Rather than being a slave to fear and anxiety, we can have freedom in Christ. Because for you and I, if we die? So what? It only gets better from here. For the Christian this world is the only hell we will ever experience. This is the worst it gets. Even if you've had a relatively good life, this world is the worst that it gets. Eternity, is better, and for far longer.

I love my dogs, and I'd be sad to leave them behind if I ended up in heaven. But I'm reminded of a passage in Matthew 6. In fact turn to Matthew 6:25-34 with me.

(wait)

This is Jesus speaking. He's addressing anxiety, and worry directly. I'm going to apply it to the pandemic here too. It talks about health, and adding days or hours to your life. So I think the pandemic is in view here also. But notice too how he talks about the birds, and lilies. God promises to take care of them, and he'll take care of my animals also when that day comes. Paul says to live is Christ, but to die is gain (Philippians 1:21). I've said before when we have been in eternity for 11 billion years, we aren't going to remember the little trifles of life. We won't remember how much we hate cancer, how much our friends let us down, we wont remember the heartache of loss, and the disappointments in life. But I want to expand that today. I don't think its going to take us 11 billion years to get to that point. Scripture says we would rather be away from the body and at home with the Lord (2 Corinthians 5:8). So when we take that last breath, the very next instant we are in the presence of our saviour. I think that will be such a glorious moment, that we won't need 11 billion years for all of those sadnesses, and trifles, and disappointments, for those losses to lose their sting. Seeing Christ in Glory seated at the right hand of the father will be so amazing that I think in that moment those other things will be washed away. Like dirt being sprayed off with a hose. We have to live with eternity in view, we have to live our lives focused on our savior. We have to have faith, not fear. All we have to do is follow Christ, and he will take care of the rest.

Its like a situation I had with my dogs last night. I had brought home two hoof treats from tractor supply. One for joy and one for cooper. When I brought cooper home from my parents, he ignored his and went straight for joys. Joy was turning inside out with anxiety. She wanted her treat, it was only fair after all.

I patiently told her to wait till after cooper goes to bed, and I'll give her his. She didn't really understand, because shes still a dog. But at the end of the day she got the better treat by waiting. That's whats in store for us.

That however starts with the Gospel.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title

**The Gospel Promised but Never Delivered: A Warm Pastoral Sermon on Anxiety That Ends One Sentence Too Early**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A personally-grounded, pastorally warm sermon on Philippians 4:4-7 that correctly identifies worry as a failure of faith, develops the command to rejoice with genuine textual observation and vivid congregational illustration, and then closes with the single most damaging final line a sermon can have — "That however starts with the Gospel" — without ever preaching it.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**PASS.**

The sermon's main point — *Anxiety is a failure of faith; bring your worries to God in prayer and rejoice in the Lord who is trustworthy and sovereign* — is demonstrably the main point of Philippians 4:4-7. The preacher stands under the text's commands rather than above them. Paul's imperatives (rejoice, do not be anxious, pray with thanksgiving) govern the sermon's argument. The text controls the structure.

**Berean Gate: PASS**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**ONE FATAL FLAG — score capped at 69:**

1. **No clear gospel presentation** — "That however starts with the Gospel" is the sermon's final line. It names the gospel as the foundation of the entire message but does not preach it. This is not an oversight; it is an incomplete sermon. The cross, the death of Christ for sinners, justification by faith, the substitutionary atonement — none are stated. The sermon is built on a foundation that is promised but never laid before the congregation. A hearer who does not know the gospel leaves having heard excellent applications but not the ground on which those applications stand.

*Note: All other potential flags are either not triggered or only marginally:*
- Application is specific and congregationally grounded: no "generic application" flag.
- Exhortation is present (the repeated "Be anxious about nothing, Take your requests to God") though formulaic by the close.
- The sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers — no cognitive load flag.
- Christ is present as the object of faith and the source of peace, though his finished work is assumed rather than proclaimed.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** — More of my anxiety and God's trustworthiness. Christ as person and Savior is not the sermon's center, though he is present as object of trust and source of joy.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** — Ideas about Him, well-illustrated and pastorally warm. The sermon describes God's trustworthiness rather than encountering His presence.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** — The covid litany and specific scenarios produce genuine application-pressure. The congregation is pointed toward prayer as the right response to anxiety. There is comfort available here; conviction of worry-as-unbelief is also present. The sermon does not merely inform — but it does not reach the level of proclamation.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Worry defined as unbelief, not weakness** (line 29): "Worry is lack of faith in God" — and the development of this as "I don't trust you, not with this God" — is theologically accurate and conscience-pressing. This is not therapy language; it is sin language. The diagnosis is correct.
2. **The Phil 4:6 application litany** (lines 61-79): Applying the same biblical command to covid, vaccines, cancer, finances, geopolitics, and more — without softening it for any case — is exactly the kind of specific, text-governed application that Perkins would recognize. "There is no part of scripture that says be anxious about nothing, except you know, when you might get sick" is a line with sermonic force.
3. **"Find your gospel appointment"** (lines 37, 77): The idea that a broken truck or a ladder injury may be a God-arranged encounter for gospel witness is theologically rich and congregation-specific. It re-frames suffering as missional rather than merely providential — and gives the congregation a specific thing to look for in their week.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **"That however starts with the Gospel" — the sermon's most disqualifying line** (final line): This is not a weakness in the preacher's gospel conviction; it is an incomplete sermon. The gospel is the foundation of everything preached here, and the preacher knows it — but the congregation hears it named, not proclaimed. The peace that passes understanding (Phil 4:7) is the promise secured by Christ's blood; that blood must be named.
2. **God's peace in Phil 4:7 is not connected to the cross** — "God will give you peace. He will guard your heart. You will be set free from the bondage of worry through the gospel" (line 83) correctly names the gospel as the mechanism of peace, but then does not explain it. The peace of Philippians 4:7 is "through Christ Jesus" — and Christ Jesus crucified for sinners is the ground. This connection is absent.
3. **No redemptive-historical awareness** — The sermon treats Phil 4:4-7 as a timeless wisdom text rather than a document written from a Roman prison by a man who expected to die. Paul's joy in the face of execution is the sermon's most powerful illustration of the command, but the full weight of Paul's circumstances (discussed in Phil 1:12-14, which the preacher knows from the Phil 1 sermon) is not brought to bear here.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not fully see how close the sermon comes to being excellent, and therefore how significant the missing gospel close is. The Phil 4:6 litany is strong. The illustrations are strong. The conscience application is strong. The sermon is one section away from being a 78-point sermon. The gospel close is not a minor addition — it is the sermon's entire foundation being finally stated. Preaching the death and resurrection of Christ at the close would not feel tacked-on; it would feel like the moment the whole sermon was building toward.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Phil 4:6 litany — the repeated application of the same command to scenario after scenario — *feels* like thorough application and feels like the sermon's structural strength. And it is genuinely effective. But the very repetition masks the fact that the sermon's theological center (the gospel) is absent. The congregation hears a strong application but no proclamation. Thorough application without proclamation is still a sermon that has not become preaching.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Philippians 4:7 promises "the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds *in Christ Jesus.*" The phrase "in Christ Jesus" is the entire weight-bearing phrase. It ties the peace to the person and work of Christ — it is the peace that comes from being *in him*, which is to say, justified, forgiven, adopted. The sermon quotes the verse but does not open this phrase. Opening "in Christ Jesus" would have been the natural transition to the gospel proclamation the sermon promises but never delivers.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach the gospel before the final line.** Replace "That however starts with the Gospel" with the gospel itself. Even 90 seconds: "Here is why you can bring your anxiety to God. Not because God is generally good, not because prayer is emotionally helpful, but because the Judge of the universe became your advocate. Christ took the sin that made you God's enemy and bore it on the cross. He rose from the dead. And if you are in him — if you have confessed with your mouth and believed in your heart — then there is no condemnation for you. The God you're bringing your anxiety to is not an uncertain judge; He is your Father. That is why Paul can say *do not be anxious*. That is why the peace surpasses understanding."
2. **Open "in Christ Jesus" from Phil 4:7.** This phrase is the key to the whole passage. The peace is not generic; it is covenantal. Three sentences on this phrase would have changed the theological register of the entire sermon.
3. **Add one moment addressing the unbeliever distinctly.** The Phil 4:6 litany applies to believers. But some in the congregation may not be in Christ. One line: "And for those of you who have been trying to manage your anxiety on your own — by positive thinking, by willpower, by new habits — here is why it hasn't worked. You cannot bring your anxiety to a God you don't belong to. Peace requires a relationship. That relationship begins with the gospel."
4. **Shorten the natural analogy cluster.** The caterpillar, astronaut, and birth canal analogies (lines 11) serve the same point; one is enough. The space gained should go to textual development.
5. **Connect Paul's imprisonment context to the passage.** Two sentences: "Paul is not writing this command from a comfortable armchair. He is chained to a Roman guard. He knows what it is to be anxious — and to choose prayer instead. When he says *be anxious for nothing*, he has earned the right to say it."

This passage (Phil 4:4-7) is well-served by this rubric. It is a direct command passage with clear indicatives and imperatives; the rubric's law/gospel diagnostic is exactly the right tool for evaluating whether the imperatives are grounded in the indicatives of grace. The sermon's failure — imperatives floating free of indicatives — is precisely what this rubric is designed to detect. Rubric fit: excellent.
