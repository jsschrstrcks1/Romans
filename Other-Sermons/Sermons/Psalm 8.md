### Opening — The Most Majestic Things in the World

I was reading an article this week about the most majestic things in the world. Lists of ten, a hundred, even ten thousand. I didn't make it all the way through ten thousand. But I got far enough to find one thing I wasn't expecting at the top of somebody's list.

Algae.

I can see it on some of your faces — *algae can't be majestic, Pastor.* You're thinking of the brown stuff that washes up on the shoreline, or the green slime growing in an aquarium you haven't cleaned in too long. But this was different. There's a river in one of the remotest parts of Colombia that nearly dries up at the turn of summer — the water gets so low and so still that the riverbed becomes a canvas. The algae that grows in that almost-still water comes in every color you can imagine. Brown, yes. Green. But also red like fire. Blue like turquoise. Dark blues like the deepest ocean. Yellows like gold. Green like jade. Every color of the rainbow growing in a perfect circle, set in the mouth of a dormant volcano, surrounded by crystal-clear water so still it looks like glass.

That takes your breath away.

I can think of a few other things like that. I've dived at Looe Key down in South Florida. If you've never been, it's the kind of place where you're surrounded by grouper as big as a man and more brightly colored tropical fish than a team of people could count in a day. I've been out west and stood at Arches National Park — those red rock formations that stretch toward heaven and remind you all at once how small you are and how big God is. I've seen Monet's water lilies. I've stood inside Notre Dame in Paris, which by its very design was meant to declare that something exists beyond the human scale. Its sheer scope is beyond anything I can describe. Some friends of mine cheered when it burned, because today Notre Dame is largely a monument to the death of Christianity in France — taken over by the government, turned into a museum of secular humanism. But the building itself doesn't know that. By its very architecture it still shouts that Jesus is Lord. Scripture says even the rocks cry out.

Have you ever seen something so majestic it just took your breath away? I'd ask for examples, but I'm pretty sure most of the husbands here would just nudge their wife.

Here's the thing, though. If you think the majesty of God can be fully summed up by the things He made, your view of that majesty is too small. And if you think it can be summed up by the things *we* make — the cathedrals and the paintings and the concert halls — it's smaller still.

### Illustration — The Most Majestic Thing

I want to tell you about something more majestic than any algae in Colombia, any reef in Florida, or any cathedral in France.

I read a story from David Platt — who served for a time as president of the International Mission Board. He was heading out for a conference, had to leave at three-thirty in the morning to make his flight. Took an Uber. He said he was half asleep when he got in the car, not exactly looking forward to the ride. But if you've ever been in a cab, you know cabbies are among the chattiest people in the world — maybe second only to someone who's been stuck home with small children a few days too long. This driver started asking questions. *Where are you headed? What kind of work do you do?*

David said, "I'm a pastor."

The cab driver — a Muslim man — stopped right there and started weeping.

He told David that he had been having a recurring dream. A tiny baby kept appearing to him every time he closed his eyes. And this baby would speak to him in clear, plain language — not like an infant, like an adult — telling him he needed to go find someone to talk to about Jesus. Muslims have a deep conviction that God would never truly condescend to come to earth and live among us. A real god wouldn't want to do that, they believe.

David said the adrenaline hit and he was suddenly wide awake. And right there in that car, at four in the morning, he shared the gospel with that cab driver. That man — a Muslim, weeping, haunted by a dream he couldn't explain — became a believer in Jesus Christ. He was later baptized and became a member of that church.

That transformation — from death to life, from darkness to light, from enemy to son — is one of the most majestic things in the world. Who else has authority over death itself? Who else can take a man whose eyes are closed and open them? Who else?

If you want to talk about the majesty of God, you don't start with a river in Colombia. You start there.

### Point 1 — Two Names for One God

I want you to notice something in the very first line. Look at your Bible. *O LORD, our Lord.* Does your translation have something similar?

Look at the first word — LORD, in all caps. You've probably noticed that in your Bible before. What does that mean? When you see LORD spelled out in capital letters, it's the translator's way of rendering the Hebrew name *Yahweh*. You may have heard that word before. It's the name God gave Himself when Moses asked. *I am that I am* — Yahweh. When He says *I am the God of your fathers, of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob* — Yahweh. When He parts the Red Sea and leads a nation of slaves into freedom — Yahweh. When Scripture speaks of God creating everything — Yahweh.

The name Yahweh should call to mind every time God has showed up. Every time He pulled through. For the patriarchs. For Israel. For the church across history. It's His personal name. Israel eventually treated the name as too holy to say aloud and used another word as a substitute. But Yahweh — that's God's name given for intimacy. The way a bride can call her husband by his name and nobody else can use it that way. The way a child can call his father *Daddy* — if I called your father Daddy, that would be strange. But you can call your father that. It's personal. Intimate. Yahweh is God's intimate name for Himself.

Now look at the second word — *our Lord*, just a capital L. In the Hebrew that word is *Adonai*. It means something different. Adonai is a title. A formal role. Like *King* or *President*. You can say "the President" and everyone knows who you mean — that's the role. In ancient Israel, if you said "the king," you knew exactly what you meant. Adonai is God's formal title. He is the Lord. The ruler. The one we are called to follow. Literally, it carries the sense of "Your Majesty."

So here is what the Psalmist is saying in the very first breath: *Yahweh — the intimate, personal God who knows our name — is also Adonai — the majestic sovereign ruler of everything that exists.* He is both at once. The one who calls Himself our God is also the Lord of the universe.

Connecting those two words is the word *our*. He is our God and we are His people. Deuteronomy 32:9 says it plainly: *"For the LORD's portion is his people, Jacob his allotted heritage."* The context there is the nation of Israel, but we are grafted into that — Gentiles brought in, made partakers of the promises.

There's a song I've always loved that captures this. You may know it — "How He Loves," written by John Mark McMillan and made famous by the David Crowder Band:

> *God loves like a hurricane, I am a tree*
> *Bending beneath the weight of His wind and mercy*
> *When all of a sudden*
> *I am unaware of these afflictions eclipsed by glory*
> *And I realize just how beautiful You are*
> *And how great Your affections are for me*
>
> *And we are God's portion and He is our prize*
> *Drawn to redemption by the grace in His eyes*
> *If grace is an ocean, we're all sinking*
> *He loves us*
> *Oh, how He loves us*

That's the heart of the Psalm. Yahweh Adonai — the sovereign majesty of the universe — has bound Himself to a people. He has made them His own. *Our* Lord.

### Point 2 — The Question That Levels You

Now look at verses 3-4. Someone read those for me.

> *When I look at your heavens, the work of your fingers,*
> *the moon and the stars, which you have set in place,*
> *what is man that you are mindful of him,*
> *and the son of man that you care for him?*

Now I need someone to open to Hebrews 2:6-9. Let me read it.

> *"What is man, that you are mindful of him, or the son of man, that you care for him? You made him for a little while lower than the angels; you have crowned him with glory and honor, putting everything in subjection under his feet." Now in putting everything in subjection to him, he left nothing outside his control. At present, we do not yet see everything in subjection to him. But we see him who for a little while was made lower than the angels, namely Jesus, crowned with glory and honor because of the suffering of death, so that by the grace of God he might taste death for everyone.*
> — Hebrews 2:6-9

Did you catch that? The writer of Hebrews takes Psalm 8 — a Psalm about the dignity of man — and reads it as a Psalm about *Jesus*. The one made lower than the angels. The one crowned with glory and honor through suffering. The one who tasted death for everyone.

The Psalm starts with a question: *What is man?* It's the right question. What do we bring to the table? Jonathan Edwards put it this way: man contributes to his salvation only the sin that made it necessary. Scripture says the best we have to offer is filthy rags. We couldn't come close on our own to being what and where God wants us to be. We needed help. Desperately. And the Majesty of the universe — Yahweh Adonai — decided to provide it.

### Point 3 — The Majesty That Stoops

He's a King. He's *the* King. He's our Adonai. And for our sake He was born in a barn.

We wouldn't want that for our own children. Even for our kids we want more than an animal stall. It's one thing for a sheep to be born into a stall. But that our King — our Yahweh Adonai — was willing to be born into one for *us*? That's not just majestic. That's astonishing.

Hebrews says He was made lower than the angels. The one who set the moon and the stars in place took on flesh. Became an infant. Needed to be fed. Needed to be taught to walk. The most majestic being in the universe, condescending — *stooping* — to enter creation as a creature.

And He did it because *what is man that you are mindful of him?* That question doesn't have a good answer. We have nothing. We deserved nothing. We brought nothing to the transaction. And yet *we are His portion and He is our prize.*

I was talking to someone recently whose neighbor's father was dying. And when the gospel came up, the response was polite and kind — but noncommittal. Like someone saying, *"I appreciate it, but I've already made other arrangements."* Polite. Not rude. But short-sighted. There are only two destinations after death in Scripture. Only two. And the one that does not include the Lord is a long, long time.

J.C. Ryle wrote in his book *Holiness* that *"there is a common worldly kind of Christianity in which many have what they think is enough — a cheap Christianity that offends no one, requires no sacrifice, costs nothing, and is worth nothing."* He's right. And John MacArthur has said that Christians ought to serve righteousness with exactly the same energy they once served sin — as slaves. You were once a slave to sin. Now you are a slave to Christ. You have been bought with a price. By Yahweh. By Adonai. By our Lord our Lord.

Following Christ is not easy in the sense that it asks nothing of you. God will work in you and draw you away from the things of this world. That process is hard. But the destination — knowing Him, being known by Him — has more in it than anything else you could serve.

---

### Gospel

It all starts, though, with the gospel.

If you're here today and you don't know Him — if you've been polite about Jesus but haven't surrendered to Him — I want you to hear what the Psalmist is saying. The God who made the moon and the stars, who set His glory above the heavens, who is majestic beyond everything in Colombia or South Florida or Paris — that God looked at you. *What is man that you are mindful of him?* He was mindful of you. He cared for you. He sent His Son to be made lower than the angels, to suffer, to die — to taste death for everyone who would come to Him in faith.

The cab driver in the dark at four in the morning, weeping and confused — God was mindful of him. God was after him. God got to him.

He will get to you too, if you'll let Him.

That transformation — from death to life — is the most majestic thing in the world.

Yahweh Adonai. Our Lord our Lord.

*How majestic is your name in all the earth.*
