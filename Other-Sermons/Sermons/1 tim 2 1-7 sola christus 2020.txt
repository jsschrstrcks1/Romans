SERIES CONTEXT

Week 1: Sola Gratia — grace alone. Salvation cannot be earned, bought from the church, or granted
by any man or institution. It comes from God through grace alone.

Week 2: Sola Fide — faith alone. God supplies faith; faith stems in part from knowing God.

Week 3 (this sermon): Solus Christus — Christ alone.

CHRIST IN ALL OF SCRIPTURE

J.C. Ryle writes:

"Christ is to be found — dimly and indistinctly at the beginning, more clearly and plainly in the
middle, fully and completely at the end — but really and substantially everywhere. Many people forget
the Old Testament in its main emphasis points forward to Christ, the Gospels discuss Christ, and the
rest of the New Testament points back to Christ. Christ's sacrifice and death for sinners, and
Christ's kingdom and future glory, are the light we must bring to bear on any book of Scripture we
read. Christ's cross and Christ's crown are the clue we must hold fast — if we would find our way
through Scripture difficulties. Christ is the only key which will unlock many of the dark places of
the Word. Some people complain that they do not understand the Bible. And the reason is very simple.
They do not use the key. To them the Bible is like the hieroglyphics in Egypt. It is a mystery, just
because they do not know and employ the key."

— J.C. Ryle [Note: J.C. Ryle (1816–1900), Bishop of Liverpool. This passage appears in both the
2020 and the later version of this sermon. The specific source (volume, page, or essay title) has
not been confirmed. Consistent with Ryle's expository prefaces, particularly "Expository Thoughts
on the Gospels." Verify and cite before using in print.]

WHY SOLUS CHRISTUS WAS NECESSARY

Martin Luther writes:

"The devil does not intend to allow this testimony about Christ. He devotes all his energy to
opposing it and will not desist until he has struck it down and suppressed it. In this respect, we
humans are weak and stubbornly perverse and are more likely to become attached to saints than to
Christ. Within the papacy they have preached about the service rendered by these beloved saints,
that one ought to rely on their merit."

— Martin Luther [Note: Source in Luther's Works (American Edition, Fortress/Concordia) not
confirmed. The substance is consistent with Luther's writings on justification and the mediation
of Christ. Verify volume and page before citing in print. This quote appears in both the 2020 and
the later version of this sermon.]

The Catholic argument: saints' merit could facilitate one's journey to Christ.

Problems with this argument:
1. We are spiritually dead apart from Christ (cf. Ephesians 2:1–5). The journey to Christ is
   initiated by Christ, not by human effort.
2. "The Gospel AND anything" is dangerous ground. Galatians 1:8, ESV: "But even if we or an angel
   from heaven should preach to you a gospel contrary to the one we preached to you, let him be
   accursed."

[Note: The pastor quotes Galatians 1:8 as "But even if we or an angel from heaven should preach
a gospel other than the one we preached to you, let them be under God's curse!" The ESV reads
"contrary to" (not "other than") and "let him be accursed" (not "let them be under God's curse").
Same paraphrase as the later version. If quoting ESV in print, use the ESV text.]

Luther writes:

"And I, too, believed and preached thus. St. Ann was my idol, and St. Thomas my apostle. I
patterned myself substantially after them. Others ran to St. James and strongly believed and firmly
trusted that, if they conformed, they would receive all they wished and hoped for. Prayers were said
to St. Barbara and St. Christopher in order to avert an early and sudden death, and there was no
uncertainty here. So completely is man by nature bent on renouncing this testimony of John the
Baptist."

— Martin Luther [Note: Same source concern as the first Luther quote. Source unconfirmed.
Appears in both versions of this sermon.]

ILLUSTRATION: SACRÉ-CŒUR, PARIS (2009)

The pastor visited Sacré-Cœur Basilica in Paris in 2009, described as "the highest point in the
city" (the hill is taller than the Eiffel Tower). Additional cost: 6 dollars and 300 steps to reach
the dome.

The pastor says the basilica "was originally going to be dedicated to the heart of Christ, but
ultimately it became dedicated to the sacred heart of Paris."

[CORRECTION — same as the later version: Sacré-Cœur IS dedicated to the Sacred Heart of Jesus
Christ. The name "Sacré-Cœur" literally means "Sacred Heart" (of Jesus). The basilica was built
as a national act of penance following the Franco-Prussian War (1870–71) and the Paris Commune. It
has always been dedicated to Christ's Sacred Heart. The claim that it shifted to "the sacred heart
of Paris" is inaccurate and should be corrected when re-preaching.]

Observation: at the saint/prayer tables inside the basilica, Jesus' table had 3 candles; Mary's
two combined tables were completely covered. The visual point stands regardless of the dedication
error.

THE SOLE MEDIATOR

1 Timothy 2:5: one God, one mediator — the man Christ Jesus. No priest can mediate; no priest can
absolve sin. That is Christ's work alone.

John 14:6, ESV: "Jesus said to him, 'I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to
the Father except through me.'"

[CORRECTION — CRITICAL: The pastor cites this as "John 14:66." John chapter 14 has only 31 verses.
The correct reference is John 14:6. The quoted text matches John 14:6 exactly. This same error
appears in both the 2020 and later version of this sermon. Correct when re-preaching.]

THE THREE ROLES OF CHRIST: PROPHET, PRIEST, AND KING

KING

Kings held court and judged. The pastor cites Solomon's judgment of the two women claiming the same
child (1 Kings 3:16–28). Accurate.

Obama clemency illustration: Obama in 2016 granted clemency to nearly 2,000 individuals, most
imprisoned for drug offenses. The pastor states most of those 2,000 are back in jail.
[Note: The Obama commutation figure is broadly accurate (1,715 commutations in his final year).
The recidivism claim — "most of those 2,000 people are back in jail" — has not been verified.
Do not repeat without a confirmed source.]

PROPHET

A prophet is the mouthpiece of God. Through his life and ministry, Christ pronounced the death of
sin.

PRIEST

Through his life, Christ demonstrated how a Christian should live and pointed all glory to the
Father.

ILLUSTRATION: SOLAR ECLIPSE / TOTALITY

"A few years back we had a solar eclipse." The pastor photographed it through overcast skies, taking
approximately 200 photos. [Note: In 2020, "a few years back" most likely refers to the August 21,
2017 total solar eclipse (the "Great American Eclipse"), which had wide coverage of "totality."]

Unlike eclipse totality (darkness), the totality of Christ brought light — the complete message of
God concerning sin and salvation.

The Catholic church sought to usurp all three roles, attacking: (1) the authority of Scripture,
(2) the central role of Christ by elevating saints, and (3) the sufficiency of Christ's atonement
by placing grace administration in the church.
