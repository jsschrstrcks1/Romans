C. God the Holy Spirit

The Holy Spirit is the Spirit of God, fully divine. He inspired holy men of old to write the Scriptures. Through illumination He enables men to understand truth. He exalts Christ. He convicts men of sin, of righteousness, and of judgment. He calls men to the Saviour, and effects regeneration. At the moment of regeneration He baptizes every believer into the Body of Christ. He cultivates Christian character, comforts believers, and bestows the spiritual gifts by which they serve God through His church. He seals the believer unto the day of final redemption. His presence in the Christian is the guarantee that God will bring the believer into the fullness of the stature of Christ. He enlightens and empowers the believer and the church in worship, evangelism, and service.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
Assessed on the source material only, for what it offers the eventual sermon:

1. **The BFM article is doctrinally rich and well-structured.** It moves through the Spirit's work in a logical sequence: His divine nature, His role in Scripture, illumination, exaltation of Christ, conviction, calling, regeneration, baptism into the Body, sanctification, comfort, gifting, sealing, guarantee, and empowering. This is a comprehensive outline that a sermon could trace and open.

2. **The Scripture reference list is extensive and spans both Testaments.** Texts like John 16:7–14, Romans 8:9–16, 1 Corinthians 12:3–13, and Ephesians 1:13–14 are anchor passages that could each sustain a full sermon. The range from Genesis 1:2 to Revelation 22:17 signals redemptive-historical breadth.

3. **The article's Christocentric framing of the Spirit's work ("He exalts Christ") is the right theological anchor.** Any sermon from this material should begin there — the Spirit's defining ministry is the glorification of the Son (John 16:14), and that is the integrating center that keeps pneumatology from becoming either cessationist minimalism or charismatic drift.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
Assessed as observations about what the eventual sermon will need to overcome:

1. **The BFM article covers too much territory for a single sermon.** It lists at least twelve distinct ministries of the Spirit. A preacher who tries to cover all of them will produce a survey with no depth. The first decision to make is which one or two ministries to anchor the sermon in.

2. **The Scripture reference list, while comprehensive, contains no obvious single governing text.** There is no one passage here that the BFM article is primarily expounding. The preacher will need to choose a governing text — likely John 16:7–14, Romans 8:9–16, or 1 Corinthians 12:3–13 — and let that passage shape the sermon's argument, with other references in support.

3. **Pneumatology is a minefield for application drift.** Without a carefully chosen governing text and a clear Christocentric anchor, a sermon on the Holy Spirit easily drifts toward either cold doctrinal recitation (orthodox but inert) or experiential subjectivism (warm but unmoored). The confessional article guards against the second error; the preacher must guard against the first.

### 11. Blind Spots

### 13. Missed Opportunity
**Not applicable to a source document.** The most significant opportunity the eventual sermon has is John 16:7–14, where Jesus identifies the Spirit's arrival as better for the disciples than His own continued physical presence. This is one of the most stunning pneumatological assertions in Scripture — the Spirit's coming is not a consolation prize but an upgrade — and it has enormous applicatory weight for a congregation that may relate to God primarily through historical-Jesus categories and undervalue the Spirit's present ministry.

### 14. Surgical Fixes
These apply to the sermon that should be built from this material, not to the existing file:

1. **Choose one governing text.** John 16:7–14 is the strongest candidate: it covers conviction, the Spirit's relationship to Christ, and the Spirit's ministry of guiding into truth — all in one passage with a clear argument. Romans 8:9–16 is the second choice if the emphasis is assurance and adoption.

2. **Structure the sermon around the BFM article's own sequence, but only the first three movements**: (a) The Spirit is fully divine — not a force or an influence; (b) The Spirit's work is Christocentric — He glorifies Christ, not Himself; (c) The Spirit's present work in the believer — conviction, regeneration, assurance, sanctification. This is more than enough for one sermon.

3. **Plan the applicatory section before the expositional section.** A sermon on the Holy Spirit must answer: "What should a believer feel and do differently because of what we just said?" The BFM article's phrase "He enlightens and empowers the believer and the church in worship, evangelism, and service" is the application anchor — it should generate three concrete, conscience-pressing uses.

4. **Write a section specifically for the unbeliever.** The BFM article's reference to conviction of sin, righteousness, and judgment (drawn from John 16:8) is one of the most direct pneumatological evangelistic texts in Scripture. Do not let it become a theological fact-point. Press it.
