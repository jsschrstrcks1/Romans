Aggie went back to the US, and the bible college she was planning to attend sent her family to Africa on vacation to meet the people her mother had impacted so heavily. All thanks to the sacrifice of 27 year olds David and svea flood. The way I See it there were at least two kind of people when things get hard. There was David. He focused on the circumstances of life. He focused on the fact his wife died, and that life was hard. That lack of dependence on God made everything more difficult. Even as a missionary the focus of his life was not on the Lord, and so when things grew hard, he grew weary of doing Good. He was tyring to do it all in his own power, and when you do that, you will ineveitibly burn out. Maybe not today, maybe not tomorrow. But as with David, you will eventually hit a circumstance you can no longer bear up under. At that point, you can get bitter – and turn away from God as David did, or turn to the Lord as and depend on his strength like aggie and svea did. 

Svea by contrast lived a life centered around the Lord. So when 5 years of discouragement came down the pipe, she said “its ok Lord, whatever you want from me”. “I know you have me here for a reason, and if this little boy is the reason than praise God”. So she shared the gospel with that little boy twice weekly for all of the time the Lord Gave her. 

In the end she died. But so what? For someone whose life is centered around Christ, death is a promotion. When we die we go on to Glory and live with our Lord and savior, the one our lives have been centered around. To live, as Paul said, is as Christ, but to die is gain. David forgot that. David allowed the difficult things he was going through to shape his thinking. Lord willing we won’t fail so spectacularly and give our children away. But when our lives aren’t built and centered around Christ, we will eventually fail. 

How do you center your life around Christ? It starts with God doing a work in your heart. Apart from God doing a work in your heart you’ll have no interest. I came across a whole bunch of great quotes this week about free will. One good one was from a guy named Toplady – poor kid. Any way, he wrote “a mans freewill cannot on its own even cure a toothache, or a sore finger, and yet madly thinks it is in his power to cure his soul”. For our purposes, it madly thinks it is in his power to persevere alone. 

Spurgeon wrote “free will has sent many men to hell, but never a man to heaven”. God has to do a work in our heart or else we have no interest in the things of God. Our hearts are bent and warped by sin towards sin. 

Martin Luther put it this way: “We are compelled to serve the kingdom of satan until we be liberated” by God. There are only two masters, and unless you’ve been freed by God, you serve the other one by default. However good you think you are, whatever good you’ve done, has been for the glory of our enemy, until God frees you and you have a relationship with him, you have no choice, no ability, no agency to do anything other than what your master desires. Even the good things we do at that point are done with wrong motives. 

But the good news is that God liberates us from that. We don’t have to stay chained. God can will and does liberate us. 

Steven Lawson says if you follow Christ you’ve given up dirt for diamonds. We’ve taken the things that don’t matter in the scope of eternity and traded them in for the things that do matter.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Pastoral Heart Without a Pulpit: A Sermon That Illustrates Powerfully But Never Arrives at the Text

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm, story-driven opening of Galatians 6:7-10 that displayed genuine pastoral care and meaningful illustrations while failing to exposit the passage, present the gospel with clarity, or call the congregation to specific response.

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: MARGINAL PASS (barely)**
A one-sentence thesis can be constructed — "Don't grow weary in doing good because your sowing has eternal consequences" — and that does arise from the passage. However, the sermon's actual center of gravity is the Flood/Aggie story, not the text. The passage is named but not opened. The Berean Gate is held open only by the thinnest thread.

**Authority Test: CONDITIONAL PASS**
The preacher does not edit or soften the text. But he also does not exposit it. The text is a banner over the sermon, not the engine driving it.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG 1 — No clear gospel presentation (TRIGGERED)**
The sermon ends with the word "Gospel." in isolation — a single-word placeholder. At no point is justification by faith articulated, the cross explained, or salvation offered in concrete terms. The Flood story communicates Christian sacrifice and perseverance, but the mechanism of salvation — Christ's atoning work, faith, repentance — is absent. This is a fatal flaw. Score capped at 69.

**FLAG 4 — Application is entirely generic (TRIGGERED)**
"Will you pursue the diamonds of the world, or go after the oranges of heaven?" and "What are you sewing in your life?" are broad directional gestures, not conscience-level applications. No differentiated address. No specific call. No one in the congregation is cornered by anything.

**FLAG 5 — No actual exhortation (TRIGGERED)**
The sermon explains (loosely) and illustrates (extensively) but never calls for response. There is no "therefore" moment. The congregation is moved by the story but never told what to believe, repent of, obey, or pursue in concrete terms.

**Score capped at 69 due to multiple fatal flaw flags.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 10/25**
- Textual Fidelity: 4/10. Galatians 6:7-10 is cited but not exposited. "Do not be deceived, God is not mocked" receives no explanation. "Sow to the flesh / sow to the Spirit" is alluded to but not unpacked. "Reap what you sow" becomes a moral truism rather than a theological declaration. "Do not grow weary in doing good" is the only verse that receives sustained narrative treatment, and even that comes through story rather than exegesis.
- Contextual Awareness: 2/5. The Galatian letter's context — the battle over justification by faith, the contrast with works of the flesh vs. fruit of the Spirit — is entirely absent. Paul's argument in chapter 6 about sowing to the Spirit vs. the flesh is flattened.
- Exegetical Clarity: 2/5. No Greek engagement. No unpacking of key terms. "Sow to the flesh / sow to the Spirit" is the theological heart of the passage and it is passed over.
- Logical Faithfulness: 2/5. The Flood story illustrates "do not grow weary" but does not arise from the text's own logic. The sermon uses the passage as a caption on a story rather than as the governing argument.
- **Fallen Condition Focus**: Named vaguely as "weariness" and "focusing on circumstances." But the deeper FCF in this text — the tendency of flesh-driven sowing to masquerade as Spirit-sowing — is not identified.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18**
- Christ-Centeredness: 4/7. Christ is present as the center of a faithful life, but the mechanism of union with Christ — how one gets there, what it costs, what it provides — is not explained. The quotes from Toplady, Spurgeon, and Luther on free will and bondage are genuinely solid and theologically precise, and they represent the best theological content in the sermon.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3. Minimal. No placement of the text in redemptive history.
- Doctrinal Precision: 4/4. The section on free will is well-handled — the quotes are well-chosen and the doctrine of total inability is rightly stated. This is where the theology is clearest.
- Confessional Alignment: 2/4. No contradiction of confessional theology. The total inability section is confessionally aligned. But the broader sermon does not demonstrate confessional precision on justification or covenant.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 5/15**

- Spiritual Differentiation: 2/5. David represents the discouraged or backslidden hearer; Svea represents the faithful. But the categories are narrative rather than pastoral. ### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Mostly self — in the good sense of "what am I sowing?" The sermon generates self-examination, not Christ-adoration.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
The congregation encountered a moving story about a missionary couple. God is present in the story as One who works providentially, but He is not encountered as holy, gracious, or specific in the sermon's address.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Primarily moved (emotionally). The Flood story produces feeling but not specific conviction or specific comfort from the text.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Flood/Aggie story is exceptional.** It is sustained, well-told, and genuinely illustrates sowing and reaping across generations. This story will stay with the congregation. It is the best illustration of Galatians 6:9 one could ask for.

2. **The free will section is theologically solid.** The Toplady, Spurgeon, and Luther quotations are well-chosen, confessionally precise, and represent genuine pastoral care for the doctrinal health of the congregation. The statement that "apart from God doing a work in your heart you'll have no interest" is theologically correct and pastorally honest.

3. **The pastoral tone is warm and authentic.** The preacher speaks as a man who lives among his people, is moved by their circumstances, and knows their pain. The COVID acknowledgment, the shed contractor frustration, and the loving tone throughout communicate genuine pastoral investment.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text is never opened.** Galatians 6:7-10 is cited, but no verse is exposited. "Do not be deceived, God is not mocked" — what does it mean that God cannot be mocked? What is the nature of the deception Paul is warning against in the Galatian context? "Sow to the flesh / sow to the Spirit" — what specifically constitutes Spirit-sowing in Paul's argument? These questions are not asked, let alone answered. The congregation heard a sermon about the passage, not from it.

2. **The gospel is a placeholder.** The final word "Gospel." is the most troubling moment in the sermon. It acknowledges that something is owed — a gospel presentation — but declines to deliver it. An unbeliever in the room would not know how to be saved. The mechanism of grace — Christ's atoning death, justification by faith, the necessity of repentance — is never stated. The Toplady and Spurgeon quotes establish the need for divine liberation but do not announce how that liberation comes.

3. **The sermon stops rather than concludes.** There is no exhortation, no call to response, no "therefore." The congregation is moved by a story and then released without direction. Application was gestured at ("oranges vs. diamonds") but never made concrete or differentiated. No one is told what to do, believe, repent of, or pursue before the next Sunday.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that a story, however true and powerful, is not a sermon. The Flood narrative is so emotionally satisfying that it creates the feeling of a complete sermon — beginning, middle, end, emotional resolution — without having done the work of exposition or exhortation. The feeling of completion is an illusion produced by narrative craft, not sermonic substance. The preacher may believe he has preached Galatians 6 because he used Galatians 6 as the text. But using a text and preaching a text are not the same thing.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon feels complete because of the Flood story's resolution. The arc of David's bitterness → Aggie's discovery → the 110,000-member church → David's deathbed reconciliation is so emotionally satisfying that it generates the *feeling* of spiritual arrival. But that feeling is narrative resolution, not gospel proclamation. The sermon's emotional climax is doing the work that the cross should be doing. The congregation felt something — but it was the feeling of a good story, not the feeling of confrontation with grace. The illustration is so strong it masks the sermon's gospel vacuum.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Galatians 6:7 — "Do not be deceived, God is not mocked" — is one of Paul's starkest warnings. The word for "mocked" (μυκτηρίζεται) means to turn up one's nose at, to treat with contempt. Paul is saying: you cannot treat God's sowing-and-reaping economy as if it doesn't apply to you. David Flood did exactly this — he treated God's economy with contempt and said "God is not good." This connection between Paul's warning and David's story is sitting right there in the text, waiting to be made explicit. The preacher walked past it. Making that connection would have turned a moving story into a genuinely textual sermon.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Open the text before the story.** Spend 5-7 minutes expositing Galatians 6:7-10 before telling the Flood story. What is Paul's context? What does "sow to the Spirit" mean in the Galatian letter? What deception is Paul warning against? The story then becomes an illustration of an already-explained text, not a replacement for it.

2. **Replace "Gospel." with an actual gospel presentation.** At minimum: name the problem (we are sinners who have sown to the flesh), name the solution (Christ bore the consequences of that sowing — God is not mocked, and at Calvary, God's justice was satisfied in Christ's death), name the response (repent and believe). This takes 90 seconds and changes the eternal stakes of the sermon.

3. **Differentiate the application.** After the Flood story, address at least three distinct hearers: (a) the David in the room — the one who is bitter at God because life has been hard; (b) the Svea — the one who is quietly faithful but weary and needs permission to keep going; (c) the one who has never started — who needs to hear that God must first do a work in their heart before they can sow to the Spirit at all. Each category gets a specific, concrete charge.

4. **Add a "therefore" exhortation.** End the sermon with a specific call: "Therefore, this week, identify one place where you have been sowing to the flesh, and by the Spirit's power, replace it with one act of Spirit-sowing. Name it. Commit to it. Tell someone." A specific exhortation, not a general aspiration.

5. **Integrate the Sindhi Samas insert or cut it.** The people-group mention currently floats free from the sermon's argument. Either connect it explicitly — "Svea Flood was exactly the kind of person being raised up that we pray for when we pray for the Sindhi Samas" — or remove it from the sermon proper and make it a separate congregational moment.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
Not triggered. This sermon is not orthodox-but-cold — it is warm but underdeveloped. The fire is present; the doctrine is thin. The inverse of dead orthodoxy.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The Flood story carries genuine eternal gravity — the weight of a life given, a generation reached, and a father reclaimed — but the sermon itself does not sustain that gravity because it never delivers the theological foundation that would give the story its full eternal significance.
