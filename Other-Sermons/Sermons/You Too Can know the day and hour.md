Sermon Title:
“You Too Can Know the Day and the Hour of Christ’s Return”
Text: Matthew 24:23–27, 36
Introduction – False Alarms and Real Hope
If you have your Bible—and I hope you do—please open with me to Matthew 24.
I want to begin with a confession: this is a sermon I never thought I’d preach. I titled it “You Too Can Know the Day and the Hour of Christ’s Return.” It might surprise you to hear that come out of my mouth—it certainly surprises me. But I believe it’s vital.

Now, as always when I speak about another individual, it’s not to belittle or make fun of him or her. It’s to compare what they said to the truth, so that we are not deceived. Because friends, people’s eternities are at stake here. This is not a game. It is vital we know the truth.
Not long ago, many of you probably saw the chatter online about September 24th. A guy named Brother Joshua was posting videos, they circulated, all over social media posts spread, and even some pulpits shouted loudly that this would be the day of the rapture. I heard one story where a couple gave all their cash to their Uber driver, saying God told them to because it was their last day on earth and they wanted to give it to someone who would still be here after the rapture. The driver was offended, filmed a video excoriating them for insulting him—and then thanked them for the money.
We as a national church have lowered our guard with these things. We need to re-orient ourselves toward the Bible. Because guess what? It’s October 5th. And September 24th came and went—just as 2012 came and went. Remember that year? The Mayan calendar was expiring. People were whispering about a rogue planet called Nibiru colliding with Earth. Books were sold on blood moons and supposed rapture windows. Fear was in the air.

Spurgeon once said, ‘When men talk of a new revelation, I turn from them.’ That’s exactly what we need to do when someone posts a viral prophecy on TikTok, or youtube. We take our prophecy only from scripture.”

This sermon title is actually named in memory of a woman I once knew, who has since passed on. She made a video every day, tagging me in it, declaring: “You too can know the day and the hour of Christ’s return.” She claimed God had revealed it to her—Nibiru, blood moons, the Mayan calendar, all of it. I finally posted the verse we’re about to read that says in the NIV, “no man knows the day or the hour,” and she replied: “I’m a woman! Duh!”
Brief Greek note: In Matthew 24:36, the phrase “no man knows” is οὐδεὶς οἶδεν—literally, “no one at all knows.” It is not gendered. It means nobody. Not mankind. Not angels. Not even Jesus in His earthly ministry. Only the Father.
So, setting aside the twisting of Greek—and the twisting of Scripture—you’d have to do to get to that conclusion. Her chosen day came, her chosen day went, and yet life went on.
So I titled this sermon lovingly, jokingly, but also seriously: “You Too Can Know the Day and Hour of Christ’s Return.”
This kind of thing is nothing new. For two thousand years, false alarms have echoed through history. But the Lord Jesus already prepared us for this:
“If anyone says to you, ‘Look, here is the Christ!’ or ‘There he is!’ do not believe it… If they say, ‘Look, he is in the wilderness,’ do not go out… ‘Look, he is in the inner rooms,’ do not believe it. For as the lightning comes from the east and shines as far as the west, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.” (Matthew 24:23–27, ESV)
So let me be clear at the start: a healthy, biblically-based skepticism toward anyone claiming to speak with prophetic certainty is not cynicism—it’s obedience to Christ. “Do your best to present yourself to God as one approved, a worker who has no need to be ashamed, rightly handling the word of truth” (2 Tim. 2:15). Approved workmen are not tossed by every wind of doctrine (Eph. 4:14); they are anchored in Scripture.
The problem isn’t just that these claims make Christians look foolish—and they do. The deeper danger is that they distract from the gospel and distort our hope.
So let’s walk through God’s Word together to see how not to fall for fake rapture claims.
Transition from Introduction
Now before we dive in, I need to give you a little warning: we’re going to be going through a lot of Scripture today. More than usual.
A pastor I once knew used to say that one of his favorite sounds in the world was the sound of people opening their Bibles—because, he said, “that’s what I think angel wings must sound like.” So don’t be afraid if your Bible gets a workout today. Let the rustle of the pages remind you of the glory of God’s Word, and of the fact that He has given us everything we need to stand firm against false prophets and false alarms.

Sermon Roadmap

This morning we’re going to look at three things together:
Why false rapture claims flourish — and how Scripture tells us to test the spirits (Deuteronomy 13 & 18; Matthew 7; Galatians 1).
What Jesus actually promised about His return — what the real coming of Christ will look like (Matthew 24; 1 Thessalonians 4; Revelation 1).
What real readiness looks like — not speculation, but sanctification (Titus 2; 2 Peter 3; Romans 6).
So get your Bible open, keep it open, and let’s walk through God’s Word together.

Point 2 — What Jesus Actually Promised About His Return
If Point 1 showed us how false prophets flourish, Point 2 shows us what Christ Himself has promised — the real thing. And as always, when I speak about false voices, I do so not to belittle them but to protect you. People’s eternities are at stake. It is vital we know what the Lord has truly said.

1. The Return Will Be Public, Not Private

Jesus makes it plain in Matthew 24:27:
“For as the lightning comes from the east and shines as far as the west, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.”
No secret codes. No hidden rapture window. No private revelation. His return will blaze like lightning across the sky — sudden, unmistakable, visible to all.
Revelation 1:7 confirms it:
“Behold, he is coming with the clouds, and every eye will see him, even those who pierced him, and all tribes of the earth will wail on account of him.”
Every eye. Not just those who can “decode” blood moons and bible codes. Not just those who follow a TikTok account. Every eye will see Him.
John Calvin said: “We are not to dream of a private coming of Christ; for He will appear openly, so that the whole world shall behold His glory.”
2. The Return Will Be Loud, Not Silent
Paul paints the picture in 1 Thessalonians 4:16–17:
“For the Lord himself will descend from heaven with a cry of command, with the voice of an archangel, and with the sound of the trumpet of God. And the dead in Christ will rise first. Then we who are alive… will be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air, and so we will always be with the Lord.”

No whisper. No hidden escape hatch. The command will thunder, the archangel will shout, the trumpet will blast, the dead will rise, and we who are alive will be caught up.
Spurgeon put it memorably: “When the Lord comes, it will be impossible for any man to mistake it for something else. He will not steal into the world secretly, but He will come with a blaze of glory.”
3. The Return Will Be Final, Not a Trial Run
Hebrews 9:27–28 declares:
“And just as it is appointed for man to die once, and after that comes judgment, so Christ, having been offered once to bear the sins of many, will appear a second time, not to deal with sin but to save those who are eagerly waiting for him.”
The second coming is not to negotiate sin but to consummate salvation and execute judgment. There will be no rescheduling. No “oops, wrong date.” No third, fourth, or fifth return. One final appearing.
Acts 1:11 adds: “This Jesus, who was taken up from you into heaven, will come in the same way as you saw him go into heaven.” Bodily, visibly, in power and glory.
The 1689 London Baptist Confession (32.2) puts it this way:
“At the last day, those of the saints who are found alive shall not sleep but be changed; and all the dead shall be raised up with the selfsame bodies, and united again to their souls forever.”

One return. One resurrection. One judgment. One glory.

4. The Return Will Be Unscheduled, Not Calculated
We come back again to Matthew 24:36:
“But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor the Son, but the Father only.”
If Christ Himself, in His humility, did not know during His earthly ministry, what arrogance it is for a YouTube or TikTok prophet to claim to know today!
And this isn’t new. Date-setters have been stumbling for centuries. In fact, I have copies of two books sitting on my shelf: one called 88 Reasons Why Jesus Will Return in 1988, and another called 89 Reasons Why Jesus Will Return in 1989. Do you know what the 89th reason was? “Because He didn’t return in 1988.”
Friends, that’s not theology. That’s self-correction by embarrassment. And every time another prediction fails, it makes the church look foolish and it makes the world scoff. But the fault is not with Christ’s promise; the fault is with man’s presumption.
That’s why 2 Peter 3:10 says:
“But the day of the Lord will come like a thief, and then the heavens will pass away with a roar, and the heavenly bodies will be burned up and dissolved.”
Like a thief. Not announced. Not advertised.
R.C. Sproul once warned: “The moment a man fixes a date for Christ’s return, he has already denied the Word of Christ.”
Application
So what did Jesus actually promise about His return? That it will be:
Public — every eye will see.
Loud — every ear will hear.
Final — once for all.
Unscheduled — no man knows the day or hour.
Glorious for those that KNOW Jesus.
This means any claim of a secret, silent, or scheduled rapture has already failed the test. And it means we can live with confidence, not confusion.
Beloved, do not be shaken by dates, blood moons, calendars, or TikTok clips. Rest in this: the same Christ who died for you and rose for you will one day return for you. As sure as the tomb is empty, the sky will split.
Spurgeon reminded his people, and I remind you: “The trumpet shall sound, and there will be no mistake about it. The Lord will come, and all the chosen will be ready.”
So let the world chase speculation. Let us cling to revelation — the Word of Christ, which cannot fail. And let us remember: our hope is not in man’s predictions, but in God’s promises.
Point 3 — What Real Readiness Looks Like
If false prophets flourish by selling fear (Point 1), and if Jesus Himself promised a public, loud, final, and unscheduled return (Point 2), then the question is this: How do we live in the meantime?

And again, let me be clear: this is not to mock anyone, but to protect you. People’s eternities are at stake. And the Bible is very plain — readiness is not about speculation, but about sanctification.
1. Readiness Looks Like Holiness
Turn to 2 Peter 3:10–12:
“But the day of the Lord will come like a thief, and then the heavens will pass away with a roar… Since all these things are thus to be dissolved, what sort of people ought you to be in lives of holiness and godliness, waiting for and hastening the coming of the day of God.”
Peter doesn’t say, “Get out your calculators.” He says, “Live holy lives.” Real readiness is not having the right date circled on your calendar; it’s living a life marked by obedience, repentance, and godliness.
The Puritan Thomas Brooks wrote: “The great thing in the day of Christ’s appearing will not be what we have known, but what we have been; not what we have spoken, but what we have done.”
2. Readiness Looks Like Self-Denial and Godliness
Titus 2:11–13:
“For the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for all people, training us to renounce ungodliness and worldly passions, and to live self-controlled, upright, and godly lives in the present age, waiting for our blessed hope, the appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior Jesus Christ.”
The very grace that saves us is the grace that sanctifies us. Grace doesn’t make us passive while we wait — it trains us, disciplines us, re-orders our desires.

David Platt has said: “The gospel doesn’t call us to comfort and safety; it calls us to abandon every competing loyalty.” That’s what readiness looks like: renouncing idols, renouncing sin, and surrendering everything to Christ.
3. Readiness Looks Like Dying to Sin and Living to God
Paul says it clearly in Romans 6:11–14:
“So you also must consider yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus. Let not sin therefore reign in your mortal body, to make you obey its passions… For sin will have no dominion over you, since you are not under law but under grace.”
In other words, you don’t prepare for Christ’s return by chasing headlines. You prepare by killing sin. John Owen said it plainly: “Be killing sin, or it will be killing you.”
The 1689 London Baptist Confession (13.1) on sanctification says: “They who are united to Christ, effectually called and regenerated, having a new heart and a new spirit created in them… are further sanctified, really and personally, through the virtue of Christ’s death and resurrection, by His Word and Spirit dwelling in them.”
That’s readiness. Not charts, not predictions, not calculations — but Spirit-wrought holiness.

4. Readiness Looks Like Hope, Not Fear
1 John 3:2–3:
“Beloved, we are God’s children now, and what we will be has not yet appeared; but we know that when he appears we shall be like him, because we shall see him as he is. And everyone who thus hopes in him purifies himself as he is pure.”
Hope purifies. True longing for Christ’s return doesn’t lead us to stockpile food or sell our belongings to an Uber driver. It leads us to purify our hearts, because we long to see Him face to face.

5. Readiness Looks Like Relationship with Jesus
At the heart of readiness is not just what you know, or even what you do — but who you know. Jesus said in John 17:3:
“And this is eternal life, that they know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom you have sent.”
Real readiness for the return of Christ is not primarily about charts, dates, or even outward activity. It’s about whether you know Him and are known by Him.
Jesus warned in Matthew 7:21–23:
“Not everyone who says to me, ‘Lord, Lord,’ will enter the kingdom of heaven… On that day many will say to me, ‘Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name, and cast out demons in your name, and do many mighty works in your name?’ And then will I declare to them, ‘I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of lawlessness.’”
That is sobering. It means you can have all the outward signs of religion, all the activity, even prophecy itself — and still be unknown to Christ.
Readiness is relationship. It is hearing His voice as your Shepherd (John 10:27). It is abiding in Him as the true Vine (John 15:4–5). It is trusting Him as your Savior, bowing to Him as your Lord, and loving Him as your Bridegroom.
Thomas Watson once said, “To know Christ is to prize Him above all else, and to be known by Him is to be safe forever.”
So let me ask you plainly: If Jesus came today — not September 24th, not 2012, not some date in a book, but today — would you meet Him as a stranger or as your Savior?

Spurgeon once said: “A little faith will bring your soul to heaven; a great faith will bring heaven to your soul.” And that’s what readiness really is — living with heaven in your soul because you are clinging to Christ by faith. Do you know him today?
Application

So what does real readiness look like?
•	It looks like holiness in a world gone mad with sin.
•	It looks like godliness and self-denial, shaped by grace.
•	It looks like dying to sin daily and living unto God.
•	It looks like hope, not fear— purifying ourselves because we long to see Christ as He is.
•	And above all, it looks like relationship with Jesus— knowing Him, being known by Him, and abiding in Him as Savior, Lord, and Bridegroom.

And don’t miss the weight of that word Bridegroom. We throw it around so easily, but it carries oceans of meaning. A bridegroom knows his bride fully—every flaw, every scar, every weakness—and yet chooses to love her anyway. That’s what Christ has done for His people. He has chosen us, not because we were lovely, but in order to make us lovely. He has pledged Himself to us permanently, safeguarding our salvation with His own blood. He knows us to the deepest part of our core, and still He says, “Mine.”

That’s not casual love; that’s covenant love. That’s security. That’s intimacy. That’s the relentless love that chips away at our rough edges and conforms us more and more into His image. And it’s the kind of love that draws us to submit to Him, to follow Him, to gladly live for Him—not out of fear, but out of joy.

That’s what readiness looks like: not a date on a calendar, but a daily walk with the Bridegroom who loved us and gave Himself for us. Its reading our bible, its living a lifestyle marked by prayer. Its being in fellowship with other believers, its sharing the gospel in season and out!
Beloved, don’t be distracted by false alarms. Don’t be deceived by TikTok prophets or YouTube predictions. True readiness is not speculation but sanctification. It’s not calculating dates but cultivating holiness. It’s not fear-mongering but faith-building.
So I plead with you: be ready, not by knowing the day, but by knowing the Savior. Be ready, not by chasing predictions, but by clinging to promises. Be ready, not by stockpiling secrets, but by walking in holiness and hope.
Because the same Christ who came once to bear sin will come again to bring salvation to those who are waiting for Him.

Final Call (revised with anchor language)
So I say again: Don’t let TikTok shape your view of the end. Don’t let YouTube set your hope. Don’t let self-proclaimed prophets feed your fear. Let the Word of God shape your hope. Let God’s promises, not man’s predictions, anchor your soul.
For one day the trumpet will sound. The skies will split. The dead will rise. Every knee will bow and every tongue will confess that Jesus Christ is Lord. On that day, there will be no more speculation, only revelation. No more false alarms, only final glory.
So the question is not, Do you know the date? The question is, Do you know the Savior — the One who has pledged Himself to His people as a Bridegroom? Do you know the One who knows you fully and has chosen to love you anyway? If you do, the next question is what are you doing about that? With Jesus return being imminent, are you sharing Christ with the lost? In the words of the late C.T. Studd “only one life, twill soon be past, only whats done for Christ will last”.
So let us live ready — not by knowing the day, but by knowing the Savior. Not by stockpiling secrets, but by walking daily with the Bridegroom who loved us and gave Himself for us. In times like these, we need an anchor. Be very sure, be very sure, your anchor holds and grips the Solid Rock — and that Rock is Christ.
And that readiness begins with the gospel: “All have sinned… but are justified by His grace as a gift, through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus.” (Romans 3:23–24)
Amen. Come, Lord Jesus.
