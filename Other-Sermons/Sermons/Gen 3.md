# Genesis 3:1-6 — The First Domino

---

We're going to talk today about the most famous Baptist heretic — a man named Toy. The best and brightest minds in our denomination called this man the crown jewel. He was the smartest in his class, beloved by all his professors. He was popular amongst the student body. He was a good preacher, shared the gospel regularly, his church loved him as pastor.

He loved the Bible deeply.

He even had an on-again, off-again relationship with someone you may have heard of. Her name was Charlotte. You probably know her as Lottie Moon. They knew each other through Virginia Baptist circles — he was a rising scholar, she was brilliant in her own right. He proposed. She declined. She was so sure that the Lord was calling her to missions that she declined to marry the love of her life because God had called her to China. She left. He remained single for several years. He started writing to her again. But by this point there had been a marked shift in Toy's theology. They became close once more, but the moment he revealed the change he'd made in what he believed, she broke things off once and for all. This change in him was so egregious Lottie Moon would no longer talk to him.

Crawford Toy resigned from Southern Seminary in 1879. Within a year he was teaching at Harvard. He broke ties with the Baptists, became a Unitarian, and by the end had rejected nearly every doctrine central to Christianity.

It was his influence — and the influence of the students he taught — that contributed to the theological drift in our denomination over the next century. He taught students who became pastors and professors, who taught more students. Who propagated this damnable heresy throughout the denomination.

Ultimately this led to the Conservative Resurgence, a time when people with solid biblical theology re-conquered the seminaries and the halls of power throughout the denomination. His actions over a century ago are still felt today. When you watch Discovery Channel and hear about the contributions of the so-called secret gospels — the liar's gospels — it was Toy's kind of thinking that led to their low view of Jesus, their low view of Scripture, and their high view of anything that casts a shadow on either.

He knocked over the first dominoes in this area.

You may have a guess at his heresy at this point, but you'd be wrong. Being against the Word of God was but a symptom of having made the decision to trust his own thoughts, feelings, and opinions over the Word of God.

Somewhere along the line, he became persuaded that the higher critics in Germany were right — that the Old Testament couldn't be taken at face value. Those thoughts led him to the conclusion that miracles cannot be true. That creation was false. That anything outside the observable, measurable world was false.

So let me explain what I mean by that. The things we can see with our own eyes — measure, smell, taste, touch, experience — that's the world we live in. But God exists mostly outside of that. He does things that affect our world. Prayers answered. Healings. When He intervenes and gives you a sense of peace. At those points He has stepped into our world. But mostly He operates on a plane we can't see, or touch, or hear from earth — unless He chooses to let us.

Toy came to believe that because he didn't see God work in extraordinary ways, God didn't work that way. So he argued that because the Bible talks about God working in extraordinary ways, where it says them the Bible must be false. He stripped everything that wasn't a work of history out of his reading of the Bible, and turned the living Word of God into a moralistic history book that couldn't fully be trusted.

Ultimately though, the long and short of it was that he thought he knew more, and better, than everyone else — and God.

There is another story in Scripture where a person follows the same course.

**Genesis 3:1-6**

*[Read Genesis 3:1-6.]*

---

It strikes me that Eve followed the same trajectory as did Toy. He started out fine, but ended in a place where he thought what he knew was better than what God said.

Eve did essentially the same thing. But first let's look at the Hebrew here a little bit.

Look at verse 6 — the ESV says the tree was "to be desired to make one wise." The Hebrew word there is *hamad* — חמד — and it literally means desirable. But I found it striking that in Exodus 20:17, where we are given the commandment not to covet — guess what word they translate to covet? *Hamad.* Same root. This has given rise to the thought that Eve was more than just passingly attracted to the fruit. That perhaps she began to obsess about it. Certainly she desired it very deeply. Seems like she came awfully close to coveting.

But like Toy, she didn't start out that way, did she? She was created from Adam's rib. She walked with the Lord every day. She was obedient to Him to this point in all things.

My study Bible notes that Eve's decision is based upon practical values, aesthetic appreciation, and intellectual gratification. These factors coalesced together and Eve decided that in that moment she didn't trust God as much as she trusted herself — and her own opinions, and feelings, and thoughts on the subject. She even trusted the snake more than God.

It's easy, isn't it, to trust one's own assessment of things over that of anyone else.

It's annoying the rest of the time, but truthfully the problem comes when you trust yourself over God. We are in our fallen state — why? Because at that original sin, we decided what we thought, what we felt, what we desired was more important than what God commanded us to do.

Sin creeps into our lives, and one of its primary vectors comes through ignoring the Word of God.

Toy, when he thought he knew better than God, when he chose to ignore the parts of God's Word that he didn't like — what happened?

Did not the same thing happen with Eve? She became obsessed, near-covetous with a thing, and it led to the introduction of sin into the world. To the corruption of all of mankind for the rest of human time, until heaven.

---

The point of all of this is to impress upon us the importance of the Word of God in our lives.

I've heard it said — and it's often attributed to John Bunyan — that either God's Word will keep you from sin, or sin will keep you from God's Word.

Which path will you choose?

---

Hopefully you will choose the first path. To that end, I propose we spend the next year reading — or for the auditory learners, listening to — the Word of God. We have a Bible reading plan in our bulletin this week. In fact, I want you to open your bulletin and pull out that section and look at it for a second.

It outlines what we are to read in the coming week. We read through it together, I'll preach on one of the passages we read through this week, and by this time next year we will have completed our reading through the Bible.

There are many benefits to reading Scripture. I found a list of fifteen. I'm not sure we are going to get through all fifteen, but I want you to know — there are more benefits than just helping keep us from sin.

This list was written by Ralph Drollinger of Capitol Ministries, which conducts Bible studies for political leaders in Washington, D.C.

He points out that spending time in the Word of God will help assure us of our salvation. He points to Romans 10:17 —

> "So faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ."

Saving faith comes from the Gospel, which is recorded in God's Word. It provides us with an objective standard for all elements of Christian life. Because of that it helps us understand the difference between someone who's saved and someone who's deceived themselves. We've talked before about being a sheep versus a goat.

It will teach, reprove, correct, and train in all righteousness, so we can be equipped for every good work. That's 2 Timothy 3:16:

> "All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work."

Scripture guides us. Proverbs 19:21 says, "Many are the plans in the mind of a man, but it is the purpose of the LORD that will stand." And Psalm 119:105 — "Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path." These things help protect us from evil. Not hardships. But from evil.

It counsels us. When you're sad, or lonely, or happy, or weary, or sick, or any of a thousand things. When you're not sure what to do, turn to Scripture. It will point you to Christ. It will offer wisdom which will guide you where God wants you to go.

Scripture will warn us when we get off track. Psalm 19:11 says:

> "Moreover, by them is your servant warned; in keeping them there is great reward."

It will sanctify you. John 17:17 —

> "Sanctify them in the truth; your word is truth."

Sanctify as it is used throughout Scripture means a setting apart for Christian maturation. One grows through grappling with the truth, and ultimate truth only stems from knowing the Scriptures. Christ's intended way of maturing His church — Ephesians 5:26:

> "that he might sanctify her, having cleansed her by the washing of water with the word."

It will stabilize you. Psalm 1:3 —

> "He is like a tree planted by streams of water that yields its fruit in its season, and its leaf does not wither. In all that he does, he prospers."

Over the years I have seen so many people wither. Come and go because they violate this principle of meditating on the Word. Unfortunately, they are like chaff which the wind drives away. They are "a double-minded man, unstable in all his ways" — James 1:8. Conversely, Joshua 1:8-9 —

> "This Book of the Law shall not depart from your mouth, but you shall meditate on it day and night, so that you may be careful to do according to all that is written in it. For then you will make your way prosperous, and then you will have good success. Have I not commanded you? Be strong and courageous. Do not be frightened, and do not be dismayed, for the LORD your God is with you wherever you go."

This passage promises prosperity and success to those who meditate on the Word day and night.

It will embolden you. Psalm 119:23 —

> "Even though princes sit plotting against me, your servant will meditate on your statutes."

Do you want to have the courage to do what is right no matter what the pressure is from others? Get into the Word. Set your mind to please God, versus others whose plans may run contrary to God's Word.

There are more. If you'd like I'll print up the whole list and pass them out next week. Even his list isn't comprehensive.

---

In any event I want to close with a challenge. The praise team is going to come up here in a moment and sing a final song. Normally this is where we give a gospel presentation and an altar call. This week I want you guys to think about something different. I want you to commit to reading through the Bible in a year. I've got a printout up front here. I feel like we take more seriously things we sign. I'm going to sign it right now. If you want these benefits, if you want to grow closer to the Lord, if you want to commit to reading the Word of God in this next year with the rest of us, please come up and sign this commitment.

If need be, I'll make this available again next week for those that weren't here this week, and those who couldn't be here will have the opportunity to participate alongside us.

Then when everyone has signed up, we will give everyone a copy to keep in their Bibles.

So while the praise team is singing, if you're willing to take this step in following the Lord, come on up and sign this commitment.

Won't you come?

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**"The First Domino": A Compelling Introduction That Never Becomes a Sermon**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A vivid historical opening that correctly identifies self-trust as the root of Eve's fall, but abandons the text to become a Bible-reading motivational talk, leaving the congregation informed but unconverted, unpressed, and unexhorted toward Christ.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: MARGINAL PASS / DRIFT**
The sermon correctly identifies the main idea of Genesis 3:1-6 as humanity's choice to trust self-knowledge over God's Word. This is a legitimate reading of the text. However, the sermon uses that observation as a platform to pivot entirely to a Bible-reading campaign. Genesis 3:1-6 is not fundamentally a text about the importance of Bible reading — it is a text about the catastrophe of the Fall, the nature of temptation, the serpent's deception, the corruption of desire, and the entry of sin and death into the world. The sermon captures one thread and pulls on it while ignoring the tapestry.

**Authority Test: CONDITIONAL PASS**
The preacher does submit to the text's claim that self-trust over God's Word is catastrophic. He does not editorialize around it. However, by departing the text so completely in the second half, he effectively stands over it as an editor who decided which parts to develop.

**Gate Ruling: Passes with concern. The sermon does not contradict the text, but it substitutes the text's full argument for a narrower homiletical premise, then leaves that premise to conduct a Bible-reading kickoff.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG 1 — No clear gospel presentation: TRIGGERED**
The sermon mentions that "saving faith comes from the Gospel, which is recorded in God's Word" (Romans 10:17) in a list of benefits of Bible reading. That is not a gospel presentation. Christ's name is not mentioned in the sermon body. The finished work of Christ is entirely absent. The cross is nowhere. A person could sit through this sermon, sign the commitment card, and have no more understanding of how to be saved than when they walked in. The gospel is assumed and never proclaimed.

**FLAG 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary: TRIGGERED**
Christ could be removed from this sermon — and He nearly has been. The name "Jesus" does not appear. "Christ" appears once in a quoted passage (Romans 10:17: "the word of Christ"). The sermon would survive intact — and in fact would not lose a single argumentative thread — if Jesus were excised entirely. This is a fatal structural failure in a sermon on Genesis 3, a text that stands at the headwaters of the entire redemptive story.

**FLAG 4 — Application is entirely generic: TRIGGERED**
"Read your Bible this year" is not differentiated application. It is the same call for every person in the room — the repentant believer, the hardened skeptic, the grieving widow, the self-righteous churchman, the person on the edge of apostasy. Nobody is cornered. Nobody is named. Nobody is distinguished.

**FLAG 5 — No actual exhortation: TRIGGERED (partial)**
The altar call is a call to sign a commitment card, not to repent, believe, or encounter the living God. "Come sign this" is not exhortation in any Reformed or Puritan sense. The congregation is not summoned toward Christ; they are recruited for a program.

**Score is capped at 69.**

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Neither, frankly. The sermon makes you think more about the importance of a discipline (Bible reading). Christ is absent. The congregation's gaze is not lifted to the Savior.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas about a principle — the danger of self-trust — but not God Himself. The Toy narrative is vivid, but vivid history is not the same as encounter.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Informed. The benefits of Bible reading are informative. The Toy narrative creates urgency. But no one is comforted with the gospel, and no specific sinner is convicted.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Crawford Toy narrative is the sermon's single best moment.** The detail about Lottie Moon breaking off the engagement specifically because of his theological shift is arresting. It grounds the abstract danger of self-trust in a flesh-and-blood life, and it connects to a figure the congregation already honors. This is what illustration is supposed to do.

2. **The *hamad* / coveting connection is solid, accessible exegesis.** Identifying the same Hebrew root behind "desired" in Gen 3:6 and "covet" in Exodus 20:17, and noting Eve's near-obsessive quality of desire, is genuinely illuminating without being pedantic. This is Greek-and-Hebrew-as-unlock, not as decoration.

3. **The sermon's oral register is authentic.** It reads like a man talking to people he knows, not like an essay being performed. The conversational rhythm, the direct address, the moment of "You may have a guess at his heresy at this point, but you'd be wrong" — these show natural preaching instincts.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Christ is absent from a text that inaugurates the need for Christ.** Genesis 3 is the text behind every atonement doctrine in the Bible. The proto-gospel of Genesis 3:15 — "I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel" — is the first whisper of the cross. Omitting it from a sermon on Genesis 3 is not a minor oversight; it is the equivalent of preaching the Exodus without mentioning Passover. The congregation left without knowing that God responded to the catastrophe of the Fall with a promise — and that the promise kept. This is the text's own climax. The sermon climaxed at the Toy parallel instead.

2. **The pivot to "benefits of Bible reading" is a category displacement.** The text calls for preaching on sin, deception, the anatomy of temptation, the catastrophe of the Fall, and the mercy of God in the face of it. Instead, the back half of the sermon becomes an annotated devotional list sourced from Ralph Drollinger. The text's weight is not "read your Bible more." Its weight is "you are fallen, death has entered the world, and only God can fix what Eve broke." The application is not wrong — Bible reading is valuable — but it is imported, not native to the text.

3. **The altar call invites people to a program rather than to Christ.** "Come sign this commitment" as the summoning moment of the sermon is not exhortation — it is administration. The people who most needed to be confronted by this passage — the person spiritually drifting like Toy, the person whose desires have become obsessive like Eve, the person who has not yet repented and believed — received no specific word. They were invited to sign up for Bible reading. The two things are not the same.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the Toy narrative, while compelling, is doing the theological heavy lifting the text should be doing. The historical story carries the weight because the text's own story (Fall, curse, promised Seed) was not preached. When a sermon's most powerful moment is about a 19th-century seminary professor rather than the text itself, that is a diagnostic signal: the text has been used as a launching pad, not a governing center.

The preacher also likely does not see how thoroughly Christ-free the sermon is. From inside the preparation process, the biblical texts cited (Romans 10:17, 2 Timothy 3:16, John 17:17) feel Christ-sufficient. But they are cited as proof-texts for a benefits list, not as proclamation. The difference between referencing Christ and proclaiming Him is the difference between this sermon and a sermon.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Toy narrative feels like preaching. It has narrative drive, historical texture, emotional stakes, and a clean parallel to the text. A congregation would feel, listening to it, that they were hearing something weighty and real. But the narrative is doing what the text should do — and the text (Fall, curse, death, promise) was never actually preached. The illusion: "We got to the heart of the matter via Toy." The reality: the heart of the matter is Genesis 3:15, and the sermon never arrived there.

Similarly, the benefits list feels substantive because it is long and scripturally footnoted. Thirteen verses cited. But a list of benefits with proof-texts is not the same as preaching. The length and the footnotes create the impression of biblical density while the text itself goes largely unexplored.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Genesis 3:15 — the *protevangelium* — is the single greatest missed opportunity in this sermon. It is the verse that redeems the entire chapter. God curses the serpent, and in that curse whispers the first promise of Christ: one born of woman will crush what the serpent started. Every subsequent act of redemption in Scripture — Abraham's Seed, the Passover lamb, the Suffering Servant of Isaiah 53, the empty tomb — flows from this verse. A sermon on Genesis 3:1-6 that does not at minimum gesture toward 3:15 has preached the diagnosis without the cure. The congregation was told they are fallen. They were not told that God acted in history to undo what Eve's act set in motion.

The threefold appeal of verse 6 (good for food / pleasant to the eyes / desirable for wisdom) was also untouched. This is the anatomy of temptation — the flesh, the eyes, the pride of life — and it connects forward to 1 John 2:16 and to Jesus' own wilderness temptation (Matthew 4) where He reverses each point. The sermon identified that Eve's desires were disordered but did not trace the shape of the disorder or show where it appears in the congregation's own lives.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach Genesis 3:15.** After the Toy parallel and the *hamad* observation, do not pivot to Bible reading. Instead, pivot to the God who responded to the Fall with a promise. "God didn't leave us in the rubble. In the very moment He cursed the serpent, He hid a word of grace inside the curse: 'her offspring will crush your head.' God was already telling us that He would send Someone to fix what Eve broke. That Someone came. His name is Jesus. He faced the same threefold temptation in the wilderness — and held." This move makes Christ necessary, not decorative.

2. **Trace the threefold temptation in verse 6 to its New Testament reversal.** Good for food (Matthew 4:3-4), pleasant to the eyes (1 John 2:16), desirable for wisdom (1 Corinthians 1:18-25). Show the congregation that Jesus fought and won the battle Eve lost, and that His victory is offered to them.

3. **Add direct address to specific categories of hearers.** The self-righteous churchman who thinks his doctrinal knowledge protects him — "Toy knew his theology, and it didn't save him from himself." The person whose desires have become obsessive — name it: the screen, the financial security, the status. The doubter — "Some of you are doing what Toy did right now. You're auditing God's claims against your own experience." Address them by name.

4. **Replace the altar call with a gospel call.** "If you are Eve — if you have chosen what you wanted over what God commanded — there is good news. The God who found Eve in the garden hiding in shame is the same God who sent His Son to bear the curse she earned. The commitment I'm asking for isn't to a reading plan. It's to the One the reading plan points to. Repent and trust Him."

5. **Relocate the Bible-reading challenge.** The commitment card and reading plan are not wrong — they are simply misplaced as the sermon's climax. They belong as a practical response to the gospel, not as a substitute for it. After a gospel call, "And because this Word points to Christ, let's commit to reading it together this year" is logical and pastorally sound.

6. **Tighten the second half.** The benefits list can be reduced to three or four points with actual sermonic development, rather than listed thirteen-item style. A list is not preaching. "The Word sanctifies you. The Word stabilizes you. The Word emboldens you — because when you know what God says, you don't need anyone else's approval." Those three points, developed with directness, will do more work than thirteen points read from a printout.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered** — the sermon does not have high theology scores that would trigger the pattern. What is present is something adjacent: *moralistic pragmatism in Reformed clothing.* The concern is not correct-but-bloodless doctrine; the concern is that the sermon's practical thrust (read your Bible) substitutes for the gospel rather than flowing from it. The danger is not dead orthodoxy but a low-grade Pelagianism of Christian discipline — "do this thing and you will grow" without grounding that discipline in what Christ has already done.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The Crawford Toy narrative carries genuine weight — a man with everything going for him who destroyed it all by trusting himself over God — but that weight is never connected to the eternal stakes of the gospel, and so it dissipates before it can become glory. **+1/5.**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Genesis 3:1-6 is narrative — specifically, the most theologically loaded narrative in the Old Testament. The rubric's framework fits it well because this text is simultaneously: (a) historical narrative, (b) doctrinal foundation (Fall, sin, death), (c) typological hinge (proto-gospel in 3:15), and (d) pastoral address (every hearer is Eve). The rubric's emphasis on gospel centrality and Christ-necessity is, if anything, *more* pressing for this text than for most — because this is precisely the text where Christ's necessity is established in redemptive history. The rubric does not strain here; it reveals exactly what the sermon needed to do and did not.
