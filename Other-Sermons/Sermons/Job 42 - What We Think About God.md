## I. What You Think About God Reveals Who You Are

A.W. Tozer opened *The Knowledge of the Holy* with this sentence: *"What comes into our minds when we think about God is the most important thing about us."*

What you think God is like reveals the trajectory your life is on, and who you are as a person right now. If you think God is all about rules and limiting fun, that says something about you. If you think God is only going to give you nice, pleasant things, that says something about you too. There are practical reasons to believe right things about God. Our beliefs about God affect the trajectory of our lives and our development as people.

Not only that — God has something to say about what we think about Him.

## II. Job 42 — God's Anger at Wrong Theology

Do you remember the book of Job? God allows tragedy to befall Job, Job does his best to remain righteous through it all, and Job's friends come along and start saying things about God that are simply wrong. I had forgotten God's response to that, but it's important, so I'm going to read it.

**Job 42:7–9 (ESV)**

> After the Lord had spoken these words to Job, the Lord said to Eliphaz the Temanite: "My anger burns against you and against your two friends, for you have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has. Now therefore take seven bulls and seven rams and go to my servant Job and offer up a burnt offering for yourselves. And my servant Job shall pray for you, for I will accept his prayer not to deal with you according to your folly. For you have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has." So Eliphaz the Temanite and Bildad the Shuhite and Zophar the Naamathite went and did what the Lord had told them, and the Lord accepted Job's prayer.

Catch that: *"My anger burns against you."* Their crime? Saying wrong things about God.

This should be a sobering reminder to pastors to rightly divide God's Word — but also to all of you. Make sure you know what you're talking about when you talk about God. Which means we need to spend real time in God's Word, so we actually *know* what we're talking about.

What God is rejecting here is their worship. But why?

## III. Eliphaz and the Prosperity Gospel

They all make a variety of mistakes, but here is a big one we can still relate to — one we hear on TV and from many pulpits today.

**Job 4:7–9 (NIV)**

> "Consider now: Who, being innocent, has ever perished? Where were the upright ever destroyed? As I have observed, those who plow evil and those who sow trouble reap it. At the breath of God they perish; at the blast of his anger they are no more."

Eliphaz tries to convince Job: *God only punishes the lost* — people outside the covenant, people who don't know Him. So obviously, Job, that makes you a lost person. You need to get right with God.

But is it true that God *only* punishes the lost? Does He *only* bless the righteous?

Turn on any Christian television channel and you're bound to hear it: God wants you to be healthy, wealthy, and wise. Buy my prayer napkin. God's going to make you rich. Can't afford it? Charge it.

It's a strange world we live in when shepherds are fleecing the flock. *[Preach a verified illustration of prosperity gospel fraud here — confirm all names, places, and dollar amounts from a reliable news source before using from the pulpit. The point stands without a specific example if one cannot be verified.]* The pattern is consistent: quoting scripture, claiming God's promises, targeting the vulnerable, and pocketing the money. The tagline is always some version of "God wants you to have an increase."

But if following God means never having trouble, then everyone who follows God must be doing fine right now. Or else maybe it just doesn't work that way.

God *does* punish sin. But He isn't only a God of punishment. He uses trouble to teach discipline, to disciple us, to draw us closer to Himself, and further from our dependence on things like presidents and prosperity. God uses trouble to work in the lives of people watching us. When the rubber of faith meets the road of life, what will those around you think of what your life says about your belief in God?

## IV. If the Prosperity Gospel Were True

If the prosperity gospel were true, then all of the disciples must have been lost.

Throughout this section, note that church tradition on martyrdom varies in reliability. Where Scripture speaks directly, it is noted.

**James, son of Zebedee** — *This one is in your Bible.* Acts 12:1–2: "Herod the king laid violent hands on some who belonged to the church... and he killed James the brother of John with the sword." That is Scripture. James is the only apostle whose death is recorded in the New Testament. Martyred. Lost, according to the prosperity gospel.

**Peter** — Walked with Jesus. Loved Jesus so much he drew his sword in the garden the night Jesus was arrested. Church tradition, recorded by Origen and preserved by Eusebius in the third century, says Peter was crucified — and by his own request, upside down, because he said he wasn't fit to die the same way Christ had. If the prosperity gospel is to be believed, he was lost.

**Andrew** — One of the Twelve, who walked with Jesus every day. Church tradition says he was a missionary to Scythia and across Asia Minor, and that he was ultimately crucified in Patras, Greece. Lost, according to the prosperity gospel.

**Thomas** — Doubting Thomas, whose doubts were put to rest by Jesus Himself commanding him to touch the wounds of His hands after the resurrection. Doubting Thomas who became, in that moment, fully believing Thomas. Church tradition — preserved in sources dating to around AD 200 — says he was a missionary to India, where he was martyred by spear. The Mar Thoma Christians of Kerala today trace their faith back to Thomas. Yet he was lost, the prosperity gospel would say.

**Philip** — Walked with Jesus, served as a missionary in Asia Minor. Eusebius records in the second century that Philip was associated with Hierapolis. Church tradition adds that he was martyred there. Lost.

**Matthew** — The former tax collector, author of the Gospel that bears his name. Church tradition largely says he was martyred in his missionary work, though early sources are not fully consistent on this point. Lost.

**Bartholomew** — Church tradition says he was martyred. Lost.

**Simon the Zealot** — Church tradition says he was martyred, though the early sources are sparse on details. Lost.

**Matthias** — The disciple who replaced Judas — church tradition says he was martyred, though the early sources differ on how and where. Martyred for his love of Jesus. Lost.

**John** — Who wrote 1, 2, and 3 John and the book of Revelation — we don't even have to go to tradition here. *Revelation 1:9*, his own words: "I, John, your brother and partner in the tribulation and the kingdom and the patient endurance that are in Jesus, was on the island called Patmos on account of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus." Imprisoned on a barren rock of an island for his faith. Irenaeus, writing in the second century, records that he eventually died of old age in Ephesus. The one apostle who was not martyred — and he still suffered exile and imprisonment for the gospel.

Tertullian, writing around AD 197, observed: *"The blood of Christians is seed"* — meaning the more the church was cut down, the more it multiplied. The saying has come down to us as "the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church," and the truth of it holds even if the phrasing has been rounded over centuries.

So either these men were all lost — or the prosperity gospel simply isn't true.

But before I close on prosperity gospel, we have one more man to consider.

**Jesus Himself** — who, for your sins and mine, was humiliated, beaten within an inch of His life, nailed to that old rugged cross, and left there until He died.

If the prosperity gospel is true — and it is absolutely not — then Jesus was lost too.

What utter nonsense.

The prosperity gospel causes people to put their hope in prosperity more than in Jesus. It may be popular and fill pews, but it isn't what Scripture teaches. It is fundamentally a different gospel, worshipping success and earthly resources. Paul — missionary across the Roman world, who wrote half the New Testament, and who was **beheaded for his faith under Nero** (early sources from the late first century onward are consistent on this) — says this in Galatians 1:8:

> "But even if we or an angel from heaven should preach to you a gospel contrary to the one we preached to you, let him be accursed." — Galatians 1:8 (ESV)

Jesus plus *anything* is a false gospel. Jesus plus Saturday worship — false gospel. Jesus plus mandatory speaking in tongues — if a church says you must speak in tongues to be saved, that's a step too far from Scripture. Anything added to the gospel is wrong, Paul says.

## V. God's Judgment Is a Kindness

People feel judged by the gospel — having to compare themselves to Christ's standard and come up short. No one likes to be judged. But being told in advance you're coming up short is a kindness.

We talked about Jonah last week — remember what Jonah preached? *Forty days and then destruction.* The Ninevites had been judged and came up short. They were awful pagans. But when they heard the sermon, what did they do? They responded in obedience to God, and God spared them for about a generation. God's judgment leads to God's mercy for the elect. God's judgment is a warning — and warnings are a wonderful thing.

Think of it this way. If you don't know Jesus as your Savior, you are walking around blindfolded, marching toward the edge of the Grand Canyon. You can't see — so you don't know how long you have until the edge. Only God does. God's judgment is a warning: *Stop. Turn around. Or you are going to walk off that cliff.* It may be uncomfortable and hard to change directions. But it is far better than the alternative.

God told Job to pray for his wayward friends, and God took them back. Christians — we need to be praying for the lost around us. Everyone in this room knows someone bound for eternity in Hell. Are you praying for those people? Are you warning them about the canyon they are marching toward?

And if you are the one lost — heading for that cliff — repent. We don't know how long you have. But we know eternity is a long time.

## VI. We All Fall Short

Romans 3:23 says we all fall short of the glory of God. We all miss the mark.

There is a street preacher named Ray Comfort, founder of Living Waters ministry. One of the things he does is walk through the Ten Commandments with people — he calls it "Way of the Master." It's only ten commandments — surely we can keep at least those across our whole lives, right?

**Commandment 9 — No lying.** Has anyone here *never* told a lie? Not even to spare someone's feelings? No one. We are all liars, and even one lie, once, is enough to have broken this commandment. Even breaking one commandment is enough to have earned an eternity separated from God.

**Commandment 4 — The Sabbath.** Honor the Lord's Day, rest from labor, keep it holy. Ever skipped church when your proverbial ox wasn't in a proverbial well? Then you've broken this one.

So far we are all liars who don't put God first — which breaks the first and second commandments as well. Four up, four broken.

**Commandment 7 — Adultery.** Jesus expanded this in Matthew 5:28: *"everyone who looks at a woman with lustful intent has already committed adultery with her in his heart."* The commandment reaches further than the physical act. Add adulterer to the list.

**Commandment 6 — Murder.** This one seems easy. Most of us have never physically killed anyone. But 1 John 3:15 says:

> "Everyone who hates his brother is a murderer, and you know that no murderer has eternal life abiding in him." — 1 John 3:15 (ESV)

Murderers, liars, adulterers, who don't put God first — and we haven't made it through ten commandments yet.

Romans 3:23: *all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God.* There are **613 commandments** in the Torah — the Jewish tradition has counted them carefully — and we couldn't get through ten without serious failure. Breaking even one is enough to ruin our standing before a holy God.

## VII. The Sheep Stall Illustration

We have sheep. When it rains, they all go into the stall — I don't blame them, especially in winter. But they go in, huddle together, stay warm, eat together — and when they need to eliminate waste, they just go right where they're standing. With nine sheep in a stall, that adds up fast.

God is holy. One hundred percent pure. God is like a white sheet — clean, new, fresh.

Sin is like the floor of that sheep stall.

When we were kids, if we had been out playing in that stall, we couldn't walk back inside tracking sheep waste, muck, and hay. Mom would have sent us to the hose first before we could come in the house. Because the clean house doesn't let the filth in.

God is clean, and pure, and holy. He doesn't let sin in. Full stop.

One day people are going to reach the gates of heaven and say, *"I went to church every week — well, almost."* And God will say: *Depart from me, I never knew you.* They'll say, *"But I kept the commandment about honoring my parents — except when I was a teenager."* Depart from me. *"But I never really murdered anyone — I just hated that one neighbor."* Depart from me. *"I was always honest — I only told that one lie to spare someone's feelings."* Depart from me. I never knew you.

**1 Corinthians 6:9–10 (ESV)**

> Or do you not know that the unrighteous will not inherit the kingdom of God? Do not be deceived: neither the sexually immoral, nor idolaters, nor adulterers, nor men who practice homosexuality, nor thieves, nor the greedy, nor drunkards, nor revilers, nor swindlers will inherit the kingdom of God.

That pretty much rules everyone out.

## VIII. But God Made a Way

But God made a way for us to be clean. To be holy.

John 3:16. Jesus paid the price for our sins. His death washes the believer. Colossians 3:3:

> "For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God." — Colossians 3:3 (ESV)

Just like my mother wouldn't let us inside until we rinsed off at the hose — Jesus died to wash us. We were all guilty, all doomed. *But God, being rich in mercy, because of His great love for us, sent His Son to die for us.*

Now read the rest of that passage — turn there if you haven't already:

**1 Corinthians 6:9–11 (ESV)**

> Or do you not know that the unrighteous will not inherit the kingdom of God? Do not be deceived: neither the sexually immoral, nor idolaters, nor adulterers, nor men who practice homosexuality, nor thieves, nor the greedy, nor drunkards, nor revilers, nor swindlers will inherit the kingdom of God. And such were some of you. But you were washed, you were sanctified, you were justified in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ and by the Spirit of our God.

*And such were some of you.* Past tense. Were. You were washed.

### Tertullian Quote
The Latin from *Apologeticus* 50.13 (c. AD 197) is: *"semen est sanguis Christianorum"* — literally "the blood of Christians is seed." Tertullian wrote neither "martyrs" nor "the church." The familiar English phrase "the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church" is a paraphrase that developed over centuries. Both accurate — the paraphrase is a fair summary of the context — but the preacher should be aware the expanded form is not a direct translation. Drafted in the body as: *"The blood of Christians is seed"* with a note that the later paraphrase holds the same truth.

### 613 Commandments
The number 613 is the standard Jewish count of Torah commandments (*taryag mitzvot*), first stated in Talmud Makkot 23b, codified by Maimonides.
