A brief, warm, evangelistically motivated introduction to Matthew 24:4–8 that raises the right question (what does the text say about the present moment?) but never exposits the passage, delivers no doctrinal content, and closes by transitioning to congregational prayer rather than proclamation.

### 3. Berean Gate

**Fail — with pastoral acknowledgment.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon cannot be said to have a main point that arises from the passage because the passage is effectively not exposited. Matthew 24:4–8 is mentioned, briefly summarized ("wars and rumors of wars, famines and earthquakes... birth pangs"), and then used as a launching pad for: (a) the school shooting and the gospel as social remedy, (b) an observation about human knowledge doubling, (c) a story about assistant coach Aaron Feis as a Christ-type. The passage's own argument — Jesus's warning against being led astray and his identification of the birth pangs as the beginning of the end, not the end itself — is never developed. The "birth pangs" metaphor is gestured at but not explained in the text's context (the disciples' question, the eschatological framework, the instruction not to panic).

The sermon as written is not text-driven. The main point of the sermon (the gospel is the answer to social violence) does not arise from the main point of Matthew 24:4–8 (do not be led astray; wars and disasters are the beginning, not the end; endure).

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* The social-remedy argument (if the shooter had known Christ, this wouldn't have happened) is pastorally true but keeps the congregation's attention on the social problem rather than on Christ's person and work.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* This manuscript does not create conditions for encounter. It is a pastoral conversation, not a proclamation.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* The congregation is directed toward prayer, which is genuine and good. But the text's own comfort and conviction (don't be alarmed by wars; these are the beginning, not the end; the Son of Man is coming in power) are never delivered.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The gospel-as-social-remedy argument is valid and urgent.** "If that boy had been reached with the Gospel, his heart would have changed" — this is theologically sound (regeneration precedes moral transformation) and culturally necessary. It deserves a full sermon of its own.
2. **Aaron Feis as Christ-type is an excellent one-sentence illustration.** "He was able to do for a few kids what Christ did for all of us" — this is vivid, true, and potentially memorable. It needed one more paragraph to become a proclamation.
3. **Directing the congregation to prayer is itself a pastoral act.** Given the context (community grief over a shooting), transitioning to corporate prayer is appropriately humble and pastoral.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Matthew 24:4–8 is never exposited.** The passage receives a one-sentence summary and is then abandoned. The congregation learns nothing about what Jesus is actually saying, why he says it, or what they are to do with it. The text is decoration.
2. **No gospel presentation exists.** The sermon ends without ever proclaiming why Christ's death matters, what it accomplished, or how someone is to respond. Three fatal flaw flags are triggered simultaneously.
3. **The sermon has no argument.** It has observations (wars are common; knowledge is doubling; a coach died heroically) and a conclusion (let's pray). These are not the components of a sermon. There is no movement from problem to solution to response.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that using Matthew 24 as a launching pad for a topical discussion of gun violence and the gospel actually undermines the authority of the text. The passage has a powerful and specific message — one that would have been directly relevant to the congregation's anxieties. Jesus tells frightened disciples not to be alarmed by terrifying events because these things must happen, and the end is not yet. That is exactly what a congregation grieving a school shooting needs to hear from the text. The preacher had the right instinct (this text is relevant to this moment) but turned away from the text to make the point, when the text was capable of making it.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The pastoral opening — addressing the school shooting directly, refusing to make it about gun politics — feels strong and courageous. It is. But that pastoral courage carries the congregation into a room where the text never appears. The strength of the opening creates the impression that a sermon is happening; the rest of the manuscript reveals that the opening was the sermon.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Matthew 24:6–8 — "you will hear of wars and rumors of wars. See that you are not alarmed, for this must take place, but the end is not yet... all these are but the beginning of the birth pangs" — is the most directly applicable text that could have been preached in response to a school shooting. Jesus tells his disciples: do not panic, do not lose faith, do not conclude that the world is out of control. These things are part of the divine plan. The Son of Man is coming in power. That message — you are not to be alarmed; this is part of the plan; the end is not the chaos you see but the glory you don't yet see — is exactly what a congregation saturated in news coverage of a mass shooting needed. The preacher had the right text and turned away from it.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild from the text.** Matthew 24:4–8 has a specific argument. Identify it: Jesus warns against (a) being led astray by false messiahs, (b) being alarmed by wars and disasters. His reason: these things must happen, but they are the beginning, not the end. The end will be his coming in glory. Preach that.
2. **Use the school shooting opener — then let the text answer it.** The opening is good. But the pivot should be: "You want to know what to do with what we saw this week? Jesus anticipated it. He told his disciples exactly what to do when the world looks like this." Then open the text.
3. **Develop the Aaron Feis Christ-type into a gospel proclamation.** That one sentence — "he was able to do for a few kids what Christ did for all of us" — deserves three paragraphs: what Feis did, what it cost him, and then: "But what Christ did was on an infinitely larger scale — he took the full weight of the wrath of God for every person who trusts him. That is the gospel."
4. **The transition to prayer can remain — but it should follow the sermon, not replace it.** Congregational prayer in response to a community tragedy is entirely appropriate. But let the sermon do its work first.
5. **State the FCF of the passage.** The fallen condition Matthew 24 addresses is: human terror and disorientation in the face of catastrophe. Jesus's prescription: do not be alarmed. That is the sermon. Preach it.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Matthew 24 is apocalyptic-prophetic discourse — one of the most debated passages in the Synoptic Gospels (near fulfillment vs. far fulfillment; preterist vs. futurist readings). This genre does stress the rubric's exposition categories, but not in a way that excuses the current sermon's non-engagement with the text. The passage is difficult, but it has a clear pastoral argument (do not be alarmed; endure to the end) that does not require resolution of the scholarly debates. The rubric fits — the passage simply needs to be preached.
