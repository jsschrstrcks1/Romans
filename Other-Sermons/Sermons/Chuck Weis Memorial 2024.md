I’ve had the priveledge of being Chucks pastor for the last few years. He was a good man, and a good friend. He was quick with a compliment, and quicker with a joke. He loved making people laugh, and sing, and playing his guitar. One thing I know for a fact is that he loved each and everyone of you. He would want us to laugh today, and have fun in memory of him. If he were here today, he’d be sitting in the back making faces at me while I preached trying to get me to chuckle, or poking someone near him. He loved to make people laugh and be happy. He was excellent at making people happy with music. I’ve gotten to see a number of his performances at Empire, and have been regaled with stories about how he would just randomly make up lines and add them in to see if people noticed. Apparently if you keep the first line and the chorus the same, almost anything goes. 

He was tenacious too. I won’t retell the whole story, but one time someone tried to steal a guitar from him, and he jumped on the hood of the kids car, and went for a ride across town, and did not stick the landing. Ended up being mostly ok, and maybe kept that kid from stealing again.  

One thing I loved is that when he heard about a need he wouldn’t say a word, He’d just go up to whomever had the need, and slip them a few bucks. It was always thoughtful, and generous, and well received. No fan fare. He wasn’t doing it for attention. He just wanted to love on people that needed it. 

I think all of those things are what lead me to our passage for this morning. 

lets read: Jn: 14 “Let not your hearts be troubled. Believe in God;[a] believe also in me. 2 In my Father's house are many rooms. If it were not so, would I have told you that I go to prepare a place for you?[b] 3 And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come again and will take you to myself, that where I am you may be also. 4 And you know the way to where I am going.”[c] 5 Thomas said to him, “Lord, we do not know where you are going. Let not your heart be troubled reminded me a lot of chuck. He had a sense of humor that he would use to light up the room. I think if he were here that would be his attitude. Don’t grieve for me, I’m somewhere better. Knowing both he and Barbara, and knowing they are believers in Jesus Christ we can rest in the surety that the moment he took that last breath he was standing face to face with Jesus. I think the second moment he was there hugging Barbara, and probably the third thing was cracking a joke. I can almost here him saying “man, no wonder people are dying to get in here”. Something like that. He would want us to rejoice to the extent we can, that he’s not suffering any more. No more confusion. He’s no longer being let down by his body in any way. He’s in the presence of Jesus. He is a strong man, and I don’t know if he’ll cry when he is reunited with Barbara, but if he does, Jesus says in scripture that he will wipe away every tear that’s cried. Since there is no more sorrow, and no more sadness, I believe he’s wiping away tears of joy as we see our savior face to face for the first time, and are reunited with our loved ones who have gone on ahead. 

I know there are people that have questions in this area. Is God good even though he took someone we love home. I think of Grandma Baker when I think of that question. She had a melanoma that had metasticized. If you know anything about cancer melanoma is one you do not want, and metasticized is bad news. Its like dumping gasoline on a fire. In conjunction with cancer, she had a lung condition that meant that she could hardly do anything that she wanted to. Hardly walk across a room without struggling. Yet when she took that last breath 15 years ago this past April, in that moment all of those issues were healed. No more cancer. No more lungs letting her down. No more confusion, and struggle and pain. Shes free to run and dance, again. Her blind eye could see again. She was healed. 

As much as I miss her, I have to ask the question, would it be better for her to be back here with me, or healed with God. For me there is only one answer. Its far better for her to be healed, and in glory with God, than here and continuing to struggle and suffer. 

This is true of Chuck too. We know he’s in a better place. A place he will be for the rest of eternity. A place we can go and join him. 

Our passage today says God has been preparing a place for us.

God has blessed me with opportunities to see a fair bit of this world, and according to scripture he made all this in six full days. The sun rose, and the sun set, it’s a day. ## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date:** 2026-03-21
**Passage:** John 14:1–6
**Genre:** Funeral/Memorial Message

---

### 1. Verdict Title
A Tender Pastoral Word with Gospel Urgency but Incomplete Gospel Proclamation

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and personally grounded memorial message that brings real comfort from John 14 and ends on a genuine gospel appeal, but leaves the explicit content of the gospel as a trailing note rather than a completed proclamation.

### 3. Berean Gate
**Pass** — with genre adjustment noted.

The main point of the sermon tracks the text's own claim: because Jesus is the Way, the Truth, and the Life, troubled hearts can rest. The preacher stands under the Word's comfort rather than above it as editor. The Scopus Test: "Because Jesus is the only way to the Father, those who believe in him need not let their hearts be troubled — and Chuck's final wish was for you to find him there." This arises organically from John 14:1–6.

*Genre note: Funeral messages operate under different homiletical constraints than Sunday expository sermons. The rubric is applied with appropriate latitude for the pastoral and evangelistic occasion.*

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**FATAL FLAW TRIGGERED — Score capped at 69.**

- **Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The sermon ends with "All of that starts with the gospel" — but the gospel is never actually presented. The mechanism of salvation (Christ's death for sin, substitution, repentance and faith) is entirely absent. Chuck's request ("make sure they know how to find me in heaven") is acknowledged but not fulfilled within the manuscript. The gospel is gestured toward but not proclaimed. This is the most serious weakness in a message preached to a room likely containing many unbelievers.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**
- Textual Fidelity (6/10): John 14:1–6 is read and the comfort of v.1 and the "I am the Way" of v.6 are applied. However, the passage is largely used as a launching pad for comfort rather than expounded. Verses 2–5 (Thomas's question, the nature of the Father's house) receive brief mention but no real exegetical weight. The passage's context — Jesus preparing disciples for his departure and the coming cross — is absent.
- Contextual Awareness (2/5): The Upper Room Discourse setting is not referenced. The disciples' grief context would have deepened the pastoral application considerably.
- Exegetical Clarity (3/5): The "I am the Way" statement is used effectively but without unpacking why exclusivity is the ground of comfort (not offense) in this setting.
- Logical Faithfulness (3/5): No eisegesis present. The connections made are fair if underdeveloped.

*FCF: The Fallen Condition Focus is grief and the fear of death/loss — well-identified and appropriate to the passage and occasion. The text was written precisely to address this condition.*

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**
- Christ-Centeredness (5/7): Christ is genuinely central — his identity as the Way, his preparation of a place, his presence in heaven. This is organic to the text.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3): The cross is briefly alluded to in Chuck's death and Barbara's prior passing as context for heaven, but Christ's redemptive work is not situated in the gospel's storyline.
- Doctrinal Precision (3/4): Heaven as prepared place, the exclusivity of Christ's way, the comfort of eternal presence — all sound. No doctrinal errors detected.
- Confessional Alignment (2/4): The exclusivity of Christ (John 14:6) is clearly affirmed and is one of the message's key strengths. However, without the atonement being explained, the confessional content is incomplete for an evangelistic moment.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 5/15**
This is the message's most significant failure. The gospel is pointed toward ("All of that starts with the gospel") but never delivered. The Synagogue Test: a morally serious person from another faith could hear this message and understand that Chuck is in a better place and that Jesus is "the Way," but would not understand what that means or how to access it. The cross is not mentioned. Penal substitution is absent. Repentance and faith are not called for. The Pascal's Wager-style appeal ("what if Chuck is right and Jesus is real?") is clever but is not a gospel call.
- Clarity of the Gospel (1/5): Gospel is named but not explained.
- Necessity of Christ (3/5): John 14:6 is clearly cited — Christ alone is the Way. The exclusivity is present.
- Cross-Centered Gravity (1/5): The cross does not appear in this manuscript.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15**
- Heart Penetration (4/5): The personal warmth and direct address ("I want you to ask yourself") reach the affections genuinely. The memory of Grandma Baker is a powerful, emotionally honest illustration of what it means to trust God's goodness in loss.
- Specificity (2/5): Application is mostly in the form of comfort and the eternal question, not differentiated by hearer type.
- Spiritual Differentiation (2/5): The message speaks primarily to the grieving believer (Perkins category 5) and gestures toward the unbeliever (categories 1–2). The backslider, the hardened, and the humbled-but-not-yet-believing are not addressed.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8**
- Call to Response (1/4): "Ask yourself, what if Chuck is right?" is an invitation to consider, not a call to repent and believe. The exhortation is incomplete because the gospel that would ground it was never proclaimed.
- Equipping for Holiness and Witness (2/4): The implicit message — live so you can be reunited with Chuck and Barbara — is present but underdeveloped.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 7/9**
- Clarity of Outline (3/4): The message flows logically: Chuck's character → text → comfort for believers → question for unbelievers → gospel appeal (truncated). The structure is natural for the genre.
- Progression (3/3): Builds toward the gospel appeal cleanly.
- Unity (1/2): The Dobson reference and creation-in-six-days section feel like digressions from the grief-comfort thread.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**
- Relevance (2/2): The Grandma Baker illustration is the sermon's finest moment — personal, specific, theologically argued, emotionally resonant.
- Clarity (1/2): The "six days to make the Grand Canyon, imagine what heaven took 6,000 years to prepare" logic is warm but slightly strained.
- Memorability (1/1): "He loved all of you — his last request was that you know how to find him in heaven" will be remembered.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**
- Reads as Preaching (2/2): Oral, warm, direct. This sounds like a man who loved Chuck and is addressing grieving family and friends.
- Conviction on the Page (1/2): The conviction is pastoral warmth, not prophetic urgency. The moment where the room needs to feel the weight of eternity and the exclusivity of Christ is present but soft.
- Cadence and Breath (0/1): Sentences are mostly uniform in length; no climactic compression at the gospel appeal moment.

### 6. Cognitive Load Modifier
**0** — The message is accessible to all hearers. No complexity burden.

### 7. Total Score
**57/100** — *Fundamentally flawed. Rebuild from the text.*

*Score cap (69) not technically reached, but the incomplete gospel brings the raw score below the cap naturally. The fatal flaw is structural, not incidental.*

**Interpretation:** The warmth and pastoral authenticity are real. But a funeral message preached to a room of likely-unconverted family and friends, that ends pointing to the gospel without presenting it, has not completed its most critical task. Chuck's own dying request — "make sure they know how to find me in heaven" — was not fulfilled on the page.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ or myself?* — Christ, modestly. The focus on Chuck's character risks shifting gravity toward the deceased.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* — Warm ideas about Him. The Grandma Baker section comes closest to real encounter.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* — Comforted, somewhat. Not convicted. The gospel call that would produce either conviction or decisive comfort is absent.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **The Grandma Baker illustration** is exemplary pastoral preaching — it takes a hard theological question ("Is God good to take someone we love?") and answers it through lived experience with genuine logic and emotion. This is what funeral preaching should do.
2. **Chuck's dying request** ("make sure they know how to find me in heaven") is a devastating and powerful pastoral pivot — the kind of personal moment that opens a room. It is used, but not fully leveraged.
3. **John 14:6 exclusivity** is stated without apology in a funeral context, which takes courage. The preacher does not soften "No one comes to the Father except through me."

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The gospel is not presented.** The sermon ends with "All of that starts with the gospel" — and stops. For a room likely full of Chuck's unchurched friends and family, this is the critical failure. The entire message points toward a door that is never opened.
2. **The cross is absent.** Not mentioned, not alluded to, not explained. The mechanism by which Chuck is with Jesus — Christ's substitutionary death for sinners — is never named.
3. **The Pascal's Wager appeal** ("What if Chuck is right and Jesus is real?") is philosophically weak as a gospel call. It presents Jesus as a possibility to hedge against rather than a risen Lord to bow before.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely doesn't see that ending on "All of that starts with the gospel" without completing the gospel reads as a structural truncation rather than a powerful pivot. To him it may feel like a clean handoff; to an unbeliever it is a sentence that trails off at the most important moment.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The warmth and humor ("I can almost hear him saying 'man, no wonder people are dying to get in here'") create the feeling of pastoral fullness. But warmth without the cross is comfort without foundation. The room may leave feeling good about Chuck and feeling warmly toward the preacher without knowing how to be saved.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
John 14:6 — "I am the way, and the truth, and the life" — is read but not unpacked as a gospel text. The three titles (Way, Truth, Life) offer a natural structure for explaining what Christ actually accomplished: he is the Way (by dying for our sins — the road is the cross), the Truth (he is what God is really like — grace and judgment together), and the Life (resurrection, not just comfort). The text itself was preaching the gospel. The sermon read it and moved past it.

### 14. Surgical Fixes
1. **Complete the gospel.** After "All of that starts with the gospel" — add 3–4 sentences: "Here is the gospel: we have all sinned and fallen short of God's standard (Romans 3:23). The wages of sin is death (Romans 6:23). But God demonstrated his love for us: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us (Romans 5:8). If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved (Romans 10:9). Chuck believed that. You can too. That is how you find him."
2. **Cut or reframe the Pascal's Wager.** Replace with a direct call: "The question isn't what-if. The question is: will you receive what Jesus offers? He made the way. Will you walk it?"
3. **Use John 14:6 as the gospel pivot.** When you read "I am the way, and the truth, and the life," pause and explain: "The way to the Father runs through the cross. That is what Jesus was heading to when he said these words. He made the way by dying in our place. Chuck knew that. That's why he's there now."
4. **Acknowledge the cross once explicitly.** Even a single sentence — "Jesus died for our sins so that the room Chuck now occupies would be available to any who trust him" — grounds the comfort in redemption rather than sentiment.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — the sermon is not cold or bloodless. The warmth is genuine. The failure is incompleteness, not sterility.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The eternal gravity is present in seed form — Chuck's deathbed request, the vision of heaven prepared over 6,000 years, the reunion with Barbara — but the cross-shaped center that would give it full weight is missing, leaving the sermon floating on sentiment rather than anchored in redemption.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Funeral/memorial messages are a distinct homiletical genre. The rubric bends appropriately here: the Perkins taxonomy of hearers, the full law/gospel dialectic, and the extended applicatory section are all less applicable to a 30-minute memorial. The core standards — Berean Gate, gospel clarity, and exhortation — apply fully and with heightened urgency, since a funeral room is one of the most gospel-ready environments a preacher will ever face. The fatal flaw is not a genre problem; it is a completion problem. The sermon was heading to the gospel and stopped one sentence short.
