## Hermeneutics — Tools for Reading the Bible

Alright are we all there? One more thing before we read the passage today, I want to give you a ten-dollar word, and hopefully some tools for reading the Bible. For starters always pray before you start. Ask God for guidance, and to help you understand what He — what God — is trying to say to you today through His word.

The tools are some preachers are trained to use for writing sermons as well. Let's start with the word — Hermeneutics. Hermeneutics is a word for the study and interpretation of scripture. *(See flags — the claim that it "literally means to squeeze the meaning out" is etymologically incorrect; the word comes from Greek hermēneuein, meaning "to interpret or translate." The towel illustration is a fine homiletical device for what we do with the text, but it is not what the word literally means. Corrected to remove "literally.")* If you were to soak a towel, and grab it by both ends and twist to squeeze all the life-saving living water out of that towel, so that you could drink as much of it down as possible, that's in a nutshell what hermeneutics looks like in practice.

One of the ways we do that is by studying the context(s) of the passage. The culture of the day, the geography, the history, the philosophy, all inform the writers and their Holy Spirit guided use of their language. For instance, if someone said "that's bad" in the 1950s — people would assume the word meant something negative had happened. But if you said "bad" in the 1990s people would assume you were talking about a good thing. So one of the things preachers do is study the language, and how it's used at that period of time. That is informed by the various contexts — historical, cultural, language, etc. I have a personal library to help me do that, it's harder for those who don't. But you can definitely study the context of the verse you're reading. If the Bible says "Go and do likewise" without the context of that command, without knowing what you were to do, you could get yourself in trouble. So read the context. *(See flags — original transcript read "God and do likewise," corrected to "Go and do likewise" per Luke 10:37 ESV: "You go, and do likewise.")*

Finally, ask a lot of questions about the text, and if you can't figure out the answers then look them up, or ask me, I'll help you as much as I can. I may not have the answer offhand, but I can look the answers up.

Here is a short list of questions that are good to ask. Some of them are obvious, some less so:
1. How does this passage fit into the big picture of scripture?
2. Does the passage talk about Christ, can we see where this passage is pointing to Christ?
3. How can I apply this? Is there a sin to forsake? A principle to adopt? A behavior to change in my life?
4. Ask the whats — what does it say, now what should I believe, and finally so what does this mean for how I live my life.

That's a good start, there are more, and I'll try to bring some more about how to better understand what you're reading each week. But for now, let's read the Bible.

## Genesis 11:4 — Self-Focus and the Sin of Eve

Look with me at vs 4, (read) do you notice anything that reminds you of Eve last week? What did she do? She thought she knew better. She did what seemed good in her own eyes, to benefit herself. She and Adam right afterwards, put herself first, they in several ways tried to take the role of God. That backfired then, and it backfires here too.

Where is their focus here? Just in this one verse, the first time they've spoken in this chapter they talk about themselves what? Five times? In just the one verse. They were focused on what they wanted, what seemed good to them, what seemed good in their eyes. They were focused on themselves and not on God.

When Adam and Eve did that, they were kicked out of the garden. What happens here? When God finally decides to act, they are scattered. Can you imagine the chaos. One minute you're talking to people in your own language, and the next minute now you're speaking German, or Greek, or French. It's probably worth mentioning that whatever language they spoke at this point, the mother language so to speak, is what some think will be the language of heaven. No way to prove that one way or the other. In any event we know from other ziggurats that were built in the centuries that followed in that valley that the tiny room at the top contained an altar. *(See flags — the original claim that the room "contained an altar where children were sacrificed" with "a metal idol, that would be heated till it glowed red, and then children would be dropped inside" conflates Molech/Tophet worship (Canaanite, associated with the Valley of Hinnom) with Mesopotamian ziggurat worship. There is no archaeological evidence linking ziggurats to child sacrifice through heated idols. Ziggurat shrines housed a cult statue of the city's patron deity and an altar for food offerings and animal sacrifice. The heated-idol image comes from medieval rabbinic midrash about Molech. The preacher's theological point about child sacrifice being evil stands — but the historical claim that it happened at ziggurats through heated metal idols is not supported. See integrity log for details.)*

Just in case anyone wasn't clear, sacrificing children is always evil. Full stop. Never is that OK. That will not get you what you want from God. Not now, not ever. They aren't a tool to use in manipulation, they are a vulnerable treasure to protect.

## The Thing They Feared

Their fear was that they would be scattered. So they began to build this tower in an effort to manipulate God so they could get what they wanted, when they wanted, because a thing looked desirable to the eye, but what did they get in the end? The very thing they feared.

## Word of Faith Critique

There is a movement out there that seeks to do just that. Lots of churches are swallowed up in it. They believe they can basically do what they want, and then when they find themselves in trouble, or lacking an earthly luxury they can just speak it into reality. They believe so long as they ask in faith, they get what they want. I have a family member wrapped up in this. They are missionaries to an island nation. They wrote a letter home one time saying that they believed they deserved a yellow Volkswagen Beetle. But if you've heard me preach before you know what the Bible says about what man deserves. We only deserve one thing, and it's not a shiny new yellow Volkswagen Beetle. It's like they see God as a cosmic vending machine. Insert prayer, receive prosperity, wealth, and comfort.

The problem is much broader than the Word of Faith movement too. How many people out there have you known that would live like they wanted through the week, and ignored God till they wanted something. How is that approach any better than what we've read tonight. Are you not trying to bargain with God, to purchase God's intervention in your life, one might say to manipulate God into doing your will? We so easily get it backwards. We are to obey God's will, not the other way around.

## Grace the Catahoula — Obeying Her Will Instead of Mine

This reminds me of my dog Grace. It's been a while since we've had a dog story. I have a Catahoula leopard dog. I absolutely adore the breed, but they are a handful and not the breed for everyone. She's strong-willed, and quick to try and take charge of the situation for better or worse. Usually for worse. You see my dog can't see or understand as much of the world as I can. She reminds me in a lot of ways of how God must see us. So a few years ago, before Grace passed away, we got some new sheep. Grace saw part of her job as protecting us from interlopers. She was very protective of me especially. She was fiercely loyal. Anyway, we bring these two new cute fluffy sheep home. One was colored like a cow. She was cool looking. The other had great genetics, and I had put a lot of hope in these two sheep improving our flock of sheep.

Anyway, Grace to this point had no issues with any sheep, we trusted her outside with them. We let her run around, they weren't sure what to think of her, but I think she'd have protected them like anyone else she considered to be part of her family.

Anyway, she must not have gotten the memo that these sheep were family. A couple days after we got them, we had them out wandering the yard. Grace saw them as interlopers and ran right over and killed them. She ran back up to us, so proud of herself for protecting us from danger. She got in a lot of trouble for that.

She had obeyed her will, instead of mine. She had done what she thought would please us, instead of what would have actually pleased us.

We do the same thing with God! When we don't know His will, when we aren't reading His word, we end up doing what we think is right, and without first talking to God, sometimes we end up doing what seems right in our own eyes, and hurting God's sheep.

## God Is Not a Vending Machine

Or worse, we treat God like a vending machine. I don't know about you guys, but there are things in my life that I've prayed for, that would not have been a benefit to me in the long run. We've talked about this before, but you remember the song "Thank God for Unanswered Prayers"? In the song, he meets up with a girl he'd once loved and introduces her to his wife. Says she was the girl he'd gone to bed praying God would let him marry one day. But that compared to his wife, the one God had in mind for him, she was nothing special. Then he thanks God for unanswered prayers. Sometimes the best answer God can give us to our prayers is no. Because like my dog, we have a limited view and understanding of things around us. Even our best and brightest only know a tiny thimble full about existence compared to God. Much less the future. Much less what is best for us. We may think we know, but because we can't see beyond the next rise we really don't. But God can.

## Application — The Four Whats

So if we were to look at some of those same questions we talked about earlier — what does the Bible say? "Men tried to manipulate God, to obey their will. In exchange God allowed them to experience what they were trying to avoid." So then what do we believe — we believe that God knows better than us. That God can see further than us. That He is better capable of understanding the things that He sees, and responding to our prayers in ways that are better than we can even ask for. That a no from God is often better than Him saying yes to our prayers. What about the final what then? In light of all this what does this mean for how I live my life? Anyone?

### Factual Claims — Corrections Required

5. **Pyramids "based on" ziggurats**: ❌ **Not academically supported.** The scholarly consensus is that ziggurats and pyramids were **independent developments** arising from different cultures for different purposes (ziggurats = temple platforms; pyramids = tombs). The chronology actually works against the claim: the Step Pyramid of Djoser (~2650 BCE) and the Great Pyramids of Giza (~2560 BCE) predate the first true ziggurats (~2100 BCE under Ur-Nammu) by 400-500 years. The preacher's underlying point — that monumental building was happening in this region — is fair, but the causal claim is unsupported.
   - **Correction applied**: Left in body text with prominent flag. The preacher should verify or remove this claim before reuse.

6. **Ziggurat staircases "one from the east, one from the west, and one from the south"**: ⚠️ **Partially accurate.** The Ziggurat of Ur did have three staircases, but all three were on the **northeast face** of the structure — one central staircase projecting outward and two flanking staircases along the wall, converging at a gatehouse. The corners (not the sides) of the ziggurat aligned to the cardinal directions. The staircases were not on three separate sides.
   - **Correction applied**: Removed the directional description ("one from the east, one from the west, and one from the south") from body text. Retained the fact that there were three staircases.

7. **Brick construction — "mud and slime, allowing them to dry in the sun"**: ❌ **Contradicts Genesis 11:3.** The ESV says "Come, let us make bricks, and **burn them thoroughly**." The Hebrew describes kiln-fired bricks, not sun-dried ones. The biblical author highlights this as noteworthy because sun-dried mudbrick was standard and kiln-firing was the advanced technique. In archaeological reality, ziggurats used *both* — sun-dried mud brick for the interior core and kiln-fired brick for the weatherproof exterior. But the biblical text specifically emphasizes the firing. The "slime" (KJV) / "bitumen" (ESV) for mortar is correct.
   - **Correction applied**: Removed the sun-drying claim from body text. The preacher should note that the Bible itself describes fired bricks — this is actually a more interesting detail to preach on (the innovation of the technology).

8. **Child sacrifice at ziggurats with heated metal idol**: ❌ **Conflation of Molech/Tophet with ziggurat worship.** The heated bronze idol description comes from medieval rabbinic midrash about Molech (Tanhuma B, 8th-9th century CE) and classical accounts of Carthaginian sacrifice. Molech worship was Canaanite, associated with the Valley of Hinnom (Tophet) near Jerusalem — condemned in Leviticus 18:21, 20:2-5, and 2 Kings 23:10. Mesopotamian ziggurats housed a shrine (cella) to the city's patron deity containing a cult statue and altar for food offerings and animal sacrifice, tended by priests. There is **no archaeological evidence** linking ziggurats to child sacrifice through heated metal idols.
   - **Correction applied**: Removed the child sacrifice / heated idol description from body text. Retained the reference to the altar at the top. The preacher's condemnation of child sacrifice is theologically sound and biblically warranted (Lev 18:21), but the historical setting was wrong. If he wants to reference Molech worship, it should be presented as Canaanite practice, not Mesopotamian ziggurat practice.

9. **Hermeneutics "literally means to squeeze the meaning out of God's word"**: ❌ **Etymologically incorrect.** Hermeneutics comes from Greek *hermēneuein* (to interpret, to translate, to explain), traditionally associated with the god Hermes, the messenger and interpreter of the gods. It does not literally mean "to squeeze." Additionally, hermeneutics is not exclusively "the English word for the study of scripture" — it is the theory and methodology of interpretation broadly (legal, philosophical, literary), though it is commonly applied to biblical interpretation.
   - **Correction applied**: Changed "It literally means to squeeze the meaning out of God's word" to describe the towel illustration as what hermeneutics "looks like in practice" rather than what the word literally means. The towel illustration is a fine homiletical device — it just shouldn't be presented as etymology.
