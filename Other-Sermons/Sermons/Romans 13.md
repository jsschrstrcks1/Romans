Before we really get started, I want to ask you guys a question. Whats the least popular chapter of the new testament? 

It strikes me that when the bible says something we don’t like, its even more important that we listen to it. Right? If you were a thief, and you read the bible where it says you shall not steal, that likely wouldn’t be popular. But what message does that thief need to hear the most? Likewise if someone is cheating on their husband, and had to sit through a sermon about why that’s wrong, it might not be the most popular part of the bible to hear. But is it any less necessary? Sometimes the exact medicine we need, is the thing we want to hear the least, but its perhaps also the thing we most need to hear. 

Lets pause right there, and start with prayer. 

I want to pull back a moment, and get the 5000 ft view of romans for a moment. We sort of have a staircase leading from romans 1 clear to romans 13 – which is where we will spend most of our time this morning. Open your bibles there if you’ve brought them and I hope you have. Romans 13. 

But heres the staircase to the 5000 ft view. It starts with the problem – romans 1-3 – we are rebels against God, and deservedly under his wrath. The infinite wrath of a Holy and infinite God. This is Pauls universal diagnosis of human kind – not that we are all basically good. Not that we make mistakes, but we apart from God we are actively surpressing the truth. Exchanging the truth of God and his word for idols. We stand guilty of that as human beings. Romans establishes this deep reality thoroughly, that we are not neutral people who occasionally disobey, we are creatures engaged in active rebellion against authority, starting with God and His authority. That’s why Paul starts with wrath, and judgement, and accountability. 

The second step is in romans 3:21 through romans 5. If Romans 1-3 were the problem, Romans 3-5 is the solution: God justifies the ungodly. Which is in itself a miracle. God doesn’t justify you because you cleaned yourself up, he justified you inspite of the fact you cant. Ephesians 2 says we are dead in our trespasses and sins, we are by nature children of wrath, sons of disobedience. What can a dead body do? Our justification cant depend upon us because we couldn’t measure up. We are justified because Christ stood in our place, and absorbed the wrath of God on our behalf. This matters as we approach romans 13 because justified people don’t live like they are still in court trying to prove themselves. We didn’t earn our righteousness because we submitted. We submitted because we have righteousness in Christ. This justification then changes our posture. We don’t need to be defensive. We can be less self saving, less “I must control everthing” because we are truly free, and eternally forgiven. I know a guy, I’m not naming names, and to be fair I know a few men who have backgrounds they aren’t proud of, and a good number who have a history that includes having spent time in jail. This one man in particular had messed up. He earned jail time. He served every single day of his sentence with honor, and distinguishment, and at the end of his term he applied for and was granted clemency. In our state, if you’re granted clemency it means you go back to having your full set of rights. If you become a criminal, your rights are taken away, you pay your debt to society, sometimes you can be eligible to have all of those rights restored. This man filled out the papers, everyone signed off, and his full rights and privileges were restored. A few years later, the man finds himself to have run afoul of the law once more, and his priveldges are revoked, and hes back in jail a second time. Clemency lost. IF justification depended upon us, we would lose it just like that man did. But we didn’t earn it, we couldn’t. we didn’t maintain it, because we cant. SO Christ maintained it by paying for all of our sins, once and for all, for all who would become children of God. Or as Paul calls them the elect. 

This gives us a new identity, or a new team jersey. Romans 6-8 – identity as men freed from sin. Enslaved to righteousness. 

You’ll recall paul said we WILL serve someone, either sin or righteousness, either the flesh or the spirit. Were either actively serving and worshipping God, or else our allegiance belongs to the enemy of God. 

Because of our new identity, we are the free-est people on earth. And can therefore be the most conscientiously ordered people on earth. 

So when paul says in romans 13 be subject, he’s not introducing oppression, he’s apply truths he’s long been establishing. When he says be subject to the governing authorities, its not because the state can save, or eternally condemn you, its because Christ is. 

Which brings us back up to romans 9-11. We had the problem, in romans 1-3, the solution in romans 3-5, a new identity in romans 6-8, and now in romans 9-11 we have assurance. We – like sheep – need our assurance. We are so quick to take our eyes of Jesus and look at the wind and the waves, like Peter did, and begin to sink. Political fear often happens when people lack an understanding of, or faith in God and his sovereignty. Fear is a faith issue. That’s not to say if you’re afraid you’re a bad Christian. Because we all have fears at times right? But the whole of scripture, and one of the stories of church history is that God is not in trouble when the nations struggle. God’s plan doesn’t wobble when rulers stumble. But God is NOT reacting moment by moment. He’s ruling, planning, carrying out action across millennia. Keeping promises across time periods that are hard for us to wrap our minds around. Weaving purposes and redemption into the tapestry of history. Stories of his faithfulness, even when you and I are unfaithful. 

Which leads us to Romans 12 – which is the ethic. What does it look like to be a living sacrifice inside real relationships? 

Romans 12 isn’t private spirituality then, its public holiness. Its God renewing our minds, is us engaging in humble service and demonstrating sincere love even when it costs us something. Its blessing our enemies – even when they are actively trying to hut us. Its refusing vengeance, even when opportunity presents. 

Vengence and justice are tools in the hands of God. They are not your stick, or my stick to beat people with. For one thing, we wouldn’t be able punish them as well as God can, and for another why fear someone who can only take your life. Remember the words of mt 10:28 - Matthew 10:28 (ESV) “And do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul. Rather fear him who can destroy both soul and body in hell.”

And that’s where romans 13:1-7 starts. 

Romans to this point, in short, has been teaching us two great realities: how God makes sinners right, and how God teaches the justified to live right. He has shown us our guilt, then our Savior, then our new identity, then God’s sovereign faithfulness, and then the shape of a living sacrifice. So when we come to Romans 13, Paul isn’t suddenly talking politics instead of gospel. He’s showing what the gospel produces when it walks into the public square—how truly saved people relate to authority, pay what they owe, love their neighbors, and live for Christ in a dark world.
Now that we have climbed the staircase—lets open the door and step into the room of the text.

Scripture cleans anything we let it filter.

So heres the big idea. Who rules our rulers? So because our rulers are ruled by God, we are to live conscientiously. Unless and until obedience to God requires disobedience to our earthly rules. If our earthly rulers command us to murder a baby, or to forsake scripture, or to violate the word of God, then Gods word compels us to disobey. Don't get it twisted. It never says don't pay your taxes to avoid funding things you disagree with. Just that if we are commanded to be disobedient to God we can disobey our earthly rulers.

We arent called to be nieve citizens. We are called and ruled by Christ. I. The command: “Be subject” (v. 1)

“Let every person be subject to the governing authorities…”

That phrase “be subject” is not the same as “celebrate.” It’s not “admire.” It’s not “endorse.” It’s not “trust.”

It’s closer to: order yourself under.

It’s the posture of a Christian who says, “I am not the center of the universe. I am not my own law. I do not get to decide what authority is legitimate just because I don’t like it.” Now I know what that immediately stirs up in the room. More so probably a few years ago than today.

Some of you hear “be subject” and you think: What about corrupt government? What about tyranny? What about injustice? What about wicked laws? What about the times when rulers aren’t a terror to bad conduct but to good? Some of you hear “be subject” and you think: What about corrupt government? What about tyranny? What about injustice? What about wicked laws? What about the times when rulers aren’t a terror to bad conduct but to good?

And I understand that. But before we sprint to the extreme cases, let me ask something closer to home—because this is where the Word starts cutting us.

How many times have we sinned with our mouths about leaders we don’t like? Not principled disagreement—contempt. Not careful critique—mockery. Not truth—a rumor we passed along because it “felt true” and it hurt the other side.
How many times have we treated a public official made in the image of God like a meme to be dunked on?
How many times have we “honored” authority when it was our guy, and then felt spiritually justified to sneer when it wasn’t?

Church, Romans 13 is not mainly hunting for your vote. It’s coming for your conscience.
Because you can be right about policies and still be sinning in your spirit.
You can claim you love truth and still bear false witness with a forwarded post.
And you can say “Jesus is Lord” while your speech sounds like you’re discipled by outrage.
Lest we forget, this is not the only passage that talks this way. “You shall not bear false witness” (Ex. 20:16).) “Let your speech always be gracious” (Col. 4:6), “Put away… malice” (Eph. 4:31), “Honor everyone… honor the emperor” (1 Pet. 2:17),
So hold your questions. Don’t throw them away. Don’t suppress them. But don’t use them as a shield to avoid what the text is actually after: whether we can live under authority with a clean conscience—honoring, truthful, restrained—because Christ is our King.

Paul will answer it—not by pretending government is always righteous, but by taking you deeper than government. He takes you to God.

Because Paul is not building submission on the goodness of rulers. He’s building submission on the sovereignty of God.

II. The reason: God is the ultimate authority (v. 1b–2)

“For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God.”

Now this does not mean every ruler is godly. It does not mean every policy is righteous. It does not mean every use of power is holy.  ...   But it does mean something that is both comforting and terrifying: God is never absent from the throne-room of history. The Lord is not reacting. He is ruling. Even the authorities who don’t acknowledge Him still answer to Him.

And Paul goes even further: “Therefore whoever resists the authorities resists what God has appointed…” Now again—Paul cannot mean that any and all resistance is always sinful, because Scripture itself gives us righteous resistance when men command what God forbids or forbid what God commands.

But here is what Paul is condemning: He is condemning the rebellious spirit that says, “No one tells me what to do.” He is condemning the attitude that cannot live under authority anywhere—at home, at work, in church, in society—because the heart has never learned to bow. And Romans has already told us what that is. It is not a personality quirk.

It is sin. Sin is not only breaking rules - doing the things that displease God; Sin is resisting God's rule. Sin is the creature trying to be king. Sometimes we elevate one of God's created beings into a place that only God should hold. That too is sinful.

So Paul is saying, “Christian, you’ve been delivered from that old kingdom. You don’t live like that anymore.” And here is one of the clearest tests of whether you understand grace: A justified man can submit without panicking. Because he is not trying to save himself.

II. The general function of government: restrain evil, commend good (v. 3–4)
“For rulers are not a terror to good conduct, but to bad…” Let’s be honest. We all know examples when that doesn’t seem true. So what is Paul doing? He is describing God’s design for government. Government—at its best—functions like guardrails on a bridge.

Guardrails don’t make you love the road. Guardrails don’t make you a good driver. Guardrails don’t save your soul. But they restrain chaos. They limit destruction. They keep society from becoming pure anarchy. And in verse 4 Paul says something staggering about the governing authority:

“He is God’s servant…” That’s diakonos language. It’s the language of an instrument. A tool. Not a savior. Not a messiah. Not a lord. An instrument in the hands of the true Lord. And Paul says the ruler “does not bear the sword in vain.”

Meaning God has built into society a real mechanism to punish evil, restrain violence, and maintain order. Now listen carefully: this is not an endorsement of every war, every use of force, every policy, every abuse of power. But it is a clear statement that Christians are not anarchists.

We do not believe the world is sustained by nothing but individual autonomy and private preference. We believe God has ordained structures—and even imperfect ones—because human sin is real. And if you doubt that, just remove the guardrails in your mind for five minutes and ask what society would become.

Paul is saying: you are not dealing with something random. You are dealing with a God-ordained reality that, in God’s design, is meant to restrain evil.

IV. The motive: conscience, not just consequences (v. 5)

“Therefore one must be in subjection, not only to avoid God’s wrath but also for the sake of conscience.” This is where the text gets personal. Paul says: the Christian is not merely thinking, “Will I get caught?” The Christian is thinking: “What honors God?” The Christian is governed from the inside. Not by mere fear of punishment, but by conscience before God.

This is Romans 12 in public.

This is worship in a world that doesn’t call it worship. This is integrity when nobody claps.

So yes—Christians should obey laws about taxes, and regulations, and basic civic order. But we obey differently than the world obeys. 

Not just because we don’t want trouble.

But because we live before the face of God.

Because our conscience belongs to Christ.

V. The practice: pay what is owed (v. 6–7)

“For because of this you also pay taxes…” Tax season is coming up, And now Paul goes straight to the place everybody hates. Taxes. Revenue. Respect. Honor.

And he says: pay what is owed. And he also says: give respect and honor where it is due. Now note what he doesn’t say. He does not say, “Agree with everything.” He does not say, “Never criticize.” He does not say, “Pretend rulers are righteous.” But he does say: don’t become crooked. Don’t become deceitful. Don’t become dishonorable. Don’t become the kind of person who sings loudly on Sunday and then cheats quietly on Monday.

And don’t become the kind of Christian who thinks the gospel gives you a pass to be a lawless citizen. It doesn’t.

Paying what is owed includes paying respect to the officials God has put over us. Whether they are red or blue. Donkeys or Elephants. Their politics party doesn’t factor in to God’s command to be respectful. Can I get an Amen? 

Some of you today are saying AMEN with enthusiasm while your man is in office. I fully expect the same spirit of respect when not your man is in office.

Grace doesn’t produce rebels.

Grace produces sons.

That starts at the cross.
