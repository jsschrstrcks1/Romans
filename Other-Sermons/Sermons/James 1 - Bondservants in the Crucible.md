# James 1 — Bondservants in the Crucible

*Raw notes — James 1:1–4*

*Thematic anchor: "The testing of your faith produces steadfastness." (James 1:3)*

---

## Opening

I've been thinking about Hurricane Idalia today. As one does when one loses their house in a storm — and then watches another one come up the coast.

That had me thinking about James chapter 1. And James naturally feeds into our third article from the Baptist Faith and Message — which addresses what the Bible says about mankind.

> Man is the special creation of God, made in His own image. He created them male and female as the crowning work of His creation. The gift of gender is thus part of the goodness of God's creation. In the beginning man was innocent of sin and was endowed by his Creator with freedom of choice. By his free choice man sinned against God and brought sin into the human race. Through the temptation of Satan man transgressed the command of God, and fell from his original innocence whereby his posterity inherit a nature and an environment inclined toward sin. Therefore, as soon as they are capable of moral action, they become transgressors and are under condemnation. Only the grace of God can bring man into His holy fellowship and enable man to fulfill the creative purpose of God. The sacredness of human personality is evident in that God created man in His own image, and in that Christ died for man; therefore, every person of every race possesses full dignity and is worthy of respect and Christian love.
>
> — Baptist Faith and Message, Article III

In a lot of ways we are like a little child — crying, afraid of the dark, dirty from sin. God is the heavenly Father who takes us by the hand and cleans us up.

Let's read James 1.

---

## I. James Announces Himself as a Bondservant — δοῦλος (v. 1)

**James 1:1 (ESV)**

> "James, a servant of God and of the Lord Jesus Christ, To the twelve tribes in the Dispersion: Greetings."

The word translated "servant" there is δοῦλος — *doulos*. It means slave. Bond-slave. Someone who belongs entirely to another — with no independent rights of their own. In this context, James is saying something very specific about himself: he sees himself as one who belongs to Christ. Everything he has, every ability he carries, the letter he is about to write — all of it is the Lord's.

That distinction matters. We see servant as a verb — as something you *do*. In the Bible it's a noun. Being a servant isn't what James *does*. It's who he *is*.

The purchase element — that we have been *bought* — is not the bare meaning of the Greek word. That comes from Paul: "You were bought with a price." First Corinthians 6:20. The two ideas go together. We belong to Christ by blood, by purchase, by His death. That ownership shapes everything.

So what does that look like in practice?

If you had a servant, what would it look like? Someone washing your dishes? Doing your laundry? Mowing the lawn? Maybe something a little more upscale — meeting you at the door with a warm towel, handling the silver, cooking, cleaning. A servant's job is to do whatever the master asks. Right?

The Bible speaks of us as servants of God. A few passages worth hearing side by side.

**Romans 6:22 (ESV)**

> "But now that you have been set free from sin and have become slaves of God, the fruit you get leads to sanctification and its end, eternal life."

**Luke 12:35–36 (ESV)**

> "Stay dressed for action and keep your lamps burning, and be like men who are waiting for their master to come home from the wedding feast, so that they may open the door to him at once when he comes and knocks."

**1 Peter 2:15–16 (ESV)**

> "For this is the will of God, that by doing good you should put to silence the ignorance of foolish people. Live as people who are free, not using your freedom as a cover-up for evil, but living as servants of God."

Dozens of passages in Scripture speak of Moses, of Paul, of ordinary believers as the servants of God.

Now let me ask you something — and this isn't me trying to make anyone feel guilty. It's a matter between you and God. If Jesus were to look at your life today, would He find He had a good servant?

I know people in this room do a lot. People mow, and clean, and decorate, and answer phones, and give out backpacks. People teach kids and lead Bible studies. People fill in when I'm out of town. People run the soundboard, the camera, the computer. You have my sincere gratitude. That's not what I'm getting at.

Being a servant is more than accomplishing physical tasks the master assigns. Did any of you watch *Downton Abbey*? The servants in a house like that were expected to carry themselves a certain way — because their behavior reflected on the master they served. Their conduct was a statement about the household.

Just between you and God. Right here. Right now.

How does your behavior reflect on God? Are you doing what He's called you to do? Are you acting as He's called you to act?

Fair to say we all fall short there. All of us — including me — have places where we could better honor our Savior through the way we live.

Here's a mirror worth picking up:

**Galatians 5:19–21 (ESV)**

> "Now the works of the flesh are evident: sexual immorality, impurity, sensuality, idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, divisions, envy, drunkenness, orgies, and things like these. I warn you, as I warned you before, that those who do such things will not inherit the kingdom of God."

Sexual immorality — who can define that? Any sex outside the confines of marriage. And marriage is one man and one woman. Not two men. Not two women. Not three or ten or however many people. One man. One woman. The Baptist Faith and Message is plain about this, and more importantly, Scripture is plain about it.

We are called to be God's servants. That means we yield our self-determination in the matters the Bible addresses and are obedient to our Master.

A few things worth noting about first-century servants — because James's original readers would have heard δοῦλος and felt the full weight of it:

- In first-century Italy, approximately one in three people were slaves. Across the empire, roughly one in five. (Holman Bible Dictionary)
- Slaves had no legal rights of their own.
- They were often well treated — sometimes better circumstances than some freedmen. Not always, but often.
- Slavery in the first century could result from war, captured enemies, or unpayable debt.

Paul calls himself *doulos* too. Think about that for a moment. Paul, who wrote more than half the New Testament. Paul, who had seen the risen Lord on the road to Damascus. Paul, who had been caught up into the third heaven. He could have been puffed up about that resume. He wasn't. He saw himself — and everything he did — as his reasonable service. A servant purchased with a heavy price.

Same as us.

We have been bought with a price. It was our actions that required the death of Christ. Our sin caused that. So shall we continue in sin that grace may abound? May it never be. Take a hard look at yourself in the mirror of Scripture and see if your life honors the price that was paid for you.

## II. God Lets His Servants Go Through the Crucible — (vv. 2–4)

**James 1:2–4 (ESV)**

> "Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds, for you know that the testing of your faith produces steadfastness. And let steadfastness have its full effect, that you may be perfect and complete, lacking in nothing."

Two of my female cousins joined the Marine Corps a number of years back. They did exceptionally well. One became an aircraft mechanic. The other a combat engineer — using heavy equipment to dig trenches and fortifications to protect other soldiers. But before they could call themselves Marines, they had to go through basic training. And the penultimate week of basic ends with the Crucible.

A crucible is a metalworking tool. You put the metal you want to work in the crucible, and then you put the whole thing in the fire. You heat it until the metal melts. Then you scrape the impurities off. What's left is cleaner, stronger, more useful than what went in.

For the Marines, it looks like this: a forced march of roughly 45 miles over 54 hours. Little sleep. Little food. Little rest. They are tested mentally, physically, morally. Heated up and scraped.

If someone ever tells you becoming a Christian will make life easier, you can know right there they are deceived — or something worse. We *will* meet trials. James doesn't say "if you meet trials." He says "when you meet trials of various kinds."

*[Personal testimony: Flood — memory of the night of. Memory of Grandpa's death. Guy's death. Lindsey's death.]*

I think about my nephew, afraid of the wind. I took him by the hand and walked through it with him. Showed him it wasn't something to fear. He was still scared. But we walked through it together.

That's what I think of when I picture Christ walking beside us in the fire. Holding our hand through something that makes us cry, and confused, and sometimes angry with Him — because we don't understand. His ways are higher than ours. And He doesn't let go.

Joseph Gilmore wrote this in 1862. I picture it every time:

> He leadeth me! O blessed thought!
> O words with heav'nly comfort fraught!
> Whate'er I do, where'er I be,
> Still 'tis God's hand that leadeth me.
>
> He leadeth me, He leadeth me;
> By His own hand He leadeth me:
> His faithful foll'wer I would be,
> For by His hand He leadeth me.
>
> Sometimes 'mid scenes of deepest gloom,
> Sometimes where Eden's bowers bloom,
> By waters calm, o'er troubled sea —
> Still 'tis His hand that leadeth me.
>
> Lord, I would clasp Thy hand in mine,
> Nor ever murmur nor repine;
> Content, whatever lot I see,
> Since 'tis my God that leadeth me.
>
> And when my task on earth is done,
> When, by Thy grace, the victory's won,
> E'en death's cold wave I will not flee,
> Since God through Jordan leadeth me.

What a God we have.

### Trials Lead to Deeper Holiness

God doesn't put His servants through the crucible for nothing. He does it to deepen their endurance — but also to deepen their holiness.

Another passage speaks of being pruned like a vine. Things that offend God get cut off and burned away. Scripture talks of being refined like gold in the fire — heated up and scraped, to produce something more beautiful and more useful for His purposes. Sometimes that means things we love, but shouldn't, get burned away.

*Dottie* — how was the year of the leg, when God put you square in the middle of the hottest part of the fire? Was it fun? And yet God used it for your increased sanctification. He deepened your faith, taught you to trust Him more deeply, love Him more dearly, see Him more clearly. When you said — if God wants a one-legged saint, or a dead saint, or a crippled saint, He can have me — that was another way of saying: *Your will, not mine.* It's a hard thing to have our stubborn will subdued by God. But sometimes it's the best thing that can ever happen to us. Not fun. Not pleasant. But worth every fire.

### Steadfastness — ὑπομένω

The third thing the fiery crucible produces in us is steadfastness.

James says it in verse 3: "the testing of your faith produces steadfastness."

That Greek word — *hupomeno* (ὑπομένω) — the lexicons define it as "the capacity to hold out or bear up in the face of difficulty, patience, endurance, fortitude, steadfastness, perseverance." It's a combination of stability and staying power.

**Christ as the model:**

**Hebrews 12:1–3 (ESV)**

> "Therefore, since we are surrounded by so great a cloud of witnesses, let us also lay aside every weight, and sin which clings so closely, and let us run with endurance the race that is set before us, looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith, who for the joy that was set before him endured the cross, despising the shame, and is seated at the right hand of the throne of God. Consider him who endured from sinners such hostility against himself, so that you may not grow weary or fainthearted."

Look at what Christ endured. It started with being born into a stinky, dirty manger — probably a cave beneath the main house, with little privacy for Mary as she gave birth among the animals. He went from King in His Kingdom to a baby in a feed trough. From issuing orders to requiring someone to wipe His backside. From all-powerful to helpless infant.

Then He grew up. At the end of His life, He was cheered as Messiah one week and scorned the next. They falsely accused Him. He had a sham of a trial and was condemned to one of the cruelest deaths ever devised. Because of our sin He was beaten nearly to death. Mocked. Humiliated. Forced to carry His cross up that cursed hill. They nailed Him to it, dropped it in the hole, and left Him to die slowly over many hours by suffocation.

At any point, He could have called a legion of angels. He could have said: *But what about my rights? My economy! My political cause!* He could have demanded fair treatment, and none of us would have called it unjust. And yet our example — our Savior — quietly bore the abuse, accepted it, and used it to bring glory to His Father.

He is the ultimate in perseverance. And because of what He did on that awful day, the lost have a chance at salvation.

If He could bear that — who are we to complain when we become uncomfortable?

*(See also Matthew 17:17ff — read at home)*

**Christians and perseverance:**

**Matthew 10:22 (ESV)**

> "and you will be hated by all for my name's sake. But the one who endures to the end will be saved."

**John 15:4–5 (ESV)**

> "Abide in me, and I in you. As the branch cannot bear fruit by itself, unless it abides in the vine, neither can you, unless you abide in me. I am the vine; you are the branches. Whoever abides in me and I in him, he it is that bears much fruit, for apart from me you can do nothing."

We must stay connected to Jesus. He is our power source. Our source of strength, and peace, and joy. He is our only hope in the midst of the storm. And we know storms are coming.

**Perseverance is the result of suffering:**

**Romans 5:3–4 (ESV)**

> "Not only that, but we rejoice in our sufferings, knowing that suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character, and character produces hope."

Perseverance demonstrates the depth of our belief to the world around us. What if how you hold up — or don't hold up — under trials and troubles of various kinds is the only Bible someone around you ever reads? Will they see Jesus through your example in the midst of the storm?

**Noah:**

I can almost hear the conversation. *Hey Noah, what are you doing?* Building an ark. *A what?* An ark. *What on earth is that, and why?* Because God told me to. It's a type of boat. *What's a boat?*

Took Noah maybe as long as 75 years to build something no one understood, for a catastrophe he had never seen and likely had a hard time imagining — all because God asked it of him. Perseverance indeed.

## III. Steadfastness: Our Rock Does Not Move

**1 Corinthians 15:57–58 (ESV)**

> "But thanks be to God, who gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ. Therefore, my beloved brothers, be steadfast, immovable, always abounding in the work of the Lord, knowing that in the Lord your labor is not in vain."

Death has no sting. We have the victory because of Jesus Christ. *Therefore, be steadfast.*

Our steadfastness — our stability — is grounded in God's unchanging nature. Scripture after Scripture says the same thing: God is constant, His word stands, He is always there, He is our rock.

> Isaiah 40:8 — "The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God will stand forever."
> Psalm 62:2 — "He alone is my rock and my salvation, my fortress; I shall not be greatly shaken."
> Isaiah 26:4 — "Trust in the Lord forever, for the Lord God is an everlasting rock."

**Matthew 7:24–27 (ESV)**

> "Everyone then who hears these words of mine and does them will be like a wise man who built his house on the rock. And the rain fell, and the floods came, and the winds blew and beat on that house, but it did not fall, because it had been founded on the rock. And everyone who hears these words of mine and does not do them will be like a foolish man who built his house on the sand. And the rain fell, and the floods came, and the winds blew and beat against that house, and it fell, and great was the fall of it."

If your house is built on anything other than Christ — might I suggest you are not yet a believer in Jesus Christ? If your efforts are based solely on your own strength, if you find yourself exhausted, having toiled and strained against the world on your own — Jesus brings rest. You can build your house on Him and rest. Because:

**Steadfastness gives resistance to spiritual assault:**

**1 Corinthians 16:13 (ESV)**

> "Be watchful, stand firm in the faith, act like men, be strong."

**Colossians 1:21–23 (ESV)**

> "And you, who once were alienated and hostile in mind, doing evil deeds, he has now reconciled in his body of flesh by his death, in order to present you holy and blameless and above reproach before him, if indeed you continue in the faith, stable and steadfast, not shifting from the hope of the gospel that you heard, which has been proclaimed in all creation under heaven, and of which I, Paul, became a minister."

**Psalm 20:7–8 (ESV)**

> "Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the Lord our God. They collapse and fall, but we rise and stand upright."

Practical tools for steadfastness under spiritual assault:
- Put on the full armor of God
- Quote Scripture — speak it out loud in the middle of the storm
- Remember God's fulfilled promises in your own life
- Keep a higher focus. Look at God, not the waves.
