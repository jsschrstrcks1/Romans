## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastoral Sketch That Never Became a Sermon — Sincere Shepherding Without Sustained Exposition**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm, context-sensitive pastoral introduction to a prayer service that connects genuine congregational need to Psalm 91's promises, but does not exposit the psalm, does not press the conscience, and ends before it has fully begun.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**FAIL — with pastoral grace acknowledged.**

Scopus Test: The sermon cannot be assigned a thesis that demonstrably arises from Psalm 91's own argument. The preacher identifies verse 1 as the thesis ("stay close to God and he will give you rest") — which is a reasonable summary of the psalm's posture — but the sermon does not develop that thesis from the text. The bulk of the content consists of listing current events, listing the psalm's promises in catalog form, and transitioning to corporate prayer. There is no point developed from the passage's own movement.

Authority Test: The preacher does not stand above the text as an editor. But he also does not stand under it as a herald. He gestures toward the text and then moves past it to congregational concerns and prayer logistics.

**Verdict**: The Berean Gate fails. This is not a sermon — it is a pastoral prayer introduction with scriptural framing. That is a legitimate and valuable thing for a pastor to do on a prayer service Sunday. But it is not a sermon, and evaluating it as one produces a failing gate. This evaluation will proceed but will heavily apply the Rubric Fit Note to contextualize the score.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score capped at 69.**

- **Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The sermon gestures toward Christ ("only those who know Jesus") and invites post-service conversation, but the finished work of Christ is not proclaimed. There is no articulation of justification, atonement, or saving faith. The one evangelistic moment ("If you don't yet know Jesus, come talk to me after the service") is the entirety of the gospel content — and it is a referral, not a proclamation.
- **Flag 2 — Main point does not arise from the text's argument**: As noted in the Berean Gate, the sermon's movement is driven by congregational context and prayer service logistics, not by Psalm 91's own argument.
- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon ends with an invitation to corporate prayer, which is appropriate for a prayer service but does not constitute a sermonic exhortation. No specific imperative is issued, no conscience is summoned, no specific belief or repentance or pursuit is called for.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 5/25**
- Textual Fidelity: 1/10. The psalm's promises are referenced in a rapid catalog ("he gives rest, peace, protection, shelter, camouflage") and a few verses are paraphrased, but no verse is opened. Psalm 91 is used as a backdrop, not as a text to be preached.
- Contextual Awareness: 1/5. The psalm's genre (wisdom psalm, dwelling in the shelter of the Most High), its probable liturgical function, its relationship to Psalm 90 (Moses' lament), and its fulfillment in Christ's own use of it during the temptation (Matthew 4) — none of this is present.
- Exegetical Clarity: 1/5. No exegetical work is visible. The text is paraphrased correctly but not opened.
- Logical Faithfulness: 2/5. What is said about the text is not wrong. The problem is omission, not commission.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 9/18**
- Christ-Centeredness: 2/7. Christ is mentioned minimally. The psalm's promises are applied to the Christian's present experience without engaging how Christ fulfills the psalm or grounds the promises. The "only those who know Jesus" line gestures at the exclusivity of the covenant but does not develop it.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3. None. The psalm is treated as direct application to the present congregation without any awareness of its original Old Covenant context or its New Covenant fulfillment.
- Doctrinal Precision: 3/4. What is stated is stated correctly — the already/not yet eschatology is a genuine and sound doctrinal contribution, well-deployed. "In this world you will have troubles but take heart for I have overcome the world" is appropriately cited.
- Confessional Alignment: 3/4. No contradictions of confessional theology are present. The already/not yet framing and the exclusive covenant language are healthy instincts.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 4/15**
- Clarity of the Gospel: 1/5. The gospel is referenced but not proclaimed. The single line inviting post-service conversation cannot carry the weight of gospel proclamation.
- Necessity of Christ: 1/5. Christ appears as the one who must be "known" for the psalm's promises to apply, but this relationship is asserted rather than grounded in his person and work.
- Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5. The sermon's warmth is genuine and its pastoral instincts are sound, but neither the cross nor Christ's work is the gravitational center. The center is congregational need and God's comfort — which is not wrong, but it is not the gospel.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 5/15**
- Heart Penetration: 2/5. The listing of specific congregational needs (job loss, melanoma diagnosis, travel, illness) is a genuine pastoral act and creates emotional connection. The people feel known. But feeling known is not the same as being pressed by the text.
- Specificity: 2/5. The congregational specificity is the sermon's strongest pastoral feature — the preacher knows his people. But the specificity is circumstantial (naming needs) rather than spiritual (pressing the heart's response to those needs through the psalm).
- Spiritual Differentiation: 1/5. No differentiation of hearers. All are addressed as people with needs who need God's protection. The person who doubts God's care, the person whose faith in protection has been shaken by suffering, the person who is trusting in something other than God — none are addressed distinctly.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 2/8**
- Call to Response: 1/4. The implicit call is "pray" — and the entire service structure culminates in prayer, which is a response. But no specific exhortation arises from the text. The congregation is not summoned to believe a particular promise more fully, to repent of self-sufficiency, or to take a specific step of faith.
- Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 1/4. The prayer list includes evangelism and the persecuted church, which shows a healthy vision. But the sermon does not equip for either — it mentions them and defers to prayer.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 4/9**

- Progression: 1/3. The sermon does not build toward anything — it accumulates material and then transitions to corporate prayer. The humpback whale illustration, in particular, does significant work before the text is introduced but its connection to Psalm 91 or prayer is not clear.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**

- Clarity: 1/2. The illustration makes its point about God's design clearly, but it occupies too much real estate relative to what it contributes to the sermon's main thrust.
- Memorability: 1/1. The humpback whale detail is memorable. Whether it will be associated with Psalm 91 or prayer is a different question.

**8. Sermonic Force — 2/5**
- Reads as Preaching: 1/2. The warm, conversational, pastoral register is appropriate for this occasion. But the manuscript reads more like a pastoral address and prayer guide introduction than a proclamation.
- Conviction on the Page: 1/2. The conviction is pastoral — the preacher clearly cares for his people and knows their needs. Theological conviction in the sense of pressing God's Word with authority is mostly absent.
- Cadence and Breath: 0/1. The rhythm is conversational but unstructured. Sentences run together, transition abruptly, and the compression that signals a climactic moment in preaching is absent.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Primarily my circumstances — my needs, my congregation's needs, the world's dangers. Christ appears as the solution and the gatekeeper of the promises, but he is not magnified.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas about him, warmly delivered. God's character is asserted (refuge, shelter, strong tower) but not unfolded in a way that produces encounter.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Warmly acknowledged — the congregation's specific needs are named, which creates a sense of pastoral care. But neither conviction nor the deeper comfort of the gospel (as distinct from the comfort of being known by the pastor) is achieved.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Genuine pastoral knowledge of the congregation.** The preacher names specific real needs — job loss, melanoma, travel, illness — and this creates authentic pastoral warmth. This is not a generic sermon addressed to "whoever might be listening." It is addressed to actual people with actual names. That is a genuine virtue.

2. **The already/not yet eschatological instinct.** The observation that Psalm 91's promises foreshadow heaven — where the enemies can't even get near the gates — is theologically sound and pastorally helpful. "What is an assault at our doorstep here will be a distant enemy in glory" is the sharpest theological sentence in the sermon. This instinct should be developed.

3. **The covenant restriction ("only those who know his name").** The preacher correctly identifies that Psalm 91's promises are not universal but covenantal — they belong to those in relationship with the Lord. This is exegetically responsible and prevents the psalm from being used as a therapeutic promise to all.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Psalm 91 is announced but never opened.** The congregation heard a list of the psalm's promises in summary form. They did not hear what those promises mean, why they are true, how they are grounded in God's covenant faithfulness, or what it looks like to actually "dwell in the shelter of the Most High." The psalm was used as a prayer guide framework rather than as a text to be proclaimed.

2. **The gospel is a referral, not a proclamation.** "If you don't yet know Jesus, come talk to me after the service" is a genuinely pastoral instinct, but it is not a gospel presentation. On a prayer service Sunday with mixed attendance, the one moment where the gospel might land carries no content. What must someone do to "know Jesus"? What has Christ done? Why does it matter? The sermon does not say.

3. **The humpback whale introduction consumes disproportionate space.** The intelligent design argument via humpback whale hydrodynamics is interesting, but it is the longest developed idea in the sermon and its connection to Psalm 91, prayer, or the congregation's specific needs is never made. It functions as a preaching warm-up — the preacher thinking out loud before arriving at the text — rather than as a purposeful introduction.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that naming congregational needs is not the same as pressing the Word into congregational needs. There is a significant difference between a pastor saying "I know you lost your job, and God is your refuge" and a preacher opening Psalm 91:2 — "I will say to the Lord, 'My refuge and my fortress, my God, in whom I trust'" — and asking: "Do you say that? Can you say that right now? What is in your heart when trouble comes — is God your first thought or your last resort?" The first is pastoral care. The second is preaching. Both are needed. On Sunday, the second is what the pulpit exists to do.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The specificity of the congregational references creates the feeling of deep pastoral connection, and it is genuine — but it can mask the absence of actual applicatory force from the text. A congregation that hears their names and needs mentioned may feel that the sermon has "gotten" them, when in fact the text has not yet been brought to bear on those needs with any precision. The congregation felt cared for; they were not necessarily challenged by the psalm.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Psalm 91:1 — "He who dwells in the shelter of the Most High will abide in the shadow of the Almighty" — is a conditional promise with a location requirement. "Dwells" is not passive. The psalm is not a blanket promise to all who feel anxious; it is a promise to those who have made God their habitation (v.9: "Because you have made the Lord your dwelling place..."). This is the pastoral hinge the sermon needed: "Are you dwelling there? Not visiting. Not attending. Not sitting on the edge. Dwelling. Have you made God your refuge — not your last resort, not your emergency contact, but your actual address?" This question, pressed into the specific congregational needs named in the introduction, would have been a genuinely searching sermon. It was not asked.

Likewise, Matthew 4 — where Satan quotes this very psalm to Jesus during the temptation — is one of the most remarkable passages in the New Testament. Christ is the true Dweller in the shadow of the Almighty. He fulfilled the psalm perfectly, resisted the temptation to test its promises, and by his covenant faithfulness has secured those promises for all who are in him. This Christological fulfillment was entirely absent. It would have grounded the gospel presentation the sermon needed.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Replace the humpback whale introduction with an introduction that establishes the stakes of Psalm 91 directly.** The congregation has real, named needs. Begin there: "Job loss. Melanoma. Illness. Kids shot in the street. War rumors. These are the things your week looked like. And Psalm 91 says there is a man who will look at all of that and say — 'He is my refuge and my fortress. My God, in whom I trust.' That is the man this psalm is written for. My question this morning is whether that is you." The introduction does the work and sets up the entire sermon without the detour.

2. **Develop one promise from Psalm 91 with full exegetical, gospel, and applicatory force.** Rather than listing all of the psalm's promises in catalog form, slow down on verse 1-2 and verse 14-16 (God's own direct speech — "Because he holds fast to me in love..."). Let those two bookends carry the sermon: our posture (dwelling, trusting, calling) and God's response (deliverance, protection, satisfaction, salvation). Show the congregation what "dwelling" looks like and what it costs to make God your actual refuge.

3. **Proclaim the gospel before the referral.** Before "come talk to me after the service," say what someone needs to know. In two or three sentences: "Jesus Christ, the Son of God, lived the perfect life of trust in the Father that this psalm describes. He died on the cross bearing the punishment for our rebellion — the rebellion that cuts us off from God's refuge. He rose from the dead, and everyone who trusts in him is brought back into the shelter of the Most High. That is how you come to know the name of the Lord. If that is new to you, I would love to talk with you after the service." That is a gospel proclamation followed by a pastoral invitation.

4. **Ask the dwelling question explicitly as the sermon's one pointed application.** "Before we go to prayer — some of you have learned to run to God when the crisis arrives. That is good. But Psalm 91 is not written for visitors. It is written for dwellers. It describes a person for whom God is the permanent address, not the emergency number. The question is not whether you believe in God. The question is whether you live there. Are you at home in God today — or are you just visiting when things get hard?"

5. **Use the prayer service structure as the sermon's exhortation.** After the one pointed application, the transition to corporate prayer becomes an exhortation: "The way you respond to this Word today is by bringing everything — the melanoma, the job, the travel, the fears about war, the neighbor who won't come to church — and laying it at the feet of the God who says 'I will be with him in trouble.' That is what we are going to do right now." The prayer service becomes the sermon's "therefore." This unifies the structure and gives the pastoral occasion the weight it deserves.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This is the most important note in this evaluation. Psalm 91 is a psalm — a poem of trust with a liturgical function — and this sermon was preached in the context of a prayer service, not a regular expository preaching service. The rubric was designed primarily for expository sermon evaluation, and it does not bend gracefully around what is essentially a pastoral prayer service introduction. The score of 34 is accurate as an assessment of this document as a sermon. It is not an accurate assessment of whether the pastor did the right thing on this Sunday. A prayer service with warm pastoral framing, a biblical text, covenant application, and extended corporate intercession may have been exactly what the congregation needed. The rubric cannot evaluate that. What the rubric can and does identify is this: if this service included a prepared manuscript meant to function as a sermon, the manuscript needs significant development. If it was always intended as a prayer service introduction rather than a sermon, it should be labeled and filed as such — and a future sermon on Psalm 91 should be written separately, with the full exposition the psalm deserves.
