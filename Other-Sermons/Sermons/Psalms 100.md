## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluated**: 2026-03-21
**Passage**: Psalm 100
**Working Title**: Psalm 100 — Exalt and Know

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Cheerful Structuralist Who Forgot to Preach"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A genuinely creative engagement with Psalm 100's mnemonic architecture that illuminates the psalm's form with pastoral warmth, but spends so much energy on structure and illustration that the sermon never arrives at gospel proclamation, conscience-searching, or actual exhortation.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Pass — with significant qualification.**

The main point of the sermon — that Psalm 100 alternates between exaltation and knowledge as a teaching device, and that we should worship God with gladness as His people — is a defensible reading of the text. The preacher submits to the psalm rather than using it as a launch pad. However, the Scopus Test reveals a problem: the sermon's driving concern is the mnemonic structure (exalt/know pattern) rather than the psalm's substantive claims about who God is and why He deserves worship. The structural observation is accurate but it becomes the sermon's center of gravity rather than a doorway into the text's theology.

**Scopus Test**: "Psalm 100 is designed to help us remember how to worship God — alternating between exaltation and knowledge." — This arises from the text but describes the vessel, not the cargo. The psalm's actual claim — "know that the LORD, he is God; it is he who made us, and we are his" — receives secondary treatment. Passes by a narrow margin.
**Authority Test**: The preacher does not edit or soften the text. Passes.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary.** The sermon is structurally completable without Christ. The passage from "Evangelism starts with the gospel" at the end indicates Christ was to be introduced, but He is not present in the sermon body. The brief mention that access to God cost "the death of Christ" (line 79) is a passing clause, not a proclamation. A devout Jew who believes in God's covenant and His creation of Israel could receive this entire sermon without offense.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The sermon ends with "Evangelism starts with the gospel." — a statement about evangelism, not a summoning of the congregation to any action. No clear "therefore" moment exists. The congregation is informed about the psalm's structure, encouraged toward worship, and reminded of God's access — but never called to a specific response.

**Score capped at 69.**

1. **The exalt/know structural analysis** — The identification of Psalm 100's alternating pattern is a genuine exegetical contribution that equips the congregation to understand how Psalms work and why structure serves theology. This is the sermon's most original and textually grounded move.
2. **The sheep illustration from personal experience** — Extended, concrete, and drawn from the preacher's own farm. ### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is absent from the sermon body.** The last line — "Evangelism starts with the gospel." — is a tautological placeholder, not a proclamation. The word "Christ" appears once in a subordinate clause about His death enabling access. The psalm's claim that "the LORD is good" invites the fullest possible meaning of that goodness — which the New Testament resolves at the cross. That resolution is missing.
2. **Psalm 100's key theological terms receive no development.** The psalm hinges on three words: יְהוָה (YHWH — covenant name), טוֹב (tov — good), and חֶסֶד (hesed — steadfast covenant love). "His steadfast love endures forever" (v. 5) is the psalm's climax and the most theologically loaded line in the passage. The sermon acknowledges it exists but does not open it. The *hesed* of YHWH is the entire reason His sheep can worship with gladness — it is the covenant foundation of everything the psalm celebrates. It receives one sentence.
3. **The exhortation to worship with gladness is undermined by the sermon's own emotional tone.** The psalm commands joyful, loud, enthusiastic worship. The sermon, though warm, is more conversational than doxological. The preacher acknowledges that some people can't feel joyful and encourages them to ask God for it — which is true — but this becomes the sermon's dominant mood. A sermon on Psalm 100 should leave the congregation wanting to shout. This one leaves them pleasantly informed.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the mnemonic structure, while legitimately illuminating, has become the sermon's subject rather than its servant. The sermon is about Psalm 100's form more than it is about Psalm 100's content. The structural observation serves the preacher's teaching instincts — which are genuinely good — but the congregation needs to hear what the psalm says, not primarily how it says it.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sheep illustration creates a strong impression of pastoral depth. It is detailed, concrete, and emotionally resonant. But it is essentially an illustration of God's general providential care — it does not connect to the psalm's covenant context, does not introduce Christ as the Good Shepherd, and does not move toward the cross. The impression of theological richness exceeds the actual theological content delivered.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 5: "For the LORD is good; his steadfast love endures forever, and his faithfulness to all generations." The word *hesed* here is not general benevolence — it is covenant loyalty, the specific, unbreakable love that God pledged to His people and that the New Testament identifies as the love that sent His Son. This verse is the psalm's doctrinal summit, the reason every prior verse can make its demands. The sermon summarizes it in one sentence: "God still chose to love us even though Adam and Eve and every human being since has let God down." This is true but small. The full weight of *hesed* — the covenant love that moved God to absorb His own wrath in Christ — is the sermon's great missed climax.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach *hesed* at v. 5.** Give the congregation the Hebrew word and its weight. Steadfast love is not sentiment — it is covenant commitment that cannot be broken. In Christ, God has fulfilled that *hesed* completely. This is where the gospel belongs in this sermon — not as a closing addendum but as the reason worship is possible.
2. **Connect the sheep imagery to John 10.** "We are the sheep of his pasture" (v. 3) → "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" (John 10:11). The psalm announces the relationship; Christ defines what it cost. Run this thread explicitly.
3. **Close with a call to worship, not a statement about evangelism.** Psalm 100 is a psalm of entrance into the temple. The congregation should be driven to corporate, doxological response. End with something like: "Shout for joy to the LORD. You are His people. He made you. His steadfast love endures forever. Now go worship — and take someone with you who doesn't know this yet."
4. **State the Fallen Condition Focus plainly.** "The problem this psalm addresses is that we are creatures prone to self-obsession and discouragement who need to be commanded back into worship. And the cure is not more effort — it is knowing who God is and what He has done."
5. **Develop the Elijah illustration.** The "eat and take a nap" moment is genuinely pastoral and connects to physical-emotional barriers to worship. But it is currently a mild aside. Give it one more sentence that drives toward the text: "Even the great prophet Elijah needed to be nourished before he could encounter God. How much more do we need the bread of life and the rest that only Christ provides before we can come before Him with joyful songs?"

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries genuine pastoral warmth and a love for the congregation, but it does not carry eternal gravity — the cross is absent, the covenant is unnamed, and the sermon does not arrive at a place where the congregation is overwhelmed by the goodness of God as revealed in Christ. The sheep illustration reaches toward unction; the gospel placeholder prevents it from arriving.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 100 is a short Todah (thanksgiving) psalm — its brevity and liturgical purpose mean it rewards theological depth over structural breadth. The rubric fits well. The mnemonic-structure analysis is a legitimate and useful addition to standard homiletical approaches, and the rubric's Exposition category rightly rewards it. However, the rubric's Gospel Centrality pressure is appropriate: Psalm 100's "enter his gates" language finds its fullest meaning in John 10 and Hebrews 10, and a Reformed exposition of this psalm should not miss that trajectory.
