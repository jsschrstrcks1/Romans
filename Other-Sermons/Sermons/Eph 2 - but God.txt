Alright. We're going to try to keep it short today. We're going to look at the next article in the Baptist Faith and Message — the theology of man, which theologians call anthropology. One of the things I love about theological anthropology — the study of what the Bible says about man — is that in showing us who we are, it also shows us a bit more about who God is.

The BF&M statement has more in it than we can fully cover today, so we're going to dive into a chapter that addresses a good deal of it. Open with me to Ephesians chapter 2.

While you're turning there, our unreached people group of the week:

**The Malay of Indonesia**

The Malay people are believed to have originated in Borneo and then spread outward into Sumatra and other regions. They descend from Austronesian-speakers who migrated from the Philippines and originally from Taiwan. Malays practiced Buddhism, Hinduism, and animism before converting to Islam in the 15th century.

That long commitment to Islam forms the core of Malay ethnic identity. To change religions is to risk your place in family and society. Pray for the few Christian believers among the Malay — that they will have Scripture in their language and resources to help them grow. Pray they keep the unity of the Spirit in the bond of peace. Pray the Malay of Indonesia will hunger to know Jesus — whom Muslims call Isa al-Masih — and will be drawn to him. Pray their hunger will be satisfied, and that the Spirit of Christ will help them.

I want to give you a bird's-eye view of what Ephesians says about us apart from Christ. Hang in there — it is quite a list.

Walkers in iniquity. Followers of the world. Followers of Satan. Sons of disobedience. Followers of the passions of our flesh and the desires of our body and mind. Children of wrath. Strangers. Hopeless. Wandered astray. Separated from God. Cut off from the Father. Without the ability to redeem ourselves. Orphans without inheritance. Unenlightened — darkened in heart. Weak. Undeserving of grace. Children tossed to and fro by the waves, carried about by every strange doctrine on the winds, swayed by human cunning and deceitful scheming. Futile in thinking. Darkened in understanding. Alienated from God. Ignorant. Hard-hearted. Callous. Greedy. Lustful. Impure. A corpse. Corrupted and deceitful. Desiring counterfeits. Thieves. Corrupt in speech. Bitter. Full of wrath, anger, clamor, and slander. Malicious. Unkind. Hard-hearted and unforgiving. Sexually immoral. Impure. Covetous. Filthy in speech. Foolish. Crude. Idolaters. Easily deceived. Deserving of God's wrath. In darkness. Unfruitful. Asleep. Foolish. Evil. Unwise. Debauched. Drunken. Rebellious. Unprotected and unarmored — unable to withstand evil.

When Isaiah says our righteousness is as filthy rags, this is what he means. The best we can do is this list. This is how God sees our best efforts on our own.

When it says we are dead in our trespasses and sins, this is exactly what it means. Have you ever felt like no matter what you did you just couldn't live up to what was expected? In this situation, that's not a feeling — it's a fact. Because of our sin nature we are incapable of meeting God's standard. First Corinthians 2:14 puts it plainly: "The person without the Spirit does not accept the things that come from the Spirit of God but considers them foolishness, and cannot understand them because they are discerned only through the Spirit."

Without the work of God in our hearts and minds, the best we can do is filthy rags. We can't hope to measure up in our own strength. And the bad news is that our best efforts earn us the full fiery wrath of God, deservedly poured out on us.

But then comes verse 4.

But God.

That is probably the best "but" in the Bible.

We are licentious — but God. We are deceitful — but God. We've wandered astray — but God! We are sons of disobedience — but God! We are rebellious and unclean and corrupt. But God, being rich in mercy and because of his great love for us — even while we hated him, even while we desired counterfeits, even while we were disrespectful and lovers of evil, even while we were covetous and sexually immoral and impure — God loved us.

Even despite our best efforts to fight him, God made us alive together with Christ. Raised up with him. Seated with him in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus. We become recipients of God's immeasurable riches — his grace and kindness toward us.

Not because we earned it. Because of God's grace, in giving us the very faith we needed for salvation.

We have a great heavenly Father. It can seem confusing that God would love us no matter how fiercely we rebel against him. But we have a dim reflection of this love within us. I know this because most of you are parents, and many of you have become grandparents. Think about your kids entering their turbulent teens. Was there anything they could have done to make you stop loving them? They can grieve you deeply. They can make it hard to love them. But I don't think any of you have ever had a child push you so far that you wanted nothing more to do with them. You still want the best for them. You still want them to be happy and healthy and to succeed. You want them to find love, comfort, peace. You still love them.

That's a shadow of how God loves us.

God loves us too much to allow us to stay in our rebellion. Those he calls to faith in Jesus Christ are then indwelt by the Holy Spirit, and because of that there is a washing and cleansing agent at work in our hearts and minds every day. Over time our desires change. The things we want change. We begin to desire the things of God over the things of the world. The poison and corruption of sin is slowly drawn out of us.

We ought to be fighting the old self to the best of our abilities as well. Billy Sunday put it in a way that makes me laugh every time: "Listen, I'm against sin. I'll kick it as long as I've got a foot. I'll fight it as long as I've got a fist. I'll butt it as long as I've got a head. And I'll bite it as long as I've got a tooth. And when I'm old, fistless, footless, and toothless, I'll gum it till I go home to glory."

That ought to be our posture — not because fighting sin will save us. It won't. But out of gratitude to God for his great love toward us.

All of it starts with the gospel, though.
