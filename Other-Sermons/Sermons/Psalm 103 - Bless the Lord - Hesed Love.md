# Bless the Lord, O My Soul
### Psalm 103 | Romans 6:23 | Ephesians 2:4–5 | 1 Peter 3:18

---

Continuing the theme of last week, we're going to be in Psalm 103 today. Psalm 103 — if you've brought your Bibles, and I hope you have.

While you're turning there, let's talk about our people group of the week.

---

## People Group: The Zaza-Dimli of Turkey

The Zaza-Dimli trace their origins to the region of the Caspian Sea, near Dailam. They have historically been fierce warriors, known for conquest. The Dimli differ from the Kurds in two ways: language and religion. They have a distinct dialect, and they are considered heretics by the Sunni Kurds. The Dimli follow Alevi Shiite religious traditions.

**Ministry Obstacles**: There is a great need for Scripture and other language-based resources in the Zaza-Dimli language.

**Outreach Ideas**: Gospel radio broadcasts will be helpful, but sustained prayer is the great need.

**Pray for the followers of Christ among them** — that the few believers will grow in sharing and discussing God's Word with their families and communities. Though there are few if any followers of Jesus among this people group, pray for those who will soon come to faith. Pray that Zaza-Dimli believers will be well grounded in Scripture and zealous to grow in the faith.

**Pray for the entire people group** — that they will live peaceful lives, establishing good relationships with the Turkish government and other ethnic groups.

Let's pray for them.

## David Alone with God

One of the commentators I read — someone much better at the Hebrew than I am — says that our passage today carries the sense of David setting himself aside from people. Alone, just him and God. As though he's crying out for the sum total of his being to worship God: heart, soul, and mind.

Interestingly, almost any time you read the English word "heart" in the New Testament — and I know we're in the Old Testament here — the Greek word reads "intestines." The people of that era believed the center of human emotion was not the heart but the intestines. If we're being honest, the heart pumps blood but doesn't have a lot to do with emotion other than moving the blood that carries the neurotransmitters sent out by the body. So the difference is less dramatic than it first appears.

Either way, David wanted his heart, soul, and mind to worship God — all of his inmost parts. My commentator believed David had set himself aside, alone with God, and was opening himself up to examination by God: making sure he was being thankful, making sure his life reflected what it means to "bless the Lord."

## What Thanksgiving Really Is

People think Thanksgiving is about turkey and stuffing. But it's about more than that. It's about being together with those we love and giving thanks to God. About examining ourselves and seeing whether we live up to the biblical standard of thanksgiving — and giving thanks for the fact that though we fail, God's mercies are new every morning. We can never run so far from God that he can't draw us back. God is so good to us.

Let's read the whole Psalm together.

## A Public Record of a Private Moment

It strikes me right away that though in the moment of writing David was alone — examining himself before God — in recording that moment in Scripture he made this very private moment very public. He's encouraging others to follow his example, I think both in private and in public.

To lead by example is in view here, and it should be how Christians live. In private we should read our Bibles and pray. In public and in private we should live a lifestyle of evangelism and worship. We should be fellowshipping with other believers — because Scripture says, as Proverbs 27:17 puts it, "Iron sharpens iron, and one man sharpens another."

Living for Christ makes a person different. Makes a person peculiar in this world. A true follower of Christ stands out from the crowd. We should live our lives as examples of God's mercy. That's uncomfortable sometimes. Who wants their failures on display? But as Paul said — *let the one who boasts, boast in the Lord* (1 Cor 1:31).

A commentary called JFB said of this passage that God is "not only intrinsically worthy of praise, but… specially suited to man's frailty." ⚠️ *Exact wording unverified — JFB (Jamieson-Fausset-Brown) is a real commentary; paraphrase may be accurate but could not be confirmed against the text.*

I had a situation a few weeks back — you all know I took in Cooper as a rescue dog. He did what puppies do and absolutely destroyed my sandals — both pairs — and my shoes. All in about five minutes. I hate shoe shopping. There's something about the process of finding shoes that I genuinely don't enjoy. Part of it is that it's hard to find shoes in my size. If a shoe store has one or two pairs in stock in my size, that's by God's providential grace alone. I've been told I might have better luck at a boatyard.

But when I find a pair I like, I go back and buy them over and over again. Shoes that are comfortable, that fit just right whether you're walking, hiking, or preaching — those are hard to find. So when I find them, I stick with them.

We have something like that with God. We were made. Then we broke ourselves in ways that only God can make up for. Or said another way: our frailty is specifically suited to God's power. Where we are weak, God is strong. Where we are broken, God can repair. Where we hurt, God brings healing. Where we struggle, God supplies his strength, his comfort, his protection and security. Where we lack, God provides.

## What David Is Thankful For

Verse 2 begins giving thanks for the ways God benefits us directly. Some people start small and build. But David goes straight to the top of the list: *Thank you, God, for forgiving our sins.*

What sins did David have to be forgiven of? We know he wasn't an attentive father — he raised children who raped and murdered and eventually tried to kill him (2 Sam 13). Then there was the murder he orchestrated as part of a conspiracy to cover up his relationship with Bathsheba.

You remember the story. One of the responsibilities of a king is to lead the army. So naturally David stays home from the war. He's in the wrong place — and then, perhaps walking the palace roof thinking of his men, he sees Bathsheba. In his lust, he sends his messengers to retrieve her. Some commentators present this as mutual consent. But that doesn't seem right. A woman summoned by the most powerful man in the nation didn't have meaningful options. My best reading is that David used his power to take what wasn't his.

When Bathsheba found she was pregnant, she contacted the king. David tried to make it look like Uriah might be the father. He recalled Uriah from the front lines, got him drunk, and sent him home. But Uriah was too honorable — even drunk — to go home while his brothers were at war. He slept in the stables. That wouldn't do for David. So he sent Uriah back to the front with a sealed order for his commander to place Uriah in the most dangerous position and then pull back — leaving him to be killed by the enemy.

That's the weight David is carrying when he says: *Forgive my sins.*

Some of you carry weight like that. Not his exact sins — yours. The thing you did that you can't undo. The person you hurt who doesn't know it was you. David knew. And he came to God anyway.

God's judgment on David was public. Their first-born son died. The prophet of God showed up and declared it was punishment for David's sin against Uriah and Bathsheba. David spent time in sackcloth and ashes, publicly mourning his sin and his son.

It's interesting that public repentance seems to have preceded the great revivals throughout history as well.

## Hesed Love — The Steadfast Love That Won't Let Go

David is thankful our sins are forgiven (v. 3), and then he gives thanks for salvation. We — even David — deserved to spend eternity apart from God. But God, being rich in mercy and because of his great love for us, has offered forgiveness. Verse 3 says we are crowned with steadfast love and mercy.

Think about that word *steadfast*. Can anyone define it?

Now — Hesed.

Remember the story of Abraham and Lot? They parted ways and Lot chose to go toward Sodom and Gomorrah — first living nearby, then beside it, then inside it. And one night Abraham pleaded for Lot's life. Hesed love is ascribed to God when he spares Lot's family the night those cities are destroyed — people who had no particular claim on God's protection, yet God extended mercy beyond what anyone had earned.

Or the story of Rahab. She hid the Israelite spies and helped them scout the city. On the seventh day, when the army marched and the walls fell, everyone in the walls was destroyed — except Rahab's household. And her family hadn't done anything to deserve it. That's Hesed: love that extends beyond what was asked, to cover those connected to the one who showed faith.

Every time Hesed love appears in Scripture, there's another dimension to it: an expectation of reciprocity. God showed Hesed to Lot by sparing his family from the destruction. He expected them not to look back, and to pursue God. Lot didn't look back — but the next thing recorded of Lot involves him getting drunk in a cave. His daughters took advantage of that, and all three paid consequences of various kinds, though none as dramatic as Lot's wife.

Rahab is the better example. She showed Hesed to the soldiers. They, and God, showed Hesed to her — her household was spared. And she is honored in the genealogy of Christ.

Or the Good Samaritan. A man was beaten, robbed, and left for dead. A Levite passed. A priest hurried on to church. Finally, a Samaritan bandaged him up, brought him to an inn, and paid for his care. When Jesus asks which of the three loved his neighbor — the concept he is describing is exactly what the Old Testament calls Hesed. The Greek word in Luke 10:37 is ἐλεήσας (eleēsas — "showed mercy"), from ἔλεος (eleos), which is the standard Greek translation of the Hebrew hesed throughout the Septuagint. The word is different; the concept is the same.

It's undeserved, steadfast love from God — and the loving response that it produces in a truly saved person as a result.

## The Billion-Dollar Debt

Let me put it another way.

Suppose you've run up a billion dollars in debt. You went out and spent like there was no tomorrow — houses, cars, clothes, gambling, liquor — knowing full well you couldn't pay it back. Now the fun is over and the creditors are knocking. You're hiding in the kitchen closet hoping they'll leave, because there is no way on earth you can pay them.

My grandmother had a story like that — not from her own doing, but from her first husband. He was an alcoholic. He'd collect his paycheck and buy rounds at the bar for strangers, while at home there were children who needed food and medicine. To get milk from the milkman, she'd place the order and pretend not to be home, so he'd leave it on the stoop. She literally hid in the pantry when the milkman came looking for money, knowing she couldn't pay. She said she purposed in her heart she would never be that low on money or food again. And to my knowledge, she never was.

In our analogy, something happens: someone opens their checkbook and pays the billion-dollar debt. Every last cent. How would you respond to that? Gratitude? Joy? Love?

That's the gospel.

Scripture says in Romans 6:23:

> "For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord."

We have spent our lives sinning, racking up a debt we cannot pay. The only settlement for that debt is death — followed by eternity apart from God. It's like a billion dollars. Insurmountable. An unclimbable mountain.

Yet Scripture says in Ephesians 2:4–5:

> "But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ—by grace you have been saved—"

And in Romans 5:8:

> "but God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us."

And in 1 Peter 3:18:

> "For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, that he might bring us to God, being put to death in the flesh but made alive in the spirit."

We deserved death and hell. Christ offers life eternal.

We have so much to be thankful for.

Sit with that for a moment. Really sit with it.

## Tesla and the Molecules

Let me tell you something interesting about molecules.

There are forces involved in holding everything together at the molecular level — the strong force, the weak force, magnetic forces, positive and negative charges. Science has gone a long way toward understanding these things, but the full picture of why matter coheres the way it does is still not complete.

A century ago, a brilliant and eventually troubled man figured out — among many other things — that sound, light, and energy travel in waves. He designed the electric motors we still use today; very little about his design has changed. He once took a small electric motor, mounted it off-balance on the wall of his building, and over the course of about an hour this small, hand-held device caused his building to shake so violently that neighbors thought there was an earthquake. Everyone thought the building was coming down, and he had to be begged to turn the device off.

His research suggested that at the right frequency, molecules lose their cohesion. Everything comes apart.

The TV show Mythbusters reportedly tested a version of this with a small motor attached to a large bridge — and in a matter of minutes, the entire span was moving several inches up and down. ⚠️ *The bridge type and location ("suspension bridge in California") could not be verified — the Mythbusters episode on Tesla's oscillator is real; specific details are flagged.*

Why does any of this matter? Because Christ could have used that principle against everyone who came after him. He was innocent. He was God. He could have caused anyone who lied at his trial, anyone who lifted a whip against him, to simply cease to cohere.

But he didn't. He allowed the trial. He allowed the beatings. He carried the cross up Calvary — obedient unto death (Phil 2:8) — because of what John 15:12–13 says:

> "This is my commandment, that you love one another as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends."

He knew what he could do. He didn't do it. That's the love.

Christ completed the law. Where the law was a flashlight in a cave, Christ is a lighthouse. And because of him, we who believe are dead to sin, dead to the consequence of the law, new creations. The old has passed away; behold, all things have become new.

---

Some of you walked in here today carrying that billion-dollar debt. You know you can't pay it. You've known for a long time. The checkbook has been opened. Every cent — paid. Not because you earned it. Not because you deserved it. That's the whole point. That's Hesed.

The question isn't whether Christ paid it. He did.

The question is whether you'll receive it.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Verses 6–22 of Psalm 103 do not exist in this sermon.** The psalm's own climax — the covenant faithfulness to "those who fear him" (vv. 17–18), the Lord's throne over all (v. 19), and the cosmic chorus of angels and all creation (vv. 20–22) — is completely absent. The sermon is not an exposition of Psalm 103; it is a meditation on vv. 1–4 with a gospel bridge. This is the sermon's structural failure.

2. **The Tesla/molecules illustration derails the momentum at the sermon's most critical juncture.** Placed just before the final gospel appeal, it imports a frame of argumentation ("Christ had power he didn't use") that is not native to Psalm 103, not native to hesed, and not native to substitutionary atonement. It stalls the sermon precisely where it should accelerate. The cross's power is here framed as restraint of force rather than absorption of wrath.

3. **Applicatory differentiation is nearly absent.** The sermon speaks almost exclusively to the unconverted hearer (the debt-carrier who hasn't received it). The converted hearer receives only generic encouragement to be thankful. The backslider, the doubter, the self-righteous moralist, and the theologically informed but emotionally cold hearer are not addressed. Perkins' taxonomy is untouched.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how thoroughly the psalm's own structure has been bypassed. The psalm is a masterwork of ascending praise — from personal to covenantal to cosmic — and the sermon stays at the personal level throughout. The preacher is preaching *about* a topic that the psalm addresses rather than *following* the psalm's argument. This is a common blind spot for topical preachers working with rich OT texts: the text becomes a launching pad rather than a sustained authority.

Additionally, the preacher may not see that the Tesla illustration, while genuinely creative, reframes the atonement in a way that emphasizes Christ's voluntary restraint of omnipotence rather than his willing bearing of divine wrath. These are not the same thing. The first is impressive; the second is saving.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The hesed word study *feels* like the sermon's expository core — and it is well executed. But it creates an illusion of textual depth because it traces a *concept* through the Bible rather than following the *argument* of the passage. The congregation leaves with a rich understanding of hesed as a cross-canonical theme but without having been led through the actual movement of Psalm 103. Word studies that travel widely can actually obscure how little time was spent in the home text.

Similarly, the multiple gospel verses at the climax *feel* like doctrinal weight — and they do provide genuine gospel content. But stringing proof texts is not the same as showing how the psalm itself arrives at this gospel reality. The doctrinal freight was imported; it was not extracted.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The psalm's cosmic crescendo (vv. 19–22) is the most significant missed opportunity in this manuscript. After all the personal thanksgiving and hesed reflection, David ends by calling the angels, the hosts, and "all his works in all places of his dominion" to bless the Lord. This is the psalm's argument: the individual soul's thankfulness is not a private emotion — it is the beginning of a chorus that encompasses heaven, earth, and all creation. A sermon that ends with individual gospel response misses the psalm's own ending, which is corporate, cosmic, and doxological. The congregation could have been swept into something much larger than themselves.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach the psalm, not just from the psalm.** Restructure the sermon to follow Psalm 103's own movement: personal gratitude (vv. 1–5) → covenantal faithfulness (vv. 6–18) → cosmic praise (vv. 19–22). The hesed word study belongs in the covenantal section. The gospel call belongs in and after the covenantal section. The cosmic ending becomes the doxological close. This restructure does not require abandoning any of the current material — it requires organizing it under the psalm's own architecture.

2. **Remove or radically reframe the Tesla illustration.** If the goal is to illustrate Christ's power and voluntary submission, there are better options closer to the text: Gethsemane ("Shall I not drink the cup the Father has given me?"), Philippians 2:6–8 (he did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped), or simply the image of Psalm 103:11 (as high as the heavens are above the earth). If Tesla stays, it must be tethered to substitutionary logic — "he could have unmade us; instead he was unmade for us" — not merely to the theme of voluntary restraint.

3. **Add direct address to at least two underserved hearer categories.** After the hesed section, address the knowledgeable-but-unmoved: "Some of you know all of this. You can trace hesed through the whole Bible. You could correct me on the Greek. And you haven't blessed the Lord all week." After the gospel call, address the already-convinced: "If you are in Christ, this is not information — this is a summons. Bless the Lord today. Out loud. In your car. At your dinner table. Make the psalm your own."

4. **Press the substitutionary weight in the gospel presentation.** 1 Peter 3:18 is quoted but not expounded. "The righteous for the unrighteous" is the engine of the gospel. Pause there. The debt analogy is strong — but debts are paid *financially*, not *substitutionally*. Someone doesn't die to pay your debt. Christ died in your place. Name the difference. That's where hesed meets the cross in its full weight.

5. **Give the exhortation to the converted hearer a specific shape.** The sermon closes by calling the unbeliever to receive. It does not close by calling the believer to bless. But the psalm opens and closes with exactly that command to the already-convinced soul: "Bless the Lord, O my soul." The sermon's conclusion should return to that opening imperative. "You who are in Christ: this is your assignment for the week. Not a program. Not a strategy. Bless the Lord."

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon has pastoral heat, genuine gospel urgency, and personal address. It is not bloodless. The fire is uneven — the Tesla section cools it — but the warmth is real.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement

The sermon carries genuine doxological aspiration — the hesed theme is handled with awe, and the closing gospel call has urgency — but the cosmic weight the psalm itself offers (all creation joining David's chorus) is left unclaimed, and the sermon settles for personal response when the psalm was aiming at universal praise.

**Bonus: +2 / 5**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 103 is a praise psalm — a genre that puts moderate pressure on the rubric's exposition categories, since the passage's primary mode is doxological rather than didactic or argumentative. However, this psalm is unusually rich in propositional content (covenantal faithfulness, hesed definition, the contrast of human frailty and divine eternity in vv. 15–17), and the rubric's exposition categories apply fully. The genre does not excuse the omission of vv. 6–22 — if anything, the psalm's own structural movement is a gift to the preacher that this sermon did not use. No significant rubric bending is required for this passage. The framework fits.
