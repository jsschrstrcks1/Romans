Thankfulness — Psalm 100; Philippians 4:4–7; 1 Thessalonians 5:16–18


Thankfulness is hard when you're down. But it's appropriate.

I heard a story this week about a pastor whose two-year-old daughter was diagnosed with terminal leukemia. That reminded me of Bob Clark and his son Rob, who faced the same diagnosis. If you know Bob, you know that God spared Rob — he went on to earn a master's degree in counseling and is practicing in Texas. That story could have gone wildly differently, but for the grace and mercy of God.

This little girl's story was similar, but harder. She was two years old. Worse, normally you have your immune system to help fight cancer — but this baby was born with the cancer already growing in her body, which meant her immune system was compromised from the start. The diagnosis also raised the possibility that her mother had leukemia and simply hadn't discovered it yet. Testing was done the same day, while they sat weeping with their toddler who had just been given a death sentence. It came back positive. The mother had it too.

Both were going to be difficult matches for donor marrow, and both needed the same type. None of the other four children, nor the brothers or sisters or parents, were suitable donors. They treated both with chemotherapy. They treated both with radiation, trying to extend their lives long enough to find a match. Even with a match, they were told that if both survived another five years, they would most likely live another fifteen beyond that — short of some other tragedy. The chemo had limited effect. The radiation seemed to slow it but not stop it. Things kept deteriorating. Days turned into weeks, weeks into months.

Each day this pastor spent time on his knees, pleading with God for his family. Each day they continued to get a little worse. The radiation being shot into their bone marrow made them very susceptible to infection.

Then one day while he was praying — in tears, for his family — a sense of peace washed over him. He got up, went home, showered for the first time in a couple of days, changed his clothes, and went back. In the hour that took, his little girl's color had returned. His wife was looking better too. The doctors were perplexed. The radiation had not been stopping the cancer to that point, only slowing it. They ran tests. They couldn't find a trace of leukemia in either patient. The hospital kept them several more days. Their immune response improved. They were free to go.

It has now been fifteen years since they were released from the hospital. Now a chaplain, that pastor takes his family back to the very room where they received their death sentence — and gives thanks to God.

Normally you don't go back to the place where your worst day happened. But we are to be thankful in all things.


Stephen Davey pointed out something interesting: Thanksgiving, which is just around the corner for us, has its roots in the Civil War. Sarah Josepha Hale — the woman who wrote "Mary Had a Little Lamb" — spent decades writing to presidents, asking them to establish a national day of thanksgiving. She finally prevailed upon Lincoln to make the proclamation during the darkest days of the war. Lincoln wanted it to be a day of national repentance and thanksgiving to God. It has become so ingrained that every president since has made the declaration. I've heard that at least once it slipped from November — though the tradition has been remarkably consistent. Even presidents sometimes forget to be thankful.

Our passage today reminds us, tells us, commands us to be thankful. Open with me to 1 Thessalonians 5.

Verses 16 through 18: "Rejoice always, pray without ceasing, give thanks in all circumstances; for this is the will of God in Christ Jesus for you."

Give thanks in all circumstances. Not some circumstances. Not the good ones. All of them.

One of my favorite preachers calls Psalm 100 the first thanksgiving hymn — and he's not wrong. It may be the oldest hymn associated with giving thanks that we have. Listen to it: "Make a joyful noise to the Lord, all the earth! Serve the Lord with gladness! Come into his presence with singing! Know that the Lord, he is God! It is he who made us, and we are his; we are his people, and the sheep of his pasture. Enter his gates with thanksgiving, and his courts with praise! Give thanks to him; bless his name! For the Lord is good; his steadfast love endures forever, and his faithfulness to all generations."

Enter his gates with thanksgiving. The command runs right through Scripture — it isn't optional.

Paul returns to it in Philippians 4: "Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice. Let your reasonableness be known to everyone. The Lord is at hand; do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."

Do you see what thankfulness produces? Peace. The peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, guarding your heart and mind. That is what is available on the other side of genuine thanksgiving.


In fact there are several lists in the Bible of what it looks like to be a Christian, or to be lost — and being thankful appears on the Christian's list every time, while thanklessness appears consistently on the list of the lost. Paul in 2 Timothy 3:2 puts the word "ungrateful" in the same sentence as "unholy, brutal, haters of good, treacherous, conceited, lovers of pleasure rather than lovers of God." Read 2 Timothy 3:2 and following later for the full context. The point is stark: there is a meaningful difference between the grateful Christian and the ungrateful lost person.

Are you grateful? Take a moment to audit your thoughts and words of the last few days. If a stranger categorized you based only on your thoughts, words, and actions of the past week — grateful or ungrateful? Do you serve God grudgingly, or with thanksgiving in your heart?

It's easy to fall into ungratefulness. When someone asks the same question for the fortieth time. When someone needs something trivial to them done at great cost to you. When you're working for a boss who doesn't see your work. When you're in the teeth of real pain and loss. But when we are ungrateful, we are elevating ourselves and our desires above the situation. We are essentially saying, with our actions: I value me more than I value your need. Like children who need to be told to lose the attitude — and I have to tell one of my nieces exactly that — sometimes we need to be told to be thankful. Our attitudes need to be pressed and molded in the Master's hands.


Let me ask you to name some things Christ went through on earth. Just call them out.

Now show me where in Scripture Jesus complained. Where did he whine about what God had asked him to do? Where did he have a fit and quit?

Quite the opposite. We know it was hard — we know that in the garden that night he wept tears of blood, because he knew exactly what was coming and how hard it would be. And yet Hebrews 12:2–3 says: "For the joy set before him he endured the cross, scorning its shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God. Consider him who endured such opposition from sinners, so that you will not grow weary and lose heart."

In John 10:11 Jesus says: "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep."

In John 10:17–18: "For this reason the Father loves me, because I lay down my life that I may take it up again. No one takes it from me, but I lay it down of my own accord. I have authority to lay it down, and I have authority to take it up again. This charge I have received from my Father."

No one takes my life from me. I lay it down. A. W. Pink wrote this:

"When Christ died, He did so of His own voluntary will. This is a point of vital importance. We must never give a place to the dishonoring thought that the Lord Jesus was powerless to prevent His sufferings — that when He endured such cruel treatment at the hands of His enemies, it was because He was unable to avoid them. Nothing could be further from the truth... all Jesus suffered was voluntarily endured. Without His own consent, none could have harmed a hair of His head... It was not the nails that held Him to the cross, but the strength of His love to the Father and to His elect people — sinners like you and me."

John 15:12–13: "This is my commandment, that you love one another as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends."

I'm as guilty here as anyone. Sometimes when something is going to require me to go out of my way, I grumble. But what was Christ's example? To love people the way he loved us. How much did he love us? He loved us this much — arms wide. He loved us to the cross and back. He loved us to death and back. And yet there are times we can't love each other enough to cross the street. Sometimes we don't love each other enough to deal with their noise. Sometimes we don't love each other enough to tell them the truth they need to hear. Sometimes we don't love people enough to work through something complicated and tiresome.

You could say it this way: sometimes I love myself so much I won't give up my time. Sometimes I love myself so much I won't deal with someone else's noise. Sometimes I love my comfort so much I won't say what needs to be said. If any of that is true of you, then it's also true that sometimes you worship yourself instead of God.

Next time you open your mouth to complain, remember that. Complaining happens when you worship yourself instead of God. When you elevate your rights above his command to love others as he loved you. In that moment, if you also tell people you worship God, you make yourself a liar.

Sometimes following God means denying yourself. Jesus said in Matthew 16:24: "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me."

Two parts. The Greek word for deny means primarily "to reject, disown" — to refuse relationship with something or someone. In this context: to disown your worldly self, to choose to live selflessly, to put away anyone's claim on you other than Jesus. When you are complaining, are you disowning your worldly desires? Are you living selflessly?

The second part — take up your cross — was meant to be shocking. Innocent people weren't crucified. Only the desperately wicked. To take up a cross was to accept public shame. And yet Jesus walked that path willingly, and could have left it at any moment. His love for you and me held him there more firmly than the nails.

So when your dog dies — give thanks. When your truck breaks — give thanks. When someone makes noise you don't like, give thanks. When someone makes life complicated, give thanks. Not for the truck, or the dog's death, or the noise — give thanks for Jesus and his death. Give thanks for the cross.

In the light of Christ's death, how bad is a noise? How bad is a truck problem? How bad, even, is the death of someone you love? God has been there. He knows what you are going through, and has been through it. There may not be much worse this side of hell than losing a child — and yet God knows exactly what that is. He watched as we brutally murdered his Son, in a way designed to imply shame Christ never deserved. In so doing, he who knew no sin became sin for us. We deserved an eternity in hell. And Jesus, because of his great love, took our punishment and wiped away our debt so that we can spend eternity with him.

That is how much Jesus loves you. In the light of that — what do we have to complain about?

That story about the pastor's wife and daughter having cancer — even if both had died, it is a reminder of what we deserve and have been spared from. That is why they go back to the same room every year. They have so much to be thankful for. And so do we.

Don't, in your complaining, spit in the eye of Christ.

As we close — Pink again: "It was not the nails that held Him to the cross, but the strength of His love to the Father and to His elect people — sinners like you and me."

That love is the reason for every thanksgiving. Enter his gates with it.
