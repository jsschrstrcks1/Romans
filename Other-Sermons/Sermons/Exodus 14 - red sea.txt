Exodus 14:1–31 — The Red Sea Crossing

We talked a couple of weeks ago about how different contexts are important. Today's passage — Exodus 14 — gains new depth when you consider the geographic, historic, and archaeological contexts.

As you may already know, Exodus 14 is the story of the crossing of the Red Sea. This is after the plagues in Egypt. Everyone has probably seen Charlton Heston's Moses at some point. Hopefully you all read this story recently too. Did you make it through your weekly reading? If not, there's always next week. It's hard to establish new habits.

So where we are in the story is right after the tenth plague. Every Egyptian was grieving the loss of their firstborn. In their grief they sent the Israelites off with lavish gifts — it was worth any cost to get these people, whose God was angry with them, out of their city. So they loaded the Israelites up with everything they could carry and sent them off.

But as we'll read, something happens. Scripture says God hardens the heart of Pharaoh. When we get to that verse, make note of it in your Bible. There are entire denominations founded on the idea that man's will is sovereign over God's. Yet time after time in this story we see God hardening the heart of a man. If God has the power to harden Pharaoh's heart, does he not have the power to soften hearts? To draw people to himself? Absolutely — and praise God for it. No one is ever too far gone for God to reach.

So when you hear people say God cannot change the heart of man, or that God always respects man's free will, you can say: that may be, but that doesn't keep him from working around it to accomplish his purposes — look with me at Exodus 14.

Let's read. Exodus 14:1–10.

First thing I want you to notice is that the text emphasizes location. In verse one they mention several landmarks and even the direction they were supposed to be facing. That caught my eye, and you know me — that triggered some research.

I found navigational charts for the Red Sea and tracked where others before me had worked out where the Israelites would have crossed. There is a place in the Gulf of Aqaba called Nuweiba — forgive me for any butchering of Arabic, but the old maps, more than a hundred years old, record the name as "Nuweiba al Muzayyinah," or roughly "waters of Moses crossing." A lot of things have had their real locations changed over the years — geographical markers, dates. Look at Christmas. Jesus was probably born in the spring, as near as we can tell. But over time the date shifted, layered over an older holiday. I don't think Christ minds. It's more important that we celebrate him than that we get the calendar right. The same kind of drift has happened with historical sites.

The place name alone wasn't enough to convince me, so I kept digging. There is an obelisk on the Egyptian side that has been defaced — little of the writing remains. Across the sea there is another pillar that still has writing on it. It commemorates Moses's crossing, placed by Solomon to honor God and the miracle he performed there — the destruction of Pharaoh's army. Small wonder the one on the opposite shore was defaced.

Then I looked at the underwater topography. Just north and south of Nuweiba, the Gulf of Aqaba drops off sharply — down to just under 2,000 meters, nearly 6,000 feet. But at the likely crossing point, the seafloor is only about 90–100 feet deep at its deepest.

When I was a kid picturing this, I had the picture-Bible image of the Israelites walking between walls of water maybe chest-high. An inconvenience, not a danger. But if this crossing is right, they were walking between walls of water nearly a hundred feet deep, along a stretch about ten miles wide by my best estimate on Google Maps. The walls of water on either side weren't a backdrop — they were enormous.

They have also found the remains of chariots there — broken and scattered across the seafloor, preserved in coral. Bones of men and horses as well. By contrast, there are very few other places in the region shallow enough for a crossing, none of them littered with anything like this evidence, none of them carrying a name like "where Moses crossed," and none of them identified by ancient tradition as the crossing site. It's a remarkable convergence.

Isn't that something? Science confirming what Scripture records. See the difference the geographical context makes? You go from two million people in chest-deep water crossing a short stretch of river to two million people passing through a supernaturally opened corridor with walls of water towering a hundred feet on either side. That changes the scale of the miracle entirely. When you hit something in Scripture that seems dry or hard to picture, that's usually a signal to go deeper. History and archaeology often open it right up.

The next thing I noticed is in verse 6: all of Pharaoh's army, and 600 choice chariots. Egyptian military design in this period produced two types. The standard chariot, mass-produced, with four-spoke wheels. And then the elite chariot — gold veneer, six-spoke wheels. The six-spoke design was an advance in engineering: it handled rough terrain better and gave more surface area for the gold inlay. A status symbol as much as a weapon.

Historians estimate there were roughly 20,000 standard chariots and 600 of the elite kind. Those 600 were almost certainly the commanders. The Egyptians of this period also hired mercenaries — Greeks, Phoenicians, others. The Greeks were approaching the end of their military prime, but they were still formidable. You remember what was said of Helen of Troy: she had a face that could launch a thousand ships. The Greeks may well have had that many. The Spartans famously told their soldiers to come home carrying their shields, or on them. Everything in Sparta was built around the fighting man.

The Reformation Study Bible notes there may have been as many as three men per chariot — a driver with a sword, an archer, and a runner. Twenty thousand chariots at three men each is sixty thousand soldiers. Trained, armed, closing fast on people who were unarmed and carrying children and belongings.

Even with two million Israelites, the Egyptians and their mercenaries would have slaughtered them. Casualties in the hundreds of thousands, maybe over a million. These were people strong from years of hard labor — but they were not soldiers. They had no weapons. Apart from direct divine intervention, it would have been a massacre.

The Israelites knew exactly how vulnerable they were. Short of God's intervention, all was lost.

I think that was precisely the point. God had to bring them there in order to deepen their faith. Look at verses 11–13. They are essentially asking God through Moses: why? Have you ever been at a point in your life where you asked that question? Everything moving in the wrong direction. Your enemies closing in from behind, and something impassable right in front of you.

I forget who said it first, but: sometimes you're the hammer, sometimes you're the anvil, and sometimes you're the iron — the raw lump of metal thrown into the crucible, heated and scraped, hammered and shaped. Forged from a useless lump into something useful.

Like any of us, the Israelites were a stubborn and forgetful people. They had to learn the same lesson over and over. I'd like to think I'd do better, but honestly, for most of us that wouldn't be true. We learn the lesson of trust, and then forget it. We — especially we Americans — are an independent lot. We have this idea that we pull ourselves up by our own bootstraps, that we are sufficient for whatever we set our minds to. The problem is we are foolish compared to God. We can barely see past the end of our face, and God sees everything — past, present, future. His perspective means he knows what's coming where we only think we know. The Israelites had to have this beaten home, and sometimes we do too.

When your problems surround you and everything looks hopeless, frequently that is when God chooses to move. All the Israelites could see was death behind them and death ahead.

But look what God was doing. Verses 15–20.

Geography again plays a role. If you look at Nuweiba on a map, there is only one way onto that beach — through a narrow canyon. Wide enough for a two-lane road, but a hard pinch point. The perfect position. When God shaped the land, he knew what would come centuries later and built in exactly what the moment would need. One column of fire and smoke at that narrow entrance was all it took — blinding the Egyptians, holding them back, buying time.

Can you imagine what it would have been like to be an Egyptian soldier at that moment? You'd already lived through frogs, boils, darkness, the death of your firstborn. Now you're chasing civilians to finish them, and a pillar of fire appears in the canyon mouth. I'd imagine more than a few of those soldiers needed some convincing to follow Pharaoh in.

Yet none of them stayed behind. What happened instead — verses 21–31 — was total and complete victory.

Verse 31 is key. God allowed all of it so that they would believe in him more deeply. Trust him more fully. Rely on him rather than themselves when things turned hard.

God brought his people into the crucible and made them face their certain doom. Then he brought them through it — not despite the impossible situation, but through it — so that He and they could have a deeper relationship.

Sometimes a person has to truly reach bottom before they are willing to put their entire will in God's hands.

I once read about a woman who was born into a troubled home. Her father was largely absent. She developed an eating disorder, convinced she wasn't pretty enough for her father to love her. She started looking for love wherever she could find it. At eighteen, her birthday request was that her mother send her to get help for the eating disorder. Counseling helped. Like many people who receive good counseling, she thought she should be a counselor herself. She went to college, then graduate school — struggling on and off with the eating disorder throughout — and eventually landed a job at a hospital while running her own private practice.

Two years in, police showed up at her office door. A patient had committed suicide. The family was suing her for negligence. It took four years to get through the trial. At the end of it they found her responsible. She lost her job, her practice, her license. She found the bottom of the bottle most nights. Then her husband left.

Finally she bought rat poison. She had decided to end it, and she wanted to be sober when she did. That night, out of the blue, a friend stopped by. They had a short conversation, and at the end of it the friend pressed a devotional into her hands. She figured reading couldn't hurt. She opened it and read a gospel presentation. She ended up weeping on her knees on the floor, asking Christ to save her.

The next morning she joined Alcoholics Anonymous. She's been sober some twenty years now. The eating disorder, she said, sort of melted away. She built relationships that hold her accountable in the areas where she's weak. But don't miss what made it possible: it wasn't until she entered the crucible, until she was in the fires of the forge, that she reached the point where she was willing to give herself to God.

As believers, sometimes we still have to go into the crucible. Not as punishment — as refinement. God is growing us, deepening our capacity to know him, trust him, depend on him.

And just as with Moses and the Israelites: God had the plan before they ever entered the situation. He had shaped the canyon, prepared the seafloor, orchestrated the release from Egypt — all of it working together, all of it aimed at drawing them back to him.

It reminds me of something I read once. A woman said the reason she loves roller coasters is the thrill of feeling like things are out of control while knowing she's actually completely safe. That's the crucible, when you're in it. Things are out of our control. But we are not out of God's. And that makes all the difference.

Let's close in prayer.
