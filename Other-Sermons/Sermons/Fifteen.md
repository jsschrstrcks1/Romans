Fifteen: When Your Best Is Not Enough
Trusted with a Silence
John 11:1-16
These verses in John 11 provide the prelude for Jesus’ dramatic raising of Lazarus from
the dead. In this stunning miracle, Jesus reveals his power (over even death), and he
demonstrates deep compassion for the people he loves.
What is perhaps most striking in the story, however, is Jesus’ delay in going to Lazarus.
For most of us, his delay is unsettling. There is a specific need – and Jesus obviously
cares – and he can certainly do something about the need – but, after hearing that Lazarus
is sick, Jesus stays where he is two more days.
Bible scholars are quick to explain that Jesus has his reasons for delaying – that Jesus
knows exactly what he is doing by staying where he is for two more days – that he has a
bigger purpose in mind. And those explanations are strong and convincing. Still, that
two-day delay is unsettling.
Christian writer Oswald Chambers had some interesting observations about this passage.
He wrote this: “Has God trusted you with a silence – a silence that is big in meaning?
Think of those days of absolute silence in the home at Bethany! Is there anything like
that in your life today? God’s silence is the sign that he is bringing you into a marvelous
understanding of himself. If God has trusted you with a silence, praise him, for he is
inviting you to understand his purposes more completely” (My Utmost for His Highest).
As we walk with God, there are many times when God is very close. Thankfully, there
are many times when he is very close. But there are also times when God seems
painfully silent. In those times of silence, we might wonder what we have done wrong.
(And it is a possibility that we have done something wrong!)
Still, Oswald Chambers’ suggestion is another possibility. Could it be that God is
trusting us with a silence? God’s silence may not, in fact, be an indication that we have
done something wrong.
God’s silence may, instead, be an indication that God is about to do something new.
Sometimes-silent God,
I am uncomfortable when you are quiet.
I am relieved to know
that you might trust me with a silence,
but I have a hard time trusting you with silence!
I am forever in a hurry.
I am in a hurry to have an answer.
I am in a hurry for things to be different.
I am in a hurry to have my hurts healed.
So I want you to speak – now.
I want you to act – soon.
I am unsettled by the two-day delays
and the delays that are much longer than that.
And I am especially unsettled
by your silence as that time passes.
I know that you will speak
when the time is right.
Give me the grace to wait
calmly
until that right time.
Amen
Questions for reflection:
1. Consider these words from Chapter Fifteen again: “For perhaps the first time
in my life, I was dealing with something that I could not fix. Prayer and
obedience and hard work and good training and godly intentions and sacrifice
– none of it seemed to make a difference.” Later in the same paragraph, Nik
says this: “I am embarrassed to admit it now, but I even wondered if maybe
this problem was too big for God.” What problems in your life, right now,
seem “too big for God”? Are you willing to place those problems in his
hands?
2. You might be encouraged to know (using Oswald Chambers’ words) that God
will trust you with a silence – but do you trust him in his silence? What do
you do when God seems quiet? Do you tend to walk away, do you wait and
hope that the silence ends, or do you dig deeper into your relationship with
God?
3. The stories of missionaries are very different. Some stories are marked by
visible and remarkable expressions of God’s activity. Other stories are
characterized by very little in the way of visible results. Occasionally, we
read about missionaries who labored for years without seeing one person
respond to the gospel. At the end of this chapter, Nik honestly asks: “Had our
efforts been worth the time, the money, and the energy invested? Was this
worth the price that we had paid?” How do you determine if your efforts and
your sacrifices are worth the price?

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Devotional Reflection Without a Pulpit: Warm Pastoral Care in Place of Proclamation

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A gentle and sympathetic engagement with John 11:1–16 that rightly locates the pastoral discomfort of divine silence but stops well short of becoming a sermon — offering comfort without exposition, and application without gospel.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**FAIL — Partial.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon identifies a genuine theme present in the passage (Jesus' delay), but the main point of the passage as John tells it — that the delay is for the glory of God and the glory of the Son (v.4), so that the disciples might believe (v.15) — is not the main point of the sermon. The sermon's central thesis is: "God's silence may be an invitation to deeper understanding." That is an application extrapolated from a secondary observation about the text, not the main point the text is making. John 11:1–16 is a Christological passage establishing the context for a resurrection miracle. The sermon never handles verses 4, 9–10, 14–15, or the disciples' response as primary theological data. It uses the passage as a devotional springboard.

*Authority Test:* The preacher does not stand above the text as editor, but neither does the sermon submit to the text's own claims. The text's climactic affirmation — "Lazarus is dead, and for your sake I am glad I was not there, so that you may believe" (v.15) — is the interpretive key. The sermon does not engage this verse. The passage's weight is borrowed but not carried.

> This sermon does not fully fail the gate — it does not contradict the text — but it does not arise from the text's own argument. It uses the passage as a frame for a devotional theme the text supports only obliquely. Evaluation proceeds with this noted as a structural weakness.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Two flags triggered. Score capped at 69.**

- **Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation:** Christ's finished work is entirely absent. The sermon mentions Jesus cares and can act, but there is no gospel proclamation. Christ's death, resurrection, justification, or the necessity of faith in him are not present. The sermon could be delivered at a self-help seminar or a grief support group without alteration.
- **Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary:** The sermon's thesis — God trusts us with silence; trust him in return — could survive completely intact without Jesus. The passage's function is illustrative of a general comfort principle, not proclamation of who Christ is and what he has done.
- **Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic:** The three reflection questions are warm but undifferentiated. Every question is addressed to "you" as a generalized spiritual seeker. There is no distinction between the unbeliever, the doubting believer, the comfortable churchgoer, or the suffering saint. "Are you willing to place those problems in his hands?" is not conscience-level application.

### 8. Core Override Questions

2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* **Ideas, and warm ones at that** — but the God of John 11 who raises the dead and declares himself the Resurrection and the Life does not appear here.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* **Mildly comforted.** The prayer section offers genuine warmth. But there is no conviction, no confrontation, no summons.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The prayer section is the sermon's best moment.** "I am forever in a hurry. I am in a hurry to have an answer..." achieves genuine pastoral voice, honest vulnerability, and an oral rhythm that the expository sections lack. If the whole piece had this register grounded in textual proclamation, it would be far stronger.

2. **The pastoral instinct is sound.** Addressing people who experience divine silence as divine abandonment is a real pastoral need. The preacher is reaching toward something true and needed. The diagnosis of the congregation's situation is correct, even if the exposition and gospel answer are absent.

3. **The Oswald Chambers citation is apt and well-placed.** "God trusted you with a silence" is a phrase worth preaching. It reframes a genuine pastoral crisis in a way ordinary people can receive. The problem is that Chambers ends up doing the theological heavy lifting rather than the text.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text's own answer to divine silence is never preached.** John 11:4 — "This illness does not lead to death. It is for the glory of God, so that the Son of God may be glorified through it" — is the sermon's thesis verse. It is the text's stated reason for the delay. The preacher identifies the delay as a pastoral problem but never delivers the text's own answer. The passage itself preaches divine sovereignty in suffering for the glory of Christ. That is the sermon that needed to be preached.

2. **Christ is present only as a character in a story, not as Lord and Savior.** The sermon uses Jesus the way a motivational speech might use a historical figure — his example illustrates a point. But John 11 is Christological bedrock. "I am the Resurrection and the Life" stands at the end of this narrative corridor. The sermon never walks the congregation toward it. An unbeliever could receive this sermon's comfort without ever needing to repent, believe, or be united to Christ.

3. **The reflection questions, while pastoral, replace application with self-examination without a gospel anchor.** "Are you willing to place those problems in his hands?" is not bad — but it assumes the hearer has a functional, living faith in a Christ who has been proclaimed. Without that proclamation, the question hovers without purchase. Willingness to trust requires a trustworthy object, and that object — Christ crucified and risen — has not been presented.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that this is a **companion reflection to a book chapter** rather than a standalone sermon. The references to "Chapter Fifteen," "Nik," and "missionaries" suggest this is a study guide or companion piece for a Christian book (possibly by or about a missionary named Nik). Evaluated as a book companion, it is warm and useful. Evaluated as a sermon, it is structurally unsuited to the pulpit. The preacher may not have intended this as a sermon at all — which would explain its shape. But if delivered from a pulpit, its absence of gospel proclamation is a serious pastoral deficit.

The second blind spot: the preacher appears comfortable allowing Oswald Chambers to be the theological voice of the piece. Chambers is a guide; he is not the text. When a secondary source does the interpretive and theological work of the sermon, the preacher has ceded the pulpit.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The **prayer section** feels like the heart of a sermon — and it may have been received as such. Its honesty and rhythm create an impression of pastoral depth. But the prayer's warmth is not purchased by prior exposition. It is not the culmination of a text-driven argument — it is a devotional aside that happens to sound like preaching. A prayer section at the center of a sermon is not a substitute for gospel proclamation. The piece *feels* pastoral precisely where it most needs to be theological.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**John 11:14–15 is the greatest missed opportunity in the sermon.** "Lazarus has died, and for your sake I am glad I was not there, so that you may believe." Jesus deliberately allows suffering — including the death of a beloved friend — so that faith might be produced. This is not a comfortable truth. It is a Christological thunderclap. The delay is not about God inviting us to deeper understanding in a general sense; it is about the Son of God orchestrating events so that the glory of God is revealed and faith is produced in those who will witness it. A sermon built on v.15 — "so that you may believe" — addresses the suffering congregation with both the hard truth (God allowed this) and the gospel hope (Christ is the Resurrection, and your suffering is not the last word). This opportunity is entirely untouched.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the sermon's thesis around John 11:4 and 11:15.** The text gives the reason for the delay: the glory of God and the faith of the disciples. Preach that. The sermon's pastoral concern — "what do we do when God seems silent?" — is legitimate, but the text's answer is not "wait patiently" in a general sense. It is "Christ is doing something for your faith and for his glory." Let the text answer the pastoral question the text raises.

2. **Insert a clear gospel section after the exposition.** The Lazarus narrative is a corridor leading to the greatest "I am" statement in John. Before landing on application, the preacher must walk the congregation toward Christ: he is the Resurrection and the Life. His own death and resurrection are the ultimate answer to the silence of God on Good Friday. The sermon on divine silence is most fully answered at the cross. Preach the cross here — not as decoration, but as the definitive answer to whether God abandons his people.

3. **Distinguish hearers in the application.** At minimum, address three categories: (a) the believer who is suffering and confused — "God has not abandoned you; Christ endured the ultimate silence so you would not have to endure it alone"; (b) the person who has walked away from God because the silence felt like rejection — call them back; (c) the person who has never trusted Christ — this passage proves he is the Lord of life and death. "Are you willing?" is the right instinct but needs these distinctions.

4. **Let the prayer come at the end, not the middle.** If the prayer section is kept, it should be the culmination of a text-driven argument, not its substitute. Move it to the close and let it be the congregation's response to what has been proclaimed, not the sermon's emotional climax in place of proclamation.

5. **If this is a book companion piece, label it as such and do not use it as a standalone sermon.** Companion reflections serve a different function than sermons. Knowing the genre protects both the preacher and the congregation from expecting something from the material that it is not shaped to deliver.
