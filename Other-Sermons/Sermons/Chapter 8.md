Chapter 8 – Effectual Calling: The Voice That Raises the Dead

Summary:

Belcher’s narrative at this stage begins pressing into how God actually brings a sinner from death to life. The Reformed confession calls this effectual calling—the moment when the Holy Spirit makes the gospel not just audible, but irresistible, life-giving, and personal.

Men often think their conversion was the result of their cleverness, sensitivity, or decision. Scripture says otherwise:
We heard because God spoke.
We responded because He awakened us.
We came because He drew us.

As John Murray said:

“The call of God is not an invitation but a divine summons.”

The emphasis here is not on the strength of the sheep, but the voice of the Shepherd.
He calls His own by name — and they come.

Key Scriptures:
•	John 10:27–28 – “My sheep hear My voice… I give them eternal life, and they will never perish.”
•	Romans 8:30 – “Those whom He called He also justified.”
•	Ezekiel 36:26–27 – “I will give you a new heart… and cause you to walk in My statutes.”
•	1 Corinthians 1:22–24 – “To those who are called, Christ the power of God.”

2.	When did the gospel become personal, convincing, and compelling to you?
3.	What keeps men from giving God full credit for their conversion?
4.	How does effectual calling destroy pride?
5.	How does this doctrine give hope for the “impossible” person in your life?

Chapter 9 – Perseverance: Kept by the Power of God

Summary:

In this portion of Belcher’s story, the question becomes: If God saves sovereignly, will He also keep?
Reformed theology answers with a resounding yes: the saints persevere because God preserves.

Salvation is not a tightrope we walk by balance, discipline, or willpower.
It is a path God guards.

Joel Beeke says:

“Perseverance is not the power of the Christian to hold onto Christ, but Christ holding fast to the Christian.”

The emphasis is security — not in our grip, but His.

Key Scriptures:
•	John 10:29 – “No one is able to snatch them out of the Father’s hand.”
•	Philippians 1:6 – “He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion.”
•	1 Peter 1:3–5 – “Kept by God’s power.”
•	Jude 24–25 – “To Him who is able to keep you from stumbling.”

⸻

Reflection Questions
1.	When have you feared losing your salvation — and how does Scripture answer that fear?
2.	Why do men tend to measure their spiritual security by their performance instead of God’s promises?
3.	How does perseverance shape your daily battle against sin?
4.	How does this doctrine reshape the way you disciple your family?
5.	What habits help anchor you to the truth that God will finish what He starts?

⸻

Group Discussion
•	How does perseverance free men from spiritual perfectionism?
•	Why does knowing God preserves us increase holiness instead of decreasing it?
•	John Newton said:
“I am a great sinner, but Christ is a great Savior.”
How does perseverance keep that tension alive and healthy?
•	Talk together about real-life ways Christian men can help each other endure suffering, temptation, and discouragement.

⸻

Prayer Focus

“Father, thank You for keeping me. Strengthen my feet, anchor my heart, and let me trust not in my resolve but in Your preserving grace.”

Chapter 10 – Justification: The Gift of Christ’s Righteousness

Summary:

These middle chapters of A Journey in Grace move toward the center of the gospel: justification — God declaring sinners righteous in Christ.
Justification is not God making us righteous internally (that’s sanctification).
It is God crediting Christ’s perfect obedience to us, and our sin to Him.

Men love to earn. Men love to achieve. Men love to prove themselves.
Justification shatters all of that.

Martin Luther said:

“The doctrine of justification is the article on which the church stands or falls.”

And he also said:

“Most necessary is it that we know this doctrine well, teach it to others, and beat it into their heads continually.”

Why?
Because nothing frees a man like knowing he is accepted by God on the basis of Christ alone.

Key Scriptures:
•	Romans 5:1–2 – “Since we have been justified by faith… we have peace with God.”
•	2 Corinthians 5:21 – “He made Him to be sin… so that in Him we might become the righteousness of God.”
•	Romans 4:4–5 – “To the one who does not work but believes… his faith is counted as righteousness.”
•	Galatians 2:20–21 – “If righteousness were through the law, then Christ died for no purpose.”

⸻

Reflection Questions
1.	Why is justification the doctrine that sets Christianity apart from every other religion?
2.	Where do you still try to contribute to your standing before God?
3.	How does justification by faith alone change the way you view your failures?
4.	How should justification affect your confidence, joy, and worship?
5.	What part of your life needs to be re-anchored in the truth that you are accepted in Christ alone?

⸻

Group Discussion
•	Why do men struggle to rest in Christ’s righteousness instead of performing for God’s approval?
•	Discuss how justification impacts your marriage, fatherhood, work life, and hidden battles.
•	Charles Spurgeon said:
“I have a great need for Christ; I have a great Christ for my need.”
How does that connect to justification?
•	What practices (Scripture, confession, accountability, Sabbath rest) help keep justification central in a man’s daily walk?

P = Perseverance of the Saints  Chapter 11 – Regeneration: The New Birth From Above

Summary:

As the story progresses, the theological focus shifts to regeneration — the new birth.
This is not moral reform, emotional stirring, or religious awakening.
It is the sovereign act of God by which a dead heart becomes alive.

Men often believe they can “turn over a new leaf.”
But Scripture teaches we need a new life, not a new leaf.

Jonathan Edwards wrote:

“The new birth is a change wrought by God, not an act performed by man.”

Regeneration is the fountainhead of faith, repentance, love, and obedience.
It is God creating what was not there.
It is the Spirit doing what flesh cannot.

Key Scriptures:
•	John 3:3–8 — “Unless one is born again he cannot see the kingdom of God.”
•	Titus 3:4–5 — “He saved us… by the washing of regeneration and renewal of the Holy Spirit.”
•	Ezekiel 36:26 — “I will give you a new heart… and a new spirit I will put within you.”
•	1 Peter 1:3 — “He has caused us to be born again.”

Chapter 12 – Conversion: Repentance and Faith as God’s Gifts

Summary:

Belcher’s book now moves from God’s internal work (regeneration) to our outward response: repentance and faith.
These are not human achievements.
They are God-given responses to God-given life.

As the Puritan Thomas Watson said:

“Repentance is a grace of God’s Spirit whereby a sinner is inwardly humbled and visibly reformed.”

Faith is the empty hand receiving Christ.
Repentance is the turning of the heart from sin to Christ.
Both flow from the Spirit’s work, not the sinner’s willpower.

Key Scriptures:
•	Acts 11:18 — “God has granted repentance that leads to life.”
•	Philippians 1:29 — “It has been granted to you… to believe in Him.”
•	Mark 1:15 — “Repent and believe in the gospel.”
•	2 Timothy 2:25 — “God may perhaps grant them repentance.”

⸻

2.	What does true repentance look like in a man’s life?
3.	Where do you personally struggle to repent?
4.	How does knowing faith is God’s gift change how you view your own salvation?
5.	How can men encourage one another toward deeper repentance?

⸻

•	How can Christian men avoid shallow, surface-level repentance?
•	Richard Sibbes said:

Chapter 13 – Sanctification: The Spirit’s Work in a Man’s Daily Life

Summary:

Here the story shifts to sanctification — the lifelong work of God making a man holy.
Justification changes your status.
Sanctification changes your life.

Men tend to drift toward extremes:
•	Legalism: “I can perfect myself by effort.”
•	Laziness: “God will do it; I don’t need to fight.”

Scripture rejects both.
Sanctification is Spirit-empowered effort.
God works in us — and we work because God works.

John Owen famously said:

“Be killing sin, or sin will be killing you.”

Sanctification is war.
It is also joy — the joy of becoming like Christ.

Key Scriptures:
•	Philippians 2:12–13 — “Work out your salvation… for it is God who works in you.”
•	1 Thessalonians 4:3 — “This is the will of God, your sanctification.”
•	Romans 8:13 — “If by the Spirit you put to death the deeds of the body, you will live.”
•	Galatians 5:16–17 — “Walk by the Spirit… the desires of the flesh are against the Spirit.”

⸻

Reflection Questions
1.	What does it mean for sanctification to be both God’s work and our work?
2.	Where do you need to engage the fight against sin more intentionally?
3.	What tends to pull you toward legalism or toward laziness?
4.	How can sanctification shape your leadership at home?
5.	What spiritual disciplines help you walk by the Spirit?

⸻

Group Discussion
•	Discuss practical strategies for killing sin — confession, accountability, Scripture, prayer, fasting.
•	Why does sanctification require community, not isolation?
•	John Owen wrote:
“He that is appointed to kill an enemy, if he leave striking before the enemy is dead, he does but half kill him.”
How does this apply to secret sin?
•	How can we encourage younger men in their struggle for purity and holiness?

⸻

Prayer Focus

“Holy Spirit, strengthen my hands for battle. Help me wage war against sin and walk in holiness. Make me more like Christ in my thoughts, desires, speech, and actions.”
