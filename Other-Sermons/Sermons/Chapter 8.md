## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Doctrinally Sound Bible Study Material Evaluated as a Sermon — A Category Error That Exposes Real Gaps

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
This document is a men's Bible study guide covering TULIP-adjacent doctrines (effectual calling, perseverance, justification, regeneration, conversion, sanctification) through summaries, reflection questions, and group discussion prompts — it is not a sermon manuscript, and evaluated as one, it fails to preach while succeeding in instructing.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**FAIL — with important qualification.**

This document is not a sermon and does not attempt to be one. There is no primary text, no exegesis, no homiletical movement from text to declaration. Each chapter selects a doctrinal topic and supports it with a cluster of verses rather than expositing a single passage and deriving its argument from it.

The Scopus Test cannot be passed because there is no scopus — no singular thesis arising from a singular passage's own argument. Each chapter is a doctrinal summary, not a proclamation.

The Authority Test is partially honored — the preacher does not stand above the Word, but he stands adjacent to it, using Scripture as confirmation of doctrinal categories already organized outside the text.

> “This material is not text-driven preaching. The organizing principle is systematic theology (TULIP), not a passage's own argument. No sermon score is assigned in the normal sense. The evaluation below diagnoses the material for what it is, and what it would need to become a true expository sermon series.”

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
Score is capped at 69 regardless. The following flags are triggered:

- **Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The gospel is gestured toward (justification chapter) but never proclaimed as a saving announcement. There is no moment where sin, wrath, substitution, and faith are held together in a declarative summons. The Spurgeon quote and Luther quotes point toward it — but quoting men who preached the gospel is not the same as preaching it.
- **Flag 2 — Main point does not equal text's point**: There is no controlling text. Romans 8:30 is cited in Chapter 8, but it is a proof-text for effectual calling, not the governing passage whose own argument drives the message.
- **Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary**: Christ appears in passing (John 10, Galatians 2:20), but the organizing center is the doctrinal category, not the person and work of Christ. ### 13. Missed Opportunity
Romans 8:28–30 — the Golden Chain — is cited in Chapter 8 but never opened. This passage contains the entire ordo salutis in five links (foreknew → predestined → called → justified → glorified) and would have provided the single driving text that could have unified all six chapters into a genuine expository series. Instead of organizing the series around TULIP categories (a systematic theology grid), the author could have moved verse by verse through Romans 8:28–30, letting Paul's own argument generate the doctrinal content. That decision — to use the novel as the organizing spine instead of a biblical text — is the missed opportunity that produces every other weakness in this document.

Ezekiel 36:26–27 is another missed opportunity. Cited twice (Chapters 8 and 11), it is one of the most powerful new covenant texts in the Old Testament — God promising to do what the law could never accomplish, replacing the stone heart, causing obedience. Opened and preached in its covenantal context, it would have produced the kind of sermonic wonder that the dry summary format cannot generate.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)
1. **Select one driving text per chapter and preach from it, not toward it.** Chapter 8 should open Romans 8:30 in context — tracking it from the Golden Chain, explaining “called” (kletoi) as effectual, different from the general call, grounded in foreknowledge and predestination. Let Paul's argument drive the doctrinal content. Every current doctrinal assertion should arise from the text, not be decorated by it.
2. **Insert a direct gospel proclamation in Chapter 10 (Justification).** After citing 2 Corinthians 5:21, stop and preach it: “Christ took what you deserve. You receive what He earned. This is not a transaction you contribute to. This is a gift you receive with empty hands. Are you trusting in Christ alone — or in Christ plus your repentance, your effort, your sincerity?” This is the exhortation the chapter is missing.
3. **Add a direct address to the unconverted in at least two chapters.** The chapters on regeneration and conversion especially need a moment where the preacher turns to the man in the room who is not yet born again: “If you have never been born again — if you cannot point to a new heart, new desires, new hatred of sin — the doctrine we have just studied is a description of what you need, and what God is able to do. Cry out to Him for it.”
4. **Reorder Chapters 11–12 to follow the logical sequence: regeneration → conversion → justification (as God's declaration) → perseverance.** The current order (calling → perseverance → justification → regeneration → conversion → sanctification) follows Belcher's novel, not the logical or biblical ordo. This confuses more than it teaches.
5. **Transform the prayer prompts into exhortatory conclusions.** Each chapter ends with a prayer. Before the prayer, add two to four sentences of direct exhortation that name the specific response the doctrine demands: “Do not merely reflect on effectual calling — rest in it. Stop second-guessing your election and start preaching the gospel boldly, knowing God will call His own. Go home and share the gospel with one man who is running from God.
