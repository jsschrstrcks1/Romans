SKUBALA — PHILIPPIANS 3:8

In our passage Paul uses a word that has caused controversy across the centuries. He says all of his past desirable achievements were garbage — σκύβαλα (skubala). This word has generated debate because it essentially means "crap," "refuse," "waste," or "dung."

A number of people over the centuries have argued that Paul's use of σκύβαλα was a form of cursing — the ancient equivalent of the modern profanity. This view is associated with an eminent Greek scholar named Ceslas Spicq. [Note: Ceslas Spicq (1901–1992) was a French Dominican biblical scholar and one of the foremost lexicographers of New Testament Greek, known especially for his "Theological Lexicon of the New Testament" (3 vols., Hendrickson, 1994; English translation of the French original). His views on σκύβαλα and its force are real and have been discussed in scholarly literature. The specific claim that he characterized it as "a curse" or "really close to a curse" should be confirmed against his actual lexical entry before being cited in print, as this is a strong claim about his position.]

Other scholars either follow Spicq or are confused by that assessment because of differing understandings of what "cursing" means. The word does appear in scholarly, philosophical, and high-society contexts in Paul's day, which makes a definitive judgment difficult.

Greek scholar Gary Manning writes: [Note: The pastor renders the name "Garry Manning." The New Testament scholar Gary Manning Jr. has written on Philippians and on σκύβαλα, but this specific quote — and its source (article, commentary, or lecture) — has not been verified. The quote is plausible and the substance is consistent with scholarly consensus, but the exact wording and source must be confirmed before citing in print.]

"Paul was not alone in using σκύβαλα as a metaphor for something worthless in the moral or religious realm. Philo and Sirach both used σκύβαλα to describe undesirable qualities that should be abandoned. Paul's interesting, and somewhat different, use of the word is to say that his desirable religious credentials (circumcision, pedigree, Pharisaism, zeal, obedience to the Law) were σκύβαλα — worthless waste — in comparison to knowing Jesus (Phil 3:4–8)."
— Gary Manning [source unverified — confirm article or book title, publication, and page before citing in print]

Whatever the word means, the heights of human achievement and possession — everything the world perceives as valuable — is at best refuse compared to knowing Jesus.

ONE EVIDENCE OF SALVATION

One evidence that a person is saved is that they desire to be more like Christ — they "know and follow hard after God." They live intentionally to be more Christlike. The absence of that desire over a sustained pattern of life — not the one-off apathies we all feel — is probably the clearest indicator that a person may be lost.

An illustration was given: a man who attended church weekly but boasted about his wealth, his sexual relationship outside marriage, showed consistent boredom in prayer services, and left during worship with excuses. No change was observed over approximately a year of acquaintance.

The caveat: a backsliding Christian is still a Christian. All believers have seasons of living less for Christ than they should. The concern is a lifestyle pattern, not individual failures.

The pastoral application: when leaders — deacons, elders, pastors — observe this lifestyle pattern in someone, the appropriate response is not shunning or rushing to judgment, but prayer, and prayerfully sharing the gospel with them. The responsibility is to gently point them to the glories of Christ, while also examining our own lives.

PHILIPPIANS 3:7–8 — PAST TENSE AND PRESENT TENSE

Piper notes a distinction between verses 7 and 8:

- Verse 7 uses the past tense: "whatever were gains." The pastor suggests this refers to the moment of Paul's salvation — when he instantly understood how far superior Christ was to all his earthly accolades.

- Verse 8 shifts tense: "I consider everything a loss." This is an ongoing present reckoning that continues long after conversion. [Note: Phil 3:7–8, ESV: "But whatever gain I had, I count as loss for the sake of Christ. Indeed, I count everything as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord. For his sake I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as rubbish, in order that I may gain Christ." The tense distinction the pastor draws is a legitimate exegetical observation; Piper and other commentators have noted it. The specific Piper reference was not confirmed by sermon/article title.]

Paul's list of earthly credentials: circumcision on the eighth day, Israelite lineage, Benjaminite tribe, Hebrew of Hebrews, Pharisee, zealous persecutor of the church, blameless under the Law. He had the political and social connections — discipled by Gamaliel, who was held in the highest regard. [Note: Acts 22:3, ESV — "I am a Jew, born in Tarsus in Cilicia, but brought up in this city, educated at the feet of Gamaliel according to the strict manner of the law of our fathers." Accurate.] In today's terms, the pastor compared this to being personally mentored by a figure like Billy Graham, John Piper, David Platt, Stephen Davey, or John MacArthur.

A commenter on the Genesis 22 passage described Abraham as "the wealthiest poor man" — he had livestock, servants, and massive wealth, but those things did not possess him. They were second in his mind and actions to Christ. [Note: The commenter was not named and the source (commentary, article, or book) was not identified. Should be sourced if used in print.]

ILLUSTRATION: THE DOG IN THE CABLE

The pastor described his dog at his parents' house, tied to a cable while he mowed and unpacked.
