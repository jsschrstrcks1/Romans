## Backstory — Jacob and Laban (Genesis 30; 31:41)

Since we last left Jacob, he agreed to work for Laban for another period of time so that he could have something to show for his time. To this point he'd married two women, but financially had earned no money since working for Laban. So they struck a deal, he'd work for another period of time and at the end of that period anything with a blemish would belong to Jacob. When it came time to pay, Laban took the sheep that rightfully belonged to Jacob and hid them so that all that could be found were spotless white or black sheep.

So he prayed to God, and was given an idea. He put three sticks from three different trees in their drinking water, and all of the sheep born after that were spotted or otherwise blemished. Some people think that the effects were temporary – that the fur was being bleached or darkened respectively. Some people say it was a direct work of God – and thus a miracle. However I read an article this week that said certain things that are eaten affect a certain type of color gene by causing variation when it normally would have been white. Specifically it was a type of mold that grows under the bark of the three types of tree branches that activates the gene that can cause color to change. Not instantly, but over time as their fur would grow out, they would go from being a solid color to being blemished. *(See flags — epigenetics hypothesis from Dr. Joshua Backon; plausible but speculative.)*

Either way, God told Jacob what to do, and Jacob did it. I love when science supports scripture. God knew that this mold would have an effect on the sheep that would benefit him, and so told Jacob what to do. Jacob, has no idea why that worked, but it did, and praises God.

To make a long story short. "These twenty years I have been in your house. I served you fourteen years for your two daughters, and six years for your flock, and you have changed my wages ten times." (Gen 31:41)

Ten times Laban changes Jacob's wages, and ten times Jacob sticks around anyway and continues to faithfully serve. That brings us to our passage tonight which starts in Genesis 31:4-21.

## Rachel Steals the Teraphim — Genesis 31:19

So I think its pretty clear Laban is a deceitful person. His daughters are kind of disgusted with him as we read in vs 14 they say "our father sold us to a foreigner, are we not now foreigners? He has sold us, he has devoured our money. What's left for us here?"

Not only that, but he wasn't worshipping the one true God. You'll see that in vs 19. Scripture says Rachel stole Laban's household gods. This I think says as much about Rachel, as it does about Laban. I had to do a little research on this. It turns out the Hebrew word used here refers to a group of idols that would have been used in divination. *(See flags — teraphim claims broadly correct but oversimplified; see integrity log for nuance.)*

Divination is a form of fortune telling. Today you might call it reading tea leaves, or a variety of other things. But the idea is that they are appealing to a higher power – not God – to learn information that they can use to gain greater power, wealth, or whatever it was they desire in their heart.

The problem is there are only four groups of entities besides people – God, Angels, Demons, and the chief of the demons, Satan. *(See flags — pedagogical simplification; standard theology treats Satan as chief demon, not a separate category.)* It is against God's nature to respond to divination, and angels don't and can't know the future, and it would be against the rules, and a violation of their nature as angels to give a message that wasn't from God. So that leaves the final two groups, Satan, and his fallen angels. So anytime you hear about divination, witch craft, wicca, and any of these other things they are seeking demonic knowledge, or power, whether they realize it or not. Their actions reflect a worship of the demonic rather than a worship of God.

In a Godly home there is no room for these idols, for idolatry, for the intentional or unintentional appeal to demons for anything. The Christian has no business participating in these things. Don't be surprised when lost people, act like lost people, and seek these types of things out. Don't be surprised if someone you're witnessing to – especially in the Moon Lake area – is involved with these things. There are druids, and a dozen other groups out there that functionally seek power from the demonic. Now don't follow their lead and engage in their practices.

Scripturally we are not to have anything to do with such practices. Galatians says in chapter 5:19-21: "Now the works of the flesh are evident: sexual immorality, impurity, sensuality, idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, divisions, envy, drunkenness, orgies, and things like these. I warn you, as I warned you before, that those who do such things will not inherit the kingdom of God."

Not only in the New Testament is this prohibited but in the book of Leviticus and elsewhere in the Old Testament, practitioners of witchcraft, of divination, of any form of the things we are talking about tonight — such people should be put to death. *(See flags — original claim of "three times in Leviticus" and "five times in the OT" is overstated; actual counts corrected in integrity log.)* Leviticus says that anyone who is actively seeking out practitioners of such things is cut off from their people. *(Leviticus 20:6 — this specific claim is accurate.)* Basically cut off from the promises and covenants that God made with the people of the Old Testament.

So we hate the sin, we don't participate in that sin, but we love the sinner. We reach out to the sinner. We share the gospel with the sinner. We disciple the sinner.

## Rachel's Motivation

So Laban was involved in some pretty sketchy stuff. So was Rachel. Think about it with me. She was at least twenty years beyond marrying age at this point. She's an adult. She has at least one child – Joseph by this point. She's been hearing the promises of God, and stories of God's provision in the life of Isaac and Jacob for two decades now. But she still steals her father's witchcraft altars. His tools for communicating with demons. They wouldn't have been worth much money. They were usually made of wood, or other commonly available things. Usually rather crude. So there was no intrinsic value. *(See flags — "crude" is editorializing; archaeological evidence is mixed; some teraphim were detailed or even life-sized.)*

The only reason I can come up with as to why she would take them is that she believed there was power in their worship. She either wanted to interrupt her father's ability to divine against them, or to use them in her own divination. Further, and we aren't told exactly which household gods she took, some of them were used to gain enhanced fertility. I suspect in light of Rachel's barrenness, that she had been spending a fair bit of time at the fertility altar. Either way she saw power there that she just couldn't leave behind.

The effects of this span history too don't they. Those of you who are familiar with your Old Testament will know that there is this cycle of idolatry and repentance that spans centuries. The people turn from God, and turn back, and return, and turn back, and turn from God and turn back. As soon as they seem like they are doing the right thing, they forget everything and run right back into trouble. We saw this with Abraham – today he would pray, and God would bless him, tomorrow he'd feel full of himself, and make decisions unilaterally. Which would always result in trouble, that God would save him from, then Abraham would repent and pray, and God would bless him. Then Abraham would forget, and get into trouble. But this cycle didn't involve idols until Rachel. From here on this will be a recurring theme in the nation of Israel.

## God Uses Imperfect People

Which brings us to our next point. God uses imperfect people who are submitted to himself.

Jacob too was no angel in this. He took advantage of his brother in a moment of weakness to gain his brother's birthright. He schemed and connived with his mother to manipulate his father into giving him the blessing due his older brother. He slept with women he wasn't married to. Married more than one woman. Purchased human beings. And was deceitful, and engaged in trickery to take advantage of his father in law Laban.

You've heard Dad talk about David on a number of occasions as well. David the murdering adulterer. Yet God called David a man after God's own heart. One of the things I love about God is that he uses real people. People like you and I – broken people to do his will.

There isn't a person in this room that has lived a perfect life. Everyone has done something wrong at some point in their life. Everyone has sinned. Everyone has fallen short of the glory of God. Scripture says in Romans 3:10 "None is righteous, no, not one." *(See flags — corrected to ESV.)* Apart from Christ we're lost and without hope.

But God made a way. He sent his son to pay the penalty for our sins. We deserved death, but Christ took that punishment. Three days later he rose again. "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life." (John 3:16) *(See flags — corrected from KJV/NIV blend to ESV.)*

God loves you and has a plan for your life. He can use you warts and all. There is nothing so bad that you can do that you can exhaust the grace of God. Man's grace is limited and finite.

### Factual Claims Verified

6. **Teraphim (תְּרָפִים)**: The preacher's claims are **broadly correct but oversimplified**.
   - ✓ Hebrew word refers to idols associated with divination (Ezek 21:21, Zech 10:2, Judg 17:5, 18:5).
   - ⚠️ "Group of idols" is slightly misleading — the plural form can refer to a single object (1 Sam 19:13).
   - ⚠️ "Usually made of wood, or other commonly available things. Usually rather crude." — Wood and clay are attested, but "crude" is the preacher's editorialization. Archaeological evidence is mixed; some were detailed, and at least one was life-sized (1 Sam 19:13-16).
   - ✓ "Some were used to gain enhanced fertility" — Widely supported by scholars. Many recovered figurines are nude goddesses with accentuated sexual features.
   - **Missing context**: The Nuzi tablets suggest possession of household gods also conferred inheritance rights — this may have been another motive for Rachel's theft. The preacher's two proposed motives (interrupt Laban's divination, or use them herself for fertility) are reasonable but not the only scholarly options.

7. **Bark mold / epigenetics hypothesis**: **Real scholarly paper, but speculative.**
   - Source: Dr. Joshua Backon, "Jacob and the Spotted Sheep: The Role of Prenatal Nutrition on Epigenetics of Fur Color," *Jewish Bible Quarterly* Vol. 36, No. 4 (2008).
   - The agouti gene model is legitimate and well-documented in mice (PNAS, NIH-funded studies). Maternal diet affects agouti gene methylation and offspring coat color.
   - Dr. Josh Klein (Volcani Research Institute) suggested host-specific fungi under the bark of the three Genesis 30:37 trees (poplar, almond, plane) would contain methyl donors (methionine, choline) that could epigenetically shift offspring coat color.
   - ⚠️ The specific application to Jacob's sheep is a plausible hypothesis, not proven science. The chain from bark fungi → methyl donors in water → sheep coat color change has not been experimentally verified in sheep.
   - **Recommendation**: If reused, present as "one interesting hypothesis" or "some researchers suggest," not as established science. The preacher's framing ("I read an article this week") is appropriately tentative.

8. **Death penalty for witchcraft/divination — counts are overstated.**
   - ❌ "Three times in the book of Leviticus" prescribes death — **Inaccurate.** Leviticus prescribes death for practitioners only **once** (Lev 20:27). Lev 20:6 prescribes "cutting off" for seekers (a distinct penalty). Lev 19:26 and 19:31 are prohibitions without stated penalties.
   - ❌ "Five times in the Old Testament" prescribes death — **Overstated.** Explicit death penalties appear **twice** in the OT: Exodus 22:18 ("You shall not permit a sorceress to live") and Leviticus 20:27 ("shall surely be put to death"). Even adding Deut 13:5 (false prophets) gets to three, not five.
   - ✓ "Leviticus says anyone actively seeking out practitioners is cut off from their people" — **Accurate.** Leviticus 20:6 specifically targets the seeker, with "cutting off" as the penalty.
   - **Note**: The preacher's broader theological point (the OT takes divination extremely seriously) is sound even though the specific counts are inflated. The corrected text softens the specific numbers while preserving the point.

9. **"Four groups of entities besides people — God, Angels, Demons, and Satan"**: **Pedagogical simplification, not standard systematic theology.**
   - Standard reformed/evangelical theology (Grudem's *Systematic Theology*, Erickson) treats Satan as the chief of demons — a fallen angel who is the leader of other demons. This makes **three** categories: God, angels, demons/fallen angels (with Satan as highest-ranking demon).
   - Some evangelical teaching programs do separate "Satanology" from "Demonology" as pedagogical sub-disciplines, which can give the impression of four categories.
   - The preacher's division is not heretical, but saying "only four groups" overstates the case and risks implying Satan is ontologically different from other demons, which is not the orthodox position.
   - **Left as-is in the transcript** (the teaching point still works) but flagged here for awareness.

The moment Rachel sits on the teraphim and lies to her father while they are literally beneath her is one of the more psychologically vivid scenes in Genesis — and it is passed over entirely. A woman so attached to her household gods that she will deceive her own father to protect them, sitting on them, hiding them with her body. That is a picture of what idolatry does to the soul: it makes us protect the idol at the cost of integrity, relationship, and freedom. The sermon does not use this.

---

### Surgical Fixes

1. **Finish the sermon.** The router anecdote is the right move — personal transparency about unrighteous anger, presumably leading to a point about needing the same grace as the imperfect people the sermon has catalogued. Complete it. Connect it explicitly to the idolatry theme: "What is your teraphim? What do you carry that you know you shouldn't — because some part of you still believes there's power there?"
2. **Press the conscience inward.** After the section on divination and the warning about lost people, turn the question: "But what about us? What are the things we carry that we know we ought to have left behind?" Name specifics: social media addiction, financial security hoarded against trusting God, relationships we know are pulling us away from worship. Make someone feel cornered.
3. **Connect the cross to the idolatry problem specifically.** The gospel currently answers the "God uses imperfect people" question. It should also answer the idolatry question: Christ did not simply use imperfect people — He freed them from what enslaved them. Rachel couldn't leave the idols. You have things you can't leave. The cross is the power by which those things are broken, not just forgiven.
4. **Add a "therefore" exhortation.** Before the benediction: "Tonight, I want to ask you to go home and find your teraphim. Not theirs — yours. Identify the thing you've been carrying. Bring it to God and lay it down." Specific, actionable, connected to the text.
5. **Simplify the angel/demon taxonomy.** Reduce this to one sentence: "Divination seeks power from a source other than God, and the only non-God source of supernatural power in Scripture is demonic." The four-category framework is a detour that slows the sermon and introduces a minor theological imprecision. Let it go.
