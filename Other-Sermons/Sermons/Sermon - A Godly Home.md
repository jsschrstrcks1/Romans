# A Godly Home
## Exodus 2:1–15

---

One thing I want you to think about before we get too deeply into our passage tonight is the courage it would take to marry — much less to have a child — in the world Moses grew up in. The life of Moses is roughly 400 years after that of Joseph. Joseph dies, then the Pharaoh that knew Joseph dies, and after a while the Egyptians became afraid of the Jews, and as a result began to enslave them. We know from Scripture their time in Egypt lasted 430 years. Moses is born at the height of the persecution and during a time when all midwives had been ordered to murder male babies.

While their names aren't given in our passage tonight, we learn from other passages that Moses' parents were named Amram and Jochebed. People couldn't make a call to their doctor and have a contraceptive ordered up. They couldn't run to the convenience store and grab a condom. Being married, living as married would mean in all likelihood having a child.

Since we've brought up contraceptives — in an ethics class I took recently, the professor said that according to his research, which he provided, chemical contraceptives cause approximately three babies each year that would have otherwise been viable to be aborted. If we believe life begins at conception, then ending a conceived life is no less abortion. So these chemical contraceptives ultimately cause around three abortions per year in a healthy young married home. If you're going to take contraceptives, be careful to do research on them beforehand, because there are many varieties that can end that spark of life.

Back in Moses' day, they had a number of options for contraception that actually have been shown by modern science to have been somewhat effective. There was a certain plant — historians believe it to be a species called silphium — that worked as a morning-after abortive compound. It was so over-harvested that it went extinct by the early centuries of the common era. In Jewish communities, citric acid was used; a fermentation of honey on fresh cotton was the common choice for those who didn't want children. Moses' parents could have fought the pregnancy with mercury. Mercury is massively toxic and causes all sorts of horrible side effects, but at the time they didn't know this, and would give a pregnant woman a preparation of it, and her body would abort the pregnancy.

The point is this: Amram and Jochebed had options. It took faith to marry. It took great faith to get pregnant. It took great faith to carry the baby to term, and great faith to hide the child as long as they did. This household must have been pretty pious to have fought so hard to have a child — even knowing they had a fifty-fifty shot of having a baby the Egyptians would kill.

Could you imagine being in that position?

## A Godly Home

One of the Founding Fathers — Patrick Henry — said this of godly homes:

> "This is all the inheritance I give to my dear family. The religion of Christ will give them one which will make them rich indeed."
> — Patrick Henry, Last Will and Testament (1798)

The same Jesus who turned water into wine can transform your home, your life, your family, and your future. He is still in the miracle-working business, and His business is the business of transformation.
— Adrian Rogers

A family is a place where principles are hammered and honed on the anvil of everyday living.
— Chuck Swindoll

What kind of spiritual leader are you at home? Are you the type that leads your family consistently toward the Lord? Or are you the type that lives one way on Sunday and another way the rest of the week? Raising hands and praising God on Sunday, but cursing and getting drunk and living for the world the rest of the week? Praise God for Moses' godly parents.

When I think of godly people I've known in my life, I think of my grandmother. She was always quick to tell you what the Bible had to say about something. She knew what the Bible had to say about things because she had spent a lot of time reading the Word, studying it, listening to people preach about it, fellowshipping with other believers over what the Bible has to say. She knew it because the Bible was the air she breathed. Not only that, but her life was marked by prayer. Something bad would happen — her first reaction was to pray. You went to her for advice, she'd pray with you. After you were gone she'd pray for you.

She spent time with the Lord every single day. Even when her eyes failed her she found ways to continue studying the Bible. She listened to great preachers on the radio. She'd listen to her audio Bible. Her life was marked with devotion to the Lord. Her last request of her sons was that they would share the gospel with the parts of her family that didn't know Jesus.

I think it's possible that Moses' family would have been something like my grandmother. What will your legacy be to your kids? To your grandkids?

## A Great Education

The second thing I noticed was that Moses had a great education. At this point in history, Egypt represented the pinnacle of the knowledge of mankind. They made things that people still marvel at today — pyramids, the Sphinx, mummies. They had knowledge that has been lost to time, especially pertaining to preserving bodies. In high school I read a book that claimed the Egyptians had better-preserved bodies than modern science was capable of — that whatever precisely they did was better than modern techniques. They represented the absolute pinnacle of the knowledge of mankind. They were the best nation around, and they knew it.

Sound familiar?

Yet what happens over the long term to the nation of Egypt? Even in the life of Moses, the nation is almost ruined. Plagues, boils, bugs, frogs, stink, and death. Ultimately every firstborn is lost, along with much of the nation's army. What good was the knowledge of man in the face of God? When God decided it was time to take Egypt down a peg, the most powerful army, the wisest men, the absolute best man had to offer — and in a very short period of time the army drowned and the firstborn men of the country all died.

So too does our wisdom faint in the face of God. Like a candle passing under Niagara Falls — the flicker of the candle is no match for the raging torrent of the waterfall. Our best efforts have an ice cube's chance. Nothing we can do as individuals can rival the efforts of the nation of Egypt, and yet the best they had to offer was washed away by the sea.

We — like Moses — were great failures.

## A Great Failure

Moses as a grown man was walking through the area where his people were working, and saw them being mistreated. Despite being raised by godly parents and having the knowledge of all of Egypt — he knew what was right, he knew what was wrong — yet when prompted by the right emotion, he chose the wrong reaction.

I think we've all been there. When we do that, it's because we are walking by sight and not by faith. Look at verse 12. He looked this way and that. He knew what he was doing was wrong, and he felt guilt about it in the moment. He knew his life could be forfeit if he were seen committing this deed. Scripture says: "whatever you have whispered in private rooms shall be proclaimed on the housetops" (Luke 12:3, ESV). The following day his own people rejected him because of his actions. He felt he was left with no option other than to run into the desert — to flee his family, to flee both his people and the people who had adopted him, to flee both his biological family and the family he had betrayed through murder.

Most of the time when we fail in this area, no one actually dies. But we can fillet people verbally. We can batter people verbally over and over, causing them much pain. Or we sin in a hundred other ways and harm those we love. Every time we sin, it required Christ to have been crucified — to have been nailed to the cross, to be scourged, and mocked, and humiliated.

Scripture says we have all sinned and fall short of the glory of God. No one is perfect — not one. We all deserve to be separated from God for eternity. But God made for us a way.

> "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."
> — John 3:16, ESV

It's that simple.

We may be like Moses, in that we have been great moral failures. We, like Moses, deserved to die. But God had other plans for us. God loved us so much He was willing to send His Son to take our death sentence for us. God did this knowing He had power over death — and the power to defeat sin and hell. The war is over. Victory has been won. Our enemy has long been defeated.

It reminds me of a Japanese soldier named Hiroo Onoda. Have you heard of him?

He was a military intelligence officer, stationed on Lubang Island in the Philippines in December of 1944. His orders were to conduct guerrilla operations and gather information until the Japanese retook the island.

Well, after he was stationed there, the Japanese lost. He was hiding in the rainforest, living off the land. We took his island. A little later, we dropped the atomic bombs, killing countless men, women, and children. We broke Japan's will to fight, and they conceded defeat. Hiroo, however, had no idea. They didn't exactly have email. He couldn't use his cell phone to call headquarters. So he faithfully fought for Japan for the rest of the 1940s, all through the 1950s recording troop and personnel movements around the island, all through the 1960s still living off the land. He was faithfully still doing his job into the early 1970s.

It was March of 1974 when he was finally found — almost 30 years after the war had ended. A young man when the war started, he had been fighting for a defeated cause for nearly three decades. He was 52 years old. His country no longer existed in any way he would recognize. His army had been disbanded. His mission ended. His cause defeated. He just hadn't gotten the message.

He refused to believe that Japan had lost the war — until they were able to track down his commanding officer, a man named Major Taniguchi, who by then was working as a bookseller. Taniguchi traveled to Lubang Island, met Hiroo in the jungle, and read aloud the formal order relieving him of duty. Only then did Hiroo lay down his weapons.

Maybe you're like Hiroo and you don't know it. Jesus has won the war. The fight is over. He's accepting all who are willing to call upon His name. Are you still out there fighting against Him? Are you still out there resisting Jesus Christ?

Because Scripture says one day every knee will bow and every tongue will confess that Jesus Christ is Lord (Philippians 2:10–11). Turn yourself over to Christ before it's too late. Because one day you'll be standing before God, and He's going to ask why He should let you in. If you can't say, *Because I deserved death, but Jesus took my punishment* — then He's going to say:

> "I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of lawlessness."
> — Matthew 7:23, ESV

If you've already come to that point in your spiritual life, then believers, I want you to think about this: every sin you commit makes you like Hiroo — still fighting a war that is already over. Scripture says we are to repent. Repent means to make a 180-degree turn away from what we were doing and toward Christ. We are to reorient our lives so that they point true north toward Christ. If you're intentionally doing things you know God would not approve of, today is the day to turn from your wicked ways and turn to Christ.

### Historical Claims
- **430 years** — Corrected from "slavery lasted 430 years" to "time in Egypt lasted 430 years" per Exodus 12:40 ESV. Note: Galatians 3:17 strongly suggests the 430 years covers Abraham's call to the Exodus, not just time in Egypt. The "long sojourn" vs. "short sojourn" debate is unresolved.
- **Midwives** — Exodus 1:15 names two midwives (Shiphrah and Puah), not "all midwives." Whether these two led a larger corps is debated. Moses was actually born under the more severe Nile decree (Exodus 1:22), after the midwife strategy had already failed. The sermon's phrasing is imprecise but the broader point (male babies targeted for death) is accurate.
- **Moses ~400 years after Joseph** — Standard chronology places the gap at ~280–330 years (long sojourn view). "Roughly 400" overstates most reckonings; the sermon may be conflating Genesis 15:13's "400 years" prophecy with the gap between the two men.
- **Silphium** — Verified: ancient abortifacient plant, effectively extinct by 1st–2nd century AD (last stalk reportedly given to Emperor Nero, 1st century)
- **Jewish contraceptive methods (citric acid, honey on cotton)** — Referenced in rabbinic literature (Talmud); real methods but centuries later than Moses' era. Acceptable as illustration.
- **Mercury as abortifacient** — Used in some ancient contexts; specific evidence for Egypt in Moses' era is thin. Acceptable as illustration.
- **Chemical contraceptives / "three abortions per year"** — ⚠️ Attributed to unnamed professor in ethics class. The claim is contested even among pro-life physicians. No medical consensus supports this specific figure. Noted as pastor's honest report of what he was taught.
- **Egyptian mummification superiority** — Pastor's honest recollection of a high school book. Defensible claim; some researchers agree.
