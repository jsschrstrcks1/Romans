One scholar points out that while the man in question may not have had personal access to Deuteronomy or Leviticus, he likely would have heard and known the ten commandments, which can be summarized by those same words – the first four commandments are about Loving God well, and the last 6 are about loving your neighbor well. So whether the man was an old testament scholar, or one who had followed Jesus and heard him preach before, or one who was just your average church goer, he asked a great question.

This whole discussion of the good Samaritan is in the context of salvation.

I think that's important to remember. The few times I've heard this passage preached, and even our VBS lesson this week tended towards a moralistic message. Simply be kind. That was our theme Wednesday night when we covered this passage – be kind. But the message here is bigger than just be kind.

If you attend my church for your whole life, and at the end of your life, the only thing that's changed is you're a kinder person then I've failed. Same thing with all the other theme nights this week. Being bold for Jesus is great. But if going to church just makes you bolder, I've failed. The same is true of most of the five themes – Faith, Boldness, Kindness, Thankfulness, and Hope.

If All we do is make you appear more moral on the outside, we have failed as a ministry, and also completely missed the depth and nuance of Jesus message here.
Someone read Vs 27 again. I believe the essence of what is being said here is that you must keep the law and the prophets. Which is what would have been taught to anyone who grew up in this time periods equivalent of church. Which is what the Old Testament taught.

But Jesus was here to bring about a new covenant, and institute a new way forward. He had come because its impossible for us to keep fully the Old Testament law.

Being a lawyer, I think he's thinking like a lawyer. About the letter of the law. that's why he asks what he asks in 29 – wanting to justify himself, he asks who his neighbor is.

I think perhaps the lawyer was thinking about Samaritans when he asked the question. Jesus knew the heart of this lawyer, and his parables speak to the hearts of everyone. Maybe the lawyer had mistreated a Samaritan earlier in the day. We don't know. But what we do know is that Jesus made the hero of the story the most reviled race of people of the time.

I read a story one time about ww2, and the jewish ghettos. There was this one particular jew in the story that had collaborated with the germans. At the end of the day he had ended up in ghetto, and ultimately in the concentration camps with the rest of his people. His betrayal hadn't done him any favors. But it did earn the hatred of his people. He had betrayed them.

The Samaritans were viewed similarly.
Depending on which source you listen to, the Samaritans were likely the remnants of a couple of northern tribes, that intermarried with both the conquerers, and the idolaters that moved in, in the vacuum of power. The result was a group of people that for a time had begun to worship idols along side the one true God, and ultimately were barred from the temple, so they made their own temple. They were viewed as traitors and worse.

In fact when the Jews decided to speak poorly of christ, they called him the two worst slurs they knew – demon possessed and a Samaritan in the same breath.

They were hated more than the enemy.

Yet that was who Jesus chose as the hero of the story. The person most different, and most hated from the perspective of the Jews.

As Christians Jesus calls us to model that kind of neighborly love. Yes. But we aren't to simply see to their physical needs and stop. Should we see to their physical needs? Yes. Absolutely! Are we doing an adequate job of seeing to their physical needs as a global church? I'd say we do better in some areas than others. Better in some countries than others.

But looking around at our own country, if every church was doing its job, do you think their would be a need for foodstamps and these other programs? I doubt it.

But its not just the physical needs. It's the spiritual needs we are called to meet as well. Remember this story was told in the context of salvation.

Apart from Christ we are little better than the man in the story. Beaten up by sin and our adversary. Bruised and broken we are unable to do anything for ourselves. Could the man in the story get himself help? He was penniless, and bleeding out in the street! Of course not.

We are just like that apart from Christ. But Christ – who is the person most different from us, and most hated by the world, came and made a way for us. He paid our debts, he healed our injuries, and supplied the holy spirit to nurse us back to health. He doesn't just take care of our health, though we have all heard stories of how God has healed someone, or have been healed. He purchases us at the highest price anyone can pay; Christ gave his life for us, so that we could live.

To be a Christian means to be like Christ. If then we are to be like Christ we need to be meeting peoples physical needs, yes! But also their spiritual needs. We MUST tell them about Jesus, and what he's done for us.

Failing to tell people about Christ is to be like the priest, or the levite, and simply walk down the opposite side of the road pretending not to see a dying man.

Don't be like the priest. Be like Jesus. Don't just walk on by the lost, pretending not to see them. We all have lost people our communities. When was the last time you shared the gospel with one of them? Ignoring the gospel needs of the lost people in your community makes you just like the priest that walks right on by. They don't have to be like you. The Samaritan was providing for his enemies. Jesus provided for you. Romans 5: 10 For if, while we were God's enemies, we were reconciled to him through the death of his Son, how much more, having been reconciled, shall we be saved through his life!
Are you sharing your reason for hope with your community? Would they even be able to tell that you are a Christian by the way you live your life? Don't just walk on by.
What about your circle of friends? Do you just walk on by their need for the gospel? As my friend Chris told me so poignantly – how much do you have to hate your friends in order to neglect sharing the gospel with them? Are you just walking on by your friends?
What about your family. Your kids, your grandkids. Are you sharing the gospel with them? Are you helping them with their doubts about the Lord? Or do you just walk on by. Pretending to be oblivious.
Don't be stingy with the gospel. Don't tell people you hate them through your silence. Don't be like the priest and levite and just walk on by. Worst of all sometimes our silence with the gospel betrays our own lack of faith in God. Maybe we don't tell the lost about Jesus because we don't yet believe in Jesus.
Gospel.
