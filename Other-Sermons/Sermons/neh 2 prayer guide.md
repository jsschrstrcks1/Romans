# Prayer Guide — ACTS

We're going to spend some time in prayer together today, and I want to walk us through it with some structure. The acrostic is ACTS: **Adoration, Confession, Thanksgiving, Supplication.** Four movements. Simple. Biblical. Let's go through them.

---

## Adoration — Praising God for Who He Is

Before we ask for anything — before we confess, before we thank Him — we stop and we recognize who it is we're actually talking to.

God knows everything. There is no situation you're carrying today where His knowledge runs out. He sees the end from the beginning. He knew your name before you were born. He knows what tomorrow holds. He is never confused, never surprised, never catching up.

He is our provider. He has never been caught short. Every breath, every meal, every unexpected grace — that's Him. The cattle on a thousand hills are His. He owns what we need before we know to ask.

He is our protector. Our shield. The psalmist didn't call Him that once as a nice sentiment — he called Him that again and again, in hard circumstances, as a fact proven by experience. God guards His people.

He is our helper. Come to Him with that word. There is no shame in needing help. He is not embarrassed by your need.

And He is our salvation. Not one of us deserves to approach a holy God. Not one. And yet through Christ, we can. We do. That is who we're speaking to.

*Praise Him. Out loud or silently — make much of Him.*

---

## Confession — Bringing Our Sin to the Light

This part is hard. But there's something about bringing darkness into the light that is also a relief.

We confess our own sins first. The things we did this week we wouldn't want anyone to see. The thoughts. The words that came out wrong, or came out exactly right in the worst possible way. The coldness of heart toward people who deserved better from us. Bring them.

And then we confess as a community. Because we live in a place where sin has real teeth.

Drugs and alcohol have their grip on people we love — maybe people in this room, maybe people just down the street. Sexual sin isn't abstract here. It's woven into our community. People we know are being told to despise the bodies God gave them, to reject His design in creating them as He did. Witchcraft and the occult aren't fringe in this area — they're practiced openly. These are real wounds in a real community.

We don't drag them out to make ourselves feel better about our own sins. We bring them because He is the only one with the power to do anything about any of it. We can't fix this. He can.

*Spend time in confession — personal, and for our community.*

---

## Thanksgiving — Remembering What He Has Done

Now we thank Him. Not in generalities if we can help it. Specifically.

Thank Him for keeping His promises. He has never broken one. Not one. The one who promised is faithful (Hebrews 10:23). That's not a sentiment — that's a record that runs from Abraham all the way to this morning without a single failure.

Thank Him for specific things He's done in your life. Name something. God is not honored by vague gratitude.

Thank Him for your family. Even the hard parts. Even the people who aren't where you wish they were yet.

I'll tell you — God is at work in the life of my sister's boyfriend. He's being drawn closer to the Lord. Through things no one planned, in ways no one expected, and I don't think he even sees it happening yet. But God is always working. He doesn't take days off. He doesn't lose track of the people we love.

And sometimes He lets us see it. He lets us glimpse what He's doing before it's finished. That's a grace. Praise Him for that.

*Give thanks — spoken or silent, specific or broad. But give it.*

---

## Supplication — Bringing Our Requests

Now we ask. And we ask boldly — Jesus told us to. "Ask, and it will be given to you" (Matthew 7:7). That is a promise.

**For our decisions as a church:** Lord, guide us on the question of our location and our building. We don't know the right path. You do. Give us wisdom. Give us unity. Give us the courage to follow wherever You lead.

**For ministry:** Help us to establish the right ministries here — things like the food pantry, ways to meet people at their actual point of need. Don't let us miss what's right in front of us.

**For workers:** The harvest is plentiful. The workers are few. That's not our assessment — that's Jesus (Matthew 9:37). Lord of the harvest, send workers. Raise them up from within this congregation. Draw them here. Multiply what we have.

**For our nation and our leaders:** Scripture tells us to pray for those in authority (1 Timothy 2:1–2). Not only the ones we agree with. Pray for wisdom on their behalf. Pray that God would draw them to Himself.

**For the items in the bulletin:** *[See bulletin for current needs.]*

**For Dave Gerner. For Kenny Duffield.** *[And for others God has laid on your heart — name them.]*

**For whatever you carried in here today.** He already knows what it is. Lay it down.

*Open it up. Pray together.*

### 8. Core Override Questions
These questions are genre-scoped to sermons and are not applied here. However, informally:

1. *Does this make me think more of Christ or myself?* — Mostly of God. The Adoration section is Christ-ward. The Confession section is honest about human sin without wallowing.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* — The document reaches toward encounter — it is designed to facilitate prayer, not describe it. That is appropriate.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* — The Confession section has genuine pastoral weight. The Thanksgiving section is specific and personal. Neither is performative.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **The Confession section names real sins in the actual community.** Drugs, alcohol, sexual sin, gender confusion, and the occult are named directly and specifically. This is not generic pulpit hand-wringing — it is a pastor who knows his community naming what is actually there. That is rare and valuable.
2. **The Thanksgiving section resists vagueness.** The prompt "God is not honored by vague gratitude" is a sharp pastoral line. The personal example of the sister's boyfriend is honest and warm — the kind of specific, human detail that makes a prayer guide feel inhabited rather than produced.
3. **The Adoration section is doctrinally grounded.** Omniscience, providence, protection, and salvation are named and briefly unpacked. The final line — "Not one of us deserves to approach a holy God. Not one. And yet through Christ, we can." — is theologically sound and affectively right for opening corporate prayer.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
These are offered not as sermon critique but as pastoral observations within the document's own genre:

1. **The Nehemiah 2 connection is entirely absent.** The title suggests an anchor in the text of Nehemiah 2 — Nehemiah's famous "arrow prayer" (v. 4), his bold request, his dependence on "the good hand of my God upon me." None of this appears. If the prayer guide is meant to teach the congregation *how Nehemiah prayed*, the text needs to show up. As written, the guide uses the ACTS framework but not the passage.
2. **The Supplication section is a list, not a movement.** The requests are specific and appropriate, but the section reads as a bulletin announcement more than a pastoral leading. There is no theological momentum — no sense that these requests are being brought to a God who is particularly able to answer *these* specific requests for *these* particular reasons. The Adoration section established who God is; the Supplication section could have drawn those threads back together ("We ask for church unity because You are the one who builds what we cannot — Nehemiah knew this when he asked for the king's favor...").
3. **The gospel is gestured at but not landed.** The Adoration section ends with a beautiful sentence about approaching God through Christ. But that sentence is parenthetical — it arrives and departs without unpacking what the cross means for corporate confession, or why thanksgiving is possible for sinners, or how Christ's intercession is the ground of every supplication. A prayer guide grounded in Christ's priestly work would be more than warm — it would be theologically electric.

### 11. Blind Spots
The guide appears to assume the congregation already knows *why* these four movements matter — their biblical warrant, their theological order, their relationship to the character of God. That assumed background is not always present. A brief word at the opening on why we adore before we confess (it is not flattery — it is orientation; we cannot rightly confess to a God we have not acknowledged), and why we thank before we ask (gratitude situates us as receivers, not demanders), would deepen the guide's pastoral usefulness without lengthening it significantly.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The personal story about the sister's boyfriend is warm and human, and it will land well with a congregation. But it also risks making the Thanksgiving section feel like the pastor sharing a personal update rather than leading the congregation into *their own* gratitude. The illustration is good — but it would be stronger if it were offered as an example and then immediately redirected: "Now you name yours." The guide does include the instruction to give thanks specifically, but the personal story lands last and leaves the congregation in the pastor's life rather than their own.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Nehemiah 2:4 — "So I prayed to the God of heaven" — is one of the most striking examples of intercessory boldness in Scripture. Nehemiah is standing before a powerful king who could have him executed for showing sadness in the royal presence, and in the middle of that terrifying conversation, he prays. Silently. Instantly. That moment is the entire prayer theology of the guide compressed into one verse: you pray to God when there is nowhere else to turn, in the middle of the world, without ceasing, because He is the God of heaven who governs kings. That text should have been the spine of this entire guide.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)
1. **Open with Nehemiah 2:4.** Quote it. Spend two sentences on what it means that Nehemiah prayed in that moment — not in a prayer closet, but in a palace throne room, under pressure. Establish that the guide is teaching the congregation to pray *as Nehemiah prayed*. Everything else flows from that anchor.
2. **Connect Adoration to the Supplication section.** The God described in Adoration — all-knowing, providing, protecting — is the God who is being asked for building wisdom and ministry direction. Draw that line explicitly in the Supplication section's opening: "We are not asking a stranger. We are asking the God we just praised."
3. **Deepen the gospel ground in Confession.** The Confession section is honest and specific. Add one sentence that grounds it: we do not confess into a void, but into the finished work of Christ, who has already borne what we are naming. 1 John 1:9 or Hebrews 4:16 would serve. This transforms confession from an exercise in self-flagellation into an act of gospel confidence.
4. **Redirect the Thanksgiving personal example.** After the sister's boyfriend story, add a prompt that explicitly turns it back to the congregation: "Now pause. What is God doing in someone *you* love — someone who doesn't see it yet? Name it." This keeps the story as an opening example and then moves the prayer outward.
5. **Add a closing sentence that returns to Nehemiah.** After the Supplication section, one sentence: something like, "Nehemiah prayed, then he acted, trusting that 'the good hand of my God was upon me' (Neh. 2:8). Go the same way." This gives the guide a textual spine front to back.
