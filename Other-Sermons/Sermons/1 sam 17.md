## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm, Wandering Survey That Gestures at Christ But Never Arrives at the Cross**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A pastoral and conversational opening of 1 Samuel 17 that models helpful counter-intuitive reading (we-are-the-Israelites typology) and guards against prosperity-gospel distortion, while failing to deliver a governing thesis, a clear gospel proclamation, or any summoning exhortation.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**FAIL — Conditional Pass (Scopus Test fails; Authority Test passes)**

The Scopus Test cannot be met: the sermon has no single dominant thesis that demonstrably arises from the passage's own argument. The sermon spends most of its energy on a textual-critical digression (the Elhanan problem), cycles through three "who are we?" frameworks without landing on one, and ends with the word "Gospel." as a placeholder rather than a proclamation. The passage's own argument — that Yahweh defeats His enemies through His anointed so that all the earth will know He is Lord (v. 46) — is touched but never becomes the sermon's governing center.

The Authority Test passes: the preacher does not stand above the text as an editor. He submits to its claims and pushes back against prosperity distortions honestly. But submission to the text without a thesis from the text is not yet expository preaching.

The evaluation proceeds because enough of the homiletical skeleton exists to score diagnostically, but the absence of a thesis is flagged as a structural failure throughout.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score is capped at 69.**

The following flags are triggered:

- **Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The word "Gospel." appears as a single-word sentence at the end of the manuscript. This is a heading, not a proclamation. Christ's finished work — His death, resurrection, the atonement, justification by faith — is never articulated. The typological David/Christ parallel in point two is the strongest gospel gesture in the sermon, but it is sketched and not preached. A listener could not reconstruct how to be saved from this sermon.

- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon explains three frameworks, warns against prosperity-gospel misuse, and ends. There is no moment where the preacher looks the congregation in the eye and says: "Therefore, repent of this. Believe this. Do this now." The congregation was surveyed, not summoned.

- **Flag 2 (borderline) — Main point drifts from the text's point**: The text's own climax is David's declaration in v. 45–47 — that this battle is the Lord's and its purpose is that all the earth will know that there is a God in Israel. The sermon never makes this the thesis. The "who are we?" organizing question is an imposed pedagogical frame, not an exegetical one.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Counter-intuitive typological reading.** The argument that we are the Israelites — spectators of a battle already won by our champion — is exegetically defensible, theologically rich, and pastorally useful. The parallel between David's victory and Christ's victory over sin, death, and Satan is the sermon's single strongest move and deserves to be the sermon's governing thesis. The detail about Goliath falling on his face (philistine cultural/religious humiliation) is excellent and original.

2. **Pastoral courage against prosperity distortion.** The rebuke of "speak it into existence" theology is pointed, theologically grounded (Spurgeon quote), and contextually specific. The preacher earns this rebuke by staying in the text (this was a specific situation, a specific enemy of God, a specific national moment) rather than moralizing. This is honest pastoral work.

3. **Theologically substantive compass illustration.** The compass/magnet illustration for total depravity and monergistic regeneration is the sermon's most pastorally penetrating moment. It is original, accessible, and theologically precise: the problem is internal corruption, the solution is divine intervention, and we cannot initiate or merit it. A congregation member who carries this illustration home has received genuine Reformed soteriology in a form they can hold.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is not preached — it is labeled.** "Gospel." as a concluding word is a heading for a section that was never written. The entire typological section (David-as-Christ / Goliath-as-Sin / we-are-the-Israelites) moves toward a gospel proclamation and then stops. What did Christ actually do? Why does His victory constitute our victory? What is the basis of forgiveness? Justification, propitiation, resurrection — none are named. The sermon leads the congregation to the door of the gospel and does not open it.

2. **No thesis, no governing argument.** The "who are we?" organizing question is a pedagogical device, not an exegetical one. It produces a survey of three frameworks rather than a proclamation of one truth. The text's own thesis — "that all the earth may know that there is a God in Israel" (v. 46) — is never made the sermon's thesis. The congregation is offered a menu, not a meal.

3. **The Elhanan digression is structurally destructive.** A 500-word grammatical-historical discussion of Hebrew vowel systems and Greek morphology before the sermon has established its thesis consumes the congregation's attention before they know why it matters. The information is defensible and even interesting, but it belongs in a footnote, a handout, or a follow-up class — not in the first third of a sermon on a text whose primary stakes are theological and christological.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the "who are we?" question, though pedagogically engaging, is a symptom of the hermeneutical habit he is trying to correct. The sermon opens by critiquing "we are David" moralizing — but then spends the entire sermon asking "who are we?" The corrective should have been: "The question is not who we are in the story — the question is what God is doing in this story, and whether we will stand under His sovereign victory or stand against it." The text is not primarily about Israel, David, or Goliath — it is about the Lord of Hosts.

Additionally, the sermon lacks awareness of its own incompleteness. "Gospel." as a final word suggests the preacher knows the sermon is not done — but the manuscript ends there. A sermon that is aware of its own gaps is not thereby pardoned for them.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The conversational warmth and humor (stubborn joke, Neil Gaiman aside, "I'm going to call on some of you") create the impression of pastoral engagement and congregational connection. These are genuine strengths of voice. But they mask the fact that the congregation was never searched, never cornered, and never summoned. Warmth without wound is comfort without the gospel. The preacher's natural relational ease allows him — and perhaps the congregation — to mistake pleasantness for preaching.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The text's own climax in v. 45–47 is the missed opportunity of this sermon. David's declaration — "I come to you in the name of the LORD of hosts... that all the earth may know that there is a God in Israel, and that all this assembly may know that the LORD saves not with sword and spear" — is the most explicitly theological statement in the entire chapter. It frames the battle not as personal victory but as cosmic declaration: Yahweh is God, the nations will know it, and He saves by means that look like weakness. This is Paul in 1 Corinthians 1. This is the cross. This is the sermon. The preacher touched it in one paragraph and moved on.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the thesis from v. 45–47.** The sermon's governing sentence should be: "God defeats His enemies through His anointed so that all the earth will know He alone is Lord — and in Christ, that victory is ours." Every framework (David/Israelites/Goliath) should serve this thesis, not compete with it.

2. **Relocate or cut the Elhanan digression.** Move the Hebrew/Greek vowel material to a handout or discussion note. If it must remain in the sermon, place it after the thesis is established — as supporting evidence that the text is reliable — not as the first thirty minutes of real estate. The congregation needs to know why this matters before they can care that it's defensible.

3. **Preach the gospel in the Israelites section.** The typological framework is the right framework and earns a full gospel proclamation. Add: what specifically did Christ do at the cross? Why is His victory our victory? Ground it in substitution and resurrection. The section currently says "total and complete victory" without explaining what that victory cost or how it is received.

4. **Write the exhortation.** The sermon ends before it ends. After the compass illustration, the preacher needs a "therefore" moment: "Therefore, stop trusting the needle of your own heart. Repent of the belief that you can find your way to God. Cry out to the one who can remove the magnet. He is willing — Christ's blood proves it. Come to Him." This is the summoning the entire sermon has been building toward and never delivers.

5. **Add direct address to the unconverted after the David-as-Christ section.** "If you are sitting here today and you are not a Christian — you are the Israelite on the hill. The battle is being fought. You did not enter it. You cannot win it. But the champion has already gone down to the valley. The question is not whether you are strong enough to fight — you are not. The question is whether you will trust His victory as your own." This is one paragraph that would transform the sermon's gospel clarity.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — the sermon does not score high enough in exposition or theology for the dead orthodoxy pattern to activate. What is present is not cold orthodoxy but incomplete proclamation: the theological instincts are warm and Reformed, but the sermon is unfinished.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
There are brief flashes of eternal gravity — particularly the compass illustration and the typological Christ-as-champion section — but the sermon does not sustain the weight it briefly carries; it ends before it arrives, and a congregation that left after "Gospel." would have glimpsed glory but not stood in it.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Old Testament narrative is well-served by this rubric with one calibration: the Fallen Condition Focus and Redemptive-Historical categories carry additional freight in narrative passages. The preacher's instinct to ask "who are we?" is the narrative preacher's occupational hazard — the rubric's insistence on a text-governed thesis is the corrective. The typological categories (Christ-centeredness, Redemptive-Historical Awareness) fit this passage naturally and were the most relevant scoring categories. No rubric strain detected; if anything, this sermon demonstrates why the rubric's gospel-centrality and exhortation categories are non-negotiable even in narrative preaching.
