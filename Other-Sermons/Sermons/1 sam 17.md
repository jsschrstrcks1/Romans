The passage we just read – the story of David and Goliath, is repeated three times in scripture. The third one is where I want to start, elhanan in Hebrew means – guess what? Beloved ONE. It Seems quite a bit different of a name. Until you start thinking about things. Hebrew isn’t written down like English is it? It uses different letters that say different things, its written right to left, instead of left to right as we do in English. They have different grammar, punctuation, and an entirely different set of rules. One massive difference at this point in time is that vowels weren’t even written down. If you were writing out my name for instance – Kenneth, you would drop both E’s, instead of Kenneth, you’d write Knnth. Further there were no spaces between words. So if you were writing out my whole name instead of Kenneth Baker, you’d write KNNTHBKR. Any way, it didn’t take them long to realize vowels were important, so rather than changing all the written material to reflect their new realization, they went back and added vowels, and grammar, and spaces with a series of dots and dashes. A single dot under the first letter means one vowel, while a single dot under the second letter means a different vowel. A single dot over the first letter means a third thing, and a single dot over the second letter means a fourth thing. On and on this goes, you can have up to three dots under and over any letter in a word there are something like 40 possibilities depending on dot and dash count, per letter, for each letter of the entire word. Now if you know Hebrew you know exactly when you look at it what those dots or dashes mean. Its like English, when you see an E, you know what E says, so when you look at Hebrew and you see two dots here, or 3 dots there, and a dash, a flying coma and so forth, you know exactly what those mean too. Just like we would see an ed at the end of a word and know that changed the word to past tense. Hastened means sped up in the past, chastened means to have punished already, fastened means to have already attached two things together, you get the idea. But unless you’ve studied Hebrew you have no idea what the dots mean, and are stuck reading the English version which merely says elhanon. Which means beloved one. David means beloved. In greek like Hebrew there are some things that when added fundamentally change the spelling of the word. Same root word, dramatically differently spelled result. We add ed, or even S to the end of a word to change the meaning. In greek you can add things to the beginning or end, depending, and that may well also change the middle of the word. I’m thinking specifically of the EIMI paradigm in greek, when it comes into play, instead of having a recognizable root word, with a couple letters as prefix or suffix, you end up with a dramatically differently spelled word all together. You might have one letter from before, and everything else has changed. So we have these sorts of things to cope with in ancient languages. Complicating factors that the average person won’t know. To be fair, I think the translators do us a disservice when they write el hanon instead of David here. It seems to be referring to David just using a different tense voice and mood. We don’t translate David to Beloved do we? So why they didn’t just identify that it’s a cognate to David, and translate Elhanon to David is beyond me. In any event they didn’t. But is that a discrepancy, or a failure in translation? I think its just poorly translated at that point.

Its kinda like this. I read a story one time about a villain that broke into a ship, and put a magnet on the ships compass. This was before the days of GPS, they had to navigate by the stars, or by the compass, and this new ship had a state of the art compass on it. The captain wanted to prove he could cross the atlantic without using the stars, only the compass. 

As you likely know, a compass depends upon the magnetic fields of the earth to point a piece of metal along the lines of earths magnetic field. Those always run north to south more or less, and so by placing a magnet on the compass the compass could no longer read the earths magnetic fields, only the magnetic field put off by the magnet. 

This meant the captain quite quickly was hopelessly lost, and whats worse, he didn’t even realize it. 

Days went by, food stores started to run low. The captain was getting desperate. And that’s when the ships engineer decided to check out the compass, found the magnet, and then found port. They were ultimately able to reach their destination, but only because that magnet was removed from the heart of the compass. 

God has to do the same thing to us. The compass needle of our hearts is compromised, and instead of pointing north towards the Lord, it points towards our own corrupt desires. But God is able to remove that, which allows us to be able to follow him. He doesn’t fix us because of anything we have done to merit fixing. We can’t earn it. We can’t even do it our selves. He has to do it for us, and he’s willing to.
