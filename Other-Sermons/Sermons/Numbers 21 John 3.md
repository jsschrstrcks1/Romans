NUMBERS 21:5-9 / 1 CORINTHIANS 10:9 / JOHN 3:13-21 — Look and Live
A typological sermon: the bronze serpent in the wilderness as a type of Christ lifted up.

---

I had a hard time this week preparing for tonight. I don't know if I was just being hard-headed, or wasn't listening, or God was allowing me to feel a little bit of stress before guiding me to tonight's passage. I normally know where I feel like God is leading me Sunday morning, and start preparing Sunday, and by Friday night I'm generally pretty happy with it — and then spend Saturday committing parts of it to memory. Not word-for-word memorization, you understand, but the main points. Anyway, despite spending hours in front of the computer this week, despite a half-dozen false starts, by Friday night I didn't have a single word written down on paper. It was a bit stressful, but I'm happy with where I feel like the Lord led me.

If you've brought your Bibles, and I hope you have, please open with me to Numbers 21:5. We're going to spend some time in three passages tonight, so be prepared to jump around a little bit.

While you're turning to Numbers 21, let me catch you up on where we are in Israel's history. This is after Joseph saved his people in Egypt. After they grew quite numerous and became a threat to Pharaoh. After they were subjugated as slaves. Then of course you remember Moses — raised in the palace, commits murder, runs to the desert, marries, has kids, and is led of God to go back and confront Pharaoh. Through the course of Moses arguing for Israel's freedom, God sends ten plagues — you remember the bugs, the boils, the blood, and ultimately the death of every firstborn child of every parent who didn't submit to God's edict and obey his instructions. This finally convinced Pharaoh to let them go. He chased them to the sea, and God parted the sea, and the Hebrew people escaped across it.

I was reading something recently about a place where thousands of chariots, people, and horse bones were supposedly found. I love it when science proves Scripture. Science doesn't have to prove Scripture for me to believe Scripture, but I do believe science done right will ultimately prove Scripture. [Note: The claim of chariot wheels, human bones, and horse bones found in the Red Sea or Gulf of Aqaba (associated with Nuweiba Beach) originates primarily with Ron Wyatt, an amateur archaeologist who made these claims beginning in the late 1970s. Wyatt was not a trained archaeologist. These claims have not been published in any peer-reviewed archaeology or Egyptology journal, and the mainstream scholarly consensus uniformly rejects them. Underwater photographs cited as evidence have been identified by marine biologists and geologists as natural coral formations, not artifacts. No peer-reviewed institution has confirmed the discovery. The pastor's sentiment — that rightly done science ultimately supports Scripture — is theologically sound; this particular claim, however, does not meet that standard and should not be cited as archaeological confirmation of the Exodus crossing.]

Anyway — where we pick up tonight, Israel has been wandering in the desert for a while. They are grumbling, malcontent, and Scripture says they were putting the name of Christ to the test. [Note: 1 Corinthians 10:9 — Paul makes this identification explicit; see below.]

Read with me what happens in Numbers 21:5-9.

    And the people spoke against God and against Moses, "Why have you brought us up out of Egypt to die in the wilderness? For there is no food and no water, and we loathe this worthless food." Then the LORD sent fiery serpents among the people, and they bit the people, so that many people of Israel died. And the people came to Moses and said, "We have sinned, for we have spoken against the LORD and against you. Pray to the LORD, that he take away the serpents from us." So Moses prayed for the people. And the LORD said to Moses, "Make a fiery serpent and set it on a pole, and everyone who is bitten, when he sees it, shall live." So Moses made a bronze serpent and set it on a pole. And if a serpent bit anyone, he would look at the bronze serpent and live.
    — Numbers 21:5-9, ESV

[Note: The ESV renders the food "worthless food" (v. 5). The NIV renders it "miserable food." The Hebrew is לֶּחֶם הַקְּלֹקֵל (leḥem haqqeloqel), something contemptible or worthless. Both translations capture the sense of the people's contempt for the manna God had provided.]

So right off the bat — for those that aren't familiar, what did they mean by "worthless food"? That's right: manna. They had been drinking from a rock and eating manna that God had made specially for them. It was a miracle that each day they had something to eat. A miracle each day that God didn't just wipe them out and start over. I think if I had to deal with as much griping and complaining, I might have done that. But thank God that I am not God, amen?

They kind of remind me of kids. You know how kids can be — maybe a little too much sugar, maybe they get a little overtired. They start to be rank, as my mom calls it. They throw fits, they cry. I happen to know of a girl who would smash her head into the floor when she didn't get her way. Her parents were mortified and would cave. Until the day her face met terrazzo. My understanding is that was the last time she ever tried to get her way by smashing her head into things.

The Israelites were at that point. They were griping and complaining. They were basically spitting on a gift God had given them. Imagine: every day you wake up hungry, and every day the ground is covered with food that nourishes you. Food that keeps you alive.

I read one commentator who said metaphorically the bread was the body of Christ, and the stone that produced water was the blood. [Note: This is not merely a commentator's interpretation — it is Paul's own identification in 1 Corinthians 10:3-4 (ESV): "and all ate the same spiritual food, and all drank the same spiritual drink. For they drank from the spiritual Rock that followed them, and the Rock was Christ." The pastor presents this as a commentator's insight, but the observation originates in Scripture itself.] And this was why it was so offensive when Moses disobeyed God and struck the rock rather than speaking to it as God commanded. [Note: Numbers 20:11-12. God commanded Moses to speak to the rock; instead Moses struck it twice. God responded that Moses would not lead Israel into the promised land because of this act.] Likewise the Israelite people rejected a free gift from God — and if that reading is correct, rejected something symbolic of God's Son himself.

Anyway — like the girl with the terrazzo floor, God put something in the path of the nation that forced them to stop.

That's when the snakes came.

I don't know about you, but I don't like snakes very much. This past summer when Hermine came through and I was evacuating my house, I came running out not watching where I was going and stepped on a four-foot snake — longer than my stairs. I don't know who was most startled. We both took off in different directions. I haven't seen it around since, which I am happy about.

Unlike my snake encounter, the Israelites encountered venomous snakes. These snakes bit everyone. Some translations use the word "fiery," which is a more literal translation but perhaps less immediately helpful. Scholars believe "fiery" was a description of the sensation experienced by those bitten — one description I found compared it to the feeling of fire pouring into an arm and moving through the body. So both words are apt. And as terrifying as snakes that were literally on fire would be, I'm glad they were ordinary venomous snakes.

We don't know the exact number of people, but probably most of them were bitten — certainly enough that they realized they had been foolish. They went to Moses, Moses prayed, and God told them to erect a bronze serpent on a pole. All who looked upon it would be healed. Which is a remarkable account if that is all we are told. But there are two other passages that inform this story that we need to look at.

Let me read 1 Corinthians 10:9 — but first a little context. Paul is writing about various things the Israelites had been brought through, and he says that these things took place as examples for us, that we might not desire evil as they did. [Note: 1 Corinthians 10:6-9 (ESV): "Now these things took place as examples for us, that we might not desire evil as they did. Do not be idolaters as some of them were; as it is written, 'The people sat down to eat and drink and rose up to play.' We must not indulge in sexual immorality as some of them did, and twenty-three thousand fell in a single day. We must not put Christ to the test, as some of them did and were destroyed by serpents." The passage header for tonight lists 1 Corinthians 10:5-9; the pastor reads verse 9 specifically.]

Ready? Verse 9:

    We must not put Christ to the test, as some of them did and were destroyed by serpents.
    — 1 Corinthians 10:9, ESV

That should catch your attention. We're given a detail left out of the Numbers passage. All the Israelites had to do was look up at the snake and they would be healed — instantly, fully, totally, perfectly. But yet some obviously didn't.

I read a letter this week by a man named Newton. He had been a slave trader, but gave his heart to the Lord and became a Christian — and later became an ally of William Wilberforce, the man who was instrumental in bringing slavery to an end in the British Empire. [Note: John Newton (1725-1807) — slave trader, converted to evangelical Christianity, became an Anglican clergyman, and in his later years became an outspoken abolitionist. He wrote "Thoughts Upon the African Slave Trade" (1788), providing moral and historical authority to Wilberforce's campaign. William Wilberforce (1759-1833) led the parliamentary effort that resulted in the Slave Trade Act of 1807 and the Slavery Abolition Act 1833.] Newton eventually became a pastor and wrote a number of hymns, some of which we still sing today. Perhaps you've heard of that obscure gem, "Amazing Grace." [Note: Newton wrote "Amazing Grace" around 1772; it was published in the Olney Hymns collection in 1779.]

Anyway, as part of his pastoral ministry Newton mentored a few other pastors. One of them — quite successful in his own right, who week in and week out saw lives being changed — wrote to Newton and said he knew that God could forgive sins; he'd been seeing lives transformed. But he didn't know if his own sins could be completely blotted out. Newton responded by preaching the gospel to him.

He said this:

    When the brazen serpent was erected in the wilderness, to cure those who must otherwise have died, the benefit was not restrained to those who had been bitten by the fiery serpent but once or a few times. The worst case amongst the people was relieved as soon and as certainly as the very slightest. The remedy was universally proposed to every person. The application was easy; it was only, look and live. But if a man had spent all his time in measuring or counting his wounds, instead of looking to the ordinance of God, he might have died, though the means of life were within his view. The sense of the evil of sin is given to quicken application to Christ, and not to discourage our approach. The Scripture has concluded all under sin, and as such we are all condemned already. But the Gospel proclaims a free pardon to everyone who, with the eye of his mind, looks for life to him who hung upon the cross.
    — John Newton [Note: This quote is attributed to John Newton and is stylistically consistent with his pastoral correspondence. The specific letter and collection have not been identified and verified. To be cited formally, the source letter should be located — likely in "The Works of John Newton" (6 vols.) or "Letters of John Newton" (Banner of Truth). The quote is presented here as delivered, with this verification flag noted.]

Think about that. The person bitten once by the serpents was just as easily, fully, and perfectly healed by looking at the bronze serpent as the man bitten three thousand times. Such is the grace of God. There is nothing you can do that is so bad that God cannot forgive you. As Jonah found out, there is no place so far you can run that God cannot find and forgive you. There are no depths of depravity you can sink to that God cannot lift you out of. No matter what you have done, no matter how fast you can run — you cannot outrun the grace of God. Because he is always waiting right there for you to look up and be healed.

Finally, that brings us to John 3. Turn there with me if you've brought your Bibles. OK, reading from the ESV starting in verse 13: [Note: The passage header lists John 3:13-21; the pastor begins reading at verse 14. Verse 13 (ESV): "No one has ascended into heaven except he who descended from heaven, the Son of Man." The reading picks up at the hinge verse.]

    And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in him may have eternal life.

    "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life. For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but in order that the world might be saved through him. Whoever believes in him is not condemned, but whoever does not believe is condemned already, because he has not believed in the name of the only Son of God. And this is the judgment: the light has come into the world, and people loved the darkness rather than the light because their works were evil. For everyone who does wicked things hates the light and does not come to the light, lest his works should be exposed. But whoever does what is true comes to the light, so that it may be clearly seen that his works have been carried out in God."
    — John 3:14-21, ESV

This, I think, is the answer to why some Israelites were lost. Verse 19 says "people loved the darkness rather than the light." I think in that moment their stubborn pride believed it was better to die than to look at the snake and continue to live in the same circumstances. People would rather die than be saved by Christ. People would rather continue to wallow in their own filth and depravity than truly give themselves over to Christ. And as is so often the case, the same is true today.

I firmly believe the people that end up in hell end up there by their own choice — just like the people who refused to look at the snake chose to die. They would be miserable in heaven and closer to happiness in hell. They run from the light. They run from Christ. They run from anything resembling godliness, so in the presence of God very God their selfish pride would be crushed like a bug underfoot.

People — if you are still drawing air into your lungs, there is still time. We have all sinned and as a result we fall short of the glory of God, Romans tells us. [Note: Romans 3:23 (ESV): "for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."] Not only do we fall short — we earn a one-way ticket to hell for all of eternity, and that is the best that man can accomplish on his own. An eternity apart from God. An eternity apart from the One who loved us so much that he sent his Son to die for us.

I heard about a boat sinking off the Skyway Bridge this week. One of the things they do when someone is in distress in the water is toss them a life ring — round, floats, with a rope tied to it. But no matter how good the throw, no matter how well the ring floats, no matter how perfect the rope, no matter how strong the back of the person holding it — if you choose not to grab the life ring, and you're under a bridge like that, you are going to drown.

It's like that for us too. We messed up, our boat is sinking, and God threw us a life ring. For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have everlasting life. As Moses lifted the serpent, so the Son of Man had to be lifted up. All who believe in Christ shall be saved.

It's that simple. Romans 10:9 says: confess with your mouth the Lord Jesus Christ, and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, and you will be saved. [Note: Romans 10:9 (ESV): "because, if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved." The pastor's version is a close paraphrase; the ESV phrasing is "that Jesus is Lord," not "the Lord Jesus Christ."]

As the praise team comes up to sing a final song, I want you to ask yourself this: Have you come to a point in your spiritual life where you have confessed with your mouth and believed in your heart? With your whole heart? Turning your back on the darkness and embracing the light?

If not, talk to me after the service.
