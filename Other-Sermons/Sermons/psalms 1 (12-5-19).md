## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date:** 2026-03-21
**Sermon:** Psalm 1 (12-5-19)
**Primary Text:** Psalm 1

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Pastoral Motivational Address on Bible Reading — Good Heart, Misread Text, and an Application That Exceeds the Exposition"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A warm, conversational sermon that uses Psalm 1 to urge Bible reading and covenant walking, but misidentifies the psalm’s primary concern (blessing through covenant obedience) as a warning list about neglecting Scripture, and delivers an application that functions as motivational exhortation rather than exposition of the text’s actual argument.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**FAIL — conditional.** Evaluation continues with penalty but score is assessed.

**Scopus Test:** The sermon’s thesis — "Christians who neglect reading their Bible will suffer consequences, like those listed in Psalm 1" — does not arise from the main point of the passage. Psalm 1 is not primarily a warning to neglecting Bible readers within a believing community. It is a wisdom psalm that sets out two ways of life — the way of the righteous (who delight in Torah and are planted in covenant life with God) and the way of the wicked (who are headed for judgment) — and invites the reader to choose the way of blessing. The psalm is about the entire orientation of a person’s life, not about a spiritual discipline that believers can neglect. The "twelve warnings" structure is read into the text rather than out of it; vv. 4-6 are descriptors of the wicked, not warnings to believers who fail to read their Bibles.

**Authority Test:** The preacher does submit to the text’s tone, but the re-framing of Psalm 1’s "wicked" as "Christians who don’t read their Bibles" represents a significant interpretive drift. The psalm does not address believers who are neglecting a discipline; it contrasts two categories of people defined by their fundamental orientation.

The Berean Gate is failed on the Scopus Test. However, evaluation continues with a score penalty, as the sermon is not a total departure from the passage — the connection between meditating on the law (v.2) and spiritual vitality is genuinely in the text.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 triggered:** The main point of the sermon (neglect your Bible and you’ll suffer these consequences) does not equal the main point of the text (two ways of life, one oriented toward Torah/God and one not, with contrasting destinies). Score cap: maximum 69.

**Flag 4 triggered — partial:** Application is specific (read through the Bible, sign a commitment card) but the specificity is in the activity assigned rather than in the text’s own diagnostic. The application does not differentiate between hearers — between the person who does not read because they are in unbelief and the person who does not read because they are busy. Psalm 1 distinguishes those two categories sharply; the sermon does not.

**Score cap applies: maximum 69.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 9/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (3/10):* Psalm 1 is read twice. The "tree planted by streams of water" image is developed with the banana tree illustration. The contrast between the righteous and the wicked is noted. But the fundamental interpretive move — treating vv. 4-6 as warnings to neglecting believers rather than descriptions of the wicked as a category — represents a substantial hermeneutical failure. The "twelve warnings" list conflates the psalm’s portrait of the wicked (their destiny) with warnings to the righteous (their potential fate if they stop reading). This is not what the text does. Additionally, the FCF is misidentified: the psalm’s fallen condition is not "Christians who don’t read their Bibles" but "the fundamental human orientation away from God’s law and toward the counsel of the wicked — a condition from which only God’s grace can redirect a person."
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5):* The wisdom psalm genre is not named. The psalm’s position as the gateway to the entire Psalter — an introduction to the way the Psalter is to be read — is not noted. The canonical significance of the opening word "blessed" (the same word that opens the Sermon on the Mount) is not developed. The connection between "delight in the law of the LORD" and the broader Torah theology of the Psalms is absent.
- *Exegetical Clarity (2/5):* The Spurgeon quote on Scripture is apt and accurately cited. The banana tree illustration is genuinely related to the planted-by-water image. But the "twelve warnings" reading is exegetically constructed from the text rather than derived from it — the sermon counts items in a poem and labels them warnings, which is not how wisdom poetry works.
- *Logical Faithfulness (2/5):* The primary hermeneutical problem is eisegesis by reframing: the text’s contrast of two categories of persons (righteous vs. wicked) is reread as a contrast of two behaviors (reading vs. not reading) available to the same person. This imports a self-help framework onto a wisdom text.

This is the theological center that would transform the sermon from a motivational talk on Bible reading into an exposition of where true blessedness is found.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The "twelve warnings" framework misreads the psalm’s genre:** Psalm 1 is a two-way wisdom psalm. Vv. 4-6 are not warnings to the righteous — they are descriptions of the wicked and their destiny. Reading them as "warnings about what happens to believers who don’t read their Bibles" imports an alien frame onto the text. A believer who plants himself in Christ and meditates on the law is described in vv. 1-3; the wicked in vv. 4-6 are not that person at risk — they are a different category of person entirely. The sermon’s entire application structure is built on this misreading.

2. **Christ is completely absent from the body of the sermon:** Psalm 1 has a rich Christological fulfillment — Jesus is the one who perfectly delighted in the law of the LORD (Matthew 4, Luke 4), who is the tree planted by water, whose righteousness is imputed to believers. The sermon treats Psalm 1 as a wisdom text about individual spiritual discipline without locating it in its New Testament fulfillment. The result is a moralistic address: do this and you’ll flourish; don’t and you’ll fail.

3. **The gospel is deferred and then replaced by a commitment card:** "Normally we talk about the gospel at the end of the service, but today I want you guys to commit to reading your bible through with us." The gospel is explicitly set aside in favor of a reading plan. This is a fatal structural error — not merely a weakness. The gospel cannot be optional to the conclusion of any Christian sermon.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher has a genuine and admirable burden for the congregation to be in the Word. That burden is real and right. The blind spot is the assumption that the application of Psalm 1 is *Bible reading as a spiritual discipline* rather than *fundamental reorientation of life toward God and away from the counsel of the wicked* — a reorientation that can only happen through regeneration and that is sustained by the Spirit, not by a reading commitment. The sermon motivates an activity without grounding it in the gospel that makes the activity possible or meaningful.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The "twelve warnings" framework *feels* textually thorough because it is systematic — twelve items, each referenced to a verse. This creates the impression of careful exegetical work. But the framework is constructed by counting items in a poem and labeling them, not by following the psalm’s own argument. The comprehensiveness of the list masks the fact that the list itself misidentifies what the psalm is doing.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The opening word of the Psalter — "Blessed" (Hebrew *asher*) — is the supreme missed opportunity of this sermon. This is the gateway to the entire Book of Psalms. Where is blessedness found? Not in a Bible reading program. In the person who is oriented toward God’s law as their delight and meditation — and in the New Testament’s fulfillment, in the person who is united to Christ, the perfectly blessed one who fulfilled all righteousness. The Beatitudes of Matthew 5 open with the same word. The psalm could have been opened as a proclamation of where true human flourishing is found — and its fulfillment in Christ could have transformed the sermon from a challenge to read more into a proclamation that the blessed life is available, in Christ, right now.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild the main point around the two ways, not the twelve warnings:** "Psalm 1 is not a list of what happens when you don’t read your Bible. It’s a picture of two kinds of people — the person whose life is rooted in God’s law and the person whose life is rooted in the counsel of the wicked — and it asks you which one you are. This is not a spiritual discipline question. It is a heart question."

2. **Name Christ as the perfectly blessed man:** "The person described in vv.1-3 — who delights in God’s law day and night, who is like a tree planted by water, who prospers in all he does — there is only one person who has ever fully lived Psalm 1. That is Jesus Christ. He delighted in the Father’s will perfectly. He never once sought counsel from the wicked. He is the tree planted by living water. And Psalm 1’s invitation is not ‘try harder to be this person’ — it is ‘be united to the one who already is this person.’"

3. **Add the gospel before the commitment card:** "Before we commit to read through the Bible together — hear why the Bible exists. It exists to show you Jesus. ‘These are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name’ (John 20:31). The goal of a year in the Word is not information — it is encountering Christ on every page."

4. **Distinguish the categories of hearers Psalm 1 actually requires:** "Some of you are already planted by the water — your roots are deep, and this sermon is a reminder to keep drinking. Some of you are like the banana tree that’s been cut off — you know the Word but you’re not in it, and you’re showing the signs: no fruit, no stability, withering. And some of you are not yet planted at all — and no Bible reading plan will fix that. What you need is not a commitment; what you need is to be planted in Christ by faith."

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered** — the sermon has warmth and pastoral care. But the absence of the gospel from the body of the sermon and the replacement of the gospel with a commitment card at the conclusion raise a concern not of dead orthodoxy but of gospel substitution. The sermon’s functional message is moralistic: do this and prosper, don’t and suffer. This is not the gospel.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon has genuine pastoral love and a real burden for the congregation to be in God’s Word — but the eternal gravity of Psalm 1 (two ways, two destinies, the blessed life found only in God) is not proclaimed, and the sermon’s conclusion leaves the congregation with a commitment card rather than Christ.

**Bonus: +1/5**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 1 is a wisdom psalm and the gateway to the entire Psalter. The rubric applies well. The FCF category is particularly diagnostic here: the fallen condition Psalm 1 addresses is the fundamental human orientation away from God and toward the counsel of the wicked — a condition that requires more than a Bible reading commitment to address. The rubric’s Berean Gate and Christ-centeredness categories correctly surface the sermon’s primary weaknesses. No rubric category is strained by the genre.
