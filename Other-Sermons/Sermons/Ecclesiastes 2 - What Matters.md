# Ecclesiastes 2:14–26 — What Matters?

---

In all the political mail, and commercials, and articles that have been being written lately, I read an interesting thing — a study by Pew Research looking at America and comparing our incomes to the rest of the world.

In the US our poverty line is $23,000 a year. Less than that, and by American standards you're poor. I'm below that figure. Probably many of us are. At $23k a year, you're living on about $63 a day. Electric, water, insurance, medical care, food, rent, and taxes all add up. In our country at that figure it's hard to make ends meet. To meet those bills we work more and more hours, longer and longer, and take fewer days off. The average American works 46 hours each week, with more than 60% of all Americans working at least 50 hours per week. That statistic includes a few outliers — kids that work a few hours a week during high school and so forth — if they weren't included, 46 would likely be 50 or more hours. 70% of American families are dual-income families where both parents have to work in order to make that $63 a day. We work more hours than most other countries. Interestingly, for the man-hours we put in, we are 400% more productive than we were 20 years ago, and more productive by a fair margin than most other countries — so much so that, despite paying higher wages per employee, it's still frequently less expensive to manufacture things here in the United States for a significant number of industries per item manufactured.

Even our poor in America are classified in the top tiers of income worldwide. If you can live off $20 a day, you are in the top 20% of incomes worldwide. $20 a day is $600 a month. $7,200 a year. Top 20%.

60% of the world lives on less than $10 a day, and a full 25% lives off less than $2 a day. How do they live off $2 a day? I have no idea. We as Americans are blessed to live in a financially prosperous time and place. Likewise we have access to information and learning that most of the rest of the world does not. We have luxuries, and food, and medicine that the rest of the world lacks. Likewise we have more and "better" — I use that word loosely, not better from a moral perspective, because it isn't; better from the perspective of the world that loves sin and its glorification — better entertainment than anywhere else in the world.

The lifestyle of our average families exceeds that of kings of previous centuries. Case in point: have you ever heard of gout? Gout used to be known as the "rich man's disease." It is tied to diet.

A friend of mine took me out for steak back in March. Anyone else like a good steak? He took a friend and I out to Longhorn Steakhouse and bought us the New York strip. It was phenomenal. Moist and flavorful. Marbled, mouthwatering, and absolutely outstanding. It was one of the best steaks I've had in a long, long time. In fact, just sitting here thinking about it makes me want a good steak.

Steak, however, is one of those occasional luxuries that Americans can afford that the rest of the world cannot. It was one of the reasons kings would get gout. Eating foods like steak can cause a buildup of uric acid that triggers the formation of crystals in your joints — which is gout. About 3.9% of American adults today have gout — roughly 1 in 25 — and that number is climbing. Point is, we, even those of us under the "poverty" line in America, are eating like kings. Many of us are living better than kings of the past.

Why does this matter? Because in our passage this morning, we're going to be reading a passage of a book intended for the wealthy. Solomon wrote to his nobles and spread his book to kings. While you and I may not be actual kings, we are part of the nation that leads all the others. In our nation, it's the people that have the power. We live and eat like kings that lack servants.

I think it's safe to say we are part of the group Solomon intended to read and take to heart this book.

We toil and work, hard, and for long periods, trying to leave a mark on the world, or to gain more luxury, or comfort, or peace, or whatever — and in the end we die, and all of it rusts and turns to dust. Yet what does Solomon say about all of that? Let's read.

**Ecclesiastes 2:14–26**

---

So the first thing I want to draw your attention to is the word *vanity*. In Hebrew the word is *hebel*. It can be translated as breath. It calls to mind a picture of hot breath on a cold day — that fog that you produce that's there for a moment and then vanishes.

I had a professor that said our lives are like hot breath on a piece of glass. It accumulates for a moment, and in a moment is gone without a trace. Solomon describes ten things as vanities throughout the book.

**First is human wisdom.** Human wisdom comes from where? The heart and mind of a human. And a human's heart and mind are what? Fallen, tainted by sin. Our view of the world is clouded by our sin nature. The closer we grow to God — the more time we spend in his Word, praying to him, in fellowship with other believers, and engaged in evangelism — the more that filthy cloud of sin-taint clears up and allows us to see things as they really are, as God sees them.

What does Scripture say about the heart of man? Jeremiah writes: "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?" (Jer. 17:9 ESV). The cure he is talking about is any kind of cure man can come up with. Our hearts do not point towards God; because of our sin nature, our hearts point us straight to hell. Every kids' show tells the idealistic young woman to trust her heart. But where does that lead? Apart from the renewing work and the power of God working in the heart of a person, where does one's heart point? Short of God re-directing our hearts towards him — or to quote the hymn, "tune our hearts to sing thy praise" — short of that, the thing our wicked hearts desire above all else is our own eternal torment.

1 Corinthians 1:25 says, "For the foolishness of God is wiser than human wisdom." Not that God is foolish, but the best we have to offer is infinitely worse than the least of what God has to offer. I heard Bob Leko say one time that God has more wisdom in his toenail clippings than we have in our whole body. Our wisdom is vanity. What's wise today is foolishness tomorrow. Our wisdom is, I think, best summed up in the debate over butter: is butter better, or margarine? Some days it's one, some days it's the other. Ultimately they decided butter is better — but tomorrow they'll change their minds. The best we can do with our finite and limited minds is a pale shadow of God's wisdom. Isaiah 55:8–9: "For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways, declares the Lord. For as the heavens are higher than the earth, so are my ways higher than your ways and my thoughts than your thoughts."

**Second and third: human effort and achievement** is vanity. There is a TV show called *Life After People*. It showed a number of famous places and followed what would happen 5, 10, 20, ultimately 200 years after man is no longer maintaining them. These great structures, symbolic of the effort of man, seldom made it to 200 years. They showed renderings of what the Sears Tower in Chicago — now called the Willis Tower — would look like. By twenty years the windows were blown out and vegetation was climbing up the outside of the building; by 100 years the entirety of the 1,400-foot-tall building was covered in vegetation, rust, and broken windows; by 200 years the whole thing collapsed under its own weight. The rubble provided shelter for wild animals. They showed the Statue of Liberty on the sea floor. They showed Mount Rushmore with its iconic faces having fallen and shattered. They showed every symbol of American might and power splintered, shattered, and fallen.

Ultimately, the best we can do with all of the might our people can summon is make things that wear out and fall down without our constant repair. Much of our technological achievement was the first to fail. Nuclear power plants, coal power plants exploding, burning, and melting down. The internet died before we lost power, and without power everything else went down. No AC, no heat. No production of gas or oil. All of our strength and effort, all of our genius and innovation — at best can produce a cloud of vapor in winter. There for a moment and gone without a trace. Vanity of vanities. All vanities. All vapor. One day all gone.

Some of you may have heard we had a dog get in with our sheep and mauled one pretty badly. Sheep are one of those things. You work hard, you do your best for them, and then one day they just give up and die. A guy I know says a sheep's life goal is death. Which is a dramatic overstatement of things — sheep are pretty hardy little animals, and if they don't give up, they can heal through almost anything. They really are remarkable. But when they give up, there is absolutely nothing we can do to keep them going. Our effort is vanity.

**The fourth was human life and rivalry.** Rivalry is pretty much useless. I was surprised to see a touch of this in other churches when we were looking for a building. Churches were afraid to take us in because they were afraid they would lose members or potential members. I'm afraid that some wanted their empire to grow, rather than the Kingdom of God. I don't want an empire. I just want people to come to know Christ.

Rivalries might cause some to accumulate more treasure or comfort, but we already discussed what comes of those. No matter how much gold people accumulate, one day when they die, not only will they not take it with them, but the object of their rivalry, their obsession, their life's work will pave the streets. Human life likewise is but a vapor. The longest average life expectancy in the world is around 87 for the ladies of Hong Kong. In America the average for both men and women is 79.

A person that lives to be 79 has had 28,835 days in their life. The first 18 years are spent in school, learning to talk, and in brain development. That leaves about 22,000 days. Of what's left, assuming you only sleep 8 hours a day, you lose another 7,000-plus days. 2,200 days are spent eating cumulatively. 6,800 days are lost to full-time employment. Another 2% of your life is spent in the bathroom. By the time you factor in time spent sick, grieving, and watching television, you're left with around 2,500 days left to do things that are meaningful and that will last beyond the test of time. From 28,835 days, you're left with 2,500.

What are you going to do with those precious few days? I want to do things that matter.

## What Matters in Life?

C.T. Studd, the famous preacher and evangelist, once wrote as part of a poem:

*"Only one life, 'twill soon be past, only what's done for Christ will last."*

C.T. Studd was quite a cricket player. He was as celebrated in cricket circles as many of our famous sports stars today — Tony Dungy, Peyton Manning, Babe Ruth, Mickey Mantle, Tom Brady, Jackie Robinson, Cristiano Ronaldo, or Messi. He was a rock star in cricket in England. He was no slouch mentally either — a graduate of Cambridge University. He basically graduated from an Ivy League-caliber school and went off to play professional sports.

There came a point, though, when he decided not to pursue cricket any further, because — in his own words — "I know that cricket would not last, and honor would not last, and nothing in this world would last, but it was worthwhile living for the world to come." So he canceled his sports contract and signed up to be a missionary overseas with a small outfit called China Inland Mission, run by a man barely known at the time who maybe you've heard of today — Hudson Taylor.

Studd was asked about why he wanted to go overseas, when he could live in the UK in comfort, prosperity, fame, and honor. He responded: "Some want to live within the sound of church or chapel bell; I want to run a rescue shop within a yard of hell."

On the mission field in China he threw himself into evangelism, church-planting, and ministry to those broken by poverty and addiction. At this point in time the UK — and even the US — were participating in the drug trade in China. We shipped opium over by the ton and sold it for great profit. Not as much from the US as from the UK, but we were not innocent. Opium addiction was rampant and we share culpability for that as a country.

He quickly gave all his money made playing cricket away to support missionaries. His father, who was quite wealthy, died and left it all to Studd. Studd could have used the money to make his life more comfortable, but instead he gave away his millions to support scores of missionaries all across China working to meet the felt and spiritual needs of the people.

He also wrote a sizeable check to support another little-known (at that time) preacher in Chicago — named Moody. That donation helped fund what became the Moody Bible Institute. Ever heard of Moody?

After ten years in China, he realized there were other mission fields that had no gospel presence. His heart was broken for the lost, and he packed up and moved to India, where he served for six more years. Then off to Africa, where he spent the rest of his life. He translated the Bible into native languages, started churches, and saw thousands and thousands of tribal people come to know the Lord as their Savior.

He was interviewed one time, and someone asked him why he would give up everything to go put his life — and the lives of his family — on the line for the gospel. He responded:

*"If Jesus Christ be God and died for me, then no sacrifice can be too great for me to make for Him."*

A life lived for Christ is what matters. Earthly wealth doesn't matter. Acclaim and honor don't matter. Comfort doesn't matter. What matters is Christ, and his priorities, and reaching people with the gospel.

---

We need to be focused on expanding the kingdom of God in quantity and quality. We need to be focusing on sharing the gospel and discipling new believers.

That's one of the reasons why I love our children's program. It shares the gospel with kids, and then they memorize Scripture and are discipled. Putting time and effort into the next generation is one of those things that will last.

I love our food pantry for similar reasons. We are able to meet people at their point of need. I don't think a week has gone by where we didn't have people that needed prayer, and there have even been opportunities to share the gospel, and opportunities for discipleship. It's a very real way for us to do things that "will last" in our community.

When evaluating whether or not to do a thing, I ask myself a couple of questions: Does it honor God, and will it give me opportunities to share the gospel and disciple people? Because those are the things that last.

Parents — pouring time and effort into your kids, telling them about the Lord, sharing the gospel with them, discipling them, reading Scripture with them — these are things that will last as well. It might take some convincing for older kids. But that is part of what it means when Scripture says we are to "raise up a child in the way he should go." We must be living examples of what it means to love God. Reading our Bible, praying, worshipping God — these are things that matter, things worth putting time and effort and finances into.

It all starts with a relationship — the gospel.

For those of you that are already believers, ask yourself: what am I doing in my life that will last? Anything? If not, and you want to honor God with your life, find something.

*Bringing conviction to my heart,*
*And from my mind would not depart;*
*Only one life, 'twill soon be past,*
*Only what's done for Christ will last.*

*Only one life, yes only one,*
*Soon will its fleeting hours be done;*
*Then, in 'that day' my Lord to meet,*
*And stand before His Judgment seat;*
*Only one life, 'twill soon be past,*
*Only what's done for Christ will last.*
