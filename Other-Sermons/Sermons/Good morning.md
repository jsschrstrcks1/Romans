## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastoral Heart Preaching Itself Instead of the Text**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm, COVID-contextual message on Mary and Martha that used the passage as a launching pad for practical encouragement about Bible reading and community, while never submitting to what the text itself actually declares.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**FAIL — Partial.**

*Scopus Test:* The text's main point (Luke 10:38–42) is Jesus' declaration that hearing His word is "the one thing necessary" — a Christological priority claim over even good and honorable service. The sermon's main point drifts into: "read your Bible more," "don't be a Martha," "stay safe during COVID," and "lean into community." These are not the same claim. The sermon cannot state a single thesis that demonstrably arises from the passage's own argument.

*Authority Test:* The preacher partially submits to the text, but the passage is functionally a springboard. Large portions of the sermon — the Walmart PPE story, the gazelle documentary, the campfire coal illustration, the hurricane flag-waver, the YOLO culture commentary — have no connection to the text. The sermon does not stay in orbit around Luke 10:38–42.

**The sermon passes the gate loosely — Christ is present and Scripture is referenced — but the main point of the sermon does not equal the main point of the text. This triggers Fatal Flaw Flag #2.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag #2 TRIGGERED: Main point does not equal the text's point.**
The text declares the supremacy of hearing Christ's word as "the one thing necessary" — a statement about the nature of discipleship, the priority of the Word over even good works, and implicitly the identity of Christ as the one worth sitting before. The sermon reduces this to a call for more personal Bible reading, community cohesion, and COVID-era spiritual discipline. The text's own declarative weight — Jesus' voice as authoritative, the posture of sitting at His feet as the defining posture of the disciple — is not the sermon's argument.

**Flag #4 PARTIALLY TRIGGERED: Application is significantly generic.**
The application ("be a Mary not a Martha," "read your Bible," "stay connected") is applied to a specific COVID moment, which gives it surface concreteness, but the application never differentiates hearers spiritually. There is no address to the doubter, the backslider, the self-righteous, the grieving.

**Flag #5 TRIGGERED: No actual exhortation.**
The sermon calls people to practical behaviors (read more, stay safe, stay connected, lean into God) but never issues a summoning imperative grounded in the text. There is no "therefore" moment. The congregation is encouraged and advised but never commanded.

**Score is capped at 69.**

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text's main claim is never preached.** Jesus' declaration — "Mary has chosen the good portion, which will not be taken away from her" — is the text's fulcrum. What is the "good portion"? Why will it not be taken away? What is the force of Jesus silencing Martha's legitimate grievance? The sermon quotes the passage but never opens it. 2. **The gospel is a note to self, not a proclamation.** The final section — "Gospel." — is a manuscript placeholder that was apparently never filled in. An unbeliever in this congregation received a description of heaven and hell but no explanation of how Christ's death and resurrection makes heaven available to sinners. Penal substitution, justification by faith, repentance — none are present. This is the most serious failure.

3. **The sermon lacks a spine.** There is no traceable argument. The Walmart PPE story (which runs longer than most of the textual engagement), the gazelle documentary, the hurricane flag-waver, and the COVID party Facebook post are all illustrations in search of a sermon that was never built. A listener cannot summarize what they were meant to believe, repent of, or pursue.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that the Mary/Martha story sits in deliberate sequence after the Good Samaritan parable in Luke 10. The lawyer asks "What must I do?" — Jesus tells the story of a man who *does*. Then immediately, Jesus visits two women: one who *does* (Martha) and one who *listens* (Mary). Luke is contrasting *doing-righteousness* with *receiving-righteousness*. Martha's busyness, like the lawyer's self-justification, is not bad — it is insufficient. The sermon that misses this misses Luke's own argument.

The preacher also appears unaware that sitting at the feet of a rabbi was the posture of a disciple — and that Mary, a woman, assuming this posture was a radical act of inclusion that Jesus affirmed. This is not merely a cultural note; it is a picture of the new covenant community where all disciples — regardless of gender, status, or tradition — sit before the Word.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The illustrations from daily life (Walmart, gazelle, campfire) *feel* like good preaching — they are immediate, accessible, and draw nods of recognition. But none of them arise from the text or return to it. The sermon *feels* earthy and practical, which masks the fact that it is not text-driven. Accessibility is not the same as faithfulness.

The pastoral identification — "I think there are a lot more Marthas than Marys" — feels like a knowing, honest assessment. But the sermon never actually applies the text's resolution to this condition. The preacher correctly diagnoses the disease but never prescribes the cure the text itself offers.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The text's greatest missed opportunity is the phrase "the good portion, which will not be taken away from her." This is a statement of security and permanence. In a COVID moment — a moment of profound insecurity, anxiety, and loss — the text is directly speaking. Martha is worried about things that can be disrupted, lost, taken. Mary has chosen something that cannot be disrupted or taken. The preacher had in his text the exact word his congregation needed for the moment, and it was never unpacked. The sermon gestures at this connection but never drives the stake.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the expository center around Jesus' declaration.** The sermon's textual work should focus on v.42: "Mary has chosen the good portion, which will not be taken away from her." What is the "good portion"? Exegete it: *meris* — a share, a portion, an inheritance. This is inheritance language. Mary is receiving something that cannot be disrupted by pandemics, death, or social upheaval. That is the sermon's COVID-relevant core, and it is right there in the text.

2. **Preach the Luke 10 sequence.** Add a brief transition from the Good Samaritan: "Last week we saw a man who *did* the right thing. This week Jesus is with two women — one who *does* and one who *listens*. Why does Jesus affirm the one who listens? Because doing without hearing is Martha's trap — and it was the lawyer's trap before her."

3. **Write out the gospel presentation in full.** "Gospel." must become a full paragraph: sin, the wrath of God, the cross, penal substitution, resurrection, faith and repentance, the call to believe. For a congregation with unbelievers present during a pandemic, this is not optional. The congregation received a heaven/hell description without a cross between them.

4. **Cut the Walmart PPE section and the hurricane man.** These sections belong to a pastoral newsletter or a midweek conversation, not a sermon. The time spent on them is time stolen from the text. Replace with the two surgical additions above.

5. **End with a summoning exhortation grounded in the text.** Something like: "There is only one thing that cannot be taken from you. Sit at His feet. Open this book. Let it speak. In a world that is taking everything — jobs, health, neighbors, normalcy — here is something the virus cannot touch. Choose it. Choose it today."

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** Dead orthodoxy requires correct theology delivered without fire. This sermon has pastoral warmth, but the theology is insufficient rather than bloodless. The issue is not inertia — it is incompleteness. The preacher has heart; the text has not yet been allowed to give the heart something to preach.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement

**+1/5.** There is a flicker of eternal gravity in the heaven/hell contrast at the close — "if God and heaven hold everything good and holy in existence, then hell is every evil thing you can imagine concentrated into one spot" — but it arrives without a cross and without the text's authority behind it, which prevents it from carrying the weight it should.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Luke 10:38–42 is narrative gospel — a brief, dense scene with a single declarative statement from Jesus. The rubric fits well. Narrative passages are sometimes less amenable to systematic exposition, but this passage has a clear didactic center (v.42) that grounds the rubric's demands for textual fidelity, gospel connection, and applicatory force. The genre does not strain the rubric here; the sermon simply did not engage the genre's gifts. The text is generous — it offers a clear thesis, a concrete contrast, a COVID-relevant declaration of security, and an implicit Christological claim. A sermon built on this text has everything it needs.
