TITUS 1:1-5 — Introduction to Titus
Introductory sermon to a new series on the book of Titus.

---

We're going to start a new book tonight called Titus. It's one of Paul's letters. It was probably written between 1 and 2 Timothy. In fact, if you remember the book of Acts, there is a story where Paul has a vision in the night that he is to go to Macedonia. So Paul — when he wakes up in the morning — redirects his entire group to Macedonia. [Note: Acts 16:9-10. The Macedonian vision belongs to Paul's second missionary journey, circa 49-51 AD. The letter to Titus was written after Paul's first Roman imprisonment, circa 63-65 AD — approximately 12 to 15 years later, not "shortly after" the Macedonia vision. The pastor appears to be using the vision as a general illustration of Paul's obedience to God's direction rather than as a strict chronological marker.] This is towards the end of his life, just a few years before he was to be captured, put on trial, and executed.

In preparing this week it struck me that when things are going well for Paul he's accused of pride, but when he's imprisoned people thought he was a failure. At this point in his life, Jews were showing up in what we would call today Christian churches, and were preaching things that conflicted with Scripture. Other places, people were rising to leadership that had all sorts of false beliefs of their own. Churches that Paul had started were wandering into heresy, being led astray by false teachers of many types, and like the Corinthian church were being torn apart from the inside by divisiveness. People he had poured his life into had abandoned him.

I think if anyone knew what it was like to be in the hardest and deepest trenches of ministry, it was Paul. There were days when maybe it felt like everything God had done through him was turning to sand. But what was Paul's response? He pressed on. He ran the good race. He didn't care what people thought of him — didn't care that some thought him proud and others thought him a failure. He only cared that he was doing what God wanted him to do, be that in Macedonia, or in Rome, where he would ultimately be imprisoned. When it came to the struggles inside the churches he had labored at the direction of the Holy Spirit to plant, he wrote letters. He sent people he had discipled to help sort out these different situations. That's the capacity we find Titus serving in.

There are only a handful of verses about Titus in the entire Bible. We know, and will read in a minute, that he was someone who had been led to the Lord by Paul. Paul calls him his son. Other than that we get a glimpse of his resume.

Paul sent him to Corinth. They were immoral, divisive, and divided. They were a mess — but Titus was the man God sent to straighten them out. [Note: Titus's mission to Corinth is documented in 2 Corinthians 7:6-7; 8:6, 16-17.] Having completed that task, Paul sent Titus to Illyricum — also known as Dalmatia. [Note: "Lyricum" as spoken; the correct name is Illyricum. Titus's presence in Dalmatia is confirmed in 2 Timothy 4:10: "Demas, in love with this present world, has deserted me and gone to Thessalonica. Crescens has gone to Galatia, Titus to Dalmatia." Paul's own work in that region is referenced in Romans 15:19: "from Jerusalem and all the way around to Illyricum I have fulfilled the ministry of the gospel of Christ."] What was remarkable about Dalmatia was its populace. I can picture Paul telling Titus to go to Dalmatia, and Titus saying, "OK, Paul — who's there?" And Paul saying: "Pirates!" Dalmatia was known for Illyrian pirates, and Paul sent Titus there to straighten out the church. Titus has quite the resume!

Now Paul was sending him to Crete. Have you ever heard the word "cretin"? [Note: The pastor connects "cretin" to Crete and to the Cretan reputation for deception. However, "cretin" and "Cretan" are unrelated words with different etymologies. "Cretan" means a person from the island of Crete. "Cretin" comes from the Latin Christianum via an 18th-century Alpine French dialect word (crestin), referring to people with congenital thyroid deficiency — a reminder that they were still human, still Christian. The two words are etymologically unconnected. The phrase about out-tricking a trickster is tied to the Cretan reputation for deception, not to the word "cretin." See Titus 1:12 below, where Paul himself quotes this Cretan reputation.] A common phrase was "to play the Cretan with a Cretan" — meaning you had out-deceived, out-tricked a trickster. You scammed a scammer, in other words. The people in Crete had their issues, to say the least.

These two men — Paul and Titus — kind of remind me of a man named Sanctus. He was a believer during a time when believing in Jesus Christ was unpopular, to say the least. In the course of living his life as a believer in Jesus Christ he was arrested and taken to trial. When asked his name, his occupation, where he lived — he gave only one answer. He said, "I am a Christian." [Note: The historical account of Sanctus (a deacon martyred in Lyon, circa 177 AD) is preserved by Eusebius in Ecclesiastical History, Book V, Chapter 1. The historical record is that his repeated answer to every question — name, race, city, occupation, status — was "I am a Christian" (Christianos eimi / Christianus sum), not "bondservant of Jesus Christ" as some retellings have it. The pastor's version of the story is consistent in spirit with the historical account; the precise answer differs. The torture included heated bronze plates applied to his body and later an iron chair in the amphitheater. He was ultimately martyred in the arena.] So they decided to try and torture the truth out of him. They heated a metal chair until it was glowing red hot, and made him sit on it. "What's your name!" — "I am a Christian." Ultimately they tied him to a post, prepared the wood for a fire beneath him, and demanded his name. But all he would say was "I am a Christian."

Men who contend for their faith in the teeth of persecution are heroes. What a contrast to today. There are "leaders" of Christianity who are compromising on core doctrines for the sake of what they call relevance — but sometimes what they mean is compromise. An author respected in his field came out and said that hell isn't eternal, and that he had come to the conclusion that there may be other ways to get to heaven. That's not what my Bible says: "there is no other name under heaven given among men by which we must be saved." [Note: Acts 4:12, ESV. Quoted accurately in substance; full ESV text: "And there is salvation in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given among men by which we must be saved."] It says — in Matthew 7 — that wide is the gate and easy is the way that leads to destruction, but narrow is the gate and hard is the way that leads to life. [Note: Matthew 7:13-14, ESV: "Enter by the narrow gate. For the gate is wide and the way is easy that leads to destruction, and those who enter by it are many. For the gate is narrow and the way is hard that leads to life, and those who find it are few." The pastor's version is a close paraphrase, not a direct quotation.] Another man — a famous missiologist, a man whose books I read in college, considered one of the pre-eminent minds in missiology — came out and said he wasn't sure hell lasts forever; not that you get out and go to heaven, but that you are annihilated by being in hell. Another man who wrote a popular set of deeply impactful, purpose-driven books came out in the last couple of years and said that the Quran reflects Jesus. [Note: These three individuals are not named in the sermon as delivered. The "purpose-driven" reference is widely understood to refer to Rick Warren. The specific claim attributed to him — that the Quran reflects Jesus — has not been verified against a primary source and should be treated as unverified until confirmed.]

Not only are leaders slipping away from what the Bible says — we shouldn't be surprised that church members are struggling theologically also. I read a Pew Research study that said 60% of all Americans affirmed the necessary essential doctrines of our faith but added in other things from false religions. [Note: Pew Research has conducted multiple surveys on religious mixing in America; the specific statistic of 60% as stated has not been verified against a primary Pew report and should be confirmed before citing again.] Some people affirmed the deity of Christ, heaven, hell — and reincarnation. Others believed that Jesus died for everyone's sins so no one would have to go to hell, which they interpreted to mean they could commit any sin they wanted with abandon. Hey, no worries — God doesn't mind if I fornicate, or lie, or steal, or cheat, or gossip, or cause division in the church, because God has forgiven everyone of their sins. All of them, and that's the end of it. That's simply not true. We don't know all of the things that Titus was being called to address, but we know there were issues — maybe some of these same issues.

Let's read Titus 1:1-5.

    Paul, a servant of God and an apostle of Jesus Christ, for the sake of the faith of God's elect and their knowledge of the truth, which accords with godliness, in hope of eternal life, which God, who never lies, promised before the ages began and at the proper time manifested in his word through the preaching with which I have been entrusted by the command of God our Savior;

    To Titus, my true child in a common faith:

    Grace and peace from God the Father and Christ Jesus our Savior.

    This is why I left you in Crete, so that you might put what remained into order, and appoint elders in every town as I directed you —
    — Titus 1:1-5, ESV

[Note: The passage was read from the NET (New English Translation) in the original delivery. The NET translates δοῦλος as "slave" — which is the basis for the pastor's discussion below. The ESV renders it "servant." Both translations are of the same Greek word. The pastor's explanation of the NET's translation choice is accurate: the NET translators at Dallas Theological Seminary made a deliberate, documented decision to render δοῦλος as "slave" throughout the New Testament, arguing that translating it as "servant" softens the meaning in ways not supported by the Greek or by other ancient literature from the period. The original NET text read in the sermon: "From Paul, a slave of God and apostle of Jesus Christ, to further the faith of God's chosen ones and the knowledge of the truth that is in keeping with godliness, in hope of eternal life, which God, who does not lie, promised before the ages began. But now in his own time he has made his message evident through the preaching I was entrusted with according to the command of God our Savior. To Titus, my genuine son in a common faith. Grace and peace from God the Father and Christ Jesus our Savior! The reason I left you in Crete was to set in order the remaining matters and to appoint elders in every town, as I directed you."]

There is that word: bondservant. Part of my process is that I like to read the passage each week in a number of different translations. My favorite is the ESV — it's usually the closest to the Greek, and usually the most clear. But this week the NET — the New English Translation — was the best. NET being the translation that Dallas Theological Seminary has been working on for a few decades. They translate that word δοῦλος — in our ESVs, "servant" — as "slave." In the notes they include with their translation they say it was a controversial move outside the school, but inside the school they were unanimous, because only in the translation of biblical literature, and only starting at the point in American and British history where we began rationalizing slave-keeping, did we move away from translating δοῦλος as "slave." But in every other ancient work from the period, the word is translated as "slave" — or more specifically, one who had sold themselves into slavery. So there is no reason to ever translate that word as servant. Bondservant is better, but doesn't fully convey the same thing as slave. Because as a slave, we don't get a say in the matter. As a slave, if you're told to do something, you do it. If you're told not to do something, you had better not. As a slave, you're not able to sit there and bargain with God: "Well, let's just wait until I feel like it," or "I'm not getting paid enough for this." And you can already see where the focus is when you start asking those questions — it's here (heart). When in reality our focus should be on God.

That's the crux of the issue for people like the ones mentioned earlier who have strayed away from Scripture. They care more about what feels good to them, and less about what God wants. The focus is on instant gratification instead of the eternal. But here is the hard truth: there may be two masters, but you can only serve one. Serving one is constricting, and erodes who you are, and ultimately sends you to hell. Being slave to the other is the only way to find freedom. There is eternal freedom in following Christ. Following Christ is the only way to true and lasting joy. Chasing what pleases you now is serving our enemy. Chasing what pleases God now serves our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ.

In the Old Testament — I'm specifically thinking of Elijah and Moses — they were both called servants of God. [Note: Moses is called "servant of the LORD" (עֶבֶד יְהוָה, eved Yahweh) many times in Scripture: Numbers 12:7; Deuteronomy 34:5; Joshua 1:1-2; and others. Elijah is referenced as God's servant in 2 Kings 9:36; 10:10 ("by his servant Elijah"). The Hebrew עֶבֶד carries the same range of meaning as δοῦλος — servant, slave, bondservant. The pastor's connection between the two is linguistically and theologically sound.] It was an incredibly honorable position. Being a slave to Jesus puts you in the ranks of Paul, of Elijah, of Moses — men who were still fallen humans, but men who tried to be obedient to God in all things. Men who changed the world. Men whom God used to do extraordinary things. Remember Elijah and the prophets of Baal? [Note: 1 Kings 18 — the confrontation on Mount Carmel.] Or Moses and the plagues of Egypt? These are men who, with God's help, changed the world. But it all started with the pursuit of God and his will. When God would teach them something new, they would implement what God showed them in their life.

The title "servant of God" had deep meaning for the Jews, and would have had a deep effect on Paul's audience. It might be like in Washington — someone walking over to a DC staffer and saying, "Hey, I'm the Vice President, and this guy — I want you to listen to him." Even though they may not like Titus's mission, they would have had respect for Paul, and his title, and that would have extended to Titus as well.

Titus had a hard job ahead of him in Crete. Check out verse 5 again with me. "This is why I left you in Crete" — basically, to choose leaders out of the charred remains of the church there. So many had strayed in numerous different ways: in sexual sin and debauchery, insubordination, arrogance, anger, drunkenness, fornication — these are just a few of the things that Paul warns Titus to be wary of in his ministry in Crete.

But think about this situation for a minute. Some new guy comes in and dismantles the church as they've always done it — removing people from positions of authority and replacing them. Titus will have been there ten minutes, and some of the others may have been there for years.

I had a friend who was a pastor at a small church in Tennessee for a while — a small SBC church with a congregation of about thirty. He'd been there about six weeks and he and the senior pastor worked together to come up with a new and fresh vision for the church. They made a new logo, a catchy saying, and spent much of their paycheck having this all printed on a new, shiny banner to go on the back wall of the foyer. Because there was an old banner there — it had been a nice banner once upon a time, but with age it had become rotten. It was faded, cracked, pieces were falling off, some of the words were no longer legible. It was in bad shape. It was a ruin, basically, and was in need of replacing.

So my friend Stephen showed up early one Sunday and climbed up on a ladder and began taking the banner down. He carefully and respectfully rolled it up as best he could, but it was so brittle that more pieces broke off, damaging it further. About ten minutes before church started, one of the long-time members — the only Sunday school teacher for the church — walked in, and he gasped, and moaned, and then shouted: "That banner has been there for twenty-two years!" He slammed the church door and went out into the parking lot, stewing and steaming. It took the pastor twenty minutes to talk him into going in to teach his Sunday school class.

It's hard to make changes in a church. The closest anti-example I could think of was a church that some of us have been to — one that would dismiss Godly people who had been serving faithfully and successfully for years, for nepotistic reasons. Whatever the reason, the process is unpleasant. When it's God-directed — as it was in Crete — it's important. But that's not to say God isn't capable of taking bad circumstances and making something good out of them. This church was, I think, the result of God's plan, knowing what difficult things would be headed for that other church. Haven't we all grown as a result of what we went through? Aren't we all closer to God now than we had been before going through the hard time? God has also given us a way to relate to people who have been hurt by churches. We have been hurt, and we drew closer to the Lord.

Sinclair Ferguson said that it basically comes down to pressure — do you allow the pressure to push between you and God, or do you allow the pressure to push you closer to God? [Note: This quote is attributed to Sinclair Ferguson but the specific source — sermon, book, or interview — has not been verified. Needs confirmation before citing formally.]

But it all starts with a relationship with Jesus Christ.
