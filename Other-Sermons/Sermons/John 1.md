# John 1 — The Logos

*Raw notes*

---

## Opening Illustration

Six blind elephants were discussing what men were like. After arguing they decided to find one and determine what it was like by direct experience. The first blind elephant felt the man and declared, "Men are flat." After the other blind elephants felt the man, they agreed.

We'll come back to that.

## Scripture Reading

John 1:1–5 (ESV)

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was in the beginning with God. All things were made through him, and without him was not any thing made that was made. In him was life, and the life was the light of men. The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.

---

## I. In Any Beginning

Right from the beginning, John is packing meaning into every word. And a little of it gets lost in translation. Let me show you what I mean.

Your Bible says "In the beginning." In the original Greek it's simpler — just *en archē*. No article. No "the." Just *in beginning*. That sounds like bad grammar in English, but in Greek it's a very precise move. John is saying: in any beginning you can imagine, *He was already there*. Before your beginning. Before the universe's beginning. Before whatever beginning you want to name — the Word was already there.

*Archē*, by the way, is the root of the word archaic. It means origin. Source. The thing that comes first.

John 1:1 is a direct answer to Genesis 1:1. But it's also written to a Greek audience that was already asking the question — and had been asking it for centuries.

---

## II. What the Greeks Already Knew

At the time John wrote this, the Greek world was not satisfied with its gods. You're at least peripherally aware of the system — Zeus, Apollo, a thousand others, including altars to gods they hadn't named yet. The Athenians even built an altar to an unknown god, just to cover their bases. Paul found that altar in Acts 17 and used it as his opening.

The desire to find *the* God — the real one, the one behind everything — was deeply built into Greek culture. And it's built into all of us. They knew what they had didn't satisfy. They couldn't put their finger on why. So they kept searching.

That search led their philosophers to an idea. They called it the *Logos*. In our English Bibles, *Logos* is translated as "Word." But it could just as accurately be translated "Reason" — the rational principle that underlies and holds together everything that exists. The creative intelligence behind reality.

Read those opening verses again with that in mind:

*In the beginning was the Reason, and the Reason was with God, and the Reason was God. The Reason was in the beginning with God. All things were made through the Reason, and without the Reason was not any thing made that was made. In the Reason was life, and the life was the light of men. The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.*

Verse 3 confirms it: *all things were made through the Logos*. Jesus was the reason the universe came into existence. He was the creative mind behind a million scenic vistas. The Grand Canyon. Every waterfall. The rainbow God used to seal his promise after the flood — that was His work too.

NASA has an app that gives you a constant stream of images from space. Nebulas the size of solar systems, shaped like a crab, built from billions of stars and probably trillions of planets. Things so intricate, so beautifully artistic, that you have no recourse but to say — *there is a God*.

I was thinking about sunrises. If the earth is roughly 6,000 years old — which puts us close to the Jewish calendar, which in recent years has hovered around 5,777 — then at 365.25 days per year, you get somewhere around 4,383,000 sunrises and sunsets over the course of human history. Each one unique. Each one shaped by a different mix of atmosphere, dust, moisture, and geography. A sunset from Hudson Beach looks different than Clearwater. Clearwater looks different than open ocean — out at sea, with no dust or particulate matter to scatter the light, the sun drops as a flat yellow disk. Nothing to diffract it. Just the ball sinking below the water.

Every one of those 4.3 million sunrises was hand-painted. For us. So that we would look up and know that something intelligent was behind all of this. So that our souls would reach for God.

That's what the Greeks were doing with the Logos. They saw the evidence. They knew their story didn't line up with what they were seeing. So they kept searching.

John walks in and says: *Boys. I found Him. Let me tell you His story.*

Anything good in this world — any light, any moment where darkness pulled back — that was Him. He is the reason we exist. He is the light that gives sight to every man. He is the Logos. Look no further.

And unlike the Greek gods — where the old gods were overthrown by new ones in an endless cycle of violence — this one existed before everything, was there at the beginning of everything, will be here through everything, and in the end drives out the darkness permanently. He wins.

John packed all of that into five sentences. We've spent twenty minutes unpacking it and we're still not done. He gave a thesis for the entire Bible — creation explained, man's search for God explained, the gospel touched, the end times touched — all in one breath.

For a first-century Greek reader, that landing would have been like a bomb going off inside their mind. Everything they had been searching for, named.

## III. The World Missed Him

Jump ahead to verse 9.

> The true light, which gives light to everyone, was coming into the world. He was in the world, and the world was made through him, yet the world did not know him. He came to his own, and his own people did not receive him. But to all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God, who were born, not of blood nor of the will of the flesh nor of the will of man, but of God.
> And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth. — John 1:9–14

John says it plainly. All you people who have been looking for the Logos — you were looking in the wrong direction. When He actually came and lived among you, you missed Him. I knew Him. I received Him. He made me a child of God. We saw His glory — glory that could only have come from the Father.

Then verse 16:

> For from his fullness we have all received, grace upon grace. For the law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. — John 1:16–17

Think about what fullness means. At the end of a meal — you've eaten everything, you're full, the meal is over. Christ's ministry reached its fullness at the cross. He came, He accomplished everything He set out to do, and He finished it. *Tetelestai* — it is finished. Grace upon grace, from a ministry that was complete.

---

## IV. Walking in the Dark

Now — the darkness that exists in the world around us.

People who don't know Christ only know the darkness.

My family, on most vacations, would find a cavern to explore. One of the things that sticks in your mind is when they tell you not to move, and flip the lights off. The darkness in a cavern is something different from ordinary darkness. It's not like waiting for your eyes to adjust in a dark room. Your eyes never adjust. No amount of time sitting there will let you see even the outline of your own hand. Because you cannot generate light. Light is external. It comes from somewhere else. There is nothing inside you on your own that is capable of driving away the dark.

That's what Paul is describing in 1 Corinthians 2:14 — *"The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned."*

Apart from Christ, a person is stumbling around in that cavern. And even when they stumble into truth, they can't fully make sense of it. They dismiss it as folly. They can't see it.

This is where the blind elephants come back in.

There's an old parable — John Godfrey Saxe wrote a famous poem about it in the 1800s, though the story itself is much older — about blind men encountering an elephant. One grabs a leg and says an elephant is like a great tree. One grabs the tail and says it's like a rope. One puts his hands against the side and says it's like a wall. Each one has a piece of the truth. None of them has the whole picture. Their disagreement leads to argument, the argument leads to conflict, and nobody ends up knowing what an elephant actually is.

That's the position of natural man trying to find God on his own. We can grasp little pieces of truth and misapply them. We can build a God in our own image. We can accept the evidence of sunrises and nebulas and still arrive at the wrong conclusion — the way Einstein arrived at Spinoza's God instead of the God of Scripture.

That's why it was necessary for God to send His Son into the world.

The Old Testament has two purposes. Primarily, it points to Christ. Secondarily, it shows us how badly we miss the mark. The law reveals the gap. The sacrificial system covers sin temporarily but can't close it. And then — John 3:16. The Logos becomes flesh. Light enters the dark world. And after His death and resurrection, He sends His Spirit to live in His people.

It's not an accident that the Spirit descended in Acts 2 as tongues of fire. In a world of darkness, there are now people carrying light inside them.

## V. What Are You Doing With Your Light?

Believers — what are you doing with yours?

Ephesians 2:1–5:

> And you were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked, following the course of this world, following the prince of the power of the air, the spirit that is now at work in the sons of disobedience — among whom we all once lived in the passions of our flesh, carrying out the desires of the body and the mind, and were by nature children of wrath, like the rest of mankind. But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ — by grace you have been saved.

You have been made alive. You carry a light that was placed in you by the Spirit of God. Are you still dragging the old self around? Still living like you're in the dark — cursing, getting drunk, living like the people who haven't been found yet? When you do that, you are pulling the corpse of your old life out of the grave and using it to cover the light.

That's absurd. And Scripture says so.

Use the light. Help others see.

---

## Gospel

If you're here tonight and you feel like you're wandering in the dark — I want to introduce you to the light. The Creator of the world. The one who died on the cross for your sins and rose three days later.

Romans 3 says all have sinned. Of course we have — we've been stumbling around in the darkness we chose. But Jesus came to give light to every man. And whosoever believes in Him shall not perish.

If you've never come to that point — talk to me after the service. It is the most important thing you will ever do.

## Preaching Notes

- **Einstein salary** — the notes say "$250,000 a year." His actual Institute for Advanced Study salary when he arrived in 1933 was $16,000. This number needs to be verified or removed before delivery. The illustration works without the salary figure; cut it if unconfirmed.

- **"Yellow sunset at sea"** — the optical claim (reduced scattering without land-based particulate matter) is meteorologically plausible; verify with a reliable source before presenting as fact.
- **Hebrew year ~5,777** — places this sermon around 2016–2017. Update the calendar reference before repreaching.
- **Duplicate paragraphs removed** — the raw notes contained the cavern illustration, the 1 Cor 2 introduction, and the gospel closing appeal each written twice. Duplicates removed in this draft.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Winsome Apologetic That Nearly Preaches — But Leaves the Conscience Untouched**

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A genuinely creative opening of John 1:1–17 that illuminates the Logos concept with real cultural intelligence and some memorable imagery, while stopping short of pressing the conscience, differentiating among hearers, or delivering a gospel proclamation with the weight the text demands.

### 3. Berean Gate
**Pass — with qualification.**

**Scopus Test**: The sermon's thesis is broadly defensible: *Jesus is the Logos the Greeks were searching for, and He is the Light the world needs*. That thesis does arise from the passage's own argument, particularly the movement from vv. 1–5 to vv. 9–14. Pass.

**Authority Test**: Pass, though the sermon's treatment of verse 5 ("the darkness has not overcome it") is quietly triumphalist without pressing the personal implication — that every unbeliever in the room is still in that darkness. The text's harder edges are present but underworked.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**One flag triggered — score capped at 69.**

- **Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: Section V ("What Are You Doing With Your Light?") is the sermon's only extended application, and it reduces to: stop sinning, carry your light, help others see. No specific behavior is named, no particular sinner is cornered, no differentiated address is offered. "Use the light. Help others see." is an instruction so broad it provides no traction. The gospel closing appeal, while present, is brief and functions more as an invitation than a proclamation.

No other fatal flaws triggered. The gospel is present (Section "Gospel" and via Ephesians 2 citation). Christ is necessary to the argument. The sermon is accessible. Exhortation exists in embryonic form. But the application failure is structural.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 18/25**

- *Textual Fidelity*: 7/10. The sermon works meaningfully from vv. 1–5 and 9–14, and the Greek wordplay on *en archē* is handled with genuine skill. However, the passage contains substantial material that goes untreated: the witness of John the Baptist (vv. 6–8, 15), the explicit statement that Christ is "the only Son from the Father" (v. 14), and the law/grace contrast in vv. 16–17 is mentioned but not developed. The text's climax — the Incarnation in v. 14 — receives less weight than the Logos discussion in vv. 1–5.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 4/5. The Hellenistic cultural context is handled well. The OT echo of Genesis 1:1 is named. The positioning of this prologue as John's thesis statement for the entire Gospel is implied but not stated.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 4/5. The *en archē* / *Logos* discussion is the sermon's exegetical high point — transparent, accessible, and genuinely illuminating. *Tetelestai* is used but not explained adequately ("fullness" at the end of a meal is an unusual and potentially misleading gloss on "it is finished" — this is the cross-cry, not a reference to completed ministry in general).
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 3/5. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is gestured at — natural man in darkness, stumbling without light — but it is left abstract rather than named concretely. The sermon diagnoses the human condition philosophically (we are blind elephants) without pressing that diagnosis into the hearer's actual spiritual situation.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness*: 5/7. Christ is clearly the center of the sermon's intellectual argument. But He is more the answer to a philosophical question than the Savior from sin and judgment. The Incarnation is present but the Atonement is barely touched. The "He died on the cross for your sins and rose three days later" gospel section is one sentence in the closing appeal — the most theologically dense claim in the sermon is the least developed.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness*: 2/3. The movement from Greek longing → OT law → Incarnation is sketched. The connection to Genesis 1 is noted. But the Covenant of Grace structure — why the law was added, what the sacrificial system signified, how Christ fulfills rather than merely supplements — is underdeveloped.
- *Doctrinal Precision*: 3/4. No serious errors. The Ephesians 2 citation is well-chosen. The brief reference to John 3:16 is accurate but cursory. The *tetelestai* gloss is imprecise (see above).
- *Confessional Alignment*: 3/4. No confessional contradictions. The total depravity implication of 1 Corinthians 2:14 is handled correctly. Regeneration is implied in the "made alive" language. Grace is present but justification is not clearly articulated.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel*: 2/5. The gospel section is present but structurally marginalized — it is a separate, brief section at the end rather than the culminating heartbeat of the sermon's argument. "He died on the cross for your sins and rose three days later" is the sum total of the atonement presentation. An unbeliever would understand *something* must be done but not why the cross accomplishes it. Penal substitution is absent. Justification by faith is absent.
- *Necessity of Christ*: 4/5. Christ is genuinely necessary to the sermon's argument. The Logos section makes this clear. The sermon does not survive without Jesus.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity*: 3/5. The sermon builds toward the Incarnation, with a passing reference to the cross. The cross does not function as the sermon's gravitational center — it is a supporting data point. The sermon moves toward cosmological wonder more than cruciform grace.

**Synagogue Test**: Partially triggered. A devout Greek philosopher who accepted the Logos concept and the ethical implications of living in the light could follow sections I–IV without finding the cross essential. The cross appears, but briefly, and is not given offensive clarity. The sermon should offend anyone who believes they can find God through philosophy or moral effort. It mostly doesn't.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15**

The image of "dragging the corpse of your old life out of the grave to cover the light" is the sermon's strongest application moment — visceral and biblically grounded. But the sermon does not press past the intellect into specific, personal conscience-searching. The hearer is told what the darkness is; they are not shown that they themselves are in it.
- *Specificity*: 2/5. "Cursing, getting drunk, living like the people who haven't been found yet" is the sermon's only specific application, and it addresses behavior rather than the heart behind behavior. - *Spiritual Differentiation*: 3/5. The sermon implicitly addresses believers (Section V) and unbelievers (Gospel section). The Perkins taxonomy is otherwise unused. **5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* Mostly Christ — the Logos material is genuinely Christ-exalting. But the application pivot pulls toward self-assessment without adequate grounding in what Christ has done.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Largely ideas about Him. The cosmological wonder is real, but God does not become weighty and personal. He remains an intellectual answer to the Greek philosophers' question.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Primarily informed. The cavern illustration touches conviction, and the Ephesians 2 passage touches comfort, but neither is pressed to its depth.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **The *en archē* exegesis (Section I)** is the sermon's finest moment — the absence of the Greek article is explained accessibly, the implication ("in any beginning you can imagine, He was already there") is drawn correctly, and the connection to *archē* / archaic is memorable without being pedantic.
2. **The blind elephants payoff in Section IV** — returning the opening illustration as an image of natural man's epistemological condition before God — is structurally elegant. "That's the position of natural man trying to find God on his own" lands with appropriate force, and the Einstein counter-example (arriving at Spinoza's God despite the evidence) is sharp.
3. **The Ephesians 2 citation and the corpse image** ("dragging the corpse of your old life out of the grave") is the sermon's single best application moment — visceral, theologically grounded, and pointed at the right target.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The gospel section is a paragraph, not a proclamation.** "He died on the cross for your sins and rose three days later" is factually correct and functionally insufficient. The sermon has spent substantial time establishing why Christ is the Logos the world needed and almost no time explaining what His death accomplished and why it is the only ground of hope. The cross must be more than the sermon's final data point.
2. **Section V applies to behavior without pressing the heart.** "Cursing, getting drunk" names sins; it does not diagnose the idolatry, unbelief, or self-trust behind them. Application that targets behavior without first exposing the motivating sin produces moralism, not transformation. The preacher should ask: *why* is a believer still pulling the corpse out of the grave? What are they trusting instead of Christ?
3. **The Incarnation (v. 14) is the passage's climax and the sermon's afterthought.** "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us" receives a single paragraph of treatment in a section whose real weight goes to the Greeks' failed search and John's triumphant announcement. The verb *eskēnōsen* — "tabernacled among us," an unmistakable echo of the Exodus tabernacle — is unused. The glory language ("glory as of the only Son from the Father") is passed over. This is where the text explodes, and the sermon walks past it.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's intellectual strength is also its pastoral weakness. The Logos material is genuinely engaging, but it creates a sermon that explains *who Jesus is* without pressing *what that demands of you now*. The apologetic mode — convincing people that Christ is the Logos — quietly displaces the proclamation mode — announcing that this Lord must be received or rejected. The congregation may leave having learned something without having been confronted by Anyone.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The illustration work in this sermon *feels* like strong application because it is vivid and concrete. The cavern, the 4.3 million sunrises, the blind elephants — these land well and give the impression of pastoral grounding. But illustrations are not application. The sermon illustrates its doctrinal points persuasively without applying them with specificity. Vivid illustration masks thin conscience-work.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
Verse 14 — *"The Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth."*

The word *eskēnōsen* (tabernacled) is a direct and deliberate echo of the Exodus tabernacle — the place where God's glory *dwelt* among His covenant people, where sinners approached through blood, where the presence of God was both holy and near. John is saying: everything the tabernacle pointed toward, this is it. The glory that filled the Most Holy Place and was too dangerous to approach without the blood of atonement — that glory *became a man and lived in your neighborhood*. This connection to the OT sacrificial system would have deepened the Incarnation section immeasurably and provided the theological bridge between the Logos discussion and the atonement the sermon never quite reaches.

### 14. Surgical Fixes
1. **Expand the gospel section into a proclamation.** The current gospel section is three sentences. Minimum: name the human problem (sin as rebellion against the Logos who made us, not just as "stumbling in darkness"), explain what the cross accomplished (Christ absorbed the wrath our darkness deserved), and call for specific response (repent and trust, not merely "talk to me"). The Ephesians 2 passage already in the sermon provides the structure — use it.
2. **Develop v. 14 with the tabernacle echo.** Add one focused paragraph on *eskēnōsen* — the glory of God that was once fenced off by sacrificial blood now walking in sandals through the marketplace. This deepens the Incarnation moment and builds the bridge to the cross that the sermon currently lacks.
3. **Redesign Section V with Perkins' uses pattern.** After the Ephesians 2 citation, address three categories explicitly: (a) *The unconvicted* — you have heard the Logos. You are still in the dark. That is not an intellectual position — it is a moral one. (b) *The believing but dormant* — you were made alive but are living like a corpse. Name the specific forms this takes (not just cursing and drinking, but self-protection, silence, comfort-seeking). (c) *The faithful* — here is the specific cost and joy of carrying light into dark places.
4. **Add the *tetelestai* correction.** The current gloss ("fullness at the end of a meal — Christ's ministry reached its fullness at the cross") is an unusual interpretation of John 1:16. *Tetelestai* belongs to John 19:30, not John 1:16. The "fullness" language here is *plērōma* — the inexhaustible abundance of Christ from which we continually receive. Reframe accordingly: not "the ministry is complete," but "from His inexhaustible supply, grace keeps coming."
5. **Sharpen the blind elephants payoff.** The illustration is used twice — once in the opening and once in Section IV — but the two uses are not cleanly differentiated. In the opening, the men feel the elephant flat (a false report). In the return, the blind men argue from partial truth to false conclusions. Clarify whether the central claim is *false knowledge* or *partial knowledge poorly applied*. Both are true of natural man; pick one or distinguish them clearly.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon does not exhibit the pattern of high exposition + low application that triggers dead orthodoxy. The exposition is strong but not exceptional. The application is thin, but the sermon has genuine pastoral warmth and is not bloodless. The flag does not apply.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The cosmological material carries genuine wonder — the 4.3 million hand-painted sunrises is the kind of image that lingers — but the sermon does not ultimately leave the congregation wanting to worship; it leaves them wanting to think more carefully and perhaps live slightly better.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
John 1:1–17 is theological narrative — a Gospel prologue with lyrical, almost hymnic qualities. The rubric fits well. The primary strain: this passage is as much a call to adoration as it is to application. The "Conscience & Applicatory Force" categories press toward Puritan-style uses that this text naturally evokes (wonder at the Incarnation, shame at the world's rejection, gratitude for the reception that makes sons of God), but those uses are affective and doxological as much as behavioral. The rubric should reward the evocation of wonder here as a legitimate form of application — the sermon partially earns that credit in its cosmological sections, but doesn't sustain it through to the Incarnation, where the wonder should peak.
