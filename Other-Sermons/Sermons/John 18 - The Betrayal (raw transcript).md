# The Betrayal — John 18:4–14
## with Luke 22:47–49 · Matthew 26:48–50
### Evening Service (raw transcript)

---

I love how the whole of scripture points towards Jesus. Luke recounts the same story we went through tonight, and adds an additional fact. The day Christ was betrayed was the feast of unleavened bread. There are lots of feasts and days of remembrance in jewish culture. There is always significance there, but I don't always bring them up.

The phrase unleavened bread should bring to mind a specific event in Israel's history. When did the Israelites have to eat unleavened bread? Anyone remember? That's right. After the final of the ten plagues in Egypt. They made unleavened bread so they would have food for the road as God led moses to lead the people out of bondage. The jewish freedom cost the Egyptians their first born son. So to remember God's deliverance of them from bondage, they instituted this feast of unleavened bread. Flat bread, bread made in a hurry. There are a lot of traditions surrounding it. So many kernels of grain must be brought for this, or that, and finally you added a number of grains on top of the other two offerings out of the joy of being free. But at the meal something kinda cool happens, and this is mostly the reason I bring it up. They have a flat bread holder — a matzah holder called a tash that has three compartments. A piece of bread goes in each. Then the bread from the middle compartment is pulled out, broken, hidden or buried, and put back after a period of time. The jews would argue that its symbolic of Abraham Isaac and Jacob. But wait, which of those was broken buried, and restored? To the careful student, you might remember that Joseph died in Egypt, and his bones were then later carried back to Israel. So that's a maybe… Less likely is the theory that the three represent priests, the levites, and the congregation. I don't think that makes as much sense as the first one. But then the people were taken, and put into bondage, then restored a few times… Possible. But the thing that makes the most sense is that the third piece of bread, the third matzah is symbolic of Jesus. But wait, didn't joseph also look like Christ? Wasn't his life used to point to christ? Likewise wasn't jesus our priest? Didn't he join our number and was then separated, broken, and returned to us? Absolutely. After the matzoh is unburied and returned, its broken into pieces and a piece given to each person present.

In the last supper, which happened shortly before our passage tonight, he says this: "This is my body which is given for you." He then refers to his body as bread. Elsewhere scripture calls him the bread of life. The other descriptions have elements of truth, but they too simply point towards Christ, who is the ultimate answer to everything. Christ was being pointed to by the tenth plague in Egypt. Christ — gods first born ultimately died so that his people could be free. Likewise, Christ is at the heart of the feast of unleavened bread. He is the middle matzoh. Jewish Tradition, and the old testament quite frequently point to Christ!

---

Alright. Back to tonight's text.

Right out of the gate John casually says something that has deep implications.

He says that Jesus knows all that would happen to him. That means he knew all along that Judas would betray him. He knew that his friend, and a man whom he poured three years of his life into, would sell him out with a kiss for about 20 grand in todays money. He knew about the false trials that awaited him. He knew they were trying to hire people to make false accusations against him. He knew that he would be scourged, mocked, beaten, and forced to carry his cross up hill. He knew what happen at the top of the hill, that his hands and feet would be nailed to the cross. He knew all these things, and the hardest thing I think was that he knew what it would be to have his father turn his face.

He could have called down 12 legions of angels, and put a stop to this right there. He could have done as the jews wanted and taken command of the army and ousted the romans, he could have done so many things. But in that and every moment Jesus chose obedience to the father.

I've said it before, but I'll say it again. It's a good thing I'm not God. In my flesh I probably would have just caused their molecules to cease to stick together. They would have evaporated instantly. Who wants to go through all that pain, and humiliation for people like me? God does.

John said Jesus knew ALL that was about to happen to him. Yet he chose obedience any way. This wasn't the example of the old testament saints. Remember Adam? Things didn't even have to be hard, and he chose to rebel against God breaking all of creation in the process. How about Abraham. Remember? Things got tough — and what did he do? Yeah… shes my sister, not my wife. Sure, take her. Marry her. I don't mind. I bet she was happy with him. When things got hard he crumbled. In that moment he had all the strength of wet bread. What about Jonah? He couldn't get past the idea of the mission, and ran away. Three days later God deposits him on the beach at Ninevah. Special delivery overnight express in the belly of a fish. We just talked about Gideon, he stood tall in the midst of the struggle, but when things calmed down he wanted the perks of being the big cheese without all the responsibility and people began to worship him and a thing he made instead of God! Even Gideon ultimately failed. Moses failed and disobeyed God at a critical point.

Don't we do the same though? Who of us could raise our hand and say they've never done anything that was disobedient to God? Of course we can't. We know from scripture that ALL have sinned, and fallen short of the glory of God.

That's one of the things that is so amazing about Christ. He knew what was in store for him, he knew the pain physical, emotional and spiritual that he would meet. He knew the weight and pull of temptation to disobey and yet he withstood it. Luke 22 says: And being in anguish, he prayed more earnestly, and his sweat was like drops of blood falling to the ground. He fully knew what was in store for him, and when the rubber met the road, what did Christ choose? He chose to obey.

I don't think the disciples at this point were fully aware of what was coming. Christ had told some of them including Judas that he would be betrayed. He was cryptic about what that would mean. I'll be here, then I won't for a time, and then I'll be back, and then I'll go somewhere that you can't follow me yet.

Notice too that there was an authority in Jesus that the souls of these men instinctively recognized. Look at vs 6. In the greek Jesus simply says "I am". He is an extra word added by the translators to help it make sense in english. But I think it dampens the impact here. All throughout the old testament what did God call himself? The I AM is definitely one of the names God uses to refer to himself, so when Jesus says that very same formulation of words that God used to describe himself, the soldiers fell down. Some commentators say they all fell on their face in worship. Scripture doesn't say that. But its possible.

There is no earthly power that can speak with such authority that men fall prostrate at the sound of his voice. But read with me what happens next vs 10.

So then Peter decides to take things into his own hands vs 10. He used his sword to chop off the ear of one of the men. He was ready to give his life in service of Christ in that moment. But he still didn't really understand. It strikes me that within just a few hours Peter who likely had little or no sword training would have fought to the death despite overwhelming odds against many well trained swords man for christ. But a few short hours later he lies to a little girl to avoid being known as Christ's associate. Even Peter failed.

Ultimately though I think Judas actions would have been hardest for me to swallow so to speak. Judas was one of the 12. He had been called by Christ himself. Mentored by Christ. He lived each day with christ shared a thousand meals with christ. Those of you who work in ministry — I think this is a point of encouragement.

When I was working with teenagers it always seemed like there was one who just didn't get it. No matter how much time and effort you poured into them, they still were lost as anything. They refused to believe in christ, but kept coming so you keep trying. Judas seems to be that kid. While God is capable of over-riding our free will (remember Pharaoh and Moses? 5 times it says and God hardened pharaohs heart) it doesn't seem to be a thing he does with regularity. Jesus being God could have done the same, but he didn't. Judas chose to continue living with Christ, but in rebellion.

Know anyone like that? People that know the right thing to do, but yet do not do it? I think we have ALL been there. Jesus was patient, even knowing what Judas would do to him he did not reject Judas.

To be fair Judas had some unholy help. It says in scripture that Satan entered into Judas. That is how we know that at least to the point where he betrayed Christ that Judas was lost, and not yet a Christian. We know from other passages of scripture that true Christians can't be possessed by a demon, or the devil. So for him to be possessed means to this point he wasn't really saved yet.

There is a lot of truth in that for those of us living actively in rebellion against God...

Its sad to me the method in which Judas chose to betray Christ. A kiss in that culture would be symbolic of friendship. It was reflective of the depth of friendship and intimacy that comes with living together for so long. You don't expect kisses from enemies, you get kisses from those you love.

A quote I've heard a number of times, that has been attributed to a number of people is that friends are the family you choose. For christ, this was just as true then. He chose these men, he poured his life into them, he lived with them for years. They were the start of the earthly family he chose. So when he was betrayed, even knowing it was coming can you imagine the pain that would have caused Christ?

Imagine if one of your kids came up to you, kissed you on the cheek, and then turned you over to the authorities for something you didn't do, but were found guilty of and executed.

Or for you golfers, imagine if mel came up, hugged you, kissed you on the cheek, so terrorists could come take you away. Missy — imagine if it were kurt.

I know neither kurt nor mel would ever do anything to intentionally hurt someone. But think about the emotional impact of what that betrayal would be like and delivered with a kiss.

Now I want you to think about something else. If we had thought about things in these terms, I think many people would live their lives in a different way.

Those of us that are believers, that go to church, that "live for christ", that sing his praises on Saturday, but go back to embracing the sin of the world on Monday are no better than Judas.

Remember that hymn, it was my sin that held him there, until it was accomplished? We are just as guilty, and responsible for the death of Christ as was Judas. Each and every one of us hold a stake in this the greatest shame of humanity. Each time we curse, or have sex outside of marriage, requires those nails to have been driven into his hands. Each time we are disobedient to God, it requires that Christ's feet were nailed to the cross. Each time every time we sin, it requires that God send his son to hang on that cross until he died.

Each and every one of us in that way then have kissed jesus on the cheek while stabbing him in the back.

You did that. I did that. We do that every day. As the praise team comes up, I want you to think about something.

When things are hard. How do you react? Do you do the things you want? Do you do what sounds good, or looks good or feels good in your own eyes, or do you follow christs example? What about when things are good. That's when Gideon really failed. When things are good how do you react? Do you do the things that you want? Do you do what sounds or looks or feels good in your own eyes? Or do you follow the example of Christ.

The only relief we can find from our sin is in the arms of Christ.

## Corrections Log

**IMPORTANT: Review before reuse. Items marked ⚠️ need correction or verification.**

### Scripture
1. **John 18:4–14 — ESV confirmed.** Text matches ESV.
2. **Luke 22:47–49 — ESV confirmed.** Text matches ESV.
3. **Matthew 26:48–50 — ESV confirmed.** Text matches ESV.
4. **John 18:5–6 *egō eimi* — accurate.** The Greek reads ἐγώ εἰμι ("I am") with no object pronoun. "He" is supplied by English translators. The ESV has a footnote acknowledging this. The connection to Exodus 3:14 is legitimate, though *egō eimi* is also used in non-divine contexts (Jn 4:26, 9:9). The soldiers falling back (v.6) is in the text.
5. **Luke 22:44 — ESV confirmed but textual note required.** The ESV includes vv.43–44 in the main text but footnotes: "Some manuscripts omit verses 43 and 44." These verses are absent from P75, Codex Vaticanus, and the original hand of Codex Sinaiticus. The NA28 prints them in double brackets. Patristic attestation (Justin Martyr, Irenaeus) predates the earliest manuscripts. Scholarly debate ongoing — not an error to cite but worth knowing.
6. **Matthew 26:53 — 12 legions of angels confirmed.** A Roman legion ≈ 6,000 soldiers, so 72,000+ angels.
7. **Satan entered Judas — confirmed.** Luke 22:3 and John 13:27. John 13:2 provides a prior stage (the devil putting it into Judas's heart).
8. **Feast of Unleavened Bread — confirmed.** Luke 22:1, 7. By the 1st century, "Passover" and "Feast of Unleavened Bread" were used interchangeably for the entire eight-day celebration.

### Hymn
9. **"It was my sin that held him there, until it was accomplished"** — from "How Deep the Father's Love for Us" by Stuart Townend (1995, Thankyou Music). Lyric wording matches exactly.

### Matzah Tash and Afikomen
10. ⚠️ **Matzah tash tradition does not predate Christ.** The three-compartment matzah holder is real and used in modern Passover seders, but it is a post-Temple development — medieval at earliest in its current form. The Mishnah and Talmud do not mention a three-pocket bag. If reusing, note that this is a modern Jewish tradition with Christian typological significance, not a tradition Jesus would have observed at the Last Supper.
11. ⚠️ **"Buried" is a Christological overlay.** The Jewish tradition says the afikomen is "hidden" (*tzafun*), not "buried." The language of burial is a Christian interpretive addition. The afikomen itself is a post-Temple substitute for the Passover sacrifice (Talmud, Pesachim 119b).
12. **Abraham/Isaac/Jacob and Kohen/Levi/Yisrael — both genuine Jewish traditions.** Both explanations for the three compartments are attested in rabbinic sources (though neither appears in the Mishnah or Talmud — they are later innovations). Additional Jewish explanation: the three measures of flour Abraham told Sarah to bake (Gen 18:6).
13. **Joseph's bones — correct.** Gen 50:25, Ex 13:19, Josh 24:32. Joseph as a type of Christ is a legitimate typological reading.

### Historical/Factual Claims
14. ⚠️ **"About 20 grand in today's money" — overestimate.** 30 pieces of silver (likely Tyrian shekels) = ~120 denarii = ~4 months' wages for a laborer. Most scholarly estimates: **$12,000–$16,000** in purchasing power. The theologically significant point is that 30 shekels was the price of a slave (Ex 21:32) and an allusion to Zechariah 11:12–13. Consider revising to "about $15,000" or "a few months' wages."
15. ⚠️ **Pharaoh's heart hardened "5 times" — incorrect count.** God explicitly hardens Pharaoh's heart in 6 narrative instances (Ex 9:12; 10:1, 20, 27; 11:10; 14:8) plus 3 predictive statements (Ex 4:21; 7:3; 14:4) = 9–10 total. The "5" may confuse with the observation that for the first 5 plagues, Pharaoh hardens his own heart, and only from the 6th plague onward does God harden it. Either correct to the actual count or reframe as "multiple times."

### Theological Claims
16. ⚠️ **"True Christians can't be possessed by a demon" — majority evangelical view but debated.** This is the dominant Reformed/Baptist position (based on 1 Jn 4:4, Col 1:13, Eph 1:13–14), but scholars like Merrill Unger (Dallas Theological Seminary) reversed his earlier position after missionary reports. The Greek *daimonizomai* means "to be demonized," not necessarily "to be owned." Pentecostal/Charismatic traditions practice deliverance ministry on believers. Present as a theological conviction rather than undisputed fact if reusing.

### Quotes and Attributions
17. **"Friends are the family you choose" — floating quotation, no reliable attribution.** Most commonly attributed to Edna Buchanan (Pulitzer-winning journalist) but not well-sourced. The sermon acknowledges the uncertain attribution ("attributed to a number of people"), which is honest and sufficient.
