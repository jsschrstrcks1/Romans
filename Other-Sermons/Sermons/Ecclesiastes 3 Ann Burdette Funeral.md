## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Warmth Carrying a Genuine Gospel, Loosely Tethered to Its Text**

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warmhearted funeral address that accomplishes its evangelistic mission with real gospel content and personal pastoral credibility, while using Ecclesiastes 3 as a launching pad rather than a governing text.

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: FAIL (qualified)**
The stated main idea — "there are at least 3 seasons or times we need to talk about today: Granny’s time, our time, and God’s time" — is loosely borrowed from Ecclesiastes 3’s language of "times and seasons" but does not arise from the passage’s actual argument. Qohelet’s point in 3:1–8 is the sovereignty of God over all times, the limits of human control, and the resulting posture of trust and fear before the One who orders the seasons. The sermon uses the word "seasons" but does not exposit the text’s argument at all. No verse is quoted, explained, or applied from the assigned passage. The "three seasons" frame is a homiletical device imposed on the text, not derived from it.

**Authority Test: PASS (partial)**
The preacher does not soften or edit the gospel. Where he engages Scripture directly — Romans 3:23, Romans 6:23, Ephesians 2:8–9, Romans 10:9–10 — he handles it faithfully and without embarrassment. He does not stand above those texts. He does stand above Ecclesiastes 3, treating it as a title card rather than as a word from God.

**Gate Decision: The sermon passes the gate on a pastoral mercy ruling.** This is a funeral address in a non-church setting. The text is announced but not exposited. The gate technically fails on the Scopus Test. However, the sermon’s real governing text is the cluster of gospel passages (Romans 3, 6, 10; Ephesians 2), and those are handled with fidelity. The evaluation proceeds with the fatal flaw noted and the score reflecting the non-exposition of the announced text.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**No fatal flaws triggered.** The gospel is present and clear. Christ is necessary. Application is specific (though thin). Exhortation is present and explicit. The sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers — this is its signal strength.

*Note: The failure to exposit Ecclesiastes 3 is a significant hermeneutical weakness but does not trigger a fatal flaw flag because the sermon’s gospel argument is carried by other Scripture, faithfully quoted and applied.*

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 10/25**
- Textual Fidelity: 3/10. Ecclesiastes 3:1–8 is announced and never exposited. Not a single verse is read aloud, quoted, or explained. The "seasons" motif is borrowed in name only. The Romans and Ephesians texts are handled with basic fidelity.
- Contextual Awareness: 2/5. No engagement with Qohelet’s argument, the book’s structure, or what "a time for every matter" means in its canonical context.
- Exegetical Clarity: 2/5. The gospel passages are explained in plain terms — this is genuine. But no exegetical work is done on the announced text.
- Logical Faithfulness: 3/5. The sermon is internally consistent and does not import foreign ideas into the texts it actually uses. The Jeremiah 29:11 reference is used without its context (exile address to Israel), which is a minor eisegetical concern.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**
- Christ-Centeredness: 5/7. Christ is central and necessary. His death, burial, resurrection, and exclusive saving role are all present. The resurrection is asserted with historical witness (500+). Not forced — it is the natural destination of the whole message.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3. No redemptive-historical placement of Ecclesiastes 3. No awareness of where this passage sits in the canon. The gospel passages are used atomistically rather than within the story of redemption.
- Doctrinal Precision: 4/4. Justification language is grace-centered. "Nothing we can do on our own" is clear. The sinner’s prayer framing carries weaknesses (see below), but the theological content leading to it is sound.
- Confessional Alignment: 3/4. No contradiction of confessional theology. The doctrines of sin and grace are handled with basic accuracy. The sinner’s prayer invitation introduces decisionism concerns, but the sermon does not assert that the prayer itself saves — it directs to trust in Christ.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 11/15**
- Clarity of the Gospel: 4/5. Sin is named concretely (commandments violated, specific categories: blasphemer, thief, murderer-in-heart). The cross is preached. The resurrection is asserted. Romans 10:9–10 is given as the response. Justification by faith is stated, not just implied.
- Necessity of Christ: 5/5. Christ is not decorative. The sermon builds to His cross as the only solution. "There is nothing we can do about it on our own" is stated twice. The Synagogue Test is failed — a devout non-Christian would be confronted by the exclusivity of Christ.
- Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5. The cross is present but not dwelt upon. The penal logic is there (we deserved death, He died in our place) but not developed with weight. The description of crucifixion is deliberately restrained ("I won’t get too graphic"), which is appropriate for context, but results in the cross receiving less gravity than the heavenly description earlier.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9/15**
- Heart Penetration: 3/5. The commandments section is genuinely penetrating — the escalating taxonomy (blasphemer → thief → murderer) is a real conscience-searching device. It is not deep Puritan surgery, but it lands. The funeral context limits the range of what’s appropriate.
- Specificity: 3/5. The application is specific in two ways: (1) the commandments are concrete violations most hearers have committed, and (2) the invitation is public and actionable. Less specific: "trust God more" level comfort sections lack grounding.
- Spiritual Differentiation: 3/5. The sermon addresses the unbeliever directly (the law section and invitation). It briefly addresses the grieving believer ("death is not the end"). It does not address the backslider, the churchgoing-but-unconverted, or the person who has prayed such prayers before without genuine faith. The believer/unbeliever binary is the only differentiation.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 6/8**
- Call to Response: 4/4. The call is explicit, public, and specific. The preacher asks for hands, leads a prayer, and gives clear next steps (find a church, talk to family, talk to me). This is genuine exhortation — it summons a response and names what that response looks like.
- Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 2/4. The post-decision counsel (find a church, tell your family) is basic discipleship. It does not equip the grieving believer for ongoing witness or faithful endurance. Ann’s example of faithfulness is mentioned but not applied as a model for the living.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**
- Clarity of Outline: 3/4. The three-seasons structure (Granny’s time, Our time, God’s time) is memorable and traceable. A congregant could follow it.
- Progression: 2/3. The sermon progresses from comfort → grief → gospel → invitation. This is a natural and effective funeral sequence. The Jeremiah 29:11 detour and the extended "God is good" section slow the momentum before the commandments section.
- Unity: 1/2. The dominant idea is ultimately "Ann is with Jesus and you can be too," but the sermon develops several competing sub-themes (heaven’s description, God’s goodness, sin’s categories) without a single unifying thesis anchored to the announced text.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**
- Relevance: 2/2. The bulk candy illustration, the cookie jar, the little nieces trying blasphemy — these are native to a Southern family congregation and land naturally.
- Clarity: 1/2. The "(visual gospel)" notation references a physical prop not described in the manuscript. What the visual communicates is assumed but not articulated.
- Memorability: 0/1. No single image or phrase is sharp enough to be carried home. Ann’s deathbed declaration ("I’ve been waiting to go there and be with Jesus for a long time — I’m ready!") is the sermon’s most memorable moment, but it is not leveraged as a rhetorical anchor.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**
- Reads as Preaching: 2/2. This reads as spoken address, not essay. Direct address is present throughout. Sentence rhythm is conversational and oral.
- Conviction on the Page: 1/2. The commandments section carries conviction. The comfort sections read more as reassurance than proclamation. Filler phrases ("I think," "I imagine," "I’m guessing") reduce the assertive force.
- Cadence and Breath: 0/1. No climactic compression. The sermon reaches its emotional peak early (Ann’s declaration) and then proceeds through gospel content without building to a final crescendo.

### 6. Cognitive Load Modifier: 0

This sermon is maximally accessible. A teenager, a grieving non-churchgoer, and an unchurched family member could follow every movement. No deduction warranted.

**Interpretation: Concerning gaps.** The sermon explains and comforts and invites — it does not yet fully preach. The primary gap is the announced text, which is never opened. The score reflects a sermon that accomplishes its evangelistic purpose but fails its expository calling.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily Christ. Ann’s story is used to magnify Christ’s faithfulness, not to celebrate human achievement. Positive.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Partially. The commandments section produces genuine encounter. The comfort sections remain in the register of ideas about God’s goodness rather than confrontation with God Himself.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Both comforted and convicted — unevenly. The convicted unbeliever has a clear path. The grieving believer receives comfort. The comfortable churchgoer is not disturbed.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Ann’s deathbed declaration as the sermon’s load-bearing pastoral moment.** "I’ve been waiting to go there and be with Jesus for a long time — I’m ready!" — this is not a rhetorical device. It is a real pastoral encounter wielded with genuine effect. The entire sermon’s credibility rests on this moment, and it earns that weight.

2. **The commandments sequence is genuine conscience-searching work.** The escalating taxonomy — blasphemer, disobedient, thief, murderer-in-heart — is specific, concrete, and includes the preacher himself ("these things are just as true of me as they are of you"). This is exactly how law is supposed to work before grace is offered.

3. **The gospel invitation is explicit, public, and pastorally complete.** The preacher names what is being responded to, leads the prayer, asks for a public indicator, gives next steps, and provides pastoral availability. This is not a vague "if you want to know more" close — it is a full summons.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Ecclesiastes 3 is never opened.** The passage is announced, a chapter and verse reference is given, and then it disappears. Not one verse is read. The phrase "a time for every season" is implied but never quoted. The sermon’s organizing concept ("three seasons") is borrowed terminology, not expository structure. This is not a minor gap — the congregation was told to open their Bibles and then had no reason to look at the page.

2. **The comfort section ("God’s goodness") is theologically diluted and structurally disruptive.** The extended passage on God’s goodness (beginning "Firstly, even in the midst of death, God has good plans for us...") meanders through general theism before arriving at the law section. Jeremiah 29:11 is used without its context (a promise to exiles, not a general life-plan promise). The section delays the sermon’s real work and its theological weight is soft.

3. **The sinner’s prayer mechanism carries unexamined decisionism.** The prayer is theologically accurate in content, but the framing ("if you prayed that prayer for the first time and meant it") locates assurance in the act of praying rather than in Christ Himself. A funeral congregation containing nominal churchgoers who have prayed similar prayers before without regeneration is given no tool to distinguish genuine faith from repeated religious performance.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that his most powerful expository material is the gospel passages (Romans 3, 6, 10; Ephesians 2) — not Ecclesiastes 3 — and that his sermon would be more honest and more powerful if it were structured around those texts with Ecclesiastes 3 as supporting texture rather than the announced primary passage. The announced text creates an expectation that is never fulfilled, and the congregation — especially any who opened their Bibles — experienced a small, unacknowledged mismatch.

The preacher also likely does not see how much pastoral and rhetorical capital Ann’s final declaration carries. It is the sermon’s most powerful moment and is placed at the beginning, then not returned to. It should be the sermon’s capstone, not its opening.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The three-seasons structure feels organized and memorable, and it is — but the organization is imposed on the text rather than derived from it. The structure creates the *impression* of expository coherence (as if the sermon is unpacking three aspects of Ecclesiastes 3) when it is actually a topical outline using borrowed vocabulary. The congregation likely felt the sermon was "from Ecclesiastes 3" when it was not.

Similarly, the extended description of heaven (gold streets, pearl gates, new bodies) feels comforting and textually grounded, but it functions as emotional scene-setting rather than as expository declaration. It is preacher-driven imagination dressed in biblical language.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Ecclesiastes 3:1–8 itself contains the sermon’s real power for a funeral: *"a time to be born, and a time to die."* Qohelet’s point is that God governs these times — not us. Ann did not choose the hour of her death; God did. And He ordained it as surely as He ordained her birth. The text drives the congregation to the God who holds the times, not to sentiment about heaven. The preacher circled this truth but never entered it. A single paragraph anchoring the sermon’s comfort in God’s sovereign governance of "the time to die" would have transformed the sermon’s authority.

The deathbed declaration ("I’m ready!") is also a missed typological opportunity — it echoes Simeon’s *Nunc Dimittis* (Luke 2:29: "Lord, now you are letting your servant depart in peace"). That connection would have given Ann’s words canonical weight without being artificial.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Read and exposit at least one verse from Ecclesiastes 3.** Begin: *"A time to be born and a time to die" — Qohelet tells us that God holds both times in His hands. Ann was born in His time. She died in His time. And she was ready, because she knew the God who holds the times.* Then proceed. This requires 60 seconds and transforms the sermon’s expository integrity.

2. **Move Ann’s declaration to the conclusion.** Open with the text. Let law and gospel do their work. Then return to Ann: "This is what it sounds like when a soul is ready. She said, ‘I’ve been waiting to go there and be with Jesus for a long time — I’m ready.’ You can be ready too. Today."

3. **Cut or compress the "God is good" section.** Replace the Jeremiah 29:11 passage with a direct movement from Ann’s story into the law section. The current transition is slow. The conscience-searching commandments section is the sermon’s real second movement — let it arrive sooner.

4. **Reframe the sinner’s prayer invitation to anchor assurance in Christ, not the prayer.** Add: "If you prayed that and meant it — not just the words, but the trust — then your confidence is not in a prayer you said. Your confidence is in a Savior who died and rose. He is your assurance, not this moment."

5. **Close with Ann’s declaration as the sermon’s final image.** The congregation should leave with her words in their ears: *"I’m ready."* Ask: "Are you?"

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** This sermon has warmth, pastoral credibility, and genuine evangelistic force. It is not bloodless. The expositor is personally present in the material, not hiding behind abstraction.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The eternal gravity is present in seed form — Ann’s readiness for death is a genuine witness to the hope of the resurrection — but the sermon does not build to a moment of transcendence; it disperses into invitation mechanics before the weight fully lands.

The rubric score of 64 reflects real weaknesses. The pastoral outcome may exceed what the rubric can measure.
