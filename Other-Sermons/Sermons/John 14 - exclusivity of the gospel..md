### 3. Berean Gate: CONDITIONAL PASS / SOFT FAIL

**Scopus Test:** The sermon's controlling thesis — there is only one way to God and it is Jesus Christ alone, therefore we must be urgent about the gospel — is defensible as an implication of John 14:6. However, the sermon does not exposit John 14:6; it uses it as a proof text within a topical argument about the exclusivity of the gospel and the urgency of missions. The sermon's main point is related to the text's content but does not arise from the text's own argument.

**Authority Test:** The preacher does not soften the text's exclusivism — he amplifies it. He submits to the exclusivity claim fully and refuses to hedge it. The authority test passes.

**Ruling:** Soft fail on the Scopus Test. The sermon's thesis is true, and it is consistent with the text. But the text's main point (Christ going to prepare a place; "I am the way" as comfort to troubled disciples on the eve of Gethsemane) is not what drives the sermon. The sermon is a biblical-theological argument for gospel exclusivity that borrows John 14:6 as its anchor verse. This is preaching from the Bible, but it is not yet expository preaching of this passage.

It is disproportionate in length for its contribution. The three paragraphs spent on Miranda could be redirected to explaining what Christ actually did on the cross. (Location: The Miranda section, approximately one-fifth of the sermon's body.)

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher is likely unaware that John 14:6 — when read in context — is not primarily a polemical text but a comfort text. Jesus says "I am the way" to disciples who are afraid, confused, and about to watch their Lord be arrested and killed. The exclusivity claim is real and must be preached. But it was spoken first to a group of frightened men who needed to know that the only way was a way Jesus Himself was going to make. This pastoral context, if entered, would make the gospel proclamation much more powerful — not weaker. The preacher's apologetic instinct led him past the text's heart.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The MacArthur extended quotation *feels* like the sermon's theological peak — it is long, authoritative, and covers important ground (shallow churches, unskilled pastors, synthetic gospels, the Reformation). But it is doing the sermon's work for it. The most theologically dense moment in the sermon is borrowed. The surrounding material does not match its depth. The quotation gives an impression of doctrinal seriousness that the rest of the sermon's structure does not fully support.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

John 14:6 occurs within a passage that contains one of the most humanly vulnerable moments in the Gospel: Thomas saying, "Lord, we do not know where you are going. How can we know the way?" (v. 5). Thomas is not mocking. He is genuinely lost. He believes Jesus is the Messiah and still does not understand. This is a portrait of the struggling, sincere, confused disciple — exactly the person sitting in most evangelical churches. The sermon never enters this scene. A sermon that opened with Thomas's honest confusion, showed how Jesus answers it not with a syllogism but with a Person ("I am the way"), and then pressed outward to the exclusivity implications — would be stronger, more compassionate toward the seeker, and still fully orthodox.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Anchor the exclusivity argument in the text's pastoral context.** Spend three minutes on v. 1 ("Let not your hearts be troubled") and v. 5 (Thomas's question). Show that the "I am the way" claim is first a comfort to confused disciples before it becomes a polemic against false religion. This makes the message warmer and not weaker.
2. **Explain the atonement explicitly before the invitation.** Between the MacArthur quotation and Romans 10:9, add one paragraph: "Why is Christ the only way? Because sin has an infinite cost that only an infinite substitute can pay. God became man, lived the life we couldn't live, died the death we deserved, and rose again — so that everyone who trusts in Him alone receives His righteousness credited to their account. That is why He is the only way. There is no other payment."
3. **Cut or sharply compress the Miranda section.** One sentence: "Not every claim to be Jesus is Jesus — and Scripture gives us the test." Move on. Redirect those paragraphs to the cross.
4. **Tighten the political idolatry section into a single pointed application.** It is the strongest applicatory moment — sharpen it to three paragraphs and then return to the text's invitation.
5. **Differentiate the evangelistic address.** The invitation addresses those who have never confessed Christ. Add a sentence to the backslider: "And some of you confessed Christ years ago but have made politics your actual lord. Repent of that today — not just to prioritize missions, but because you have made a lesser thing your greater love." This reaches a category the sermon currently misses.
6. **Close on the cross, not on urgency.** The sermon's final note is the scale of lostness (3 billion) driving Christians to action. This is good but incomplete. End on what Christ has done, not on what we must do — let the gospel's indicative carry the imperative.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon has genuine evangelical heat and pastoral urgency. The application is real if underdeveloped. This is not a cold, correct sermon — it is a warm, slightly underpowered one.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The 3 billion / 9-11 comparison carries real eternal gravity, and the final invitation is spoken with genuine pastoral weight — this sermon clearly comes from a man who believes in Hell and loves people enough to warn them.

+2/5

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

John 14 is within a Farewell Discourse — a highly pastoral, intimate, narrative-theological genre unique to the Gospel of John. The rubric's categories fit well; no strain. However, the rubric's emphasis on the FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) points specifically to v. 1 — "Let not your hearts be troubled" — as the text's own diagnosis of the human condition: fear and confusion about where we are going and how to get there. A sermon that named this FCF explicitly (we do not know the way; we are afraid we will be lost) before preaching the gospel answer would be more fully compliant with the rubric's Chapell-derived exposition standard. The rubric is well-suited to this text; the sermon simply did not use the tools the rubric describes.
