INTRODUCTION: WHAT ARE WE THANKFUL FOR?

Thanksgiving is one of the better holidays — right up there with Christmas and Easter.

In listening to people preach this week it struck the pastor that one of the purposes of mankind is
to give thanksgiving to God. We ought to be thankful for our very existence. God didn't have to
create us. Had he not decided to, we would never have had the opportunity to see a pretty sunrise or
sunset. We'd never have had the opportunity to worship God, to choose. We never would have even
come into being.

Further, we should be thankful for his continued patience with us. God created us perfect, in a
perfect world [Note: Genesis 1:31, ESV: "And God saw everything that he had made, and behold, it
was very good." This is a standard theological reading; accurate.], and as soon as he asked us not
to do something, we ran right over and messed things up — just like any kid told not to take a
cookie from the jar. At that moment, he would have been just in obliterating mankind and starting
fresh. What's a week's work to a God of infinite time? He could have made us differently so we
didn't screw up so badly so quickly, but he chose to continue working with us — even though we had
taken a sledgehammer to his perfect creation, shattering his reflection that he had so painstakingly
imbued everything with.

[Illustration: You ever spend a bunch of time working on something, trying to make something nice
and special, only to have a kid come along and trash it before you had a chance to gift it or put it
on display? That's basically what we did to God.]

He would have been within his rights to destroy us. Instead, he died for us. For this we should be
thankful.

We should be thankful for every nail hammered into the hands of Christ, thankful for the lashes
Christ endured, thankful for Christ's substitutionary atonement for our sins.

[Note on "39 lashes from the cat of nine tails": The pastor refers to 39 lashes. The "39 lashes"
figure is derived from Jewish flogging practice — Deuteronomy 25:3 capped flogging at 40 stripes,
and Jewish tradition administered 39 to avoid accidentally exceeding the limit. Paul records this
practice in 2 Corinthians 11:24: "Five times I received at the hands of the Jews the forty lashes
less one." However, Jesus was flogged by Romans, who were not bound by Jewish law and had no
prescribed limit. The Gospels record the flogging (Matthew 27:26; Mark 15:15; John 19:1) but give
no number of lashes. The "39 lashes for Jesus" figure is a popular preaching tradition without
Scriptural or historical basis. Similarly, "cat of nine tails" is a term from 17th-century British
naval practice; the Roman instrument was a flagellum — leather straps studded with bone, metal, or
glass. Both terms should be used with caution in formal settings, though they are common in
evangelical preaching to convey the severity of the flogging.]

He has done so much for us, we owe him so much gratitude and thanks. And even then he did not stop.

CREATION AND HEAVEN

Earth took six days to complete [Note: The pastor says "7 days." Genesis 1-2 describes six days of
creative work and one day of rest (Genesis 2:2-3). The work of creation was accomplished in six
days; the seventh was the day of rest. "Six days" would be more precise.] — earth, our solar
system, our universe, our galaxy, and there are millions of galaxies out there. Every scenic vista,
every sunset, every mountain, all implicitly or explicitly created that first week.

God has been working on heaven, as far as we know, ever since. Earth is pretty amazing. Just imagine
what our God could create with a year. Or ten years. Now ten thousand years. What a place heaven
will be. What a wonderful God we serve.

THE TEXT: EPHESIANS 5:4 AND PAUL ON THANKSGIVING

When Paul commands believers to stop sinning, he also commands them to put thanksgiving in its
place:

    "Let there be no filthiness nor foolish talk nor crude joking, which are out of place, but
    instead let there be thanksgiving."
    — Ephesians 5:4, ESV [Verified: matches ESV exactly.]

Paul knows that we are always acting. We either act with sinful, thankless hearts — which leads to
foolish talk or crude jokes — or we act with thankful hearts and thereby please God with our speech.

The pastor cited John Piper from an article titled "Violence, Ugliness, and Thanksgiving" to the
effect that the key to unlocking a heart of gratitude is a strong belief in God as Creator,
Sustainer, Provider, and Hope-giver, and that without a deep sense of indebtedness to God the
spring of gratitude runs dry. [Note: The article title is plausible for a Desiring God piece and
the substance matches Piper's teaching on gratitude. The exact wording of the quote could not be
independently verified; the quote has been summarized rather than reproduced to avoid
misattribution.]

PASTORAL APPLICATION: THANKSGIVING REPLACING BITTERNESS

The pastor noted that a counseling professor in a class he is currently taking observed: any time we
are engaged in bitterness, ugliness, disrespect, or violence, we have stolen worship from God and
are worshipping how a thing makes us feel. We have supplanted God on his throne and placed our
backside where it should never go.

So when you find yourself feeling sorry for yourself, bitter, or angry — not righteous anger, but
self-centered anger — it is time to pray and ask God to help you replace those feelings with
thanksgiving.

[Personal illustration: The pastor described a person he knows whose apparent spiritual gift is
condescension. After an incident of condescension that week, he wrongly allowed it to keep him
angry for two days — not throwing-things angry, but enough to cloud his outlook and taint his
interactions. It was not until the second evening, sitting down with Scripture as part of the
church's read-through-the-Bible-in-a-year initiative, that he began to re-adjust his focus. He
needed to look to the Lord rather than to himself. He needed to be thankful to God for allowing
this person to be in his life — for their rough edges chipping away at his. In the language of his
counseling professor: sometimes my idol is me. That person's rudeness helped him smash an idol and
replace it with thanksgiving.]
