CORRECTIONS APPLIED / OUTSTANDING ITEMS

1. CORRECTED — "Columbian prison" → "Colombian prison." Colombia is in South America;
   "Columbian" is not the correct demonym.

2. NOTE — Greek tense distinction (Romans 6:1 vs. 6:15): The pastor's point is legitimate but
   the explanation slightly imprecise. He says "the word for Sin is in a different tense."
   What actually differs is the verbal construction:
   - Romans 6:1: "ἐπιμένωμεν τῇ ἁμαρτίᾳ" — shall we *continue in* sin (ἐπιμένωμεν = present
     subjunctive of ἐπιμένω, to persist/remain; ἁμαρτίᾳ is a noun, dative case). The
     continuous/habitual sense is carried by ἐπιμένωμεν, not by "the word for sin."
   - Romans 6:15: "ἁμαρτήσωμεν" — shall we sin (aorist subjunctive of ἁμαρτάνω). The aorist
     can indicate a discrete or punctiliar act, which supports the "occasional" reading.
   The interpretive observation is sound; the grammatical explanation should be corrected if
   this material is used in a teaching context: it is the verb form, not "the word for sin,"
   that differs between the two verses.

3. NOTE — Great Pumpkin quote: The pastor attributes "It does not matter what you believe, as
   long as you are sincere" to Linus in "It's the Great Pumpkin, Charlie Brown" (1966). This
   is a paraphrase/summary of Linus's worldview, not a direct quotation. Linus's actual
   emphasis is on the sincerity of the pumpkin patch (e.g., "It must be the most sincere
   pumpkin patch..."), not a general philosophical claim about belief being irrelevant. The
   rhetorical point the pastor is making (sincere feelings are not enough) is valid; just do
   not present this as a direct quote from the special.

4. VERIFIED — 2 Corinthians 5:17 (ESV): "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new
   creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come." Sermon renders the verse
   accurately; citation is correct.

5. VERIFIED — Hebrews 13:5 (ESV): "Keep your life free from love of money, and be content
   with what you have, for he has said, 'I will never leave you nor forsake you.'" Sermon
   quotes accurately and cites correctly.

6. MINOR — "cat of 9 tails" → standard rendering is "cat-o'-nine-tails."

7. MINOR — Bee/orchid illustration: the botanical mechanism is slightly imprecise. Orchids do
   produce pollen; approximately one-third of orchid species are nectarless (offering no
   reward to pollinators). Bee orchids (Ophrys) specifically mimic female bees to attract
   male bees — the bee leaves without a mating opportunity, not because there is no pollen.
   The rhetorical point ("looks promising, delivers nothing") is effective and stands; the
   specific claim that the bee "can't find pollen" is the inaccurate element. Minor enough
   that it does not need correction in re-preaching, but worth noting if used in a
   teaching context.

8. NOTE — "Locked Up Abroad" (National Geographic Channel, later rebranded "Banged Up Abroad"):
   the show is real. The pastor's story is a composite/illustrative account, not a claim about
   a specific episode. Fine as illustration.

9. NOTE — Personal names used: Lilly (pastor's granddaughter), Dottie (prayer-chain contact),
   Bob (pastor, probably at Jasmine church). All personal illustrations — no verification
   needed, but retain as context clues for dating/congregation identification.

10. NOTE — Two duplicated passages in original (dictation artifacts); de-duplicated below:
    - The closing appeal ("Maybe you're here...") — five paragraphs, each duplicated.

TRANSCRIPT (clean — duplicates removed, corrections applied per log above)

INTRODUCTION

Romans 6:15 — Can we sin occasionally?

Romans 6:1 talks about sin — I didn't bring it up at the time, but I'm telling you now that
the word for sin in that verse in the Greek means sin that we return to over and over and over.
It's something we start and continue in until the point where Christ saves you from it.

Tonight, in verse 15 we see the word sin again, but in the Greek it's in a different tense, and
what this second repetition does is not only add its weight to the first verse, but it is also
talking about those things we do occasionally. I've mentioned I sometimes speed before. It's
something I try hard not to do, but you get behind that wheel, and spend an hour on the road,
eventually you aren't as attentive to that little white needle and what it's telling you to do.
In that moment, sometimes you stray into speeding.

[NOTE: See correction log item 2 on the Greek. The distinction between 6:1 and 6:15 is real
but rests on the verb forms, not on "the word for sin" itself.]

But this could be any sin that you only do occasionally — this time instead of talking about
addictions or other sins you do over and over on a daily basis, this time it's things that only
come up every so often, or you might call them "one-off" sins.

ILLUSTRATION: GOSSIP IN THE CHURCH

Like that time you get caught up in a conversation and are drawn into gossiping. That's easy to
do, and happens often in a church — "hey friends, we need to pray for so-and-so, they are
caught up in such and such." Was it your news to tell? Was it your business? Better to just say
there is a need for prayer and keep them in your prayers, unless they specifically ask you for
prayer or to alert your praying friends. Make sure you do in that case.

I made a mistake once. A friend of mine's uncle was dying of leukemia. This was while we were
back at Jasmine. I was visiting my friend, and his grandmother came up to me and asked me to let
my church know — a church she had visited a few times — that her son was struggling and in need
of prayer. She said, "Make sure you tell Pastor Bob next time you see him." This was on a Sunday
evening after we had already had church. I agreed and promised I would tell Pastor Bob. The next
time I saw him, I had forgotten. I saw her again the following week, and seeing her reminded me.
But I thought, "I'll probably see him Wednesday — I'll let Pastor know then." Another week came
and went. The following Saturday her son passed away from leukemia. She showed up at church and
went to talk to the pastor about it, and was hurt and angry when she found out I had dropped
the ball.

When someone asks you for prayer, grab their hand right then and pray. Don't put it off. If
they ask to be put on the prayer chain, let me or Dottie or someone know and we will make sure
it happens.

THE DANGER OF "JUST THIS ONCE"

But Paul's point was: don't let the occasional sin tempt you by saying "it's only just this
one." Because it usually isn't. People generally have more resistance to things like that before
they start down that road.

I was splitting wood one time, and I hit the log with an axe. I kind of hit the side of the
log, but the split, instead of following the grain of the wood, went across it at a diagonal
until the split had pierced the heart of the log. Sometimes these "one-off" sins — these
occasional sins — turn into things much more difficult to kick. Sometimes these one-off sins
split you to your core.

ILLUSTRATION: THE COUPLE WHO SKIPPED CHURCH

I know this girl, and I'm not going to say who. She went to church every week, every time the
doors were open as a kid. She loved VBS as a kid, and as an adult loved creating that kind of
imaginary world for kids, loved working with the kids at VBS. She went on every youth event,
gave her heart to the Lord, helped set up and tear down — you know the type I'm talking about.
She was always there, always helping, always trying to do the right thing and follow the Lord.

One day she meets this boy, who was similar — spent lots of time at church, helped out, and so
forth. In fact it was at a church event that they met and realized they liked each other. Several
years later they were married in that same church.

So their wedding was on a Saturday, and they left on their six-day honeymoon that Sunday. No
big deal — it's just one week, right? Well the following Sunday, "we were just so tired." The
following Sunday, "well, he's not feeling good." The following Sunday, "well, she's just not
feeling that good." The following Sunday, "yeah, we are just so busy we needed a time of rest."
And on and on like this they went. About half a decade later the two of them had been to church
a grand total of three times since they'd been married.

You know where they are today? The boy demanded a divorce, and after the third time the girl
gave in. The boy said he never really liked or wanted to go to church anyway — it was just
better than being home. And the girl is hurt and angry with God for allowing her marriage to
fail.

I had a very frank conversation with her one day, and I said, "Why are you mad at God? You guys
didn't read your Bible together, you didn't go to church, you didn't keep him at the center of
your marriage like had been suggested — you did everything you could to keep God out of your
marriage. In this instance, this marriage failing is not God's fault. You both fought to keep
him out, and so don't be surprised by the end result."

Sometimes these occasional sins split us to our core. This sweet girl has been out of church
for probably ten or fifteen years now. Last I knew she was engaged to some new guy who at this
point in his life doesn't believe in God.

THE COST OF EVERY SIN

Even if an occasional sin doesn't split you to your core, even if it's just something simple or
over something stupid — don't do it. Don't say it's fine because it's a little white lie and I
never normally tell lies, so this one little tiny lie is okay just this once. Don't do it!
Christ had to shed his blood for that white lie. Every time you commit a sin, Christ's blood
had to be shed for that sin. Every time you commit a sin, Christ's hands had to be nailed to
that cross. Every time you commit a sin, Christ had to be beaten with a cat-o'-nine-tails.
Every time — no matter how minor the sin — Christ had to die at the hands of the mocking
masses.

WHICH MASTER DO YOU SERVE? — ROMANS 6:16

Which master do you serve? Verse 16 — if you present yourself to anyone as an obedient slave,
you are a slave of the one whom you obey.

Think about your life — in fact, just think about these last few days. Who did you obey? Can
you honestly say you did as Christ would have wanted in every situation? Or were you doing what
made you feel comfortable? What made you happy for the moment? In short, were you serving
Christ, or our enemy — through action or inaction? When we have those moments of weakness where
we are at odds with what Christ Jesus would have us do, we are indeed sinning.

PAUL'S POINTED QUESTION — ROMANS 6:20-21

Paul in verses 20 and 21 asks us a very pointed question. He says — and this is the KB
standard paraphrase version — he says: "So when you guys were slaves, those things you did that
you are now ashamed of — how exactly did those things benefit you at all? Looking back, can't
you see that you were a slave? Trapped, you might say? Imprisoned? That life of sin was slavery
for you."

Perhaps you don't know Jesus as your Lord and Savior. In that case, your life of sin is a
prison for you now. You are locked away.

ILLUSTRATION: LOCKED UP ABROAD

Have you ever seen that show "Locked Up Abroad"? It was about people who were approached by
drug cartels or kidnapped and forced into drug mule situations. They would be promised money,
vacations, cars — whatever their weakness was, the drug lords would find it and promise to give
it to them if only they would put this little tiny package in their suitcase. Or in their shoe,
or wherever.

The point is that good and upstanding citizens would be made an offer — just make this little
compromise for us today, and we'll give you a pile of money tomorrow. "It's just pot," one guy
said. "It's practically legal everywhere in the US anyway, it doesn't hurt anyone — not really.
And besides, you'll get an all-expenses-paid trip down the Amazon whenever you want — airfare,
hotel, tour guides and translators for you and your wife."

That little tiny compromise — carrying a vacuum-sealed brick of marijuana — caused him to be
locked up in a Colombian prison for 35 years. He was a young man when he went in. His wife
died, and he left the prison at nearly 70 years of age.

For us, though, the stakes are higher. If you don't know Jesus, even a little compromise
results not in a 35-year sentence — it's an eternal life sentence without possibility of
parole. If you know Jesus you'll have to face him one day.

THE WEIGHT OF FACING SOMEONE YOU'VE WRONGED

Have you ever accidentally done someone wrong, and then seen them later and put two and two
together? I mentioned my friend's grandmother earlier. I remember the sadness and guilt I felt
when I ran into her again at church that Sunday, realizing what I had done. I felt terrible. It
was an unintentional breakdown in communication — sure — but as a result of my actions someone
was hurt.

The son would have died anyway — God knows the number of our days. God may have lengthened
them; he may not have — who can say. Ultimately that was God's choice, and lots of people were
praying for this guy, and God delivered the answer by taking the son home to heaven. However,
where I was guilty — sinful, you might say — was in not following through and fulfilling my
word to her.

We've all done at least one thing in our lives that unintentionally hurt someone we care about.
So you know that awful feeling of having to face them, knowing you wounded them. Now imagine
instead of some earthly individual — picture having done that to Christ. Every time your wife
says "do these pants make me look fat" and you lie to her, Christ is there signing a contract
in blood to cover that sin. Every speed limit broken, every unclean action you take — Christ is
there. Christ is watching. Christ says, "Yes, Father — I'll pay for that one too."

THE NEW CREATION — 2 CORINTHIANS 5:17

For someone who doesn't yet believe in Jesus Christ: the moment you become a Christian, all
the sins of your past are washed away. You get a blank slate, a fresh start.

In a small way, joining witness protection is like salvation — new life, new job, new vehicle,
new name, new start. Scripture says that when we are saved we become a new creation: "The old
has passed away; behold, the new has come." (2 Corinthians 5:17) Your old self is dead and
gone and buried. Behold, the new has come! Isn't that a great promise?

DON'T BE DECEIVED BY ORCHIDS

Any of you like honey? I have a story about a bee that tried to make honey from an orchid.

An orchid is a fantastic picture of the enemy's schemes. We all know how bees work — they fly
from flower to flower, picking up pollen here, dropping it off there, and eventually fly home
and make honey. So one day this one bee was flying around from flower to flower doing what bees
do. She sees this really pretty blue orchid out of the corner of her eye and decides to fly
over and check it out. The orchid looks like a flower, it smells like a flower, and even acts
like a normal flower. So the bee flies over and lands and starts trying to find the pollen. She
looks left and looks right. Everything seems right, but there is absolutely nothing in that
flower for her. She thinks her sisters have beaten her to it and decides to check out the other
flowers. Orchid after orchid the little bee flies in, lands, searches and searches and searches
for what she needs — and can't find it. She spends all day searching through the orchids but is
left empty, hungry, and with nothing to show for her day's work.

[NOTE: Botanically, orchids do have pollen; roughly one-third of orchid species offer no nectar
reward. The bee orchid (Ophrys) specifically deceives bees by mimicking female bee appearance.
The illustration's rhetorical point — "looks like the real thing, delivers nothing" — stands;
the specific "no pollen" mechanism is imprecise. Fine for preaching; see correction log item 7
if used in a teaching context.]

So many things in life promise to be what we want. People try to fill those needs with cars, or
possessions, or power, or sexual things, and yet like the orchid the real thing we need isn't
there. What we need, friends, is God. What we need is a relationship with God. These other
things may look like they are going to fill the emptiness, but in the end they weren't designed
to fulfill — they simply leave you feeling empty and wanting more. Christ is the one thing that
fulfills, the one thing that doesn't leave you feeling empty and alone. He is the only one you
can depend upon, and the only one that won't leave you when you need him.

Just a few weeks ago we were working through Hebrews 13:5 which says: "Keep your life free from
love of money, and be content with what you have, for he has said, 'I will never leave you nor
forsake you.'"
[VERIFIED: Hebrews 13:5, ESV. Quoted accurately.]

BENEFITS OF OBEDIENCE

Holiness — becoming more Christ-like; his desires, his purposes and plans become our desires.

Eternal Life — salvation, which begins with obeying the prompting of his Spirit, is culminated
in eternal life.

Happiness — this is another benefit of obedience. You have all had or known kids in your life.
Lilly got in trouble this week. They have a color scheme for telling the kids how their behavior
is in relation to expectations. If you don't do anything good or bad, you stay on green. If
you're outstandingly good you get purple; if you're really rotten that day they move you down
to red. Lilly has been on red all week. Every day they reset her to green, every day she works
to earn a red. She ignores the teacher and is blatant about not doing what she's told — to the
point where she has been on red consistently every day this last week. So partway through the
week they had parent-teacher conferences. Lilly had been lying about what color she'd been
getting. The teacher surprised her parents with the reality of the situation. Lilly got in so
much trouble — she got a lecture, had to mop the floor, clean the kitchen, clean the bathroom;
she was grounded from video games and TV — basically the things she enjoys (except church) were
taken away and substituted for the things no one likes to do. She can earn them back, of
course. But that's beside the point.

If Lilly had obeyed the teacher and her parents — if she had not lied or been disrespectful and
willfully obnoxious about her desire not to obey — wouldn't she have been happier? Happiness
comes through obedience. That's not to say life is always going to be candy and flowers if we
just obey, because there are still going to be trials that we go through, as you all know. But
there is more happiness in obedience than can be found in sin.

OUTWARD SINCERITY IS NOT ENOUGH

Remember the Great Pumpkin? Linus said — and this is a summary of his view, not a direct quote
from the special — that it doesn't matter what you believe, as long as you are sincere.
[NOTE: The actual special (1966) has Linus emphasizing the sincerity of the pumpkin patch as the
condition for the Great Pumpkin's appearing — not a general claim that belief doesn't matter.
The pastor's rhetorical use of the illustration is sound; do not present this as a direct
Linus quote. See correction log item 3.]

No amount of sincerity will result in presents on Halloween. Back in colonial America there were
a couple of times when the settlers would fill up a ship with what they believed to be gold.
When it arrived in Europe, the goldsmiths would try to melt it down and instantly realize it
wasn't really gold — it was fool's gold. The settlers couldn't necessarily tell the difference.
But the goldsmith knew.

God too knows you to your core. He knows whether you are truly saved or not. Who cares if you
can fool me, or your friends or family, into thinking you're a certain way — what matters is
not what people think. What matters is what God knows. God knows who you are and everything
about you; he knows your actions both good and bad. He can't be deceived or manipulated. He
knows; and one day you'll stand before God, and Jesus is either going to welcome you into the
kingdom, or he's going to say, "Depart from me — I never knew you."

You can't simply settle for sincere outward conformity — you have to be transformed inwardly by
the power of God to truly be saved.

I've said it a hundred times and I'll say it again: if you stick your face in a fire you'll get
burned. It will be obvious to everyone around you that you've been burned. In the same way,
when you experience God, who you are — the very inmost part of your being, your soul — is
marked in just that same way. It's obvious to everyone around you that you're different now,
that you've been changed.

A guy in our church had a chance to share the gospel with someone the week before last. His
friend is dying of cancer, and this guy talked to him and was able to say: "You've got to know
God's real because of the change in my life. You know who I was, and you can see who I am now."
The dying friend wept, and agreed that this guy's life had been dramatically changed. My
understanding is that he wants to hear more about Christ. Praise God for that!

CLOSING APPEAL

Maybe you're here and you realize you need to make some changes in your life. Maybe you're a
slave to the sin in your life and you want to be made free from that — when the song plays in
a minute, come on up and we'll talk and pray together.

Maybe you've been looking for a church home and would like to join — come talk to me.

Maybe you've never been baptized and you're ready to take that second step of obedience — if
so, come talk to me.

Maybe you've been a Christian in name only, and you're ready to take that first step as a
believer in Jesus Christ — if so, come talk to me. It's not embarrassing. I'll be rejoicing
with you. We love you.
