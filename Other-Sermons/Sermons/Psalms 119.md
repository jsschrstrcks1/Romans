## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluated**: 2026-03-21
**Passage**: Psalm 119:161–168
**Working Title**: Psalm 119:161–168 — The Treasure of the Word

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"A Winsome Plea for Bible Reading That Mistakes Illustration for Exposition"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A genuinely motivated sermon on the value of Scripture that generates real warmth through the Brother Andrew story and the treasure hunt illustration, but treats Psalm 119:161–168 as a pretext for a Bible-reading challenge rather than opening the stanza's specific claims about peace, love of the law, and seven-times-daily praise.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Pass — with substantial drift.**

The main theme — Scripture is a treasure most Christians neglect, and we should prize it — is consistent with Psalm 119's general direction. The sermon does not contradict the text. However, the Scopus Test reveals a serious problem: the specific stanza (vv. 161–168) makes at least six distinct claims — princes persecute without cause, the psalmist stands in awe of God's word, he loves the law, peace comes to lovers of the law, he hates falsehood, he praises God seven times daily, and he hopes in God's word — and the sermon engages only two of these (peace from v. 165, abhorring falsehood from v. 163) in passing. The sermon's actual subject is "why we should read our Bibles," which is a theme that could be preached from dozens of passages. Psalm 119:161–168 functions as a title card, not as an argument being opened.

**Scopus Test**: "The Bible is a treasure we neglect, and we should read it." — Does not arise primarily from this stanza; it is a general principle consistent with the Psalter but not the argument of this specific passage. Narrow pass on goodwill; the sermon barely survives the gate.
**Authority Test**: The preacher does not distort the text. Passes.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 — Main point does not equal text's point.** The text's main point (in this stanza) is the psalmist's account of his own posture toward God's word: awe before it, love for it, peace from it, hatred of falsehood, and praise seven times daily. The sermon's main point is that the congregation does not read their Bibles enough. These are related but not identical. The text is descriptive/doxological; the sermon is prescriptive/hortatory. The sermon applies a theme to the congregation that is consistent with the psalm's spirit but not driven by the psalm's own argument.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The word "Gospel" at the end is a placeholder. The sermon makes a general appeal to read one's Bible, but there is no specific "therefore" moment — no call to what the congregation must now believe, repent of, or begin doing this week. "How many of you have cracked open your treasure outside of church this week?" is a convicting question, but asking a question is not the same as issuing a call.

**Score capped at 69.**

- Exegetical Clarity (5): 2/5. No word studies. No original language. The compass illustration is the sermon's most extended "exegetical" move — using it to explain how Scripture corrects direction — but it interprets the theme of the psalm rather than the text of this stanza.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Neither — it directs attention primarily toward Scripture as an object of value, and toward the congregation's discipline (or lack thereof) in reading it. Christ is absent.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about the Bible's value. The Brother Andrew story is the closest moment of genuine encounter — faith under pressure, answered prayer. But even there, the God encountered is not clearly Christ.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Convicted on the specific point of Bible neglect. This is the sermon's genuine strength. But no broader conviction, no confrontation with deeper sin, no comfort for the person who is drowning.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Brother Andrew story** — Extraordinarily well-told. Prayer answered, faith displayed, the contrast between persecuted believers and comfortable Western Christians — this is the sermon's finest moment and it lands with the weight of eternity.
2. **The Bible-neglect challenge** — "Don't raise your hands. Why are we so ambivalent as a people towards one of the greatest treasures mankind has ever discovered?" This is pointed, pastoral, and genuinely convicting. It names the specific spiritual failure of many congregation members with enough force to penetrate.
3. **The Fallen Condition Focus is accurate and well-named** — Spiritual ambivalence toward Scripture in the midst of material abundance is a precise diagnosis for this congregation and this cultural moment. The contrast between seventy Bible translations and a pastor in China who considers a few pages to be wealth is devastating.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Only 2 of the stanza's 8 verses are engaged.** Verses 161, 162, 164, 166, 167, and 168 receive no treatment. The psalmist's "awe at your word" (v. 161), his "great spoil" (v. 162), his seven-times daily praise (v. 164), his hope in the LORD's salvation (v. 166), his keeping of the testimonies (v. 167), and his love of the precepts (v. 168) — all absent. The sermon cannot claim to be an exposition of Psalm 119:161–168 when six of its eight verses are skipped.
2. **The gospel is a placeholder.** "Gospel" at the end of the manuscript is structurally identical to the same problem in the Psalm 46 and Psalm 100 sermons in this batch — a tag indicating where the gospel would go without actually containing it. As written, this sermon contains no gospel.
3. **The sermon approaches moralism.** The movement from "read your Bible" → "your priorities will align with God's" → "you'll love properly" describes sanctification as the result of a discipline rather than as the fruit of union with Christ. This is not heretical but it is incomplete and dangerous — it implies the reader can achieve transformation through Bible consumption rather than through Spirit-wrought faith in Christ mediated by the Word.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the illustrations have become the sermon. The Brother Andrew story, the Jarrod safe story, the treasure hunt, the compass illustration — these are all excellent and the congregation may not realize that the text has been largely absent. The sermon feels like it has engaged Scripture because it talks about Scripture. But talking about Scripture and opening a specific passage are different things.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The three opening illustrations create a powerful cumulative effect — by the time the text is introduced, the congregation is primed to value what the preacher is about to preach from. This is genuinely good homiletical instinct. But the cumulative weight of the illustrations slightly obscures the fact that the text itself receives only about one-third of the sermon's attention, and even that engagement is thematic rather than exegetical.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 164: "Seven times a day I praise you for your righteous rules." — This is extraordinary. The psalmist praises God seven times a day because of His word. Not despite his circumstances. Not in addition to his anxieties. Because of the word. This verse is the emotional and doxological climax of the stanza — the person who truly treasures God's word does not merely read it; he erupts in praise because of it. The sermon's entire argument about the value of Scripture could have found its climax here: "The person who truly opens this treasure doesn't just read it — he worships. Seven times a day. That's the Christian who has found what Fenn's treasure hunters are still looking for."

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Open the stanza systematically.** Work through vv. 161–168 with even brief attention to each verse. The acrostic structure (shin/sin stanza) may be worth noting as part of the psalm's form. Give each claim one sentence of development minimum.
2. **Develop v. 164 as the climax.** "Seven times a day I praise you." This is where the sermon should peak — not the Brother Andrew story, as wonderful as it is. Move Andrew earlier and v. 164 to the close.
3. **Connect Torah to Christ.** "The law of the LORD is perfect, reviving the soul" — and Christ is the one who perfectly loved and fulfilled that law. The New Covenant promise is that this same Torah-devotion is written on our hearts by the Spirit. This is how you preach Psalm 119 as a Christian without allegorizing it.
4. **Preach the gospel in the manuscript.** Replace "Gospel" with: "What enables a person to love God's word? Not discipline. Not guilt. Not comparison to Brother Andrew. It is the new heart that Christ purchases — the Spirit who writes God's word on the heart so that you want to open it, want to obey it, want to praise seven times a day. You cannot manufacture this. You must receive it from Christ."
5. **Close with a call.** Not just "crack open your Bible" but: "This week, open your Bible. Ask God to give you a new heart that loves what the psalmist loves. Ask Him for the peace of v. 165 that comes not from your circumstances but from your God. And if you've never trusted Christ — the Word made flesh — today is the day to begin."

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The Brother Andrew story carries genuine eternal weight — it is a story about the cost of the word and the faithfulness of God. The sermon's overall gravity is lower because it ends in a placeholder and never arrives at Christ. The unction is present in the illustration; it does not sustain through the sermon to its close.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 119 is wisdom/Torah poetry — its genre rewards slow, detailed engagement with individual verses rather than broad thematic sweeps. The rubric's Exposition category appropriately penalizes the thematic approach here. The illustration-heavy structure is particularly risky with this passage because Psalm 119's value is in its density; preaching around it rather than through it means the congregation never experiences the texture of the poet's devotion. A sermon on any single stanza of Psalm 119 should probably spend 70% of its time in that stanza, not 30%.
