## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm Pastoral Plea Against Idolatry That Stops Short of the Cross**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A personally transparent and relationally engaging opening of Jeremiah 17's cursed/blessed contrast that identifies political idolatry with real pastoral insight, but fails to cross the full distance from the idol to Christ's finished work as the only sufficient cure.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**Pass — with significant reservations.**

The Scopus Test: The sermon's thesis — "Don't trust in man; trust in God alone" — does arise from Jeremiah 17:5-8. The cursed man / blessed man contrast is the text's own argument, and the sermon stays in that orbit.

The Authority Test: The preacher submits to the text's claims, including the uncomfortable call to political sobriety. He applies the passage honestly to himself and his congregation.

**Reservation:** The sermon barely touches Jeremiah 17:9-10 ("The heart is deceitful above all things"), which is the text's own diagnostic for *why* idolatry persists. That omission weakens the passage's full argument. The passage's movement from idolatry to the deceitful heart to God's searching judgment to the psalmist-like cry of the prophet (vv. 14-18) is largely unpreached. The sermon derives its point *from* the text but does not *exhaust* the text's point.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — Christ is present but not necessary.** The sermon mentions Christ and concludes with "In Christ Alone," but never explains why Christ is the only solution to the idolatrous heart. The reference to "In Christ alone, my hope is found" is decorative. What did Christ actually do? Why is trust in Him categorically different from trust in a politician? The penal substitution language appears in the hymn lyrics but not in the preacher's own proclamation. A devout Jewish person or Muslim could have sat through this sermon and agreed with 90% of it without being confronted by the offense of the cross.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The sermon circles, identifies the problem, offers warmth and illustration, and ends with "Where is your hope" — but never issues a clear summons. What must the listener now *do*? Repent of specific idolatry? Trust Christ in a defined way? The sermon explains and identifies but never summons. The final line "But all of that starts with the gospel" points *toward* an exhortation that never arrives.

**Score is capped at 69.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (6/10):* The cursed/blessed contrast of Jeremiah 17:5-8 is treated faithfully, and the political application is not illegitimate — Jeremiah himself was writing against trust in Egypt and political alliances. But the sermon treats only two of the eighteen verses in the pericope (17:1-18). Verses 9-10 (the deceitful heart), vv. 11-13 (the partridge proverb and the forsaken fountain), and vv. 14-18 (the prophet's cry) receive no treatment. This is a significant omission.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5):* No engagement with the historical context of Jeremiah's Judah, the political trust in Egypt that the passage addresses, or the book's overall movement. The contemporary application (politics) is present but the ancient meaning is underdeveloped.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5):* The text is read and referenced, but the sermon relies on quotation more than explanation. The preacher does not open the passage — he quotes it and illustrates around it.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5):* The core application (political idolatry) is logically drawn from the text. But the Fallen Condition Focus is only partially named — political anxiety is identified as the symptom, but the heart's default toward self-trust (the text's own diagnosis in v. 9) is never articulated as the root cause.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7):* Christ is present — "In Christ Alone" is quoted in full, and the hymn's theology is rich. But Christ is introduced as the *replacement* for a bad idol rather than the one who rescues us from our very capacity for idolatry. The connection is pastoral but not robustly theological.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (1/3):* No placement of Jeremiah 17 in the story of redemption. The passage's connection to Jeremiah's suffering, the coming exile, or its fulfillment in Christ (who alone trusted the Father perfectly) is absent.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4):* Generally sound. No heresy present. But the sovereignty of God and the deceitfulness of the heart are touched lightly rather than pressed with confessional weight.
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse (3/4):* No contradictions of confessional theology. The sovereignty of God in politics is affirmed with good instinct. But the Covenant of Grace and its implications for why Christ's trust succeeds where ours fails is absent.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (2/5):* The gospel is alluded to through the hymn but not articulated by the preacher. Justification by faith is never stated. Why must a sinner trust Christ specifically? The sermon answers "because everything else fails" — which is pragmatic, not gospel.
- *Necessity of Christ (2/5):* The Synagogue Test is partially triggered. A devout Jew or Muslim could agree: trust God, not politicians. The uniqueness of Christ — the incarnate Son, the crucified substitute, the risen Lord — is not proclaimed from the pulpit in the preacher's own voice.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5):* The hymn "In Christ Alone" contains rich cross-centered content. But the sermon itself does not move toward the cross under its own power. The gravity comes from borrowed lyrics, not the preacher's own proclamation.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10/15**

- *Heart Penetration (4/5):* This is the sermon's strongest category. The personal confession of political idolatry is disarming and pastorally warm. "I was unintentionally worshipping politics" — this lands. The checkbook/day-planner diagnostic is concrete and searching. Real hearts are touched here.
- *Specificity (4/5):* The political application is specific to the congregation's world. Biden and Trump are named. The "if you've spent more time talking about Biden than Jesus" confrontation is pointed and appropriate.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon essentially addresses two categories: the political idolater (who needs rebuke) and the grieving/anxious (who needs comfort, illustrated by the grandfather story). The self-righteous person who feels no political anxiety is not addressed. The unbeliever is gestured at but not summoned. The backslider is not reached. The Perkins taxonomy is largely unengaged.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8**

- *Call to Response (1/4):* "Where is your hope" is a question, not a summons. The sermon describes the problem and points to the solution but never crosses into direct command. There is no "Therefore, repent of this. Turn from this idol. Trust Christ now."
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The sermon does equip — practically, it tells people to vote but not to be consumed by politics. The grandfather illustration points toward witness. But the corporate dimension of the church as a community bearing testimony against political idolatry is absent.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* The sermon has a discernible arc: personal confession → diagnosis → text → illustrations → gospel pointer. A listener could follow it, though not easily sketch its structure.
- *Progression (2/3):* The sermon moves but meanders. The Boniface illustration is lengthy and the aircraft carrier material feels tangential. The story of the friend's grandfather is powerful but arrives late and is not fully integrated into the argument.
- *Unity (1/2):* The dominant idea (trust God, not politics) is present throughout. But secondary themes (sovereignty, voting, worship) compete for attention without being firmly subordinated.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

- *Relevance (2/2):* The illustrations are from the congregation's world — politics, voting, the military, personal confession. Highly accessible.
- *Clarity (1/2):* Boniface is effective as an idol-smashing image. The aircraft carrier illustration adds color but doesn't illuminate the theological point. The grandfather story is the most powerful illustration — but it arrives without being tied directly to the text's argument.
- *Memorability (1/1):* The personal confession ("I was worshipping politics") and the grandfather's death-bed faith are genuinely memorable. These will travel home with the congregation.

**8. Sermonic Force — 4/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2):* This reads and sounds like a real man talking to real people. The oral cadence is natural, the direct address is genuine ("I don't know about you guys"), and the confessional tone is earned.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The conviction is pastoral and warm, but it does not sharpen into a blade. There is no moment where the sermon roars. The preacher exposes but does not press.
- *Cadence and Breath (1/1):* Sentence variation is natural and unforced. This is the voice of a man who preaches — not a man who writes essays.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Mostly of my own political habits. Christ appears at the end as the better option but is not magnified as the one who rescues us from our idolatry. The congregation leaves thinking about their political consumption more than about Christ's sufficiency.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Mostly ideas. The sermon is warm and engaging, but God does not feel weighty. He is presented as the better bet, the sovereign background, the reliable foundation — not as the consuming fire before whom political idolatry is a crime that required the blood of His Son.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Some of both — the political idolater is named and identified, and there is real pastoral warmth. But conviction is incomplete because the cross is not proclaimed, and comfort is insufficient because it rests on God's general sovereignty rather than on Christ's particular atoning work.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Personal vulnerability disarms the congregation.** The preacher's first-person confession — "I was unintentionally worshipping politics" — is specific, credible, and earned. This is not a rhetorical move; it reads as genuine testimony. Few preachers are willing to confess idol worship from the pulpit. This lowers defenses and prepares the congregation to receive the diagnosis.

2. **The political application is specific and culturally precise.** Naming Biden and Trump by name, applying the "if you've spent more time talking about X than about Jesus" diagnostic — this is not generic application. It is applied to the actual, named idols of the congregation's world. This is a strength of conscience application that the rubric rewards.

3. **The grandfather illustration is the sermon's most powerful pastoral moment.** The story of the dying grandfather and the praying unbelieving grandson is emotionally real and theologically suggestive. It gestures toward resurrection hope, the difference between earthly and eternal foundation, and the tragedy of placing final hope in this life. In a better-constructed sermon, this illustration could carry the full gospel weight.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The cross is borrowed, not proclaimed.** The sermon's single most substantive gospel content appears in the lyrics of "In Christ Alone" — lyrics written by Keith Getty and Stuart Townend, not by the preacher. "The wrath of God was satisfied / For every sin on Him was laid" — these are the words that need to be proclaimed from the pulpit in the preacher's own voice, explained, applied, and pressed into the conscience of the idolater. Instead, they are sung (or read). This is not preaching the gospel. This is pointing at a sign that says "gospel ahead."

2. **Jeremiah 17:9 — the most diagnostic verse in the passage — is skipped.** "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?" This verse is the text's own answer to why political idolatry persists. The sermon diagnoses political anxiety as the problem but never names the deceitful heart as its engine. Without verse 9, the sermon's application is behavioral ("stop trusting politicians") rather than regenerative ("you need a new heart"). The difference is the difference between moralism and the gospel.

3. **The sermon ends without a summons.** "Where is your hope" is the final interrogative. It is a good question — but it is not a call to response. The sermon has created gospel-readiness in the congregation and then withheld the gospel imperative. There is no "Therefore, repent" — no "Come to Christ now" — no "Christian, return your trust to the one who bought you." The congregation is left holding a question without a commanded answer.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that by leading with political application for so long before reaching the text, he has taught his congregation that the Bible is a resource for political wisdom rather than a revelation of the living God. The Boniface story, the aircraft carrier illustration, and the voting anecdote are all interesting — but they collectively train the ear to expect a cultural commentary rather than a divine address. "Thus says the Lord" is the rubric's name for a reason: the sermon should feel like God speaking, not a wise friend reflecting. The preacher is more pastor than herald in this manuscript, which is not without value, but it means the word "thus" carries insufficient authority.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon *feels* gospel-centered because it ends with a full reading of "In Christ Alone." This is the illusion. A congregation that hears those lyrics sung or read will leave feeling that the gospel was preached. But the preacher did not preach the gospel — he quoted a hymn that contains the gospel. The difference matters. The hymn's theology of penal substitution is sound; the sermon's gospel content is borrowed. Evaluation must account for what the *preacher* said, not what the hymnwriter said on the preacher's behalf.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Jeremiah 17:9-10 is the theological heart of this entire passage and it received no treatment: "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it? I the Lord search the heart and test the mind." This is the passage's own diagnosis of why human trust-objects always fail — not because politicians are weak (the sermon's argument), but because the heart that reaches for them is broken. This leads directly to the gospel: only God can search and heal what only God can see. The Savior who searches the heart and died for its every deceit is precisely what the text is pointing toward — and the preacher walked past it.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach Jeremiah 17:9 as the hinge of the sermon.** After establishing the political idol, stop and ask: why do we keep trusting in man? Because the heart is deceitful above all things. This is not merely an Old Testament observation — it is the diagnosis that demands a Savior. The idol-smashing is not the solution; heart-transformation is. Only then does Christ's work become necessary rather than helpful.

2. **Remove the gospel from the hymn and place it in the preacher's own mouth.** Before reading or singing "In Christ Alone," the preacher should proclaim in his own words: what Christ did on the cross, why the wrath of God had to be satisfied, what it means for a political idolater to have all his sins laid on Christ. The hymn can *confirm* what has been proclaimed — it should not *replace* the proclamation.

3. **Close with a direct summons, not a question.** Replace "Where is your hope?" with: "Christian — your hope is not in your party, your candidate, or your country. Repent of that idol and return to the one who bought you. And if you have never trusted Christ — this is your moment. The same God who is sovereign over every election is sovereign over your heart. Come to Him." This takes thirty seconds and transforms the ending from reflection to exhortation.

4. **Tighten the introduction by half.** The Boniface story and the personal political testimony are both valuable, but together they occupy more than a third of the sermon before the text is opened. Consider cutting the aircraft carrier material entirely (it makes a patriotic point, not a theological one) and shortening the Boniface story to its essential image: an idol was smashed and God was worshipped. Move to the text faster.

5. **Address the unbeliever explicitly.** The grandfather story opens a clear door to the unbeliever in the room — the friend who could not hope beyond this life. The sermon should stop at that moment and say: "If that's you — if your hope is entirely in this world — then the text has a word for you: *cursed*. Not as a condemnation to leave you there, but as a diagnosis to drive you to the only physician. The one who said 'cursed' also said 'blessed' — and what separates the two is not politics but Christ."

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** This sermon is warm, personally engaged, and pastorally alive. The problem is not bloodlessness — it is incompleteness. The fire is present; the fuel is insufficient. This is a sincere shepherd who has not yet fully preached.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
This sermon carries genuine pastoral warmth and personal vulnerability, but it does not yet carry eternal gravity — it leaves the congregation thinking about their political habits rather than trembling before the God whose curse and blessing divide humanity and whose Son alone is the bridge between them.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Jeremiah 17 is prophetic oracle literature — a form that blends pronouncement, wisdom contrast (the cursed/blessed psalm-like structure of vv. 5-8), and the prophet's personal lament (vv. 14-18). The rubric applies well to this passage, but the evaluator notes that the sermon treated only the wisdom-contrast section and omitted the prophetic lament entirely. A fuller treatment of this pericope would benefit from acknowledging the prophet's own cry ("Heal me, O Lord, and I shall be healed") as a model of where the text wants to arrive — the trusting soul crying out to God in affliction, not merely resolving to trust in the abstract. The rubric's categories are well-suited to this genre and did not require significant bending.
