## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Verdict Title**: A Gospel Crossroads Buried Under a Doctrinal Detour

**One-Sentence Summary**: A sermon on 2 Kings 25 that reaches for genuine gospel urgency and lands real moments of pastoral directness, but is structurally derailed by a lengthy preamble on divine sovereignty that belongs to a different sermon, leaving the text itself underexposed and the conscience only partially pressed.

---

### Berean Gate

**Scopus Test**: The sermon's stated concern is the destruction of Jerusalem and the consequences of disobedience — and from that it draws the analogy of the hearer at a crossroads before God. This is loosely derivable from 2 Kings 25, though the main point of the text (the covenant judgment of God on a faithless nation, the fulfillment of prophetic warning, exile as the wages of disobedience) is present in embryo but never fully developed. The sermon's thesis — "trust God, not your heart, or face destruction" — arises from the text's general atmosphere more than from its specific argument.

**Authority Test**: The preacher does not stand above the text or soften it. The hard edges of covenant judgment are named. The text is not exploited as a springboard for a morality lecture with no divine authority behind it — there is genuine submission to the Word.

**Verdict**: **Conditional Pass.** The main point is in the neighborhood of the text's point, but the sermon spends more time on John 6 and divine sovereignty than on 2 Kings 25 itself. The Berean Gate is passed narrowly.

---

### Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — Christ present but not essential**: Christ is mentioned at the crossroads close, but the bulk of the sermon could survive without Him. The sovereignty section (John 6, Lydia, Acts 13:48) does not move toward Christ crucified; it argues a theological point about divine omnipotence. The gospel appears at the end but as a destination rather than a driver.

**Flag 4 — Application is partially generic**: "Don't trust your heart, trust God" is a real call, and the specific names (Kurt, Kenny Duffield) give it real texture. However, the sermon does not distinguish hearers — it addresses the unbeliever primarily and leaves believers without specific application.

**Flag 5 — Exhortation is present but thin**: The closing "which way will you choose?" is an invitation, not an exhortation. It does not tell believers what to do, does not give the convicted sinner specific next steps beyond "choose Christ," and does not address what repentance looks like.

**Score cap applied: 69 maximum.**

### Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 13/25**

- *Textual Fidelity* (10 pts): **5/10** — 2 Kings 25:1-12 is read and briefly contextualized. The preacher draws the right macro-lesson (disobedience leads to destruction). But the passage itself receives only one section of focused treatment. Jeremiah 38 is referenced helpfully. The text's specific verses — the siege, the flight, the slaughter of the sons, the blinding, the exile — are not opened with any exegetical care. The diaspora/remnant point is noted but not developed. The fall of the temple's cultural significance is illustrated effectively. Fallen Condition Focus: named correctly as "trusting the heart over God" — but it stays at the level of observation, not excavation.
- *Contextual Awareness* (5 pts): **3/5** — The preacher correctly places the text at the end of Israel's covenant history, connects it to Jeremiah's prophetic ministry, and nods toward the exile. No redemptive-historical grounding beyond the immediate narrative.
- *Exegetical Clarity* (5 pts): **2/5** — No Greek or Hebrew engagement. No verse-by-verse movement. The passage is described more than opened.
- *Logical Faithfulness* (5 pts): **3/5** — No eisegesis. The analogies are appropriate. But the John 6 block at the opening is not tied to 2 Kings 25 — it is a sermon-within-a-sermon that is never fully integrated.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness* (7 pts): **4/7** — Christ appears at the climax as the one to whom the hearer must come. This is appropriate. But there is no organic connection from 2 Kings 25 to Christ — the sermon jumps to the gospel invitation without showing the redemptive-historical thread that connects exile and restoration to Christ's work.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* (3 pts): **1/3** — The text is placed historically (late monarchy, Babylonian exile, Jeremiah's ministry), but no redemptive-historical movement is traced. The exile as covenantal judgment that foreshadows the curse borne by Christ is entirely absent.
- *Doctrinal Precision* (4 pts): **3/4** — The doctrinal content is sound. Irresistible grace is asserted clearly and defended with good texts. The gospel at the end is clear: sin, death, the need for Christ. Pascal's Wager is invoked — this is a weak apologetic move (not heretical, but not as strong as the preacher likely intends).
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse* (4 pts): **4/4** — The sovereignty section is explicitly and correctly Reformed. No contradictions of confessional theology. The treatment of effectual calling (John 6:44, 65; Acts 13:48; Acts 16:14) is solid and appropriately argued.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 8/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel* (5 pts): **3/5** — The gospel is present and named. Sin, death, and the need for Christ are all stated. But justification by faith is not explicitly articulated. "Choose to follow Christ" is the language of the close — which is volitional without being theological. What exactly does it mean to follow Christ here? What happens to sin?
- *Necessity of Christ* (5 pts): **3/5** — Christ is necessary to the invitation, but not woven into the body of the sermon. He is the destination, not the atmosphere.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity* (5 pts): **2/5** — The cross is not preached. Eternity in hell is pressed; Christ's atoning work is mentioned but not magnified. The sermon moves toward the crossroads choice, not toward the cross itself.

**Synagogue Test**: A devout Jew or Muslim familiar with moral seriousness could follow most of this sermon and not be deeply offended until the final paragraph. **Partial trigger.** The closing is genuinely christological, but arrives late and thin.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10/15**

- *Heart Penetration* (5 pts): **4/5** — The "think about your own life" moments are genuinely searching. The specific naming of Kurt and Kenny Duffield is the strongest moment in the sermon — it names actual people from the congregation's world and gives the conscience a real face to look at. Jeremiah 17:9 ("the heart is deceitful above all things") does real work here.
- *Specificity* (5 pts): **4/5** — "Dad, think about that" is direct. The named men, the motorcycle club, alcoholism — these are concrete and grounded. This is the sermon's greatest strength.
- *Spiritual Differentiation* (5 pts): **2/5** — The sermon primarily addresses the unbeliever at the crossroads. Believers, backsliders, the hardened, and the humbled are not specifically addressed. Perkins' taxonomy: the sermon reaches category 4 (humbled, not yet believing) and category 7 (mixed congregation) — but not categories 2, 3, 5, or 6 with any specificity.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Call to Response* (4 pts): **2/4** — "Choose to follow Christ" and "trust God" are called for. But the believer is not told what to do. The unbeliever is given a crossroads but not a specific next step (come to me afterward? Pray right now? Repent and believe?).
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness* (4 pts): **2/4** — The sermon ends at the moment of decision. It does not send the church out with anything — not a call to hold each other accountable, not a vision for what faithfulness to God looks like next week.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**

- *Clarity of Outline* (4 pts): **2/4** — The structure is: [Sovereignty preamble → John 6 texts → 2 Kings 25 text → Temple context → Jeremiah 38 warning → Heart application → Crossroads/gospel close]. The sovereignty preamble is a sermon-within-a-sermon that disrupts the flow. A listener would struggle to identify the main thread.
- *Progression* (3 pts): **2/3** — There is movement from text to application to invitation. The progression is present but interrupted.
- *Unity* (2 pts): **1/2** — Two competing ideas: divine sovereignty (can God override free will?) and covenant disobedience (what happens when we trust our hearts?). They are adjacent but never merged into one dominant idea.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

The Pearl Harbor/Hiroshima analogy is less clear (Pearl Harbor was an attack on a military installation, not a cultural hub — and the emotional register shifts oddly). Pascal's Wager illustration is philosophically borrowed and only partially applied.
- *Memorability* (1 pt): **1/1** — "Don't trust your heart, trust God" with the named examples (Kurt, Kenny) will be remembered.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching* (2 pts): **2/2** — This reads like spoken preaching. Oral cadence, asides, direct address ("Dad, think about that") — it is unmistakably a sermon transcript. Good.
- *Conviction on the Page* (2 pts): **1/2** — The conviction level is uneven. The sovereignty section reads like an argument rather than a proclamation. The application section ("Kurt ended up in the motorcycle club") has genuine heat.
- *Cadence and Breath* (1 pt): **0/1** — Sentence variation is limited. The longest paragraphs are the most argumentative; the most urgent moments are not the most compressed. The sermon does not build to a climax — it arrives at one somewhat accidentally.

### Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily myself — the crossroads framing centers the hearer's choice more than Christ's accomplished work. Partially.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about Him, mostly — the sovereignty section argues for God's power without making it worship-inducing. The application section comes closest to an encounter.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Convicted — partially. The personal names and "trust God not your heart" land. But the conviction is not followed by sufficient gospel comfort.

---

### Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Congregational specificity with names**: "Kurt ended up in the motorcycle club, Kenny Duffield ended up becoming an alcoholic" — this is the kind of direct pastoral address that the Puritans called a "use of conviction." It corners the listener in the best way.
2. **Reformed doctrinal backbone**: The sovereignty section, while structurally misplaced, is theologically confident and correctly handled. The string of texts from John 6 through Hebrews 12 is a real doctrinal argument, not a proof-text parade.
3. **Contextual illustration of the temple**: The mosque analogy to explain the temple's centrality in Jewish life is genuinely clarifying — original, specific, and cross-cultural without being gimmicky.

---

### Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The preamble is a different sermon**: The opening divine sovereignty argument (triggered by a video of a Baptist preacher) is a detour that takes up roughly 30% of the sermon before the primary text is read. It never integrates with the 2 Kings 25 material. The preacher should either preach a separate sermon on effectual calling or weave the sovereignty material into the 2 Kings 25 argument directly (e.g., "Zedekiah's inability to trust God despite Jeremiah's warning is exactly what John 6:44 describes — no one comes unless the Father draws him").
2. **The text is described, not opened**: 2 Kings 25:1-12 is given one paragraph of contextual description. No verse is examined in detail. The fall of Jerusalem — the blinding of Zedekiah, the killing of the sons before his eyes, the burning of the temple — is mentioned but not pressed. These details are not decorative; they are the weight of covenant judgment, and they are what give the sermon's crossroads metaphor its moral seriousness.
3. **The gospel close is volitional, not substitutionary**: "Choose to follow Christ" and "trust God over your heart" are true but incomplete. The sermon does not tell the hearer what the cross accomplished, what sin costs, or what Christ did that makes the crossroads real. Pascal's Wager at the climax is a particularly weak substitute for penal substitution — it frames Christianity as the better bet, not as the only rescue from real wrath.

---

### Blind Spots

- The preacher likely does not see that the sovereignty preamble, which feels like theological context, actually competes with the sermon's main argument rather than serving it.
- The direct address to "Dad" is one of the best moments — but the preacher may not realize it works precisely because it is differentiated. The rest of the sermon doesn't differentiate at all: it speaks to the undecided nonbeliever and mostly leaves everyone else without a word.
- The sermon ends three times (Pascal's Wager → Jeremiah 17:9 loop → crossroads close). This dissipates the climax.

---

### Illusion of Strength

The doctrinal sovereignty section feels authoritative because it cites many texts. But a list of texts asserting a doctrine is not preaching that doctrine — it is cataloguing it. The strength of the Reformed conviction is real, but the form it takes in this sermon makes it feel like a theological brief rather than a proclamation. The congregation may leave remembering "God is sovereign" without knowing what to do with that truth in the context of 2 Kings 25.

---

### Missed Opportunity

The text itself provides the most devastating illustration the preacher never used: the blinding of Zedekiah. He watched his sons die. Then his eyes were put out. Those sons — the last thing he ever saw — were the consequence of trusting his own heart. That image is the sermon. It gives the crossroads metaphor visceral weight. It is the moment Jeremiah 17:9 becomes flesh and blood. The preacher passed over it entirely.

---

### Surgical Fixes

1. **Cut or relocate the sovereignty preamble.** Either preach it as its own sermon (an excellent topic), or compress it to two sentences and tie it directly to Zedekiah: "Zedekiah couldn't come to God on his own — that's what John 6 means. And God, in His grace, sent Jeremiah to draw him. He refused the draw."
2. **Open the text verse by verse.** Walk through vv. 1-12 with attention to at least three specific details: the length of the siege (18 months — what does that kind of slow suffering feel like?), the killing of the sons (the last thing Zedekiah saw), and the exile of the remnant. Let the text do the heavy lifting on "consequences of disobedience."
3. **Replace Pascal's Wager with penal substitution.** The crossroads framing is good. But what lies on the gospel side of the crossroads needs to be Christ's actual work: "He bore the exile we deserved. He went into the darkness so that we could come out of it."
4. **Add a word for believers.** One sentence: "And for those of you who are already His — you are not at the crossroads anymore. But you live among those who are. Will you be Jeremiah, or will you keep silent?"
5. **End once.** Identify the climax (the crossroads image), land there, and stop.

---

### Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered. The sermon has pastoral heat, even if it lacks structural tightness. The heart penetration and specificity scores are among the higher marks in this evaluation. This is not cold orthodoxy — it is warm preaching that needs sharpening.

---

### Weight of Glory Assessment

There are real embers here — the personal names, the Zedekiah parallel, the crossroads imagery — but the sermon does not yet carry the weight it is reaching for, largely because the text itself is not opened deeply enough to generate the gravity the close demands.

### Rubric Fit Note

2 Kings 25 is Old Testament historical narrative at the end of a covenant history arc. The rubric bends slightly here: redemptive-historical placement is especially important for this genre, and the rubric's Christ-centeredness category rewards organic covenantal connection. A sermon on the exile that does not reach toward the exile that Christ bore (the curse of the covenant, Galatians 3:13) has missed the deepest vein in the passage. The rubric does not strain the text — it actually reveals what is missing from this sermon by naming the redemptive-historical category explicitly.
