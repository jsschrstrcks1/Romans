# Why
## Exodus 5:22–6:13
*Evening service — ca. 2018 (post-Hurricane Irma, September 2017)*

---

I heard a story this week about a famous Christian of centuries past. Despite being a Christian he had become depressed to the point where he had decided to take his own life. He planned it out, he bought some poison, went home and in the night drank it down. He expected to die, but one of his friends turned up out of the blue, connected the dots, got the doctor there who was able to reverse the effects of the poison and save the guys life. Later that day when he came back around he decided he would cast himself into the Thames river which was around the corner from his home. He ordered a coach to come pick him up, and decided to jump. But the coach driver saw what he was doing, connected the dots, and held him back, and returned him to his home. He decided at that point he was going to fall on his knife and thus end his life. But when he fell, the knife broke and left him little more than scratched. Dude can’t even die. So  he went outside into the barn, gathered a length of rope, tied a noose and hung himself. But he didn’t make the rope quite right, and it merely strangled him instead of breaking his neck. Before he ran out of air a friend came in and was able to get him down before he died. His friend told him to read his bible, he ended up reading Romans 3:25, where he truly met God for the first time. Later he wrote this – which might be familiar to some of you:

God moves in a mysterious way His wonders to perform,
He plants His footsteps in the sea and rides upon the storm.
Deep in unfathomable mines of never-failing skill
He treasures up His bright designs and works His sovereign will.

I don’t think there is a single person in existence that hasn’t experienced discouragement. In fact statistically many if not most of us will experience depression at least once over the course of our lives. Even Christians sometimes end up in that place. Cowper from our story tonight sure did. Another famous Christian named Mueller did as well. He ran orphanages, he cared for the sick, and dying, for widows. He fed thousands and shared the gospel with tens of thousands. But never felt like he had done enough, never felt like he was sufficient for the task(s) at hand. Never felt like he was doing enough. 

We read about Isaiah last week who had some of the same feelings of inadequacy and discouragement. All of these men seem to circle back to the same question that Moses is going to ask in our passage tonight. A question – if we are honest with ourselves most of us have asked as well. The question is why. 

Steven Davey was preaching recently and he said when you’re asking why, God introduces himself. We see that today in our passage, read with me Exodus 5:22-6:13.

So leading up to this point, we all know the story of Moses. He was born to some Hebrew people in Egypt during a time of Hebrew slavery. The Egyptians were afraid of the growing power of the Hebrews so they put them in slavery. Yet their numbers continued to rise. The threat continued to grow, so it got to a point where Pharaoh ordered all the sons of the Israelite people, of the Hebrew people to be killed. It was around this time that Moses was born. So in fear for his life he was put in a basket, and set to float down the river. Mothers can you imagine the fear you would have to have felt to think your child is better off at the mercies of a river than left in your own care? I think they must have had a ton of faith in God, and probably had a good idea of when and where the princess was going to be when they let the child float free. What's more I think the princess must have had a good idea the child was not Egyptian. But anyway, Moses was rescued by the royal family, raised with all the privileges, education, and opportunity afforded to a king of the most powerful nation on earth at the time. He was trained in the same way any future pharaoh might have been. At some point he figures out where he’s from, and which people are his. He connects the dots. He sees one of his countrymen being abused and he beats the slave master to death. 

Even a prince of the realm is still subject to its laws, and so he flees into the desert, to the midianites. He starts life over, as a pauper. Finds a wife, and has children. And then one day God calls him back into Egypt. Long story Short, God tells him to march into Pharaoh’s chambers and tell him to let his people go. Moses was afraid, he felt inadequate as a public speaker. He felt like he wasn’t up to the task that God had called him to. But he takes his brother Aaron and obeys God. Marches into Pharaoh's court throws wide the doors and demands in front of everyone that Pharaoh let his people go. 

Pharaoh basically laughs at him and makes life more miserable for the Israelites. You want me to let your people go? Fine. They can work later, harder, without straw. They can make my bricks without straw. 

That’s about where we found moses tonight. His every effort was thwarted, his obedience to God made life more difficult. His own people wouldn’t follow him, and Pharaoh sort of mocked his requests. 

So here moses is, vs 22, in the slough of despond, asking God why. 

Have you ever read *Pilgrim's Progress*? Its an allegory to life, and follows a man named “Christian”. He meets a man named evangelist who points him towards the path. But along the way Christian was talking and not paying attention and fell into the Slough of Despond. He struggles, and struggles and finally is sent a helper to get him out. 

Bunyan describes the slough like this: 'This miry Slough is such a place as cannot be mended; it is the descent whither the scum and filth that attends conviction for sin doth continually run, and therefore is it called the Slough of Despond: for still as the sinner is awakened about his lost condition, there ariseth in his soul many fears, and doubts, and discouraging apprehensions, which all of them get together, and settle in this place; and this is the reason of the badness of this ground'

The scum and filth that comes with conviction of sin. Apart from Christ we are blind to our sin and wretchedness. Were unable to see just how bad we truly are. Its like those who are blind suddenly being able to see the world in all its wonder and horror. When people truly see they are dreadfully sinful, that they only thing they have contributed to their salvation is the sin that made it necessary they often fall into the slough. 

For long time Christians the trap is little different. The more clearly we see God, the more clearly we see how much further we have to go. The more we survey with clear eyes the depths of our own depravity and the distance between where we are and where God wants us to be, or the length and breadth of the task at hand and the limited contribution we have spent the years of our lives making, that can lead us to falling into this slough. The same slough Moses is in, The same one Isaiah was in last week. The same one I’m sure Hosea was in. Or Jeremiah. Or Mueller, or Cowper. 

The same one we can fall into when we hear our kids are struggling, hurting, and away from God. 

So what does God say to Moses, how does he respond to Moses plea from this slough of despond? He says: I am the Lord. 

You may remember I quoted Steven Davey earlier, when you ask God why, he usually responds by introducing himself. What's he do here? I am God he says 7 times in chapters six and seven. He reminds them they have a covenant with him. That they are his people. That he will deliver them from slavery. That he has hope for them, and a future. A promised land. 

Maybe that’s you today, and you’re hurting and worried about your kids, or for any reason you find yourself in the slough of despond. I want you guys to stop and think for a moment what God has brought you through thus far. 

Most recently was the hurricane. Most of us in this room thought we would return to our homes to find a debris field. Any of you have that happen? No? That was God at work in our life. That was the hand of God resting on our homes and protecting them from the storm. I am absolutely 100% convinced that is exactly what happened with my home during Irma last year. I’ve told most of you the story, but just as a reminder. The morning we evacuated for Irma my dog died. We buried her up on a little hill, under a tree. Afterwards I packed up the small list of things that are irreplaceable, and put them in my truck. Just before I left, I thought, you know. I should check the hurricane straps on my 10 x 40 mobile home. Just to make sure they are secure. 

They weren’t. One was almost completely rusted through. I was stunned. But I accepted it, I prayed to God and said Lord, this house is yours, you’ve let me live here for nearly 10 years, and I would love to live here for many many more. But if its your will that it should be taken from me, then I’ll praise you. But if its your will for it to remain, then I’ll praise you still. I have a little weather station mounted on a pole in my front yard. Every where around my house there were winds of over a hundred miles an hour, winds that should have destroyed a 10 x 40 mobile home with bad tie downs. Should have at least tipped it over. But you know what? My little weather station only felt winds of 30 or 40 miles an hour. What else is that but the hand of God. 

There are two big trees at either end of my house, one lost several limbs. Two of the limbs were big enough to crush my house, but somehow they fell into the wind and away from my house. One landed on my porch, and damaged it, but only a little. 

So when you’re faced with the Storm, when your struggling with WHY God is allowing you to go through these things, look back at what the Lord has brought you through, so that you can have faith going forward. Confidence in the Lords work going forwards. 

Dottie has quite a story of what the Lord has brought her through. She nearly died. Shattered leg in a freak accident fixed with a hypoallergenic metal rod that she ended up being allergic to. Caused a massive infection that nearly killed her, so they put her on antibiotics to stop the infection, but those nearly killed her too. They said that she would never walk, then that she would need a cane for the rest of her life, but do you see her with a cane? Certainly not. Why? Because God is able. Because he is God. I am God, he says to Moses 7 times. Its as though he’s saying just because you can’t see it right now does not mean I am not at work. God has plans and sometimes those plans involve bringing us to a place where we have to surrender to him. To give up. To submit fully to him. When we start asking God HOW, I think that’s when he shows us his power. Moses Asks how in vs 12, and in the very next Chapter God says I’ve got this. Let me show you a little bit of the plan. 

I’m going to harden his heart and make things so bad for the Egyptians that when you leave, they are going to give you silver and Gold, and food, and supplies for your journey home. Trump says “were going to build the wall, and mexico is going to pay for it”. God did basically that here – Fully outfitted expedition for 3 million people, and their slave masters paid for it. 

It wasn’t until moses stopped asking why, and started asking how that things changed though. 

Why is a question we all ask from time to time, but it strikes me why is a self centered question. Its not so much the why, as the rest of the words that follow. When we ask why, don’t we usually add “is this happening to me” “Why me” Why not someone else. We ask Why because our focus in on our self. I’ve said it before, but the word I is the root of all sin. I want, I think I need, I feel, all these things are at the heart of the sinful actions we take. I want to be more comfortable, well that can be idolatry. I want to be more secure can be too. I don’t want to depend on God to take care of my food needs, so I am going to steal. I don’t want to depend upon God for my happiness and enjoyment, so I’m going to drink, do drugs, have sex outside of marriage… You see where I’m going? I think Why is a phase we have to go through, and is a step as part of our growth, but its important not to get stuck there. 

When you ask God How, you assume he has a plan, you have accepted your role in it. Ok God I followed you, and now I’ve slipped into this slough and I don’t know how to get out, but I know you have a plan, and I can trust you. In *Pilgrim’s Progress*, when Christian came to that point, help came and pulled him out. Pointed him towards God, and sent him on his way. 

---

Maybe you’ve never come to a point in your spiritual life where you’ve truly asked God "how" — because you’ve never met the One who has the plan. Cowper had heard about God his whole life. God was already moving in every one of those four failures — in the rope that didn’t break right, in the friend who walked in. He was present before Cowper knew his name. That’s what the gospel is: the news that God was moving toward you before you ever moved toward him. The great I AM who said that seven times to Moses — he stepped out of eternity, took on human flesh, lived the life we couldn’t live, died the death we deserved, and walked out of a grave. So that the slough doesn’t have to be the end of your story. If you’ve never come to him in repentance and faith — if you’ve never moved from why to how — come find me after tonight.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date:** 2026-03-21

---

### 1. Verdict Title
*A Pastor's Honest Lament Sermon — Pastoral Heart Strong, Exegetical Spine Thin*

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and moving evening sermon on Moses in the slough of despond that meets discouraged people where they are and rightly points them toward the God who introduces himself in the "why," but whose exegetical engagement with Exodus 5:22–6:13 is too thin to fully bear the sermon's theological weight.

---

### 3. Berean Gate
**Scopus Test: PASS**
The sermon's thesis — that when we ask God "why," he introduces himself, and that the pathway from lament to faith runs from "why" to "how" — arises from the passage. Exodus 6's repeated "I am the LORD" declarations in response to Moses' complaint is the text's own answer to Moses' question. The sermon's main point is the text's main point.

**Authority Test: PASS**
The preacher submits to the text. The hard reality — that God's answer to Moses' "why" is not an explanation but a self-disclosure — is not softened.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Flag #5 — Exhortation is present but underdeveloped.** The sermon closes with a genuine gospel invitation and a call to move from "why" to "how." The exhortation is real, but it arrives in a single paragraph at the end after a long pastoral narrative. For an evening service with a likely discouraged audience, the call could be more specific and more sustained.

**Flag #4 — Application has generic elements.** "Look back at what the Lord has brought you through" is sound pastoral counsel but is stated generally without differentiating hearers. The personal testimonies (Hurricane Irma, Dottie) are specific, but the applicatory framework they serve remains fairly broad.

**Score cap: 69 (marginal — flags are present but mild enough to warrant consideration that actual score may fall below cap naturally)**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**
- Textual Fidelity (5/10): The sermon reads and references Exodus 5:22–6:13 but does not expound it in detail. The passage's key moves — Moses' complaint (5:22–23), God's covenant response with the fourfold "I will" (6:6–8), and the sevenfold "I am the LORD" — are gestured toward but not opened. The most important exegetical observation (God responds to "why" by saying "I AM") is the sermon's organizing principle, but the specific verses that deliver this (6:2, 6:6, 6:7, 6:8, etc.) are not walked through.
- Contextual Awareness (3/5): The background narrative (Moses' birth, the burning bush, Pharaoh's refusal, the increased burden) is helpfully provided. The redemptive-historical arc — God's covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob being the ground for his response (6:3–5) — is briefly mentioned but not developed.
- Exegetical Clarity (3/5): The "seven times 'I am the LORD'" observation is accurate (corrected in the preaching notes) and well-deployed. The distinction between "why" and "how" as a hermeneutical key is creative and pastoral, though it is inferred from the text rather than derived from it. The Fallen Condition Focus (discouragement in the face of apparent futility in God's service) is well-named.
- Logical Faithfulness (3/5): No eisegesis. The Cowper, Mueller, and Bunyan material is brought in as illustration and support, not as competing texts. The move from "why to how" is the sermon's argument — it is plausible but not textually demonstrated (Moses does ask "how" in 6:12, but God does not directly answer "how" — he continues to disclose himself and command action).

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**
- Christ-Centeredness (4/7): The gospel paragraph at the end is the most Christological content: "the great I AM who said that seven times to Moses — he stepped out of eternity, took on human flesh, lived the life we couldn't live, died the death we deserved, and walked out of a grave." This is excellent and genuinely Christological. However, it arrives only in the closing paragraph. The body of the sermon engages the text's theology of God's faithfulness without developing the Christological trajectory — how the "I AM" of Exodus becomes the "I AM" of John's Gospel.
- Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3/3): The Cowper story (meeting God through Romans 3:25), the connection from Exodus to the gospel, and the "I AM" trajectory are handled with genuine redemptive-historical instinct even if not formally named.
- Doctrinal Precision (3/4): Theologically sound. The distinction between discouragement and faithlessness, the theology of God's covenant faithfulness, and the gospel invitation are all precise. Minor issue: the Trump reference ("God did basically that here — Fully outfitted expedition for 3 million people, and their slave masters paid for it. Trump says 'we're going to build the wall, and mexico is going to pay for it'") is a pastoral illustration that dates poorly and mixes political association with theological argument. This should not be repeated in reuse.
- Confessional Alignment (3/4): Sound throughout. No confessional contradictions.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 9/15**
- Clarity of the Gospel (3/5): The gospel paragraph is clear: incarnation, sinless life, substitutionary death, resurrection. It is the best gospel paragraph in the service, but it arrives after most of the sermon is complete.
- Necessity of Christ (3/5): Christ is necessary to the closing argument ("the slough doesn't have to be the end of your story"), but he is not organically present in the body of the sermon. The "I AM" of Exodus is not connected to Christ's "I AM" claims in John — a missed opportunity that would have made him necessary throughout rather than only in the close.
- Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5): The cross appears explicitly in the final paragraph. It is clear and well-stated. The sermon does not orbit it throughout, however.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9/15**
- Heart Penetration (4/5): The sermon genuinely searches the discouraged heart. The Bunyan description of the Slough of Despond is one of the richest pastoral texts in English literature, and it is used well here. The congregational application — "Maybe you've never come to a point in your spiritual life where you've truly asked God 'how'" — is searching.
- Specificity (3/5): The Hurricane Irma personal testimony and Dottie's story are highly specific. The "kids away from God" application is specific and names a real congregational pain. The broader application ("look back at what the Lord has brought you through") is sound but somewhat generic.
- Spiritual Differentiation (2/5): The sermon addresses primarily one category of hearer: the discouraged believer in the slough of despond. The unbeliever is addressed in the closing paragraph. The backslider, the self-righteous, the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled are not distinctly addressed.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5/8**
- Call to Response (3/4): The closing gospel paragraph is a genuine call: "come find me after tonight." For an evening service, this personal invitation is appropriate and non-manipulative.
- Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4): The sermon equips for perseverance and faith in the slough, but not for holiness or witness in the specific sense. The church is comforted but not sent.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 7/9**
- Clarity of Outline (3/4): The structure is clear: introduction (Cowper) → Moses in the slough → God's "I AM" response → "why" to "how" → personal testimonies → gospel close. Not formally outlined but followable.
- Progression (3/3): The sermon builds well from discouragement to God's self-disclosure to trust.
- Unity (1/2): The "why vs. how" distinction is the unifying concept. The Trump illustration briefly disrupts the flow. The personal testimony section (Irma, Dottie) is substantial and somewhat displaces the exegetical development.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**
- Relevance (2/2): The Cowper story is masterfully used — the four failed suicide attempts followed by Romans 3:25 is one of the most powerful conversion stories in church history, and it is deployed at exactly the right moment. The Hurricane Irma testimony is genuine and specific to this congregation's experience.
- Clarity (1/2): The Bunyan citation is long but appropriate. The Trump analogy is unclear in the sermon context and should be removed.
- Memorability (1/1): "Dude can't even die" is a line that will be remembered. Cowper's hymn quoted at the moment of conversion is moving.

**8. Sermonic Force — 4/5**
- Reads as Preaching (2/2): This manuscript reads like a pastor talking to his people on a Wednesday evening. The conversational register is right for the occasion and audience.
- Conviction on the Page (1/2): The conviction is pastoral and gentle rather than sharp. Appropriate for the evening setting and the discouraged audience.
- Cadence and Breath (1/1): Good sentence variation. The short paragraph structure works well for the evening register.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* A mix. The personal testimonies occupy substantial space, and the Christ-focus comes primarily at the close. God is present throughout but Christ is mostly at the end.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* The Cowper story and the hymn produce genuine encounter. The Hurricane Irma section is testimonial rather than God-encountering.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Comforted, genuinely. Conviction is light. The discouraged hearer is met with pastoral warmth.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Cowper introduction is masterful.** Four failed suicides, then Romans 3:25, then "God Moves in a Mysterious Way" — deployed in the opening paragraph of a sermon about discouragement. This is pastoral imagination at its best. The congregation would be arrested from the first sentence.

2. **"When you ask God why, he introduces himself."** The organizing principle of the sermon — the movement from "why" to God's self-disclosure of "I AM" — is theologically sound, pastorally rich, and genuinely derived from the text. This is what makes the sermon worth developing.

3. **The gospel paragraph at the close is excellent.** "The great I AM who said that seven times to Moses — he stepped out of eternity, took on human flesh, lived the life we couldn't live, died the death we deserved, and walked out of a grave" — this is clear, Trinitarian, and Christologically precise. It is the best single paragraph in the sermon.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The exegetical spine is too thin.** The passage — Exodus 5:22–6:13 — is one of the richest in the Old Testament for covenant theology, God's self-disclosure, and the ground of redemption. The fourfold "I will" of 6:6–8 (I will bring you out; I will deliver you; I will redeem you; I will take you as my people) is barely touched. The sermon uses the text as a platform for pastoral illustrations more than as a foundation to be opened.

2. **The Trump analogy should be removed.** "Trump says 'we're going to build the wall, and mexico is going to pay for it'" imports a political association that dates the illustration and potentially alienates members of the congregation based on political identity. The underlying point — that God caused Israel's oppressors to fund the Exodus — is excellent and does not need the political illustration. The point stands on its own.

3. **Christ is largely absent from the body of the sermon.** The "I AM" of Exodus is the most direct textual pathway to Christ in the entire Old Testament (John 8:58: "Before Abraham was, I AM"), and this sermon walks up to that door and does not open it. The Christological connection would not be forced — it would be organic, redemptive-historical, and textually grounded.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher is likely unaware of how much of the sermon's theological weight is carried by the Cowper illustration rather than by the text itself. The Cowper story is so powerful that it does much of the affective and persuasive work, but if it were removed, the sermon's textual foundation would be exposed as thin. The sermon's strength is in its pastoral register and illustrations, not in its exegetical engagement.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal testimonies (Irma, Dottie) *feel* like they are grounding the sermon in specific pastoral reality — and they are. But they also displace exegetical development and produce the impression that the sermon is more specific than it is. The testimonies serve the congregation they were given to beautifully, but they are not transferable exegetical content.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Exodus 6:3–5 — "I appeared to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob, as God Almighty, but by my name the LORD I did not make myself known to them. I also established my covenant with them... I have also heard the groaning of the people of Israel..." — is the specific content of God's answer to Moses' "why." God's answer is not an explanation of the suffering — it is a covenant promise grounded in his own character and faithfulness. This is richer than "God introduces himself" alone. It says: *I remember my covenant. I hear the groaning. I will act because of who I am and what I promised.* This is the exact content that would have deepened the entire sermon's pastoral argument.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Develop the fourfold "I will" of Exodus 6:6–8.** "I will bring you out; I will deliver you; I will redeem you; I will take you as my people" — walk through these four promises slowly. Each one is the answer to a specific aspect of Moses' despair. "I will redeem you" in particular carries the entire weight of substitutionary atonement in seed form.

2. **Add the "I AM" → John 8:58 connection.** One paragraph: "The God who said 'I AM the LORD' seven times to Moses is the same one who stood in the Temple and said to the religious leaders, 'Before Abraham was, I AM' — and they picked up stones to kill him. He is the same 'I AM' who walked out of a grave. Your 'why' does not go to an abstract force. It goes to the living God who has skin in the game."

3. **Remove the Trump analogy.** Replace it with: "God outfitted a three-million-person expedition — and the oppressors paid for it. That's what it looks like when the God who says 'I AM' moves."

4. **Move the gospel paragraph earlier.** The Christological closing paragraph should come after the "I AM" section — not at the very end. Let Christ be the center of the second half of the sermon, not the last paragraph.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Exodus 5:22–6:13 is a narrative passage with embedded covenant theology. The rubric fits the text well, though the lament/discouragement genre invites a slightly gentler evaluative tone. The rubric's emphasis on distinguishing hearers (Perkins) is appropriate here: the discouraged believer, the unbeliever who hasn't yet asked "how," and the believer who is stuck in "why" are all present in a congregation hearing this passage — and the sermon addresses primarily the first and third categories. The rubric correctly identifies this gap.
