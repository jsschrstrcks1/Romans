## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Date Evaluated**: 2026-03-21
**Sermon File**: `galatians 1.md`
**Primary Text**: Galatians 1:1–12

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**"The Solas Proclaimed, But the Text Left Behind — Enthusiasm Without Exegesis"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A passionate defense of the Reformation solas that uses Galatians 1:1–12 as a launching pad rather than a governing text, delivering genuine gospel warmth while leaving Paul's actual argument — the astonishment of apostolic desertion — largely unexplored.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**PASS — with significant qualification.**

The sermon's main point (the gospel must not be added to or taken from) is present in the text. Paul does warn against "another gospel" and grounds that gospel in revelation rather than human tradition. So the gate is passed.

However, the Scopus Test reveals a drift: Paul's primary burden in Galatians 1:1–12 is not a general lesson about the five solas — it is a specific, urgent apostolic rebuke of churches that have abandoned a specific gospel they previously received, with Paul's astonishment and his defense of his apostolic authority as central moves. The sermon teaches a true doctrine that the text supports, but it does not follow the text's own argument. This is a soft pass that barely clears the gate.

**Authority Test**: The preacher submits to the text's claims and does not soften Paul's anathema. He handles the "let them be accursed" passage directly and with appropriate weight. Authority test: pass.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**No fatal flags triggered. Score is not capped.**

Notes:
- Gospel is present and clearly stated — not merely assumed.
- Christ is necessary to the argument.
- Application, while generic in places, does press toward response.
- Accessible to ordinary hearers — this is a clear strength.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (6/10)*: The sermon covers the anathema (vv. 8–9), the source of the gospel as revelation (vv. 11–12), and the "please-men" passage (v. 10). But vv. 1–5 — Paul's apostolic greeting, the self-giving of Christ for our sins, and the rescue from "the present evil age" — receive no treatment at all. The passage's opening christological declaration is the ground of everything Paul argues; it is skipped. The judaizers and circumcision issue is introduced but never exegeted from the actual text — it floats as background.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5)*: The Reformation context is deployed enthusiastically but overwhelms the first-century Galatian context. The book's flow (Paul's unique pattern of no thanksgiving, immediate rebuke) is noted — that is good — but the redemptive-historical location of Galatians in the Paul/Jerusalem tension is absent.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5)*: The meaning of "anathema" is not explained. The phrase "gospel of human origin" (v. 11) is read correctly but without development. Greek is not engaged.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5)*: No eisegesis proper, but the sermon imports Reformation categories as the primary framework rather than letting Paul's own logic govern. The five solas are Paul's theology but they are not Paul's argument in these twelve verses.
- *Fallen Condition Focus*: Partially identified — the tendency to add to or subtract from the gospel. But the specific FCF Paul addresses (churches being bewitched away from a received gospel by authoritative-sounding teachers) is not named.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (5/7)*: Christ is present and necessary. The sole mediator point (1 Tim. 2:5) is well deployed. The cross is referenced. However, the penal substitutionary core — which Paul's own greeting in vv. 3–4 supplies ("who gave himself for our sins to deliver us from the present evil age") — is passed over without comment.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3)*: The Reformation as historical moment is narrated well. The text's location in the first-century controversy is noted but not developed.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4)*: Grace, faith, works, justification — handled competently. The baptism discussion is handled with care. Mormonism/JW examples are useful.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4)*: The five solas are stated clearly. No confessional errors. The faith/works distinction is articulated correctly, with James 2 handled in proper sequence (faith first, then works).

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 11/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (4/5)*: Justification by grace through faith is stated plainly and repeatedly. Ephesians 2:8–9 is quoted correctly. The sermon passes the Synagogue Test — a devout moralist could not sit through this comfortably.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5)*: Christ as sole mediator is the sermon's sharpest point. This is well done.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5)*: The cross is present but not dwelt upon. The sermon moves toward Christ as mediator and away from false gospels, but the cross itself does not carry the emotional or theological weight of the moment. Law/gospel order is present implicitly but the law does not land long enough before grace relieves it.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15**

- *Heart Penetration (3/5)*: The closing "if you've been following another gospel" invitation is genuine. The plea to teachers not to add to or dilute the gospel has pastoral teeth. But the conscience is addressed generically — no moment where a particular category of hearer is cornered.
- *Specificity (2/5)*: The examples (Mormons, JWs, tongue-speaking, baptism) are illustrative but function as doctrinal instruction rather than conscience-pressing application. "Be careful in your teaching" (closing line) is the application — but it addresses only teachers in the room, not the whole congregation.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (3/5)*: The closing invitation distinguishes unbelievers from believers. Teachers are addressed at the end. But the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled, the doubter, and the backslider are not differentiated. Perkins' taxonomy is largely untouched.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Call to Response (2/4)*: The closing invitation is present but brief. "Come talk to me" is an invitation, not an exhortation. The "therefore" moment — where truth becomes demanded obedience — is gestured at ("be careful in your teaching") but never fully landed.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4)*: The sermon sends the congregation back to scripture as the standard and implicitly calls for discernment. But the church is not sent out as a body; the exhortation is individualistic and teacher-focused.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4)*: The sermon has a recognizable flow: intro (Galatians' importance) → Luther/Reformation background → judaizers/false gospels → five solas → mediator point → closing appeal. It is followable.
- *Progression (2/3)*: The sermon builds to the mediator argument, which is its sharpest point, then diffuses in the working-out-of-salvation section. The Luther quotes appended at the end disrupt any sermonic landing.
- *Unity (1/2)*: The people-group feature, the Luther insult generator tangent, and the broad historical canvas compete with the passage itself for the sermon's center of gravity.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**

- *Relevance (2/2)*: The Mormonism, JW, and baptism examples will land in this congregation. The reformation history is engaging.
- *Clarity (1/2)*: The Luther anecdote is colorful but functions as entertainment rather than illumination of the text.
- *Memorability (0/1)*: No single image or illustration is likely to be carried home. The Luther insult quotes at the end — while verified as authentic — function as an appendix, not a landing point.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2)*: Oral cadence is strong. Direct address is present ("someone define it for me," "what are works?"). This sounds like a man talking to a room.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2)*: The preacher's conviction about the gospel is evident. But the manuscript's density of Scripture-grounded assertions is uneven — large sections of Reformation history and illustration carry the weight where the text should.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1)*: The sermon does not build to a clear climax. The closing quote from Luther ("condemned the holy gospel and replaced it with the teaching of the dragon from hell") is the strongest line — but it appears as a repeated citation rather than a sermonic climax earned by the preceding argument.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* Primarily Christ — the mediator passage and the solas keep Christ central. Yes.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Mostly ideas, organized around a genuine conviction. The sermon informs more than it confronts.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Largely informed. The closing appeal offers comfort to the seeking, but the middle sections are more doctrinal survey than pastoral press.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Sole Mediator argument (1 Tim. 2:5)**: The clearest, sharpest point in the sermon. The cascade from "Who can mediate? Not Muhammad, not the governing body of the JWs, only Christ" is well-structured and lands with force.
2. **Gospel clarity**: Ephesians 2:8–9 deployed correctly. The faith/works/James 2 sequence is handled carefully, avoiding both legalism and antinomianism. This is not a given; many sermons fumble this.
3. **Accessibility**: The sermon is genuinely understandable. A new believer, a high schooler, a first-time attender — all can follow the main point. This is not accidental; it reflects a pastoral instinct to communicate clearly.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Vv. 1–5 are entirely skipped**: Paul's christological greeting — "who gave himself for our sins to deliver us from the present evil age, according to the will of our God and Father" — is the doctrinal ground of everything Paul argues. Skipping it means the sermon argues *for* the gospel from the Reformation rather than from the passage's own christological foundation. This is the sermon's most significant expositional failure.
2. **Application does not differentiate hearers**: The closing appeal addresses seekers and teachers. The bulk of the congregation — ordinary believers who may be adding cultural traditions to their gospel understanding, or who are passive about the gospel they claim to hold — is not addressed. "Be careful in your teaching" speaks only to those who teach.
3. **No exhortation to the body**: The sermon ends with an invitation (come talk to me) and a warning to teachers. There is no corporate "therefore" — no moment where the congregation as a body is called to a specific response arising from the text. The sermon informs and invites; it does not summon.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher does not appear to see that the Luther material — however enthusiastically deployed and thoroughly verified — is doing theological work the text should be doing. Luther is being used as the warrant for the sermon's claims when Paul's own apostolic authority (which he defends in this very passage) should be that warrant. The irony is significant: a sermon about the authority of Scripture ends up grounding its argument more in Luther than in the text.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The five solas section *feels* like the sermon's strongest theological moment. It is well-organized, the Latin terms are explained, the logic is clear. But this is Reformation theology *applied to the text*, not the text's own argument exegeted and proclaimed. The strength is real — the content is accurate — but it is the strength of a systematic theology lecture inserted into a sermon, not the strength of Paul's own argument proclaimed from Paul's own structure.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Paul's astonishment in v. 6 — "I am astonished that you are so quickly deserting him who called you in the grace of Christ" — is the emotional hinge of the entire passage. Paul is not calmly teaching doctrine; he is expressing pastoral shock at a community he loves abandoning the gospel that transformed them. The sermon could have opened with this note: *What would it take to astonish an apostle?* That question drives straight into both the weight of the gospel and the gravity of abandoning it. Instead, the passage's emotional register — urgent, alarmed, grieved — is entirely absent from the sermon.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach vv. 1–5 before vv. 8–9**: The gospel Paul defends in the anathema is the gospel he announces in the greeting. "Who gave himself for our sins to deliver us from the present evil age" is the substance of the gospel at stake. Exposit this before explaining why another gospel is damnable.
2. **Use Paul's astonishment as the sermon's hook**: Replace the Reformation history opener with v. 6 — "I am astonished." Ask: what would Paul say if he walked into a church today that had drifted? Then stay in the text long enough to show what the drift was and why it was fatal.
3. **Differentiate the closing application**: Add a section after the teacher warning that speaks directly to the ordinary church member: "Some of you are not teachers — you never lead a Bible study. But you do share opinions about spiritual things. You tell your neighbor what you think it takes to be right with God..." This addresses the person in the pew who may be quietly adding conditions to grace without knowing it.
4. **Add an exhortatory "therefore" moment**: End the sermon with a corporate call — not just an invitation for seekers and a warning to teachers. "This week, when someone asks you how to be right with God, what will you say? Paul tells you. By grace, through faith, in Christ alone. Say that. Nothing less. Nothing more."
5. **Trim or relocate the Luther insults appendix**: As sermon content, the four Luther quotes at the end of the manuscript deflate any sermonic conclusion. If they serve a purpose (demonstrating Luther's vigor), place one inside the body as illustration. Remove them from the conclusion.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The preacher's genuine warmth, the accessibility of the sermon, and the clear gospel presentation prevent the dead orthodoxy diagnostic from firing. This is not bloodless preaching — it simply needs more text and more conscience.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries the weight of genuine conviction about the gospel's uniqueness and the danger of distortion — but it does not yet rise to the eternal gravity Paul himself brings to these twelve verses, where the stakes are not merely doctrinal accuracy but the eternal souls of communities being lured from the only Savior.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Galatians 1:1–12 is a epistolary rebuke — a genre with its own rhetorical logic (salutation, occasion, thesis, grounds). The rubric applies well here; this is expository preaching territory with clear applicatory potential. No genre strain. The passage is well-served by the full rubric framework.
