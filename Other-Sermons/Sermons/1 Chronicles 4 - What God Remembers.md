# 1 Chronicles 4 — What God Remembers

*Raw notes — 1 Chronicles 4:5–10 (Jabez)*

---

## Introduction — The Prayer of Jabez

There has been a lot of discussion about Jabez over the last few years. A lot of people bought the book *The Prayer of Jabez* by Bruce Wilkinson. Some didn't think it was descriptive of how we should pray. Others say it's the best thing they've ever done.

I'm on the fence between the two.

I talked a while back about context — historical, geographical, cultural, and the other possible contexts the Bible can have. With this passage, a lot of that background information is simply not there. Which makes it tricky ground.

All we know about Jabez is that he was more honorable than his brothers. Honorable from God's perspective? Honorable in what way? The semantic range of the Hebrew word for honorable (כָּבֵד, *kaved*) includes a number of things — from wealthy to genuinely deserving of honor, to something dull and heavy. The same root can be used for glory, for burden, and for something difficult to move. So even the one adjective we have about this man carries ambiguity.

We also know that there was a city named Jabez — mentioned in 1 Chronicles 2:55. Whether it was named after this man or not, we don't know. Likely it was. But there is no proof one way or the other.

So what we end up with is a man whose circumstances have been lost to history. Under the best circumstances, it's hard to say exactly why God does what He does. This time, all we know of the circumstances will fit in a thimble.

Let's read our passage, and then talk some more about Jabez.

**1 Chronicles 4:5–10 (ESV)**

> Ashhur, the father of Tekoa, had two wives, Helah and Naarah; Naarah bore him Ahuzzam, Hepher, Temeni, and Haahashtari. These were the sons of Naarah. The sons of Helah: Zereth, Izhar, and Ethnan. Koz fathered Anub, Zobebah, and the clans of Aharhel the son of Harum. Jabez was more honorable than his brothers; and his mother called his name Jabez, saying, "Because I bore him in pain." Jabez called upon the God of Israel, saying, "Oh that you would bless me and enlarge my border, and that your hand might be with me, and that you would keep me from harm so that it might not bring me pain!" And God granted what he asked.

## Why Is This Even in the Bible?

I like this passage for us right now, and for a few reasons.

We are back in a part of the Bible that is difficult to read. So-and-so begat so-and-so who begat so-and-so and so-and-so. It can be difficult to trudge through. When you see passages like this in Scripture, you have to ask yourself: *Why did God put this in His Word to us?* If the Bible is His love letter to us, are the genealogies a rabbit trail?

I don't think that for a moment. It may seem that way. It's easy to assume. But I think all of these endless genealogies tell us something about who God is. There are at least three things.

---

### 1. God Remembers — Even People Nobody Else Remembers

Imagine you were in God's place. Generation after generation of your children come and go — born, have kids, and die. Generation after generation for something like ten thousand years.

I don't remember everything I read in the newspaper yesterday. I voted this past Tuesday, and I had done some research ahead of time into each candidate. The big positions were easier to remember — governor, attorney general, ag commissioner. But some of the smaller but no less important positions were harder. Especially judges. Having good, godly judges matters enormously. In my district we had something like twelve or fifteen judges running for five or six positions. With the statewide races, you hear more than you want to about everyone. But the judges — unless you are actively seeking out information about each one, you just don't know.

So I spent an hour or so reading about each, praying, and making a list before I went to the precinct. If I didn't have those names written down, I'd never remember them standing in the voting booth.

But God has a better memory — better knowledge — than I do.

We believe that God's Word is God-breathed. 2 Timothy 3:16 says so. We use the phrase "verbal plenary inspiration" to describe that process — God worked through the personalities and words of human authors to give us exactly what He intended.

Verse 6 here talks about a man named Tekoa. Who was Tekoa? We don't even know. His significance has fallen out of the collective knowledge of mankind. Maybe he was the ancient equivalent of a Nobel laureate, or a great athlete, or a master engineer. We have no idea. Maybe he was someone the world noticed, or maybe not. All we know right now is that Tekoa was something special to God. Someone God wanted to remember. Someone God felt was worthy of inclusion in His love letter to mankind.

Tekoa may look like a speed bump to us — some random name on the way to more interesting things. But to God, he mattered.

To God you matter. To God I matter. He chooses to remember us too. He loved us enough to send His Son to die for us on the cross. If I had a son who was going to die a brutal death to save a group of people, I think I would want to get to know those people. I'd memorize their names. I'd watch their lives from as close as I could get, so that I could know what kind of impact my son's blood had purchased. Was his blood well spent?

God doesn't worry the way we would. But the point is: Tekoa mattered to God. And so do you.

---

### 2. God Is Orderly

The second thing these lists tell us about God is that He is orderly.

He doesn't only know everything — He remembers things in order. He sat there and whispered name after name after name into the ear of the writer of Chronicles. Tribe by tribe, family by family, generation by generation. Sometimes He remembered the mothers of these children as well.

There is an order to it.

If you were depending on my brain to repeat back even a chapter's worth of these names, I could probably give you most of them with some practice — but I'd be surprised if I got them all in the right sequence without help. God is an orderly God. He is an all-knowing God. He is a loving God. And He remembers even people like Tekoa that the rest of mankind has forgotten.

Tekoa may not have done much that was great in the eyes of the world. But he did something worth God's attention. I'd rather be remembered by God than by man any day. If I'm going to make a difference, let it be for God. If my life is going to count, let it count for God. If the few years we have on this earth are going to have an impact, let them have an impact for Him.

---

### 3. These Lists Help Prove the Accuracy and Authority of Scripture

It strikes me that many archaeologists still use the Bible as an authority on where to look for historical sites.

There are two basic kinds of proof for the reliability of the Bible. The first set is internal — things that separate writers of Scripture, who didn't know each other and usually didn't read each other, said that support each other. Person A lived five thousand years ago and said Person B would live and accomplish X, Y, and Z. Person C, who lived three thousand years ago and never read Person A's writings, wrote about all the things Person B did — and Person B accomplished exactly X, Y, and Z. The Bible supports itself internally. And at no point does it contradict itself. I would sooner doubt my own sanity than I would the integrity of the Bible.

There has also been a lot of external evidence found to support Scripture. I was in London about ten years ago on a missions trip. I visited the British Museum. One of the most remarkable things in their collection was a gold and silver figurine — a ram standing against a thicket, intricately hand-crafted. The connection to Genesis 22 is unmistakable when you see it. Whether that specific piece is directly tied to Abraham's story or not is debated among scholars — the excavator Leonard Woolley named it "Ram in a Thicket" after Genesis 22, though the dates and direct connection remain discussion points in archaeology. But what it does show is that the world of the ancient Near East that Scripture describes was real — the materials, the craftsmanship, the religious symbolism all line up with the text.

In case after case, what archaeology unearths almost always says the same thing the Bible says. The Bible is not a science textbook, but where it speaks of science, it has done so accurately. It is not a geography textbook, but where it speaks of geography, archaeologists use it today to locate things the Bible describes — because it is accurate as a source. It is not a history textbook, but where it speaks of history, we can know that it is speaking with authority.

These genealogies — these long lists of names — are part of that record. They are the scaffolding of human history, preserved by the God who watched it all unfold.

## The Passage: Jabez (vv. 9–10)

So not only do these lists tell us that God is precise and orderly, and that God is all-knowing and remembering, but they tell us something about who God sometimes chooses to bless.

**1 Chronicles 4:9–10 (ESV)**

> "Jabez was more honorable than his brothers; and his mother called his name Jabez, saying, 'Because I bore him in pain.' Jabez called upon the God of Israel, saying, 'Oh that you would bless me and enlarge my border, and that your hand might be with me, and that you would keep me from harm so that it might not bring me pain!' And God granted what he asked."

Jabez's name was a play on the Hebrew word for pain and sorrow. In a day and age when you were meant to grow into the meaning of your name, that's quite a millstone to carry from birth. Imagine what it would have been like as a kid: *Hi, what's your name?* Pain and hurt.

As I mentioned in the introduction, we don't know the backstory. He may have had a rough life and proved his faithfulness in the midst of it. He may have had any number of things happen. All we know is what Scripture says — that he was more honorable than his brothers.

I don't think that means dull and heavy. Not in light of what he does next.

---

### Hebrew Poetry Here — General to Specific

Any of you like ice cream? My mom loves ice cream — especially the strange varieties. We'd watch cooking shows together, and nothing made her happier than when they tossed something bizarre into an ice cream machine. We saw pork ice cream on there one time, and she about lost her mind.

The reason I bring that up is that ice cream is a good way to think about what the Hebrew poetry is doing here.

There are a couple of statements made in Jabez's prayer, and then each one is repeated and made specific. The initial statement is general. The repeated statement is specific.

*"Lord, bless me"* — that's general.
*"and enlarge my border"* — that's the specific form. You want to be blessed in a particular way: more land. More land meant more livestock, more grazing, more farming, more room for your family to grow. It was the best kind of wealth you could have at that point in time because it enabled everything else.

*"Let your hand be with me"* — general.
*"that I might not feel pain"* — specific.

But it's deeper than just "I don't want to feel bad." It's as though Jabez feels the weight of his name. The doom of it. It's one of those names that's a little too on the nose.

Like if you were to call your child Depression today. Or Suicide. Or Serial Killer. The kind of emotional baggage a name like that would carry today — that's what this name carried then. It was a dark and bleak word. So when Jabez asks that he not be caused harm, he is saying something like: *Look, God. I have been saddled with this bad name, and I would love nothing more than to not live up to what it means.* He knows the only way out of his problem is through God.

That's still true today. No matter what the problem is — you and God are the majority. The only way through it is with His help. There is no other way.

## What Made This Prayer Effective?

I keep thinking about James 5:16, which says the prayer of a righteous person has great power as it is working.

**James 5:16b (ESV)**

> "The prayer of a righteous person has great power as it is working."

I think that verse may be part of the key to what happened when Jabez prayed. God moved in response for at least three reasons:

1. The prayer was **humble** — he did not demand, he asked.
2. The prayer expressed **faith** — he went to God, not to his own resources.
3. The prayer acknowledged **dependence** — he believed the only way through his problem was through God.

When you are having troubles in life, that is exactly where you need to be. On your knees, humbly talking to God, acknowledging that He is the only way you will manage to get through.

But all of that starts with a relationship with God. As we talked about at the start — you matter to God. He knew the total measure of you, and decided He would send His Son to die for you.

### Corrections from Raw Notes

**"Psalms speaks of the globe of the earth" — flagged and removed.**
The verse the preacher is thinking of is almost certainly **Isaiah 40:22 (ESV)**: *"It is he who sits above the circle of the earth, and its inhabitants are like grasshoppers."* The Hebrew word is חוּג (*chug*) — "circle, vault, or arch." This is not in Psalms, and the word is "circle" not "globe." Whether the word implies a spherical earth is debated among scholars; the verse clearly suggests a curved or domed earth rather than a flat one, but the full spherical reading is an inference. The passage does not appear in Psalms. Recommend: if this point is made from the pulpit, cite Isaiah 40:22 precisely, use the word "circle" as the text says, and present it as suggestive rather than definitive.

**"Ram in a Thicket" dates — flagged.**
The preacher says the British Museum piece is dated "about 2000 years BC" and connects it to the time of Abraham ("right in that same ball park"). The actual museum pieces (there are two — one at the British Museum, one at the Penn Museum in Philadelphia) were excavated by Leonard Woolley from the Royal Cemetery at Ur and are dated by archaeologists to approximately **2600–2400 BC**. Abraham is generally dated to approximately 2000–1800 BC. The figurine predates Abraham by at least several hundred years, possibly longer.

Woolley himself named the piece "Ram in a Thicket" after Genesis 22 — but this was a poetic connection by the excavator, not a scholarly conclusion about the object's relationship to Abraham's specific story. The piece does powerfully illustrate the cultural world of Mesopotamia from which Abraham came; the visual connection to the Genesis narrative is real and striking. But the date discrepancy is significant enough to flag. Do not present the dates as matching from the pulpit. The safer and still compelling point: the ancient Near East that Scripture describes was real, and archaeology continues to confirm its world.

**James 5:16b translation** — raw notes quoted KJV: "the prayers of a righteous man availeth much." ESV: "The prayer of a righteous person has great power as it is working." Corrected in body.

### The Prayer of Jabez (Book)
Bruce Wilkinson, *The Prayer of Jabez: Breaking Through to the Blessed Life* (Multnomah, 2000). The book sold over nine million copies. The primary criticism was that it read a one-verse prayer out of its genealogical context and built a prosperity-adjacent "prayer formula" around it. The primary defense was that it encouraged people to pray boldly and specifically. The preacher's "I'm on the fence" framing is accurate to the genuine interpretive debate and is honest from the pulpit.

### Hebrew: כָּבֵד (*kaved*) — "More Honorable"
The root כ-ב-ד has a wide semantic range: heavy, weighty, dull, rich/wealthy, glorious, honored. In context, "more honorable than his brothers" most likely carries the sense of moral and perhaps social distinction — not wealth, given the contrast with the name Jabez (pain/sorrow). The preacher's observation about the semantic range is accurate. The point that the context points toward genuine honor rather than mere wealth is sound.

### The Name יַעְבֵּץ (*Ya'betz*)
The name is a wordplay on עֹצֶב (*'otsev*) — "pain, sorrow." The mother's explanation in v.9 — "because I bore him in pain" (*be'otsev*) — confirms the wordplay. Whether this indicated a painful birth, difficult personal circumstances, or was intended as a curse of sorts is unknown. The preacher's pastoral handling (a dark name to carry, a millstone) is exegetically appropriate.

### Verbal Plenary Inspiration
Reference to 2 Timothy 3:16 as the basis for verbal plenary inspiration is accurate. "God whispered into the ears of the author" is a pastoral popularization of the doctrine — theologically fine, not a misstatement.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel section is absent.** This is not a nuanced weakness — it is a structural hole. The placeholder *"[Gospel presentation here.]"* marks the exact center of what makes a sermon evangelical. The warmth of "God loves you and sent His Son" without the mechanism of justification by faith is not the gospel — it is a premise for the gospel. The sermon as written cannot convert the lost or assure the doubting.

A sermon on the prayer of Jabez — a man who asked boldly and received — is an almost inexhaustible resource for calling the congregation to actual, specific prayer. That call never comes.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher may not see that the "fence" posture on Wilkinson is actually a missed opportunity to press the applicatory distinction clearly. The sermon's honest middle position on *The Prayer of Jabez* could serve as the exegetical frame for the whole message — teaching the congregation *how* to read a text rather than *what formula to extract from it*. That hermeneutical lesson would give the sermon its spine and resolve the structural looseness.

The preacher also may not see that the very genealogical context he handles so well is itself an argument for the sovereignty of God over human history — which, if pressed, becomes a natural bridge to the doctrines of grace and to Christ as the culmination of the genealogical record (Matthew 1 and Luke 3 both begin where Chronicles ends). That connection is not forced — it is built into the genre.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The hermeneutical candor (admitting what we don't know about Jabez) reads as strength — and it is a genuine virtue. But it can function as a shield against the applicatory pressure the text still demands. "We don't know the backstory" is true. It is also not the last word. The text still makes a clear claim: a man called on God in honesty and faith, and God answered. That claim does not require backstory to be preached with force. The humility about what we don't know should not suppress the authority of what we do know.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
The name *Jabez* — pain, sorrow — is the sermon's richest vein. The preacher touches it but does not mine it. A man whose very name was a sentence of suffering prayed that his life would not fulfill its namesake, and God answered. This is an argument for prayer in the face of what feels predetermined. It is also a trajectory toward Isaiah 53 — where the Suffering Servant takes the name "pain and sorrow" upon Himself so that we need not be defined by it. That connection is not artificial typology; it arises from the word itself. The sermon reaches the edge of this and turns back.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Complete the gospel section before delivery, and let it arise organically from the Jabez motif.** Jabez prayed to be delivered from the doom of his name. The gospel is that Christ bore the doom of our name — sin, condemnation, death — so that we bear it no longer. This is not an artificial gospel injection; it is the theological completion of the passage's own argument.

2. **Add a specific exhortation at the close.** After the gospel section, call the congregation to pray like Jabez — specifically, honestly, with acknowledged dependence. Name what they might be carrying. Give them permission to ask large. But also distinguish: the converted pray differently than the unconverted. Address both.

3. **Cut or relocate the apologetics section (archaeology, biblical accuracy).** This material belongs in a separate teaching on the reliability of Scripture. In its current location, it stalls the sermon's movement toward Jabez and costs the application sections their time. If one point must be retained, keep the "God remembers even Tekoa" point — it is native to this sermon. The internal consistency argument and the British Museum digression are not.

4. **Differentiate your hearers.** The sermon currently speaks to one kind of person: someone going through difficulty who needs to be encouraged to pray. Add a brief address to (a) the person who has stopped praying because they believe God won't listen, (b) the person who prays but only in generalities and wonders why they feel distant from God, and (c) the self-sufficient person who has not prayed because they haven't yet felt the need. The text addresses all three.

5. **Anchor the "God remembers" point in covenant theology.** God does not remember Tekoa the way a human might remember a noteworthy person. He remembers him because Tekoa is part of the covenant people. Divine remembrance in Scripture (see Genesis 8:1, Exodus 2:24) is covenantal action, not biographical recall. This would give Point 1 its proper theological weight and connect it to how God remembers us — not through sentiment but through the blood of the covenant.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon is too warm and too incomplete for this diagnostic. Dead orthodoxy requires a high theology score paired with applicatory inertness. This sermon's theology is not yet high — the gospel section is absent. The pastoral voice is genuinely warm. The failure here is incompleteness, not coldness.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
There are genuine flickers of glory in this manuscript — particularly in the meditation on Tekoa, the forgotten man who mattered to God — but the sermon has not yet been brought to the temperature where eternity becomes palpable; what is warm must become weighty before it can bear the full gravity of what it means to be remembered by God.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This passage is a genealogical text with an embedded narrative fragment — a genre that genuinely strains a preaching rubric designed for propositional or argumentative texts. The rubric bends rather than breaks here. The "Fallen Condition Focus" (Chapell) is harder to identify in genealogical material, but the preacher has effectively located it in the weight of Jabez's name and the implied helplessness before God. The lower scores in gospel centrality and exhortation are partly attributable to the sermon's incomplete state, not entirely to the genre. Where the rubric applies most cleanly — exposition, structure, sermonic voice — the diagnosis is reliable.
