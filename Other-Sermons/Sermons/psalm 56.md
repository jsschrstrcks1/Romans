# Psalm 56 — Is Jesus Worth It

---

## The Slough of Despond

John Bunyan, a believer in Jesus Christ who lived in the 1600s, wrote one of the best books of all time after the Bible. It's called *Pilgrim's Progress*. Bunyan was in jail for over a decade, and while in horrible prison conditions he penned this classic work of literature — an allegory of the Christian life. It follows a man named Christian who meets another man named Evangelist, who tells him the city he lives in will be destroyed. Weighed down by his burden of sin, Christian and his companion Pliable set out for the Wicket Gate where all true believers start their journey. Along the way they encounter a huge scar in the ground they call the Slough of Despond. This ugly pit was full of quicksand and bogged down anyone who tried to cross it. There was no way around. One could only cross. So Pliable and Christian try, and immediately sink to their necks. Christian's burden — his sin — makes him sink all the faster, and makes it even harder to get out. Pliable, disgusted and angry, wiggles free and runs off back the way he came.

I gotta think in that moment, Christian had to be asking himself — is this worth it? Is following Christ worth going through dark, nasty pits of despond? Worth going through troubles that threaten to overtake and kill him? He struggles, and struggles, and struggles, and is just about to give in when the King sends a man named Help to pull him out and guide him to the Wicket Gate. Help explains to him that there were steps all along, placed right through the middle of the Slough by direction of the Lawgiver, and if he had but looked, he would have found them. Christian asked why, if God were truly good, He hadn't sealed off the Slough, had it filled in so that other travelers wouldn't be endangered. Help replies that the King could fill it in, but that it's important that the travelers cross the Slough. To learn to depend on the help of God. To learn to be careful to follow the path the King laid out. To cause unwary travelers to ask themselves the question — is Jesus worth it?

---

We watched the movie *Insanity of God* a while back, and it was absolutely amazing. It was heartbreaking, convicting, and inspiring all at once. In a nutshell, this is the question Nik Ripken had to find an answer to. Nik — a graduate of Southern Baptist Theological Seminary, a missionary of more than two decades on the mission field — had to come to grips with this. He knew he was willing to give his life for the Lord. He and his wife had come to grips with that a long time prior. But when his own son was taken from him, and when he found himself drowning in the Slough of Despond, he had to ask himself — is Jesus worth it?

## David Fleeing to the Philistines

We're going to spend most of our time tonight in Psalm 56. While you're turning there, I'll set the stage a little bit. David is on the run from King Saul. He's desperate enough to flee to Gath — deep in Philistine territory. The Philistines, you'll remember, are the wicked and evil people that plagued the people of Israel for centuries — clear through the judges, and I think nearly to the end of David's life. You remember Samson — the strongest man who ever lived, who struck down a thousand Philistines with the jawbone of a donkey. The woman he loved, Delilah, convinced him to reveal the secret of his strength and had his hair shaved off while he slept, allowing the Philistines to gouge out his eyes. They brought him out to mock him at a great feast in the temple of their god Dagon. But they didn't realize his hair had grown back and his strength had returned, and so he grasped the two great pillars that held up the temple and pulled them down, killing more Philistines in his death than he had in his life.

This same wicked nation — David is now among them, and they've seized him. Let's read.

---

So you see — he's being chased. He's being attacked. They injure his cause, conspire, lurk, stalk him. They want to take his life — verse 6. This is a pretty dire situation, and it makes me wonder what was being said. They were twisting his words, trying to bring about his ruin — verse 5.

In the next psalm, Saul is pursuing him, and he says they are like ravenous beasts. Like lions.

Which reminds me of *Pilgrim's Progress*. After the Slough, Christian has to climb this tall hill, and he reaches the top exhausted. But in order to get inside Palace Beautiful, he has to brave a pair of lions. He doesn't know they are chained. He doesn't know that if he just sticks to the middle of the path he will be fine. All he knows is two great big, huge, hungry, roaring lions are right there, acting like they are going to kill him if he gets too close. He can't see the chains that restrain them. All he knows is that his path lies between the lions. He has to ask himself — is Jesus worth it? — as he passes between them. On the other side, he can see there was never any danger. But until he mustered up the faith, all he could see were their teeth and claws.

I love the allegorical nature of *Pilgrim's Progress*, and I love how true to life the situations are.

When he faced the Slough — it wasn't until he called out for help that the King sent Help. With the lions, the battle was already won. But he couldn't see that. All he knew was there were hungry lions ahead of him, and that he might die so that he might live for Christ.

## Stories from the Persecuted Church

There was another story about a man who had grown up without a father around. His father, you see, had become a Christian. He started reading the Bible to his kids. His kids started telling their friends about the Bible, and the stories in the Bible, and before long their parents wanted to hear these things too. The man tried hard to turn people away. He wasn't a pastor, he would argue. He just wanted to worship God and for his kids to know God's Word. They wanted the same, and they eventually argued him into allowing them to attend. Before long, over 70 people were meeting together, worshipping God together, studying Scripture together, and the police couldn't turn a blind eye any longer. So they arrested the man and took him to jail.

His boy was just a few months old. Ten years later, they encountered a man wrapped in a sheet. Beaten nearly lifeless, naked except for the sheet — and right away they knew who he was. The boy had grown up seeing pictures of his father. The mother wept and held her husband, and the little boy who'd never met his father hugged him tight and declared that he was so proud of his father for following Jesus and not turning his back.

What a slough to travel through. Talk about being surrounded by lions. But in his mind, and in the mind of that precious ten-year-old boy, the answer was a resounding yes — Jesus is worth it.

---

Another story from the movie. Nik was traveling the world to see how Christianity survived in hostile places. He found a contact in China. He was to be at a certain meat market at a certain time, wearing a certain color shirt. At the appointed time he was met by a Chinese individual who led him to a van, and drove him deep into China where he met with Chinese church leaders. The first day he interviewed four men, and the four men were so blessed by it that they insisted the interviews take place in front of everyone. So the next day they had a series of interviews. They pulled him up on stage and let him ask his questions. By the end of the day, the Chinese pastors wanted to ask Nik some questions, and he relented.

The number one most requested question — the thing that was burning in the hearts and minds of all the Chinese believers — was: has the Gospel of Jesus made it to any other countries? Do free people know about Jesus?

He told them the truth, and named off the countries he could think of that knew about Jesus, and then he told them about Somalia. About how he had been a missionary there. How his missionary work had cost him the life of his son, and how when he left Somalia, his family were the last known believers in that country. How he felt defeated.

He was tired, and went straight to bed. The next morning he heard people raising a commotion, and ran outside, and found his interpreter, who told him to shut up and listen. Which amused him, since he didn't speak Chinese. But when he finally listened, and as they walked through the ranks of these pastors, he realized they were on their knees praying for Somalia. So moved were they by his story that as a group they had committed to getting up an hour earlier than normal so they could spend an hour on their knees praying for people in places like Somalia — for the rest of their lives, or until Somalia had been reached completely with the gospel.

When was the last time we were so moved by anything that we were willing to give up sleep?

---

May it cost me all that I am — I want to follow Christ. May it cost me my life. The life of my family. The life of any children I may one day have — I want to follow Christ. Whether that is here, or there, or China, or Iraq, or wherever He may lead me — I want to follow Christ.

In the words of David — what can man do to me? It's as though he says: so what? You can take my life, but you can't separate me from my Savior. You can hurt my body, but you can't take my joy. You can maim and disfigure me, but my trust is in the Lord, where my help comes from. Verse 10 — "whose word I praise." And when everything conspires against me, this is how I will know my God is for me. What can a mere man do in light of the good God we serve?

## The Invitation

Let me ask you this. Have you come to a point in your spiritual life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins? Have you said, "Yes, Jesus — You're worth it"? Because that's where it all starts.

And I think decisions for yourself are easy. But let's go to a hard place. What about your finances? Is Jesus worth a paltry ten percent? Or harder still — is Jesus worth risking a relationship with your kids? Because I know some of us have family, kids, maybe grandkids that don't know Jesus as their Savior. I know we all have friends, and family, loved ones that don't know Jesus as their Savior. Is Jesus worth risking those relationships? Do you have enough faith that Jesus is real to risk offending them with the gospel?

I hope so.

If you want help learning to share the gospel, come talk to me. Talk to Kenny Duffield. Talk to my dad. We can help you learn. Movies are a great segue. There are maybe five or six stories, and all movies and books are based upon one of those. One of those stories is the story of Christ — a hero that gives it all up, or nearly all, for the greater good of another, or of mankind. If you can pick that up and run straight to the cross, you'll be fine. You've all spent a fair amount of time in church. You've heard the Scriptures. And in that moment, when God puts someone in your path that needs the gospel, He pulls those things out of you.

Kurt — you had a chance to share the gospel with a brother before he passed a while back. Are these things I'm saying true? Have you ever had training to share the gospel? But didn't God give you the words anyway?

Scripture says God's Word is written on our hearts, and I have experienced this to be true. When I was a little guy, the school I attended had us memorize different Bible verses. And if we could repeat them back, we got a colored card with the Scripture and reference on it to keep. I had scores of these cards. I can't sit there and repeat them all from memory thirty-some years later — but when I need them, they are there.

I was in the UK sharing the gospel with an English convert to Islam. He was forceful, almost mean, with the things he had to say about Christians. But the rest of the team was talking to people that were less ferocious and more receptive. So since he was the loudest mouth around, I decided to talk to him directly, and keep him from reinforcing the weak around him — and it was amazing to see God work.

He'd run off at the mouth about how Christians are this and Christians are that, and God would put a Bible verse and reference in my mouth. I'd hand him the Bible and read it out loud with him and to him. He would make another accusation, and God would give me another verse. It was like an epic sword fight from some old movie. In the end, he packed up his things and left. Some of those verses, I hadn't thought about more than once or twice since kindergarten. But God brought them right back to mind when I needed them. They were written on my heart, and when I didn't know what to say, our Lord patiently read them back to me.

---

God is good. All the time.

I challenge you this week to give up a little bit of your morning — maybe get up a little early, or go to bed a little late — and pray for the people in your life that don't know Christ. Because I'm here to tell you that hell is hot, and heaven is sweet. Everyone will go to one of those places. What about you? What about your kids? Your family, your loved ones, your friends? Where will they go?

Dad is going to come up here and sing, and while he does — maybe God is calling you to take that first step of faith, and find salvation. If so, come talk to me.

Maybe He's calling you to be baptized. If so, come talk to me.

Maybe you're looking for a church home, and would like to join and be a part of the decisions and direction of the church. Come talk to me.

Or maybe — just maybe — God is calling you to go on a foreign missions trip. Maybe short term, or maybe long term. If so, I want you to come talk to me. I want to take a trip next year, and would like to talk to any who are interested in going to another country for a week to tell people about Jesus. To pray for people, to maybe help meet some physical needs. If that's you, come talk to me.

**Pilgrim's Progress Corrections (verified against Bunyan's text):**
- ⚠️ "1500s" — **corrected to "1600s."** Bunyan lived 1628–1688. His dates fall entirely in the 17th century.
- ⚠️ "Slough of Despair" — **corrected to "Slough of Despond"** throughout. "Despond" (despondency) is Bunyan's word. "Despair" is a common misremembering.
- ⚠️ "Christian indignantly demanded" — **softened to "asked."** In the text, Christian asks Help a question about the Slough; he does not demand anything. Help provides the explanation.
- ⚠️ "Pliable… when Christian calls to him for help he runs off" — **adjusted.** In Bunyan's text, Pliable becomes disgusted and angry, scrambles out on the side nearest the City of Destruction, and abandons the journey entirely. He is not asked for help and refuses; he simply deserts.
- ⚠️ "great steaks in the ground, or chains" — **corrected to "chains" only.** Bunyan's text says "The lions were chained, but he saw not the chains." There are no "stakes in the ground" anywhere in the passage. The porter Watchful tells Christian to keep to the middle of the path.
- ⚠️ "Palace Wonderful" — **corrected to "Palace Beautiful"** (Bunyan's name for the house past the lions).

**Samson/Delilah Corrections (verified against Judges 14–16 ESV):**
- ⚠️ "his evil wife" — **corrected.** Delilah was not Samson's wife. Judges 16:4 says "he loved a woman in the Valley of Sorek, whose name was Delilah." His earlier Philistine wife (Judges 14) was a different woman from Timnah.
- ⚠️ "cuts his hair" — **corrected.** Judges 16:19: "She called a man and had him shave off the seven locks of his head." Delilah did not cut his hair herself.
- ⚠️ "slayed scores of philistines with the jawbone" — **corrected to "a thousand."** Judges 15:15–16: Samson struck down 1,000 men. "Scores" (40–100) significantly understates the number.
- ⚠️ "they tie him to two great pillars… their huge party" — **corrected.** Judges 16:23–30: The Philistines brought Samson to the temple of Dagon during a sacrificial feast. He was not tied to the pillars — he asked a boy to lead him to the pillars so he could lean on them, then grasped them voluntarily and pulled them down. The text says "the dead whom he killed at his death were more than those whom he had killed during his life."

**Psalm 56 Historical Context — corrected:**
- ⚠️ "David has just slayed a bunch of Philistines… This same wicked nation is now hunting david" — **corrected.** The superscription of Psalm 56 reads: "when the Philistines seized him in Gath." This references 1 Samuel 21:10–15, where David fled *from Saul to* the Philistines at Gath. He was not being hunted by Philistines — he went to them voluntarily seeking refuge, was recognized by Achish's servants, became afraid, and feigned insanity to escape. The psalm's enemies are the Philistines, but David went to them, not the other way around.

**Nik Ripken / *Insanity of God* Verification:**
- ⚠️ Seminary: Sermon said both "Southern Baptist Theological Seminary" and "Southeastern Baptist Theological Seminary" — **contradictory; corrected to Southern.** Nik Ripken holds M.Div. and D.Min. from Southern Baptist Theological Seminary (Louisville, KY). He has spoken at Southeastern as a guest, which may explain the confusion.
- ⚠️ "his own son was taken" — **verified.** Timothy Ripken died on Easter Sunday, 1997, in Nairobi, Kenya, at age 16, from cardiac arrest brought on by a severe asthma attack. The family was stationed in Kenya while ministering in Somalia. To be precise: Timothy died of a medical emergency while the family served as missionaries overseas, not from persecution or violence. The sermon's phrasing "his missionary work had cost him the life of his son" is accurate in the sense that they were overseas because of mission work.
- ⚠️ **Prison story** (the father arrested, boy reunited after 10 years) — **could not be verified against known *Insanity of God* sources.** The well-known "Dmitri" story from the movie involves 17 years of imprisonment (not 10), and its climax features 1,500 prisoners singing a worship song (not a reunion with a son). The sermon's version may be a different story from the film, or a conflation of multiple accounts told from memory. Preserved as the pastor delivered it, since the specific source cannot be confirmed.
- ⚠️ **China story** — **core confirmed.** Nik did travel to a secret gathering of Chinese house church leaders in a remote, heavily persecuted region. The leaders asked whether the gospel had reached other countries. When told about Somalia and the loss of Nik's son, they committed to waking an hour earlier each day to pray for persecuted believers. Specific details (meat market contact, 19-hour drive, "around a hundred" leaders vs. ~150–170 in published accounts) could not be individually verified but the narrative arc matches published retellings.
