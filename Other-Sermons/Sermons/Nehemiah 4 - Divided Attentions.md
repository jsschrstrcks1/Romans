How much progress were they making with a sword in one hand? Were they being effective at either job? If you're carrying a sword, you're not carrying a shield. Offense, no defense. If you're carrying a shield, you're not swinging a sword — you're neither protecting yourself nor covering your friends. And even if the enemy never shows, it probably takes two men now to do what one man did before. Their attentions were divided.

Are our attentions divided?

Are we being salt and light?

I heard a story from a pastor friend of mine. He has three kids, and his wife was out of town, so he was making breakfast one morning. They'd never had oatmeal — much less North Carolina, southern-style oatmeal. So the kids watched him pull out that cardboard tube, cook it on the stove, and fill three bowls. They were already making faces. Said it didn't smell like anything. Bet it tastes like that too.

Then came the butter. He smothered all three bowls. Brown sugar on top. Stirred it in while it was hot — because, like most southerners with their tea, he likes his oatmeal so sweet the sugar is the dominant flavor. Mix it while it's hot, it absorbs more. That's how he does it.

The kids tasted it. His oldest boy looked up and said, *"Dad, is this some kind of dessert? It tastes like sugar."*

When he told me that this week, it hit me.

The Bible never says we're called to be the sugar and light. Some of us could stand to be a little sweeter — I'll grant you that. But we're called to be salt and light. And there's no good meal where salt is the dominant flavor. Salt preserves. Salt enhances. Salt pulls out what's already there. But it isn't meant to be the only thing you taste.

It strikes me that today — maybe more than any other point — churches are loaded with strategy and vision and purpose statements. Programs to reach this group, help that group, tell people about Jesus in every conceivable way. And yet, despite all that movement, the church isn't seeing a lot of movement *toward* Jesus. Churches are busier and noisier than ever. And nearly every denomination is on the decline.

Why?

Compromise. People are making compromises on Scripture. They're carrying a sword in one hand and dragging a stone with the other. They're trying to sync Christ with the world.

That word — syncretism. Do you know what that is?

It's a term that came from the Catholic Church in the 1700s, meaning literally "with Crete." What it's come to mean is this: take two competing, contradictory ideas and merge them into a third thing that everyone can live with.

Halloween, for instance.

Halloween has roots in Druidism and pagan worship. The Catholic Church had a day set aside to remember those martyred for Christ. They shifted that day to coincide with the Gaelic festival of Samhain — and now we have a third thing. Do Christian martyrs have anything to do with pagan ritual? Nothing. Samhain involved the butchering of cattle and massive bonfires built to ward off evil spirits. Syncretism took those bonfires and shrank them down into a cute little pumpkin on your front porch. It took the demonic protection totems — the faeries, the charms — and replaced blood sacrifice with candy. And called it Halloween. All Hallows' Eve.

Here's what else falls on October 31st.

Reformation Day. The anniversary of the day Martin Luther — without whom we'd probably all still be Catholic — nailed his 95 theses to the door of the church he served. Ninety-five places where the Catholic Church needed to reform itself. That day is what we mark as the beginning of the Reformation — the movement that produced the Protestant church. That day gave us the five solas: *sola scriptura, sola fide, sola gratia, solus Christus, soli Deo gloria.* By Scripture alone, by faith alone, by grace alone, through Christ alone, to the glory of God alone.

Those were the battle cries. Men were ostracized for them. Excommunicated. Many were killed. All of it to be remembered on October 31st.

Instead, we put on costumes.

Instead of sitting at the Lord's table and remembering his body and his blood, we gorge on candy. Instead of remembering his grace, we demand treats from our neighbors — and threaten dirty tricks if they don't deliver.

That's syncretism. Two competing things forced into a third.

I saw it up close in Haiti. While we were there, they had something called the *ra ra* — a kind of proto-carnival. The whole thing revolves around voodoo priests seeking to be possessed by stronger demons. They dance through the streets with their congregations until a spirit takes over. When one procession meets another, the two priests fight — sometimes to the death. The winner takes both groups.

The most disturbing part? Often the voodoo priests and the Catholic priests were the same people. Worshiping Satan six days a week — intentionally or not — and giving lip service to the Lord on Sunday.

That is syncretism.

So these wall builders were trying to do two things at once, and weren't much good at either. Do we not do the same? Is there anything in your life you're doing at the expense of time with the Lord? What are you dragging with you while you're trying to do what God called you to?

MacArthur notes this was probably the first time most of these people had ever held a weapon expecting to use it. These were farmers. Winemakers. Potters. Some had been slaves. They knew plows. They did not know swords.

And yet — if the Lord is with you, who can stand against you?

Archaeologists have found remnants of Nehemiah's wall. Twenty-four, twenty-five hundred years later. Part of it still stands. A small dog could probably knock it over — and there it is. When the enemy came for God's people, they left with nothing. As far as we know, not a single person got a paper cut.

Remember Gideon. Three hundred men against an army over a hundred thousand — plus the perhaps half million camp followers traveling with them. Gone. Without injury. Or Jericho. March around the walls and blow the trumpets. Every wall fell except Rahab's apartment. Or David and Goliath. Or Jonah and Nineveh.

When God chooses to move, who can stand?

---

As believers, we need to be in lockstep with God. When he moves, we move. When he withdraws, we withdraw.

We Americans have a hard time ceding control. We decide what we should do, then we do it. Then somewhere in the middle — after the decision's already made — we talk to God. Not to ask what he wants. To ask him to bless what we already chose.

I know a couple who grew up in the church. Discipled here. Married here. Spent the rest of their marriage blaming each other for why they weren't in church. Ended up in divorce. And they were furious at God — they'd done everything right and he hadn't made it work.

In truth, they'd done whatever seemed right in their own eyes and expected God to bless it.

Don't fall into that.

Sometimes God asks you to do things you won't understand. Fight a war with a horn instead of a sword. Go to the land of people you can't stand and tell them about him. Build a wall with one hand and keep your sword ready with the other.

Disobedience leads to failure. Obedience is the key.

Have any of you read Pilgrim's Progress?

Right near the beginning, Christian and his friend Pliable are walking the path when they stumble into something Bunyan calls the Slough of Despond. A stinking, sucking pit. Quicksand to the waist.

We go through things that don't make sense. A stretch in the hospital. A hard season. All of us have a past before Christ — and sometimes you wonder what you could have done for the Lord without all that weighing on you.

But God lets us go through things for a reason. Even when we can't see it.

Sometimes it's for a single gospel conversation.

Kurt was in the hospital this week and had some good conversations about the Lord while he was there. While my dad was in the hospital, I had a chance to share the gospel with one of the men working on my parents' drain field. And Kenny Duffield — any time he's been in the hospital, he's passed out New Testaments and talked to people about Jesus. For a season it felt like he and Lois were our missionaries *to* the hospital.

To sit with Kenny Duffield for any amount of time is to hear about our Lord. I love that about him.

Take heart. God is moving — in these things and in every thing.

Whether you can see the when and the where and the how or not, it almost always comes down to one question.

*Will you trust me?*
