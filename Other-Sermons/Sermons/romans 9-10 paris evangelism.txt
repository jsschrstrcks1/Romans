TRANSCRIPT (clean — duplicates removed, errors corrected in place per corrections log above)

OPENING / PASTORAL CONTEXT

Open with prayer. Go around the room praying for the victims of the Paris attack.

So I was lying in bed the other night after reading about the attack in Paris, thinking through how
to respond to something like that, and I hit on three things that I think are important to consider
as we watch what's going on and continue to interact with the Muslims that are flowing into and out
of our lives.

FIRST: EXPECT GRIEF

Firstly — and I don't know about you guys — but I have some friends in Paris still, missionaries
working with mostly Algerian immigrants, but some of them are specifically working with
Muslims, in Paris, in the arrondissements that were attacked.

A lot of times people's knee-jerk reaction to this kind of thing is to get a little angry. I think
there is room for that. People died. The friends and families of those left behind are grieving,
and anger is a part of grief. Anytime something like this happens, at least for me, I remember
9/11 — and while I don't know anyone who was killed in 9/11, it did feel in some ways personal,
that I was somehow attacked in all of that. So for many I think things like what happened last
night make 9/11 a fresh wound again. I can't imagine what those who were actually affected by
9/11 must be going through. So let's make sure we keep them in our prayers as well.

The point is: while what has been done is offensive and can make us angry, we need to — now more
than ever — react to the Muslims we come across with the love only Christ can give. They may not
deserve it. They may not be easy to love sometimes. But in a lot of ways they are like you and me,
and how we have treated God. Our sin — my sin — was enough to cause Christ to come and die. We are
no more or less deserving of salvation than they are, and God extends to them the same offer He
extends to us.

THREE RESPONSES

So: firstly, expect some grief from places you may not expect.
Secondly, love those Muslims in our communities like Christ would.
Thirdly, be proactive in your evangelism with these particular folks.

I was talking to someone this week who made the awful statement that he wished someone would line
up all the Muslims and shoot them. I said I'd be all for eradicating false religions, but that
guns weren't the answer — evangelism is the answer.

Florida has one of the largest Muslim populations in the South, with hundreds of thousands of
Muslims across the state. We encounter them every day at grocery stores, at gas stations, malls,
and other places of business. We drive past their mosques. But what are we doing? Do we engage
them? Or do we hold back? What would Christ have done? What would Paul have done?

We read this passage a couple of weeks back, but there are some important things I think warrant
a second look.

Read Romans 9:1-3.

QUOTES ON EVANGELISM

I gathered up a few quotes from a few men who take evangelism every bit as seriously as Paul does
in the passage. Let's start with:

Charles Spurgeon — the Calvinist Baptist — said this:

  "If sinners be damned, at least let them leap to Hell over our dead bodies. And if they perish,
  let them perish with our arms wrapped about their knees, imploring them to stay. If Hell must
  be filled, let it be filled in the teeth of our exertions, and let not one go unwarned and
  unprayed for."
  [VERIFIED: Sermon No. 349, "The Wailing of Risca," December 9, 1860, Exeter Hall, London.]

Let me ask you this — are you putting that much effort into your evangelism? For many of us I
think we fall — and I can fall — far short of this man's godly example.

Another famous person you may have heard of who is a Calvinist and believes in evangelism is John
Piper. Piper is a Baptist like we are and has written something like 42 books on the desperate
need for evangelism, discipleship, and missions. 42 books demanding that the church live in
obedience to the Great Commission. 42 books saying God's mercy to us demands that we share the
Gospel. That if we aren't sharing the gospel we are living in disobedience — I think he even said
rebellion. Yet he is a Calvinist.

Another person you may have heard of who is both Calvinist and passionate about evangelism is
David Platt — he is the head of the Southern Baptist Convention's International Mission Board.
[NOTE: Accurate as of 2014–2018.] To even join the mission board you have to be able to
demonstrate a lifestyle of evangelism, a lifestyle of people responding to your evangelism. He
preached a message one time saying that if tomorrow he woke up and during his morning prayers he
felt like God was telling him to go to Syria and share the gospel with the lost and plant churches,
that he would resign from his post and move to Syria, or Iraq, or Afghanistan. He says his life
is a blank check made out to God, and that he's willing to do whatever it takes to get the Gospel
to unreached people. He says sharing the gospel is an obligation held by every believer, owed to
the lost world over.

I have come to a point in my personal life where I've said, "God, whatever you want me to do,
whatever it looks like, no matter the cost to me personally, I'll do it." There was a time where
I was praying pretty seriously about going to Afghanistan or Iraq — not too awful long after 9/11
— because when I say "whatever the cost," I meant it. Because I knew that right at that moment
they were more open to the gospel than at any other time. Because I knew they were desperately in
need of the life-saving truth of the gospel.

Another famous person you've likely heard of is Jonathan Edwards. He is hailed as the smartest
man America has ever produced. He is the man who wrote:

  "Resolved, never to do anything which I would be afraid to do if it were the last hour of my life."
  [VERIFIED: Resolution 7 of Edwards' 70 Resolutions. Original reads "I should be afraid" — minor
  wording variation. Acceptable but note if presenting as exact quotation.]

This is the same man who, in summary of his life's resolve, committed: first, to live for God's
glory above all else; and second, that even if no one else would, he still would.
[NOTE: These are paraphrases of Edwards' spirit and intent, not direct quotations from his 70
Resolutions. The actual texts of Resolutions 1 and 2 read substantially differently.]

This is the same man who is basically the father of hellfire-and-brimstone preaching. You've
heard of the famous "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God," where he preached that the lost are
dangling by a string over a pit of fire, and the horror of horrors — the string catches fire and
is burning. The sinner is about to fall to their certain, inexorable, eternal, flaming punishment
unless one accepts Christ. Edwards was one of the men God used in the Great Awakening. God used
him and others to reach tens of thousands of people.

Another man deeply influential in evangelism — and actually the only Calvinist Methodist I'm
aware of — was George Whitefield. He was the most extensively traveled preacher of his age. He
crossed the Atlantic 13 times and preached approximately 18,000 sermons over more than three
decades. Let that sink in — 18,000 sermons. Whitefield poured out his life on the road for the
gospel.

PAUL'S ANGUISH — ROMANS 9:1-3

Now let's look at Paul. Look with me at Romans 9:1-3.

Paul says:

  "I am speaking the truth in Christ — I am not lying; my conscience bears me witness in the Holy
  Spirit — that I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart. For I could wish that I
  myself were accursed and cut off from Christ for the sake of my brothers, my kinsmen according
  to the flesh." (Romans 9:1-3, ESV)

He is basically saying that if it meant his people, his extended family, could go to heaven, he
would gladly be cut off from Christ — read that as sent to hell. He would literally go to hell to
see lost people come to know Christ. He says he lives in unceasing anguish over the lost.

How far are you willing to go? I don't know about you, but sometimes I feel uncomfortable.
Sometimes it's awkward or unpleasant to share the gospel with certain people. They get angry.
They become belligerent and verbally abusive. I had a guy chase me away from his house one time
at gunpoint for offering to show him in the Bible where he could find eternal life.

My dad had an experience last week — the day mom had her surgery. You can ask him about it later.
No one drew a gun, but the guy was a beast as soon as the name of Jesus Christ was mentioned. So
often we let little things like these keep us from sharing the gospel with people.

What's the worst they can do? Hurt our bodies? Send us to live with our Father in heaven? I heard
someone say, "God won't send you anyplace His grace can't keep you." I think that goes for your
families too — I see the looks on your faces. But what if that guy with the gun had shot me? What
would have happened to my family?

Listen. We've talked about God's sovereignty before. We know that nothing that happens surprises
Him. We know that nothing that happens is beyond His power to stop, and that everything He allows
to happen has a reason. That God has a plan for you, for your good. That all things work together
for the good of those who love God and are called according to His purpose. We know that no matter
where He sends you, what He asks you to do, or what comes of that, He will take care of you — and
your family as well.

I firmly believe that if instead of me being chased by a guy with a gun, it had been, let's say,
Bob Leko — and if he had been shot by that crazy person — that God would have taken care of Melody
and the kids. Further, if it isn't your day to go, it's not going to happen anyway — gun or not.
If it is your day, then it doesn't matter if you hide out in a saferoom. There is no hiding from
or frustrating of God's plan. God is sovereign.

Remember Jonah? God was sovereign over the wind and waves. He was sovereign over a big fish. He
was sovereign over Nineveh, and He was sovereign over Jonah. Jonah's best efforts to openly defy
God's will resulted in the exact same result. What was it God asked Jonah to do? Preach repentance
to the Ninevites. What was it Jonah didn't want to do? Exactly. He didn't want to preach to the
Ninevites. What was it Jonah did in the end? Preached to the Ninevites. As a result the Ninevites
repented and turned to the Lord. It doesn't matter how hard you fight against God's will — in the
end He's going to win. Far better to move with God than to fight it.

APPEAL

Are you like Paul? Or like David Platt, or Spurgeon, or Whitefield — willing to go wherever the
Lord leads you to share the gospel with people? If not, you need to check your heart.

Can you — like Paul — say that you would rather go to hell than see someone, anyone else, miss
out on heaven? Are you actively doing what Spurgeon implored us to do — forcing people to leap to
hell over your body, with your arms about their knees? We are obligated — Platt said — to share
the gospel with the lost. We were even commanded by Christ to make disciples. Is that reflected
in your life?

Giving up the comforts of North America and moving to Syria may seem foolish. It may seem like
someone is throwing their life away. But in truth it glorifies God. In truth it is a wise
decision. Going to the unreached is one of the best ways your life can be spent.

Revelation talks about how one day there will be people from every — to quote the Greek —
ta ethne — every people, every tribe, every nation, every people group, every language,
represented before God one day. Would it be cool to be used by God in the process of making sure
one of those groups is represented there? What an honor, that God would use one of us to do
something like that. God and Christ want us to be involved in His plan for mankind.

He went to the cross for you. But are you willing to cross the street to share the gospel for Him?
[Pause.]

Paul had unceasing anguish. Paul was willing to go to hell — to throw himself into damnation. How
much is a soul worth to you? Would you go to hell if it meant your kid or your grandkid would be
saved? Have you thought deeply about the reality that people we love are heading for hell?

David Platt says the only people who can think in those terms are those who KNOW who they are,
KNOW where they've been, KNOW what they owed, and KNOW that their great God paid that price for
their sins. Another person said that frequently people who do not share the gospel do not have a
proper view of their sin, the cross, and what exactly their salvation cost — that people who
refuse to share the gospel are sometimes not saved. Let that sink in for a moment. I think it's
more nuanced than just those two things. But it's nevertheless true in many circumstances.

I think especially for people like me who have been saved for a long time — 27 years for me, by
the way — it's easy to let scales creep in and disguise the promptings of God to share the gospel.
It's easy to let calluses grow over the urgent need for the gospel that surrounds us. It's easy
to think, "Well, God will just send someone else." He probably will. But that doesn't make you any
less disobedient in that moment.

ILLUSTRATIONS

Listen. You probably won't be smooth the first time. A friend of mine named Tim — I remember the
first time he ever shared the gospel with someone. He said all sorts of off-the-wall things —
true things — but things that might freak out someone who isn't familiar with church language.
He told them they were going to get washed in the blood of Jesus. That their old self would get
crucified on the cross with Jesus. That they were going to get born again. Just those three things
ought to have scared this person off. But God had been working in this man's heart, had been
calling him, had prepared his soul for that moment — so that I think it didn't matter what Tim
said. The man was ready, and he accepted Christ in that moment.

I heard a story of another woman. A lesbian feminist activist. Her little nephew pleaded with her
to go to church. So she did, one Sunday. The pastor happened to be preaching on women's roles in
the church. He had a biblical view, a conservative view. And she sat there stewing about how this
church sought to repress women. Then the man shared the gospel and talked about God and His love.
It broke her heart in that moment. She repented of her sins, became a Christian, and within a few
years she married a man at that same church. She has a couple of kids now.

If God can use a man who tells another man it's blood-bath time, then God can use you! If God can
use a pastor who preached against feminism to reach a lesbian feminist, He can use you! And I know
— if God can use a donkey (remember Balaam's donkey?), He can certainly use me too.

CLOSING APPEAL

It's time for us to become sold out for the gospel. It's time for us to turn from this sin of
laziness with the gospel and embrace Christ's last command — to make disciples and to baptize
them. That is probably the most important thing you can do with your life. Whether it's your gift
or not, that doesn't matter. God asks us to try. He asks us to tell people about His Son. He asks
us to reach the lost. Are you ready to do that?

Jonathan Edwards said he vowed never to do anything now that he wouldn't do in his last hour. If
it was your last hour, how would you spend it? Wouldn't you spend it telling people about Jesus?

I know there are a number of things in my life that I wouldn't do in that last hour. Make the
things you do in life matter. Make your legacy one of sharing the gospel. Do the things that
matter in life, at the expense of the things that don't.
