## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date**: 2026-03-21
**Sermon**: Joshua 10 — The Power of God
**Primary Text**: Joshua 10 (with context from Joshua 7–9)

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**An Evangelistic Appendix Wearing an Expository Costume**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A sermon that begins with narrative context for Joshua 10 but rapidly abandons the passage in favor of a freestanding gospel presentation and doctrinal overview of soteriology, leaving the text's own argument — God's faithfulness in battle, his promise-keeping power, his sovereign victory over the coalition of kings — almost entirely unpreached.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test**: FAIL. The stated theme is "The Power of God," but the sermon's actual content is a basic soteriology lecture (what it means to be a Christian: calling, justification, adoption, sanctification, perseverance, glorification) built around the magnet analogy. The passage Joshua 10 is never actually opened, quoted directly, or exegeted. The sermon uses Joshua 10 as a narrative springboard and then launches into freestanding evangelism.

**Authority Test**: The preacher does not misrepresent the text, but he does not submit to its argument either — because its argument is never engaged. Joshua 10 is about God's promise to Joshua ("Do not fear them, for I have given them into your hands" — v. 8), the miraculous nature of the victory (the sun standing still, the hailstones), and the demonstration of God's power over the enemies of his people. None of this is preached.

**Berean Gate**: The Scopus Test fails. A rubric-stopping failure is borderline here — the sermon is not hostile to the text and does use its narrative context — but the sermon does not say what the text says. The evaluation continues but the failure is prominent.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 — Main point does not equal text's point**: Joshua 10's argument centers on God's faithfulness to his promise, his miraculous intervention in battle, and the theological claim that no one can stand against the God who fights for Israel. The sermon's main point is "pray before you act, and become a Christian." These are not the same point.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation from the text**: The altar call is present and the ABC gospel formula is used, but exhortation arising from the text's own imperatives is absent. "Come to the altar" is not the exhortation that Joshua 10 demands from its hearers.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ or myself?* — More of the salvation process than Christ himself. The magnet and tattoo illustrations keep the focus on one's own spiritual state.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* — Ideas. The power of God in Joshua 10 — hailstones, the sun standing still, the routing of five kings — is recounted but not proclaimed.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* — Primarily informed. The Gibeonite parallel briefly convicts, but the sermon moves quickly to information.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Gibeonite parallel is genuinely strong**: "God risks his own people to protect those who lied to them — doesn't that sound like something we've heard before?" is an organically christological move that arises from the text's own narrative. This is the best moment in the sermon.
2. **Doctrinal completeness**: The ordo salutis is stated more fully here than in most evangelical sermons — calling, justification, adoption, sanctification, perseverance, glorification. The congregation receives more soteriological instruction than usual.
3. **Honest evangelistic directness**: The altar call, though formulaic, is clear and unashamed. The preacher is not embarrassed to call sinners to Christ.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text is never preached**: Joshua 10 contains one of the most dramatic displays of divine power in the Old Testament — the sun standing still, hailstones killing more enemies than Israel's swords, God fighting from heaven. None of this is exegeted, proclaimed, or applied. The text served as a narrative runway for a freestanding gospel presentation.
2. **The sermon is entirely evangelistic; no word to believers**: A congregation of predominantly converted people sits through a soteriology lecture addressed almost entirely to the unconverted. The sermon offers them nothing from the passage — no strengthening of faith, no specific application of God's power to their current battles, no implication of what it means that God fights for his people.
3. **The ABC formula trivializes justification**: "Accept, Believe, Confess — it's simple, straightforward, easy, and essential" reduces the new birth to a three-step process. The sermon earlier mentioned the Holy Spirit's work of regeneration preceding faith, but the ABC close implies a human-decision model that is in tension with the ordo salutis presented just above it.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see the irony of a sermon titled "The Power of God" that never actually preaches the power of God displayed in Joshua 10. The battle is the illustration for the abstract point; but the battle's theological meaning — that God fights for his covenant people, that his word is always fulfilled, that no earthly coalition can stand against him — is precisely what makes the sermon possible, and it goes unproclaimed.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The soteriological survey (calling → glorification) creates an impression of doctrinal depth. But depth requires development, not listing. Naming all the stages of salvation in three paragraphs without unpacking any of them is not deep preaching; it is a catalogue. The congregation may feel they received sound doctrine — they received a list of correct terms.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Joshua 10:8 — "Do not fear them, for I have given them into your hands" — is a sovereign war-oracle. God declares the victory before it is fought. This is a direct window into the nature of divine sovereignty and covenant faithfulness. Applied to a struggling congregation: "What coalition is arrayed against you? What five kings surround you? God has already spoken over your battle." That would have been a sermon. The sun standing still (10:12-14) — the LORD fighting for Israel — is the most spectacular theophanic battle narrative in the entire conquest. It was unpreached.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild around Joshua 10:8 as the thesis**: "The LORD said to Joshua, 'Do not fear them, for I have given them into your hands.' The battle belongs to God — and that truth changes everything." All application and gospel content should flow from this.
2. **Preach the sun standing still**: This is the passage's own display of power. Proclaim what it means that God suspended creation itself to honor his promise to his people. Then ask: this same God is your God. What does that mean for today?
3. **Retain the Gibeonite parallel but develop it**: The move from "God protected people who didn't deserve it" to "He did the same for you in Christ" is genuinely strong. Give it three times the space and make it the gospel bridge.
4. **Reduce the ordo salutis survey or replace it with a focused gospel moment**: Choose one element — justification, or new creation (2 Cor. 5:17, already quoted) — and dwell there, rather than racing through all seven stages.
5. **Address believers specifically**: After the gospel call, turn to the church: "For those of you who already know Christ — God has given your enemies into your hands. He has spoken that word over your life. The question is whether you will march by faith, or send only 3,000 men without asking him first."
6. **Align the closing formula with the ordo salutis presented earlier**: Either lead with human response (ABC) or lead with divine initiative (calling, regeneration). Do not do both in the same sermon without acknowledging the tension.
