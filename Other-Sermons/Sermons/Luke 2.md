Wadsworth Longfellow lived during the time of the Civil war. They had a cast iron wood burning stove in their kitchen and one day tragically he heard an un-earthly scream escape his wifes lips in their shared kitchen. He ran to find her laying on the floor silently burning. Her apron had caught fire in their cook stove. The fire was so hot it quickly melted her skin, and caused her clothing to act as a wick to her fat. By the time he found her, it was already to late. Gripped by grief he threw himself on top of his wife using his body to try to extinguish the flames and save her life. Fire hot enough to use your body fat as fuel is too hot for you to live through generally speaking. Especially back in the days of the civil war when leaches were proper medicine. By the time they were able to bring the doctor around she had died. Henry in extinguishing his wifes flaming body, received extensive third degree burns. This was Christmas day. He spent much of the next year in a deep depression. His friends pleaded with him to get help. He was despondent over the death of his wife. In march his eldest of six children ran away to join the army. Once the army figured out the truth of things they wrote for and received henrys permission for his son to be in the army. His depression deepend. In april, a few short weeks later his son returned home sick with pneumonia. Henry was glad to have the time with his son and relished every moment. But by may his illness had lifted and he returned to the army. On thanksgiving day his eldest son was shot through the left shoulder, the bullet exiting under the right shoulder blade. The damage was extensive. The bullet knicked his sons spine. They didn't have xrays or cat scans back then, they couldn't use modern technology to look at the spine and see if it was damaged. So they lashed him to a stretcher and carried him to new church in Virginia. A time later, Henry received a telegram explaning what had happened to his son. He immediately set off on foot for Virginia arriving Christmas morning to find his son unconscious and likely paralysed. Racked with infection his chances of survival were grim, his chances of ever walking again grimmer still.

Henry wept. It was the anniversary of his wifes passing, and here he was holding his son in his arms not sure if he would live much less ever walk again. In that moment of over powering grief he wrote his song I heard the bells.

Reading this passage (Luke 2) on Christmas is a tradition in my family, and I hope it becomes one in yours. Before presents are opened, often before we even ate breakfast we would sit in the living room and read most of this chapter. Our salvation begins the moment Christ is born, and is consummated with his death on the Cross. It's a time symbolic of God being separated from his Son, and so we celebrate by bringing family together. It's a time to remind us of the Gift God and Jesus gave us, and so we give gifts. Experiencing life, and death, happiness, sadness, grief, loss, and temptation. He experienced everything any human does and overcame it.
