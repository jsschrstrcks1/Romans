## Processional / Opening

Today, as we join Paul Zinda and Kerry Williams Compton in marriage, we celebrate them as they begin a new family together. Yet we also know that this new branch of the family tree will be strengthened and enriched by the love, traditions, and knowledge of their family roots.

On behalf of the bride and groom I want to thank everyone for coming. A marriage is the most sacred relationship that people can enter into. So I think it is fitting that we begin this ceremony with prayer.

---

## Opening Prayer

Our Father, we come today as family, friends, those who are physically present and those whom we love who are now with you, asking your blessing upon these two lives and this home being established. You have made us so that we are incomplete without the other so that we yearn for someone whom we can love and whose love we can receive.

We are thankful for the love we see here, and even more, for the love we feel from you. May we never take your love for granted. Grant them patience, gentleness, forbearance, and understanding. Protect their home from those forces that would break it apart.

We ask for health, for long life, for the fulfillment of every good dream. May their love continue through life and, finally, blend into life eternal.

In Jesus name we pray, amen.

## Homily

I want you to notice something critical in this passage. Every sin a person can commit comes back around to a single letter. I. I, then, is the antithesis of love.

Love is patient — not when someone feels like it, there are no escape clauses — love is simply patient. Patient when the toothpaste is rolled the wrong way, patient when you're running late and don't have a plan. In all things and all circumstances love is patient. If you find yourself in a situation where you are not feeling patient, stop and consider for a moment whether or not it's because you are putting yourself before your spouse. Is your need to leave in a hurry, getting there as soon as possible, conflicting with their need to have a couple unlikely contingencies planned for? In that moment neither of you are being loving. When conflict arises — and it will — always be quick to ask: am I being loving in this situation?

The same is true of kindness. If you are both being kind in a situation you will also be demonstrating Christlike patience. I know a couple, good friends of mine, who struggled with not dishonoring each other. When things were bad, he would look drawn and be uncharacteristically reserved. She by contrast was talking badly about him to the members of the church where he was pastor. Saying mean and hurtful things simply to make him look bad. Her dishonoring manner was one of the contributing factors in their ongoing struggle. Perhaps if there had been more kindness they would be in a different place right now.

Next look with me at the last set of descriptors. It says love always protects, trusts, hopes and perseveres. In your arguments never say *never* or *always*. Because always and never are usually not true, and they discourage your partner from trying in that area. Always trust your partner to have your best interests at heart. Never lose hope, and in the dark days that may come, always persevere. A friend said sometime back that the only reason he is still married to his wife after forty some years is that she has doggedly persevered. She said the same about her husband. That his stubbornness, and refusal to give up on her, is one of the main reasons they have stayed together as long as they have.

In truth, a marriage is a lot like a triangle. In this triangle God is at the top, and you both are the corners at the bottom. As long as you two are keeping Christ at the center, and growing in your relationship with God, you two will grow closer together. Paul, as man of the house, biblically it is your job to disciple your wife, to read the Bible with her, to pray together. In addition to all the other benefits of prayer, prayer is an important part because in prayer we can bare our soul to the Lord in front of our spouse in ways we may hesitate to do at other times. Prayer then helps keep you close to each other. A professor once said that you have to keep your hand on the pulse of a community lest you lose touch and become ineffectual — the same is true in a marriage. Prayer helps you keep track of each other's pulse — where they are with various situations in life, how they are growing and changing, and what's bothering them. Even if these things are openly discussed in your household it's still vitally important to bring them before the throne of God.

Scripture says it's better to have never made a vow than to make one that you can't keep.

## Vows

"Will you, Paul Zinda, have Kerry Williams Compton to be your wife? Will you love her, comfort and keep her, forsaking all others cling and remain true only to her in sickness and in health, whether rich or poor, as long as you both shall live?" *(I will)*

*(Repeat for Kerry)*

## Ring Exchange

*(Rings)*

"With this ring I thee wed, and all my worldly goods I thee endow. In sickness and in health, in poverty or in wealth, 'til death do us part."

---

## Ring Words

Let us seal these vows with rings. Rings are symbolic of many things. First they are made of precious metals and as such are valuable. Second, while they have a beginning, once started a ring continues on without end. And so as a sign of your love, and in remembrance of your vows, exchange rings.

---

## Community Prayer

This next part is a little untraditional at a wedding, but I think is one of the more important things we will do today. Let's all gather around Paul and Kerry, lay hands on them and each take turns praying for them, asking for blessings for them, for protection, long life, happiness, and anything else the Lord places on your heart. Dad — why don't you go ahead and start, and when it seems like everyone is done, I will go ahead and close.

*(Prayer)*

*(Return to your seats)*

## Pronouncement

By the power vested in me by the state of Kentucky, and before God and these loved ones I now pronounce you husband and wife. You may kiss the bride.

Ladies and gentleman I am proud to have the honor to present to you for the very first time, Mr. and Mrs. Paul Zinda!
