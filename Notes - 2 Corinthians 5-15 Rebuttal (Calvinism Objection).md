# Notes — 2 Corinthians 5:15 and the Objection Against Definite Atonement

*Study notes for preaching and conversation. ESV throughout.*

---

## The Objection

The text reads:

> *"and he died for all, so that those who live might no longer live for themselves but for him who for their sake died and was raised."*
> — 2 Corinthians 5:15

The objection runs like this: Paul says "all." Not "some." Not "the elect." All. If Christ died for all, then he died for every individual human being — which means Limited Atonement (or Definite Atonement, the preferred term) is false. The "L" in TULIP must go.

This is a common objection. It sounds straightforward. And it fails for reasons the text itself supplies — not reasons imported from a theological system.

---

## Why This Objection Doesn't Hold

### 1. You Cannot Isolate Verse 15 from Verse 14

The objector lifts verse 15 out of a sentence that began in verse 14. Read the whole movement:

> *"For the love of Christ controls us, because we have concluded this: that one has died for all, therefore all have died; and he died for all, so that those who live might no longer live for themselves but for him who for their sake died and was raised."*
> — 2 Corinthians 5:14-15

Notice the logic: *one has died for all, therefore all have died.*

That "therefore" (Greek: *ἄρα*, *ara*) is not decoration. It's a logical inference. If Christ's death is "for all" in the universal sense — every individual human being without exception — then "all have died" must carry the same scope. Every human being has died in Christ.

And that means every human being is saved.

The objector must choose. Either "all" is universal in both clauses, which yields universalism (which Scripture plainly denies — see Matthew 25:46, Revelation 20:15). Or "all" is qualified in both clauses, which is exactly what the Reformed position argues. You can't play the "all" card in verse 15 while quietly pocketing a more limited "all" in verse 14.

The grammar of the sentence will not allow selective universalism.

#### Going Deeper: Why the Two "Alls" Must Be Coextensive

The *ἄρα* ("therefore") creates a direct logical dependency between the two clauses. The "all" in "all have died" is not a separate group from the "all" in "died for all." It is the *same* group, now described from the other side of the transaction. One died *for* them; *therefore* they died *in* him. The inference flows from union, not merely from coverage.

This is federal headship logic — the same logic Paul deployed in Romans 5:

> *"Therefore, as one trespass led to condemnation for all men, so one act of righteousness leads to justification and life for all men. For as by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous."*
> — Romans 5:18-19

Adam's sin condemned those *in* him. Christ's obedience justified those *in* him. "All" in each case means everyone within that covenant headship — not every individual ever born, but every member of that representative relationship.

Paul is doing the same thing in 2 Corinthians 5:14. Christ is the second Adam. He acts as the head and representative of a people. He dies *for* them — in their place, bearing their guilt. *Therefore* they die *in* him — their death is accomplished in his. This is not a description of every human being. This is a description of everyone Christ represents.

#### The Arminian Cannot Escape Without Breaking the Logic

Watch what happens when the Arminian tries to make "all" universal:

He says: "He died for all" means every individual human being without exception.

If that's true, then "therefore all have died" means every individual human being has died in Christ.

Dying in Christ is union language. Paul uses it everywhere:

> *"I have been crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me."*
> — Galatians 2:20

> *"Do you not know that all of us who have been baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death? We were buried therefore with him by baptism into death, in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, we too might walk in newness of life."*
> — Romans 6:3-4

Dying with Christ is not a universal experience. It is the exclusive possession of those who are in Christ by regeneration and faith. If "all have died" meant every human being has died with Christ, then every human being would also rise with Christ — which is universalism.

So the Arminian has to do something the text does not do: he has to *break* the logical connection. He has to read "died for all" as universal, and then quietly limit "all have died" to mean something like "all have died *potentially*" or "all have been placed in a position where they *can* die to themselves." But that is not what the aorist tense allows.

#### The Aorist Tense Closes the Escape Hatch

Both verbs in verse 14 are aorist: *ἀπέθανεν* ("has died" / "died") and *ἀπέθανον* ("have died" / "died"). The aorist in Greek describes a completed action. Not an ongoing process. Not a potential. Not a possibility that awaits human cooperation.

*One died.* — Completed. Done. The cross is a historical event.

*Therefore all died.* — Also completed. Done. The consequence is just as accomplished as the act that caused it.

The Arminian reading requires the second clause to describe a potential or conditional state: "all *can now die* to themselves if they choose." But the grammar won't carry that weight. *ἀπέθανον* is not a subjunctive of possibility. It is an indicative of accomplished fact. The death is real. It has already happened. The only question is whose death it was — and Paul has already told us: it was the death of those for whom Christ died.

#### The Penal Substitution Problem

Here is the corner the objector cannot escape if he holds to penal substitutionary atonement — as most who raise this objection do.

Penal substitution means Christ actually bore the specific guilt of specific sinners. He did not bear a general, floating mass of guilt hovering over all humanity. He bore guilt that belonged to particular people and was transferred to him in his death. God's wrath was actually poured out on him — not theoretically, not potentially, but actually and completely.

Now: if Christ bore the actual guilt of every human being without exception — satisfied the actual divine wrath against every individual — then the debt of every individual has been paid in full. Justice has been served for everyone.

If that is true, then God cannot in justice send anyone to hell for their sin. The punishment has already been served. You cannot punish a debt twice. If my debt is paid, my creditor cannot demand payment again.

This is why universal atonement, held consistently alongside penal substitution, collapses into universalism. The only way to avoid universalism while holding universal atonement is to quietly abandon penal substitution — to say Christ didn't actually *pay* anyone's debt, he just *made payment possible* or *removed the legal barrier*. But that is a different gospel.

The Calvinist position is the only one that holds penal substitution and avoids universalism: Christ bore the actual guilt of a particular people — the elect — and therefore those people are saved with certainty. His atonement is not a potential benefit. It is an accomplished redemption. Those for whom he died, *died*. Those who died with him, live.

### 2. The Result Defines the Group

Verse 15 doesn't just say Christ died for all. It tells you what that death accomplished:

> *"so that those who live might no longer live for themselves but for him who for their sake died and was raised."*

"Those who live" — this is not every individual human being. This is a particular group: people who have been changed. People who now live for Christ rather than for themselves.

That is the effect Paul describes. Not a potential effect. Not something that might happen if they respond correctly. The death of Christ produces a people who live for him.

If Christ died for every individual without exception, and only some live for him, then his death accomplished nothing certain for most people. It purchased a possibility, not a people. That is not what the text says. The death is effective. The result is a living people. The "all" Christ died for are the same "all" who now live for him.

---

### 3. "All" in Paul Does Not Mean "Every Individual Without Exception"

The objector is assuming a definition of "all" that the Greek word (*πᾶς*, *pas*) does not require and that Paul does not consistently use.

Look at the same word in the same author:

**Romans 5:18:**
> *"Therefore, as one trespass led to condemnation for all men, so one act of righteousness leads to justification and life for all men."*

If "all men" in the second clause means every individual without exception — universal justification. If the objector presses verse 15 with universal scope, he must press Romans 5:18 the same way. But he won't. Because he doesn't believe everyone is justified.

So he limits "all" in Romans 5:18. Which means he already agrees that "all" can be limited by context.

The question is never whether "all" can be limited. It always is. The question is: limited by what?

**1 Corinthians 15:22:**
> *"For as in Adam all die, so also in Christ shall all be made alive."*

Same construction. Two groups, both called "all." Paul is not saying every human being is made alive in Christ — he distinguishes between those who belong to Adam and those who belong to Christ. The "all" in each arm of the parallel is coextensive with its head. All who are in Adam die. All who are in Christ live.

Paul uses "all" to mean "all within a given group." Every time. The context determines which group.

---

### 4. The Surrounding Context Points to a Covenant People, Not All Humanity

Read the verses around 5:15 and notice who Paul is actually talking about:

**Verse 17:**
> *"Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come."*

The new creation is not universal. It belongs to those who are "in Christ." That phrase — "in Christ" — is union language. It marks out a people, not all people.

**Verses 18-19:**
> *"All this is from God, who through Christ reconciled us to himself and gave us the ministry of reconciliation; that is, in Christ God was reconciling the world to himself, not counting their trespasses against them, and entrusting to us the message of reconciliation."*

Notice "reconciled *us* to himself." Paul speaks as one of the reconciled. He is not describing all of humanity. He is describing a people — the same people entrusted with the message of reconciliation. And that message goes out *to* the world, not from it.

**Verse 21:**
> *"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."*

"For our sake." "We might become." These are particular pronouns. They do not refer to all humanity. They refer to those who are in Christ, who have been reconciled, who are new creations.

The context of 2 Corinthians 5 does not support a universal reading. It supports a covenant community reading — the people of God, Jew and Gentile, united in Christ.

---

### 5. The "For All" May Mean "All Without Distinction" — Jew and Gentile

This is where Paul's pastoral purpose in the passage matters. He is establishing the basis for his ministry to all nations. The point of "he died for all" in context may be the breaking down of the Jew-Gentile wall: Christ died for all kinds of people — not Jew only, but Gentile also. Not one nation, but all nations.

This is how "all" functions elsewhere in Paul. In Galatians 3:28:

> *"There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."*

"All" is breaking down categories, not listing every individual. The force is "all without distinction," not "all without exception."

If Paul's concern in 2 Corinthians 5 is the scope of his apostolic mission — and it clearly is, given the "ministry of reconciliation" language in verses 18-20 — then "he died for all" is saying: this gospel is for every people, every tribe, every nation. Take it everywhere. No one is outside the offer.

That reading fits the context perfectly. And it fits Definite Atonement perfectly. The gospel is offered to all without distinction. The atonement is effective for all without exception who are in Christ.

### 6. The Trilemma That Doesn't Resolve in Favor of Universal Atonement

John Owen pressed this argument in *The Death of Death in the Death of Christ* (1647), and no Arminian has successfully answered it. The question he poses is simply this:

For whose sins did Christ die?

There are only three possible answers:

- All the sins of all men
- All the sins of some men
- Some sins of all men

The third option — some sins of all men — leaves everyone still guilty of the sins Christ didn't cover. No one is saved. Discard that.

The first option — all sins of all men — means every sin of every person has been paid for. If sin's debt has been settled for everyone, then God cannot in justice condemn anyone for sin. Everyone is saved. That is universalism. Scripture denies it.

The second option — all sins of some men — is Definite Atonement. Christ bore the full weight of the sins of a particular people. His atonement is complete. It is not a possibility — it is an accomplishment. The people for whom he died are saved.

The objector who presses "he died for all" as universal atonement has not escaped this trilemma. He has simply chosen the first option — and must live with its consequence, or quietly qualify it every time someone pushes back.

---

### 7. The Preposition "For" Does Not Carry Universal Weight

The Greek preposition *ὑπέρ* (*huper*), translated "for," means "on behalf of" or "in place of." It is the language of substitution. Christ stands in for those he represents.

But substitution requires a specific substitute. When a man goes to prison in place of another, he serves the sentence of a specific person — not of everyone in the courtroom. The substitution is particular by definition.

If Christ is the substitute — bearing the actual guilt, suffering the actual wrath, absorbing the actual penalty — then for whose guilt did he stand? For whose wrath did he suffer? The word *huper* does not answer that question by itself. Context answers it.

And the context of 2 Corinthians 5 answers it with "those who live for him." Not all humanity. The people his death produces.

---

## Summary: What 2 Corinthians 5:15 Actually Says

When you read the verse in context — not in isolation — it does not teach universal atonement. It teaches:

1. Christ's death is the death of all who are represented by him. (v. 14 logic)
2. That death produces a living people — those who now live for him rather than themselves. (v. 15 result)
3. Those people are new creations, reconciled to God, counted righteous in Christ. (vv. 17-21)

This is Definite Atonement. Specific representation. Actual accomplishment. A particular people purchased at the full price of the Son's blood.

The "all" is not all of humanity. It is all of those who are in Christ — every one of them, without exception, without remainder.

---

## A Word on the Offer of the Gospel

Nothing in Definite Atonement limits the free offer of the gospel.

The command to repent and believe goes to every person. The invitation is genuine. Christ is sufficient for all. The barrier is never Christ's unwillingness or the inadequacy of his work — it is the sinner's unwillingness, and that unwillingness is overcome by grace alone.

We preach to everyone. We offer to everyone. We implore everyone — exactly as Paul does in verse 20: *"We implore you on behalf of Christ, be reconciled to God."* The imploring is real. The reconciliation is accomplished. The call goes everywhere.

The Calvinist preaches a wider gospel than he is often credited with. He preaches to sinners who cannot save themselves — and tells them that Christ has saved to the uttermost those who come to God through him. That is not narrow. That is mercy that cannot fail.
