2 Timothy 3:1–5 — Heathens Gonna Heath


One of the things that jumped out at me in our text today — which will be 2 Timothy 3 — is the phrase "lovers of self."

There is so much done these days in the name of self-love. I looked up the phrase and found this definition from the Brain and Behavior Research Foundation: "Self-love is a state of appreciation for oneself that grows from actions that support our physical, psychological and spiritual growth."

So far so good.

"Self-love means having a high regard for your own well-being and happiness. Self-love means taking care of your own needs and not sacrificing your well-being to please others. Self-love means not settling for less than you deserve."

They go on to recommend: talking to and about yourself with love, prioritizing yourself, giving yourself a break from self-judgment, trusting yourself, being true to yourself, being nice to yourself, setting healthy boundaries, forgiving yourself when you aren't being true or nice to yourself.

Like so many things in life, they took some decent ideas and gave them a twist that made them indecent.

Sometimes we need a good kick in the pants — and sometimes we need to give it to ourselves.

The one that gets me is prioritizing yourself. In certain mental health contexts I can see needing to do that, up to a point. But think about it in terms of the majority of people who are mentally healthy. If this life were all there is, if this were as good as it gets, then working for this world would make a certain kind of sense. But this isn't all there is, and it isn't the best it gets. The best is yet to come. This life has its deprivations for a little while. But the next one more than makes up for anything we lack here. Any joy we surrender here is garbage, Paul says, compared to Christ.

I probably won't fill this room preaching that the best this world has to offer is garbage. But that doesn't make it any less true, or any less worth preaching. Our country is full of people who put themselves first. That's why 56 percent of men reportedly don't take care of their own children. Fifty-six percent. One in two men — not raising their kids, not paying child support, not supporting the mother. One in two.

If good parents know anything, it's that sometimes you have to put your child first. But that's not the only thing that comes first. Our priorities should be God, then family, then ourselves. Modern self-love says to flip that — put yourself at the top. Trust yourself. Trust your heart.

But what does the Bible say about our hearts? "The Lord saw that the wickedness of man was great on the earth, and that every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually." — Genesis 6:5. Or as Calvin put it: the heart of man is a perpetual factory of idols.

Our value doesn't come from within us. It comes from God. I read a book called True North built around the idea that apart from God, the compass of our heart points away from him — away from the things God wants us to do, be, desire, and experience. But with God's help, the compass can point north again. True north.

With God, there is nothing we can't work through together. We don't need love of self so much as we need love of God.


"Lovers of money" comes next in Paul's list. We have all met people for whom the dollar was their god. First Timothy 6:10 says money is the root of all kinds of evil. The Amplified Translation puts it this way: "For the love of money [that is, the greedy desire for it and the willingness to gain it unethically] is a root of all sorts of evil, and some by longing for it have wandered away from the faith and pierced themselves [through and through] with many sorrows."

Most of the time I've heard this verse quoted, it comes from the King James, which is the only translation that reads "the root of all evil." Every other worthwhile translation has "all sorts" or "all kinds." The difference matters.

The Greek word translated "all kinds" is πάντων (*pantōn*), the genitive plural of πᾶς (*pas*). You may recognize the prefix *pan-* from "pantheon" — *pan* meaning ALL, *theon* (lowercase g) meaning gods. *Pantōn* is simply the inflected form of that same root: "of all things," "the entirety of." And the word translated "evil" can be rendered evil in its widest possible sense. Paul wants us thinking very broadly here.

The reason the ESV, NIV, and NASB moved to "all kinds of evil" rather than "all evil" is probably this: the source of *all* evil isn't a what — it's a who. Either way, the text is clear. Money leads to problems. More money, more problems. Whether it's all evil or all kinds of evil, love of money is the root according to Scripture.


Pride is next. If you've never struggled with pride, raise your hand. Just kidding, of course. I think every one of us has — whether we've done good work and let it go to our heads, or we've bought into the pop psychology narrative that we have to think better and better of ourselves, or we've had too much pride in our country. We've all been there. And it's prideful to think you haven't struggled with pride.

Arrogance follows. Then haughtiness, a sense of superiority, abusiveness, disobedience to parents, ungratefulness, unholiness — verse 3 — heartlessness, being unappeasable, slanderous, without self-control, brutal, not loving good — verse 4 — treacherous, reckless, swollen with conceit, lovers of pleasure rather than lovers of God — verse 5 — having the appearance of godliness, but denying its power.

I think the most pernicious entry on the whole list is that last one: having the appearance of godliness. The outer form without the inner reality.


I've been reading *The Way of Kings* by one of my favorite authors. I've read it a few times. There's a character named Amaram who is renowned throughout the story as a man of honor — at a time when honor among the nobility is essentially a fairy tale. The main character is a soldier in Amaram's army, and he is proud to serve under him. Until one day Amaram betrays him — has him branded and sold into slavery.

Not too different from what Joseph's brothers did to him. Jealous of his coat and his relationship with their father, when the moment came they threw him in a pit and sold him to a passing caravan. Can you imagine the betrayal? Someone you loved, selling you to strangers? Someone you trusted, turning on you in your most vulnerable moment?

It also brings to mind Peter and Judas, on the night Jesus was betrayed.

Both were among the Twelve — men Jesus had sent out to do miracles in his name. One writer observes that while Judas struggled with greed, Peter exhibited pride and a quick temper. There are several moments in the Gospels where Peter boldly claimed he would go to war for Jesus, that he would always do whatever it took for his Lord. That arrogance was so strong that even when Jesus predicted his denial, Peter could not change course. Matthew records it: "Peter answered him, 'Though they all fall away because of you, I will never fall away.' Jesus said to him, 'Truly, I tell you, this very night, before the rooster crows, you will deny me three times.' Peter said to him, 'Even if I must die with you, I will not deny you!' And all the disciples said the same."

And yet — on the night Jesus was betrayed, Judas kissed him on the cheek to mark him for arrest.

And Peter? Three times before the rooster crowed, he denied knowing him. The first time it was to a servant girl — someone of considerably lower station, both because she was female and because she was a servant. Imagine walking through a jail and having an inmate point at you and say, "That's the one." How much weight would anyone give that accusation? Not much. She was the least threatening person in the room. And yet when she spoke, Peter denied Jesus and walked away in a huff.

But at the end of the day there is a key difference between these two men — both of whom let Jesus down.

Judas called Jesus "Rabbi." That's a name of respect, but it stops well short of acknowledging who Jesus actually was. Notice that every other disciple called Jesus "Lord." Think about it — you wouldn't address the queen of England simply as "ma'am." It's respectful in most situations, but for the queen it's a slight. She's the queen and you know it. Less than "Your Majesty" is a failure to honor who she is.

Judas calling Jesus "Rabbi" betrays his inner belief. His orthopraxy — the way our beliefs shape our actions — reveals that he had real doubts about whether Jesus was the Messiah. He saw Jesus as a wise teacher who did impressive things.

Peter, by contrast, knew who Jesus was. He recognized him as Lord. He understood the truth of the gospel — that Jesus was more than a man, more than a teacher. That he was God.

Judas had the appearance of godliness. Peter was godly.

Both men sinned. Both betrayed Jesus. The difference is that Peter knew who Jesus was, and acknowledged him as Lord.


You and I are going to fall short somewhere, sometime. Most of us, multiple times. Sometimes we will be greedy, or bad neighbors, or we will worship at the altar of political idolatry. Somewhere, sometime, we are going to pull a Peter or a Judas — betray Christ with a careless word, a selfish act, something more. Maybe pride. Maybe love of money. Maybe love of self.

I love this hymn — I wish we sang it more:

*How deep the Father's love for us,*
*How vast beyond all measure,*
*That He would give His only Son*
*To make a wretch His treasure.*
*How great the pain of searing loss —*
*The Father turns His face away*
*As wounds which mar the chosen One*
*Bring many sons to glory.*

*Behold the Man upon a cross,*
*My guilt upon His shoulders.*
*Ashamed, I hear my mocking voice*
*Call out among the scoffers.*

It was my sin. My selfish pride. My greed. My idolatrous heart. My love of self. My love of money. My arrogance. My abusiveness. My disobedience. My ungratefulness. My heartlessness. My slander. My brutality. My love of pleasure. And yours too.

*That held Him there*
*Until it was accomplished.*
*His dying breath has brought me life —*
*I know that it is finished.*

*I will not boast in anything,*
*No gifts, no pow'rs, no wisdom,*
*But I will boast in Jesus Christ,*
*His death and resurrection.*
*Why should I gain from His reward?*
*I cannot give an answer.*
*But this I know with all my heart:*
*His wounds have paid my ransom.*

"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." — Romans 3:23.

That's where we start. And the gospel is where we go from there.
