# Judges 6:36–40
## *Lord, Just Once More*

**Text:** Judges 6:36–40

> "Then Gideon said to God, 'If you will save Israel by my hand, as you have said, behold, I am laying a fleece of wool on the threshing floor. If there is dew on the fleece alone, and it is dry on all the ground, then I shall know that you will save Israel by my hand, as you have said.' And it was so. When he rose early next morning and squeezed the fleece, he wrung enough dew from the fleece to fill a bowl with water. Then Gideon said to God, 'Let not your anger burn against me; let me speak just once more. Please let me test just once more with the fleece. Please let it be dry on the fleece only, and on all the ground let there be dew.' And God did so that night; and it was dry on the fleece only, and on all the ground there was dew."

---

## Introduction

We're in Judges 6 tonight, starting at verse 36. If you brought your Bible, I hope it's already open — if not, follow along.

Let me catch you up from last week. Gideon was standing in the middle of an angry mob. He had torn down the altar to Ba'al in his father's field — a massive structure — knocked down the Asherah pole beside it, and burned two bulls on the rubble. The people of the town and the surrounding area came looking for his head.

His father Joash stepped out and cut the crowd off cold. *If Ba'al is a god, let Ba'al defend himself.*

The crowd dispersed.

But war is coming. A confederation of kingdoms — Midianites, Amalekites, and the peoples of the East — has crossed the Jordan and is camped in the valley of Jezreel. They are Ba'al worshipers, all of them, and they are furious about what happened to that altar.

And God has already told Gideon: *Go in this your strength. I will be with you.*

Not suggested. Not implied. Directly commissioned. By an angel who appeared to this man personally and sat with him at his threshing floor.

And Gideon puts out a fleece.

---

## The Anatomy of a Fleece

First thing I want you to see: a fleece demonstrates belief. You don't lay wool on a threshing floor and ask God to act on it if you don't believe God exists. There's faith underneath this — even if it's not strong faith. Most of us have been here. I've been here. You believe enough to ask. You just don't quite believe enough to move without a sign.

God can honor these. He does, situationally. He honored this one.

But notice what's actually happening. Gideon has already received his orders. God didn't come to him with a question — he came with a command. And Gideon is saying, essentially: *I believe you could do this. But before I'm willing to obey you, you're going to have to prove it to me.*

That is not faith. That is a negotiation.

And yet — God stoops to it. Twice.

First night: wet fleece, dry ground. He wrung the wool out in the morning and filled a bowl with water. Everything around it was bone dry.

Second night: Gideon goes back. *Please don't be angry with me. Just once more.* Dry fleece, soaked ground. God did it again.

God didn't rebuke him. He didn't lecture him. He just honored the request, both times. I find that extraordinary. It tells you something about the God we serve. He is not fragile. He is not offended by the weakness of a man who believes *in* him but doesn't quite believe *him* yet. He stooped to a wet blanket on a threshing floor — twice — because the man he called was still becoming the man he called him to be.

---

## Why Gideon Asked

Two motivations. Both are in the text.

**Fear.** Remember — Gideon tore down those altars at night. Not in the afternoon. Not in front of witnesses. At night, when no one could see him, because he was afraid of his father and afraid of the townspeople. And that was a small act compared to what he's being asked to do now.

The force assembling against Israel was enormous. We learn from Judges 8:10, after the battle, that 120,000 men had fallen and 15,000 were still running — meaning Gideon was going up against roughly 135,000 men. This wasn't one kingdom. It was a coalition. Midianites, Amalekites, peoples of the East — all of them together, pointed at Israel. Judges 7 tells us their camels were without number, like sand on the seashore. They filled the valley like locusts.

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery cited 85,000 cavalry in addition to the 135,000 infantry. This figure does not appear in the biblical text of Judges 6–8. The Bible describes the enemy's camels as beyond counting but gives no cavalry count. Please verify the source before preaching it, or drop it. The 135,000 total is confirmed from Judges 8:10.*]

It is a reasonable thing to be afraid of. Even a brave man measures what he's walking into.

**Peace.** Gideon had named his altar *Jehovah-shalom* — "The Lord sends peace." That name tells you who this man was before the battle and who he still wanted to be. He wasn't looking for a fight. He knew what war cost — to the enemy, to his own men, to the families those men wouldn't come home to.

Charles Spurgeon observed that Gideon had lived largely in peace and would have preferred to continue that way. [*Editorial note: Attributed to Spurgeon in the oral delivery. Please verify the specific source — sermon title or volume in the Metropolitan Tabernacle Pulpit series — before preaching it as a Spurgeon quote. If it can't be confirmed, the observation stands without the attribution.*]

God called him a mighty man of valor. But the man himself wanted quiet fields and a quiet family. Those two things don't contradict each other. Sometimes the men who hate war most are the most careful with the lives of the men they lead into it.

## God Uses Broken People

Here's what all of this tells us, and it's not a small thing.

God didn't call a man who had it together. He called a man who was afraid. Who demanded proof before he'd obey. Who asked God twice to act before he was willing to move.

The old saying holds: God doesn't call the qualified. He qualifies the called.

David — a man after God's own heart — had a man killed to cover what he had done with that man's wife. [*Editorial note: The oral delivery uses the word "rape" to describe David's relationship with Bathsheba. This is a serious interpretive claim. The Hebrew text of 2 Samuel 11 describes David sending for Bathsheba and taking her; Nathan's parable frames it as the rich man seizing the poor man's lamb by force. Given the extreme power differential — a king summoning a subject — many scholars, including conservative ones, argue the encounter cannot have been genuinely consensual. This is a defensible reading, but it is interpretive. If you preach it this way, do so intentionally and be ready to explain it. If not, "had a man killed to cover what he had done with the man's wife" carries the moral weight without the contested claim.*]

Paul, who wrote roughly half the New Testament [*Editorial note: The oral delivery says "two-thirds." Paul authored 13 letters, 14 if Hebrews is included. The New Testament contains 27 books. 13 of 27 is approximately 48 percent. "Roughly half" is accurate; "two-thirds" is not.*] — stood in a place of honor while men and women were stoned to death for their faith in Jesus Christ. He approved. He considered it good work.

God used him to write the letters the church has been reading for two thousand years.

If he can use people like that, he can use you. That is not a comfort — it is a declaration. Apart from Christ, we are incapable of doing good. But God looks at the mess and says: *I can use that one.* What a mercy — to be allowed to participate in service to the living God.

---

## The Weight of Command

What Gideon couldn't escape was the weight of the lives that were going to depend on his decision.

I read a book about George W. Bush a while back — his account of the days after September 11th. He was being moved from place to place, everyone competing for his attention. People wanted a statement, a response, some sign that someone was in charge. He was slow to give one, and he took criticism for it.

What the book said was that he had spent that time in prayer. In his flesh he wanted to go to war — that was his immediate instinct. But he also knew that men were going to die for whatever decision he made. He wasn't willing to make it without first bringing it to God. He prayed for guidance. He prayed that if they went to war, those men's blood wouldn't be spent wastefully. He prayed for a quick conclusion and for the possibility of peace on the other side.

Like him or hate him — that was about the most useful thing he could have been doing in those hours.

John Bunyan put it this way: *"You can do more than pray after you have prayed, but you cannot do more than pray until you have prayed. Pray often, for prayer is a shield to the soul, a sacrifice to God, and a scourge to Satan."*

Do nothing until you've prayed. After you've prayed, you have options.

Both Gideon and Bush felt the weight of the lives that would be spent on their behalf. Neither entered it casually.

## A Shadow of Gethsemane

As I've been sitting in this passage this week, I keep seeing another garden.

Turn with me to Luke 22:41.

> "And he withdrew from them about a stone's throw, and knelt down and prayed, saying, 'Father, if you are willing, remove this cup from me. Nevertheless, not my will, but yours, be done.' And there appeared to him an angel from heaven, strengthening him. And being in agony he prayed more earnestly; and his sweat became like great drops of blood falling down to the ground."

Gideon is on the eve of a battle for his people's freedom from an enemy that has crushed them. Christ is on the eve of a battle for ours — against sin, death, hell, and Satan. Both knew what was coming. Gideon knew the cost of war. Christ knew what the cross was going to be.

He would be scourged. He would carry his own cross through the city, mocked by the people he had come to save. Soldiers would gamble for his clothing at the foot of the cross. And then they would nail him.

We say *hand* and picture the palm. But the biblical use of that word, in that era, included the wrist and the lower forearm. The nails went there — through the bones of the wrist — because the forearm could bear the weight of a man. Through the palm alone, the hand tears. [*Editorial note: This is a commonly cited claim in evangelical teaching on crucifixion, associated with the research of Dr. Pierre Barbet and others. It is widely taught but not universally settled by archaeologists and anatomists. Consider qualifying it: "medical researchers who have studied Roman crucifixion believe the nails went through the wrist rather than the palm."*]

He went through all of it willingly. He asked the Father if there was another way. When the answer was no — he went.

Now watch where the shadow of Gideon holds, and where it breaks.

Gideon named his altar *Jehovah-shalom*. The Lord sends peace. He named it before the battle — before anything had been decided, before a soldier had moved. As if the peace was already on its way. Jesus is the peace that altar was pointing toward. He himself is our peace — Ephesians 2 — having broken down the dividing wall through the blood of his cross.

Gideon was threshing wheat when the angel found him. Christ will separate wheat from chaff finally and completely at the last judgment.

Gideon's ministry began in secret — hidden, at night, with only a handful of servants watching — and became very public before it was over. Christ's ministry had the same movement: thirty years of silence in Nazareth, then water into wine at Cana, and then not quietly at all.

But here is where the shadow breaks.

Gideon was protected. Jesus was handed over. Gideon's three hundred went home. Jesus went to the grave. Gideon was betrayed by no one. Jesus was betrayed by a friend.

And both of them won.

Gideon delivered his people from the Midianites. Jesus delivered his from sin and death. Gideon saved them for a generation. Jesus saved his for eternity.

## The Gospel

That's why he went through it. Not as an example. Not as a teacher. As a substitute — taking what we had coming so that we could receive what he had earned.

If you've never trusted him — tonight is the night. He went through all of it because he wanted a relationship with you. His Father wanted one with you.
