A few weeks ago we spent half the service in prayer — for our country and for each other. We are going to do that again tonight. There is such a tremendous need for prayer around the country and around the world.

## I. A Call to Prayer

### Law Enforcement

This thing with police being hunted by criminals just breaks my heart.

I have the utmost respect for anyone who is or was in the armed forces, or in law enforcement. I told Wyley — and I need to tell the girls — these people are heroes. They put their lives on the line to protect us. A cop is the noble shield between us and the criminals. Yes, there are some bad apples. But those are the exception, not the rule. We need to be in prayer for the men and women who put their lives on the line to keep us safe.

### The Persecuted Church

We also need to be praying for the persecuted church.

There has been another massacre. Eight thousand Nigerians murdered by Boko Haram. Do we hear about it on the news? No. From the same church, nearly two hundred young girls were kidnapped — and it is speculated that they were sold into sex slavery. Likely forced to take drugs. In another part of Nigeria, twenty-four Christians were murdered by Boko Haram.

In Sudan, students are being radicalized by their colleges. Three groups of students have come out and publicly declared their allegiance to Islam. In Egypt, a man was arrested for distributing Bibles. A house church in Djibouti was discovered meeting illegally, and every person present had a finger chopped off.

We sit here in America in our comfortable complacency while our brothers and sisters are being persecuted and sent to jail around the world.

We sit here with two cars in the garage, air conditioning in our houses, hot clean water from our faucets — in luxury compared to the rest of the world. And yet, if I were to ask for a show of hands, statistically less than half of you would have read your Bible in the last week. Only two of you would have encouraged someone you know to attend any church. And of those two, only one would have shared the gospel.

I like to think the people in our church are better than that. But it is still something we need to be thinking about, and praying about. We have to live with gospel intentionality.

## II. Romans 4 — Abraham

We're in Romans 4 tonight.

Some people wonder how we can relate to God. Abraham is a good example of that. God spoke — Abraham listened. God commanded — Abraham obeyed.

This is review, because we have talked about Abraham in Hebrews. God told Abraham to head out into the desert. So Abraham packed up everything he owned, and basically moved a small city out into the wilderness. Along the way, God blessed him.

Someone might say: see — Abraham did something, and the result was salvation. But the truth is, Abraham had to believe before he ever would have wandered out into that desert with his family and all his possessions. He was so confident in God that he put not only his own life on the line, but the lives of his wife, his children, and several thousand friends and employees. Faith came first. The obedience followed.

Then God told Abraham to sacrifice his son. His only son. The son that he loved.

Heartbroken, Abraham trusts God and heads up the mountain. They collect wood, build an altar. He ties his son up and places him on it. As he moves in with the knife, God sends a way out. A ram, caught in a thicket.

I cannot even imagine how relieved he would have been. My anxiety would be through the roof. I would be able to hear my own pulse. Body flooded with adrenaline. And then — there is the ram, stuck in the thicket.

That last part makes me smile, and for other reasons.

You remember the ram we had — Razzle? He loved to head-butt everything. Anyone. He chased my little cousin and pinned her against the fence. He rammed my uncle from behind and knocked him clean over. He head-butted every single fence post he could reach every time we walked or drove past. The fence, trees, even the hand that fed him.

I can just see Razzle out in the woods, charging through a tangle of vines, and getting himself stuck right there — until the Lord's appointed time. Several thousand years later, and a ram still acts like a ram.

## III. The Dog, the Door, and Waiting on the Lord

That reminds me of my dog this week.

She is doing really well — thank you all for asking. She goes in to be spayed on the 25th. But she figured something out this week. She figured out that if she pushes hard enough on the middle of my mobile home door, it warps just a little and she can get out.

When she was a puppy I tried to keep her from learning that. I would lock her inside, and then hold the door. She would hurl herself at it trying to get out, but no avail. So up until Thursday night, she saw that door the same way she saw any other wall — a solid obstacle. Something she couldn't get through.

Thursday night I ran out to David Garner's house to drop something off. In the twenty-five minutes I was gone, she got so desperate to find me that she charged the door, warped it just enough that it popped open, and she got out. When I got home, she was frantically searching the yard. Running from one end to the other, back and forth in a pattern. She was trying to catch my scent so she could follow me.

Praise God she didn't find it.

But here's the thing. That door was more of a mental obstacle than a physical one. She couldn't see past it — and so she grew tired of trying. The barrier was mostly in her mind.

Our bondage to sin is a lot like that. We can't see past it sometimes to the victory that is on the other side. All we can see is the obstacle. We don't know that — like my door — it is not nearly as big an obstacle as it appears.

What is it Pastor Bob says? You and God are the majority.

It is true here too. And if she had just waited a few more minutes, she wouldn't have had to beat her head against the door alone. I would have opened it for her. God is willing to do that for us as well. He is capable of removing these obstacles. Sometimes we just have to wait on the Lord.

> *But they who wait for the LORD shall renew their strength; they shall mount up with wings like eagles; they shall run and not be weary; they shall walk and not faint.* — Isaiah 40:31

What obstacles do you have in your life? Where are you weary? Where could you use a little more waiting on the Lord? This promise is for you.

## IV. Romans 4:4 — Wages, Not a Gift

Let's focus in on verse 4.

*"Now to the one who works, his wages are not counted as a gift but as his due."* (Romans 4:4, ESV)

We can't manipulate God into giving us what we want. You can't help enough old ladies across the street to obligate God into saving you. You can't just go to church and expect that God is going to save you one day.

As we are going to see in a couple of weeks — the wages of sin is death. Before Christ, we sin like it is our job. From the moment we wake up to the moment we go to sleep. Anything we do, or try to do, is done from a place of sin. We can't help but be sinful, because apart from Christ our righteousness is as filthy rags. Without him, the best we can do is like a used tissue. And what we deserve for that — our wage for that — is death.

We are rightly deserving of death and separation from God. If anything, our due for the life we have lived is not salvation. Our wages are death.

But praise God — he did not give us what we deserve.

We deserve to burn in hell. But we are given life. We deserve punishment, but are made princes and princesses. We deserve hell fire and brimstone, but God gives us eternal life.

## V. The Chains We Were In

I was reading about the slave trade this week — how slave ships worked in this country hundreds of years ago.

I came across a couple of accounts that really made me think.

They used to pack men, women, and children into the slave decks like decks of cards — to quote one account. The slave deck on a hundred-ton ship was about five feet tall, bisected. Men and children were made to crawl in, lie on their sides, and lean against the next man or child. Women had a similar deck — except frequently the women were not bound, so that they could be made available to the sailors, willingly or otherwise.

The men were chained to each other and to the boat.

One particularly grisly account — written by a Christian man of some note, a man who became a friend of William Wilberforce — described how, in order to send a message to the rest of the slaves, captors would take the most spirited man among them and cut his fingers off one at a time, in front of everyone. Then the hand at the wrist. The arm at the elbow. At the shoulder. The foot at the ankle. The leg at the knee. At the hip. And finally, the head. They would toss the body over the side, and keep the head as a warning.

This man wrote that the Africans believed they had been captured by savages — because they had never seen man behave toward man in that way. Murder, yes. But a slow, deliberate, torturous death designed to produce fear in everyone who witnessed it? It was beyond their comprehension.

Another account described what happened when ships needed to outrun pirates or, later, the law. They would throw men and women over the side to lighten the load so the crew could reach safety.

These are the images that should come to mind when we read about bondage to sin.

Apart from Christ, we are those men, women, and children — chained together, dismembered for sport and for control, tossed over the side like so much ballast at the whim of the devil.

That is our state apart from Christ. That is the state of our loved ones who don't know him.

How can we not try to free them? How can we not point them toward the keys to their freedom? And if you are in that bondage — how can you not reach out, take the key, and be set free?

God loves you. He has a plan for your life. A plan for your welfare and not for harm. A plan that ends in eternity in heaven. It is that simple. If you have never come to a point where you have asked Jesus to pay the price of your sins, please do not let another moment pass.
