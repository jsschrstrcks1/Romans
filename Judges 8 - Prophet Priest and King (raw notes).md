# Judges 8:22-end — Gideon: Prophet, Priest, and King

## Opening — The Worm Preacher

I heard a story this week about an old preacher back in the 50s who was preaching against vice and towards being holy. He was one of those preachers that said "don't drink or smoke or chew, and do not go with girls that do." He's not a pastor whose church I would have attended long though, he had a thing against chocolate. Any way, he brought up four jars at the start of the service and set them on an altar, one was a jar of hard liquor, another a jar full of smoke, a third a jar full of chocolate, and finally a fourth full of healthy good fertile soil. In each jar he put a worm.

At the end of the service one jar at a time he opened the jars. The worm in the jar of liquid was dead, the worm in the jar of cigar smoke was dead, the worm in the jar of chocolate was smiling, but also dead. The worm in the good soil? Still kicking. The preacher preached in a way that implied that if you can just be good enough (and skip chocolate) maybe you can reconcile yourself to God.

That is a lie from the pits of hell. Lets look at Gideon.

**Read Judges 8:22-end**

## Gideon as King

In our passage tonight I want you to notice that Gideon sort of acted as a defacto king. The New American Commentary gives five reasons. I'll give you a couple.

For one thing a king can make any demand he wants of his people and expect them to comply. He told his subjects "from all of Israel" that they had to give him a golden ear ring each. The sum total was said to be 1700 shekels of gold. I looked up this unit of measurement and sought to translate it into modern day terms, and found that there is a discrepancy regarding the weight of a shekel. One source says 4 ounces each, the other says 11 grams each. The difference is significant. If the shekel weighed 4 oz, 1700 would have been about 8 million dollars in today's money. If 11 grams, its still a princely sum at $800,000. Either way it's a lot of money, and would have been a significant demand on his people. This is on top of all of the golden camel collars that were taken off at least 100,000 perhaps 500,000 camels. This would have been millions upon millions of dollars. It would have had deeper significance though. Who is it that collects fealty from vassals? Taxes from a subject? He declines the title, but takes actions that suggest he is the defacto king.

Earlier in the chapter for instance some of his family is killed, and he invokes a custom of the day used by kings. When the family of royalty was killed, the army would go after the blood of the killers. Not until the killers blood had been shed could there be peace when a member of the royal family was killed. So when his brothers, the sons of his mother were killed, he marshaled the army on his personal business of revenge, of seeking the blood of his enemy. Finally, he secured all of the symbols of power of the people he conquered not for Israel but for himself. Remember the crescent shaped symbols he took from the leaders? In any case these are the actions of a king.

## Gideon as Prophet

Gideon likewise was to some extent a prophet. You may see on TV and hear other people declaring themselves to be prophets. The prophetic ministry has two parts — foretelling and forthtelling. By that definition technically any preacher is a prophet as they are forth telling. But these days when people declare themselves prophets they do so because they think they know God's will or plan for the future. God doesn't necessarily operate that way these days. While nothing is outside of the scope of God's power, the canon of scripture is closed. It has been for almost two thousand years. If God is coming down and speaking to people about what's to come shouldn't we be recording it? Isn't scripture God's word? Shouldn't they be written down if he's revealing things like that to people? But if someone is going to claim the name of prophet, one who foretells, one must also accept the consequences of being wrong. In bible times if someone claimed to be a prophet and was wrong once, they were to be stoned. I know a guy that tracks people that claim to be prophets and has an online index of all the things people have prophesied. Pretty much every day someone proves themselves wrong, and he makes a post declaring so and so to be a false prophet. He's always crushed, and I frequently remind him that God doesn't normally operate in this manner any more.

But as I said it was different in bible times, and we know for a fact that God spoke to Gideon through an angel, giving him a glimpse of the future. Gideon was a prophet.

## Gideon as Priest

Vs 26ff — How many of you remember what an ephod was in the old testament? David in 1st Samuel 30. David had been out at war, and while gone their city had been sacked, and all the women kidnapped. The city burned, and when they found it David was not sure what to do. So he sent for the ephod of the high priest ⚠️ *[Note: 1 Sam 30:7-8 says Abiathar brought the ephod to David; David inquired of the Lord through it but did not wear it himself]* and prayed to God seeing if he should pursue those who had taken their women. God told him to pursue them, and David obeyed, and it was a great victory. But this is the way the old testament talks about the ephod. It was a garment worn by the high priest himself. It was made of 28 unique types of material ⚠️ *[Note: the number 28 may be a confusion with Exodus 28 (the chapter describing the ephod); actual distinct materials number ~20-25 depending on how counted]*, with precious gems, gold, and onyx worked into the getup. Rashi the ancient Jewish writer said an ephod is like a ladies riding girdle. Modern scholars would liken it to an apron, with gold and onyx shoulder pads. In any case the religious and theological significance would have been obvious. In making and possessing this ephod, he was setting himself up as a high priest.

## Unlike Christ — Gideon Failed in All Three

Unlike Christ, who is a good prophet, a good priest, and a good king, Gideon failed in all three areas.

### Failed as King

He wanted the perks of being a king without the responsibility. He took a tax, he kept the signets of power, but eschewed the title. He returned to his little tiny town, and lived an extravagant lifestyle funded by the tax he collected. But it doesn't appear that he really governed. People viewed him as a king, they put their faith in him as king, but Gideon was only king when there was gold in it for him.

### Failed as Prophet

When things were tough, he demanded God do miracles to prove himself. What does the new testament say about those who require signs and wonders? Matthew 12:39 — But Jesus replied, "Only an evil, adulterous generation would demand a miraculous sign." Again asking for a sign is one thing, but demanding God's obeisant obedience is a whole different ball game. Its an effort at manipulation and its wrong.

### Failed as Priest

It struck me hard this week that Gideon basically erected an idol towards the end of his ministry in the exact same place he tore down an idol at the start of his ministry. Read with me vs 27 — "and all Israel whored after it." If this had been an ephod used in worship of God, do you think God would have had the author of Judges use such strong negative language? God calls his people whores. He may have had some similarities to Christ, but he failed in all the most important ways. Lets review.

Gideon — one of the best men in the old testament. Certainly the best man of his generation. Caused an entire nation to repent, God used him to defeat a hundred thousand soldiers without raising his sword. Yet the people put their faith in this timid man rather than in the Lion of Judah. His body had not been cold long before the entire nation of Israel had again turned their back on God and turned to these pagan idols.

## Old Testament Types — Dim Reflections of Christ

Lets look at it another way.

Every time we see an image of Christ in the old testament its meant to show us just how far short man's efforts are of reaching God's standards. Gideon tried to manipulate God. Struggled with fear. Gideon doubted God's word. But God used Gideon any way to point to the one who would come later and be able to fulfill all of the prophecies all throughout scripture. There are something like 350 prophecies about Christ in the bible, all written at least 400 years before Christ with many many written thousands of years before Christ.

The whole old testament is yearning, is groaning for its resolution that can only come in Christ. Everything points forward to Christ. Every effort by man falls short. Lets think about a few of those.

- **David** was good, and he looks like Christ a little bit, but he fails to live up to the standard and had people murdered.
- **Moses** was good, and he wrote the law that proves we fall short, but it proved he fell short of God's standard as well.
- **Adam** was the first man, and one of only two men ever born without sin. Even Adam failed to live up to God's standard.
- **King Solomon** the wisest man to ever exist. Was so earnest and served with abandon and so God promised him any one thing he wanted, and Solomon asked for wisdom. God gave it to him, and despite the auspicious start, he quickly chose the foolish path and married hundreds of women, and then allowed them to turn his head and lead him down the primrose path of idolatry. Molech, and hundreds of other abominable gods Solomon made sacrifices to. Solomon fell short in many ways, clearly. Can you imagine having 700 wives and 300 concubines? Craziness. Ladies can you imagine the headaches that would cause? The drama? The infighting? A foolish choice indeed.

These heroes of the faith were good men, but compared to Jesus they are dim memories. Compared to the one who loved you enough to die for you, they are like but a dim reflection. Its like when a kid draws something for you, vs Monet, or Rembrandt, or Picasso, or Dali, or Gonsalves. The sentiment is sweet. But your seven year old doesn't have a chance to compete with an old master. But what God did was he set aside his Monet, and chose the best you could offer. He chose Curt, and Bob, and Greg, despite the fact the best they could do was draw a lopsided structurally unsound house that's too small for the people standing in front of it. Kristina, he traded his perfect son, who had never sinned, who had done everything exactly perfectly right, for the sum total of you, every good and bad thing you've ever done. He traded his son — the son whom he loves — for you. Humbling isn't it?

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm Pastoral Heart Reaching for Typology — But Not Yet a Sermon**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A pastoral opening of Judges 8:22–end that correctly identifies Christ as the fulfillment Gideon could only shadow while falling short in structured exposition, gospel proclamation, conscience application, and actual exhortation.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**PASS — with conditions.**

**Scopus Test**: Marginally passes. The sermon's thesis — "Gideon failed as prophet, priest, and king; only Christ fulfills all three" — does arise organically from the passage's trajectory and from the broader Judges narrative logic. The main point is defensible as a text-driven claim.

**Authority Test**: Passes. The preacher does not soften the text's hard edges; "all Israel whored after it" is cited directly and commented on honestly. The preacher submits to the text's negative verdict on Gideon.

**Condition**: These are raw notes, not a finished sermon. The evaluation will treat them accordingly and flag where the scaffolding is not yet load-bearing.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Two flags triggered. Score capped at 69.**

- **Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: There is no differentiated application. The sermon names individuals (Curt, Bob, Greg, Kristina) in the closing illustration but this is not application — it is personalization of an illustration. No hearer is told what to believe, repent of, or change. The conscience is not pressed in any category of Perkins' taxonomy.

- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: The sermon ends mid-illustration. There is no "therefore" moment. The congregation is asked to feel humbled ("Humbling isn't it?") but is never summoned to respond. No call to faith, repentance, obedience, or pursuit is made. The sermon explains and illustrates but never commands.

*Note: Flag 6 (inaccessibility) is NOT triggered — the register is appropriately populist and the congregation would be able to track the main movement.*

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 13 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10 pts): 6* — The sermon covers the passage's key episodes but incompletely. The ephod, the tax, the blood vengeance, and the idolatry are noted. However, the crucial verses on Gideon's seventy sons, Abimelech, and the post-Gideon collapse (vv.29–35) are not developed. The argument is suggestive rather than demonstrated from the text. The preacher works *around* the passage more than *through* it.
- *Contextual Awareness (5 pts): 3* — There is awareness that Gideon is set within a larger Judges cycle, and the typological arc toward Christ is theologically sound. But the canonical context of the Judges decline pattern and how Gideon fits within the book's downward spiral is not named.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5 pts): 2* — The ephod discussion contains a factual imprecision (the "28 materials" claim is uncertain; the 1 Samuel 30 reference to David mischaracterizes how the ephod was used — David did not wear it, Abiathar brought it). Exegetical detail is used illustratively rather than interpretively.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5 pts): 2* — The Fallen Condition Focus (FCF) is never named. The sermon does not articulate *why* this text exists or what human condition it was written to expose. The implied FCF (the futility of human saviors) is present but undeveloped.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7 pts): 5* — The typological move is legitimate. Gideon-as-dim-reflection-of-Christ is organically rooted in the passage rather than imposed from outside. The OT types section extends this well. However the connection remains at the level of structural contrast (prophet/priest/king) rather than covenantal depth.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3 pts): 2* — The 350-prophecy claim and the groaning-of-the-OT language are present and directionally sound, though the number "350" is casually asserted without grounding.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4 pts): 3* — The sermon is not doctrinally careless. The closing exchange language ("he traded his son for you") is warm and pastorally motivated but imprecise — it risks implying a commercial transaction rather than a substitutionary atonement. Penal substitution is gestured toward but not named or clarified.
- *Confessional Alignment (4 pts): 2* — No contradiction of Reformed theology, but no engagement with covenant theology despite the obvious opportunity the prophet/priest/king framework provides. The munus triplex is invoked without covenantal scaffolding.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (5 pts): 2* — The gospel is present in seed form. "He traded his perfect son for you" is moving but not a gospel proclamation. Justification by faith is not articulated. What must someone do with the fact that Christ succeeded where Gideon failed? This is never answered.
- *Necessity of Christ (5 pts): 3* — Christ is necessary to the sermon's argument — the whole structure depends on contrasting Gideon's failure with Christ's success. This is genuine gospel necessity, not decoration.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (5 pts): 2* — The sermon moves toward Christ but stops short of the cross. There is no mention of the atonement, death, resurrection, or what Christ's success as prophet, priest, and king actually accomplished for sinners.

*Synagogue Test*: A morally serious non-Christian could sit through most of this sermon without offense. The Christ-section would be uncomfortable but the gospel is not sharp enough to draw blood. Partial flag — not a full moralistic drift but the gospel is significantly underpowered.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 5 / 15**

- *Specificity (5 pts): 2* — The personalization of the closing illustration (naming congregants) is a strength, but this is emotional specificity, not applicatory specificity. No concrete direction is given about what any hearer should now do or stop doing.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The typological framework is organically legitimate.** The prophet/priest/king structure is not imposed on the text — it genuinely arises from what Gideon does in Judges 8. This is theologically sound and provides a memorable scaffolding that a congregation can carry home. The contrast structure ("Gideon failed in all three; Christ succeeded in all three") is the right spine for this passage.

2. **The worm illustration is an excellent opening corrective.** Dismissing moralism as "a lie from the pits of hell" in the very first minutes establishes the right anti-moralistic trajectory and shows pastoral discernment. The preacher instinctively knows what he is fighting against.

3. **The personalized closing illustration carries genuine pastoral warmth.** Naming specific congregants and speaking directly to them ("Kristina, he traded his perfect son for you") is a high-risk, high-reward move that shows the preacher knows his people. The instinct is correct and the move is worth keeping.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The sermon has no exhortation and no application — it ends mid-illustration.** The final sentence ("Humbling isn't it?") is a rhetorical question, not a summons. The congregation leaves with a warm feeling and zero imperative. This is the most urgent structural deficiency. A gospel that generates no command has not yet become preaching.

2. **The gospel is present as sentiment but absent as proclamation.** "He traded his son for you" is moving but theologically incomplete. What does Christ's fulfillment as prophet, priest, and king actually accomplish for the sinner? What did his death as the true high priest accomplish that Gideon's ephod-idolatry pointed toward in negative? The atonement — penal substitution, propitiation, justification by faith — is entirely absent. An interested unbeliever would not know how to be saved from this sermon.

3. **The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is never named.** The sermon implicitly diagnoses the human problem as misplaced trust in human saviors, but this is never surfaced as the specific fallen condition the text addresses. Without naming the disease, the cure (Christ as the true and sufficient Prophet, Priest, and King) cannot land with diagnostic force. The hearer needs to understand *what is broken in them* before the solution has weight.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see that **the worm illustration and the gospel ending are actually saying the same thing** — both are anti-moralistic, both are pointing to grace. But the sermon never connects them. The worm preacher's error (self-reformation) and Gideon's error (being a failed human savior) and the congregation's implied error (trusting in human effort) are all facets of the same disease. The sermon could be dramatically tightened if this connection were made explicit: the worm preacher is us; Gideon is our best human effort; Christ is the only one who doesn't die in the jar.

Additionally, the preacher may not see that **Gideon's specific failures are more spiritually searching than the sermon uses them**. Gideon's demand for signs despite having already heard from God speaks directly to the congregation member who says "I'll believe when God proves himself to me." That is a live pastoral issue in any congregation and the text addresses it directly. This application potential is walked past.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The **personalization of the closing illustration** (naming congregants) feels like strong application but is not. Naming someone in an illustration is emotional intimacy, not applicatory force. Application tells a specific person what they must now do differently because of what the text says. Saying "Kristina, God traded his son for you" is beautiful but does not yet answer the question every hearer is implicitly asking: "So what do I do with that?" The emotional warmth of personal naming can mask the absence of actual direction — and in this sermon, it does.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**The ephod connection is the theological heart of the sermon and it is almost entirely undeveloped.** Gideon erects an ephod — a priestly instrument — in the same location where he tore down the altar of Baal. The preacher correctly notes this ("same place he tore down an idol... erected an idol"). But the theological depth here is extraordinary: Gideon's ephod becomes a functional idol precisely because it substitutes a religious form (priestly garment) for the living God. This is the most sophisticated and searching part of the passage — it addresses the congregation member who is religious but not worshiping God. Reformed congregations are especially susceptible to this. The text is directly addressing the danger of orthodox-looking idolatry, and the sermon walks past it in two sentences.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (4)

1. **Build the FCF into the introduction and let it govern the whole sermon.** Name the human condition this text addresses: "We keep looking for a human savior — someone who will rescue us, lead us, stand between us and our enemies. Every generation produces a Gideon. Every Gideon fails." This one sentence would do more structural work than any other single addition.

2. **Develop the Gideon-as-priest section into the sermon's theological climax.** Gideon's ephod is where the passage's theological gravity lies. The preacher should linger here. Contrast: Aaron made a golden calf when Moses was delayed; Gideon made a golden ephod when his prophetic ministry was over. Both are the same spiritual move — replacing the living God with a religious form we can control. Then pivot: "Jesus is the high priest who doesn't build an ephod — he is the ephod. He is the garment of righteousness we wear before the Father."

3. **Give the gospel a clear propositional backbone before the closing illustration.** Before naming congregants, articulate the gospel plainly: "Christ fulfilled every role Gideon failed. As prophet, he spoke God's word without requiring signs. As priest, he didn't make an idol of the instruments of worship — he *was* the sacrifice. As king, he didn't take the gold for himself — he gave himself for us. And he did this to accomplish what you cannot: to stand before God in your place." Then the personalized illustration becomes the emotional landing of something already grounded.

4. **Add a direct exhortation before the final line.** After the Monet/child illustration, add a clear summons: "So here is what I am asking you to do today. Stop looking for your Gideon. Stop putting your faith in the next leader, the next movement, the next revival. Turn to the one who has already won. Repent of your misplaced confidence. Trust in Christ — the prophet who speaks with final authority, the priest who offered himself, the king who reigns forever. That is the only faith that will not be buried with your hero."

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

**The rubric fits this text well with one qualification.** Judges 8 is Old Testament narrative, and the rubric's gospel centrality categories (15 pts) require more careful application. The standard is not that every OT narrative sermon must explicitly preach justification by faith, but that the sermon must organically connect the text to Christ and the gospel. This sermon earns credit for attempting that connection — the prophet/priest/king framework is a legitimate and historically grounded hermeneutical lens (the *munus triplex* traces to Calvin). However, the rubric's exhortation and application categories (23 combined points) apply with full force regardless of genre: even narrative preaching must call for a response. The genre does not excuse the absence of application. The rubric bends slightly on how the gospel is presented (OT narrative requires a longer canonical bridge) but does not bend on the requirement that hearers be addressed, searched, and summoned.
