# Sermon — Trusting God in Ambiguity
### Matthew 2:1–23

---

*[Read Matthew 2:1–23]*

---

## The Magi

When Matthew says "wise men from the east" — we picture three bearded kings on camels, riding out of somewhere exotic. The Christmas carols have settled on three because there were three gifts. Scripture never says how many there were. It says *wise men. Magi.*

Who were they? Scholars have debated this for centuries. The word *magi* — from which we get the word "magic" — referred to a class of scholars, astronomers, and royal advisors associated with the courts of the ancient Near East. Persian, Babylonian, possibly Arabian. Men who studied the heavens and interpreted what they saw for kings. These weren't street-corner fortune tellers. These were the scientists of their day — learned men with royal access who took a star seriously enough to travel hundreds of miles on its account.

And they ended up at the wrong address.

They came to Jerusalem. To the palace. It makes sense, doesn't it? If a king has been born, start at the palace. But Herod hadn't gotten a birth announcement. And Herod was not a man who handled surprises well.

---

## Herod

Herod the Great was one of the most capable political operators of the ancient world — and one of the most dangerous men who ever lived. By the time the Magi knocked on his door he had been ravaged by disease, and a mind that was never quite stable had curdled completely into something violent and paranoid. He had already executed his own sons, his wife, his wife's brother, his mother-in-law. Anyone he suspected of reaching for what he had — they died.

The historian Josephus describes his final years in terms that make your skin crawl. He ordered the arrest of prominent Jewish leaders before his death, with instructions to execute them the moment he died — so that there would be mourning in Israel when Herod passed, whether Israel felt like mourning or not. His own family talked him out of it. The order was never carried out.

This is the man who sent the Magi south to Bethlehem — about six miles from Jerusalem — with instructions to report back when they found the child.

He had no intention of going to worship.

---

## An Apparent Problem

Before we go further — some of you may have a study Bible with a note on this passage flagging a tension with Luke 2. Luke tells us that after the birth and the circumcision and the presentation at the temple, the family returned home to Nazareth. Matthew has them still in Bethlehem when the Magi arrive — in a house, not a stable. How does that work?

Here's the honest answer: we're dealing with two accounts that cover the same events from different angles and with different emphases. Luke compresses. His summary in 2:39 — "when they had performed everything according to the Law of the Lord, they returned into Galilee, to their own town of Nazareth" — skips the Egypt episode entirely. Matthew focuses on the Egypt trip, the massacre, the return. Neither account tells you everything that happened.

The Magi almost certainly didn't arrive the night of the birth. Matthew says they came to a *house*, not a stable. Herod killed boys two years old and under based on when the Magi first saw the star — which means Jesus could have been anywhere from a newborn to a toddler when they arrived. The family was still in Bethlehem. Luke's summary account and Matthew's detailed account fit together when you lay them side by side without forcing either one to tell a story it wasn't trying to tell.

Now — some of you, when you hear "apparent discrepancy," feel a knot form in your stomach. I want to address that directly.

---

## Can We Trust What We Have?

The question behind the question is always: *Is this book reliable?*

Here's what I want you to know.

We have approximately 5,800 manuscripts of the Greek New Testament — partial and complete. That number doesn't include the roughly 10,000 Latin manuscripts, or the thousands of copies in Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, and other ancient languages. Add it all up and you're looking at somewhere north of 25,000 copies of the New Testament in various languages. The earliest fragments date to the second century — within a generation or two of when the original documents were written.

Compare that to anything else from the ancient world.

Aristotle lived in the 4th century BC. We have 49 manuscripts of his work. The earliest copy dates to roughly 1,400 years after his death. Scholars still consider him well-attested by ancient standards — because by ancient standards, 49 copies is a lot.

Plato: roughly 7 surviving manuscripts. Earliest copy more than 1,200 years after he wrote.

Caesar's *Gallic Wars*: about 250 manuscripts.

The *Iliad* of Homer — the second most-attested work from the ancient world — has around 1,800 manuscripts. That sounds like a lot until you remember that the New Testament has it outnumbered more than three to one in Greek alone, and the *Iliad* copies show considerably more variation between them.

The New Testament has no serious competition in terms of manuscript evidence for any ancient document. None. And across those 5,800+ Greek manuscripts, the agreement is extraordinary. The variants that exist are mostly spelling differences and occasional duplicate lines. The variants that actually affect meaning or doctrine are a fraction of a percent. What we are reading now is, with overwhelming confidence, what was written down in the first century.

Jesus himself treated the Hebrew Scriptures as reliable — he quoted them, argued from them, staked his identity on them. Paul writes in 2 Timothy 3:16:

> *All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness.* — 2 Timothy 3:16 (ESV)

Not some Scripture. Not the comfortable parts. *All of it.*

The Jewish scribes who copied and preserved the Old Testament — the Masoretes — treated every word as sacred. They counted letters. They knew the middle word of every book. They kept fragments in small boxes — *tefillin*, sometimes called phylacteries — worn on the arm and head during prayer as a physical act of reverence toward the text. They were not careless men.

God said his word was reliable. God's Son treated it as reliable. And the manuscript evidence backs the claim. Trust it.

## Back to the Magi

The Magi found the child.

They gave three gifts, and each one says something.

Gold — the gift for a king. Jesus Christ is King.

Frankincense — the incense of the priesthood, burned in the temple, offered before God. Christ is our High Priest. He stands between God and man. He is the mediator, the one way to the Father. If you don't know him — I'll give you a chance before we're done tonight.

Myrrh — an embalming spice, used in burial. They gave the infant a burial preparation as a gift. John 19 tells us that when they took Jesus down from the cross, Nicodemus brought myrrh and aloes to wrap his body. These scholars who came to worship a baby brought him the spice that would one day be laid on his corpse.

They didn't know that. But God arranged the gifts.

## God Meets You Where You Are

Then they were warned in a dream not to go back to Herod, and they went home by another road.

God spoke to them through a dream. These were men who had come to the child following a star — astrology, basically. God doesn't endorse astrology. But that night he used what they already knew — the sky, the stars, the language of signs — to reach them.

Hear me on this: there is nothing you have done that puts you beyond his reach.

David was a murderer and an adulterer. God called him a man after his own heart. Paul was hunting Christians for execution when Jesus stopped him on the road to Damascus. Thomas refused to believe the resurrection had happened even when his friends were telling him they'd seen it — and Jesus showed up anyway, specifically for Thomas, and held out his hands. How many tax collectors did Jesus eat dinner with? How many prostitutes did he stop and speak to as if they mattered? As if their next step was the most important thing in the world?

God will use whatever door is open.

If a star is what gets you moving, he'll put a star in the sky.

## Get Up and Go

That night an angel came to Joseph in a dream.

> *"Rise, take the child and his mother, and flee to Egypt, and remain there until I tell you, for Herod is about to search for the child, to destroy him."* — Matthew 2:13 (ESV)

The Greek word for "flee" here is a present imperative — the tense of continuous, ongoing action. Not "run to Egypt and settle in." *Start running and don't stop.* The command carries a specific force: don't wait. Don't plan. Don't pack the shop. Don't wake the neighbors. Go now. Keep going until I tell you to stop.

And the destination? Egypt. That's it. Egypt is over a million square kilometers. There's no address. There's no contact waiting. There's no "when you get there, find the house with the blue door." There is a direction and a promise and a child to protect.

Joseph got up in the middle of the night and went.

He was a carpenter. He almost certainly couldn't bring his tools. His income, his trade, the entire physical infrastructure of his livelihood — left behind in the dark. He obeyed a command that gave him a direction, no timeline, no specifics, and no explanation beyond "there is a man who wants your son dead."

That's what obedience in the dark looks like. Not obedience when you have the full picture. Not obedience when the path is lit. Obedience when you have a direction and a promise and nothing else.

Elijah sat by a brook that God had directed him to — a specific brook, by name — and drank from it and was fed by ravens. Then the brook dried up. No explanation. Then God sent him to a widow in Zarephath. A widow — not a wealthy household, not someone with resources to spare. A woman who was preparing what she believed would be her family's last meal. *Go to her.* Elijah went. The oil and flour didn't run out until the drought ended.

He had a direction. He had a promise. He went.

Some of you are at the dried-up brook right now. The thing that was sustaining you is gone. And God is telling you to move — toward something that doesn't make sense on paper, toward a situation that doesn't look like provision. He is not lost. He is not surprised. He has been here before.

## What God Does Not Promise

While Joseph and Mary and the child were somewhere in Egypt, Herod figured out that the Magi had outmaneuvered him. He calculated the timeframe. He ordered every boy in Bethlehem two years old and under killed.

Bethlehem was a small town. Scholars estimate roughly twenty children died.

Matthew quotes the prophet Jeremiah:

> *"A voice was heard in Ramah, weeping and loud lamentation, Rachel weeping for her children; she refused to be consoled, because they are no more."* — Matthew 2:18 (ESV)

Twenty families. Twenty boys. Twenty sets of parents who did not get an angel in the night. Who did not get a dream. Who did not get a warning. Just soldiers at the door.

I need to sit with that for a minute, because I think we owe it to the text.

If God does not spare his own Son from being born into danger — from spending his first years as a refugee, hunted by a king, hiding in a foreign country — what exactly are we expecting for ourselves?

God allowed his Son to be mocked. To be beaten. To be tortured by the very people he came to save. He was nailed to a piece of wood and left to suffocate slowly in public, in front of his mother.

Don't let anyone sell you a version of the gospel that says the moment you follow Jesus, your life smooths out. That's not the gospel. That's a sales pitch. Look at what happened to the men and women who followed Jesus most closely. Peter was crucified. Paul was beaten, jailed, and executed. Believers were thrown to animals, burned alive, shipwrecked. The road that Jesus walked led to a cross — and he told us to pick up our own.

> *"In the world you will have tribulation. But take heart; I have overcome the world."* — John 16:33 (ESV)

Both halves of that sentence are true. Trouble is real. And he has overcome it. He doesn't promise you won't go through the fire. He promises the fire doesn't get the last word.

Jonah went through the belly of a fish before he got to Nineveh. The path God chooses is not always the comfortable one. But it is always the right one.

---

## The Weight of What's Coming

Here's what I believe with everything in me: the difficulty of this life — even the worst of it, even grief that doesn't end, even sickness, even loss — will feel like a bad dream when we wake up on the other side.

No sadness. No grief. Reunited with the people we've lost. And best of all — the one who loved us enough to go to that cross will be right there.

How do we sit on that and not tell everyone we know?

If you know someone who is lost — and you do, you know someone who is dying and doesn't have Christ — and you know what's on the other side of this life, how is it possible to just not say anything? How do we hold something this important and stay quiet about it?

We can't. We shouldn't.

---

## Gospel

If you've never given your life to Jesus Christ — if you've never acknowledged that you are a sinner and that you need a savior — tonight is the night to do that.

Not because your life will suddenly get easy. It won't. Joseph's didn't. The disciples' didn't.

But because Jesus Christ is the King, and the Priest, and the one who took death into himself so that you wouldn't have to face it alone. He is the way to the Father. The only way. And the door is open right now.

If you want to talk, I'm here. Don't leave tonight without having that conversation.

## End Notes

**"Magi sent north about 6 miles to Bethlehem" — direction corrected.** Bethlehem is approximately 6 miles *south* of Jerusalem, not north. The raw notes say "north." This has been corrected to "south" in the manuscript. Verify the direction against a Bible atlas before preaching.

**Homer manuscript count — raw notes corrected.** The raw notes state "we only have three copies from before the 1800s AD" and "60% agreement between manuscripts." Both figures are inaccurate. Homer's *Iliad* has approximately 1,800 surviving manuscripts — it is the second-best-attested work from the ancient world. The correct apologetics argument is still powerful: the New Testament's 5,800+ Greek manuscripts outnumber Homer more than three to one, the NT manuscripts date far closer to the originals, and NT textual agreement is substantially higher. The manuscript uses the correct figures. **Do not use the figures from the raw notes.**

**Plato/student confusion — raw notes corrected.** The raw notes say "Plato's writings have been lost to time, we only have a few of the writings of his student." Plato's dialogues have largely survived — roughly 36 of them, most extant. The standard apologetics comparison is: *Plato himself* (~7 manuscripts, earliest copy 1,200+ years after he wrote). Plato's teacher was Socrates, who wrote nothing. Plato's student was Aristotle, covered separately. The manuscript uses the correct information.

**"Josephus offered Herod intel, was tortured" — removed.** Josephus the historian (born ~37 AD) was not a contemporary of Herod the Great (died 4 BC). Josephus could not have provided Herod with intelligence about a conspiracy — he was not yet born. The account of Herod's cruelties comes *from* Josephus's writings (*Antiquities of the Jews* and *The Jewish War*), but Josephus was a chronicler, not a participant. The vivid account of Herod's behavior is sourced from Josephus's historical record but restructured accurately in the manuscript. **Recommendation:** If you want to use a specific Josephus quote about Herod's character, locate it in *Antiquities* Book 17 and cite it directly. The detail about the torture and the "kill them" scene should be verified against a specific passage before preaching.

**Herod's illness.** The raw notes say Herod "had syphilis." Josephus describes a constellation of symptoms in Herod's final years, but the specific diagnosis is disputed among scholars — proposed diagnoses have included chronic kidney disease, diabetes with gangrene, and other conditions. "Syphilis" is not the consensus. The manuscript uses "ravaged by disease," which is accurate and defensible.

**"Swallowed by a fish" listed among disciples' sufferings — corrected.** The raw notes list being swallowed by a fish among the hardships faced by Christ's disciples. Jonah was an Old Testament prophet — he predated Christ by roughly 800 years and was not among Jesus's disciples. The manuscript handles this by referencing Jonah separately and accurately as an Old Testament example of obedience through difficulty.

**"Not from the east relative to Israel."** Matthew 2:1 (ESV) explicitly states the Magi came "from the east" (Greek: ἀπὸ ἀνατολῶν). If the sermon intends to argue they came from a different direction, this would require engaging with the text directly, since "from the east" is what Matthew says. The manuscript presents them as coming "from the east" consistent with the text and notes that their precise homeland is debated (Persian, Babylonian, possibly Arabian). **Recommendation:** If you have a specific source supporting a different geographic origin, surface it and interact with Matthew 2:1 directly rather than stating it as fact without engagement.

**"Approximately 20 children in Bethlehem."** This is a figure derived from estimates of Bethlehem's 1st-century population (typically estimated at 300–1,000 people). It is a reasonable scholarly estimate, not a figure from the text. Usable in preaching if presented as an estimate.

**"99.5% of manuscripts are exactly the same."** This figure appears in popular apologetics but the scholarly framing is more precise: roughly 92–95% of the New Testament text has no meaningful textual variant. The remaining variants are overwhelmingly spelling differences, word-order variations, and duplicate lines — not theological content. The stronger and more defensible claim (used in the manuscript) is that no variant affects any doctrine of the Christian faith. **Recommendation:** If you use a percentage from the pulpit, source it — Daniel Wallace's work on NT textual criticism is a reliable reference.

**2 Timothy 3:16 (ESV) — verified exact.**
**John 16:33 (ESV) — verified exact.**
**Matthew 2:13 (ESV) — verified exact.**
**Matthew 2:18 (ESV) — verified exact.**
**Matthew 9:37 (ESV) — "laborers," not "workers" as in the raw notes.**

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Warmth and Honest Exegesis Undermined by a Gospel That Arrives Too Late and Leaves Too Quickly**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A faithful and historically grounded opening of Matthew 2:1–23 that displayed genuine pastoral courage in sitting with suffering while failing to ground its gospel appeal in the atonement and leaving the conscience largely unsearched.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with qualification.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — that God can be trusted in ambiguity, darkness, and suffering because he is sovereign even when he does not explain himself — does arise organically from the passage's own movement: Joseph's obedient flight, the Bethlehem massacre, the exile and return. The text supports it.

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text's hard material. The massacre is not softened. The absence of an angel for the twenty families is honored rather than explained away. The preacher does not stand above the text as editor. He stays under it.

*Qualification:* The sermon's dominant energy is apologetic (defending textual reliability) rather than expository (opening the passage's own argument). The manuscript defense section — while accurate and helpful — occupies a disproportionate amount of the sermon's real estate and temporarily becomes the main point. The Berean Gate holds, but the sermon flirts with using Matthew 2 as a platform for a Bible-reliability lecture.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**One flag is triggered, partially.**

- **Flag 4 — Application is largely generic.** The application points are true and pastoral but fail to differentiate hearers with specificity. "Some of you are at the dried-up brook right now" is the strongest moment of direct address — but the instruction amounts to: *keep going, God is trustworthy.* No one in particular is cornered. The self-righteous are not addressed. The grieving who have lost faith are not distinguished from those who are merely anxious. The backslider is not named. This is below the fatal flaw threshold but proximately close.

- **No full fatal flaw cap applied.** The gospel is present, Christ is necessary to the argument, and the sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers. The flag above is registered as a significant weakness rather than a hard cap trigger.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
   Mostly Christ, but the apologetics section briefly redirects attention to the evidential case for the Bible rather than to the person of Christ. Net: *more Christ than self*, but not decisively.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
   Partially. The suffering section — "twenty families, twenty boys" — is a genuine encounter with God's costly sovereignty. The gospel section does not sustain that weight. *Ideas about God more than God himself.*

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
   Comforted, yes. Convicted, minimally. Informed substantially (perhaps too substantially in the apologetics section). The sermon comforts more than it convicts, which is not wrong — but a sermon on Matthew 2 that produces no conviction at all has left something on the table.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The massacre section is the sermon's finest moment.** "I need to sit with that for a minute, because I think we owe it to the text" is a model of pastoral courage. The preacher does not rescue God from the hard text. The observation that God did not spare his own Son from danger — and therefore our expectations of a smooth life are misaligned — is theologically sound, pastorally honest, and congregation-serving. This section earns the sermon's keep.

2. **The Magi gifts as prophetic Christology.** Gold/King, Frankincense/Priest, Myrrh/burial — this is handled with concision and genuine insight. The point that God arranged the gifts, not the Magi, is organically made and theologically rich. It does not feel forced.

3. **"God will use whatever door is open."** The section on God reaching the Magi through astrology — without endorsing astrology — is a strong pastoral move that opens genuine evangelistic warmth without theological compromise. The line "If a star is what gets you moving, he'll put a star in the sky" is memorable and true, rooted in the text.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The apologetics section displaces the redemptive-historical argument Matthew is actually making.** Matthew 2 is not primarily a chapter about Bible reliability. It is a New Exodus narrative — Herod as Pharaoh, the slaughter echoing Egypt, the return from Egypt fulfilling Hosea 11:1 ("Out of Egypt I called my son"). Matthew is showing his Jewish readers that Jesus is the New Moses, the true Israel, the fulfillment of everything the first Exodus pointed to. None of this appears in the sermon. The congregation learns how many manuscripts Homer has but does not learn what Matthew was doing. This is a significant expository failure.

2. **The gospel is present but not proclaimed.** The gospel section (final two paragraphs) tells the unbeliever what Christ *is* (King, Priest, the one who took death into himself) but does not tell them what Christ *did* in a way that addresses the wrath of God, the mechanics of substitution, or what it means to be justified. "You are a sinner and you need a savior" is announced but the *why* of the cross is not opened. A hearer without prior Christian knowledge does not know why Christ had to die, only that he did.

3. **Spiritual differentiation is nearly absent.** The sermon addresses two categories: the person who is suffering and needs to trust God, and the unbeliever who needs to come to Christ. The self-righteous, the backslider, the person who intellectually assents to all of this but has never been broken by it, the grieving person whose suffering has hardened rather than humbled them — none are addressed. The sermon diagnoses one condition (anxiety in suffering) and offers one remedy (trust God). A congregation is never that uniform.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that the apologetics section signals a pastoral anxiety about the congregation's confidence in Scripture that, while understandable, reshapes the sermon around an implied objection rather than around the text's own argument. The result is a sermon that defends the Bible without fully preaching it. The preacher may also not see that the gospel invitation — "tonight is the night to do that" — arrives at the end of a sermon that has not yet pressed the hearer on *why* they need the gospel beyond a general acknowledgment of sin. The comfort of Matthew 2 has been proclaimed before the weight of human lostness has been felt.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The apologetics section *feels* strong — the manuscript statistics are accurate, the Aristotle/Homer comparisons are genuinely striking, and the preacher handles the Luke 2 tension with intellectual honesty. A congregation hearing this section will likely feel their confidence in Scripture bolstered. But this is an illusion of strength: the sermon has spent substantial time defending Scripture *about* the text rather than preaching *from* the text. Textual apologetics is not exposition. The section leaves the congregation better equipped to argue for Bible reliability at a dinner party than to understand what Matthew 2 is doing as a piece of redemptive-historical theology.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The Bethlehem massacre and the mothers who received no angel is the sermon's richest theological vein — and the preacher dips into it but does not mine it. The text here is crying out for the doctrine of the cross as the ground of God's solidarity with the suffering. Why can the Christian trust God in suffering? Not merely because "he has overcome the world" (though that is true), but because God himself entered suffering — was himself the refugee child, himself the victim of state violence, himself the one who cried out in desolation on the cross. The suffering of Christ as the ground of our trust in God's presence in suffering is the gospel connection the text is begging for, and it is mentioned but not made the sermon's center of gravity. This is where Matthew 2 touches Romans 8:32 ("He who did not spare his own Son...") and the preacher passes it by.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Cut the apologetics section by half and redirect the second half toward Matthew's New Exodus argument.** Keep the brief acknowledgment of the Luke 2 tension (it is genuinely pastoral). Keep the affirmation that Scripture is reliable. Remove the Aristotle/Plato/Homer/Caesar list — it is lecture material, not pulpit material. Use the recovered real estate to show the congregation what Matthew is doing: Jesus is the New Moses, the true Israel, the fulfillment of the Exodus. This is the exegetical payoff the section currently lacks.

2. **Develop the gospel around the cross as the ground of God's solidarity in suffering.** The sermon's main idea — trust God in the dark — needs a theological foundation that goes deeper than "he has overcome the world." Insert a passage showing that God did not spare his own Son from the suffering the Bethlehem children suffered, and that the cross is therefore the guarantee that God is not absent in our worst moments. Romans 8:32 and Isaiah 53 are both available. This transforms the gospel section from an invitation at the end of a talk into the theological spine of the entire sermon.

3. **Add direct address to the hardened and the self-righteous after the suffering section.** Before moving to eschatological hope, insert a paragraph that addresses the person in the congregation who has *not* been humbled by suffering — who has heard about the Bethlehem massacre and filed it as historical information rather than a word to them. Name that posture. Press that conscience. "Some of you have been through something worse than Joseph's midnight flight and you haven't softened — you've hardened. You've concluded that God is either absent or unjust. Tonight's text is for you too."

4. **Sharpen the exhortation.** The gospel invitation is present but structurally isolated at the end. The "therefore" moment — what must the *believer* now do differently, having heard this sermon? — is absent. Add a specific call to the believer: something like, "If you are following Christ tonight, here is what this text demands: Stop waiting for the full picture before you obey. Joseph got a direction and a promise. That is what you have. Take the next step."

5. **Realign the sermon's climax.** The sermon currently climaxes at the eschatological hope section ("No sadness. No grief. Reunited...") and then descends into the gospel invitation. The climax should be the cross — the place where God's trustworthiness in suffering is most fully demonstrated — with eschatological hope as the *application* of that climax, not the climax itself.
