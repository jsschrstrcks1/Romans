# People Group & Evangelism — *The Blood of the Martyrs*
## Korea: Robert Thomas, the 1907 Revival, and Us

*[Note: This is the pre-sermon People Group / Evangelism segment. Flows into Judges 6.]*

---

We normally start here by lifting up a people group that needs our prayers. Tonight I want to do something a little different. I want to share a story of God showing off — God answering the prayers of his people in a way that should embarrass our unbelief and reignite our hope.

A friend of mine recently returned from a missions trip to Korea. He told me this story. I want to share part of it with you tonight.

## The Missionary and the Ship

There was a young Welsh missionary working in China. He had gotten there and begun to do good work — but God gave him a heart for Korea. A Korea that was closed to outsiders. A Korea that didn't want what he had.

So he began to pray. And he prayed and he prayed.

Eventually he scraped together enough money to commission copies of the Scriptures in a language the Koreans could read. He found a ship headed that way and got himself aboard as a translator. His name was Robert Thomas.

He made his first trip to the Korean coast in 1865. They wouldn't have anything to do with him. They sent him away.

He went back to China. Gathered himself. And went right back.

The second trip — 1866 — he booked passage on an American trading vessel called the General Sherman. [*Editorial note: The oral delivery calls this a "wooden sailing ship" and later "a little wooden row boat." The General Sherman was actually a 187-ton side-wheel steamer — a steam-powered vessel. Thomas was not sent to shore on a rowboat; the ship sailed up the Taedong River toward Pyongyang and eventually ran aground on a mudbank. Corrected wording in manuscript accordingly.*] The ship sailed up the Taedong River toward Pyongyang. Korean officials ordered them to leave repeatedly. The crew didn't. Hostilities broke out — cannons, gunfire. The Koreans eventually launched a burning boat into the General Sherman and set it ablaze.

Robert Thomas opened his case of Bibles and began throwing them to the Koreans lining the shore. Shouting, *Jesus, Jesus*, in Korean. He kept going until he had nothing left to throw. They killed him at the water's edge.

One government official named Pak Yong-Sik gathered up some of the Bibles that had been thrown onto the riverbank. He used the pages to wallpaper rooms in his house. People read the gospel off his walls. Years later, when the Christian community in that region discovered what was written there, they came from all over to read it.

The church father Tertullian wrote in the second century — this is from his *Apologeticus*, written around 197 AD:

> "The more you kill the more we are. The blood of Christians is seed."

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery quotes a longer passage attributed to Tertullian, beginning "We are not a new philosophy but a divine revelation..." The famous line — "semen est sanguis Christianorum" / "the blood of Christians is seed" — is confirmed from Apologeticus, chapter 50. The longer extended quote circulating online and in sermon illustrations appears to combine and paraphrase multiple sections of the Apology rather than quote a single passage directly. Use only the verified core line, or verify the full extended passage against a scholarly edition of the Apologeticus before preaching it.*]

The blood of Christians is seed.

Robert Thomas was killed in 1866. He was twenty-nine years old.

## The Book of 2 Corinthians 5:20

> "Therefore, we are ambassadors for Christ, God making his appeal through us. We implore you on behalf of Christ, be reconciled to God."

Robert Thomas understood that. He was an ambassador. God was making his appeal through him, to a people who had no interest in the message — and Thomas kept delivering it until he couldn't anymore.

Fifty years later, the seed came up.

## Pyongyang, 1907

It is 1907. The year is hard. Korea had just gone from being governed by its own rulers to being a Japanese protectorate — an indirect consequence of Japan's victory over Russia in 1905 and the Treaty of Portsmouth. This was not the first time two foreign powers had fought a war on Korean soil, and it would not be the last. The Boxer Rebellion had swept through China just a few years earlier in 1901, and the reverberations were still being felt across Asia.

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery says Germany was "funding the war efforts on both sides" of the Russo-Japanese War as a power play. This is not historically accurate. Germany's government and banks funded Russia — a Russo-German loan of 500 million marks was extended to the Tsar. Japan was funded primarily by American banker Jacob Schiff through Kuhn, Loeb & Co. Germany's sympathies in the conflict were clearly with Russia. This claim should be dropped or corrected before preaching.*]

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery refers to Pyongyang as "the capital of united Korea." This is incorrect. The capital of the Korean Empire in 1907 was Seoul (then called Hanseong). Pyongyang was where the revival happened, and it later became known as the "Jerusalem of the East." The manuscript has been corrected.*]

Christianity had only been permitted in Korea since the 1880s. It was still young, still fragile. Less than one percent of the entire country followed Jesus Christ.

The church leaders and missionaries working Korea gathered in Pyongyang for a meeting. Just over fifteen hundred Christians came — likely nearly all the pastors and missionaries in any part of the country at the time. Leading up to that gathering, people had been praying. Every pastor knew about it. Missionaries were praying. Christians in other countries were praying. The Koreans themselves were praying — because things were bad spiritually, financially, and politically, and they knew it.

The meetings began. The preachers stood up to preach.

And then something happened that no one had planned.

The weight of their own sin became so overwhelming that the preachers could not continue. They stopped preaching. They began to confess — publicly, in front of fifteen hundred people. And it wasn't long before the entire congregation was doing the same thing. Men laid prostrate on the floor and wept for God's mercy. The impulse to pray was impossible to resist. One pastor approached a missionary and grabbed him: *Pastor, pastor — this is my sin. Is there any hope for me?* And laid prostrate and wept.

What happened that night happened the next night. And the next. And the next.

Revival swept the country. Village by village, people began waking early before work to gather and pray together for hours. The city of Pyongyang became known as the Jerusalem of the East. By 1910, there were more than two hundred thousand Christians in Korea. And by the 1980s, there were more than ten million followers of Jesus in South Korea alone.

Robert Thomas threw his Bibles into the water in 1866. The harvest came up forty years later.

## Now — Us

Pasco County, for a number of years, was the least reached county east of the Mississippi River. We've since been surpassed by Miami-Dade. I'm not sure that's a comfort.

Mark 16:15:

> "And he said to them, 'Go into all the world and proclaim the gospel to the whole creation.'"

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery has "preach the gospel to all creation." ESV says "proclaim the gospel to the whole creation." Corrected above.*]

There are more than a hundred Muslim households within a mile of our front door right now. A whole county — practically unreached — surrounding us. In the United States there are over a hundred unengaged unreached people groups that have relocated here from around the world, congregating in cities and suburbs — places like Pasco. [*Editorial note: Missions statistics on unreached people groups in the US shift regularly. Verify the current figure with IMB or Joshua Project before preaching.*]

How many times do we drive past the food pantry and see people who are lost? How many times do we see someone wrecked on drugs or alcohol, or selling themselves, and just drive right past — because it's uncomfortable, because we don't know what to say, because we've decided it's not our problem?

Spurgeon preached this — from his sermon "The Wailing of Risca":

> "If sinners will be damned, at least let them leap to hell over our bodies; and if they will perish, let them perish with our arms about their knees, imploring them to stay, and not madly to destroy themselves. If hell must be filled, at least let it be filled in the teeth of our exertions, and let not one go there unwarned and unprayed for."

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery has "sinners be damned" and "dead bodies." The verified text from "The Wailing of Risca" reads "sinners will be damned" and "our bodies" (without "dead"). Corrected above. Source: Charles H. Spurgeon, The Spurgeon Series 1859 & 1860, New Leaf Publishing Group, p. 1613.*]

What have you been involved in lately that would make it hard for the people around you to go to hell?

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery includes a second Spurgeon quote at this point — "Never let us speak of the doom of the wicked harshly, flippantly, or without holy grief. For the loss of heaven and the endurance of hell, must always be themes for tears. For to live without Christ is grief enough, but to die without Him, is an overwhelming horror, that should grind our hearts to powder as we fall on our faces and cry, 'Oh God have mercy upon them, for thy grace and love's sake.'" This quote could NOT be confirmed in any Spurgeon archive, sermon index, or scholarly database. Do not attribute it to Spurgeon from the pulpit until the source is found. The sentiment is consistent with Spurgeon's documented teaching on hell and the lost, but the specific wording and origin are unverified. If verified, it belongs here. If not, the paragraph that follows — "We have scales on our hearts that only God can rip free" — stands well without it.*]

We have grown comfortable with the idea of people going to hell. We've allowed our hearts to grow hard to where people we say we love are going to spend eternity.

## Evangelism Is Not About Your Ability

Here's the thing: you don't have to be good at this.

Someone read for me 1 Corinthians 1:17.

> "For Christ did not send me to baptize but to preach the gospel, and not with words of eloquent wisdom, lest the cross of Christ be emptied of its power."

[*Editorial note: The oral delivery has "not with wisdom and eloquence." ESV reads "not with words of eloquent wisdom." Corrected above.*]

Paul — well educated, trained as a Pharisee under Gamaliel, a man God used to write roughly half the New Testament [*Editorial note: "most of the new testament" in the oral delivery. Paul authored 13 letters, 14 if Hebrews is included. 27 books total. Approximately 48%. "Roughly half" is accurate.*] — even Paul said he was not eloquent when it came to the gospel. Not polished. Not smooth.

If Paul needed God's help, don't expect it to be easier for you. But here's what that means: God supplies the opportunity. God supplies the words. God did the work on the cross. You just need to make your mouth available and see what he does with it.

Robert Thomas made himself available. He made two trips to a country that didn't want him. He threw his Bibles into the river while they were killing him. He died at twenty-nine, and never saw a single convert.

He didn't need to see it. God was keeping count.
