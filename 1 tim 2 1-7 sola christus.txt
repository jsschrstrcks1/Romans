REFORMATION HISTORY INTRODUCTION

This is the third week of the Reformation series.

MARTIN LUTHER — BACKGROUND

Luther was born to a father who was a copper miner and smelter — a man who had worked his way up
from modest origins to a position of some prosperity. [Note: Hans Luder (Luther's father) was a
copper miner/smelter in Mansfeld who became a successful leaseholder of mining operations. "Wealthy
copper merchant" as the pastor phrases it is an approximation; "prosperous copper smelter" would be
more precise. The description of him as wealthy is defensible by Luther's adult years.]

Luther's father paid for his schooling. Luther was sent to study law.

He earned his bachelor's degree and had begun work on his master's in law when he quit and entered a
monastery. [Note: Luther received his BA in 1502 and MA in 1505 at the University of Erfurt. He
began law studies in 1505 but entered the Augustinian monastery in Erfurt in July 1505 after a
lightning storm near Stotternheim, in which he vowed to become a monk if he survived. Accurate in
substance.]

[Transcription artifact: "monetary" in original dictation — corrected to "monastery."]

He subsequently received his doctorate in theology. [Note: Luther received his Doctor of Theology
degree from the University of Wittenberg in October 1512. Accurate.]

He asked one of his mentors — Johann von Staupitz, vicar-general of the German Augustinian
congregations — what to study, and was told he could not go wrong by studying Christ. [Note: The
substance of this counsel — Staupitz directing Luther toward Christ and Scripture — is consistent
with historical accounts of their relationship. However, the specific wording "you can't go wrong by
studying Christ" is a paraphrase, not a verified direct quote. The specific source (letter, table
talk, or secondary account) has not been confirmed. Verify exact wording before citing in print.]

Luther was studying Romans 1:17 — "the righteous shall live by faith" — when the doctrine of Sola
Fide crystallized for him. [Note: Romans 1:17, ESV: "For in it the righteousness of God is revealed
from faith for faith, as it is written, 'The righteous shall live by faith.'" The quoted portion is
accurate and is itself a quotation of Habakkuk 2:4.]

The more he studied Scripture, the more he saw the distance between what he had been taught and what
the Bible said. This led to his 95 Theses, which he posted on the door of the Castle Church
(Schlosskirche / All Saints' Church) in Wittenberg. [Note: The pastor spells it "Witenburg" in the
original transcript — corrected to "Wittenberg" here. The traditional date is October 31, 1517.
Whether Luther physically nailed the theses to the door is debated by some historians, but the
traditional account is widely accepted.]

Luther was later excommunicated and condemned as a heretic. People sought his life. [Note: Luther was
excommunicated by Pope Leo X on January 3, 1521, through the papal bull "Decet Romanum Pontificem."
He was also condemned at the Diet of Worms (April 1521), placed under imperial ban by the Edict of
Worms. Accurate.]

Luther spent significant effort translating the Bible into German so that ordinary people could read
it for themselves.

[CORRECTION — CRITICAL: The pastor says Luther translated "from Latin to German." This is
historically inaccurate. Luther translated the New Testament from the GREEK original into German,
published in September 1522. He translated the Old Testament from the HEBREW into German, completing
the full Bible in 1534. While the Latin Vulgate existed and was the church's official Bible, Luther
deliberately went back to the original Greek and Hebrew, not the Latin. This distinction matters
theologically — Luther's point was to go back to the original languages, not simply to translate the
church's Latin version. This should be corrected if the sermon is re-preached or published.]

All of this — the theses, the translation work — cost Luther his standing in the church and his
safety, so that we could read, understand, and study the Bible freely.

THE BAPTIST LINEAGE

We are halfway to the birth of the Baptists.

Luther founded a new church apart from Rome, with the support of German aristocracy. [Note: This is
a reasonable description of the formation of Lutheran state churches under the cuius regio, eius
religio principle established at the Peace of Augsburg (1555), though the broader picture of German
princes supporting the Reformation for a mix of religious and political reasons is accurate.]

This led to others identifying further areas where the Reformation needed to continue. The Latin
phrase for this is semper reformanda — always be reforming. [Note: The full classical phrase is
typically rendered "Ecclesia reformata, semper reformanda est" — the reformed church is always to be
reformed. The shortened form semper reformanda is in common usage and accurately conveys the idea.]

[CORRECTION: The pastor uses "siempre fidelis" as a comparison, as the Marine motto meaning "always
faithful." This is a mixed-language error. "Siempre" is Spanish; the Marine Corps motto is the
LATIN "Semper Fidelis." The correct comparison is: semper reformanda — always reforming; Semper
Fidelis — always faithful. Corrected in this transcript.]

The second group in Germany was the Anabaptists. [Note: The original transcript reads "annabaptists"
— corrected to "Anabaptists." The Anabaptist movement emerged in the 1520s in Zurich, Zurich, and
across German-speaking lands as a more radical wing of the Reformation. Calling them "the second
group in Germany" is a simplification — the movement was geographically diverse from the start.]

Some Anabaptists were theologically sound; others held to views that were heterodox. The pastor
notes that some of the sound ones eventually connected with the stream of reform associated with John
Calvin in Geneva, Switzerland.

[NOTE — HISTORICAL OVERSIMPLIFICATION: The pastor's narrative — Anabaptists move to Geneva,
study under Calvin, sail to America, become Baptists — is historically problematic. John Calvin was
not a patron of Anabaptists; he had sharp theological conflicts with them. Calvin and the Anabaptists
disagreed on infant baptism, ecclesiology, and the relationship of church and state. Calvin had
Michael Servetus executed (1553) and was generally hostile to the radical reformation streams the
Anabaptists represented. The more historically defensible Baptist lineage runs through the English
Separatist movement — Puritans and Separatists who were influenced by Reformed (Calvinist) theology,
separated from the Church of England, some of whom emigrated to the New World for religious freedom.
The first Baptist church in England is generally dated to 1609 (John Smyth in Amsterdam) or 1612
(Thomas Helwys in London). The pastor's broad point — that Baptist distinctives developed through
the Reformation stream, not as a continuous pre-Reformation tradition — is historically sound. The
specific chain of Anabaptists → Calvin → America → Baptists is not. This should be researched and
revised before re-preaching.]

A group of these people boarded a ship and sailed to the New World seeking religious freedom. Over
time, this stream was distilled down into what became the Baptists.

THE TRAIL OF BLOOD — J.M. CARROLL

There is another lineage theory that many people were taught, written by a man called J.M. Carroll
(James Milton Carroll). He wrote a book called "The Trail of Blood" (1931).

[CORRECTION: The pastor says "james carrol." The correct name is J.M. Carroll — James Milton
Carroll (1852–1931), a Texas Baptist pastor. "James Carroll" is a different person entirely
(a contemporary Catholic writer). The book is "The Trail of Blood." Corrected here.]

Carroll attempted to connect Baptist history in an unbroken line all the way back to John the
Baptist — linking every group that opposed Rome and practiced any form of water ceremony (sprinkling
or immersion). The idea was that Baptists could trace their heritage back to John the Baptist himself.

The pastor's assessment: the theory does not hold up.

Problems with the Trail of Blood theory:
- Many groups included were steeped in theological heterodoxy and would have been offended to be
  associated with orthodox Christianity.
- The theory requires an unbroken chain of transmission, but there are large chronological and
  geographical gaps between groups — separated by a century and a continent in an era without
  long-distance travel.
- Some groups listed as passing the torch to each other were historical enemies; one exterminated the
  other rather than handing anything down.

The more coherent account: everyone was Catholic. Then Huss, Luther, Calvin, Zwingli, and
Melanchthon — among others — worked to reform a church that had forsaken Scripture.

[CORRECTIONS:
(1) "Zwingly" in original transcript — corrected to "Zwingli" (Ulrich Zwingli, 1484–1531, Swiss
reformer in Zurich).
(2) "Knox" is mentioned alongside Huss as men who "gave their very lives." Jan Hus DID give his life
— he was burned at the Council of Constance in 1415. John Knox (1514–1572), however, did NOT die as
a martyr. Knox died of natural causes. He was a man of great courage who faced persecution and
imprisonment, but he was not killed for his faith. The characterization that Knox "gave his very
life" is historically inaccurate and should be corrected when re-preaching. The full phrase used was
"some — like Huss and Knox, gave their very lives." Huss: accurate. Knox: inaccurate.]

WYCLIFFE

A few years before Luther, John Wycliffe in England believed that Christians should be able to read
God's message to them in their own language. He translated the New Testament into English
(from the Latin Vulgate) in the 1380s. [Note: Wycliffe's translation, completed around 1382–1384,
was the first complete Bible in English. It was translated from the Latin Vulgate. Accurate in
broad strokes.]

Wycliffe was hated by the English church. He died of a stroke in 1384 while a condemned man. In
1428, by order of the Council of Constance, his bones were exhumed, burned, pulverized, and the
ashes thrown into the Swift River. [Note: This is historically accurate.]

The pastor noted that he has personally held a page from an early English New Testament produced
around Wycliffe's era.

PEOPLE GROUP: KASHMIRI PANDITS

The Brahmin Kashmiri Pandits claim to have inhabited the Kashmir valley in northern India for over
3,000 years. The word "Pandit" means "a wise man" or "scholar of Hinduism."

Beginning in 1989–1990, tens of thousands of Kashmiri Pandits were forced to flee their homes
under threat from Islamist militants, who presented a three-part ultimatum: convert to Islam, leave,
or die. The majority fled. Many live today in displacement camps in Jammu and elsewhere in India.
When the Indian government declared it safe to return, most Pandits chose not to because of the
continued presence of extremists in the region. [Note: This account is consistent with historical
record. The displacement of Kashmiri Pandits in 1989–1990 is one of the largest refugee crises
in post-partition India. Accurate.]

CHRIST IN ALL OF SCRIPTURE

J.C. Ryle writes:

"Christ is to be found — dimly and indistinctly at the beginning, more clearly and plainly in the
middle, fully and completely at the end — but really and substantially everywhere. Many people forget
the Old Testament in its main emphasis points forward to Christ, the Gospels discuss Christ, and the
rest of the New Testament points back to Christ. Christ's sacrifice and death for sinners, and
Christ's kingdom and future glory, are the light we must bring to bear on any book of Scripture we
read. Christ's cross and Christ's crown are the clue we must hold fast — if we would find our way
through Scripture difficulties. Christ is the only key which will unlock many of the dark places of
the Word. Some people complain that they do not understand the Bible. And the reason is very simple.
They do not use the key. To them the Bible is like the hieroglyphics in Egypt. It is a mystery, just
because they do not know and employ the key."

— J.C. Ryle [Note: J.C. Ryle (1816–1900), Bishop of Liverpool. This passage is consistent with
Ryle's expository writings, particularly material found in the prefaces to his "Expository Thoughts
on the Gospels." The specific source (volume, page, or essay title) has not been confirmed. Verify
and cite before quoting in print.]

WHY SOLUS CHRISTUS WAS NECESSARY

Martin Luther writes:

"The devil does not intend to allow this testimony about Christ. He devotes all his energy to
opposing it and will not desist until he has struck it down and suppressed it. In this respect, we
humans are weak and stubbornly perverse and are more likely to become attached to saints than to
Christ. Within the papacy they have preached about the service rendered by these beloved saints,
that one ought to rely on their merit."

— Martin Luther [Note: This is a Luther quote. The specific source — sermon, lecture, commentary,
or volume of Luther's Works — has not been confirmed. Verify the source in Luther's Works (LW,
American Edition, Fortress Press / Concordia) before citing in print. The substance is consistent

PROBLEM WITH SAINTS' MERIT

The Catholic argument was that saints' merit could facilitate one's journey to Christ. The pastor
identifies two problems:

1. We are spiritually dead apart from Christ (cf. Ephesians 2:1-5). The work of coming to Christ is
   initiated by Christ through the Spirit, not by human effort. A dead man cannot start anything.

2. "The Gospel AND anything" is dangerous. The Gospel is sufficient and complete. Galatians 1:8,
   ESV: "But even if we or an angel from heaven should preach to you a gospel contrary to the one
   we preached to you, let him be accursed."

[Note: The pastor quotes Galatians 1:8 as "But even if we or an angel from heaven should preach
a gospel other than the one we preached to you, let them be under God's curse!" The ESV reads
"contrary to" (not "other than") and "let him be accursed" (not "let them be under God's curse").
The meaning is identical; the wording differs. The NIV reads closer to the pastor's phrasing.
If quoting ESV in print, use the ESV text.]

Luther writes:

"And I, too, believed and preached thus. St. Ann was my idol, and St. Thomas my apostle. I
patterned myself substantially after them. Others ran to St. James and strongly believed and firmly
trusted that, if they conformed, they would receive all they wished and hoped for. Prayers were said
to St. Barbara and St. Christopher in order to avert an early and sudden death, and there was no
uncertainty here. So completely is man by nature bent on renouncing this testimony of John the
Baptist."

— Martin Luther [Note: Same source concern as the first Luther quote above. The source in Luther's
Works has not been confirmed. The two quotes appear to be from the same sermon or lecture. Verify
and cite before using in print.]

ILLUSTRATION: SACRÉ-CŒUR, PARIS (2009)

The pastor visited Sacré-Cœur Basilica in Paris in 2009.

[CORRECTION: The pastor says the basilica "was originally going to be dedicated to the heart of
Christ, but ultimately it became dedicated to the sacred heart of Paris." This is inaccurate.
Sacré-Cœur (French: "Sacred Heart") IS dedicated to the Sacred Heart of Jesus Christ — this was
its explicit purpose from construction (begun 1875, consecrated 1919). It was built as a national
act of penance following France's defeat in the Franco-Prussian War (1870–71) and the Paris Commune.
The dedication has always been to Christ's Sacred Heart, not to "the sacred heart of Paris." The
pastor may have misunderstood a guide's comments at the time. This should be corrected when
re-preaching.]

Observation from the visit: the tables of candles (representing prayers offered to various saints)
showed far more candles at Mary's stations than at the station for Jesus. The visual illustration
remains valid as an observation regardless of the dedication error above.

THE THREE ROLES OF CHRIST: PROPHET, PRIEST, AND KING

KING

Kings held court and made judgments about guilt. The pastor cites Solomon's judgment of the two
women claiming the same child (1 Kings 3:16–28). Accurate.

The pastor notes that President Obama in 2016 granted clemency to nearly 2,000 individuals,
most imprisoned for drug offenses, and states that most of those 2,000 are back in jail.
[Note: Obama's commutation record is accurate in broad terms — he commuted 1,715 sentences in
his final year in office, the largest single-year commutation record in American history, primarily
for drug offenses. The claim that "most of those 2,000 people are back in jail" is a recidivism
assertion that has not been verified. Recidivism studies on Obama commutations do exist but are
contested. Do not repeat this statistic without a confirmed source.]

Christ as King: He is the one who judges guilt and grants pardon. The application to salvation is
accurate.

PROPHET

A prophet is the mouthpiece of God. Through his life and ministry, Christ pronounced the death of
sin. [Note: "As priest, He through his life demonstrated how a Christian should live" — this
conflates the prophetic and priestly roles slightly, but the general application is clear and
accurate in substance.]

ILLUSTRATION: SOLAR ECLIPSE / TOTALITY

The pastor described photographing a solar eclipse through cloudy skies, taking around 200 photos
and discarding most. [Note: No verification needed — personal illustration. The eclipse reference

The Catholic church sought to usurp all three roles — prophet, priest, and king — pulling them into
