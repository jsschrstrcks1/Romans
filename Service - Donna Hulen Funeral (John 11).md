# Service — Donna Hulen Funeral
## March 7, 2021
### Passage: John 11:33–43

---

On behalf of the family, I just want to take a moment to thank you all for coming, for the flowers, and the food. It's deeply appreciated.

---

Donna Hulen was a woman with a beautiful heart, a warm smile, and she was deeply loved by all who knew her. I have had the privilege of being her pastor for the last number of years, and so I got to go visit her a few times in her last few days. This one day in particular I had received permission from the administration to come visit her outside of normal hours because they knew her time was running short. I got to the front door of the hospital, and the man at the door was giving me a little bit of a hard time — I kept explaining, *I'm in the book, they gave me permission, we're approaching the end of her life, and she's asked for me.* He eventually relented and told me I could go inside, and if security would let me in, I was good to go. So I did. But security wasn't there. I waited maybe fifteen minutes and went back to the man at the door and threw myself on his mercy a little bit.

He asked me who I was there to see. I gave him Donna's name, and his demeanor instantly changed. He had a big smile on his face and he said, *Absolutely. Let me go get the book and I'll let you in. I love Ms. Donna — she's probably the nicest person in this hospital.* So he printed out my pass and let me in.

I think to know Donna was to love her. She had this vivid, crystal-clear memory of things that had happened decades ago, and she loved to tell those stories in great detail. She was always ready with a hug, a smile, and a story.

---

As it happened, I was able to be there on her last evening. She was so excited that night. The nurse had made her a cookie and had arranged for her to have an ice cream sundae. She hadn't been allowed to eat for a few days, and ice cream was already supposed to be a rare treat for her. So when I got there she started telling me, and she was just wiggling with excitement — her face was glowing — and she said, *Guess what! I'm gonna have me some ice cream!*

We talked a fair bit that night, and in the nights leading up to it, and one of the things that stood out to me was her confidence that when she took her last breath she was going to go see Jesus. She made sure she told me that she hoped to see all of her family and friends up there with her in heaven one day too.

Shortly after, she leaned back and fell asleep. It was very peaceful.

---

And now we know from Scripture that she's somewhere her body can't betray her anymore. She is free from diabetes. She's free from that wheelchair and walker. She's free from pain and sorrow. She's reunited with her loved ones who believed in Christ and have gone on before her. Scripture tells us that when we take that last breath, we wake up the next moment in glory with our Savior Jesus. And I'm convinced he looked at Donna and said, *Well done, my good and faithful servant.*

Donna wasn't perfect, but she was forgiven. I'm not perfect either. None of us are. But those who have a relationship with Jesus Christ are welcomed home with open arms — just like Donna was.

Scripture says there is no sorrow in heaven, but it also says that God will wipe away every tear from our eyes. Heaven is such a wonderful place that we will shed tears of joy, and when we do, our Savior wipes them from our eyes.

---

I can't stand up here behind this pulpit without opening God's Word. So if you've brought your Bibles — and I hope you have — please open with me to the Gospel of John, chapter 11. John is in the New Testament: Matthew, Mark, Luke, John. If you get to Acts or Romans, you've gone too far. If you open your Bible about in the middle, you'll be in Psalms — just skip ahead toward the end, and you'll find Matthew, Mark, Luke, or John. Turn to John chapter 11, and you'll be right where we need to be today.

Just to give you a little back story on this passage: In the weeks leading up to today's text, Jesus got word that his friend — a man he loved like a brother — was sick and might die. Jesus told his disciples, *It's okay. We need to keep doing what we're doing, because this is not what will kill Lazarus in the end. He's going to be okay.* So knowing his friend was ill, Jesus kept doing what God wanted him to do. And when the time came, he traveled on to where Lazarus lived. But by the time he got there, Lazarus had died.

Some of the people were glad to see him. Some were angry. Here Jesus had the power of God on earth, able to do basically anything — and yet he tarried. And by the time he arrived at the home of Lazarus, Lazarus was dead. It would be hard to understand why Jesus allowed his dearly loved friend to die. Hard to understand why he hadn't hurried over.

---

*Read: John 11:33–43 (ESV)*

---

## Point 1: Jesus Wept

I think it's important to notice something here — Jesus wept.

So many Christians, especially those from more reserved traditions, try to put on a brave face. But Jesus wept. It's okay for us to weep.

I think this shows us something about Jesus's humanity. He knew what loss was. He experienced the depths of grief — not just for Lazarus, but many believe Jesus's earthly father Joseph had passed away before Jesus went to the cross. So he has been where we are. He knows what we go through. He chose to go through it too. And even though Jesus already knew how the story would end, he still wept. He wept for his friends who were grieving. He wept for Lazarus. He may have wept because of their unbelief.

He wasn't moved once. In verse 33, we see he was *deeply moved in his spirit and greatly troubled.* In verse 35 — the shortest verse in the Bible — we read simply: *Jesus wept.* And in verse 38, he was *deeply moved again* as he approached the tomb. Three times in a few moments, Jesus was undone by love and grief.

If Jesus is our role model — and he is — then it is okay to experience emotions at a time like this. It's natural. God made our emotions, and he can handle them. We all miss Donna. But even in the midst of weeping, we still have hope.

Jesus knew what he was going to do with Lazarus. He knew he would be raising him. And we have hope too. Because we know we aren't temporal beings. We are not just here for a few decades and then gone. We are eternal beings — here on earth experiencing life for a season before entering into eternity with our loved ones who believed in Christ, and more importantly, with our Savior Jesus. So for those of us who are followers and believers in Jesus Christ, we know that death isn't goodbye. Death isn't the end. Death is just the beginning of a wonderful, beautiful eternity.

Donna wouldn't want us crying too much. She loved life, and ice cream, and her family. She'd get so excited about seeing her family again that she'd mention it for weeks ahead of time. She truly loved people — and dessert. I think people and dessert were her two favorite things. But above all, she wanted to see her family again in heaven. She mentioned that several times on her last day here on earth.

---

## Point 2: The Unbelief of Jesus's Friends

The second thing I want to talk about is the unbelief of Jesus's friends.

Look with me at verse 39. *"But, Lord," said Martha, the sister of the dead man, "by this time there is an odor, for he has been dead four days."*

Martha's main concern wasn't that Lazarus smelled bad. Her point was: *Come on now — he's been dead. Really dead. For four days. You can't fix this.*

Now think about who Martha is. This is the sister of Mary — the woman who anointed Jesus's feet with perfume worth a year's wages. This is Mary who boldly sat at the feet of Jesus as a disciple in a culture where that was taboo, whom Jesus commended for setting aside the expectations of her day to focus on what was truly important. And Martha — the one who was so determined that everything be perfect for Jesus's meal that she worked and fussed and missed her chance to sit at his feet.

Both of these women knew Jesus. And yet when the rubber of faith meets the road of life, where are they? Martha is present with Jesus, but she can't stretch her faith far enough to believe he could raise her brother. And Mary — according to verse 20 — had stayed home. Jesus had to specifically ask for her by name before she would come out to meet him.

I think their unbelief is part of what moved Jesus to tears. He even told Martha back in verse 23 that Lazarus would rise again. Yet in verse 32, Mary throws herself at his feet and says almost accusingly, *"Lord, if you had been here, my brother would not have died."*

I don't think they fully overcame their unbelief until verse 43, when Lazarus came walking out of that tomb, still wrapped in his burial clothes.

The key verse is verse 40. Jesus said to her: *"Did I not tell you that if you believed you would see the glory of God?"*

For Donna, that's a reality. She had faith. She believed. And now she stands face to face with our Savior, hearing from his precious lips, *Well done, my good and faithful servant.*

---

## For Those Who Are Still Here

My question now is for those of us who remain. This is something Donna asked of me — to do my best to make sure she gets to see everyone she loves again. It is my honor and privilege to explain to you how that can happen.

Donna knew she was going to heaven because she had a relationship with Jesus Christ. She wasn't trusting in herself. She wasn't trusting in being a good person. She was trusting in the One who said, *"I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live."*

Here is the gospel — the good news — of Jesus Christ:

- **Romans 3:23** — *"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."*
- **Romans 6:23** — *"For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord."*
- **Romans 5:8** — *"But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us."*
- **John 3:16** — *"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."*
- **Romans 10:9** — *"If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*

If you want to see Donna again one day — if you want to know the same confidence she had when she leaned back in that hospital bed — it doesn't start with being good enough. It starts with being honest: I have sinned, I cannot save myself, and I need Jesus. And it ends with trusting in the One who rose from the dead so that death would not be the final word over your life.

Donna wanted this for every person she loved. Let's honor her by receiving it.

---

*Closing prayer. Closing song.*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title

**A Tender Pastoral Word with a Genuine Gospel Offer — Constrained by Genre, Not by Timidity**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A pastorally warm funeral message on John 11:33–43 that honors the deceased, offers genuine gospel clarity to the grieving, and weeps alongside the congregation — but sacrifices expositional depth and conscience-pressing for the pastoral comfort the moment demands.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**PASS** — with contextual acknowledgment.

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's main thesis — that Jesus weeps with the grieving and that death is not the end for those who believe — does arise from John 11. The resurrection-and-life declaration and the weeping of Jesus are both native to the passage.

*Authority Test*: The preacher submits to the text's claims. He does not soften Jesus's deity, the reality of sin, or the necessity of faith. He handles the passage honestly rather than as a springboard for generic comfort.

*Contextual note*: The Berean Gate is applied here understanding the genre pressure of funeral preaching. The passage is not exhaustively exposited, but the sermon does not contradict the text or misuse it.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

No fatal flaw flags triggered.

- The gospel is clearly presented in the closing section, with five explicit Scripture references and a coherent articulation of sin, substitution, faith, and resurrection.
- Christ is essential — the message does not survive without Jesus.
- Application is specific to the occasion and audience (the grieving, the unconverted, the family members Donna named).
- Exhortation is present and personal — the congregation is summoned to receive the gospel on Donna's behalf.
- Accessible: the explanation of how to find John in a Bible, the simple outline, and the plain language make this trackable by any hearer.

*Note: The score is not capped. The sermon is evaluated on a pastoral-occasion curve — see Rubric Fit Note (17).*

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 16 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity* (10): 6/10. The three "deeply moved" moments are noted and well-used. The "Jesus wept" observation is textually accurate and pastorally fitting. The treatment of Martha and Mary in Point 2 draws from surrounding context (vv. 20, 32, 40), showing some contextual awareness. However, the exposition remains at the surface — the Greek *embrimaomai* (deep groaning, even anger) behind "deeply moved" is left unexplored, which would have strengthened both the emotional permission and the theological weight of Christ's empathy. The sermon does not treat what v.40 means in its original covenantal context — the glory of God as the telos of Lazarus's death.
- *Contextual Awareness* (5): 3/5. The backstory retelling (vv. 1–16) is adequate but simplified. The Lazarus narrative's role in John's Gospel — as the sign that precipitates the Sanhedrin's decision to kill Jesus — is absent. This isn't fatal for a funeral, but it would have deepened the passage's weight considerably.
- *Exegetical Clarity* (5): 3/5. The meaning is conveyed adequately. The explanation of Martha's "four days" concern is good. But the sermon doesn't show its exegetical work — conclusions are asserted rather than derived.
- *Logical Faithfulness* (5): 4/5. No significant eisegesis. The connection to Donna's confidence is appropriate. The claim about Joseph's death is offered carefully ("many believe"), which is honest handling of an inferential point.

*Fallen Condition Focus*: Implicit but underdeveloped. The FCF is grief without hope — present in the text and in the room. The sermon identifies it but does not name it clinically enough to corner those who grieve without faith.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 14 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness* (7): 6/7. Christ is central. His humanity, empathy, divine power, and role as resurrection and life are all affirmed. The connection to the gospel at the close is organic and not forced.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* (3): 1/3. The sermon treats the Lazarus account as a story of Jesus's empathy and power without situating it in John's sign-theology or the redemptive-historical movement toward the cross. The raising of Lazarus is the proximate cause of Jesus's own death — a profound irony the sermon does not surface.
- *Doctrinal Precision* (4): 4/4. Sin is stated (Romans 3:23), grace is stated (Romans 5:8, 6:23), faith is stated (Romans 10:9, John 3:16). No theological sloppiness. Justification is presented as gift, not achievement.
- *Confessional Alignment* (4): 3/4. No confessional contradictions. The doctrines of grace are not surfaced (nor required by the occasion). The gospel presentation is confessionally sound. The "relationship with Jesus" language slightly softens what could be more precise — "trusting in Christ's finished work" rather than primarily "having a relationship" — though the surrounding verses do the doctrinal heavy lifting.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 12 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel* (5): 5/5. The Romans Road presentation is explicit, Scripture-grounded, and sequenced correctly — sin, wages, substitution, love, faith. An unbeliever in the room would understand the mechanics of the gospel.
- *Necessity of Christ* (5): 4/5. Christ is essential. The sermon cannot survive without Jesus — his weeping, his power, his death and resurrection are the load-bearing pillars. The "good enough" language is specifically disavowed ("It doesn't start with being good enough").
- *Cross-Centered Gravity* (5): 3/5. The cross is present (Romans 5:8, "Christ died for us") but not developed. Penal substitution is gestured at rather than proclaimed. The resurrection is foregrounded (appropriate given the occasion), but the cross as the ground of forgiveness deserved a moment of explicit proclamation.

*Law/Gospel Dialectic*: The law does land briefly — Romans 3:23 names universal sin, and Romans 6:23 names the wages. This is adequate but compressed. The hammer swings lightly; the balm flows freely. Given the room full of grief, a heavier hammer would have been pastorally misjudged — the rubric acknowledges this.

*Synagogue Test*: Does not pass the synagogue — Christ's necessity and the gospel's exclusivity are clearly stated. The "only in Jesus" claim is not softened.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration* (5): 3/5. The closing section on the gospel genuinely presses — "If you want to know the same confidence she had when she leaned back in that hospital bed." That image is concrete and arresting. But the earlier points are more emotionally comforting than conscience-searching.

- *Spiritual Differentiation* (5): 2/5. The sermon addresses primarily two groups: believers (be comforted) and the unconverted (receive the gospel). The backslider, the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, and the knowledgeable-but-unmoved are not directly addressed. Given the limited time and occasion, this is somewhat forgivable — but the grieving room likely contains all of Perkins' seven, and several go unaddressed.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 6 / 8**

- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness* (4): 3/4. The sermon does not leave hearers merely comforted. It places a missional impulse on them — honoring Donna's wish by receiving what she received. This is a form of witness-framing, though it stops short of explicit equipping for ongoing discipleship.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* **Christ.** The message orbits the weeping, powerful, resurrecting Jesus, not the preacher or the deceased.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* **Partly both.** The grief of God incarnate is touched. The explicit gospel at the close gives God's actual voice. But the sermon is warmer than it is weighty.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* **Comforted primarily; convicted where it mattered.** The unconverted in the room were genuinely pressed. Believers were genuinely comforted. The conviction, though present, was brief.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Gospel clarity in the closing section.** The Romans Road sequence (3:23, 6:23, 5:8; John 3:16; Romans 10:9) is well-sequenced, explicitly grounded in Scripture, and personally framed around Donna's dying wish. This is among the clearest evangelistic moments a funeral sermon can produce.
2. **Concrete, humanizing illustration.** "She was wiggling with excitement" over the ice cream sundae, combined with the hospital doorman anecdote, gives the congregation something to carry. The illustrations don't compete with the text — they prepare the room.
3. **The "deeply moved three times" exegetical observation.** Noting that Jesus is "deeply moved in his spirit and greatly troubled" (v.33), weeps (v.35), and is "deeply moved again" (v.38) across a few verses is a genuine textual insight that normalizes emotion without sentimentalizing it.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The Lazarus narrative's redemptive-historical weight is absent.** The raising of Lazarus in John's Gospel is the sign that precipitates the Sanhedrin's plot to kill Jesus (John 11:45–53). Lazarus is raised so that Jesus can die. This is the profound irony the text was written to announce — and the sermon does not touch it. The occasion allows for it to be brief, but its absence leaves the passage flatter than the evangelist intended.
2. **Point 2 (the unbelief of Mary and Martha) is introduced but not pressed.** The section opens a convicting vein — "when the rubber of faith meets the road of life, where are they?" — but then softens into Donna's confidence rather than pressing the hearer on their own unbelief. The point names a diagnostic and then steps back from it.
3. **The cross is gestured at, not proclaimed.** Romans 5:8 ("Christ died for us") appears in the gospel section, but how Christ's death saves — substitution, propitiation, the bearing of wrath — is not explained. For a room full of people who may never sit in this church again, the mechanics of the atonement deserved one sentence of clarity.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the Martha/Mary unbelief point, if it had been pressed — "Some of you are exactly where Mary was. You're staying home. Jesus is asking for you by name" — would have been the most convicting moment of the service, far more than the Romans Road at the close. The pastoral instinct toward comfort in a grief setting may have restrained what the text was offering.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The eulogy material at the opening feels like it earns relational authority — and it does. But it also creates an emotional momentum toward comfort that slightly inoculates the congregation against the convicting edge the text itself presses. The warmth of the Donna stories is genuine, but it may have inadvertently softened the room before the gospel arrived, so that the gospel landed more as encouragement than as a summons.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 40: *"Did I not tell you that if you believed you would see the glory of God?"* — This is Jesus's rebuke to unbelief in the face of prior promise. It is a word addressed to people who heard the promise, nodded, and then collapsed when circumstances contradicted it. That is the room at a funeral. That verse deserved a direct address: "Some of you believed once. You sat in a church. You heard the Word. And then something happened — a loss, a disappointment, a season of silence from God — and you stopped believing. Jesus is asking you the same question he asked Martha: *Did I not tell you?*"

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Add one sentence on the atonement in the gospel section.** After Romans 5:8, add: "He didn't just die alongside us — he died *instead of us*, taking the wrath we deserved so that the life we couldn't earn could be freely given." This takes the cross from gesture to proclamation.
2. **Press the Martha/Mary unbelief point into the room.** At the close of Point 2, add direct address: "Some of you are in that same place today. You've heard the Word. You may have sat in this room before. But when grief arrived, your faith collapsed. Jesus is still asking you: *Did I not tell you?*"
3. **Briefly surface the Lazarus-death-of-Jesus irony.** One sentence would suffice: "John tells us that when the religious leaders heard Jesus had raised Lazarus, they decided to kill him. The man who gave Lazarus his life back gave his own life up as a result. The resurrection of one friend cost Jesus everything — and that cost is exactly what purchased your salvation."
4. **Tighten the hospital doorman story to a single purpose.** If kept, close it with an explicit bridge: "That's what faith does — it opens the door. Not because of anything you've done, but because your name is in the book." This converts eulogy material into sermonic currency.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

**Genre strain is real and should be acknowledged.** This is funeral preaching — a distinct homiletical genre with its own constraints, expectations, and pastoral necessities. The rubric was designed for expository preaching to a regular congregation; it bends under the weight of a funeral setting.

Specifically:
- The deceased must be honored, which consumes time that would otherwise go to exposition.
- The congregation is emotionally raw and likely mixed (believers, skeptics, the unchurched), which presses toward accessibility over depth.
- The occasion demands pastoral comfort, which appropriately weights the gospel's balm over the law's hammer.
- Funeral preaching's primary aim — gospel proclamation to the unconverted in a moment of maximum openness — is achieved here.

The rubric is applied with these constraints in mind. The score of 75 should be read as: *within genre constraints, this is a solid and faithful funeral sermon with several specific, achievable improvements.* Evaluated against the rubric without genre adjustment, the score would fall into the 60–68 range due to exposition and applicatory depth. The genre adjustment is not a grace curve — it is hermeneutical fairness.
