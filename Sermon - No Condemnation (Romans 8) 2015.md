**No Condemnation — Romans 8:1–8**

Therefore is a great word. Anytime you see *therefore* — or *wherefore*, if you're reading an older translation — you need to stop and ask what it's there for. What came before? What is Paul's *therefore* pointing back to?

Chapter 8 is the culmination of everything Paul has been building since chapter 1.

Chapter 1: a man apart from God is sinful. Ephesians 2 puts it plainly — people apart from God are by nature children of wrath.

Chapter 2, verse 12: *"all who have sinned without the law will also perish without the law."* It is not the hearers of the law but the doers who will be justified.

Chapter 3, verse 23: *"for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."*

Chapter 4: Jesus was delivered up for our trespasses.

Chapter 5: and this was done while we were yet sinners.

Chapter 6: the wages of sin is death — but the gift of God is eternal life.

Chapter 7: the law isn't a prison. It illuminates our sin. We are dead to sin, dead to the consequences of the law.

And so chapter 8 opens with verse 1: *"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."*

R.C. Sproul taught that this means freedom from condemnation in both senses — freedom from judgment, and freedom from punishment. While we should pursue holiness, Paul seems to be reminding us that we are going to fall short of the perfect standard this side of heaven. We push on toward the goal. But we do not reach it fully until glory.

---

I heard a preacher named Chuck Lawless talk about this. He was speaking on holiness growing by degrees — small choices compounding over a lifetime. He put it in terms of those old sailing ships. The great three-masted wooden vessels that brave men steered across open ocean without GPS, without radar, navigating by the stars at night and the sun by day. If their navigation was off by even half a degree, they could end up hundreds of miles off course. Aim for Pennsylvania, end up in Cuba. It's a testament to their courage and skill that they usually ended up close to where they intended.

For us, though, Lawless pointed out something important: as long as we turn toward Christ, being off course is not a tragedy — it's an opportunity. Make one decision each day to do something that draws you closer to the Lord. Over the course of a lifetime, that half a degree of daily faithfulness compounds into a dramatically different person than you would have been.

Maybe you're a newer believer. For you, that daily decision might simply be opening your Bible. I can speak from personal experience — it makes a difference. Looking back, I can see the contrast clearly between seasons when I was in the Word and seasons when I wasn't. I was always steadier, always dealt with hardship better, always more anchored when I was spending time in Scripture. We know from the Word that it does not return void. It's an imperfect analogy, but think of it like laundry. If you throw clothes in the washing machine and leave out the water and the soap, the machine will still go through its motions — but your clothes will come out dirty and smelling like they went in. So it is with us. If we call ourselves Christians but don't spend time in the Word, we go around stinking like a pile of dirty laundry. The machinery of church attendance is going through the motions, but the cleaning agent isn't there.

Maybe you're already spending time in Scripture. Maybe for you, the half-degree correction you need to make is in sharing the gospel with the lost.

A friend of mine named Chris helped me understand this. He was a committed atheist when I knew him — or said he was. And as is so often the case with people who say they're atheists, he was angry. Angry with God. I've always found that telling. You can't be angry at someone you believe doesn't exist. Being angry at someone presupposes they exist. At worst he was agnostic, or maybe a deist, but not a true atheist. Still, he wanted nothing to do with the God he claimed wasn't there.

Around the time I met him, he was fourteen. His grandfather had just died. His father had been largely absent, and his grandparents had raised him. His grandmother had health issues and moved into a care facility. He ended up staying with a neighbor he was convinced despised him. As his grandfather was dying, he prayed: *God, if you're real, don't let my grandpa die.* The next day, his grandfather died. He decided then and there that if God existed, he hated Him.

He and I knew each other through a video game we both played. I was sixteen, maybe. He knew I was a Christian — I was open about praying, open about what the Lord was doing in my life. But because of the contempt he would show, I never pushed. I never tried to share the gospel with him.

Years later, he confronted me about it. He said — and this has stayed with me — *"Either you don't really believe what you say you do, or you don't care about me as a friend. Because if you believed, and if you cared, you'd have tried to convert me."*

I was floored.

Are there people in your life who feel that way? Taking the step of sharing the gospel with someone you love is not optional. It's what love demands.

Verse 2: *"For the law of the Spirit of life has set you free in Christ Jesus from the law of sin and death."*

That verse makes me think of Colossians 3:3 — *"your life is hidden with Christ in God."* Apart from Christ, we are not hidden anywhere. We are exposed. We are enslaved.

Think of the slave ships of the Middle Passage. Have any of you seen the film *Amistad*? I couldn't watch more than the first few minutes. What I saw was nauseating. It sent me to read more about the actual conditions aboard those ships — and what I found was no less vile than the film.

Captives were taken aboard, stripped and examined. The men were packed below decks and secured by leg irons. The space was so cramped that each person was forced to crouch or lie down. Women and children were kept separately — sometimes topside, which allowed them slightly more movement but exposed them to violence and abuse from the crew. The air below was foul and suffocating. Seasickness. Oppressive heat. No sanitation. Epidemics of fever, dysentery, and smallpox moved through the holds constantly. Captives endured these conditions for roughly two months, sometimes longer. Historians estimate that on average, approximately one in four did not survive the crossing.

John Newton, the former slave trader who became a Christian and an abolitionist — the man whose pen gave us "Amazing Grace" — wrote in his journal during his slave-trading years: *"This morning buryed a woman slave (No 47) Know not what to say she died of for she has not been properly alive since she first came on board."* The conditions were inhuman.

A man who had himself been enslaved and later made free described the horror this way in his memoir:

> *"...the whole ship's cargo were confined together, it became absolutely pestilential. The closeness of the place, and the heat of the climate, added to the number in the ship, which was so crowded that each had scarcely room to turn himself, almost suffocated us. This produced copious perspirations, so that the air soon became unfit for respiration, from a variety of loathsome smells, and brought on a sickness among the slaves, of which many died. This wretched situation was again aggravated by the galling of the chains, now become insupportable; and the filth of the necessary tubs, into which the children often fell, and were almost suffocated. The shrieks of the women, and the groans of the dying, rendered the whole scene of horror almost inconceivable."*

That is Olaudah Equiano, writing in *The Interesting Narrative of the Life of Olaudah Equiano* (1789).

For those slaves, the horror between the decks lasted two months. If you die without knowing Jesus Christ as your Lord and Savior, what waits is not two months. It is eternity. Jesus himself described it — in Mark 9:48, quoting Isaiah — as the place *"where their worm does not die and the fire is not quenched."*

Verse 8: *"Those who are in the flesh cannot please God."*

That is to say, if you have not come to the point Paul describes in Romans 10:9–10 — *"if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved"* — then you are not yet saved. We have talked about hell before. But think about it plainly: Scripture is clear that those who reject God face His just judgment. Psalm 7:11 says: *"God is a righteous judge, and a God who feels indignation every day."*

Hebrews 10:31 warns: *"It is a fearful thing to fall into the hands of the living God."* Jonathan Edwards took that verse as the foundation of his famous 1741 sermon — and the weight of it is not diminished by the centuries between then and now. A God who could speak a universe into existence with a word — who created the sun, which has burned for the entire span of human history and will continue burning past the end of our age — a God who tuned gravity and planetary motion with such precision that the whole system keeps spinning as He designed it — what kind of judgment can that God conceive of?

I took my nephew to WonderWorks this week. Have you been? It's a science-themed amusement park. They have those giant coin funnels — you drop a coin in along the side and it circles around and around, spinning faster and faster as the funnel narrows, before finally dropping into the bucket below. That's actually a decent illustration of how gravity works. Imagine a stretched bedsheet with a bowling ball in the middle — that's the effect of mass on space-time. The planets are placed just so, circling at just the right distance. Nobody gets too close and cooks. Nobody drifts too far and freezes. The whole finely-made machine keeps running, for as long as God wants it to. A God who engineered all of that with a word — what kind of eternity can He prepare for those who spend their lives in rebellion against Him?

We were all of us slaves to sin. Every one of us deserved His wrath. But Christ died — the just for the unjust, the righteous for the unrighteous, the holy for the unholy. Therefore those who believe in Jesus Christ are dead to sin. We are alive in Christ. No longer slaves, but sons and daughters of the King of Kings.

Isaiah 64:6 says that the very best we can produce on our own — our righteous deeds — are like a polluted garment before God. The Hebrew is even more graphic than the English lets on. But Christ took that. He took our filthy condition and gave us His righteousness in exchange.

Do you remember the ending of *Braveheart*? William Wallace, about to be drawn and quartered, screaming at the top of his lungs: *Freedom!* He never lived to see it. He died for a freedom Scotland wouldn't taste for years. Christ, like Wallace, was killed in the fight for freedom — but the freedom He won is not the kind that can be taken away. Wallace lived by the sword and died by the sword. Christ had never sinned — had never been a slave to anything — and yet He chose the cross. John 3:16: *"For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."*

Maybe tonight you recognize yourself in verse 8 — in the flesh, in a place where it is impossible to please God. You know you can't make it on your own. You want heaven. You don't want what's waiting on the other side of a life without Christ. If that's you, you'll have a chance to respond in a moment.

Maybe you've been thinking about joining the church. Come talk to me.

Maybe you've never been baptized. Come talk to me.

I want to close a little differently tonight. With every head bowed and every eye closed.

You've heard about what happened in Oregon and Arizona these past weeks. In Oregon, a gunman walked into a classroom and stood the students in a line. He asked each one: are you a Christian? Those who said yes were shot. He went down the line. Are you a Christian? Bang. Are you a Christian? Bang.

How many of you, if a gunman put a weapon to your head and asked that question — knowing what he had already done — could say *yes*? Raise your hand if you could.

If you couldn't raise that hand, that tells you something about both your priorities and whether your faith is something you actually possess. Put your hands down.

Now — if a plane came through the roof of this building right now, and we all stood before the gates of heaven: how many of you know, without doubt, that God would let you in?

If you don't know, today is the day. Those students in Oregon thought they had full lives ahead of them. We aren't promised tomorrow. Don't let fear stand between you and eternity. Come find me after the service. Come while the praise team sings. Just please — don't let another day pass without settling it.

Gospel.
