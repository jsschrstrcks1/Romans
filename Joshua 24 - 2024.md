## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date**: 2026-03-21
**Sermon**: Joshua 24 — 2024
**Primary Text**: Joshua 24:6-15

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**A Sermon About Gratitude That Forgot to Be a Sermon About the Text**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A warmly applied contemporary-relevance sermon that uses Joshua 24 as a launching pad for extended American-blessing statistics and personal anecdotes, then arrives at "choose this day whom you will serve" without adequately proclaiming the gospel that makes the choice possible.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test**: MARGINAL PASS. The sermon's thesis — "God has blessed us abundantly; therefore choose to serve him rather than idols" — does loosely arise from Joshua 24:14-15. The "choose this day" structure is the text's own. The problem is that the bulk of the sermon is not derived from the text at all; it is derived from global income statistics, agricultural data, missionary anecdotes, and American abundance observations. The passage is used as a frame, not as a text to be opened.

**Authority Test**: MARGINAL PASS. The preacher submits to the passage's general call to covenant faithfulness and does not soften God's demands. However, the theological weight of Joshua 24 — God's recounting of the entire covenant history from Terah to the conquest as the basis for exclusive covenant loyalty, and the solemnity of Joshua's "as for me and my house" — is substantially reduced to "count your blessings and watch less TV."

**Berean Gate**: PASS (loose) — the sermon argues what the text argues at a general level but does not open the text.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary**: The sermon ends with a placeholder: "Gospel" (literally the word on its own line, suggesting the gospel presentation was to be improvised or inserted). No actual gospel is preached in the manuscript. The call to choose God over idols is made without any explanation of how such a choice is possible, what Christ has done to make the covenant relationship available, or why the cross is the reason anyone can approach God. This is not an oversight — it is a structural vacancy.

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: "Watch less TV" and "read your bible more" are genuine applications of the general principle, but they do not arise from the specific argument of Joshua 24. The idol-identification (TV shows, status, relationships, substances) is broad-list application that could be appended to any sermon in any series.

**Score capped at 69.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics (25 pts) — 11/25**

Joshua's covenant renewal speech is not opened.
- *Contextual Awareness (5)*: 3/5. The preacher correctly situates the passage at the end of Joshua's life, as the conquest is largely complete. The Achan narrative is referenced as recent context. The broader covenantal structure is gestured at but not developed.

- *Logical Faithfulness (5)*: 2/5. The opening twelve paragraphs on American exceptionalism and global wealth comparison are not derived from the text. They are a sustained contemporary parallel that occupies the majority of the sermon's real estate.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ or myself?* — More of America's blessings and my own TV habits. Christ is the subject of one verse and a placeholder.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* — Data about blessings. The God who delivers and demands is described but not proclaimed.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* — Mildly convicted by the TV illustration; primarily informed about comparative global wealth.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The TV/idol illustration**: The "don't raise your hand, but how many watched your show without opening your Bible" move is pastorally sharp and conscience-pressing. It is specific, unanswerable, and gracefully delivered. This is what good application looks like.
2. **Consistent applicatory logic**: The sermon maintains a coherent argument — Israel was blessed and turned to idols; we are blessed and turn to idols — that is structurally sound and emotionally persuasive.
3. **Personal testimony grounds the abstract**: The healing of the preacher's mother and personal names (Bob, Julie, Rob) make the blessing section feel like testimony rather than lecture. Pastoral warmth doing real work.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is literally a blank**: The call to choose God over idols cannot be meaningfully answered without the cross — without knowing that Christ has made access to God possible, that repentance and faith are how one "chooses God," and that the power to do so comes from new birth, not willpower. The sermon leaves that entire structure implicit and unspoken.
2. **The text is twelve times shorter than the opening illustration**: The American-blessing section runs twelve dense paragraphs; the actual text engagement runs roughly two. The proportions invert the sermon's stated purpose.
3. **The "choose this day" call is issued without power**: Joshua 24:14-15 is one of the most famous covenant-commitment texts in Scripture. The sermon arrives at "choose this day" as a mild suggestion after a statistics lecture, rather than as the thunderclap of a dying leader demanding covenant faithfulness from the people of God.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher is recreating the rhetorical move of Joshua 24 (recounting God's blessings before calling for covenant fidelity) but substituting contemporary data for the text's own recital. The result is a sermon where the preacher's own observations carry the theological weight that the biblical text should carry. The preacher likely does not see that the text has already done this work — and done it better.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The abundant illustrations create an impression of a full, well-prepared sermon. The statistics feel like research. But research that crowds out the text is not exposition — it is displacement. The congregation may leave feeling they received a rich and specific message, when in fact the text's own argument was barely opened.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Joshua 24 is structured as a covenant lawsuit and covenant renewal ceremony. God himself speaks (vv. 2-13) before Joshua speaks (vv. 14-15). The structure alone is preachable: God recounts the history before he demands the response. The sermon notices this ("It is almost as though God is saying I've been faithful... Will you be faithful to me?") and then does not develop it. That single line is the emotional and theological heart of the passage and deserves a full section, not a sentence.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Fill the "Gospel" placeholder before preaching again**: This is non-negotiable. Christ bore the judgment that Israel's idolatry and our idolatry deserves. He is the one through whom covenant relationship with God is now possible. Write this out; do not improvise it.
2. **Reduce the opening statistics section to three illustrations maximum**: One poverty comparison, one security illustration, one food illustration. Recover the space for the text.
3. **Preach Joshua 24:2-13 as God's own speech**: Spend time on the fact that God himself recites his own faithfulness before making his demand. This is the text's own homiletical structure and it is remarkably powerful.
4. **Give "choose this day" the climactic weight it deserves**: The sermon's conclusion should thunder at this point. Call the congregation to a specific act of covenant declaration: "Whom will you serve? Name your God. Declare your loyalty."
5. **Address the covenant community primarily, evangelism secondarily**: Joshua 24 is addressed to people who already know God's acts. The sermon's primary applicatory audience is the believer who has allowed idols into the camp, not the unbeliever.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered. The sermon is warm and pastoral. The deficiency is gospel absence, not cold correctness.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries genuine pastoral affection for the congregation and a real desire for their covenant faithfulness, but the weight of Joshua 24 — a dying leader's final covenant call — does not come through the manuscript.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Joshua 24 is a formal covenant-renewal speech. The passage is particularly rich for gospel proclamation because the covenant God is calling the people to renew is the Abrahamic covenant fulfilled in Christ. The rubric fits well. One structural note: this text is addressed to God's people, not to pagans — the primary applicatory audience is the covenant community, and the evangelistic close is secondary to covenant-renewal address. The sermon has this ordering inverted.
