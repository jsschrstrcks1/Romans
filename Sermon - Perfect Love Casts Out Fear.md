# Perfect Love Casts Out Fear
## Hebrews 11:1 / 1 John 4:18

*[Note: Brief devotional delivered before a congregational testimony service. Not a full expository sermon.]*

---

I was asked to open up the service for anyone who'd like to share what God is doing in their lives. I love hearing those stories — God at work in real people in real time — and so I agreed to it. We'll get there in a few minutes. But you guys know me. I've got to talk for a little while first.

Turn with me to Hebrews chapter 11, verse 1.

---

I think most of us have things in our lives that we are afraid of.

I remember being five or six, about to stay the night at my grandmother's. My grandmother liked her TV loud. I mean *loud*. Her TV had a hundred volume settings and she kept it around ninety-nine. If a hundred and twenty had been an option, she'd have gone there. My dad tucked me in, turned out the lights, and closed the door. I don't remember exactly what did it — probably some shadow, or a sound — but something spooked me. I went running back out after him.

He walked me back in. Turned the lights on. Sat with me for several minutes telling me there was nothing to be afraid of. Told me I had to face my fears or they would cripple me. I trusted my dad. Before long he left — went after whatever he and mom had planned. I slept through the night. And I don't think I've ever had a real issue with the dark since.

Let's read.

> Now faith is the assurance of things hoped for, the conviction of things not seen.
> — Hebrews 11:1 (ESV)

---

We have a lot of fears, don't we. Financial fears. Social fears. Relational fears. Fears about our stuff, our kids, our future. Every one of us has serious situations in our lives that can make us feel afraid.

One day I'll tell you more about some of the things in our life that we've had to trust God through. There are some I can talk about. We've talked about Irma a fair bit — that storm required a lot of faith. My dad has shared about his father, my grandfather. Did he die a Christian? Is he in heaven or isn't he? We'll never know this side of eternity. And then there was the night the water just kept coming. Up over the planks of the dock. Up across the yard to the steps. One at a time, up and over our steps, until water started coming into our house. Into our cars. Into our dresser drawers. Up over the dresser, up over the freezers, over the tools, the books, the toys. Over everything we owned, one inch at a time, until pretty much everything we had was ruined — soaked in sewage, eventually covered in mold. We didn't even have cleaning supplies left to start putting it back together. A mobile home doesn't take much to ruin. You don't get much from insurance on one either.

And I remember a little girl who had lived with us being murdered by her babysitter. Little wonder I wasn't allowed to ride my bicycle out of the yard until I had my driver's license. Can you imagine that, parents? A little child — an infant, really — who had lived under your roof, murdered by an acquaintance. What fears would that stir in you? I was young enough, or self-assured enough, or protected by God enough, that I wasn't really phased by the fear side of it. I was sad she was gone. But it never occurred to me to be afraid.

Time after time after time our family ran headlong into situations where we were underwater, or out of our depth, or in a place where we just couldn't.

But God could.

A year or two after the flood, God provided us a beautiful home. We had just enough money to have it moved in, lifted up onto stilts, and brought up to code. I think we had a hundred dollars left over. In the mobile, my sister lived in a room smaller than some closets I've had — dresser built into the wall, barely enough space between the bed and the wall to walk in sideways. In the new house, her bedroom was as big or bigger than the master bedroom in the old mobile. We went from a propane heater that didn't always work to central heat and air. From eight hundred square feet to two thousand. We loved that mobile home. We were sad to see it go. But it's as though God was saying, that night in March of '93 — *just trust me. I have something better around the corner.*

And with the little girl who was murdered — God protected my sister and me. He didn't let that trauma shape the view of the world that was still forming in us. He kept us from being afraid of everyone. That kind of thing does happen to kids who go through something like that.

The point is this: God is active and working and moving in every situation we're involved with.

---

I came across a quote this week from a commentary on Hebrews that landed on me:

> "Fear is the result of distrust, of taking the eye off God, of being unduly occupied with difficulties and troubles."

That stings a little. When we have these bouts of fear, what we're really saying to God is: *I don't trust you to handle this.*

Open with me to 1 John 4:18.

> There is no fear in love, but perfect love casts out fear. For fear has to do with punishment, and whoever fears has not been perfected in love.
> — 1 John 4:18 (ESV)

Love is the opposite of fear. So when we are gripped with fear, we aren't loving God the way we should. When we love God properly, we are focused on Him — not on the waves. The commentator could have written the same sentence this way: *Fear is the result of not loving God fully, of taking the eye off Him, of being occupied with our difficulties and troubles.* Same truth, different angle.

And here is the long and short of it:

**Fear is the evidence of things in our hearts that we love as much or more than God.**

Think about it. When we fear for our physical needs, we're telling God we don't trust him to follow through on his promises. We're demonstrating that we love our own comfort more than we love Him. We're saying, in effect, that what He's done and is doing isn't good enough.

Imagine you're visiting a friend out of state — let's say Pastor Bob and Sherry, out in Texas. How mortified would you be if your kid turned to them and said their hospitality wasn't meeting your family's needs? Bob and Sherry go out of their way to love people. Can you imagine how that would land? Yet that's exactly what we do to God when we entrenched in fear about physical provision. We're telling Him His care is insufficient.

Shameful. And we all do it.

It's not just physical needs. Financial needs. Any area where fear takes hold and won't let go. I know a young woman who struggles with singleness — afraid she'll never meet the right man, never settle down, never have a family. She has to be careful. Because that fear, if she isn't watchful, becomes distrust of God.

And here's one that really stings: politics.

Everyone has a view. Everyone is passionate about it. Everyone wants their candidate to win. And everyone has fear of the opposing candidate. Fear of what Hillary or Joe Biden would do to the country. Fear that the country won't survive it. But what does God say?

> Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God.
> — Romans 13:1 (ESV)

If someone is in authority, they have been placed there by God. That means when a Republican is in charge, God put them there. Before you amen that — the other side of that coin is that if a Democrat or an Independent is in charge, God placed that person there as well. If Hillary had won, it would have been because God ordained it — because God had placed her in the highest office in the land. I'm personally grateful that didn't happen. Trump has done better than I expected. But the point is: God has our politics in hand. We should absolutely vote. But the results are in His hand. So what do we have to fear?

And when we *do* fear — what are we saying? *I don't love you enough to trust you with a political candidate I disagree with. I don't love you enough to trust you with my singleness. I don't love you enough to trust you with my kids.*

Is that what we want to say to God?

---

God has it all under control.

I think back to that little girl — a toddler, really — who was murdered. God had a purpose even in the midst of that horror. That absolute worst nightmare for a parent. We don't know what would have happened to her had she lived. But you know who does? God. The same God we love. The same God we can trust. The God who safeguards our eternity can certainly safeguard our short, fragile lives.

The God who sent His Son to earth in humility, to live a sinless life, to pay our sin debt through a death He did not deserve — that God is worthy of our love. And He is worthy of our trust.

Hate isn't the opposite of love. Fear is.

Let's pray — and then we'll open it up for some testimonies.

---

*[Prayer and testimony time.]*
