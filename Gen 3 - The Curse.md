My dad always said there are two kinds of snakes. Dead ones, and those whose heads haven't been chopped off yet.

I've never been a fan of snakes.

Our old farrier used to tell stories about his best friend jumping into Moon Lake and being killed by a nest of baby moccasins. My grandfather told me about being chased by a rattlesnake. I almost grabbed a pygmy rattler in my grandmother's garden one day — didn't see it until my hand was about six inches away. I had a dog get bitten on the lip as a puppy. She came close to dying. Sick for a week. Uncle Mike kept her going on Gatorade and yogurt, and she made it. And another friend — as a baby — was bitten by a rattlesnake. Spent several grim weeks in the hospital.

Snakes are part of living in Florida. Most of them want nothing to do with you, some of them want to hurt you, and a few of the good ones eat the bad ones. But I'm not fond of any of them.

Tonight in the passage we're reading, we'll find out how snakes came to be hated nearly universally — and why they are the way they are.

Did you notice something last week? It didn't hit me until this week. Neither Eve nor Adam was surprised to see a talking serpent. Makes you wonder whether other animals spoke. Anyway — open to Genesis 3. Read with me starting at verse 9.

*[Read Genesis 3:9–end.]*

---

**The Curse**

The snake is cursed first. And it's a severe one.

The text says he shall be cursed above all other animals — which implies the other animals are affected by the fall as well, just not to the same degree. But things get serious when God tells the serpent it will crawl on its belly.

One commentator I read pointed out that snakes have vestigial bones in their bodies. Vestigial meaning a bone that isn't really needed anymore — like our appendix, which we're told we can live just fine without. These snakes have a set of vestigial bones back by their business end that don't do much. They help stabilize things during mating, though they're not required even for that. Scientists have found that these bones are not entirely unlike a human femur. The big bone in your leg. And on top of that, the boa constrictor and certain pythons have what appears to be a pelvis — and normally the only animals with a pelvis are ones that walk upright.

Scripture doesn't come out and state it plainly. But in a passage about Satan, he's called the great serpent and described as being cast down out of the sky. It's not enough for me to say with full confidence that all snakes once flew. But I think it's safe to say some of them walked.

I love that God left these signposts in the creature itself. Because if evolution is true, snakes took a catastrophic step backward. The apex predator — the one that supposedly stood upright at the top of the food chain — somehow traded legs for a belly crawl. Either evolution made a spectacular mistake, or God created snakes a certain way and then cursed them into something lesser. One of those is true. And I think we all know which one it is. Don't you love it when science shows up to confirm what Scripture already said?

A creature that may have been quite majestic was cast down. Now it eats the dust.

That sounds almost comedic — like something the kids might do with mud pies in the backyard. But it's real. Snakes do consume dust. Most of it passes through without any nutritional benefit. But a small amount of it gets deposited into something called Jacobsen's organ, which helps them smell and track prey. Eating dust doesn't feed them. It helps them find food.

I'll be honest — I'm grateful that didn't make it into man's curse.

---

Now — the final part of the serpent's curse is a double entendre. One set of words. Two meanings. Two targets.

First, there is enmity placed between women and snakes. As much as most men don't love snakes, most women are even less enthusiastic about them. That's not cultural — that's covenantal.

But the deeper meaning — the more important one — applies to the real enemy. Because in this passage, Satan himself is cursed.

Let me back up for a moment and say a word about who we're dealing with.

A demon is a fallen angel. Satan is their chief. Before the fall of the angels — which was a separate event from the fall of man, and likely happened before it — Satan was an angel of some rank. One passage describes him as more beautiful than the others. Around a third of the angels fell with him. All angels, fallen or not, are incorporeal — beings without physical bodies. Demons can inhabit an unbeliever. Angels appear to be forbidden from doing so. And most importantly: all angels, fallen or not, are created beings. Subject to and subservient to God.

So the final part of the curse applies to Satan as well. Eve's seed will have his heel bruised. But *he* will crush the serpent's head.

I've bruised my heel before.

I was coming down a flight of stairs that hadn't been fully attached to the house yet. I didn't know that. I started down, got a couple steps in, and the whole flight pulled away from the wall. I fell about six feet and landed on one foot — heel first — right on the corner of a stair tread. Limped for a few days. Maybe a small bone shifted. It was painful. And then it was over.

Temporary. Short-term.

So who is Eve's seed? Who was born of a woman without any involvement from a man?

Only one person in human history. Jesus.

What we have here, buried in the curse, is the first gospel promise in Scripture. Satan will strike Jesus. But Jesus will crush Satan's head. We see it fulfilled in Revelation 20:10:

*"and the devil who had deceived them was thrown into the lake of fire and sulfur where the beast and the false prophet were, and they will be tormented day and night forever and ever."*

Jesus survived with a bruised heel.

---

One more thing before we close — and I want you to see this.

After they sinned, Adam and Eve sewed fig leaves together to cover their shame. I brought one in. They're substantial for a leaf. But think about what they had to sew with — vines, probably — and imagine walking anywhere in that. It wouldn't last long. It worked. It covered what needed covering. But it wasn't going to hold.

And that's the picture of the old covenant. Man constantly working to keep sin covered. Every transgression required a pure, unblemished animal to die. The blood of bulls and goats, over and over again, maintaining a covering that was never quite enough.

God stepped in and made them clothing from animal skins. More durable. More complete. But notice what that required. Something had to die. Where there had been no death, no violence — now there was. The first blood shed in this world was shed to cover the shame of the people God made. It was pointing forward. Death would now be the cost of sin, and God himself would one day provide the final, permanent covering.

---

I want to go back to the heel.

In the week before the crucifixion, Christ's closest friends couldn't stay awake to pray with him for a single hour. One of them sold him to the religious authorities for thirty pieces of silver. When they came for him, another one drew a sword and cut off a soldier's ear — proving he still didn't fully understand what Jesus had been teaching. Christ was taken to an illegal trial in the middle of the night, where paid witnesses couldn't even get their stories to match. Then handed to Rome. Tried again. And condemned.

The beating that followed was not a punishment. It was a death sentence.

The Roman flagrum was designed to lacerate. The operator would swing it so the leather strips wrapped around the body, then snap it back to tear on the way out. Ancient writers described men who did not survive it. Rome used it to weaken the condemned before crucifixion — not to kill them, not yet.

That is what Jesus walked to the cross carrying.

Nailed through hand and foot. Hung until he couldn't breathe. Mocked while he died.

And it was not Rome that put him there. Not Pilate. Not the Pharisees. It was my sin that held him there. Yours too.

*"It was my sin that held him there until it was accomplished. His dying breath has brought me life — I know that it is finished. Why should I gain from his reward? I cannot give an answer. But this I know with all my heart — his wounds have paid my ransom."*
— Stuart Townend, "How Deep the Father's Love for Us," 1995

He died.

And three days later he walked out of the tomb. Over five hundred people saw him after the resurrection — 1 Corinthians 15:6. Five hundred witnesses.

The head was crushed. The heel recovered.

---

Maybe tonight you've never come to a place where you've asked Jesus to rescue you. He's already done the hard part. Romans 10 says confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord, believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead — and you will be saved. If you've never done that, come talk to me tonight.

Maybe you're looking for a church home. Come talk to me. Maybe you've got a prayer need. Come talk to me. Maybe you just want to come to the altar and pray — it's open.

Praise team, come on up.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**"The Serpent's Skull and the Savior's Heel — Promising Gospel Instincts, Thin Applicatory Nerve"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A pastorally warm and narratively engaging opening of Genesis 3:9–end that lands the protoevangelium with genuine gospel clarity, while failing to press the conscience of specific hearers and closing before the application fully arrives.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS**

- **Scopus Test**: The sermon's thesis — that the curse on the serpent contains the first gospel promise, and that Christ fulfilled it by crushing Satan's head while suffering only a bruised heel — demonstrably arises from Genesis 3:14–15. The main point of the sermon is the main point of the passage's climax. Pass.
- **Authority Test**: The preacher does not soften the text. He submits to the curse language, treats the serpent's degradation as real, names Christ as the seed, and applies the cross without apology. Pass.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No fatal flaw flags triggered.** Scores are uncapped.

Notes:
- The gospel is presented, though briefly. No fatal flaw on gospel absence.
- The application is general and thin (see weakness below), but not entirely absent — it does not trigger the fatal flaw threshold.
- The sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers. No seminary training required.
- There is an exhortation at the close (the altar call). It is brief and tethered primarily to conversion, not to the full range of hearers.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1: Exposition & Hermeneutics — 17 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity (7/10)*: The core of the exposition tracks the text faithfully — the serpent's curse, the enmity, the protoevangelium in v.15. However, significant sermon time is spent on vestigial snake bones and natural history tangents. The woman's curse and Adam's curse receive almost no treatment. The text from v.16 onward is effectively skipped. The passage's full argument is not opened; the sermon selects what serves the thesis and leaves the rest.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5)*: The connection to Satan via Revelation 20:10 is appropriate. The Christological fulfillment is handled well. But the immediate narrative context — the interrogation of Adam and Eve, the relational rupture between humans and with God — receives no development.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5)*: The dual application of the seed/heel/head language is explained with clarity and memorable illustration. The "double entendre" framing is useful.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5)*: The natural history detour (vestigial pelvises, pythons, Jacobsen's organ) is presented as textual confirmation but risks functioning as eisegesis via extra-biblical science. The statement "Scripture doesn't come out and state it plainly... but I think it's safe to say some of them walked" is speculative and occupies disproportionate sermon real estate. The FCF (fallen condition focus) is never explicitly named — what human condition does this text address? Death, alienation, the need for a substitute — these are present by implication but never named as the wound the text is healing.

**Category 2: Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (5/7)*: The Christ-connection is organic — v.15 is genuinely the protoevangelium, not a Jesus-jump. The connection is real and well-drawn. Points lost because the mechanism of atonement (penal substitution) is described viscerally via the passion narrative but not named or unpacked theologically.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3)*: The animal skins / fig leaves / old covenant / new covenant movement is genuinely good redemptive-historical theology. Organic, not forced.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4)*: The passion narrative is described with raw, powerful detail, and "my sin held him there" is appropriately substitutionary in register. However, justification is not named, and the mechanism of how the crushed head translates into the hearer's salvation is left implicit.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4)*: No contradictions of confessional theology. The sovereign curse, the demonic taxonomy (briefly), and the substitutionary suggestion are all within 1689 parameters. Not enough soteriological precision to award full marks.

**Category 3: Gospel Centrality & Force — 10 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (3/5)*: The passion narrative and Stuart Townend lyric carry the gospel's weight, but justification by faith is never defined. Romans 10:9 is cited at the close, but the "confess and believe" formula is issued as instruction, not as a full gospel presentation that explains *why* this saves. An unbeliever might understand that Christ died, but not the precise nature of the exchange.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5)*: Christ is essential to the argument. The sermon cannot survive without him. V.15's seed is identified as Christ and only Christ. This is the strongest gospel element.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5)*: The passion section is powerful. But the sermon's center of gravity is the serpent's biology and natural history. The cross arrives late and is delivered with pastoral urgency but limited theological unpacking. The law/gospel dialectic is underworked — there is no moment where the hearer is stripped of self-trust before the gospel is offered. The Hammer is absent; the Balm is present, but without the wound the balm treats, it functions as comfort without prior conviction.

**Category 4: Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration (2/5)*: "It was my sin that held him there. Yours too." This single sentence is the most penetrating moment in the sermon. But it is almost immediately relieved by the Townend lyric and the resurrection, without time for the law to do its work. The conscience is touched but not searched.
- *Specificity (2/5)*: The application is nearly entirely generic. "Maybe tonight you've never come to a place where you've asked Jesus to rescue you." This is the only differentiated call. No address to the person who has heard this their whole life. No address to the believer living in secret sin. No address to the one who doubts the resurrection. No address to the person whose response to snakes in the text is curiosity rather than fear.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (3/5)*: The conversion appeal at the close acknowledges at least the unbeliever category. But Perkins' seven categories are essentially unreached: the backslider, the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, the knowledgeable-but-unmoved (the church member who has heard this a hundred times), and the grieving are all left without address. The sermon primarily serves the category 2 hearer (ignorant but teachable) and partially addresses category 4 (the humbled).

**Category 5: Exhortation & Response — 5 / 8**

- *Call to Response (3/4)*: There is an explicit call — "come talk to me" — with a Romans 10 anchor. The call is genuine, though it is limited to conversion. The believer is not exhorted to anything specific.
- *Equipping for Holiness and Witness (2/4)*: The sermon does not send the church out. The altar call is pastoral and appropriate, but there is no "therefore" moment that tells the believer what to do Monday morning with the protoevangelium. No corporate dimension, no witness equipping, no call to live under the promise of the seed.

**Category 6: Structure & Logical Flow — 6 / 9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4)*: The sermon has a discernible shape: snake intro → curse on serpent (natural history) → protoevangelium → fig leaves / animal skins → passion narrative → altar call. Each section is followable. But the transitions are informal and the structure is largely associative rather than architecturally intentional.
- *Progression (2/3)*: The sermon builds toward the cross, which is correct. But the natural history detour in the early-middle section slows the momentum and is never fully integrated into the theological argument. The question "why does any of this matter for me?" is deferred too long.
- *Unity (1/2)*: The sermon has two or three competing centers: serpent biology, the protoevangelium, and the fig leaves / animal skins sequence. Each is individually coherent, but they do not build toward a single unified point with force. The conclusion ties them together inadequately.

**Category 7: Illustration & Imagination — 4 / 5**

- *Relevance (2/2)*: The snake stories are native to this congregation's world — Florida, farms, rural familiarity. The stair fall / bruised heel illustration is well-chosen and concrete.
- *Clarity (1/2)*: The vestigial bone discussion illuminates an interesting data point but occupies more space than its theological contribution justifies. It distracts as much as it illuminates.
- *Memorability (1/1)*: "Dead snakes and those whose heads haven't been chopped off yet" will be remembered. The heel-bruise illustration is memorable and well-executed.

**Category 8: Sermonic Force — 4 / 5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2)*: This reads as oral preaching throughout. Short sentences, direct address, personal stories. No essay problem here.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2)*: The passion narrative section is genuinely gripping. But the natural history section reads more like curiosity than conviction — interesting information, not urgent proclamation.
- *Cadence and Breath (1/1)*: The short, declarative cadence at the close ("He died. And three days later he walked out of the tomb.") earns its moment. The variation between narrative and declarative registers is handled well.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Primarily Christ. The protoevangelium and the passion narrative orient the sermon outward. The opening snake anecdotes are long but not narcissistic.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Partially. The passion section — "it was my sin that held him there" — is the closest the sermon comes to the felt weight of God. The natural history section keeps God at a safe intellectual distance.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Primarily informed, with a moment of comfort at the close. Conviction is largely absent. The hammer was never swung, so the balm relieves a wound the congregation was not made to feel.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The protoevangelium identification is textually rooted and Christologically precise.** The dual application of v.15 to both physical enmity between women and snakes and to the spiritual enmity between the seed and Satan is genuinely well-handled. The Revelation 20:10 connection is appropriate and not forced.

2. **The fig leaves / animal skins sequence is the sermon's best theological moment.** The redemptive-historical movement from man's inadequate covering → sacrificial covering → final covering is compact, textually grounded, and will preach. "Something had to die" lands with appropriate weight.

3. **The sermonic voice is native and unaffected.** The snake stories, the stair fall, the personal detail — these are from this preacher's actual world, not borrowed from another man's sermon. The congregation will trust this voice.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The passage's second half is unpreached.** The woman's curse (v.16), Adam's curse (vv.17–19), the expulsion from the garden (vv.22–24), and the naming of Eve (v.20) are treated as absent text. The sermon is subtitled "The Curse" but preaches only the serpent's curse. The fallen condition focus — what this text exists to address — is never named. The congregation's curse is the most personally urgent part of the passage and it was not opened.

2. **The conscience section is a single sentence that is immediately relieved.** "It was my sin that held him there. Yours too." This is a powerful, exact sentence. But it is followed immediately by a hymn lyric and then the resurrection and then the altar call. The law has no time to work. The conscience is touched and then immediately reassured. This is the sermon's most significant pastoral weakness: comfort was offered before conviction was established.

3. **The natural history detour consumes disproportionate real estate.** The vestigial bones, Jacobsen's organ, and walking snake speculation occupy approximately a third of the sermon's space. This is the section a congregation will remember as interesting — but it is the section least tied to what this text demands. The speculative claim that snakes once walked is handled with appropriate epistemic humility ("I don't think it's safe to say with full confidence..."), but the disproportionate time investment signals that the preacher finds this more interesting than urgent.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher does not appear to see that the audience most in need of this sermon is not the unconverted newcomer (addressed at the close) but the longtime church member who has heard the protoevangelium many times and sits unmoved — "knowledgeable but unhumbled" in Perkins' taxonomy. This category is never addressed. The sermon assumes that familiarity with the gospel is conversion, and never asks whether the hearer truly believes the promise.

The preacher may also not see that the passage's greatest pastoral weight is the curse on Adam and Eve — the difficulty of work, the pain of childbirth, the hostility of the ground — because these are the conditions his congregation actually lives in. The serpent's curse is historically interesting. Adam and Eve's curse is existentially present.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The passion narrative section reads as the sermon's emotional high point — and it is moving. The Roman flagrum description, the illegal trial, the "not Rome, not Pilate" redirection — these feel like the sermon's climax, and in some respects they are. But this section, for all its visceral force, does not arise from the text. Genesis 3:15 says "he will crush your head and you will strike his heel." The sermon's passion narrative is not an exposition of this verse — it is a free-standing gospel sermon grafted onto the text. The strength is real; the textual grounding is assumed, not demonstrated.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The passage's climax, textually speaking, is not the protoevangelium alone — it is the expulsion from Eden and the placement of the cherubim with the flaming sword (vv.22–24). This is the first exclusion from God's presence, the first broken shalom, the first picture of what sin costs in relational terms. The garden is barred. Man cannot return on his own. This is the wound that the whole of Scripture spends 66 books healing. The sermon moved past it without pause. The weight of exclusion — and therefore the weight of re-inclusion in Christ — was forfeited.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific)

1. **Name the fallen condition explicitly before preaching the gospel.** After the opening sections on the serpent, pause and say something like: "This is the condition we are all born into — cursed, broken, bearing the weight of what happened in this garden. That is why this promise matters. Not just to them. To you." Give the law time to land before the gospel relieves it.

2. **Preach Adam and Eve's curse, even briefly.** The sermon is titled "The Curse" — but only the serpent's curse is opened. Even two minutes on the woman's pain and the man's futile labor would anchor the sermon in the congregation's lived experience. This is the connection between the ancient text and Monday morning.

3. **Realign the sermon's climax to the text's climax.** The protoevangelium is in v.15. The sermon's emotional climax is in the passion narrative (late sermon). The text's own structural climax is the expulsion in vv.22–24. Consider whether a paragraph on the closed garden — and what it means that Christ has reopened access (Hebrews 10:19–20) — would give the sermon a more textually honest conclusion.

4. **Add differentiated address after the gospel section.** Before the altar call, address the congregation in categories: "If you have never trusted Christ — this promise is for you, come. If you have trusted him — this promise means the war is won. If you are wandering — the seed has already crushed the head; there is nothing left to keep you away." Three sentences. Three hearers. The call becomes pastoral, not merely evangelistic.

5. **Trim the natural history section by half.** The vestigial bones and Jacobsen's organ are genuinely interesting. One paragraph is sufficient. The second and third paragraphs on walking snakes and Jacobsen's organ slow the sermon's momentum at its most critical early-middle stage without proportional theological payoff. The science-confirms-Scripture point is made with one example.

**Not triggered.**

The sermon does not score high enough on exposition (17) or theology (13) to trigger the dead orthodoxy pattern. The warmth and pastoral directness of the voice prevent inertness. The sermon's weakness is not coldness but incompleteness.

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
**+2 / 5**

The sermon carries genuine pastoral warmth and the passion narrative section briefly achieves real gravity. But the protracted natural history discussion keeps God at an intellectual distance for too long, and the compressed conscience section means the sermon does not linger long enough in the weight of what Christ bore for the hearer to feel it fully. There are moments where eternal significance breaks through — the "something had to die" sequence especially — but they are too brief to constitute unction.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Genesis 3 is OT narrative with embedded poetry (the curse oracles) and a covenantal hinge point (the protoevangelium). The rubric fits well here: the redemptive-historical category (Category 2) is the natural evaluation grid for this passage, and the gospel centrality categories (Categories 3–5) are exactly where narrative passages like this must be tested. The passage is not lament or wisdom literature — it is foundational covenantal narrative, and the rubric's weight on exposition and gospel is entirely appropriate. No genre strain noted. The rubric bends slightly in Category 4 (Conscience) because Genesis 3 does not supply a direct NT applicatory framework — but this is a minor strain, not a disqualifying one.
