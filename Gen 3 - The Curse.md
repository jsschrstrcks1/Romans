My dad always said there are two kinds of snakes. Dead ones, and those whose heads haven't been chopped off yet.

I've never been a fan of snakes.

Our old farrier used to tell stories about his best friend jumping into Moon Lake and being killed by a nest of baby moccasins. My grandfather told me about being chased by a rattlesnake. I almost grabbed a pygmy rattler in my grandmother's garden one day — didn't see it until my hand was about six inches away. I had a dog get bitten on the lip as a puppy. She came close to dying. Sick for a week. Uncle Mike kept her going on Gatorade and yogurt, and she made it. And another friend — as a baby — was bitten by a rattlesnake. Spent several grim weeks in the hospital.

Snakes are part of living in Florida. Most of them want nothing to do with you, some of them want to hurt you, and a few of the good ones eat the bad ones. But I'm not fond of any of them.

Tonight in the passage we're reading, we'll find out how snakes came to be hated nearly universally — and why they are the way they are.

Did you notice something last week? It didn't hit me until this week. Neither Eve nor Adam was surprised to see a talking serpent. Makes you wonder whether other animals spoke. Anyway — open to Genesis 3. Read with me starting at verse 9.

*[Read Genesis 3:9–end.]*

---

**The Curse**

The snake is cursed first. And it's a severe one.

The text says he shall be cursed above all other animals — which implies the other animals are affected by the fall as well, just not to the same degree. But things get serious when God tells the serpent it will crawl on its belly.

One commentator I read pointed out that snakes have vestigial bones in their bodies. Vestigial meaning a bone that isn't really needed anymore — like our appendix, which we're told we can live just fine without. These snakes have a set of vestigial bones back by their business end that don't do much. They help stabilize things during mating, though they're not required even for that. Scientists have found that these bones are not entirely unlike a human femur. The big bone in your leg. And on top of that, the boa constrictor and certain pythons have what appears to be a pelvis — and normally the only animals with a pelvis are ones that walk upright.

Scripture doesn't come out and state it plainly. But in a passage about Satan, he's called the great serpent and described as being cast down out of the sky. It's not enough for me to say with full confidence that all snakes once flew. But I think it's safe to say some of them walked.

I love that God left these signposts in the creature itself. Because if evolution is true, snakes took a catastrophic step backward. The apex predator — the one that supposedly stood upright at the top of the food chain — somehow traded legs for a belly crawl. Either evolution made a spectacular mistake, or God created snakes a certain way and then cursed them into something lesser. One of those is true. And I think we all know which one it is. Don't you love it when science shows up to confirm what Scripture already said?

A creature that may have been quite majestic was cast down. Now it eats the dust.

That sounds almost comedic — like something the kids might do with mud pies in the backyard. But it's real. Snakes do consume dust. Most of it passes through without any nutritional benefit. But a small amount of it gets deposited into something called Jacobsen's organ, which helps them smell and track prey. Eating dust doesn't feed them. It helps them find food.

I'll be honest — I'm grateful that didn't make it into man's curse.

---

Now — the final part of the serpent's curse is a double entendre. One set of words. Two meanings. Two targets.

First, there is enmity placed between women and snakes. As much as most men don't love snakes, most women are even less enthusiastic about them. That's not cultural — that's covenantal.

But the deeper meaning — the more important one — applies to the real enemy. Because in this passage, Satan himself is cursed.

Let me back up for a moment and say a word about who we're dealing with.

A demon is a fallen angel. Satan is their chief. Before the fall of the angels — which was a separate event from the fall of man, and likely happened before it — Satan was an angel of some rank. One passage describes him as more beautiful than the others. Around a third of the angels fell with him. All angels, fallen or not, are incorporeal — beings without physical bodies. Demons can inhabit an unbeliever. Angels appear to be forbidden from doing so. And most importantly: all angels, fallen or not, are created beings. Subject to and subservient to God.

So the final part of the curse applies to Satan as well. Eve's seed will have his heel bruised. But *he* will crush the serpent's head.

I've bruised my heel before.

I was coming down a flight of stairs that hadn't been fully attached to the house yet. I didn't know that. I started down, got a couple steps in, and the whole flight pulled away from the wall. I fell about six feet and landed on one foot — heel first — right on the corner of a stair tread. Limped for a few days. Maybe a small bone shifted. It was painful. And then it was over.

Temporary. Short-term.

So who is Eve's seed? Who was born of a woman without any involvement from a man?

Only one person in human history. Jesus.

What we have here, buried in the curse, is the first gospel promise in Scripture. Satan will strike Jesus. But Jesus will crush Satan's head. We see it fulfilled in Revelation 20:

*"And the devil, who deceived them, was thrown into the lake of burning sulfur, where the beast and the false prophet had been thrown. They will be tormented day and night for ever and ever."*

Jesus survived with a bruised heel.

---

One more thing before we close — and I want you to see this.

After they sinned, Adam and Eve sewed fig leaves together to cover their shame. I brought one in. They're substantial for a leaf. But think about what they had to sew with — vines, probably — and imagine walking anywhere in that. It wouldn't last long. It worked. It covered what needed covering. But it wasn't going to hold.

And that's the picture of the old covenant. Man constantly working to keep sin covered. Every transgression required a pure, unblemished animal to die. The blood of bulls and goats, over and over again, maintaining a covering that was never quite enough.

God stepped in and made them clothing from animal skins. More durable. More complete. But notice what that required. Something had to die. Where there had been no death, no violence — now there was. The first blood shed in this world was shed to cover the shame of the people God made. It was pointing forward. Death would now be the cost of sin, and God himself would one day provide the final, permanent covering.

---

I want to go back to the heel.

In the week before the crucifixion, Christ's closest friends couldn't stay awake to pray with him for a single hour. One of them sold him to the religious authorities for thirty pieces of silver. When they came for him, another one drew a sword and cut off a soldier's ear — proving he still didn't fully understand what Jesus had been teaching. Christ was taken to an illegal trial in the middle of the night, where paid witnesses couldn't even get their stories to match. Then handed to Rome. Tried again. And condemned.

The beating that followed was not a punishment. It was a death sentence — stopped one short of forty.

Old naval records describe how the cat-o'-nine-tails worked. The operator would swing it so the tails wrapped around the body, then snap it back to do maximum damage on the way out. By eight lashes bones were visible. By thirty-five, organs were being struck directly. By thirty-nine — which is where they stopped — a man's insides were largely outside.

That is what Jesus walked to the cross carrying.

Nailed through hand and foot. Hung until he couldn't breathe. Mocked while he died.

And it was not Rome that put him there. Not Pilate. Not the Pharisees. It was my sin that held him there. Yours too.

*"It was my sin that held him there until it was accomplished. His dying breath has brought me life — I know that it is finished. Why should I gain from his reward? I cannot give an answer. But this I know with all my heart — his wounds have paid my ransom."*

He died.

And three days later he walked out of the tomb. Over five hundred people saw him after the resurrection. Five hundred witnesses.

The head was crushed. The heel recovered.
