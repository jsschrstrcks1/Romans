- **Jonathan Edwards quote**: The sermon cites Edwards for the extended passage beginning "[God is] able to save to the uttermost all that come unto God through him..." The opening clause appears to be Edwards citing **Hebrews 7:25** (KJV: "he is able also to save them to the uttermost that come unto God by him, seeing he ever liveth to make intercession for them") within his own theological commentary on the covenant. The subsequent language — "God did not see it fit that man should be trusted to stand in his own strength a second time... It is not fit that in a covenant of grace, wherein all is of mere, free, and absolute grace, that the reward of life should be dependent on the perseverance of man" — is thoroughly consistent with Edwards' federal theology on the covenant of grace versus the covenant of works. *Specific source in Edwards' works has not been confirmed. The attribution is theologically plausible and widely circulated. Verify against Edwards' collected works before print use.*
- **"He's Still Working on Me"**: The pastor describes this as a "children's song." It was written by **Joel Hemphill** (1980) and is widely used in Vacation Bible School and children's ministry contexts, though it was composed for congregational use broadly. Calling it a "children's song" is a common description but a slight category imprecision. The lyrics quoted in the sermon are accurate to Hemphill's text. ✓
- **Brain illustration**: The claim that "the remaining half of your brain can fully control every aspect of your entire body" after hemispherectomy is an overstatement. The procedure is real (particularly performed on children with severe epilepsy), and significant functional recovery is possible, especially in young patients. "Every aspect" is too strong a claim medically, particularly for adults. This is an illustration, not a medical claim — used here to convey intimate knowledge, not surgical outcomes.
- **R.C. Sproul on "preservation"**: Accurate. Sproul did prefer "preservation of the saints" over "perseverance" to emphasize that it is God's work, not man's effort, that keeps the believer. His concern about the term "perseverance" implying human effort is well-documented in his teaching. ✓

This is our fourth and final sermon on the scriptural doctrines of grace.

We talked about our own inability to save ourselves — the extent to which sin has corrupted us and fogged up our glasses so we can't see clearly. We talked about how, as a result, we naturally didn't want anything to do with God. So God had to come and save some of us. It wasn't because of merit. Simply because he's good and kind and gracious while still being fully just and holy. We talked about how there is nothing so bad that we can do to cause Christ or God to reject us. Finally we are going to talk about one of those very Baptist phrases — one that builds on and assumes all the rest of these things we have talked about. It's called "once saved always saved," or to use the words of the reformers, they called it perseverance of the saints.

That sort of makes it sound like it has something to do with man's effort. It doesn't. However there is a group of people out there that believe we have to work to maintain our salvation. If we slow down, we can lose it, or we can choose to give it back. Because of that, R.C. Sproul likes to call it preservation of the saints. That has its own issues — but the name is not as important as the question of whether or not the idea is based in scripture. Fortunately for us, it is. Otherwise, we would have no hope.

---

Back when I went to Trinity I went to school with this guy named Josh Havel. I really didn't know him too well, but I met his sister while she was visiting campus. He and I became friends because she and I became friends.

Anyway, Josh held to that position — that salvation could be lost or given away. That's one extreme on the scale. At the extreme other end, people believe that if you hold a position like that about God then you're not really saved. So while Rachel was visiting Trinity, within a week of her having been saved, a group of four or five guys at this other extreme end got together and kind of attacked her with the Bible. Flogged her with scripture and told her she was still lost because she didn't believe in eternal security. Obviously that's nonsense. No one can point to any place in scripture that says unless you believe in eternal security you are going to hell. They had part of it right. The Bible does support eternal security. But they had part of it twisted too. There is no verse in scripture that says "if you get man's role in salvation messed up you're going to go to hell."

Long story short, her brother and I got to be pretty good friends. I'd joke with him about whether he had found his salvation or not, and if he had, to be careful not to leave it on the roof of his car — it might get lost again. Sometimes he'd respond, "Fortunately for me someone turned it in at Walmart, so I got it back." Or, "I left it in my other pants."

---

This issue is one where there are good Christians on both sides. However, I believe the scripture is clear regarding God's perspective.

It builds upon all these other things we've been talking about. If man is unable to save himself — unable to believe in God, unable to do even a single thing truly good with pure motives — then how can we expect that we can maintain our salvation? We can't earn it, we couldn't catch it if we were trying to chase it. We couldn't do anything to achieve salvation on our own. If our salvation were up to us, heaven would be occupied by God alone. Even if we could clearly see what was required for salvation, we would — according to Paul in 1 Corinthians 2:14 — consider it foolishness and refuse to pursue it. So God had to work in us to bring us to a point where we could even see him, and work in us to draw us toward himself. And so it's no surprise to me that God has to work in us to continue to draw us toward himself after we are saved. He has to hold onto us to keep us from running too far away.

Jonathan Edwards says basically that God left it up to us the first time, and we failed dramatically. So knowing we would fail, God came up with a new and better covenant. A covenant is basically God's "deal" with us for salvation — one that didn't depend upon us, because as a race mankind has proven we can't be depended upon. The rest here is a direct quote from Edwards:

> [God is] able to save to the uttermost all that come unto God through him, and ever lives to make intercession for them. God did not see it fit that man should be trusted to stand in his own strength a second time. It is not fit that in a covenant of grace, wherein all is of mere, free, and absolute grace, that the reward of life should be dependent on the perseverance of man, as dependent on the strength and steadfastness of his own will. It is a covenant of works, and not a covenant of grace that suspends eternal life on what is the fruit of a man's own strength.
>
> — Jonathan Edwards *(Note: Edwards here cites Hebrews 7:25 — "able to save to the uttermost" — within his theological commentary on the covenant. The specific Edwards work has not been confirmed; verify before print use.)*

We know Ephesians says, "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast" (Ephesians 2:8–9 ESV). I think we've pretty thoroughly understood that we cannot be depended upon to save ourselves.

---

Alright, I need some volunteers. We read the whole thing last week — we're going to hit the highlights this week. Someone read John 6:37, someone else verse 39, a third person can read verse 44, a fourth can read verses 63 and 65. We have another passage for later. But for now let's start with verse 37.

> "All that the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never cast out."
> — John 6:37 (ESV)

We talked about that last week — that's election. Who can tell me who or what that is?

That's right. Because we can't and won't choose God for ourselves — because instead of God we chose sin, and continued to choose sin for centuries upon centuries — God had to make a way. He made that way through Jesus. But we still wouldn't choose God. So God, through the Holy Spirit, begins working in us before salvation to draw us toward his Son. The elect are those people in whom God, through the Holy Spirit, begins a work. In our passage today, the elect are those who are given to Jesus. All who are given to Jesus will never be cast out. You can never do anything so bad that Christ can't forgive you. All who are given to Christ will remain Christ's.

Ok, someone read verse 39.

> "And this is the will of him who sent me, that I should lose nothing of all that he has given me, but raise it up on the last day."
> — John 6:39 (ESV)

Not only will you not be cast out, but Christ is actively working to keep you.

It strikes me that people are so much like sheep. We had this one sheep — I forget her name. She was half Katahdin and half babydoll sheep. Babydoll sheep are cute. They have extra wool that grows on their face. They sort of look like they have unusually large eyes as well. Anyway, we were given this sheep. We go out there one day and we see her limping. It's not a broken leg, and she is still eating. Anyway, we start looking into what to do. It was this long involved process — daily shots of multiple kinds, we had to force a vitamin drink down her throat, we had to make sure she didn't lay down more than so many hours a day. It was stressful, time-consuming, and neither the sheep nor mom and I liked having to do this — and it went on for weeks and weeks. We didn't want to lose this sheep we loved. After three or four months, we went out in the morning and found her dead. All the medical things were done just right. But yet she still died.

And what did Jesus say? *"And this is the will of him who sent me, that I should lose nothing of all that he has given me."* Where we as humans fail, God succeeds. Before, Jesus said he won't cast any out. And now he says he won't lose anything of all that God has given him.

Not only can you not lose your salvation — you can't get rid of it either. I'm reminded of that song: *"He's still workin' on me, to make me what I need to be. It took him just a week to make the moon and stars, the sun and earth and Jupiter and Mars. How loving and patient he must be, because he's still workin' on me."* *(Written by Joel Hemphill, 1980.)*

Jesus is still working on each and every one of us, drawing us ever closer to him — so that one day when we die, we can spend eternity with him. This also brings him glory, that he would reach down and grab dirty old sinners like you and me, and rescue us from the noose of sin we've all gladly fastened around our hearts.

---

We read verse 44 last week, but it's well worth reading again. Someone read it for me.

> "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him. And I will raise him up on the last day."
> — John 6:44 (ESV)

Because we are so lost in our trespasses and sins apart from God — because our vision is so clouded, because we've so radically corrupted every fiber of our being with sin — we no longer know which way is up. So God has to lovingly guide us back to the fold. Jesus said, "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him."

Alright, verses 63 and 65:

> "It is the Spirit who gives life; the flesh is no help at all. The words that I have spoken to you are spirit and life."
> — John 6:63 (ESV)

> "And he said, 'This is why I told you that no one can come to me unless it is granted him by the Father.'"
> — John 6:65 (ESV)

The flesh is no help. Only when God has begun to do a work in us can we possibly have any interest in him.

---

The good news is that makes it easy for someone to know if they are truly saved or not. Search your heart. Really dig deep. Evaluate your motives. Why do you come to church? Do you come simply because it makes you feel good? Is it for the friends who have become or are becoming like family? And what about reading your Bible? Do you read your Bible because it's expected of you, or because you want to better know the Savior that died for you?

Long story short — is your interest really in him? Or is your interest in a peripheral benefit? If your primary interest is in something other than Christ for Christ's sake, then probably you aren't yet saved.

---

One more passage before we close. I want you to make a note of where to find this one: John 10:25–30.

While you're turning there, Jesus had just finished a big talk about his role as the good shepherd. He talks about how if a wolf comes, he fights the wolf — but if you were being helped by a hired hand, the hired hand would run. Jesus loves us, because we are his own. He is willing to lay down his life for us so that his mission from God can be fulfilled. He rightly says that he is the good shepherd.

He says something remarkable that I want to touch on briefly. He says he knows us as he knows his Father. I think those of us who have been Christians for a long time will kind of gloss over that and take it for granted — but we shouldn't. God the Father and Jesus are one. They are two persons of the same God. It's like the left side of your body versus the right side. Does your right brain not know what your left half is doing? Of course it does. Everything known to one is known to the other. All who are given to God are as known to Jesus — and to God — as intimately as you know your own body.

That's a pretty intimate level of knowing. That means you have never had a thought, made an action, or kept a secret that Jesus didn't also know.

I heard a preacher say once: you need to give Jesus all your deep dark closets and darkest secrets. Preacher — he was there when you did those things. He knows you. There isn't a thing he didn't know about you. He knew and knows you better than you could ever know yourself. The most remarkable part is that despite intimately knowing the fullest extent of our radical corruption — despite having measured the deepest, most disgusting part of our sinful souls — he chose to love us anyway. He chose to die for us anyway. He chose to maintain our salvation going forward.

Ok, are we all in John 10:25?

> "Jesus answered them, 'I told you, and you do not believe. The works that I do in my Father's name bear witness about me, but you do not believe because you are not among my sheep. My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand. My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all, and no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand. I and the Father are one.'"
> — John 10:25–30 (ESV)

Simple, really. If you don't believe, you haven't yet had God's renewing work begin in you, and so you are lost. If you don't believe, you aren't one of Jesus' sheep.

So in our other passages, we learned that God wouldn't reject us. That Christ was actively working to keep us. And here we learn that Christ will *not* lose you. He won't reject you, you can't reject him, and no one can snatch you away.

Once he has put his call on your heart, you can't be lost. When we hear the phrase "perseverance of the saints," it's not us that perseveres — it's God's work through Christ that perseveres. A benefit of that for us is eternal life with Christ.

Isn't that comforting and reassuring? That for the true believer, you can't be so bad, you can't run so far or so hard that you can get away from God — because God through Christ is actively working to pursue you. He's already decided he won't reject you, and he knows that when you see him, really see him for the first time, you won't reject him either.

---

As we close, we would normally close with a song. But I want you to spend a moment searching your soul.

Why do you come to church? Why do you call yourself a Christian? Is your interest really in him? Or is your interest in a peripheral benefit? If your primary interest is in something other than Christ for Christ's sake, then probably you aren't yet saved — you're still a person lost in your trespasses and sins. Scripture says you're a corpse. But what better gift can a corpse be given than life?

Think about the motivations of your heart. And in a moment, we're going to close in prayer.

*(Pray to end abortion.)*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Doctrinally Grounded Comfort with a Hollow Exhortation and a Missing Gospel Center

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and structurally coherent opening of John 6 and John 10 on perseverance that builds genuine theological assurance while failing to present the gospel clearly, press specific consciences, or issue an actual call to response.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS** — with qualification.

- **Scopus Test**: The sermon's thesis — that God preserves the elect through Christ, not by human effort — arises demonstrably from John 6:37–65 and John 10:25–30. Pass.
- **Authority Test**: The preacher submits to the text's declarations and does not soften them. He faithfully represents Christ's "I will never cast out," "I will lose nothing," and "no one shall snatch them." Pass.

*Qualification*: The sermon occasionally treats the texts as proof-texts for a pre-formed doctrinal conclusion (a series capstone) rather as a passage freshly opened for its own argument. The authority question is passed, but the hermeneutical posture leans more toward confirmation than discovery.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Two flags triggered. Score capped at 69.**

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation**: The sermon assumes the gospel rather than proclaims it. Ephesians 2:8–9 is cited but never unpacked. The cross is referenced obliquely ("He chose to die for us anyway") but the mechanism of atonement — substitution, propitiation, justification by faith — is entirely absent. An unbeliever listening would not know *how* to be saved, only that God preserves those he has chosen. The gospel is background music, not the melody.

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: The closing examination ("Why do you come to church? Why do you read your Bible?") is the same generic self-examination prompt that appears in nearly any evangelical sermon. No specific sinner is cornered. No particular congregation pattern is named. No believer is given a concrete word to carry home. No backslider, no doubting saint, no self-righteous churchgoer is addressed distinctly.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Partially Christ. The sermon genuinely centers on what Christ does to preserve us, not on what we do. The closing self-examination, however, redirects the hearer inward without returning them to Christ.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas. The sermon describes God's faithfulness warmly, but there is no moment where God's holiness, weight, or glory becomes present. The tone is consistently conversational and comfortable.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Informed and mildly comforted. The conscience is not pressed. The unbeliever is told to examine himself but never encounters the law's demand or the gospel's supply with any force.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Doctrinal accuracy and inter-series coherence**: The sermon correctly builds on the prior doctrines of grace and handles perseverance/preservation with genuine confessional fidelity. The Edwards quote on the covenant of works versus the covenant of grace is the theological high point — and it is correctly framed.

2. **Sheep illustration from personal experience**: The story of the limping sheep receiving weeks of diligent care before dying despite everything is native to both the preacher's life and the text's own imagery (John 10:28 — "they will never perish"). This is the sermon's most incarnate moment and its best illustration.

3. **Accessible and oral**: The manuscript reads like a man who is comfortable in front of people and is genuinely convinced of what he is saying. The interactive elements (calling on volunteers to read) and the conversational cadence serve ordinary hearers well.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is absent as proclamation**: Ephesians 2:8–9 is the closest thing to a gospel statement, but it is cited rather than preached. There is no presentation of penal substitution, no articulation of what Christ's death accomplished, no clear path for an unbeliever from conviction to faith. The sermon tells the lost person to examine his heart but never tells him what to do with what he finds there. This is the most serious deficiency.

2. **The closing examination is unanchored and repeated verbatim**: The self-examination prompt appears identically in the middle of the sermon (after John 6:65) and at the close — "Why do you come to church? Why do you read your Bible? Is your interest really in him?" Repetition requires escalation; it receives none. More critically, the examination ends with a diagnosis ("probably you aren't yet saved") but no remedy. The preacher diagnoses the corpse and then suggests it think about its condition without offering the resurrection.

3. **The exhortation is absent**: The sermon ends with an invitation to introspection, not a call to anything. No believer is told what to do with the assurance just given. No unbeliever is told how to respond to the gospel. The congregation is informed that God preserves the elect, but they are sent home without a "therefore." The sermon never reaches the imperative edge.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that assurance preaching without gospel grounding can produce a false security as easily as true comfort. The sermon's implicit logic — "examine whether God is drawing you; if he is, you're safe" — leaves the anxious saint without a Christ-centered anchor and potentially confirms the presumptuous one. A sermon on preservation that does not ground assurance in the objective work of Christ at the cross (not just in the subjective experience of interest in Christ) is incomplete at the point where it matters most.

Additionally, the preacher does not appear to see the missed opportunity in John 6's original context: this passage was preached to people who had just been fed miraculously and then abandoned Jesus when the bread of life discourse got hard (v.66). The sermon's own text is about perseverance in the face of difficulty — but that pastoral edge is never developed.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The humor in the introduction (the Josh Havel "lost salvation in other pants" riff) signals pastoral warmth and relational ease — and the congregation likely laughed and relaxed. This warmth is genuinely present. But the informal register established by the humor lingers through the entire sermon and prevents the preacher from reaching the weight the text demands. The sermon *feels* like it is about a heavy doctrine, but it never actually becomes heavy. The lightness reads as pastoral accessibility but masks theological thinness.

Similarly, the string of John 6 texts — five verses read sequentially with commentary — creates the *appearance* of careful exposition. But the texts are organized to confirm a predetermined doctrinal point rather than to open the passage's own argument. It looks like exegesis; it functions more like proof-texting with warmth.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

John 6:66 — "After this many of his disciples turned back and no longer walked with him" — is the pastoral crisis that gives the entire discourse its urgency. Why does Jesus say "I will lose nothing" and "no one can come unless the Father draws him"? Because people are walking away. The doctrine of preservation is not abstract comfort; it is Christ's answer to apparent defection. A sermon that opens with that crisis ("Some of you may fear that the people walking away in your own life — or the times you've walked away — mean the doctrine isn't true. Jesus preached this very doctrine to a crowd that then abandoned him.") would have completely transformed the applicatory force of everything that followed.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Add a gospel center after John 6:39**: After "Christ is actively working to keep you," insert a paragraph on *why* and *on what basis* Christ keeps the elect. The answer is the cross. Ground assurance in the objective work of penal substitution before offering the subjective comfort of preservation. Without this, the sermon offers the benefits of the atonement without the atonement itself.

2. **Anchor the closing examination to Christ, not to self**: The current ending ("Think about the motivations of your heart") turns the hearer inward without a return vector to Christ. Following the Puritan "uses" pattern: use of conviction ("If you are examining yourself and finding nothing — hear the bad news: you are lost"), then use of consolation anchored to the text ("But Christ says he will not cast out any who come to him. Come. Come now."), then use of direction for the believer ("If God has given you to Christ, live today as one who cannot be snatched away — and stop living as if you might be").

3. **Use John 6:66 as the sermon's opening crisis**: Begin with the departure of the disciples as the pastoral problem this sermon addresses. This transforms the sermon from a doctrinal confirmation exercise into a genuine pastoral answer to genuine pastoral fear.

4. **Differentiate hearers explicitly**: After the John 10 passage, address at least three distinct types: (a) the anxious believer who fears his sin disqualifies him — comfort with "no one shall snatch them"; (b) the presumptuous churchgoer who uses eternal security as permission to drift — warn with the examination Christ applies to the Pharisees ("you do not believe because you are not among my sheep"); (c) the unbeliever who has never come to Christ at all — offer the gospel and a clear path.

5. **Let John 6's Bread of Life context do work**: One sentence connecting Jesus as the true manna — the bread that endures to eternal life, unlike the manna in the wilderness that had to be gathered fresh each day — would give the preservation doctrine its redemptive-historical grounding and make the sermon feel like it is opening John 6, not merely using it.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — the sermon does not score high enough on exposition or theology to trigger the dead orthodoxy pattern. The warmth present prevents the "correct but bloodless" diagnosis. The more accurate diagnosis is: *gospel-incomplete warmth* — the sermon is pastorally affectionate but theologically thin at the point where the gospel must land.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The sermon carries genuine personal conviction about God's faithfulness and has moments of warmth that honor the grace it describes, but it does not produce the weight that a passage declaring "I give them eternal life and they will never perish" ought to produce — primarily because the eternal life given is not grounded in the one who had to die to give it.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
The rubric fits this passage well. John 6 and John 10 are didactic discourse with strong doctrinal claims and direct applicatory force — exactly the kind of material the rubric is calibrated to evaluate. The gospel centrality and conscience categories appropriately flag the sermon's gaps. The one slight strain: the sermon is explicitly a series capstone, and evaluating it as a standalone piece means the prior three sermons' gospel content (which may have been robust) is invisible to this evaluation. The gospel may have been more fully presented in Sermon 1 or 3 of the series. That does not remove the obligation to present it here — a person could attend this sermon alone — but the evaluation should note that the series context is a partial mitigating factor.
