Genesis 1–2 — Beautiful, But Not Safe


We talked last week about how the first few passages of the Bible are some of the most twisted — and tonight we hit another big one I've been hearing a lot lately. There's a popular preacher out there saying God created the earth once in Genesis 1, then destroyed it and started over in Genesis 2. If you've been following along at home, you'll notice that nowhere in Genesis 1 or 2 does it say "and God said, oops, let's start over." In fact, the opposite is true. He looks at what he made and calls it very good.

This part of Scripture ought to be taken literally — but there's an element of poetry in it too. In American poetry we lean on meter and rhyme. Hebrew poetry works differently. One of its primary structures is chiasm. Genesis 1 is full of these small, tight Hebrew poems. When you get home, compare verses 1 and 4, then 2 and 5, then 3 and 6. The first three days divided things. The last three filled those same things. Day 1 divided light and dark — Day 4 filled those spaces with the sun, moon, and stars. Day 2 divided sky and water — Day 5 filled them with birds and fish. Day 3 divided land and sea — Day 6 filled the land with animals and man. This is not accidental. This is an orderly God telling an orderly story.

You can also see something in it that's common to virtually every oral tradition society. They use repetition as a mnemonic — a memory device — so stories can be passed down without corruption. God created things in a step-by-step, orderly way that was designed to be easily remembered and shared for oodles of generations. That's not coincidence. That's inspiration. God knew how oral tradition societies worked. He knew how we remember. He told the story, and inspired the writing of it, in a way that would resist corruption across thousands of years. He wrote us a love letter, and then defended it.


On to Genesis 2:5–15.

Up to this point everything has been created perfect. We talked last week about the anthropic principle — how God built into creation such intricate balance that life could not only exist but thrive. For thousands of years, probably five to ten thousand, creation ran exactly as designed.

Not only was everything created perfect, but that perfection reflected the image of God — especially in man, but in nature to some extent as well.

My grandmother was an excellent painter. She'll probably never be famous, but we love her work. I had put my name on the back of a painting I liked, happy to have that as my inheritance. But she had another painting — two Indian kids. Those were her favorites. She'd painted them a long time ago, and they'd been fixtures in her kitchen for as long as I could remember. I just figured they'd always been there. One day I was talking with her and she got a little sad — no one had asked about those paintings. She got a little teary-eyed and said, "I want those to go to someone special. Someone who will appreciate them the way I do. You see, I put so much of myself into a painting — especially this one." Then she gave them to me. I was pretty floored.

But that fits here so well. She poured herself into her work. Her gifts, her vision, her way of seeing the world — all of it is on that canvas. A great deal about her can be learned from her art. Where her reflection was limited by her skill with a brush and palette, and by the medium itself, God faced no such limits. His reflection in creation was perfect.

We were working on the sign for the church this week — ask dad, he has it on his phone, I'd love your input. We're using a photo taken on a day with no wind at all. Moon Lake was completely still, flat as glass, and it reflected the setting sun, the boat dock, and the trees on the far bank in a way that was absolutely beautiful. That was how God created things to be. Not a partial reflection. Not a rippled one. Still. Clear. Perfect. That was the gift he gave us — and made us stewards of.


After all of that, on the seventh day God rested. Not because he was tired. He didn't need to recharge after creating a universe. He rested because he wanted to set an example. It is easy to skip the day of rest — to add in one more appointment, knock out one more errand. But rest matters. God says he made the Sabbath for man. We need time to slow down, to reconnect with our families, to focus on him.

Some people get pretty wound up about the Sabbath — is it Saturday or Sunday? Do we need to hold an evening service? But God says the Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath. In Middle Eastern countries, churches meet on Friday nights while the mosques hold their main services. Here, most people gather on Sundays. Whatever the day, I'm glad they're meeting. The point isn't the name of the day. The point is that we take real time — to rest, to be with our families, to be with God.

I'll admit I chuckle a little when some churches tie themselves in knots over Saturday versus Sunday, because whatever day it is, it is not a restful day for your pastor, your children's workers, or anyone else with responsibilities. So yes — take time to rest. Don't neglect your soul. Spending time with God in the morning is like taking a bath. You wouldn't go to work without one. Don't neglect the Scriptures. They wash the soul.


I was reading about a wave of tropical activity heading our way when I started thinking through the next section. And it occurred to me that we can see different things about God in different parts of creation.

Take the mountains. There's majesty in a mountain. But mountains move, and when they do, they aren't safe. The Grand Canyon — I have nothing to support this, but I believe it was probably formed during the flood. It is breathtaking in scope. I've never been, but I'd love to go. The design, the intricacy, the sheer scale of it — God made that for us to see, to appreciate, to know him better by. And yet people get lost in it. People get sick trying to explore it. Wildlife eats them. It is not exactly safe.

The Gulf, right here. You all know it. Beautiful, vast, sometimes perfectly still. At other times it swallows massive ships. When a tropical storm rolls through — you know exactly what I mean — trees down, power lines down, flooding. You remember how it flooded back behind Jasmine, almost reached the parsonage those times? Gorgeous and dangerous at the same time. Anywhere you live, you have that tension. Michigan, Ohio — heavy snows that trap you inside for days. Out west, Death Valley, a place not named for its comfort. Mudslides. Wildfires burning entire towns. Earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis.

That brought to mind something C.S. Lewis said in The Chronicles of Narnia. There's a lion in those books that represents Christ — Aslan. He dies for the sins of others and comes back to life. Early in the story, one of the girls asks whether he's safe. The answer she gets is: "Safe? No. But he's good."

Lewis got that right. God is good. But God is not always safe.

I can already see some of you raising a hand in your mind — what about "my God shall supply all my needs"? What about the birds of the air, whom God feeds, and how much more will he care for you? All of that is true. Every word of it.

But let me make two more points and then we're done.

Creation reflects God. God is good, but not necessarily safe. And now — what that looks like in a life.

I've told this story before, so forgive me. I had an aunt — Aunt Judy. She had schizophrenia. She wanted with everything in her to serve the Lord, and she committed her path to him. That path led her through some very dark places. She was raped. Her husband left her. Her children were taken away. Her mother — my grandmother — had her institutionalized. Which, at that time in our history, meant jail. To get her the mental health help she needed, she had to go through the jail system first, and my grandmother had to put her there. Could you imagine having to make that call?

It eventually led to a diagnosis and medication that helped Judy function. But not so well that she could live entirely on her own. She lived in a halfway house for people with mental illness. Her movements were monitored. She lost much of her freedom and a fair amount of her dignity.

But she had told God: wherever you want me, Lord, I'll go.

Turned out he needed her to be a missionary to a mental health facility.

When dad was cleaning out her apartment after she passed, he found bags upon bags of letters. Because of her illness, she'd never thrown anything away. In those bags — letter after letter from people thanking her for sharing the gospel with them. For discipling them. For meeting needs. For being Christ to people who might never have heard the gospel any other way.

Her story had some wild turns. When she stowed away on a ship bound for Europe, she wasn't exactly playing it safe. When she tried to walk across Turkey penniless and sick, that wasn't safe either. But as strange as her path was, I feel certain she was in the Lord's will. When the best doctors in America failed her, God put her on a ship to Europe, where she ran into a man who knew a researcher in New York on the cutting edge of schizophrenia treatment. That doctor helped heal her mind.

From knowing her, I know beyond a doubt that she knew God was using her right where she was. I brought up a few times the idea of her moving, or living on her own — she was close enough to normal that it seemed possible. But each time she said no. God needs me here a while yet. She was his missionary, and on some level she knew it.

Following Christ is sometimes hard. Sometimes God allows us to go through things to shape and mold us, to equip us for purposes we don't fully understand. The hows and whys may not make sense to us. They make sense to him. And while following him is not always safe in this life, it guarantees our eternity. In the scope of eternity, a hundred years is a breath — there for a moment, and then gone. But eternity is long.

And that's one more way God isn't "safe." Maybe someone within the sound of my voice has never reached the point of asking Jesus to pay the price for their sins. If that's you, when the praise team comes up in a minute, come talk to me.

Because even the best things we do apart from Christ, Scripture says, are like filthy rags. Romans 3:23 — all have sinned and fallen short of the glory of God. Not most. All. That sin separates us from God. It makes us subject to his wrath. And our God is a big God. Scientists have found pulsars — a type of star — that take 89 million light-years just to complete one orbit. They estimate there may be a thousand such stars in the part of space we've been able to study. Scripture says the heavens are his footstool. 89 million light-years is staggering on its own. A thousand of those? We serve a big God. It is a fearful thing to fall into the hands of an angry God.

But God loves you too much to leave you there. Romans 5:8-9 — while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. We deserved death. He chose to take our punishment. And unlike every other religion, our Savior defeated sin, death, hell, and Satan — and is fully alive, seated at the right hand of the Father. Buddha is dead. Muhammad is dead. The founder of Scientology is dead and gone. Jesus is the only one death couldn't hold.

So if you've never asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins, come talk to me.

Maybe you want to rededicate — we had a rededication this week. If that's you, come talk to me. Maybe you're ready to join the church. If so, come talk to me.

If you have anything you'd like prayer for, or just want to come forward and talk to God, the altar is open.
