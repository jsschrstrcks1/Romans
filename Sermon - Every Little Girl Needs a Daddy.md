### People Group of the Week: Abai Sungai of Malaysia

The Abai Sungai are a small fishing tribe of about 1,500 people in Malaysia. They have a unique language, and to the best of anyone's knowledge there has never been a missionary to this people group. They are adherents of a folk type of Islam — they take traditional Muslim beliefs and superimpose other views on top, so they believe in the Indonesian evil eye and so forth. We need to pray for a missionary to go to them, learn their language, and share the gospel with them. To translate the Word of God into their language. That God would even now be filling them with dissatisfaction with Islam, and in the words of the hymn, tuning their hearts to sing God's grace.

---

We used to have little missions moments in the service. I want to get back to that. As believers we are part of a global community of faith. There are believers on every continent in the world, and they are serving in different contexts, and we should stay with them in prayer. Not only that, but there are people groups all over the world that have not yet heard the gospel, that do not yet have the Scriptures in their own language, that have no missionaries serving in their context. We need to keep them in prayer too.

Let's read Galatians 3:27-4:7.

> For as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ. There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus. And if you are Christ's, then you are Abraham's offspring, heirs according to promise.
>
> I mean that the heir, as long as he is a child, is no different from a slave, though he is the owner of everything, but he is under guardians and managers until the date set by his father. In the same way we also, when we were children, were enslaved to the elementary principles of the world. But when the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son, born of woman, born under the law, to redeem those who were under the law, so that we might receive adoption as sons. And because you are sons, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, crying, "Abba! Father!" So you are no longer a slave, but a son, and if a son, then an heir through God.
> — Galatians 3:27-4:7 (ESV)

### Lily and Mercedes

I heard a story this week by a preacher at Southeastern. He and his wife had been trying to have children for years and were not able to. They managed to get pregnant once, but their little boy Dax lived only a few days outside the womb before he passed away. The doctors told them that the pregnancy and delivery was so traumatic to his wife that she would never be able to have children. He said neither he nor his wife took the news well. They both became angry and bitter. They both had about a five-year battle with depression and anger with God over why they couldn't have children of their own.

Then one day, about the same time, God had put in both their hearts that they should adopt. So they prayed about it for an additional year and signed up with an agency hoping to adopt a little girl. The agency told them it could be a while — a year or maybe more. The agency said in no circumstances will you be able to adopt a child faster than nine months. It just doesn't happen.

So imagine their delight when three weeks later they got a call saying they had the perfect little toddler for them — Lily. Three more weeks, and they were a family.

Now as it happens, Lily has two older siblings. One is in a mental health facility, and the other — a teenager — is in an orphanage. They wanted to keep the girls together, but the teenager, thirteen-year-old Mercedes, had just been through too much, and the agency said she's not healed up enough from her experiences to be placed with a family yet. So they stay in contact, and once a month, if Mercedes has been good, they can take her out for a date — an afternoon at the mall, or to see a movie and dinner — giving her a glimpse at what life with a family can be.

Lily and Mercedes' story is heartbreaking. Their mother was a drug addict. She did whatever she had to in order to get her next dose of drugs. Hunger was common. Seeing their mother doing drugs and other things to get drugs was their daily way of life. Seeing theft and prostitution. Their mother doing anything and everything — not for them, not to provide for their needs — but to feed that addiction she had to drugs. One day, as is often the case, the authorities found out, and the children ended up with Baptist Children's Homes.

Lily, at three, would ask Paul and his wife every night: "Will we have food tomorrow?"

Can you imagine the circumstances that would lead a little three-year-old to ask those kinds of questions?

One day at dinner, Mercedes was talking with the preacher — whose name is Paul — and his wife, alone for a moment, and she said, "You know, I'm really happy for Lily." Paul said, "Well, why is that?" Mercedes replied:

"I'm so glad Lily has a daddy. I never knew my daddy, and I think every little girl needs one."

### Adoption in Scripture

I am quick to tell people they cannot morally kill their baby, but I need to be just as quick to advocate for adoption. Want to hear a statistic that broke my heart? If every Christian family in America — even if just every Southern Baptist family — adopted one child today, there would no longer be any orphans tomorrow.

Adoption is a very biblical concept. The Old Testament is littered with references and commands regarding orphans and the fatherless, but also with Israel's status as his children, his people, his nation — his adopted family. Beyond that, it is spelled out in a number of stories regarding individuals.

Moses was adopted by Pharaoh's daughter — like we are adopted by the King of Kings and Lord of Lords. Esther was raised by her cousin Mordecai. In the New Testament, Paul talks a lot about this concept. You've heard me talk about Ephesians 2 a fair bit — that we are by nature children of wrath, sons of disobedience. But this concept is also all throughout Romans and Galatians and Corinthians. John talks about it too — right at the beginning of his book:

> But to all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God, who were born, not of blood nor of the will of the flesh nor of the will of man, but of God.
> — John 1:12-13 (ESV)

Or Paul in Galatians — what he says is central to the gospel:

> But when the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son, born of woman, born under the law, to redeem those who were under the law, so that we might receive adoption as sons.
> — Galatians 4:4-5 (ESV)

Our redemption through Christ isn't just penal substitutionary atonement — that means he didn't just get us out of trouble because of our rebellion against the law. He didn't just take our place and pay our punishment with his blood. He also adopted us as his sons and daughters.

For God, adopting us was costly.

> For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God.
> — 2 Corinthians 5:21 (ESV)

Or John 3:16 — who knows that one by heart? It cost Christ his life, a thing he willingly laid down for you and me so that we could have a share in his righteousness, be shielded from God's wrath, and adopted as sons.

### Sam and Abby Come Home

While we are talking about adoption, I want to give you an update on my friends who have been trying to adopt Sam and Abby for a few years now. You'll remember last year they had a setback and ended up leaving Taiwan without the kids. You'll be happy to know they got the call to come get the kids and are now spending their second night back in the States as a family.

I saw a picture that made me tear up a bit. Their new daughter Abby was sitting next to her new dad Joshua on the plane, her little hand wrapped tightly around her dad's finger. I can't imagine what that would be like for a little girl — leaving the country she knows, the language she knows, the food she knows, the people she knows — for people she doesn't, for a language she doesn't, for a culture she doesn't, somewhere far enough away that she won't be seeing her friends anymore. The only one she has for comfort is her new dad, and she was clinging tightly to him.

What a picture of what we have in Jesus.

They didn't have to adopt these kids. They didn't have to travel halfway around the world to bring them home. They didn't have to open their home and their hearts to these two precious children. But they chose to. And now these two who had no home now have one. These kids who had no family now have one. These kids who lived in a third-world orphanage now have beds of their own, toys of their own. The guarantee of food every night.

Adoption isn't cheap. It cost Josh and Sara a lot. Ever looked at airfare for four from another country? I looked it up — just in short-notice airfare they are looking at around ten thousand dollars. Not counting hotel stays for a week, food there in a foreign country for a week, whatever they needed to eat or drink on the plane. And let's not forget this is the second time they have made this trip. Last time they stuck it out for a month living off credit cards, trying to get their kids home.

But whatever it cost them financially, it's nothing compared to what it cost Jesus to bring us home.

### The Western and the Tainted Heart

If Jesus is willing to give up everything — even his life — for you, so that you can share in the wonderful joy of relationship with God, so that we can share in being heirs to Christ — Christ paid the ultimate price so that we can have everything God, the Creator of the universe, has to offer. How can we do anything else but respond in obedience?

Some earthly things are empty pleasures. God telling us no is for our own good, whether we can see or understand it from where we are.

I read a western once. Any of you like westerns? I think it was a Louis L'Amour western. I forget all the details of the story. But one scene stood out in my mind. The would-be hero was searching for his kidnapped fiancée. He was creeping through the woods towards this campsite. He could hear her, and the kidnapper. The kidnapper is explaining his convoluted plot, how his next step is to kill her and destroy the hero with grief. The hero's trying to find his way to a spot where he can shoot the black-hat-wearing bad guy, and finally finds a spot where he can get a clear shot. He can only just see the hat through the brush, but he knows that's where the kidnapper's voice was coming from, and you know, the back of the head is just under the edge of the hat, so he's pretty sure he can shoot the bad guy and save his fiancée.

So he takes careful aim, and fires.

In that same moment three shots ring out. What he didn't know was that his best friend had gotten there just a few minutes ahead of him. He was hiding in the woods just outside the camp too. Who the hero actually shot was his best friend — at almost the exact same moment that his best friend shot the bad guy. Unfortunately the third shot was the bad guy shooting his fiancée.

He thought he was saving her by shooting the bad guy, but because of where he was, and his perspective on the situation, he didn't have the whole scene in view, and the whole thing ended in tragedy. His enemy was dead — but at the cost of his best friend and future wife.

We are like the hero in that story. Our vision is tainted by sin just like the hero's vision was tainted by the woods and by it being night. We can't see the whole picture — just a little corner, just like the hero could only see part of the hat. So sometimes when we take aim and shoot at things that we think will bring us happiness, all we end up doing is compounding our own grief. Like drugs. They may make someone feel a certain way for a moment, but what are the long-term effects? Or alcoholism? Or pornography? Other sexual sins like adultery, fornication, homosexuality — they're going to do the same thing.

Our heart is wicked and evil and can't be trusted. Scripture says that, and if we look around we can see it. What we lust after in our flesh because of sin and our enemy is our own ruin. So can God ask us to do things our heart doesn't want? Yes! Because that's the loving thing to do! If our hearts want ruin, and ruin is the opposite of what God wants, should we trust our own hearts or God?

### God's Son Was the Price

So we're like that little girl Lily. We are hungry and desperate and functionally alone. Worse than that, we have trusted our hearts and sinned against a perfect and holy God that gave us rules to keep us from ruin. In our sinning, we run headfirst at — and lovingly embrace — our own death.

I think a couple of things happen at that moment. For people like that little girl Lily — God weeps. For Abby and Sam — God weeps. God weeps for Lily and Mercedes. We are commanded to weep with those who weep. We know the book of Jeremiah — God wept repeatedly for his wayward children. He wept for the land they ruined through disobedience, and he wept for the mountains, and he wept for the desert places. He wept for his people. And I think he wept for his Son.

God's Son was the price that was paid for your adoption.

Say that with me: **"God's Son was the price paid for my adoption."**

So God wept. I don't think it would be crossing the line to say God weeps for the lost as well. Not only those who are predestined to life but haven't found Jesus yet, but for those who will reject God into an eternity in hell. Not in a hand-wringing, powerless God watching on the sidelines kind of way — because that's not God. But in a way that grieves the eternal torment those who reject his Son will justly endure.

For those whose names were written in the Lamb's Book of Life before the foundation of the world, God in his timing begins a work in our hearts. He begins drawing us to his Son. He begins working his cleansing power in our hearts and minds. Our heart is a piano out of tune. Instead of playing beautiful music for the glory of God, apart from God it plays the siren song of sin. God begins tuning our heart to sing his praise. To sing of his redeeming power in our lives.

This work results in our salvation — so that one day we can be with God in heaven. But all of that begins with salvation. Which starts with the gospel.

The "fullness of time" framing (4:4) is noted as the hinge of redemptive history. Galatians 4:4-5 is correctly identified as "central to the gospel" and explained in terms of penal substitution plus adoption. John 1:12-13 and 2 Corinthians 5:21 are used organically as cross-references.
- *Contextual Awareness* (4/5): The placement of the adoption metaphor in Paul's argument — following the Abrahamic covenant discussion in Galatians 3 — is implicitly present in the "heirs according to promise" reference. The OT adoption examples (Moses, Esther) are used illustratively and appropriately. The Western analogy (Josh and Sara in Taiwan) and the Lily/Mercedes story ground the abstract in the concrete.
- *Exegetical Clarity* (3/5): The penal substitution explanation ("he didn't just get us out of trouble because of our rebellion against the law — he didn't just take our place and pay our punishment with his blood — he also adopted us as his sons and daughters") is clear and precise. The Greek/Hebrew work is absent, but the concepts are well-explained.
- *Logical Faithfulness* (3/5): No eisegesis. The sermon's moves are all text-grounded. The Louis L'Amour western illustration is a slight departure from the text's logic — it makes a sanctification/guidance point that is not precisely where the Galatians passage is going — but it does not distort the text.

**FCF** (Fallen Condition Focus): The sermon correctly identifies the FCF as spiritual orphanhood — the condition of being enslaved, "children of wrath," outside the household of God. This is the specific condition Galatians 4 addresses, and it is named concretely through the Lily and Mercedes story.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 14/18**

- *Doctrinal Precision* (4/4): The distinction between penal substitution and adoption is correctly made and clearly stated: Christ's work is not only judicial (taking our punishment) but also relational (bringing us into the family). This is a theologically precise and important point.
- *Confessional Alignment* (2/4): No doctrinal errors. The adoption theology is confessionally sound. The deficit is the incomplete gospel — a sermon that reaches Galatians 4:4-5 and stops before applying it has left its soteriological argument unfinished.

**Law/Gospel Dialectic**: The law (our slavery, our orphanhood, our heart-trust) is present in the western story and in the Lily/Mercedes narrative. The gospel (God's costly adoption through Christ) is stated but not proclaimed. Only half the dialectic is delivered.

1. **The Mercedes illustration is exceptional**: "I'm so glad Lily has a daddy. I never knew my daddy, and I think every little girl needs one." Applied to spiritual orphanhood, this is the kind of image that opens a hearer's chest before the gospel fills it. ### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The sermon is incomplete** (closing section): The manuscript does not contain a gospel. "Which starts with the gospel" is not a gospel. This is the most urgent revision needed — not because the rest is weak, but because the rest has earned a gospel proclamation that never comes.

2. **The exhortation is entirely absent**: Even if the gospel were written, the sermon lacks a clear summons. What must the hearer do? "Come talk to me" (not present here, but implied) or a generic call to respond is insufficient. The adoption passage gives the preacher everything he needs: "And because you are sons, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, crying, 'Abba! Father!'" — this is an exhortation embedded in the text: cry out to God as Father. Use it.

3. **The western illustration is the weakest illustration in the sermon**: It is specific and memorable on its own terms, but it makes a guidance/limited-vision point ("we can't see the whole picture") that does not connect organically to adoption. The point it makes — "trust God's guidance over your sinful heart" — belongs to a "Don't Trust Your Heart" sermon rather than to this one. The sermon's theme is not "trust God's guidance" but "receive God's adoption." A tighter illustration would have made the same point (our spiritual blindness) without changing the subject.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see that the sermon's most structurally significant failure is the one he has explicitly flagged in the verification log. The awareness is there: "⚠️ Sermon ends at the gospel bridge." But the flag suggests the gospel is a section to be added, whereas it is actually the sermon's reason for existing. The adoption theme in Galatians 4 is not the main point alongside the gospel — adoption *is* the gospel. The distinction between penal substitution and adoption is not "substitution is the main thing plus bonus adoption" — adoption is the relational content of what substitution secured. The gospel proclamation, when it is written, should reflect this: being adopted into God's family *is* what being saved means.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sermon *feels* complete because the illustrations are so strong and the build is so well-paced. A congregation moved by the Lily/Mercedes story and the Sam/Abby photograph may feel they have received a full and powerful sermon when the climactic proclamatory moment has not yet occurred. The emotional resonance of the material can substitute for gospel proclamation in the hearer's perception — which is precisely what makes this a dangerous place to stop.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Galatians 4:6 — "And because you are sons, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, crying, 'Abba! Father!'" — is quoted in the passage reading but never preached. This verse is the sermon's natural climax: the orphan who has been adopted now has a cry that was not possible before — "Father." The Spirit produces the cry. The cry is the evidence and experience of adoption. A sermon on every little girl needing a daddy, about spiritual orphanhood, that ends without the congregation crying "Abba, Father" in some sense — has stopped one verse short of its own conclusion.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Write the gospel and complete the sermon**: The gospel proclamation should include: (a) our condition as spiritual orphans, enemies of God by nature (Ephesians 2:3, already referenced); (b) Christ's incarnation and law-keeping on our behalf (Galatians 4:4 — "born under the law, to redeem those under the law"); (c) his substitutionary death (2 Corinthians 5:21, already quoted); (d) his resurrection; (e) adoption as the content of salvation — not just forgiveness but family; (f) a clear call: repent of living as an orphan, trust Christ, and be adopted into God's family.

2. **Replace the western illustration with a shorter illustration native to the adoption theme**: The western story is effective, but it belongs to a different sermon. Consider instead a brief illustration of a child who has been adopted but still flinches at loud voices, still hoards food, still waits for the rejection to come — and the parent's patient, daily work of showing the child that the adoption is real. This is the spiritual condition of believers who have been adopted but live as orphans.

3. **Preach Galatians 4:6 as the sermon's climax**: "Because you are sons, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, crying, 'Abba! Father!'" — This is the experiential content of adoption. The cry of "Abba" is the Spirit-given evidence that you have been brought into the family. Close with an invitation: "If you have never cried out to God as Father — today is the day. Not as an employee to a boss, not as a defendant to a judge, but as a child to a Father. This is what Christ purchased for you."

4. **Add a concrete advocacy application**: The sermon mentions adoption statistics ("if every Southern Baptist family adopted one child...") but does not summon the congregation to consider it. If the sermon is going to make this claim, it should issue a gentle but specific call: "Is there a family in this room that should be praying about adoption? This is not for everyone — but it is for someone."

5. **Add a brief address to the believer who still lives as an orphan**: The adopted son who still lives like a slave (Galatians 4:1-3) is a Perkins category the sermon leaves unaddressed. "You were adopted, but you live as though the adoption might be reversed. You still perform. You still fear rejection. You still hoard. The Spirit in you cries 'Abba' — hear him."

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered** — the sermon is warm, pastorally alive, and has genuine emotional and doctrinal engagement. The gap is incompleteness, not coldness.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

This sermon has more genuine weight-of-glory potential than almost any other in this batch — the Lily/Mercedes image, the Sam/Abby airplane photograph, and "God's Son was the price paid for my adoption" are sentences that carry eternity in them — but the weight is not yet delivered because the sermon ends before the proclamation that would make it land; the gravitas is present, the detonation is not.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

The rubric fits this sermon well. Galatians is a Pauline epistle with a clear didactic argument, well-suited to the rubric's expository framework. The rubric correctly identifies the gospel-as-incomplete as the central failure. One mild strain: the rubric's emphasis on "conscience and applicatory force" rates this sermon moderately, but the emotional and applicatory work done by the Mercedes illustration may exceed what the rubric's categories can capture — some of the sermon's most searching pastoral work is done by narrative rather than by explicit application, which the rubric measures less effectively than it measures direct didactic application.
