# Old vs New — Hebrews 1:1-14
### Raw transcript (~2014–2016, Obama era)

---

Old word from God, new Word from God.

The Old Testament showed God's superiority over idols — Elijah and the prophets of Baal at Mt. Carmel. Showed God's supremacy over our own stubborn will, over the weather, over animals — Jonah. Showed God's sovereignty over enemy nations — Gideon, Samson. God is sovereign over every giant in our life — David and Goliath.

The Old Testament shows God's sovereignty over so many aspects of life.

The New Testament is like the crescendo of the orchestra and proves through Christ God is sovereign over death! Christ is sovereign over sin. Addiction. Sexual immorality. Idolatry of every kind. Over the weather. Over demons. Everything God showed his sovereignty over in the Old Testament, Christ shows his sovereignty over in the New!

Christ's victory is complete and total. His sovereignty is total.

If God is sovereign over all these things, he can handle your troubles too. If he can overcome an army of thousands with an army of hundreds, he can help you meet your bills. He can help you deal with unruly neighbors, and family stresses. He can give you the courage to share the gospel with your kids. He can break the cycles of addiction. He heals the lame, and sets captives free! The sovereignty of God is complete and total over everything through Christ Jesus.

---

Jesus demonstrated God's righteousness. Vs 8–9.

Jesus was still new to the ministry, and so the Devil himself tried to push Christ to sin three times.

First was the bread. Jesus was hungry. I've never fasted for forty days, but at that point I imagine you would be near death. The body needs food, and likely no more than on that 40th day. 40 is significant: Moses fasted in the desert for 40 days, Elijah fasted in the desert for 40 days and was tested by the brook, the Israelites wandered through the desert for 40 days.

Jesus could have turned a stone to bread. It was within the scope of his power. Would have demonstrated a lack of obedience, and lack of faith in God and God's sovereignty to take care of Christ's physical needs.

God always comes through with our needs, even if not in the time frame we would have liked.

Christ succeeded where the Israelites failed. The desert was a time of testing for them, to see if they would follow God. The Israelites failed, Adam failed, Moses failed, Abraham failed, and I would have failed. Only Jesus had a chance at succeeding and praise God he did!

The second temptation according to Matthew — and I won't get into it, but in the Greek, in Matthew the way he forms the sentence seems to indicate that he was repeating the temptations in order, unlike Luke, who in this case ordered them according to his own interests. Luke had a strong interest in the temple. Anyway, the second temptation: Satan tries to get Jesus to toss himself off the top of the temple saying that surely God wouldn't allow him to die.

This is in part a mocking of Christ. Just a moment ago, Christ in essence told Satan that God could be trusted to provide for him physically, that God wasn't going to let him die. Satan more or less comes back saying "maybe so, why don't you prove that. If God will protect you, then he will surely save you if you jump off the temple."

Interesting to note that the Devil changed Jesus' physical location. Whether he picked Christ up and flew him, or did something more instant like the teleporter on Star Trek, the Devil "took" Christ from one place to another. I'm happy this doesn't regularly happen to servants of God!

Jesus in his response alludes to what happened at Massah in Exodus 17:1-7. The Israelites were thirsty. That was to be expected after such a long walk in the desert. Instead of depending on God to meet their needs, they grumbled against Moses. Instead of depending on God to meet their needs they wished for the slavery of their old life in Egypt.

How often do we do this? Scripture says when we do this we are like a dog returning to its vomit.

We have victory over sin, freedom from its bondage and yet so many times we return to it over and over and over.

As the old saying goes, sin will keep you from the word, or the word will keep you from sin. *(See flags — commonly attributed to John Bunyan, but no verified primary source in his published works. Also attributed to D.L. Moody and Leonard Ravenhill.)*

Rejoice in the Lord and again I say rejoice. Whatever is true, whatever is noble, whatever is right, whatever is pure, if it is lovely, admirable, excellent or praiseworthy think on these things.

Keep your focus off yourself, keep your focus on God, and everything else fades away into the background.

When you feel temptation: Flee if possible, like Joseph. Focus on God always — Jesus memorized scripture for just that reason. Rejoice in the Lord. Think about the attributes of God. Spend some time in prayer. Get your mind off yourself!

Finally Satan tried to tempt Christ by offering him the kingdom, without paying the price for it on the cross. It's like every cartoon bad guy: just when he thinks he has the hero in his trap, he offers the hero a place at his side; or death.

Christ chose death, and in his death he (Col 2:14) nailed the record of our wrongdoing to the cross. Heb 1:3 says he made purification for us on the cross.

Perhaps the most difficult point prior to the crucifixion and God turning his back on his son, was the night before. His closest friends let him down. One of his friends betrayed him. Cried tears of blood knowing what would come, and still he was obedient unto death.

Christ took on the sins of the world, took their punishment, defeated death and Satan and set us free!

David Platt says Jesus took the cup of God's wrath, drank down every drop, flipped it upside down at the cross and said it is finished.

Vs 12 — if the 80 or 90 years that a person can reasonably expect to live is to God as the changing of clothes might be to us, then who we elect to be our president must be like a breath of air. Air is necessary, air is important. From the perspective of eternity, who leads America is a small thing. Politics are a small thing. To the sovereign triune God nuclear weapons are a small thing. Barack Obama (or any other president) might think he's hot stuff, but where was he when God hung the stars in the sky? God who is sovereign over everything, can't be usurped by any man. Our God is sovereign even over our leaders.

So as the election cycle warms up — yes, vote. No, don't let fear, and fear mongering by the media make you anxious.

Philippians 4:6: "Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."

If this one wins, or that one wins — so what? Whoever will be our leader will be our leader not by our vote, but by God's sovereignty. Yes, vote. But don't be surprised when God allows a leader to be raised up for our punishment. Maybe a true believer will be elected, or maybe we'll get another punishment. But in the end, be anxious for nothing. Let God's peace guard your heart in Christ Jesus.

---

Maybe you don't yet know Christ. If that's the case, come talk to me. Maybe you have been looking for a church, if that's the case come talk to me. Maybe you've been too focused on yourself and not focused enough on Christ. If that's the case, and you can come up, then come on up and talk to God. Make things right! Set your eyes upon Christ, the author and perfector of our faith! As the worship team comes up and sings a song, be thinking about these things. There is no judgment to be found here, just Jesus.

4. **Matthew 4 / Luke 4 — temptation order confirmed.** Matthew uses temporal adverbs (*tote*, *palin*) indicating chronological sequence; Luke uses generic conjunctions (*kai*, *de*). Scholarly consensus: Matthew's order is chronological, Luke's is thematic (climaxing at the temple, fitting Luke's Jerusalem emphasis).

7. **Elijah fasted/traveled 40 days — confirmed.** 1 Kings 19:1-8. Sustained by angelic food for a 40-day journey to Horeb.
8. **1 Kings 18 — Elijah and the prophets of Baal at Mt. Carmel — confirmed.** Appropriate reference for God's superiority over idols.

### Scripture — Needs Correction
9. ⚠️ **Hebrews 1:3 — "purification for us on the cross."** ESV actually reads: "After making purification **for sins**, he sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high." Two issues: (a) "for sins" not "for us"; (b) "the cross" is not in the verse — that is interpretive addition. The theology is sound, but if quoting directly, use the actual wording.
10. ⚠️ **Colossians 2:14 — "record of our wrongdoing."** ESV actually reads: "by canceling the **record of debt** that stood against us with its legal demands. This he set aside, nailing it to the cross." The sermon says "record of our wrongdoing" — close in concept but not the ESV wording. The ESV uses legal/financial imagery ("record of debt," "legal demands").
11. ⚠️ **"80 or 90 years" — wrong numbers.** The lifespan reference is drawn from Psalm 90:10, which reads: "The years of our life are **seventy**, or even by reason of strength **eighty**." The sermon inflates this to "80 or 90." Correct to 70 or 80 if reusing.
12. ⚠️ **Israelites wandered "40 days" — should be 40 YEARS.** Numbers 14:33-34: "Your children shall be shepherds in the wilderness **forty years**." The 40-year sentence was based on the 40 *days* the spies explored the land (one year per day), but the actual wandering was 40 years. Significant factual error.

### Factual Claims — Needs Correction
13. ⚠️ **Elijah "tested by the brook" during 40-day fast — conflation of two events.** The brook Cherith episode (1 Kings 17:1-7, fed by ravens during drought) is a completely separate and earlier event from the 40-day journey to Horeb (1 Kings 19:1-8, sustained by angelic food after fleeing Jezebel). These are years apart. Do not combine them.
14. ⚠️ **Massah — "wished for the slavery of their old life in Egypt" — conflation.** In Exodus 17:3 the Israelites accuse Moses of bringing them out to die of thirst, but they do not explicitly long for Egypt's slavery. That language appears in Exodus 14:12 ("it would have been better for us to serve the Egyptians"), Exodus 16:3 (meat pots of Egypt), and Numbers 11:5 (fish, cucumbers, melons). The grumbling at Massah is about water, not nostalgia for Egypt.

The wording is close to ESV but not exact (ESV 4:8: "whatever is **honorable**" not "noble"; "**if there is any** excellence, **if there is anything** worthy of praise, **think about** these things"). Minor differences; the sense is preserved.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Sovereignty Preached, Gospel Assumed — A Scattered Survey That Never Lands**

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A raw-transcript survey of Hebrews 1 that correctly exalts Christ's sovereignty over all things while failing to drive any single truth home with sustained exposition, conscience-searching specificity, or a clear gospel proclamation.

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: MARGINAL FAIL**
The sermon announces itself as an exposition of Hebrews 1:1-14 ("Old Word from God, New Word from God"), but the body of the sermon spends the majority of its energy on the temptations of Christ (Matthew 4 / Luke 4), which is not in Hebrews 1 at all. Hebrews 1 argues for the absolute supremacy of the Son over angels and over the prophets — the sermon's stated thesis ("Christ is sovereign over everything the OT showed God sovereign over") is a reasonable derivation from the chapter's Christology, but the sermon never returns to the text's own argument. Hebrews 1:1-4 is the hinge — the Son as the culmination of revelation, the heir of all things, the exact imprint of God's nature, the one who made purification for sins — but none of these ideas are opened. The sermon preaches sovereignty through illustrative examples rather than through the actual argument of the text.

**Authority Test: PASS (barely)**
The preacher does not stand above the text as editor. He does not soften or omit. He is genuinely under the text's general trajectory. But he departs from the text's specific argument so completely that the text's voice is largely absent.

**Gate Verdict: Marginal — the sermon is not built from Hebrews 1's own argument. It uses Hebrews 1 as a launching pad and never returns. Evaluation continues but score capped considerations apply.**

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 — Main point does not equal text's point (TRIGGERED)**
Hebrews 1's controlling argument is the Son's absolute superiority over the angels as the ultimate and final Word of God. The sermon's main point — sovereignty over life's troubles — is derived loosely from scattered verses but is not the text's own argument. The sermon essentially uses Hebrews 1 as a sovereignty proof-text rather than expositing the text's Christological argument.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation (BORDERLINE)**
The closing call ("come talk to me," "make things right," "set your eyes upon Christ") is an altar-call gesture, not a specific exhortation arising from the text's argument. What must a person now believe, repent of, or pursue as a result of the Son's supremacy over angels? The sermon does not say.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Mostly Christ — the sovereignty of Christ is genuinely exalted. But the application section ("he can help you meet your bills") tilts the gravity back toward the self.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about Him. The Christology of Hebrews 1 — the Son as the exact imprint of God's nature, sustaining all things by his powerful word — is never opened in a way that produces encounter.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Informed, with some comfort. The conscience is touched but not searched.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **OT-to-NT Christological arc (opening section).** The sweep from Elijah to Jonah to Gideon to David, landing on Christ as the crescendo of God's sovereign action, is genuinely stirring and tracks the spirit of Hebrews 1:1-2. "The New Testament is like the crescendo of the orchestra" — this is a rare, earned image.
2. **The temptation narrative.** The handling of Matthew 4 — especially the observation that Christ succeeded where Israel, Adam, Moses, and Abraham all failed — is theologically sound, well-paced, and genuinely engaging. "Only Jesus had a chance at succeeding and praise God he did" is a strong pastoral declaration.
3. **Political application of divine sovereignty (Heb 1:12 section).** The connection from God's eternal unchangingness to electoral anxiety is pastorally wise and culturally specific. "Barack Obama might think he's hot stuff, but where was he when God hung the stars in the sky?" is effective plain speech.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Hebrews 1 is never actually preached.** The sermon announces Hebrews 1:1-14 but delivers a sermon on Matthew 4 with Hebrews 1 used for occasional proof-texts. The text's controlling argument — the Son is the final and superior Word of God, superior to angels, the heir of all things, the exact imprint of God's nature — is never opened. Hebrews 1 is the launching pad; it is never the destination.
2. **The gospel is assumed, never proclaimed.** The cross appears (Heb 1:3, Col 2:14, Gethsemane) but justification by faith is never articulated. An unbeliever sitting through this sermon would know that Christ is sovereign and that he died, but not how that death saves, what must be believed, or what repentance looks like. The closing call ("come talk to me") defers the gospel to a private conversation rather than proclaiming it from the text.
3. **No structural spine.** Three separate messages (sovereignty survey, temptation narrative, political anxiety) share 1,500 words without a unifying thesis. A listener cannot take home one clear truth because one clear truth was never established.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the temptation narrative — which is the strongest section of the sermon — belongs to a different text entirely. Matthew 4 is being preached under the address of Hebrews 1. The preacher is drawn to the material he finds most vivid and teachable, and the actual text of Hebrews 1 (which is denser and more theologically demanding) receives the thinner treatment. This is a pattern worth watching: the sermon gravitates to illustrative narrative and away from the letter's argument.

The practical sovereignty application ("he can help you meet your bills") is genuine pastoral care but risks domesticating Christ — moving from "sovereign over death" to "sovereign over your neighbor dispute" flattens the Christology of the opening. The sermon climbs to "sovereign over death!" and then descends without warning to a list of personal inconveniences.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The opening sovereignty survey feels like a strong introduction — and it is energetic, biblical, and directional. But it gives the listener the impression that the sermon will open Hebrews 1's own argument about the Son's superiority. That promise is never kept. The strength of the opening creates an expectation that the body of the sermon cannot satisfy, because the body departs entirely from the text.

The closing altar call ("there is no judgment here, just Jesus") feels warm and pastoral. But it is theologically incomplete — there is judgment, and the gospel is precisely that Christ bore it. Softening the call to "no judgment here" removes the offense of the cross that makes the invitation meaningful.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Hebrews 1:1-3 is one of the most theologically dense Christological passages in the New Testament. "He is the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature, and he upholds the universe by the word of his power." This text contains everything the sermon wanted to say about sovereignty — and it grounds that sovereignty not in illustrative examples from the OT, but in the ontological identity of the Son. A single sustained exposition of Hebrews 1:1-3 would have accomplished what the entire sermon attempted through accumulation of examples. The text's own argument is more powerful than the sermon built around it.

The "better than the angels" argument (Heb 1:4-14) was entirely bypassed. For a congregation prone to spiritual distraction or drift (the letter's occasion), the argument that Christ is the final and superior Word — that to turn back from him is to turn back from the fullness of revelation — carries urgent pastoral force. This opportunity was not taken.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild the structure around Hebrews 1:1-4 as the sermon's spine.** The text itself provides the outline: (a) God spoke through prophets — partially, in fragments (v.1); (b) God has now spoken through his Son — finally, fully (v.2); (c) The Son's identity: heir, creator, radiance, exact imprint, sustainer, purifier, enthroned (vv.2-3). Every OT sovereignty example the sermon uses is an illustration that belongs under this structure, not before it.
2. **Move the Matthew 4 material to a separate sermon or subordinate it clearly.** The temptation narrative is better suited to a sermon on Matthew 4, Luke 4, or the high priest theme in Hebrews 4:15. If kept, it must be tethered explicitly to Hebrews 1's argument — e.g., "This is the one Hebrews 1 calls the exact imprint of God's nature — and here is how he walked out that obedience..."
3. **Articulate the gospel from the text.** Hebrews 1:3b — "after making purification for sins, he sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high" — is a complete gospel statement in one clause. Open it: What is sin? What is purification? What does the sitting down mean? This is the text's own evangel.
4. **Add differentiated address at the conscience level.** After the sovereignty section, speak directly to the person who trusts their own resources (not just their neighbor's behavior, but their own competence and control). "Some of you are not anxious about who leads the country — you're anxious because you believe, functionally, that you are sovereign over your own life. This text confronts that."
5. **Replace the vague closing call with a specific exhortation.** "Come talk to me" defers the gospel. A closing like: "The Son who is the exact imprint of God's nature made purification for your sins and sat down — because the work is finished. Will you trust that finished work today, or will you keep wandering in your own Massah?" ties the exhortation to the text and makes it specific.
6. **Correct factual errors before any reuse:** 40 days should be 40 years (Israelite wandering); "80 or 90" should be "seventy, or even eighty" (Psalm 90:10); Elijah at the brook Cherith is a separate event from the 40-day journey to Horeb — do not combine them.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon has pastoral warmth and genuine gospel instinct. The problem is structural and expository, not the bloodlessness of correct-but-cold theology. This sermon wants to preach — it just hasn't found the text it's preaching from yet.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
