First of all, I have to say this is an unusual Christmas message — but I think you'll see by the end that it makes sense.

Today we're going to be talking about developing a theology of weakness. We all have areas of weakness, don't we? I don't know about any of you, but I'm not going to be winning any triathlons in the near future. But I don't just mean physical weakness. You know what I mean? There are areas where we just fall short. Short of what God wants, short of what we expect of ourselves. We just don't measure up. Do we?

---

## I. The Defeat That Produced a Book

I've been reading a book about this by a man named J.I. Packer. He's a professor at Regent College in Vancouver, British Columbia — an Anglican. A number of years ago when the US and Canadian Anglican churches began to pull away from Scripture, to change their view of its integrity, and as a consequence their view of who God is, Packer was one of the loudest voices calling the church back to God.

Ultimately, after years of battle, Packer left the Anglican Church of Canada, along with his congregation, and they joined the Anglican Province of the Southern Cone — the Anglican body covering South America — without physically leaving Canada.

He says it felt like defeat. Like losing something good and whole that had been together for centuries — and on his watch, it broke. It took a while for him to come to grips with all of this, and in the wake of that defeat, he wrote a book about how God works through brokenness. It's called *Weakness Is the Way: Life with Christ Our Strength* — which is very polite Canadian for "Sometimes God wants us broken."

I have a friend who frequently asks me, "Why is God letting this or that happen?" Why did I lose my son? Why did this person or that person have to die? Why is God allowing me to crash into the financial rocks? Those are all natural questions. I don't think there is an easy answer here, but I think we're going to touch on it as we look at 2 Corinthians 12 — scratching the surface on a theology of brokenness.

---

## II. What We Worship Instead of God

While you're turning there — it strikes me that as Americans, we have this idea that comfort is good. That security is good. That prosperity is good. That freedom is good. We hold these ideals up and pursue them hard, don't we? In our Declaration of Independence, we declare as our inalienable rights: life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness. We hold these things up as the paradigm — as the lens through which we view the world. You've heard of rose-colored glasses? As Americans, our lenses are colored by the right to pursue comfort, wealth, and security. Our own happiness is the tint on our lenses.

This is at the heart of almost all of the problems in our country today. People have begun to acknowledge the so-called right for homosexuals to marry. They base this argument on the homosexual's "right" to happiness. I've heard some of my cousins say, "Let them love who they love — what does it harm?" The argument for abortion is similar: if it's too hard for the mother, let her abort that baby. What kind of life, or what amount of happiness, would that child have if the mother doesn't want it? The mother thinks it would affect her happiness. The world thinks it would affect the baby's chance at happiness. We worship happiness.

If the government could give me a little more health insurance, then I could be happy. If the government could just build a wall and offer me a little more security, then I'd be happy. Hope I didn't just smash anyone's sacred cow there — but in the extreme, it's just as true. How can we be happy if we haven't provided for our own security? How can our kids be happy if we haven't provided for their security, their health care, their financial future? Often our goal for our kids is that they have a better life than we did. What's at the heart of that? Happiness. We see areas where we could have been happier and try to make sure the kids are happy in those areas, don't we? We worship the misguided desires of our evil hearts on the worship altar that belongs to God.

When I was a kid, I really didn't like medicine. I took it, and I don't remember complaining much. Taking your medicine when you were sick was an inevitability in my house growing up. My parents loved me. They wanted me to get better, and so they made sure I took the full course of medicine the doctor prescribed.

Folks, I hate to tell you this — but when you worship your own happiness, the medicine is brokenness. You may not even be worse on this point than your neighbor. You might be better at this than the person sitting next to you in the pew, or up here in the pulpit. But as Americans it's almost hardwired into us to be this way — to idolize our own happiness. To put our happiness at the center of our worship, so much so that this happens even in the church. If you have even one drop too much — and I'd hazard to guess that as a people Americans each have an ocean too much of this — if there is even one drop too much, God is going to allow you to go through things that will slowly burn that idol away.

## III. The Passage: 2 Corinthians 12:7–10

This is what Paul said. Are you with me in 2 Corinthians 12?

> "So to keep me from becoming conceited because of the surpassing greatness of the revelations, a thorn was given me in the flesh, a messenger of Satan to harass me, to keep me from becoming conceited. Three times I pleaded with the Lord about this, that it should leave me. But he said to me, 'My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness.' Therefore I will boast all the more gladly of my weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may rest upon me. For the sake of Christ, then, I am content with weaknesses, insults, hardships, persecutions, and calamities. For when I am weak, then I am strong."
> — 2 Corinthians 12:7–10, ESV

The first thing I want you to notice is that word *conceited.* I looked up the Greek word there in the *Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament Based on Semantic Domains* — one of the better Greek-to-English dictionaries that exist — and it says the Greek word means to be inflated with a sense of one's own importance. Doesn't that sound a little bit like what we've been talking about? If we are consumed with a desire to pursue happiness at the expense of God, aren't we prioritizing ourselves above God? Aren't we elevating or inflating our sense of our own importance?

So we can see the problem.

---

## IV. A Thorn in the Flesh

How did this play out in Paul's life? Paul says he had a thorn in the flesh. We don't know exactly what it was. But he had an area where he was weak. Sound familiar?

I have those too.

My first semester at Trinity Bible College — I was 17. I didn't know any better, so when the school suggested — when Laura Blick suggested — I take 15 credit hours, which is five classes, one of which being a third-year Greek class, I just went along with what she had to say.

I didn't realize that alongside 15 hours of class time came 45 hours each week of expected homework. For every hour I'd spend in class, they expected and assigned three hours each week of homework. Of reading. Of writing. Two of my classes were Bible classes. The third was a counseling class, a Greek class, and then one night a week I had to drive back to campus for a three-hour, once-a-week class that started right after dinner. The Bible classes I loved, and the history professor was one of my favorites. I enjoyed the counseling class too — but that Greek class was a struggle. We had to memorize dozens of new words each week. Each new quiz could pull from any of the words learned to that point. We had to memorize hundreds of case endings — like how we have *-ed* or *-s* at the end of words, and the presence of that ending affects whether the thing is happening now, has happened, or will happen once or more than once. In Greek there are hundreds of these that affect the tense, voice, and mood of a word. Hundreds. In Greek sometimes the letters are added at the end, and sometimes throughout the word — the *eimi* paradigm, for instance, was especially difficult. Some words you end up with only two or three letters of the original word being present, and the rest are dramatically different.

Anyway — it was a tough semester. I never felt like I had studied enough, so I was studying for Greek in my counseling class too, and I ended up failing both. Up to that point I had never failed at anything. Failing two classes cost me thousands of dollars. I had to retake them. It slowed down the graduation process. It started me out with a really bad GPA. All things that would be the source of struggles for years to come. I had a thorn in the flesh.

Paul had a thorn in his. We could likely all point to a thorn that God has allowed to be in our lives.

---

## V. Beauty for Ashes

Don't let your insufficiency discourage you. Don't let it get you down that you aren't good enough — because none of us are good enough. If we were good enough, Jesus wouldn't have had to come. We are all broken. We all fall short. The best we can do is ashes. The prophet Isaiah wrote that one day God would exchange our ashes for something beautiful — beauty for ashes (Isaiah 61:3).

Before we go further with that — what are ashes symbolic of in the Bible? Does anyone know? When do biblical people bring the ashes out? It's symbolic of mourning, isn't it. A bone-deep mourning. I think it's safe to say that those of us who have been Christians for a while have felt that bone-deep mourning. Maybe it's when you revisit your pet sin that you know God hates. Or when you drop the ball in sharing the gospel with someone. I've felt that way before. You know what I mean?

I feel like in a very real way, the things we are allowed to go through in life are designed to grind us down to the point where we are useful to God. Have you ever seen the process grain goes through as it's being milled? I saw a video this week of buckwheat being milled to flour. Buckwheat in its natural state is hard as a stone. It's pretty much useless in its natural state. Even if you intend on planting it, the seed has to die before a plant can spring forth. You can eat it like rice, but even then you're exposing it to fire and boiling water for half an hour or more to make it soft enough to eat. But my favorite is milling. My grandma used to make really great buckwheat pancakes, and in order for there to be pancakes there has to be milling. Good buckwheat pancakes are a beautiful thing — but buckwheat in its natural state is useless.

So to mill buckwheat to make pancakes, you dump the grain whole into a hopper — 50 or 100 pounds at a time. Or more for a big mill. A mechanism moves the grains a short distance until they reach the place where the magic happens. Where the hard stone of the buckwheat is turned into useful flour. Two great big heavy stones on a motor are spun in opposite directions, and the little buckwheat grain — the little buckwheat rock — is caught between the two and shattered into flour.

I think that is a picture of what Paul's talking about.

The human apart from Christ, in its natural state, is next to useless. We're hard little rocks. But in following Christ we are transformed into a thing of far more beauty. In our analogy, after being pulverized and crushed and mixed with sugar and seared on a hot pan, we become something beautiful. We become pancakes.

Beauty for ashes. I think that's a fair trade.

---

## VI. The Promise

There are parts of it that are hard. But God has given us promises, hasn't he? Our passage gives us one. God says in verse 9:

> "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness."
> — 2 Corinthians 12:9, ESV

When we are hungry, it gives God an opportunity to provide food — he promises he will provide all our needs.

When we are unhappy, it gives God the opportunity to be the source of our happiness.

When we are lonely — and we shouldn't be lonely; we are a group of people that has become like family — but if we find ourselves lonely, it gives God a chance to help us find belonging, and acceptance, and fellowship with him.

In every area where we are insufficient, it's okay — because God is sufficient.

Don't let that lull you into apathy — keep pushing forward.

But when we are weak, it just means God's at work. He is refining and improving us. God's just making pancakes. Making beauty from ashes. Substituting his strength for our weakness.

---

## VII. Why Christmas Fits

I know this isn't a typical picture of Christmas — but it still fits.

It was our weakness that caused the fall. Adam and Eve sinned, bringing sin and its guilt into the human race, passing it to everyone who would ever come after.

It was our weakness that made us unable to keep the law after the fall — the law that demanded a righteousness we could not produce.

And so because of our weakness, God sent his Son Jesus to make up the difference. We deserved death. But Jesus took that death. Beauty from ashes.

But even that wasn't the end, was it? We know from history and from Scripture that there were more than five hundred witnesses who saw Christ after he rose from the dead (1 Corinthians 15:6). Nothing is more ashes than death, and nothing more beautiful than Christ dying for us and Christ rising again.

> "Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends."
> — John 15:13, ESV

Yet we weren't even friends at that point. We were not even born — and for a period in our lives, most of us were hostile toward God. And yet he chose to die for us anyway. Beauty from ashes.

But it all starts with a relationship with Jesus. If you were to die today and don't know what would happen next, come talk to me. If you're interested in joining the church, come talk to me.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The cultural diagnosis in Section II is sharp and specific.** The claim that Americans idolize happiness, and that this idolatry extends into the church, names something real. Connecting the comfort-worship of our culture to the need for Paul's theology of weakness is an excellent homiletical move. The political examples (healthcare, border security) are brave, concrete, and non-partisan in their application — they do not spare either side's sacred cow.

2. **The buckwheat/pancake illustration is genuinely excellent.** It is native to this text, it carries the sermon's central idea, it is concrete and warm, and "God's just making pancakes" is a line the congregation will carry home. It serves the theology rather than decorating it.

3. **The Christmas connection in Section VII is theologically coherent and structurally satisfying.** Tracing the weakness → law-demand → incarnation → substitution → resurrection arc in a single focused section demonstrates real theological instinct. The Incarnation as God's answer to human weakness (rather than human greatness) is a genuine insight that fits the passage.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is compressed to two paragraphs after six sections of setup, and it arrives without force.** Section VII contains the most important content in the sermon — Christ's death and resurrection as the ultimate beauty-for-ashes — but it receives less development than the story about Greek class. The substitutionary atonement ("We deserved death. But Jesus took that death") is accurate but thin. No unbeliever in that room would understand what it means to trust that death, why faith is necessary, or what repentance looks like.

2. **The exhortation to the believing congregation is absent.** After establishing a compelling diagnosis (we idolize happiness; God uses brokenness to burn the idol away), the sermon never returns with a specific call. What must the man in financial collapse *do* differently? What must the woman who has been demanding happiness from God *repent of*? What does *boasting in weakness* look like on Monday morning? The sermon explains Paul's theology but never summons the congregation to live it.

3. **The immediate context of 2 Corinthians 10–13 is entirely absent, and this impoverishes the text.** Paul is not writing a devotional on the virtue of weakness — he is defending his apostleship against men who despised his weakness as disqualifying. The thorn exists in a polemic context. The super-apostles boasted in strength; Paul boasts in weakness because that is where Christ's power rests. The sermon loses the urgency and irony of the passage by treating it as therapeutic rather than polemical. This missed context would have made the idol-of-strength diagnosis in Section II significantly more powerful.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how much the sermon is structured around the hearer's comfort rather than the hearer's confrontation. The warmth that is a pastoral strength becomes a theological weakness: the sermon is never willing to leave the congregation uncomforted. Even Section II, which begins to corner the happiness-idolater, resolves quickly into reassurance ("the medicine is brokenness" — but that line is softened rather than pressed). The result is a sermon that diagnoses without persisting in conviction long enough for the law to do its work before the gospel relieves it.

There is also a blind spot around the sermon's audience differentiation. The sermon is written as though the congregation is essentially uniform — all struggling with weakness, all needing comfort. But there are almost certainly people in the room who are not struggling at all — who are prosperous, comfortable, and spiritually complacent. The sermon has no address to them except through the abstract cultural critique of Section II.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Packer introduction reads as intellectual and pastoral authority — it establishes credibility, it connects weakness to lived experience, it introduces the book's theme. But it functions as a long delay before arriving at the text. The sermon does not open 2 Corinthians 12 until Section III (nearly halfway through). The Packer material, while excellent for introduction, creates an illusion of theological weight that the subsequent treatment of the text does not fully deliver on. The listener may feel the sermon is theologically substantial because it begins with J.I. Packer — but the exegesis of the passage itself is modest.

Similarly, the personal Greek class story creates the feeling of pastoral transparency and relatability — but at two full paragraphs, it is the longest single block in the sermon. The payoff ("I had a thorn in the flesh") is proportionally thin. The length of the illustration signals importance; the substance does not match the signal.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The sermon's best missed opportunity is the phrase "a messenger of Satan to harass me" (v.7). The thorn is not merely a difficulty — it is *Satanic* in instrument, and yet God sovereignly ordained it to keep Paul humble. This is one of the most striking theological claims in the passage: Satan's messenger serving God's sanctifying purpose. A sermon on the theology of weakness that does not press this tension — that God uses even demonic instruments to produce holiness — misses the most arresting element of the text. The congregation needed to wrestle with the question: *Is the very thing crushing me a messenger of Satan — and might God be using it anyway?* That question was available and was not asked.

The Christmas connection also had room for more: the Incarnation as the ultimate act of divine weakness (God taking on human flesh, born in poverty, dying in shame) would have extended the "weakness is the way" theme into Christology far more richly than was developed.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Compress the Packer introduction and the Greek class narrative by half, and use the recovered time to open 2 Corinthians 10–13 with one sentence of context.** The congregation needs to know Paul is defending his apostleship to people who despise his weakness — that one sentence transforms the passage from devotional advice into a gospel polemic.

2. **Preach "a messenger of Satan to harass me."** After identifying the thorn as anti-conceit discipline, stop at this phrase and ask the congregation: "Does it strike you that God uses even a messenger of Satan for his purposes? The very thing you've been praying to escape may be exactly what God is using to hollow you out for Christ." This is the most overlooked sentence in the passage and the most pastorally useful.

3. **Expand the gospel in Section VII to at least three substantive paragraphs.** State what the unbeliever must do: not just "come talk to me," but "hear me now — the beauty God offers is not just strength in your weakness, it is a clean record before God that Christ purchased with his own blood. Repent. Believe. That exchange — your sin for his righteousness — is the only beauty from ashes that eternally matters."

4. **Add a specific exhortation for believers after Section VI.** Apply Perkins: after comforting the weak and grieving, turn and address the comfortable: "Some of you are not weak right now — and that may be the most dangerous place to be. God's refining work is coming. Don't wait for the thorn to drive you to him. Run to him now, in your strength, before God has to strip it." Then give the believer a specific practice: confess the idol, name the comfort you have been placing above God, and bring it to the cross this week.

5. **The conclusion needs one clear climactic note.** Currently the sermon ends three times — the Resurrection, the John 15:13 quote, and the gospel close. Land the plane on one image. Tie the final note back to the pancake illustration or to the Christmas theme with a single line of weight: "The same God who crushed the grain and raised the Son — he is making something beautiful of you. That is the Christmas promise."

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries genuine pastoral warmth and a theologically sound instinct — it glimpses the glory of a God who meets us in weakness — but it does not yet carry the eternal gravity of a sermon that leaves the congregation undone before the cross and rebuilt by it.
