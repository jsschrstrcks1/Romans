**Introduction — “Whose Slave Are You? The Freedom That Binds Us to Righteousness”**
If you’ve brought your Bible—and I hope you have—open with me to Romans 6:15.
Before we dive in, let’s look outward and pray for our People Group of the Week.
**Today: the Libyan Arab people of Libya**. They live mainly along the North African coast; many families that were once nomadic are now settled in towns and cities. The primary religion is Islam, access to Christian information is restricted, and proselytizing is prohibited—real obstacles for gospel witness. **Population**: ~1,626,000. Of those, only 0.04% are evangelical (≈ 650). Libya under Gadaffi was a huge supporter of Palestine, rendering aid and resources to those seeking to harm Israel.
A lot of people feel like the answer to things like this is more violence, and that may eventually be what happens—especially since they still have two different governments. However, wouldn’t a better way be to share the gospel? Every person bent toward evil that becomes a believer in Jesus Christ is reoriented—by God’s grace, mercy, and love—towards God.
The population of Libya is almost entirely Sunni Muslim.
Pray for the Lord to reveal Himself—even through dreams and visions—and awaken hunger for truth. Pray for open doors and protection so that believers can share wisely and courageously. Pray for the few believers to be well-taught, unified, and bold in love for Jesus, and compassionate toward the lost.
Could I have two volunteers to pray?
Now, last week we said it this way: **when grace reigns, sin cannot**. Grace takes the throne in a believer’s heart, and the old master is pushed out. Paul doesn’t stop there. In our text today he presses a sharper question: *Whose **slave** are you?* The gospel never frees you into neutral territory; freedom always comes with a new allegiance.
Picture a field—Bannockburn, Scotland 1314. Remember the movie *Braveheart*, where William Wallace screamed *“**Freeeeedommm**!”*? This is back in that time frame. On one side stands Robert the Bruce, the rightful king of Scotland, rallying a smaller force under his banner. On the other side, Edward II with a massive English army. It wasn’t just land at stake; it was allegiance. Every man stood under a banner. No one fought in the middle. And when the battle ended, only one banner still flew.
Paul says the Christian life is like that. You’re either under sin’s banner or under Christ’s. And when grace takes hold, it’s like the rightful King riding in—the usurper is driven out, and a new reign begins.

**Point 1 — Grace Exposes Your Master (Romans 6:15–16)**
Paul starts with the question he knows is coming:
“What then? Are we to sin because we are not under law but under grace?”
His answer is short and sharp: *By no means!* (Greek: **μὴ γένοιτο — mē genoito**, a strong idiom meaning “May it never be!” or “Absolutely not!”). It’s one of the strongest *No’s* in the whole Bible.
Why so strong? Because grace isn’t a permission slip—it’s a transfer of allegiance. Verse 16:
“Do you not know that if you present yourselves to anyone as obedient slaves, you are slaves (*δοῦλοι** — **douloi*, from **δοῦλος — doulos**, meaning ‘bondservant’ or ‘slave,’ one completely owned and obligated to serve) of the one whom you obey, either of sin, which leads to death, or of obedience, which leads to righteousness?”
In other words, who you obey tells the truth about who your master is.
Back at Bannockburn, you could tell a man’s loyalty by the standard he stood under and the orders he followed. No Scottish soldier took commands from Edward. No English knight followed Bruce. Your banner told your story. Paul is saying the same thing: **your obedience is your banner**.
Let me ask you this: Which banner does the testimony of your life fly? Do your actions point toward Christ? Or somewhere else?
So hear Joshua’s old challenge fresh:
“Choose this day whom you will serve” (Joshua 24:15).
There is no middle ground. If you obey sin, you serve sin and fly sin’s banner. If you obey God, you belong to Him and fly God’s banner. Grace pulls off the mask and shows who’s really calling the shots in your life. The gospel does not free you to wander without a master; it frees you to serve the only Master who gives life.
And here’s where we bring it home—because obedience is not occasional. People tend to think, *Well, I’m mostly under God’s banner*. But God does not take “part-time allegiance.” As Spurgeon said:
“If Christ is not all to you He is nothing to you. He will never go into partnership as a part Saviour of men. If He be something He must be everything, and if He be not everything He is nothing to you.”
John writes:
“Whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil, for the devil has been sinning from the beginning. The reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the works of the devil. No one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God’s seed abides in him, and he cannot keep on sinning because he has been born of God” (1 John 3:8–9, ESV).

Notice the phrase *practice of sinning* — this is about a continual lifestyle, not a single failure. The Christian’s life should not be marked by slavery to sin because **the old master has been overthrown**.
Think of someone in Robert the Bruce’s army who, in the morning, fights the king’s enemies, but in the afternoon, sneaks over and attacks Bruce’s own men. That’s what it’s like when we try to live with split allegiance. And in our congregation, this is where the rubber meets the road—drug abuse, gambling in secret, pornography, foul language, and yes, political idolatry. It’s not the man in the Oval Office who saves you from your sin. These are banners of the wrong kingdom, and they cannot fly alongside Christ’s banner in your life.
Point 2 — Grace Changes Your Allegiance (Romans 6:17–18)

Paul shifts from exposing the master to celebrating the change:

“But thanks be to God, that you who were once slaves (δοῦλοι — douloi, slaves in total subjection) of sin have become obedient from the heart to the standard of teaching to which you were committed, and, having been set free (ἐλευθερωθέντες — eleutherōthentes, freed, released from bondage) from sin, have become slaves (ἐδουλώθητε — edoulōthēte, were made slaves) of righteousness.”

Grace doesn’t just reveal your old master—it transfers your loyalty to a new one.
Notice the direction of the change: “obedient from the heart.” This isn’t begrudging compliance. It’s not doing the right thing while wishing you could still serve sin. It’s a heart made new (Ezekiel 36:26–27), one that delights in the law of God (Psalm 40:8).

Paul says it’s the standard of teaching—the apostolic gospel—that shapes you. The Greek phrase here is τύπον διδαχῆς — typon didachēs, literally “pattern or mold of teaching.” It’s as though God poured you into a new mold, and the mold is the gospel itself. You no longer take your shape from sin’s pattern, but from Christ’s.
And don’t miss the passive voice in verse 18: “you were set free” … “you became slaves.” God is the actor. He broke the chains. He placed you in His service. Grace isn’t your clever escape—it’s His sovereign rescue.
This is why the believer cannot take credit for the transformation. As R.C. Sproul once said:
“We are not sinners because we sin; we sin because we are sinners. And we are not righteous because we do righteous things; we do righteous things because God has made us righteous in Christ.”
And so the banners have changed. You’re no longer under the black flag of sin; you’re under the white banner of the Lamb. This is why Paul says in Colossians 1:13–14 (ESV):
“He has delivered us from the domain of darkness and transferred us to the kingdom of his beloved Son, in whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins.”
Point 3 — Grace Produces Holiness (Romans 6:19–23)
Paul knows the slavery metaphor is limited, so he qualifies it: “I am speaking in human terms, because of your natural limitations.”
But the truth remains—just as you once presented your members (your body, your mind, your desires) as slaves to impurity and lawlessness, so now you must present them as slaves to righteousness leading to sanctification (ἁγιασμός — hagiasmos, holiness, set-apartness).
Verse 21 cuts deep:
“But what fruit were you getting at that time from the things of which you are now ashamed? For the end of those things is death.”
Sin promises life, but the harvest is always death. Holiness, by contrast, bears fruit that lasts—purity, joy, peace, and ultimately eternal life. Verse 22:
“But now that you have been set free from sin and have become slaves of God, the fruit you get leads to sanctification and its end, eternal life.”
And then the famous verse 23—often used in evangelism but here the conclusion to a call to holiness:
“For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift (χάρισμα — charisma, a gift of grace) of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.”
Slavery to sin pays you in death—it’s a wage you earn. But God’s slavery is different. His “payment” is a gift, not earned but freely given—eternal life in Christ.
This is why holiness isn’t optional for the believer. Grace doesn’t just forgive sin—it dethrones it and grows something entirely new. As John MacArthur says:

“True salvation is not only justification; it is also sanctification. God does not save you to leave you the way you are—He saves you to make you like His Son.”
Conclusion — The Call to Choose Your Master
So where does this leave us? It leaves us back on the field, with banners waving and kings calling. And the Spirit of God is saying through Paul: Choose your Master.
Joshua said to Israel,
“Choose this day whom you will serve … But as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD” (Joshua 24:15, ESV).
Paul says in Philippians 3:13–14 (ESV),
“Forgetting what lies behind and straining forward to what lies ahead, I press on toward the goal for the prize of the upward call of God in Christ Jesus.”
And in Galatians 2:20 (ESV),
“I have been crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me. And the life I now live in the flesh I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me.”
David Platt in Radical reminds us that this call is not to casual Christianity but to a cross-bearing allegiance:
“We have to give up everything we have to follow Jesus. We have to love Him in a way that makes our closest relationships in this world look like hate in comparison. And it’s worth it.”
Beloved, the question is not Will you serve?—you already are. The only question is Whom will you serve? If it’s sin, the banner you fly leads to death. If it’s Christ, the banner you fly leads to life.
So for the one hiding sin in the shadows—be it drugs, gambling, pornography, foul speech, or political idolatry—the call is the same as it was to the crowds on the day of Pentecost:
“Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins” (Acts 2:38, ESV).
The rightful King has come. The usurper has been dethroned. His banner over you is love. And when that banner is raised, there is no other worth serving.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Faithful Banner, Thin Gospel Center — Holiness Preached Without the Cross That Produces It**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A textually engaged opening of Romans 6:15–23 that drives the slavery metaphor with real pastoral specificity and strong exhortation, while leaving the cross and justification as background assumption rather than proclaimed power — the very grace Paul anchors sanctification in.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — that grace transfers allegiance and that Christians must now live as slaves to righteousness — arises directly from Paul's argument in vv. 15–23. The main point of the sermon is the main point of the passage. Pass.

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text's claims, does not soften the slavery language, and applies the uncomfortable implications directly. The hard words about pornography, drug abuse, and political idolatry are named from the pulpit. Pass.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 — Partial concern (not triggered):** The main point holds. However, the sermon's governing metaphor (battle/banner) occasionally threatens to carry more theological weight than Paul's argument does.

**Flag 3 — TRIGGERED: Christ is present but not proclaimed as essential to the sermon's transformative logic.** The sermon names Christ, cites "Christ who lives in me," and closes with an evangelistic call, but it never explains *how* the cross is the engine of the transfer of allegiance. The Sproul quote is the closest thing to an explanatory gospel moment, but it is not preached — it is cited and moved past. A sermon on Romans 6 that does not explain union with Christ in his death and resurrection (which Paul spends 6:1–14 establishing as the foundation for vv. 15–23) has left the gospel as scaffolding rather than structure. The sermon *could* be heard as: "God has transferred your allegiance — therefore obey." This is not the full news. The fuller news is: "You died with Christ, you rose with Christ, therefore the power of sin over you is broken *because you are in Him.*" That foundation is absent here.

**Flag 5 — Partial:** Exhortation is present and commendable — the sermon does call for response. However, it calls for response primarily from those already in the faith without adequately distinguishing or summoning the unbeliever or the genuinely unconverted in the room. The Acts 2:38 citation at the close is good but arrives without prior gospel proclamation to those who haven't yet believed.

**Score Cap: Not applied** — Flag 3 is a concern at the level of "partially triggered" given the evangelistic closer; the Christ-reference is present. However, the flag is noted prominently and will significantly affect the Gospel Centrality score. No hard cap applied; natural scoring will reflect the gap.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 19/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (8/10):* The sermon works through vv. 15–23 in sequence, explains key Greek terms (mē genoito, douloi, eleutherōthentes, hagiasmos, charisma), and keeps the passage's argument visibly in play. However, the critical exegetical foundation of Romans 6:1–14 — union with Christ in death and resurrection — is not drawn forward as the ground for vv. 15–23. Paul's argument in the second half of Romans 6 *presupposes* what he established in the first half. Ignoring that foundation makes the application feel more Pelagian than Pauline.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5):* The book flow is acknowledged superficially ("last week we said..."), but the redemptive-historical placement — Paul's argument against antinomianism in the larger Romans 1–8 arc — is underdeveloped.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5):* Greek terms are handled well and explained accessibly. The typon didachēs treatment is particularly useful.
- *Logical Faithfulness (4/5):* No significant eisegesis. The Bannockburn illustration's logic is consistent with the text's own logic. The use of Joshua 24:15 is appropriate if slightly too voluntaristic given Paul's emphasis on divine sovereignty in vv. 17–18.

**Category 2 — Theology & Doctrinal Integrity: 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7):* Christ is named, cited in Galatians 2:20, and held up as the banner. But the sermon's Christ-connection is motivational rather than ontological. Paul's point is not merely "choose Christ's banner" but "you have been united to Christ — act like it." The sermon's Christology is present but thin at the root.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* Colossians 1:13–14 is used helpfully. The transfer-of-kingdoms motif is consistent with biblical-theological categories. But the Adamic/Christic parallel (Romans 5:12–21 feeding into Romans 6) is entirely absent.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4/4):* No doctrinal errors. The Sproul quote on justification/sanctification is precise. The hagiasmos treatment is sound.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4):* Broadly Reformed. The passive voice emphasis ("you were set free... you became slaves — God is the actor") is excellent and confessionally solid. Missing: clear treatment of regeneration as the ground of the obedience being demanded.

**Category 3 — Gospel Centrality & Force: 9/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (2/5):* The gospel is assumed rather than proclaimed. The cross appears in reference (Galatians 2:20) but is never explained as the mechanism of the transfer. An unbeliever listening would understand that they need to "choose Christ's banner" but would not understand *why the cross makes that possible* or *what it means that Christ died for sin.* Justification is gestured at via Sproul but not explained.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5):* Christ is not optional to this sermon's conclusion. The Galatians 2:20 citation is strong. The Acts 2:38 closer names Christ's name as the ground of forgiveness. This is real.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5):* The sermon moves toward Christ but not specifically toward the cross. The cross as penal substitution — Christ bearing the wages of sin so that the gift of eternal life could be given — is never named, despite verse 23 providing the perfect occasion.

*Synagogue Test:* Partially triggered. A devout Muslim could hear much of this sermon and not be offended by the necessity of Christ's blood. The slavery/allegiance metaphor, the call to choose your master, and the fruit-bearing sections could survive in a morally serious religious context without the cross being the specific offense. This is a real concern.

**Category 4 — Conscience & Applicatory Force: 11/15**

- *Heart Penetration (4/5):* The specific naming of drug abuse, gambling, pornography, foul language, and political idolatry is courageous and specific. This is a real strength. The sermon presses on the conscience with real content.
- *Specificity (5/5):* This is the sermon's strongest category. The congregation is addressed with their specific sins named. The split-allegiance image ("fights in the morning, attacks Bruce's men in the afternoon") is vivid and personally cutting.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon largely addresses those already inside the faith — the believer who is struggling with split allegiance. The unbeliever is addressed only at the end. The backslider is implied but not directly addressed. The humbled-but-not-yet-believing hearer, the knowledgeable-but-unmoved hearer, and the self-righteous hearer receive no distinct address. Perkins' taxonomy is largely collapsed into one type.

**Category 5 — Exhortation & Response: 6/8**

- *Call to Response (4/4):* The conclusion is a genuine call. "Choose your Master" is not vague. The Acts 2:38 repentance call is explicit. The "hiding sin in the shadows" address is a real exhortation to real people.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The sermon does not explicitly equip. It calls for allegiance but does not show the congregation *how* to fight sin, how to present their members to righteousness practically, or how to bear witness to a watching world. The call is right; the equipping is thin.

**Category 6 — Structure & Logical Flow: 7/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* Three points are clear and memorable: Grace Exposes Your Master / Grace Changes Your Allegiance / Grace Produces Holiness. The structure follows the passage's flow. A listener could sketch this.
- *Progression (3/3):* The sermon builds — from exposure to transformation to fruit. This is clean.
- *Unity (1/2):* The Libya prayer section at the opening is jarring and breaks the sermon's unity. It is not integrated into the sermon's argument and leaves the listener uncertain whether they are in a missions moment or a sermon. This is a structural cost.

**Category 7 — Illustration & Imagination: 4/5**

- *Relevance (2/2):* The Bannockburn/Braveheart illustration works well — the congregation likely knows it, and the medieval battle metaphor is genuinely native to the slavery/allegiance argument.
- *Clarity (2/2):* The split-allegiance soldier image (fighting for Bruce in the morning, attacking Bruce's men in the afternoon) is one of the sharpest illustrations in the sermon and directly maps onto the theological point.
- *Memorability (0/1):* The banner image is strong, but it competes with the Braveheart connection and the repeated use of Bannockburn. A single, cleaner image would be more memorable than multiple uses of the same location. The congregation may remember Bannockburn; they may not remember what it meant.

**Category 8 — Sermonic Force: 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2):* The manuscript has oral cadence. Direct address is present ("Let me ask you this," "Beloved," "So for the one hiding sin in the shadows"). The breath-length paragraphs in the conclusion work well.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The sermon's strongest convictional moment is the naming of specific sins (Point 1 close). But several sections read more like explanation than proclamation — particularly the Point 2 Greek word study section, which is informative but not hot.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1):* The manuscript does not demonstrate strong sentence-length variation at climactic moments. The conclusion is reasonably compressed, but the transition from Point 2 to Point 3 is flat.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Partially. The exhortation to "choose your master" places significant weight on the hearer's decision and allegiance. The sermon's grammar is frequently imperative ("choose," "present," "fly the banner"). Paul's own grammar in Romans 6 is predominantly indicative before it is imperative — "you *were* set free" before "now *present* yourselves." The sermon leans toward the imperative without adequately grounding it in the indicative. This tilts the listener toward self-focus even while pointing to Christ.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Ideas are more prominent than encounter. The Spurgeon, Sproul, MacArthur, and Platt quotations are well-chosen, but a sermon full of human voices — however faithful — can crowd out the voice of the text itself. God is discussed more than present.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Convicted — genuinely so. The specific naming of sins and the split-allegiance image land. This is the sermon's clearest success. A person hiding pornography in this congregation would feel addressed. That is real preaching.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Courageous applicatory specificity.** The naming of drug abuse, gambling, pornography, foul language, and political idolatry by name from the pulpit is the kind of pastoral courage the rubric rewards. This is not generic. This is a shepherd who knows his flock.

2. **The passive voice exegesis in Point 2.** "God is the actor. He broke the chains. He placed you in His service. Grace isn't your clever escape — it's His sovereign rescue." This is doctrinally precise, beautifully expressed, and consistent with Paul's grammar in vv. 17–18. It is the theological high-water mark of the sermon.

3. **The Bannockburn split-allegiance soldier image.** "Fighting the king's enemies in the morning, but attacking Bruce's own men in the afternoon." This is a native, memorable illustration that directly maps onto the theological argument. It is specific enough to be sharp and concrete enough to be remembered.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The cross is absent as the engine of transformation.** The sermon reaches its destination (holiness, new allegiance, slavery to righteousness) without showing the road (union with Christ in his death and resurrection). Romans 6:1–14 is the foundation Paul laid before vv. 15–23, and that foundation — "you died with Christ, you were buried with him, you were raised with him" — is what gives the imperatives of vv. 15–23 their indicative power. Without it, the sermon's call to allegiance sounds like moral effort under divine expectation rather than Spirit-enabled participation in Christ's own death and life. This is the sermon's structural gospel failure.

2. **Verse 23 is treated as evangelistic decoration rather than the climax of Paul's holiness argument.** The preacher notes that v. 23 is "often used in evangelism but here the conclusion to a call to holiness" — which is a correct and insightful observation — but then does not actually use it as a call to holiness. The free gift of eternal life as the *alternative wage* to death is noted but not pressed into the conscience. What would it look like to receive that gift *today, in the moment of resisting sin*? That question goes unasked.

3. **The Libya prayer section is structurally disconnected.** The opening missions prayer — however valuable in the life of the congregation — is not integrated into the sermon's argument, breaks the rhetorical momentum, and forces the preacher to rebuild the stakes from scratch after four paragraphs of missions content. If the missions moment is a regular part of the service order, it should precede the sermon text reading rather than open the sermon. As positioned, it costs the sermon its first impression.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see how voluntaristic his closing is. The conclusion's repeated call to "choose your master" — while not Arminian in intent — lands with the rhetorical weight of a decision-theology altar call. Paul's own point in vv. 17–18 is that the Romans did *not* choose their new master; they were *transferred* by God's sovereign act ("you were set free," "you became slaves" — passive voice throughout). The sermon names this in Point 2 with the Sproul quote, then functionally undoes it in the conclusion by placing the initiative back on the hearer. The theology says "God does the transferring." The application says "Now you choose." These are not contradictory, but they must be more carefully sequenced.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The volume of quotations — Spurgeon, R.C. Sproul, John MacArthur, David Platt — creates an impression of doctrinal weight that partially substitutes for original expository development. Each quotation is apt. But the cumulative effect is a sermon that *cites* Reformed theology more than it *preaches* it. The Sproul quote on justification/sanctification is especially valuable — but it is cited and left, rather than preached through. A congregation that hears Sproul quoted has not necessarily heard the truth Sproul was explaining. The preacher's own voice needs to carry the theological content, with quotations as confirmation, not as primary delivery vehicle.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Verse 19's admission — "I am speaking in human terms because of your natural limitations" — is a significant pastoral and hermeneutical moment that the sermon passes over quickly.** Paul himself is being transparent about the limits of his own metaphor. A preacher who pauses here and says "Paul is doing something remarkable — he's correcting himself mid-argument to keep us from misunderstanding grace as slavery" would open up the congregation's imagination about how carefully Paul is protecting his gospel. The slavery metaphor is the *best available* human analogy, and even Paul considers it inadequate. The sermon notes it briefly and moves on. This is the text's own invitation to nuance — and it was not taken.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach Romans 6:1–14 forward into vv. 15–23 in the introduction.** Before the three points begin, one paragraph connecting "last week we established that you died with Christ and rose with Christ" to "now Paul draws out the implication" would ground the entire sermon's logic in the indicative rather than the imperative. Thirty seconds of theological bridge-building would change the sermon's entire doctrinal register.

2. **Preach verse 23 as the holiness climax, not the evangelistic footnote.** The sermon correctly identifies v. 23 as a holiness text. Now preach it that way. What does it mean that every moment of sin is choosing a wage that pays in death, while the gift of life is being withheld from you by your own allegiance? The tension between *wage* (earned, deserved) and *gift* (unearned, given) is the sharpest doctrinal edge in the passage. Press it.

3. **Rework the conclusion to lead with the indicative before the imperative.** "The King has come — not merely to give you orders, but to give you His own life so that you could live under His banner" before "therefore, choose your master." The imperative must follow the indicative, not precede it, or the sermon becomes law rather than gospel.

4. **Add distinct address to the Perkins hearer who is "knowledgeable but unhumbled."** This congregation likely contains people who know the slavery metaphor, affirm confessional Reformed theology, and continue in habitual sin without real alarm. The sermon names the sins but does not specifically corner the person who knows all this and is unmoved. A direct "Some of you can explain this doctrine and still go home to pornography tonight — this sermon has not yet touched you, and you know it" moment would add pastoral force.

5. **Relocate or integrate the Libya section.** Either move it before the sermon opening text (as a service element, not a sermon element), or — more powerfully — briefly integrate it at the close: "There are 650 believers in Libya who fly this banner at the cost of their freedom. What banner are you flying in comfort?" This turns the missions moment into the sermon's most powerful illustration.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon has real pastoral heat and specific applicatory force. The conscience-piercing naming of specific sins and the direct address to "the one hiding sin in the shadows" demonstrate pastoral engagement, not bloodless orthodoxy. The fire is present. The cross is what needs strengthening, not the temperature.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries genuine pastoral gravity — a shepherd preaching to real people about real sins with real courage — but stops short of the eternal weight that comes when the congregation is made to feel not merely the cost of sin but the infinite worth of the One who paid it.

**Bonus: +2/5**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Romans 6:15–23 is epistolary argument — Paul at his most logical and sequential. The rubric's emphasis on exposition, gospel centrality, and conscience application is well-suited to this genre. The Law/Gospel dialectic category is especially native here, since Paul himself is working the tension between sin's wage and grace's gift. No genre strain. The rubric fits this passage well and applies without modification.
