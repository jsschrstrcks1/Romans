**Love Poured Out, Wrath Satisfied: The Gospel at Full Strength**
*Romans 5:1–11*

**People Group of the Week: The Solanki Rajput of India**
This week, we remember the Solanki Rajput people of India. Numbering around 350,000, they are a Hindi-speaking people known for their proud warrior heritage and noble caste status. Their cultural pride and ancestral identity form spiritual strongholds—barriers to recognizing their need for grace and the humbling lordship of Jesus Christ. And yet, as the Lord declared to the prophet Jeremiah:
“Behold, I have put my words in your mouth… to pluck up and to break down, to destroy and to overthrow, to build and to plant” (Jeremiah 1:9–10, ESV).
Our God is able to tear down every obstacle that sets itself against Christ. Let us now go before the throne of grace in prayer:

**Sermon Transition**
Let’s return now to our study of Romans—a letter that has stripped away every false hope of self-righteousness and has unveiled the terrifying majesty of God’s wrath against sin. In the opening chapters, we have stood in the courtroom of divine justice, watching the whole world fall silent beneath the Law’s judgment. And yet, we have also heard of a righteousness revealed apart from the Law—freely given, received by faith, anchored not in our merit but in the grace of God.
Now, here in chapter 5, we arrive at the heart of it all. Not only the mechanics of justification, but its motive: the unrelenting love of God.
Romans 5:8 reads:
“But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.” (Romans 5:8, ESV)
Let us imagine the scene: a guilty criminal, condemned beyond question, stands before the throne. The Judge—the King Himself—rises to declare the verdict. But instead of sentencing the man, the King steps down, bears the full punishment, and then does something even more shocking: He adopts the criminal as His own child. This is not mere leniency. It is not cold acquittal. It is love—holy, costly, substitutionary love.
As John Owen once wrote:
“The love of God is most wonderfully displayed in the redemption of sinners by Jesus Christ, who endured the wrath of God in their stead.”
This is the heartbeat of the gospel. Not love as the world defines it—fleeting, sentimental, soft—but love poured out, wrath satisfied. The gospel at full strength.
Imagine a tree standing in the eye of a hurricane. Branches whipping, roots groaning under the pressure of the wind, yet it does not fall. Not because the wind is mild, but because the roots go deep. The storm reveals its strength. This is a picture of grace in the life of a believer—grace that roots us even in the fiercest gales of life. As the lyrics by John Mark McMillan go:
“Love’s like a hurricane, I am a tree
Bending beneath the weight of His wind and mercy
When all of a sudden, I am unaware
Of these afflictions eclipsed by glory
And I realize just how beautiful You are
And how great Your affections are for me.”
Romans 5 is Paul pulling back the curtain on what justification produces in the life of the believer. It is the gospel, not diluted or sentimentalized, but at full strength. And as we journey through this passage, we discover a few important realities:

**I. The Fruit of Justification: Peace with God and Access into Grace (Romans 5:1–2)**
Paul begins by declaring the fruit of justification:
“Therefore, since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ. Through him we have also obtained access by faith into this grace in which we stand, and we rejoice in hope of the glory of God.” (Romans 5:1–2, ESV)
This peace is not a fleeting feeling or the absence of conflict; it is legal, covenantal peace. The enmity that once stood between us and God has been fully removed. We are no longer under His wrath but at peace with Him.
What do we know of God’s wrath? It was the wrath of God that made Jesus pray for the cup to be removed. It was the wrath of God that made Jesus cry tears of blood. Jonathan Edwards wrote, “It’s a fearful thing to fall into the hands of an angry God.” Who could know the reality of those words more fully and deeply than Jesus Himself—one-third of the Trinity, fully God and yet fully man. The full, holy, infinite, perfectly just, well-deserved wrath of our infinite God was something we had earned for ourselves. And yet—
Remember Romans 3:25–26, where Christ is presented as the **propitiation** (that is, the sacrifice that satisfies God’s righteous wrath against sin):
“whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith. This was to show God’s righteousness, because in his divine forbearance he had passed over former sins. It was to show his righteousness at the present time, so that he might be just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus.” (Romans 3:25–26, ESV)
This peace is the result of that propitiation. We now stand—not striving, but resting—in a grace secured by Christ’s finished work.
This is **Sola Gratia** (grace alone—the unmerited favor of God), and **Sola Fide** (faith alone—trusting solely in Christ’s work rather than our own efforts)—by which we receive this peace. It is not our merit, but God’s mercy and Christ’s obedience credited to us.
John Calvin captures this beautifully:
“We have peace with God not by our own merit but by Christ’s mediation.”
“But Paul doesn’t stop at peace. He presses further—perhaps even uncomfortably so. For the next fruit of justification is found not in ease or comfort, but in the furnace of affliction.
**II. The Fruit of Justification in Suffering: Hope That Does Not Put Us to Shame (Romans 5:3–5)**
Paul moves from peace and access to a paradox:
“Not only that, but we rejoice in our sufferings, knowing that suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character, and character produces hope, and hope does not put us to shame, because God’s love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been given to us.” (Romans 5:3–5, ESV)
How can that be? The world flees suffering. Our flesh resists it. But the justified saint walks a different path. He sees the flames of trial not as punishment, but as purposed fire—the forge of God’s refining love. I can’t tell you how many times I’ve heard people say, “Circumstances were hard, so I knew God didn’t want me to do it.” That’s based in the illogic of the prosperity gospel. Just once I want to hear, “Things were hard, so I knew my enemy wanted to stop me, but God prevailed.”
We rejoice in suffering, not because we enjoy pain, but because we know its purpose. “Suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character, and character produces hope.” This is the hope that has roots, not in circumstances, but in the covenant. A hope that will never put us to shame, because it is anchored in the love of God, “poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been given to us.”
But what of the moments when affliction makes God feel distant? What of the shadows that sometimes fall over the soul? Here, the Puritan Richard Sibbes offers a word of comfort:
“Measure not God’s love and favour by your own feeling. The sun shines as clearly in the darkest day as it does in the brightest. The difference is not in the sun, but in some clouds which hinder the manifestation of the light thereof.”
The sun has not stopped shining. Justification has not been revoked. God’s love has not cooled. It is not the covenant that changes—but our perception, which suffering often clouds. Yet even in that obscured moment, the justified saint remains in the sunshine of God’s steadfast love, though their tears may keep them from seeing it.
The love that justifies does not abandon in trial. The same love that satisfied wrath is the love that sustains us in weakness. And the Spirit—God’s own seal—whispers it still: “You are Mine. You are loved. Christ has died for you.”
But indeed, He loves us too much to leave us as He found us. His love chips away at the wrong things in us through trials and difficulties. God is carving away the useless things and making us a better, more useful, more beautiful creation—better suited for His purposes.
*“Love like a hurricane, indeed—who can resist its power, and who would dare wish to?”* Even the best of us could never hope to succeed. God’s love overwhelms, not with sentimentality, but with substance. The kind that holds you when everything else falls apart. The kind that refines and roots and readies you for eternity.
Or as the 1689 London Baptist Confession puts it:
“The most wise, righteous, and gracious God doth oftentimes leave for a season His own children to manifold temptations and the corruption of their own hearts… to try them, and to make them more watchful against all future occasions of sin, and for sundry other just and holy ends.” (1689 London Baptist Confession, 16.5)
Or, more succinctly in the words of Spurgeon:
“God is too good to be unkind, and He is too wise to be mistaken. And when we cannot trace His hand, we must trust His heart.”
And if ever we are tempted to doubt the love that holds us, Paul anchors our assurance in the unshakable fact of history:
“For while we were still weak, at the right time Christ died for the ungodly.” (Romans 5:6, ESV)
Not the lovely. Not the worthy. For people like me. For people like you.
**III. The Fuel of Justification: Christ Died for the Ungodly (Romans 5:6–8)**
“For while we were still weak, at the right time Christ died for the ungodly. For one will scarcely die for a righteous person—though perhaps for a good person one would dare even to die—but God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.” (Romans 5:6–8, ESV)
Not the moral, not the promising, not the cleaned-up. The ungodly. That is who Christ died for. And this isn’t a love earned—it’s a love poured out. A love shown most clearly “while we were still sinners.”
You and I will never find a greater display of love than the cross of Christ. That love did not begin the moment we believed; it began before we knew we needed it.
“God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.” (Romans 5:8, ESV)
God’s love is not the soft, permissive kind that ignores sin. It is the strong, holy, covenant-keeping kind that deals with sin fully and finally in the person of Christ. The holy and perfect eternal wrath of an infinite God demanded justice. And the holy and perfect eternal love of an infinite God provided it—in Himself.
The cross is where infinite wrath and infinite love meet. The storm of justice did not pass us by; it struck Christ instead. The hurricane of divine judgment bent Him low so that we might stand tall in grace. He was pierced that we might be pardoned. He was crushed that we might be claimed.
And what does this mean for us now?
“Since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God.” (Romans 5:9, ESV)
The wrath has been spent. The love has been poured out like a hurricane impacting a tree. The war is over.
“We also rejoice in God through our Lord Jesus Christ, through whom we have now received reconciliation.” (Romans 5:11, ESV)
**IV. From Wrath to Reconciliation: The Ongoing Work of Christ (Romans 5:9–10)**
Paul continues:
“Since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God. For if while we were enemies we were reconciled to God by the death of his Son, much more, now that we are reconciled, shall we be saved by his life.” (Romans 5:9–10, ESV)
Justification by blood secures our salvation from future wrath. The cross was not a mere token; it absorbed the full cup of God’s wrath.
The death of Christ reconciles us—re-establishes relationship—and His resurrection guarantees our ongoing salvation.
The burden of guilt and condemnation is removed—like Christian’s burden falling off at the cross in *Pilgrim’s Progress.*
The 1689 London Baptist Confession reminds us:
“Those whom God effectually calls, He freely justifies, not by infusing righteousness into them, but by pardoning their sins and by accounting and accepting their persons as righteous…” (1689 London Baptist Confession, 11.1)
Our hope is secured, and our lives are hidden with Christ:
“For you have died, and your life is hidden with Christ in God.” (Colossians 3:3, ESV)
Shielded from wrath.
“We were once enemies. But now? Now we boast in God. The war is over. The rebel has been made a son. And the courtroom gives way to the throne room—where we sing, not plead.”
**
V. Joy in God: The Response of the Justified (Romans 5:11)**
Paul concludes:
“More than that, we also rejoice in God through our Lord Jesus Christ, through whom we have now received reconciliation.” (Romans 5:11, ESV)
This joy is not merely happiness at what God has done for us but deep rejoicing in God Himself—the covenant God who redeems, reconciles, and restores.
Jonathan Edwards reminds us,
“True peace and joy arise from a sense of God’s pardoning love.”
This is the hope that fuels our sanctification, the assurance that we are God’s beloved children, and the anticipation of future glory.
**“Peace with God. Access to grace. Hope in suffering. Salvation from wrath. Reconciliation through Christ. This is no ordinary message. This is the gospel at full strength—and it leaves us in awe.”**
**Love Poured Out, Wrath Satisfied**
So then, beloved—having been justified by faith, we have peace with God.
Not a ceasefire. Not a handshake over enemy lines. Peace—true, blood-bought, wrath-absorbing, heaven-opening peace. For we must remember what stood between us and God: not mere misunderstanding, but divine wrath. Not a moment of frustration, but the holy and eternal wrath of a perfect and infinite God. The wrath of God is not a divine tantrum. It is not arbitrary, capricious, or cruel. It is His pure revulsion against all that contradicts His holiness. It is justice in its most radiant, terrifying perfection. It is the burning holiness of the One whose eyes are too pure to look upon evil, whose throne is established in righteousness, and whose law demands perfect obedience—not because He is a tyrant, but because He is holy.And His wrath is eternal because He is eternal. Infinite because He is infinite.
As A.W. Pink put it: “The wrath of God is the revulsion of His being against that which is the contradiction of His holiness.”We dare not diminish that wrath, for in doing so we diminish the cross. But see, beloved—that very same eternity, that very same infinity, that same blazing holiness—is also the measure of His love.
His love, too, is holy. His love, too, is infinite. And just as His wrath is not fleeting or shallow, neither is His mercy. He does not love in half-measures. He does not extend grace with a guarded hand. He pours. Like rain in a hurricane. He overflows. He gives the Son. What kind of love bears the full fury of infinite wrath? Only infinite love. What kind of righteousness could satisfy perfect justice? Only a perfect Christ. “Love’s like a hurricane, I am a tree, Bending beneath the weight of His wind and mercy.” This love does not merely comfort—it conquers. It does not merely feel—it fulfills. This is not sentimental affection—it is holy affection, forged in eternity, proven at Calvary, and poured out into our hearts by the Holy Spirit.
And when we truly behold this love—when the gospel ceases to be theory and becomes thunder in our bones—then, and only then, can we say:
“When all of a sudden, I am unaware of these afflictions eclipsed by glory,
And I realize just how beautiful You are,
And how great Your affections are for me.”
This is not poetic exaggeration.
This is gospel reality.

“But God shows His love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.”
Romans 5:8. You were not lovely when He loved you. You were not safe when He saved you.You were still a sinner. Still an enemy. Still under wrath. And yet—Christ died for you.**Not because of your goodness, but because of His.**
John Flavel wrote,
*“He who spared not His own Son… must have had a great design of love toward us. O the admirable love of God, who would not only reconcile His enemies, but do it at such a cost!”*
So look up, child of God. You are not under wrath. You are under love. You are not condemned. You are carried. You are not forsaken. You are filled.
And all of this—**sola gratia, sola fide, solus Christus**—is not because of your grip on God, but because of **His grasp on you**.
“Christ died for us.” That is the gospel at full strength. And it is enough.
Brothers and sisters, we are not hoping for peace—we have it. We are not begging for access—we stand in grace. We are not left guessing at God’s love—we have seen it at Calvary. The justified life is not a tightrope walk—it is a Spirit-secured, Christ-purchased, God-glorifying reality.
And so *“We bend beneath His mercy - Not **crushed, but** carried.* *Not **ashamed, but** assured.* Perhaps struck down, but certainly not destroyed. We are *Justified. Loved. Reconciled.”*
“Oh, the deep, deep love of Jesus, Vast, unmeasured, boundless, free— Rolling as a mighty ocean In its fullness over me.”

Love too great to leave us where we are. Which is a source of great hope for those that don’t yet know Jesus. He loved you enough to die for you, on the cross. to absorb your portion of God’s wrath. 

He knew you better than you know your self, and even knowing the dark corners of all the darkest closets in your soul, he still chose to die for you. He still chose to satisfy the wrath of God.
