# John 11 — Lazarus

Story about Becca. Bridge Church Pastor Nathan. Cancer neuro blastoma. In her breast, in her kidney, in her liver, and in her brain. His baby girl is probably going to die. What is his response?

> Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds, for you know that the testing of your faith produces steadfastness. And let steadfastness have its full effect, that you may be perfect and complete, lacking in nothing.
> — James 1:2–4

May we be as faithful when the lives of our loved ones are on the line.

Its easy, well easier to consider your own mortality. I went to Pensylvania a few years back and as part of induction into their missionary corps I went through a week long job interview and personality test. First night they started to put us into boxes. What animal are you most like. Cormorant. Trinity – coming to grips with my own mortality.

The second to last day my team member, my friend was taken hostage. Its one thing to consider your own mortality. Its quite another to have a friend being cut up into pieces and delivered to your door. We seasoned believers are ok with giving our lives to and for the Lord, but what about the lives of our kids? Our grandkids? If their death disturbs you, and it should. What about their salvation? Do you know where they are going to go? If you don't, and you aren't bothered by that, then you have a little bit of a heart problem there. Come talk to me later.

That brings us to our passage tonight. John 11:1-53. Were going to be reading about Lazarus.

## Follow Christ Regardless of What May Come (11:1–16)

Vs 4 This illness does not lead to death. It is for the glory of God, so that the Son of God may be glorified through it.

Jesus knew in advance that Lazarus was going to die, and be resurrected. Notice vs 5 Jesus loved Martha and her sister and Lazarus.

Jesus had been going around doing miracles, challenging the authority of the religious leaders, and some thought even Rome. The more he did to demonstrate that God was real, and he was God's son, the more the leaders reviled him. Last week, we read in 10:39 that "Again they sought to arrest him, but he escaped from their hands."

They were playing a game of whack-a-mole. Have you ever played whack-a-mole? Its one of those games wyley and I almost always play at Stop & Play. Except one of them you have to whack alligators, and for the other you have to hit prarie dogs. Both of us working together usually get a pretty good score. But the Pharisees all of them working together still weren't able to catch and arrest Christ. He was public enemy #1. If the FBI had been around back then he would have been #1 on the 10 most wanted list.

The disciples were afraid, but catch Jesus response – when we walk in the light, we don't have to worry about stumbling. (vs 9). In other words, we are doing what God wants, we are walking in accordance to his will, and so come what may, I still will follow.

Check out Thomas's reaction in vs 16 – they had no hope that Christ would survive this trip, but they were still willing to go with him. Better to die with their Lord, he seems to be saying, than to die apart from him. The disciples were willing to follow Christ regardless of what may come. Just as Jesus was willing to follow his father no matter what may come.

## Jesus Wept (11:35)

I don't want to get too bogged down here. But I think we should definitely look at vs 35 for a minute. Can someone read that for me? ESV says "Jesus wept."

Jesus knew why he was there, we saw that back in Vs 4 – he was going to raise his dear friend from the dead. Yet he wept. I think a lot of people see God as one who doesn't understand what its like to be human. He doesn't know what its like to be tempted or experience loss. God doesn't understand the human condition people have said. That's ridiculous. Christ came to earth to be incarnated into a man so that he could save us, but through that process he felt what it was like to be tempted. Not just tempted, tempted by Satan himself. You and I will likely never experience temptation from the devil himself, but Christ did, and Christ overcame. Here in this passage he experienced deep loss when a man he loved like a brother died. One commentator, I think wrongly, wrote that Jesus was crying happy tears, because he knew they were about to worship Him when he raised lazarus from the dead. That too I think is ridiculous. Emotions can be illogical. I think he was feeling the loss, even for a short time, and in conjunction with the mourning going on around him he had compassion on them and wept. It was a moving moment, and Christ is compassionate and was moved. He wept. Knowing he would see his brother again he wept.

DiPronio's daughters in the wake of their grandfathers death. No tears, happy happy happy. Trying to be "super spiritual". Remember when we go through loss its ok to cry, because Jesus wept. We know we will see them again, and so did Christ, and he wept.

## Lazarus as a Picture of Christ — and of Us

So not only should we follow Christ regardless of what it costs, not only does Christ understand and has shared in our emotions, he too weeps. Not only all that, but this is also somewhat of a picture of Christ.

Both resurrected by the power of God given to Christ. Both were dead for at least part of four days. Lazarus had been in the tomb four days (John 11:17). Christ was buried for three days before his resurrection.

Lazarus was also like us. He had a sickness that no matter what he tries, will end up in his death. So too apart from Christ we have a terminal illness – namely sin – that will ultimately result in our death.

Like us, Lazarus needed someone capable of intervening on his behalf. He wasn't capable of saving himself. We are incapable of reaching God on our own, we needed a mediator. A propitiation.

Like us Jesus Christ fully man and fully God came, finding us fully dead (and stinking) he resurrected us.

Eph 2:1-4 calls us sons of disobedience. Says we are by nature children of wrath. Says we (apart from Christ) are dead in the trespasses and sins.

A couple of years ago I was on my way home from somewhere, and found my road cordoned off with police tape. A bunch of cop cars, a coroner, a crime scene unit, a couple of dogs, and the whole 9 yards. The police force was out in force, and blocking my yard. So I drove on the grass, and pulled into mom and dads driveway. Officer came over, wanted to know who I was, and why I was there. I handed him my drivers liscense to prove I belonged.

Come to find out a girl had been murdered in the woods. Apparently someone downwind noticed the smell and called 911. She had been there for several days. She was totally incapable of life. She was exactly like we were apart from God. Turned out that her boyfriend killed her, they caught him, and he's going to be in jail for a long time. But just like the girl, just like Lazarus, just like that little baby girl facing death at 10 weeks old, apart from Christ we are worse than dead, we are bound for an eternity in hell. An eternity separated from God.

## Gospel Invitation

For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God, we are all sick. None of us can do anything about it. Apart from Christ you're like the zombies on that popular tv show the walking dead. But praise God he loved us too much to leave us there.

Maybe you're here tonight and you've come to realize you are one of the walking dead. Maybe you have been to church for a while, maybe you're embarrassed, but there is nothing to be embarrassed about. We love each of you. We want to see you again in heaven. Don't trade eternity for a moment of embarrassment.

Some of you, maybe you've gone back to rolling in the filth of the world. Tonights the night to repent and return to God.

In a minute, the praise team is going to come up here and sing a song, and while they are singing I want everyone to close their eyes and bow their heads.

While he's singing if you are sitting there stinking because you are dead like Lazarus, or because you as a Christian have been revisiting the sins of your past, I want you to raise your hand, and after the song, we will all as a group say the sinners prayer together.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Pastoral Warmth With a Thin Exegetical Floor and a Shallow Gospel Center

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A heartfelt pastoral opening of John 11 that connects suffering and Christ's compassion with genuine warmth while falling short in rigorous exposition, doctrinal precision, and conscience-searching application.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test:** The sermon's governing thesis is loosely "Follow Christ regardless of what may come, because he understands our grief and has the power to raise the dead." This is *present* in the text but does not represent John 11's main point, which is the self-declaration of Jesus as "the resurrection and the life" (v. 25) and the escalating sign that drives toward the cross (vv. 45–53). The sermon's main thesis is an application derived from a supporting detail, not the text's own climactic argument. The Scopus Test is marginal — the sermon does not contradict the passage, but its main point is not the passage's main point.

**Authority Test:** The preacher does not edit the text offensively, but he moves past the hard material quickly (particularly John 11:25–26 and the faith demand Jesus makes). He does not soften explicitly, but he avoids the hardest edge of the text.

**Berean Gate Result:** Marginal Pass. The sermon is text-adjacent rather than text-governed. It does not fail outright, but it drifts from the passage's own climax and argument.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — Christ is present but not *necessary* to the sermon's argument in its full force.** The sermon's typological section (Lazarus as picture of Christ and of us) is the strongest theological move, but the Person and finished work of Christ are not proclaimed with the force that renders Him irreplaceable to the argument. The gospel invitation section partially addresses this, but it arrives late and thin.

**Flag 4 — Application is substantially generic.** "Follow Christ regardless of what may come" and "it's okay to cry" and "raise your hand if you're dead like Lazarus" are not differentiated address. The self-righteous churchgoer, the doubter, the grieving parent, and the nominal attender are never addressed with distinct specificity.

**Flag 5 — The exhortation is present but reductive.** The call to response (raise your hand, say the sinner's prayer together) is a mechanical altar-call structure rather than a summoning of the will to believe and obey. The congregation is directed to a procedure, not cornered by truth.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10):* 6/10. The sermon draws from the text — specifically vv. 4, 9, 16, 35, 17 — but skips the passage's theological and narrative center entirely. John 11:25–26 ("I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live") receives no treatment whatsoever. This is the climax of the entire chapter and the interpretive key. Its absence is a significant exegetical failure. Verses 1–53 are cited as the preaching unit but fewer than 10 verses are actually engaged.
- *Contextual Awareness (5):* 3/5. The connection to ch. 10 (the arrest attempt) is noted, which shows some awareness of narrative flow. But the redemptive-historical context — John 11 as the final and greatest sign before the Passion, the sign that seals Jesus' death sentence (vv. 45–53) — is never mentioned.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5):* 2/5. No original language engagement. Interpretive assertions are made without demonstration (e.g., "he wept because of compassion, not happy tears" — this is a valid reading but it is asserted, not argued).
- *Logical Faithfulness (5):* 3/5. The Lazarus typology section is a legitimate move. No serious eisegesis, but ideas are more imported (the James 1 opening, the James passage as governing framework) than discovered from the text's own argument.

*Fallen Condition Focus:* Partially named — sin as terminal illness is stated. But the FCF is never located precisely in the text. The sermon answers "we are all dead sinners" but not "why does *this* passage exist — what specific human condition is John 11 addressing?" John 11 addresses the human terror of death and the demand to believe that Jesus is the resurrection before the evidence is visible — that more specific FCF is not named.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7):* 4/7. The typological move is genuine and useful. But the Great "I AM" declaration of v. 25–26 is bypassed, which means the sermon never arrives at the text's own Christological apex. Christ is present in the sermon — but not at full height.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3):* 1/3. No redemptive-historical framing. The sign as escalation toward the cross, the connection to the Passover, the meaning of resurrection in the context of the coming resurrection of Christ — none of this is surfaced.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4):* 3/4. The doctrine of original sin and total spiritual death is stated with reasonable force ("dead in trespasses and sins," Eph. 2:1–4). Propitiation is mentioned but only by name, without explanation. The nature of justification and how one is united to the risen Christ are underdeveloped.
- *Confessional Alignment (4):* 3/4. No contradiction of confessional theology. The use of Eph. 2 is a solid doctrinal move. The altar-call structure and the "sinner's prayer together" imply a decisionist framework that sits in mild tension with a confessional understanding of regeneration preceding faith, but this is latent rather than explicit.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 8/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (5):* 3/5. The gospel is present — sin, death, Christ's intervention, resurrection life. But penal substitution is not stated. How Christ actually accomplishes what Lazarus' resurrection pictures is not explained. The gospel invitation is more "raise your hand" than "here is the finished work of Christ, believe it."
- *Necessity of Christ (5):* 3/5. Christ is necessary to the sermon's typology but not argued as necessary in a way that would arrest a self-righteous moralist. The Synagogue Test: a devout Jew familiar with resurrection hope and the necessity of God's gracious intervention could hear much of this sermon without being confronted by the scandal of the incarnate, crucified, and risen Christ as the *only* mediator.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (5):* 2/5. The sermon never arrives at the cross. The communion section closes with 1 Corinthians 11 but the connection between Lazarus' resurrection and Christ's atoning death is not drawn in the sermon body. The movement is toward the resurrection without the cross as its foundation.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15**

- *Heart Penetration (5):* 3/5. The opening illustration (baby Becca, cancer) is emotionally arresting and genuinely pastoral. The murdered girl illustration is jarring but serves a point. These land with some force. But the heart is reached emotionally more than it is searched theologically.
- *Specificity (5):* 2/5. Applications are largely universal: "it's okay to cry," "follow Christ regardless," "don't trade eternity for a moment of embarrassment." These are true but not specific to any particular person or situation in the congregation.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (5):* 3/5. The sermon does make some distinctions: those who are "dead like Lazarus," backsliders "revisiting the sins of the past," and believers needing to know where their children stand. This is a partial Perkins exercise. But the hardened churchgoer, the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled, and the genuinely grieving believer needing comfort beyond "it's okay to cry" are not separately addressed.

*Perkins categories addressed:* The ignorant-but-teachable (gospel invitation), the backslider (partial), believers facing grief (partial). The knowledgeable-but-unhumbled and the hardened are not addressed. 3/7 categories partially reached.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 4/8**

- *Call to Response (4):* 2/4. A response is called for — raise your hand, say the sinner's prayer. But this is a procedure, not a summons. The congregation is directed to an action without being compelled by a truth-declaration. The "therefore" moment is procedural ("raise your hand") rather than proclamatory ("Believe on the Lord Jesus Christ, and you will be saved — and you must believe *now*").
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (4):* 2/4. The sermon does not send the church out. The closing is inward-facing (sinner's prayer, communion) without any vision for what the congregation does with this truth on Monday.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (4):* 3/4. The outline is legible: Follow Christ regardless / Jesus Wept / Lazarus as picture / Gospel Invitation. A listener can track it.
- *Progression (3):* 2/3. The sermon moves forward but the sections do not build to a single climax. The typological section (section 3) is the strongest and most theologically dense — it should be the culmination, but it precedes rather than anchors the gospel invitation.
- *Unity (2):* 1/2. Three distinct themes compete: (1) follow Christ amid suffering, (2) Christ understands grief, (3) Lazarus as picture of sin/death/resurrection. These are related but not unified under one dominant idea. The sermon's thesis sentence cannot be stated cleanly.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**

- *Relevance (2):* 2/2. The illustrations are drawn from the preacher's actual life and congregation: the baby Becca story, the Pennsylvania missionary training, the murdered girl on the road, DiPronio's daughters, whack-a-mole at Stop & Play. These are local and specific — exactly right.
- *Clarity (2):* 1/2. The Pennsylvania/hostage illustration is vivid but its connection to the sermon's argument is not clear — it pivots to children's salvation without a clean landing. The murdered girl illustration lands with force but may distract from the point by its graphic nature.
- *Memorability (1):* 0/1. No single illustration crystallizes the sermon's main idea in a way the congregation will carry home next week.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2):* 2/2. Yes — this is clearly a preached manuscript. Oral cadence, direct address, conversational idiom. "Can someone read that for me?" "Have you ever played whack-a-mole?" These read from a live voice.
- *Conviction on the Page (2):* 1/2. The manuscript has warmth but not theological density at the moments of greatest pressure. The gospel section is the least forceful writing in the sermon — at the moment of greatest theological weight, the prose flattens.
- *Cadence and Breath (1):* 0/1. No climactic compression. The sermon does not build toward a sentence that roars. Sentences are uniformly conversational throughout — there is no moment of deliberate, short-form declaration that drives the nail.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Partly Christ — the typological section points outward. But the grief illustrations and the opening are more anthropocentric than theocentric. The answer is "mixed, leaning toward self."
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about Him, primarily. The sermon reports God's compassion and power without creating encounter with His holiness or His glory.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Emotionally warmed and partially informed. True conviction — the kind that strips self-trust — is absent. True comfort — the kind that rests on a stated and proclaimed gospel — is underdeveloped.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Pastoral warmth and local illustration.** The opening illustration about baby Becca is arresting and real. The use of personal, congregation-specific illustrations (Stop & Play, DiPronio's daughters) creates genuine connection. This is a preacher who knows his people.
2. **The Lazarus typology section is the theological heart and it is legitimate.** "Like us, Lazarus needed someone capable of intervening on his behalf... A propitiation." The use of Eph. 2:1–4 to ground the illustration is the sermon's single strongest expository move.
3. **The sermon distinguishes between the unbeliever and the backslider** in the gospel invitation, showing at least a partial pastoral instinct for Perkins' taxonomy. "Some of you, maybe you've gone back to rolling in the filth of the world. Tonight's the night to repent and return to God."

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **John 11:25–26 is completely absent.** The entire sermon is built on John 11 but never arrives at Jesus' defining self-declaration: "I am the resurrection and the life. Whoever believes in me, though he die, yet shall he live, and everyone who lives and believes in me shall never die. Do you believe this?" This verse is the interpretive key to the whole chapter, the climax of the narrative, and the text's own gospel summons. Preaching John 11 without v. 25 is like preaching the Prodigal Son without the father running. The sermon skips its own apex.
2. **The gospel invitation is procedural, not proclamatory.** The shift to "raise your hand... we will all say the sinner's prayer together" reduces the gospel summons to a social procedure. The finished work of Christ — His atoning death, His bodily resurrection as the basis of our justification — is never announced with declarative force. The congregation is directed to a hand-raise without being given a cross to trust.
3. **The structure fragments the text's single argument.** John 11 is a unified narrative with a single climax: the final, greatest sign that Jesus is the resurrection and the life, escalating toward the crucifixion. The sermon divides this into three loosely related themes (discipleship cost, grief, typology) that do not build toward each other. The text's own narrative drive is lost.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that he has preached *around* the sermon's most powerful verse (11:25) rather than from it. The "follow Christ regardless" thesis is true and useful but it is *application derived from a secondary character* (Thomas) rather than the text's own declaration spoken by Christ. The sermon's governing thesis belongs to Thomas; the text's governing declaration belongs to Jesus. The preacher is preaching the supporting character's response instead of the main character's proclamation.

Additionally, the sinner's prayer structure may be so familiar to this preacher's tradition that its theological and pastoral limitations are invisible to him. The congregation is brought to a procedure at the moment they most need a proclamation.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The illustration section feels like the sermon's strength — the stories are vivid, local, and emotionally resonant. But emotional resonance is not applicatory force. The murdered girl on the road produces visceral response, but the sermon does not then press the conscience *theologically* — it does not ask "and how have you deadened yourself to the smell of your own sin?" The illustrations move the feelings without then moving the will. The emotional warmth of the preacher's voice creates a sense of pastoral depth that the theological content does not fully support.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

John 11:25–26 is the missed opportunity. Jesus does not wait until Lazarus is raised to make his declaration — he makes it *before the stone is rolled away*, while Lazarus is still dead. This is the text's own homiletical structure: the Lord demands faith *before the evidence appears*. "Do you believe this?" is the question Jesus puts to Martha — and through the narrative, to every hearer. This is a profound pastoral and evangelistic opportunity: the sermon could have cornered every person in the room with the same question Jesus put to Martha: *Do you believe this? Not after you see a miracle. Now. While the stone is still in place. Do you believe this?* That moment — which is the text's own dramatic and theological center — is entirely missing from the sermon.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Make John 11:25–26 the sermon's spine.** Rebuild the outline so that every section answers the question "Why should you believe that Jesus is the resurrection and the life?" Section 1 (Thomas) answers: *because he is worth following to death*. Section 2 (the weeping) answers: *because he is not indifferent to your grief*. Section 3 (the typology) answers: *because you are exactly the kind of dead man he came to raise*. Then v. 25–26 closes the case: *and he has declared it with his own mouth. Do you believe this?*
2. **Proclaim the cross before the invitation.** Before the gospel invitation, add one paragraph: "How does Jesus have the authority and power to raise the dead? Because he himself went through death as our substitute. He took the penalty our sin earned. He bore the wrath we deserved. And the Father raised him — the first of many. Because he lives, those who are united to him by faith will live also." This is not optional decoration — it is the foundation of everything the typology section promises.
3. **Replace the sinner's prayer procedure with a direct address summons.** Instead of "raise your hand and we will say the sinner's prayer together," close with Jesus' own question to the room: *"Do you believe this?"* Then address distinct hearers: the one who has believed for years and needs to believe it afresh; the one who is sitting stinking and knows it; the one who has been trusting their church attendance. Summon them to *believe a specific thing*, not perform a specific action.
4. **Cut or reframe the Pennsylvania/hostage illustration.** It introduces the theme of suffering and a friend's danger, but then pivots abruptly to children's salvation, creating a logical gap the listener must bridge unaided. Tighten the transition or replace with a cleaner illustration native to the text.
5. **Add direct address to the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled.** There is no word in this sermon for the person who knows all the right answers, has been in church for years, and has never been arrested by grace. A sentence like "Some of you know everything I've said tonight and it has moved you not at all. That is a more dangerous position than the person who's never heard the gospel" would sharpen the applicatory force considerably.

The glory is glimpsed but not inhabited.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

John 11 is narrative gospel text — the rubric's categories apply well here. Narrative sermons stress the exposition category differently than epistolary sermons; the preacher is tasked with surfacing the theological argument *through* the story rather than from a propositional structure. The rubric bends slightly in that "exegetical clarity" in a narrative sermon is less about Greek word studies and more about identifying the narrative's own argument (which here is: the sign escalates, the demand is faith, and the cost of the sign is the cross). The rubric handles this adequately. No genre strain that distorts the evaluation.
