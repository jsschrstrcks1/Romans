We're going to be in Proverbs chapter 13 this week — page ________ in the large print pew Bibles. While you're turning there I want to talk to you about generation gaps.

I heard Paul Washer preach on this not too long ago. He mentioned that he had been talking to one of his friends — another Baptist preacher — who had been telling him about this book.

Before we go any further: Paul Washer has his bachelor's from a state university where he studied philosophy and logic formally. He then went on to one of the six Southern Baptist seminaries — Southeastern's sister school, Southwestern, in Texas — where he earned a Master of Divinity. As part of his M.Div. he studied logic and philosophy again at a higher level, along with four years of theology and deep Bible study. After that he spent ten years on the mission field in Peru, then started a missions organization to help send Peruvian missionaries around the world. He serves now in leadership of that organization, and as a preacher.

Anyway, his friend said, "Hey, I have this book I want you to read." So after a while it finally came up on his reading list, and he began skimming it. Despite his extensive studies in logic, philosophy, and theology he was still having a hard time with it — the logic was higher level, the theology much deeper than most theology texts go today. So the story goes, he stopped to get a glass of water, and when he came back the book had flipped closed to the back cover. There was a picture of several children and an old-fashioned looking schoolmarm. He read the preface and found out this was a reprint of a book that had been used to train Puritan children in primary school.

**[FLAG: What is the book? Identify before preaching. Could be the Westminster Shorter Catechism, the New England Primer, or another Puritan primer — confirm the specific title.]**

He said he was shocked to find out that in the days of the Puritans, 7–12 year olds were studying the deep questions of Scripture with higher-level logic than what is being taught in seminary today.

He said 7-year-olds were being taught about impeccability versus peccability — could Christ have sinned if He wanted to? That kind of deep, esoteric theology is what these little children were studying. And it struck him just how great the generation gap is between us and the Puritans.

I asked Google and found out that we barely talk about the Puritans these days compared to the end of the 1800s and the beginning of the 1900s. The amount of time we spend studying and talking about what the Puritans had to say has dropped by over 700%.

**[FLAG: Verify the 700% figure and what it actually measures — Google Ngram data, a study? Don't state this as fact without confirming the source and methodology.]**

Now I don't agree with everything the Puritans had to say, but they said a lot of good things. One of the primary questions on their hearts and minds was how the Word of God applies to every facet of life. Things like that are worth thinking about and studying Scripture to find answers to.

I read in another place that the average child spends between 3 and 7 hours playing video games and/or watching television after school. By contrast, it was required that Puritan children — once they could read — would spend at least three hours studying Scripture each day.

**[FLAG: Both statistics need citations. The screen time figure is plausible but cite the source. The 3-hour Puritan study requirement needs a verifiable source.]**

The level and depth of theological education of the 1600s Puritan child was as great — or greater, according to Washer — than his own modern 21st century pastoral seminary education.

What we teach our seminarians today is less comprehensive than what we taught 7–12 year olds a few hundred years ago.

What does that mean for the average Christian then? How do you think you or I would measure up compared to 7-year-olds from a few hundred years ago?

Before we open God's Word, I want to talk about one more thing.

The decline of Puritanism in our country correlates to something else — something not inherently evil in itself, but something that at a certain point began unpinning education from the Word of God. Namely, the public school system.

The public school system has been a good thing in many ways. More people have access to education than perhaps would have under a different paradigm. It has leveled the playing field in a lot of ways. And I recognize that just because the rise of public school correlates to the decline of Puritanism does not necessarily mean one caused the other. But I think it is important to note: as public schools increased in prevalence, Puritanism decreased. I think this is a symptom of a deeper problem. As a country we began to shift our focus from what God was telling us about Himself and the things found in His Word, to the things that man had figured out. We began to see the revelations we were able to learn through observation and experimentation as more important for kids to know. Public school is the mouthpiece for the priorities of a people. It's not necessarily good or bad on its own — but it very much reflects where we are as a nation. When that's a good thing, it's a very good thing. And when it's not, well — you see where we are today.

---

Just so you don't think I hate science and think that learning is bad: I've spent decades after high school pursuing education, some of which was in the sciences. I loved chemistry. My chemistry professor was one of my favorites. Even when we made mistakes, if we could explain why the mistake happened and what we observed as a result, he would still give us a good grade — because we had learned.

Case in point: the first lab, we had to capture hydrogen and oxygen, stick lit matches into containers of each, and at one point put one container over or under the other. If done right, when you stuck the match between the two open jars it would extinguish itself — lacking the proper stoichiometry. However, one kid did it the opposite way. Instead of the gases staying separate, they mixed, and when he stuck the match between the jars, the one on top exploded across the room into the wall. He jumped up and my professor shouted: "Yes! Whoever did that just got an A — if you can tell me why it happened."

Science is an important part of our lives and it should be studied. But it needs to be kept in its proper perspective — kneeling at the cross of Christ. Scripture is not a science text, but where it speaks of science it is the most authoritative source we have.

Scripture refers to the earth as a circle or sphere thousands of years before the earliest Greek philosophers speculated about its shape.

**[FLAG: The verse is Isaiah 40:22 — "It is he who sits above the circle of the earth" (ESV). The Hebrew word is חוּג (chug), which can mean circle/vault/sphere. Eratosthenes (~240 BC) calculated the earth's circumference; Aristotle (~350 BC) argued for a spherical earth. Isaiah 40 is dated ~700 BC — that is a legitimate point. BUT the original draft said "before Isaac Newton wrote a paper about his theory" — Newton's Principia is about gravity, not earth's roundness. Rephrase to reference the Greek philosophers, not Newton.]**

When Scripture speaks on a subject, it is authoritative. Sometimes we're just not listening.

Imagine what the world would be like if instead of playing Fortnite for ten hours, our kids spent ten hours studying the Word of God. What would our next generation look like? Wouldn't that be a worthy goal? Parents and grandparents, aunts and uncles — it all starts with your example in the home.

Let's read: Proverbs 13.

*"A wise son hears his father's instruction, but a scoffer does not listen to rebuke."* (Proverbs 13:1, ESV)

---

I was watching Live PD the other night — the last little bit of it. They had a deputy from Pasco on, and his K-9 partner "Shep." They were talking about a case where the deputy had pulled over a kid on a motorcycle who had been popping wheelies going down US-19. So the officer pulled the kid over. He looked like he was 13 or so — pimply faced, voice cracking. The kid had built the motorcycle from parts that were going to be tossed out. He repaired them, welded the frame together, and completely built the bike from scratch. But popping wheelies on 19 is dangerous and against the law. In the process of the stop the officer found a small amount of marijuana in a baggie in the kid's sock.

The deputy said he had a choice at that point. He could charge the kid with felony possession, or he could order him to participate in a diversion program that involved community service and staying clean of drugs and alcohol. The deputy chose the diversion program, and made the kid call his mom and explain what he'd been doing.

Later in the show the deputy was asked how often the diversion program works. He replied: only about 1 in 10 kids follow through with completing the requirements. The kids who fail end up with felony charges and time in a detention center.

He explained that kids just don't think adults know what they're talking about. So when an adult or a cop tells them drugs are bad, they say in their heads, *you just don't know, you don't understand. I KNOW. I UNDERSTAND. And for me, this is what's good.* And they continue down the same paths that so many kids do — and end up strung out on drugs and alcohol.

---

It's that attitude — that *we* know better than our parents — that is so troublesome. And it's an attitude fed and fostered by our culture.

When I was a kid we watched programs about good versus evil. Superman versus supervillains. Optimus Prime and the Autobots versus the Decepticons. Batman and Robin versus basically everyone. But today the cartoons show parents as idiots and kids as the wise, worldly, knowledgeable ones. Kids are told their parents just don't understand, so you have to go out and try it yourself. Sneak out, go to that party, drink that, smoke that, try that — see if it works for you. Because — and I hate this phrase — "YOLO." You only live once.

---

So not only have we shifted away from listening to God's Word as our authoritative source and toward the knowledge of man, but we've shifted away from listening to our elders.

This started in the late 1950s and early 1960s. Young people were deciding that drugs and sex were fun and that their parents clearly didn't know anything about either. We had the sexual revolution — to the greatest extent to that point in history, we began to have generational gaps. Young people were convinced they knew more than their parents about everything.

This is memorialized in their music, their art, and even their politics. Remember the protest music of the late '60s? The song that stands out in my mind is "War" by Edwin Starr. He says: *"War — huh — good God, y'all — what is it good for? Absolutely nothing. Say it again! War ain't nothing but a heartbreaker — only good for the undertaker."*

There is so much good in our passage this morning I had trouble picking out just a few things to talk about. I thought about talking about the verse about the rod — *"Whoever spares the rod hates his son, but he who loves him is diligent to discipline him"* (Proverbs 13:24, ESV). **[FLAG: "Spare the rod, spoil the child" is a paraphrase, not a Bible quote. The actual ESV text is above — use the real verse.]**

It's easier sometimes to indulge our kids than to fight with them, to help them think and behave rightly. Or the verse about the righteous being content while fools continue to pursue the lusts of their flesh.

But ultimately I felt like the Lord was leading us to close with verse 20. Can someone read that for me?

*"Whoever walks with the wise becomes wise, but the companion of fools will suffer harm."* (Proverbs 13:20, ESV)

We ultimately become like those we spend time with. The influences we allow into our hearts and minds affect our hearts and minds over the long term. My mom says, "Garbage in, garbage out." And I think that's true. Worse than affecting your own life, the influences you allow in — and the control they have over your heart and mind — will affect your kids, and how they see and interact with the world.

How do you want them to be? How do you want them to think about these things?

Because in order for them to see things differently — in order to slow or stop this cultural slide we've been on for centuries — we are going to have to BE different. We're going to have to THINK different. We're going to have to LIVE different. We're going to have to return to the Book the Puritans loved so much. And we can recover it the same way we lost it — one household at a time. Because it all starts with you.

Be a strong Christian who loves and respects God and His Word. Be one who loves your Savior and puts Christ first in your life. Raise strong, smart kids who are tough enough to be independent from the world but dependent upon Christ. Raise them to love God as much as — or more than — you do.

Murder among the Puritans was all but unheard of. You know why? The Bible says not to. Adultery? Almost completely unheard of. Why? The Bible says not to.

Not just because the Bible says not to — but because of WHY the Bible says not to. Because these things are sin. Because sin separates us from God.
