## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date:** 2026-03-21
**Passage:** John 6:43–65
**Evaluator:** Thus Says the Lord Rubric v1.0

---

### 1. Verdict Title
A Sincere Defense of Grace That Preaches Total Depravity More Than Sola Gratia — Solid Convictions, Rough Execution

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warmly earnest sermon on sola gratia that correctly roots the doctrine in John 6 and Ephesians 2 and lands several genuine theological punches, but is built more as an extended exhortation to examine oneself than as an exposition of the text’s own argument about grace.

### 3. Berean Gate
**Scopus Test: MARGINAL PASS**
The sermon’s thesis — that salvation is entirely by grace, that man contributes nothing, and that apart from God’s drawing no one comes to Christ — is genuinely present in John 6:44 and 6:65, the primary verses cited. The sermon does not depart radically from this point. However, the text itself (vv. 43–65) carries a more specific argument: Jesus’ discourse on the Bread of Life, the grumbling at the offense of the incarnation and the cross, and the Christological claim that grounds election in the Father’s gift. The sermon reduces this to two verses about divine drawing without addressing the broader discourse.

**Authority Test: PASS**
The preacher holds nothing back on human depravity and divine initiative. He submits to the text’s hard claims.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Flag 3 — Christ is absent or unnecessary:** This is a close call, but Christ’s *finished work* — his death, resurrection, and substitutionary atonement — is never proclaimed. The sermon argues that grace is necessary (correct) but does not proclaim what that grace accomplishes through the cross. The closing gospel tag ("If you ask him and mean it, you will be saved") assumes the gospel rather than proclaiming it. Christ appears as the one who "died for us" in one line but is not preached as Savior with any depth.

**Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic:** The examination of self ("Is your relationship with God marked by obedience rooted in faith?") is repeated but not differentiated. The tare-among-wheat image is vivid, but no specific hearer category is addressed with pastoral precision.

**SCORE IS CAPPED AT 69.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

The intervening verses (43-64) — Jesus’ claim to be the bread from heaven, the Jews’ grumbling, the hard saying about eating flesh and drinking blood, and the disciples who turned back — receive no treatment whatsoever.

- *Logical Faithfulness (5):* 2/5 — The sermon spends more time on Ephesians 2, the BFM, the Boyce quote, and the self-examination application than on the text itself.

### 8. Core Override Questions
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* Myself — the dominant application is self-examination. Christ is invoked but not magnified.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Ideas, primarily — the doctrine of grace is explained but not felt as grace extended to the hearer in this moment.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Convicted of the possibility of self-deception, but not comforted by the grace that was supposedly the subject. A sermon on grace should leave people more grateful for grace.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)
1. **Total depravity is preached with genuine force** — The dead-body illustration, the John 6:44/65 citation, and the summary of Ephesians 2 present human inability clearly and compellingly. This is well done.
2. **Confessional grounding is solid** — The BFM quote and the doctrinal framing on election and perseverance are accurate and appropriately placed for a Reformation-anniversary sermon.
3. **The tare-among-wheat application** — The warning to comfortable cultural Christians who can "look real good and still be as lost as a goat" is exactly the kind of honest pastoral probing a grace sermon requires.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)
1. **The text is barely opened** — John 6:43-65 contains Jesus’ bread of life discourse, the grumbling at the hardness of the teaching, and the mass departure of disciples. None of this is addressed. Two verses are cited; twenty are ignored.
2. **Sola gratia is argued but not felt** — The sermon proves that grace is necessary and that we contribute nothing. But grace-as-gift, grace-as-overwhelming-beauty, grace-as-the-thing-that-makes-you-weep — this is absent. The sermon is a legal brief for grace rather than a proclamation of it.
3. **The gospel call is hollow** — "If you ask him and mean it, you will be saved" is not false, but it is theologically thin. What are you asking him? On what basis? What has he done? The invitation presupposes a gospel that was never preached.

### 11. Blind Spots
The preacher may not see that a sermon on sola gratia that ends with an extended self-examination section (are you really saved? examine your obedience) inadvertently undermines the very doctrine it is defending. Grace is proclaimed and then immediately placed under conditions of obedience-verification. The congregation may leave more anxious than grateful.

### 12. Illusion of Strength
The Billy Sunday quote and the Jonathan Edwards quote *felt* like strong content — they are authentic and forceful. But they function as borrowed theological weight rather than arising from the text’s own argument. Quotes are not exposition.

### 13. Missed Opportunity
John 6:63 — "It is the Spirit who gives life; the flesh is no help at all." This is the theological climax of the passage — the Spirit alone enlivens, the flesh contributes nothing. It is the exegetical ground for sola gratia in this very text. The sermon cited vv. 44 and 65 but missed the verse that most directly supports its own thesis.

### 14. Surgical Fixes
1. **Expose John 6:63 as the text’s own argument for sola gratia.** The sermon should pivot here: "The Spirit gives life; the flesh profits nothing. This is the reformation doctrine not as a theological system — but as the words of Jesus Himself."
2. **Preach the indicative before the imperative.** The self-examination section must be preceded by the gospel: what Christ has done, why the cross is the ground of grace, how grace comes to the dead. Then — and only then — call people to examine themselves.
3. **Replace the closing invitation with a gospel proclamation.** Three sentences: the sin, the cross, the call to faith. Then invite response.
4. **Differentiate the application.** Address the doubting believer ("Your obedience will be imperfect — that is why you need grace, not why you should doubt it"), the nominal believer ("Dead religion is not grace"), and the unbeliever separately.
5. **Remove or sharply compress the zombie illustration.** It trivializes the weighty doctrine of spiritual death.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered** — the sermon is not doctrinally cold. The passion is genuine. But the Law/Gospel balance is inverted: the sermon uses grace as the context for self-examination rather than self-examination as the context for grace. The hammer falls without the balm fully following.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
The sermon carries genuine urgency and theological seriousness, but grace-as-glory — the kind that makes Paul burst into doxology in Romans 11 — is not yet present here.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
John 6 is a Johannine discourse passage (the bread of life), which is neither epistle nor psalm — it is extended first-person teaching by Christ with a strong narrative and dialogical context. The rubric applies fully. The sermon’s failure to engage the discourse’s own argument (the offense of the cross, the grumbling, the mass departure) is a straightforward expository gap, not a genre limitation.
