# Study Bible Notes on Habakkuk

Two study Bible treatments. Useful as compact orientation pieces and for verse-level notes on the text — lighter than the Baker/McComiskey encyclopedia piece, but sometimes the tighter notes are the right tool.

1. [Faithlife Study Bible](#1) — introduction + theme overview
2. [Nelson Study Bible (NKJV)](#2) — introduction + verse-by-verse notes

<a name="1"></a>
## 1. Faithlife Study Bible — Introduction to Habakkuk

**Source:** John D. Barry et al., *Faithlife Study Bible* (Bellingham, WA: Lexham Press, 2012, 2016).
**Format:** Introduction only — background, structure, outline, themes.

### Central thesis statement (from this source)

> *"The book of Habakkuk examines injustice from the experience of a righteous person crying out to God for a remedy. God responds to the prophet: Be patient, observant, and steady in your faith, for my judgment will happen at the appointed time... Trust God because He is both powerful and just."*

Compact summary. Usable as a single orienting sentence in any of the series sermons.

### Background details

- **Name etymology:** Habakkuk likely from the Hebrew word for "embrace." *Embraced by God* — possibly — or *one who embraces God.* The name ambiguity itself is pastorally interesting.
- **Possible dual role:** Priest + prophet. Musical instruction at end (3:19) suggests Levite. Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and Zechariah also served in both offices.
- **Date:** Late seventh century BC, when Babylonians were overtaking Assyrians as dominant ANE power.
- **Ethnic note:** Chaldeans = Semitic people in southeastern Babylonia who migrated from Aram; OT sometimes uses "Chaldeans" for "Babylonians" generally (Gen 11:28; Job 1:17; Ezek 23:14). Hab follows this usage.

### Structure as this source reads it

- Oracle (1:1–2:20) + hymn-like prayer (3:1–19)
- Two complaint/answer cycles in the oracle:
  - Complaint 1 (1:1-4): why do wicked go unpunished in Judah?
  - Answer 1 (1:5-11): Babylonians will be God's instrument of judgment on Judah
  - Complaint 2 (1:12–2:1): how can God use wicked nation to judge Judah?
  - Answer 2 (2:2-20): God also will judge Babylon; short-term use, long-term accountability
- Closing prayer (3:1-19): prophet declares faith despite future calamity

### Themes

> *"We still find ourselves in Habakkuk's predicament — we wrestle with God as we try to understand the presence of evil in the world... Habakkuk reminds us to shift our questions from the evil surrounding us and instead focus on the ways of God."*

The "shift from *the evil around us* to *the ways of God*" is a usable pastoral move for sermon 2 or sermon 3.

## 2. Nelson Study Bible (NKJV) — Introduction and verse-by-verse notes

**Source:** Earl D. Radmacher, Ronald Barclay Allen, and H. Wayne House, *The Nelson Study Bible: New King James Version* (Nashville: T. Nelson Publishers, 1997), at Habakkuk.

### Central introductory claim

> *"Habakkuk was unique among the prophets in that he asked questions of God. Most prophets were filled with messages: 'Hear the word of the Lord.' ... But again, Habakkuk was different. To raise these questions was — for our sake — part of his message."*

That is exactly the move sermon 1 already made and sermon 2 should reinforce: Habakkuk's questions are not defects in the prophecy — they *are* the prophecy. Part of what God is teaching through this book is that faith asks questions.

### Dating chronology

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 612 BC | Nineveh falls to Medes and Babylonians |
| 609 BC | Death of King Josiah |
| 605 BC | Nebuchadnezzar's reign begins |
| 605 BC | First Judean captives taken to Babylon |
| 586 BC | Jerusalem falls to the Babylonians |

Useful for sermon 2 introduction — shows Habakkuk's "short" timeline. Between Josiah's death and Babylon's first deportation is only four years. The Chaldean ascendancy that God says Habakkuk "would not believe" (1:5) was on the horizon but not yet visible. The prophet is hearing an answer about an event that will come within a few years.

### Verse-by-verse notes directly load-bearing for sermon 2 (Hab 1:5-11)

**1:5 — *"Look among the nations"***
> *"The international scene during Habakkuk's lifetime was full of turmoil, with Assyria on the decline and Babylonia on the rise. work a work: The Hebrew words suggest that something ominous and impressive was about to occur."*

The ominous/impressive tension is exactly right. God's "work you would not believe" is not good news — it is awe-inducing and terrifying at once.

**1:6 — *"I am raising up"***
> *"God controls the nations for His own purposes (see Dan. 2:21), sometimes indirectly and at other times directly."*

The Dan 2:21 cross-reference is a useful companion text for sermon 2 — *"He changes times and seasons; he removes kings and sets up kings."* Compact Daniel-confirmation of the Habakkuk claim.

**1:6 — *"Chaldeans" = Babylonians***
> *"Under Nebuchadnezzar, Babylon quickly had become the dominant power of the day."*

**1:7 — *"Their judgment and their dignity proceed from themselves"***
> *"The Babylonian system of law and order had no regard for other legal systems."*

Sharp line. The Babylonians make their own law. Useful for sermon 2 as one sentence in the Chaldean description.

**1:8 — *Horses swifter than leopards***
> *"The Palestinian eagle is a bird of prey, a vulture. These images from the animal kingdom present a vivid picture of the ferocious nature of this world power."*

Small exegetical point worth carrying: the "eagle" in Hab 1:8 is more likely a vulture than the American-flag eagle. The picture is of a scavenger, not a patriotic symbol.

**1:9 — *"gather captives like sand"***
> *"The Babylonians resettled numerous conquered peoples with little regard for them as individuals."*

**1:10 — *"deride every stronghold"***
> *"The Babylonians mocked human systems of fortification, bursting through any defense they encountered."*

### Verse-by-verse notes for later sermons

**1:12 — *"Are you not... my Holy One"***
> *"Habakkuk's point seems to be that God's holiness should have prohibited Him from using a 'dirty' instrument like Babylon to accomplish His purposes in judging and reproving His own people."*

The "dirty instrument" framing is sharp. Carries directly into sermon 3.

**2:3 — *"Though it tarries, wait for it"***
> *"God knows His plan and the outworking of all things in accordance with His purposes. The godly are responsible to study and proclaim His revelation while awaiting its fulfillment."*

Directly portable to sermon 4. The godly are responsible to study + proclaim *while* awaiting — waiting is not passivity.

**2:4 — *"the just shall live by his faith"***
> *"True righteousness before God is linked to genuine faith in God. A proud person relies on self, power, position, and accomplishment; a righteous person relies on the Lord."*

Clean, confessionally safe one-sentence summary of the verse. Portable.

**2:14 — *"the earth shall be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD as the waters cover the sea"***
> *"The true knowledge of God in the time of His kingdom on earth will be like the waters — all-embracing, inescapable, and fully enveloping."*

Beautiful sentence. Usable in sermon 5 as a landing image on Hab 2:14.

**2:18 — *Word focus on* pesel *(carved image)***
> *"This term is related to a verbal root meaning 'to hew out stone' or 'to cut or carve wood.' A pesel is an image or idol in the likeness of a human being or animal made from stone, wood, or metal... Both Isaiah (Is. 40:19, 20; 44:9-20) and Habakkuk (2:18, 19) mock those who would put their trust in images made with mere human hands."*

Usable for sermon 5 (the fifth woe on idolatry). The Isaiah 40/44 cross-references are sermon-worthy.

**2:20 — *"the LORD is in His holy temple; let all the earth keep silence before Him"***
> *"These words are not set in a context of worship of God by His people. The Lord is sovereign and holy. He looks at the nations and holds them accountable. keep silence before Him: The call to silence is not an invitation to worship, but a command to reflect on the terrible state of all who fall into the hands of the angry God."*

Sharp corrective. This verse is often used devotionally as a call to quiet reverence in worship. In context, it is a call to silence under judgment. Worth a sentence in sermon 5.

**3:3 — Teman and Selah**
> *"Teman is a poetic reference to God's appearance at Sinai (see Deut. 33:2). Selah is probably a musical term, but its exact meaning is unknown. It may indicate a sudden shouting of 'Amen,' a moment of silence, or a musical chord."*

Honest on what "Selah" means — which is: we are not sure. Don't overclaim in sermon 6.

**3:11 — sun and moon stood still**
> *"This is an allusion to the battle of Gibeon (see Josh. 10:12, 13). Habakkuk portrayed God as a Warrior armed with a bow, arrows, and a spear (see Ex. 15:3; Ps. 18:14; 77:17)."*

Carries directly into sermon 6 — the Joshua 10 cross-reference is essential for reading the poem as a rehearsal of God's historic warfare on behalf of His people.

**3:19 — *"like the deer's feet"***
> *"God will give those who live by faith the same confidence that a surefooted deer has in climbing mountains (see Mal. 4:2). Like a victorious army, the righteous with God's strength will occupy the high hills."*

Mal 4:2 cross-reference is worth holding for sermon 6's closing image.

## What carries forward to sermon 2 specifically

1. **The Dan 2:21 cross-reference** — *"He removes kings and sets up kings"* — as the compact companion text for God's claim to be raising up the Chaldeans.
2. **The ominous/impressive tension** in "work a work" — God's work in 1:5 is not good news; it is awe-inducing and dreadful at the same time.
3. **The "eagle = vulture"** exegetical note — small but sharpens the picture.
4. **The "Babylonian self-derived law" note** on 1:7 — the Chaldeans make their own rules, which is exactly what Judah accused her own corrupt judges of doing in sermon 1. The parallel is intentional.
5. **The 612/609/605/586 timeline** — grounds the prophecy in a visible chronology the congregation can feel as "soon."

## Filter issues

- Both sources are broadly evangelical, both hold verbal inspiration, both are safe on the main Reformed moves around Hab 2:4.
- The Nelson Study Bible uses NKJV; sermon draft uses ESV. Watch for quoted phrases that track NKJV wording rather than ESV.
- Neither source takes a strong position on dispensational eschatology for Hab 2:14 / 3:3-15. Both are compatible with a Reformed covenantal reading without forcing it.
