# Matt Chandler — Habakkuk Series

**Source:** Matt Chandler, *Matt Chandler Sermon Archive* (The Village Church, 2011), Hab 1:1–3:17.
**Preached:** January – April 2011

Eleven-sermon expository series through Habakkuk. Preached during Chandler's first year with diagnosed brain cancer. Personal cancer story interwoven throughout. Full transcripts freely available from The Village Church.

## Quick reference: load-bearing material for **sermon 2 (Hab 1:5-11)**

Chandler's Part 2 is directly on the sermon 2 text. His central move is to flip the reading of Hab 1:5 from terrifying to disorienting-but-merciful: before the content of God's answer lands, the stunning fact is that *God heard and answered.* That is the simple truth he builds the sermon on.

**Core Chandler moves for sermon 2:**

1. **The Super Summer correction.** Same point Platt made — Hab 1:5 is constantly misused as a missions theme verse. *"The problem with that is in the context of the verse. Because the rest of what we're going to read today is about how God's going to use this wicked nation to judge Judah and destroy them. That's just an odd t-shirt verse."* Useful as a teaching-moment sentence: this verse is usually torn from its context; listen to what it actually says.

2. **The Hagar parallel (Gen 21:15-17).** *"You've got this moment in time where you have the expanse of the world, all of humanity on the world, and God's aware of a boy crying under a bush in the middle of nowhere... The Bible characters are there to show you how God operates in a very real world."* Hagar is not Habakkuk, but the pattern is identical — God hears the voiceless. Portable as a callback showing that Hab 1:5 is one instance of a pattern: God hears.

3. **The Israel-at-the-Red-Sea parallel.** The Egyptian army pursuing Israel after Passover — God hears and parts the sea. Chandler's frame: *"You see in the middle of Exodus this Egyptian army in pursuit of the people of God who slam into the Red Sea and have nowhere to go. So they cry out to God and yell at Moses, 'Have you lead us out here to kill us?' And God hears them and responds."* Same pattern again.

4. **The rhythm thesis.** *"God hears and He responds, and it's not because people are awesome. God is just aware of what's going on in the lives of people. It's one of His character attributes. It's His omnipresence."* Useful as the sermon 2 spine — the shocking content of God's answer should not obscure the staggering fact that God answered at all.

5. **The "God says yes more than you think" argument.** Chandler presses on why congregations feel God is silent: we mis-credit common grace. *"More often than not, we'll ask God to do things, God does things through appointed means, through skilled tools and instead of giving Him the credit, we don't give Him the credit and we give the credit to others."* Portable as a diagnostic sentence in the application section.

6. **The "sometimes He says no because of disobedience" argument.** Chandler layers in the hard teaching: Matt 7:7-11, John 15:7, 1 John 3:21-22, 1 Peter 3:7, 1 Peter 3:12. *"If you are bitter, unforgiving, angry, resentful, jealous or proud, you should not expect to hear 'yes' a lot from the Lord."* Too much for sermon 2 (it's a tangent from the text), but worth noting for sermon 4 or a future sermon on prayer.

**Chandler's filter issues:** His series has significant personal cancer material. That's his anchor, not yours. Don't borrow it — your funeral anchor from sermon 1 already does what his cancer story does for him. Also, his concluding remark *"salvation belongs to God"* and extensive "you can't lose your salvation" argument in Part 10 is confessionally compatible but should be handled with the nuance you already bring from Romans.

## Quick reference: load-bearing material for **other sermons in the series**

### For sermon 1 (already preached) — Chandler Parts 1 and 3
- **Part 1** identifies three problems when the congregation is not honest with God and others: (a) worship becomes impossible, (b) we're forced to pretend all is okay, (c) we forget the cross. The "issue-driven, not cross-driven" diagnosis is portable.
- **Part 3** presses hard on the reality that God is a just judge. Chandler's argument: *"Our culture loves just judges... We love justice until someone starts to talk about God being just. Then all of a sudden we don't like it."* Useful framing.

### For sermon 3 (Hab 1:12–2:1) — Chandler Part 4
Chandler's Greek-mythology opening (Prometheus and Zeus, the story of man becoming aware of his mortality) is a long illustration of human limitation. The biblical payoff is the same as MacArthur's: man is limited in time and scope, God is not. *"No one has lied to you more than you have. No one has betrayed you more than you have betrayed yourself."*

### For sermon 4 (Hab 2:2-5) — Chandler Parts 5 and 6
- **Part 5** develops God's unlimitedness as the answer to our limitedness. The Joseph-in-Egypt narrative (Gen 45:4-8) as the model: *"You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good."* Romans 8:28-39 as the climax. Portable as the frame for how sovereignty pastorally lands.
- **Part 6** is Chandler's treatment of Hab 2:4. His definition of faith from Hebrews 11:1 (*"assurance of things hoped for, conviction of things not seen"*), the brake/chair illustrations for practiced faith, and Ephesians 2:8-9 on faith as gift. His "faith comes by hearing" move from Romans 10:14-17. Also his account of the man in Mark 9 who cried *"I believe; help my unbelief"* — portable for sermon 4's pastoral turn.

### For sermon 5 (Hab 2:6-20) — Chandler Parts 7 and 8
- **Part 7** is the discipline/wrath distinction. *"Judah gets discipline, but the Chaldeans get wrath."* Hebrews 12:3-11 as the biblical anchor. Chandler's personal cancer framing here is powerful but personal to him — don't borrow. The distinction itself (discipline has a redemptive purpose; wrath is punitive) is directly load-bearing for sermon 5 on the five woes.
- **Part 8** covers idolatry from Hab 2:18-20. The "monkey temple" opening (his India trip) is a vivid image for how we recognize idolatry as absurd in other cultures but not our own. His four idols observed pastorally in Dallas: *self* (external beauty, mind, vibe), *spouse/significant other*, *children*, and *control*. Ends with ten diagnostic questions for identifying idols that could be absorbed into the sermon's application:
  1. What consumes most of your thoughts and feelings?
  2. What motivates the things that you do?
  3. What are you most afraid of?
  4. What brings the highest amount of frustration or anger into your life?
  5. What is one thing that can change your mood in a second?
  6. What would your friends say is your favorite topic of conversation?
  7. What are some things that you feel you can't live without?
  8. What brings you solace?
  9. What do you yearn for?
  10. What is one thing that you wish God would do for you?

### For sermon 6 (Hab 3:1-19) — Chandler Parts 9, 10, 11
- **Part 9** is the key piece for sermon 6. The horizontal/vertical distinction: *"There is a huge difference between being captivated by someone and being captivated by what someone can do. Those are not the same thing."* Chandler's thesis: we are good at horizontal theology (what God has done) and bad at vertical theology (who God is). Hab 3 is the turn from one to the other. Augustine, Luther, Calvin, Owen quotations on the yearning for God directly. *"The two gospel-assaulting lies that keep us from pursuing: (1) I'm not good enough; (2) I don't need Him."*
- **Part 10** is the "remember and rejoice" pattern. Three things to remember and rejoice in: (1) God saved us (Ephesians 2:13; Colossians 2:13-15); (2) God is sovereign, we are not abandoned (Matt 28:19-20, Romans 8:26, Galatians 5:15-17); (3) God started this and will finish it (Philippians 1:6, Ephesians 1:13-14, Romans 8:28-39). Portable structural move for sermon 6.
- **Part 11** is the closing landing — a glimpse of Christian maturity from Hab 3:17-19. Two marks: (1) God is the foundation of deepest joy (not a means to another goal), (2) God is the strength (no confidence in self). *"If Christ is your greatest joy, all circumstances push us toward Him."*

## Personal anchor model

Chandler's personal cancer story is woven through almost every sermon in this series. In Part 2 he processes common grace through his own medical experience. In Part 7 he uses the discipline/wrath distinction to frame his own diagnosis as merciful, not punitive. In Part 11 he returns to it at the close.

**For your own sermon 2:** your funeral anchor (the eighteen-year-old nephew) already does the work Chandler's cancer does for him. The model is portable in pattern but not content — a preacher with a personal loss processed through Scripture is more credible than a preacher with a clean life story, and Chandler shows the pattern works. Don't borrow the content; you already have your own.

## Filter issues

- **Cancer frame** — his, not yours. Don't borrow.
- **Brain-cancer healing theology** — Chandler's statements about his cancer being healed are pastoral for him at that moment but the cancer did ultimately take his life in 2024. The "I believe He has healed me" framing should be held lightly.
- **Individual Chandler-isms** — He uses some rhetorical moves (*"Just so we're clear, y'all, …"*) that are his voice, not yours. His Texas/Village Church register is different from your southern Baptist register.
- **Healing testimony in Part 2** (four-year-old boy and the "malignant bone cancer" that turned out to be a benign growth from a fractured leg) — verify before borrowing; it's his church story, not yours.
