## Quick reference: load-bearing material for **sermon 2 (Hab 1:5-11)**

The "Strangeness of God's Ways" sermon is directly on the sermon 2 text. Key MacArthur moves to consider absorbing or pushing back against:

### MacArthur's main thesis on Hab 1:1-11
**God's ways are MYSTERIOUS, MISUNDERSTOOD, but always MORAL.** That's his three-point structural arc.

### "Mysterious" — three sub-categories
1. **Mysterious in inaction** — God is silent in the face of grave evil. Why doesn't He smash false teachers on the spot? Why doesn't He keep the church pure? Why doesn't He answer prayer for revival?
2. **Mysterious in unexpected providence** — When God finally does answer, the answer is harder than the silence. *"You think it's bad, Habakkuk? It's going to get worse."*
3. **Mysterious in unusual instruments** — God uses pagan, godless nations to discipline His covenant people. The Cyrus parallel (Isa 44:28; 45:1) — God calls Cyrus *"my shepherd"* and *"my anointed"* even though Cyrus was a pagan king. *"God ain't stuck for carrier pigeons."*

### "Misunderstood" — two categories
1. **By careless religious people** — They sit in churches singing hymns and ignore Scripture's warnings about judgment. They quote 2 Peter 3:4 — *"Where is the promise of His coming? All things continue as they were from the beginning"* — and assume because judgment hasn't come, it won't.
2. **By the world** — Hab 1:11 *"imputing this his power unto his god"* — the Chaldeans will conquer Israel and credit themselves and their god, never realizing God was using them.

### "Moral" — the core conviction
- God is the Lord of history (Rom 13:1 — *"the powers that be are ordained of God"*)
- There is a divine plan in history
- There is a divine timetable
- The key to history is the Kingdom of God
- *"Suffering always precedes glory"* — football illustration; biblical principle for Israel, the church, and Christ Himself

### Specific exegetical observations from MacArthur on Hab 1:1-11
- Two Hebrew words for "cry" in v.2 (same as Rogers' note)
- "The law is slacked" = paralyzed (same as Rogers)
- *"I will work a work in your days which you will not believe"* (v.5) — God is essentially saying *"if I told you, you wouldn't believe it."* Habakkuk wanted explanation; God gives him action that defies explanation.
- The Chaldean swiftness imagery (vv.6-9) — eagles, wolves, leopards — paints them as overwhelming military force
- v.11: *"imputing this his power unto his god"* — sets up Habakkuk's second complaint in v.12

### Useful quotations from MacArthur (verifiable from this source)
- *"God ain't stuck for carrier pigeons"* — MacArthur attributes to a Canadian friend
- *"God's clock is never off one split second"*
- *"If you're a Christian, ask yourself this: how does this relate to the Kingdom of God?"*

### Where MacArthur's framing helps your sermon 2
1. The three-fold "mysterious" frame is directly portable. Sermon 2 could borrow the structure: God's answer is mysterious because (a) it's not the answer Habakkuk wanted, (b) it's worse than the silence, (c) the instrument is unholy.
2. The Cyrus parallel — *"my shepherd... my anointed"* — is a powerful proof that God using pagan instruments is a *pattern*, not an exception. Habakkuk was not wrong to be shocked, but the shock should be tempered by knowing this is how God has always worked.
3. The "suffering precedes glory" axis — useful as a callback to Hab 3 ("Yet I will rejoice") and to Romans 8:18 ("the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed"). The congregation just spent 39 weeks in Romans; this lands.

### Where MacArthur's framing should be filtered
- His "Communism" / "world events" tangent (sermon 3 below) carries political weight that conflicts with the political guardrail. Use the structural insight ("don't be more concerned about world events than about the purity of God's church") without the partisan illustrations.
- His tangent on the Tribulation, Antichrist, and pre-trib Rapture in the third sermon is dispensational; the preacher's 1689 confessional position is historic premillennial or amillennial-friendly. Skip the dispensational eschatology.

## Sermon 1: The Strangeness of God's Ways

**Text:** Habakkuk 1:1-11
**Three-point outline (MacArthur):**
1. God's ways are MYSTERIOUS
   a. Mysterious in inaction
   b. Mysterious in unexpected providence
   c. Mysterious in unusual instrument (the Chaldeans)
2. God's ways are MISUNDERSTOOD
   a. By careless religious people
   b. By the world
3. God's ways are MORAL

### Opening framing

MacArthur frames Habakkuk as the answer to "the problem of history" — the modern objection that has replaced the older "problem of science." The argument: *"How can a God like the one you claim in the Bible let the world get in such a mess? Or for that matter, how can the God that you claim is the God of the Bible let the church get in the mess that the church is in?"*

### Key observations on the text

- **Hab 1:2-4** — "Sin, immorality, vice were rampant. Those in government were slack and indolent. And those who applied the law applied it dishonestly and justice was nowhere to be found."
- **Hab 1:5** — *"You think it's bad now, you haven't seen anything yet."* God's answer is harder than the silence.
- **Hab 1:6-11** — "An utterly pagan godless people [comes] to destroy Israel."

### Key MacArthur quotations

> "If you ever see a book on Christianity and science, the great traditional books were written at the very beginning of this century and at the latter part of the last century because that was the day in which science was the problem. But today that's not the problem. Today is the problem of history."

> "There's a basic biblical principle that says what precedes glory? Suffering always precedes glory. Well we don't pray for that, do we? All we want is the glory."

> "God ain't stuck for carrier pigeons."

> "Cyrus was a pagan king and yet God said, 'I'm going to use Cyrus to free Israel from bondage.' God uses some strange instruments to carry out His judgment."

> "The key to the history of the world is one concept, get it and never forget it. The key to the history of the world is the Kingdom of God — that is the key."

### Contemporary application MacArthur draws

Why does God let liberalism into the church? Why doesn't He strike false teachers dead? Why no revival in America? Why doesn't God answer specific prayers for healing or salvation of loved ones? *"His inaction is mysterious."* But just as God did judge Israel through the Chaldeans, God will judge sin in His time and on His timetable. The wait is not absence; it is timing.

### What carries forward to sermon 2

The "you think it's bad now, it's going to get worse" framing is the spine of sermon 2. MacArthur articulates it more bluntly than Rogers does. Sermon 2 should not soften the shock — God's answer to Habakkuk is genuinely worse than the silence. The pastoral move is not to comfort the congregation away from that reality but to teach them to live within it.

The Cyrus parallel is a strong move to absorb. After exposing the Chaldean horror in vv.6-11, lift the camera and show that God using pagan instruments is His pattern — Cyrus was called "my shepherd" and "my anointed" by name in Isaiah 44-45. The congregation needs to see this is not an exception God made for Babylon. This is how God works in history.

## Sermon 2: The Prophet's Perplexity

**Text:** Habakkuk 1:12-2:20
**Outline (MacArthur):** The question and the answer.

### Habakkuk's two-fold question (Hab 1:12-17)
1. Why does God let sin go unjudged?
2. Why does God use the Chaldeans (who are worse than Israel) as His instrument?

### MacArthur's central insight: how Habakkuk works the problem

Habakkuk does NOT stand in the middle of the problem trying to solve it. Instead, he *backs away from the problem to stand on what he knows about God.* In Hab 1:12-13 he stacks five truths he knows about God:

1. **God is eternal** — *"Art Thou not from everlasting"*
2. **God is self-existent** — *"O LORD"* (Yahweh = "I AM")
3. **God is holy** — *"my Holy One"*
4. **God is almighty** — *"O mighty God"*
5. **God is faithful** — *"We shall not die"* (anchored in the Abrahamic covenant)

MacArthur calls this Habakkuk's solid ground. *"There's only one way to tackle problems in this world. Back off and stop and think about the God that you know."*

### MacArthur's three principles for problem-solving from Hab 2

1. **Stand on what you know about God** (Hab 1:12)
2. **Wait** (Hab 2:1 — *"I will stand upon my watch... and will watch to see what He will say unto me"*)
3. **Commit the problem to God** — *"Commit thy way unto Him and He will bring it to pass"* (Ps 37:5)

### MacArthur on Hab 2:1

> "The second great principle in solving problems. Wait. You just stand back there on that solid ground and say, 'Okay, I'm waiting.'"

> "I'm going to stand here, God, and I'm going to set myself on a tower and I'm going wait until I see the answer." Then the funny part: *"and I'll figure out something to say when You tell me how dumb I was not to trust You."*

### MacArthur on Hab 2:4

> "There are only two options staring us in the face: number one either I take the bare word of God and I live by it, or number two I don't and I live by my own rules; the only two options in life. It's that simple. Faith means I take the word of God and I act upon it."

### MacArthur on Hab 2:14

> "For the earth shall be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD as the waters cover the sea." — global eschatological hope set in the middle of judgment oracles.

### What carries forward to sermon 3 and beyond

- The "five truths Habakkuk knows about God" framework is exactly the spine for sermon 3 (Hab 1:12-2:1 = "I Will Take My Stand"). Borrow this directly. Habakkuk's second complaint is not a new problem — it's a man who has steadied himself on what he knows before he asks his next question.
- The "stand on what you know, then wait, then commit" three-step framework is sermon 3's pastoral close.
- The two-options framing of Hab 2:4 (*"either I take the bare word of God and I live by it, or I don't"*) is sermon 4's spine.

## Sermon 3: Praise the Lord Anyhow

**Text:** Habakkuk 3:1-19
**Outline (MacArthur):** The four parts of any prayer:
1. Petition (Hab 3:2)
2. Remembrance and praise (Hab 3:3-15)
3. Confession of fear (Hab 3:16)
4. Adoration (Hab 3:17-19)

### MacArthur on Habakkuk's petition (Hab 3:2)

Habakkuk does NOT pray for deliverance, escape, ease, or even that Jerusalem be spared. He prays only that God's work be fulfilled and that mercy be remembered in wrath.

> "When you can look at the problems around you and the problems in your life and the problems in your world and say 'God I don't care about those things, all I care about, God, is that whatever happens to me or anybody else that You be glorified.' What a tremendous prayer."

### MacArthur on the theophany (Hab 3:3-15)

A sweeping vision of God acting in Israel's history — Sinai, the Exodus plagues, the Red Sea, the conquest. Habakkuk reminds himself of God's faithfulness across history.

> "We just look back and we just start remembering God's continued constant never ending, never hesitating, never stopping faithfulness. And we take it from there."

### MacArthur on Hab 3:16

The "I trembled" verse. MacArthur notes the spirit-flesh duality: *"The Spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak."* Habakkuk's faith is intact; his body still shakes at what is coming.

> "Don't let your circumstances corrupt your concept of your relation to God."

### MacArthur on Hab 3:17-19

> "He says God I don't care if all of nature goes crazy. I don't care if nothing's right in the whole world, I'm going to love You and rejoice in You — and translated that's praise the Lord anyhow."

### What carries forward to sermon 6

- The four-part prayer structure (petition, remembrance, confession, adoration) is sermon 6's spine.
- The Spirit-flesh distinction in v.16 is the pastoral move for the congregation member who is genuinely afraid even while genuinely trusting. Both can coexist.
- The "praise the Lord anyhow" framing is the title-line for the close of the series.
