## 1. Exposition on Habakkuk 2:1-11

**Source:** C. H. Spurgeon, "Watching to See," in *The Metropolitan Tabernacle Pulpit Sermons*, vol. 45 (London: Passmore & Alabaster, 1899), 239-240.
**Format:** Verse-by-verse exposition appended after sermon #2622 ("Watching to See").

A short verse-by-verse running commentary on Hab 2:1-11. Compact and pastoral. Reads as a preacher walking the congregation through the text after the main sermon. Useful as a model of how to handle the same text without expanding into a forty-five minute exposition.

### Spurgeon on v.1 — the watchman's posture

> *"I shall look to God, and I shall also look to myself. There shall be an expectation as I gaze upward to my Lord, and there shall also be an examination as I look within at my empty, guilty, good-for-nothing self."*

A two-direction posture: **expectation upward** and **examination inward**. Portable as the structure for sermon 4's introduction. The watchman's tower is not just a place to wait for God — it is also a place to be honest about the watchman.

### Spurgeon on v.2 — write the vision plainly

> *"It is not for us to bury the gospel under a mass of fine words, but to set it forth in the simplest and clearest possible language; for it is not the power of human words that God blesses, but the truth itself as it is applied to the heart by his Spirit."*

Spurgeon ties Hab 2:2's "make it plain upon tables" to 2 Cor 3:12's "great plainness of speech." Useful as a homiletical aside in sermon 4 — the prophet was commanded to write so that even the runner could read; the preacher should likewise speak so the hearer can follow.

### Spurgeon on v.3 — "though it tarry... it will not tarry"

> *"Is that a contradiction, 'Though it tarry... it will not tarry'? No: to us, it appears to tarry; but, in God's way of reckoning, it does not really tarry. To our impatient spirits, it seems long in coming; but God knows that it will not be a moment beyond the appointed time."*

Resolves the apparent contradiction in v.3 the same way 2 Peter 3:8-9 does: divine and human reckoning are not on the same scale. Directly portable as a single line in sermon 4's treatment of "the vision is yet for the appointed time."

### Spurgeon on v.4 — the grand text

> *"This grand text was quoted by Paul when he wrote his Epistles to the Romans, the Galatians, and the Hebrews. It proves that Old Testament saints understood New Testament life. David and Abraham lived by faith, even as Paul and Peter and the other apostles did."*

A one-sentence summary of the entire Hab 2:4 → Romans/Galatians/Hebrews thread. Note especially the second sentence: *"It proves that Old Testament saints understood New Testament life."* This is exactly the move sermon 4 needs to make against any dispensational reading that treats OT salvation as different in kind from NT salvation. Same gospel, same faith, two testaments.

### Spurgeon on v.5-7 — the Chaldean's appetite

> *"Their great kings enlarged their mouths like Gehenna, and they seemed as insatiable as the very maw of death itself."*

> *"What an idle task it is for a man to go on perpetually hoarding, heaping together more than he can possibly enjoy himself..."*

Spurgeon reads the Chaldean appetite as a portrait of unchecked covetousness in any age. Useful for sermon 5 (the five woes), not sermon 4. Also gives a model for handling the woes pastorally — Spurgeon does not rage; he names the absurdity ("an idle task") and lets that do the work.

### Spurgeon on v.8-11 — the stones cry out

> *"Let all men know that, sooner or later, God will execute justice even upon the greatest nations. If they will be destroyers, they shall be destroyed."*

> *"'There is a something in the world,' says one, 'that makes for righteousness.' Indeed there is, only it is more than a something; it is God himself who is ever working in all things towards the vindication of his own righteous and holy law."*

The closing line is a sharp Reformed corrective to the moral-arc-of-the-universe instinct. It is not "a something" — it is God. Useful for sermon 5 or 6 as a single quotable sentence.

### What carries forward

1. **The two-direction watchman posture** (expectation upward, examination inward) → sermon 4 introduction.
2. **The Hab 2:4 → Old Testament saints understood New Testament life** sentence → sermon 4's bridge from Habakkuk to Paul's three quotations.
3. **The "though it tarry... it will not tarry" resolution** → sermon 4's treatment of v.3.
4. **The "stones cry out" Reformed corrective** → sermon 5 or 6 as a single quotable line.

## 2. A Message from God to His Church and People

**Source:** C. H. Spurgeon, "A Message from God to His Church and People," in *The Metropolitan Tabernacle Pulpit Sermons*, vol. 12 (London: Passmore & Alabaster, 1866), 685-696.
**Date:** Sunday morning, December 16, 1866
**Text:** Habakkuk 3:2 — *"O LORD, I have heard thy speech, and was afraid: O Lord, revive thy work in the midst of the years, in the midst of the years make known; in wrath remember mercy."*

A whole sermon on Hab 3:2, organized around the three movements of the verse. Preached in the immediate wake of multiple national catastrophes in late 1866 — the Hartley Colliery disaster shadow still on the country, the cholera outbreak fresh, banking panic — though Spurgeon does not name them. He simply says: *"I have been afraid this week, as I have heard the voice of God in this land."* Directly load-bearing for sermon 6.

---

## Quick reference: load-bearing material for **sermon 6 (Hab 3:1-19)**

This is the single most valuable Spurgeon piece for sermon 6. Spurgeon takes Hab 3:2 — the prophet's response after hearing God's terrifying answer — and treats it as a model prayer for a church under judgment.

### Spurgeon's three-part structure

> *"There are three things in the text; an alarming voice, an appropriate prayer, and a potent argument — 'in wrath remember mercy.'"*

Tight, memorable, biblical. Sermon 6 already plans Hab 3 as the prophet's prayer that follows the vision; Spurgeon's three movements line up with the verse's grammar and could carry a section structure for sermon 6.

### Spurgeon's "alarming voice" — the mower vision

> *"I bowed my head to receive the message, and the voice said, 'Cry,' and when I said, 'What shall I cry?' the answer came to me as to Isaiah of old, 'All flesh is grass, and all the goodliness thereof is as the flower of the field: the grass withereth, the flower fadeth: because the spirit of the Lord bloweth upon it; surely the people is grass.' Then I thought I saw before me a great meadow wide and far reaching, and it was like to a rainbow for its many colours, for the flowers of summer were in their beauty. In the midst thereof I marked a mower of dark and cruel aspect, who with a scythe most sharp and glittering, was clearing mighty stretches of the field at each sweep, and laying the fair flowers in withering heaps. He advanced with huge strides of leagues at once, leaving desolation behind him, and I understood that the mower's name was Death."*

Vintage Victorian Spurgeon — a sustained allegorical vision. Too florid in this exact form for the contemporary pulpit, but the *pattern* is preachable. The mower is Death; Death is advancing; Death will reach this congregation. Hab 3:2 is the prophet's response in the face of that. Could be compressed to a single image in sermon 6 if the context (a recent funeral, a sober congregation) calls for it.

### Spurgeon's "Niagara of souls" vision

> *"I saw a precipice, whose frowning steep overhung a sea of fire. Leading up to its brink I saw a road exceeding broad, a road which was crowded from side to side with a thronging multitude... As I looked at that end which none of them could see, I saw a cataract of souls, falling in ceaseless, headlong stream into depths unutterably profound."*

The most famous image from this sermon — Spurgeon's vision of the broad way as a Niagara Falls of souls plunging into hell. Should not be borrowed wholesale; the imagery is too dated. But the underlying claim — that the broad road is not a metaphor for general unbelief but a description of an actual destination — is sermon-worthy.

### Spurgeon on the church's stagnation

> *"What about the work of the Methodists, and the work of the Congregationalists, and so on? May God prosper them according as they walk in his truth! but the way to come to the core of our prayer is to cry, 'O Lord, revive thy work; whatever is of thee, whatever is thy truth, whatever is thy Spirit's work in the hearts of men, whatever is genuine conversion and vital godliness — Lord, revive it.'"*

The pivotal pastoral move of the sermon. Spurgeon refuses to pray *"prosper my work"* and instead prays *"revive thy work."* The diagnostic is sharp: when we pray for ministry, are we praying for our preferred denomination/church/program — or for whatever God Himself is doing in His own people?

Directly portable to sermon 6. This is exactly the move Habakkuk himself makes — from "rebuke me" to "revive *thy* work in the midst of the years." Habakkuk does not pray for the restoration of his own circumstances. He prays for the restoration of God's own work.

### Spurgeon on the "old gospel"

> *"We must have it back. It comes to this — our ministers must return to the same gospel which John Bunyan and George Whitfield preached. We cannot get on with philosophical gospels; we must bring together all these new geological gospels and neological gospels, and semi-Pelagian gospels, and do with them as the people of Ephesus did with the books — we must burn them, and let Paul preach again to us. We can do without modern learning, but we cannot do without the ancient gospel. We can do without oratory and eloquence, but we cannot do without Christ crucified."*

Quotable. The sentence *"We can do without modern learning, but we cannot do without the ancient gospel"* is portable as-is. Functions in sermon 6 as part of the call to return to first things — Habakkuk in chapter 3 is doing exactly this: returning to the old story (Sinai, Egypt, the Exodus) as the ground for present trust.

### Spurgeon on "in wrath remember mercy" — the potent argument

> *"If God were to say to the churches in England, 'I will have nothing to do with you; you have been so idle, so worldly, so purse-proud, so prayerless, so quarrelsome, so inconsistent, that I will never bless you again,' the churches of God in England might remain as astounding monuments of the justice of God towards the people who forsake his ways... We cannot therefore appeal to merit, it must be mercy."*

The theological hinge of Hab 3:2: the prophet does not appeal to Judah's deserving — he appeals to God's mercy. *Cannot appeal to merit; must be mercy.* That is the entire ground of Christian prayer in any season of judgment, including this one.

### Spurgeon's closing pastoral landing

> *"While I have been addressing Christians, my object has been to bless the ungodly too, and I do trust that some here who are not converted will enquire, 'What then is God's voice to me?' May you be led to seek salvation, and remember you shall find it, for whosoever trusts Christ shall be saved. If there be a man, woman, or child among you who will now humble himself under the hand of God, and look to the crucified Saviour, you shall not perish, neither shall the wrath of God abide upon you, but you shall be found of him in peace in the day of his appearing."*

Model of how Spurgeon turns a sermon to the congregation into a sermon to the unconverted in the room. Sermon 6 will need exactly this turn.

## What carries forward to sermon 6

1. **The three-part structure of Hab 3:2** (alarming voice / appropriate prayer / potent argument) — could carry the sermon's section structure, or at least frame the introduction.
2. **The "revive *thy* work, not *my* work" diagnostic** — directly portable as the sermon's central pastoral claim. Hab 3:2 is a prayer that surrenders the prophet's preferred outcome and asks for God's own work to be revived.
3. **"We cannot appeal to merit; it must be mercy"** — the theological hinge of the verse. A single sentence that could carry the sermon's gospel call.
4. **"We can do without modern learning, but we cannot do without the ancient gospel"** — quotable line for the sermon's "return to first things" move.
5. **The "alarming voice" pattern** (without the Victorian allegory) — a recent funeral in the congregation gives sermon 6 its own alarming voice; Spurgeon's pattern of letting the alarming voice drive the prayer is the model.

## Filter issues

- **The Victorian allegorical visions** (the mower, the Niagara of souls) are too florid for the contemporary pulpit. The *pattern* is borrowable; the prose style is not.
- **Spurgeon's anti-Catholic / anti-ritualist polemic** (the "locusts of priestcraft," "Anglican Popery," "millinery of the Church of Rome") is of its time and place. Skip — it would not pass the Synagogue Test for a Catholic visitor.
- **Spurgeon's Baptist denominational statistics** are specific to 1866 England. The *concern* (church not advancing in proportion to population growth) is portable; the numbers are not.
- **Spurgeon's sweeping moral condemnation of London** ("dishonesty," "drunkenness," "fornication," "Popish idolatry") is again of its time. Modern preaching can name sins specifically without the catalog-style invective.
- **The Hartley Colliery / cholera context** — Spurgeon does not name it, but he is preaching in the wake of multiple national disasters. That is exactly the situation a sermon 6 in 2026 (preached after a young person's funeral and amid local grief) would be in. The model holds even when the particulars differ.
