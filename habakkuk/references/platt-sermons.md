# David Platt — Habakkuk References

Two Platt sermons that bear on Habakkuk:
1. [Questioning God](#1) — Habakkuk 1-3 (whole book) — *(directly on Habakkuk)*
2. [Freed in Christ](#2) — Galatians 3 — *(extensive Hab 2:4 treatment via the Galatians quotation)*

<a name="1"></a>
## Sermon 1: Questioning God (Habakkuk)

**Source:** David Platt, "Questioning God," in *David Platt Sermon Archive* (Birmingham, AL: David Platt, 2010), 2667-2675.
**Date:** August 29, 2010
**Text:** Habakkuk 1-3 (whole book)

A whole-book sermon framed by the 5-year anniversary of Hurricane Katrina. Platt opens with personal narrative — his own house in New Orleans destroyed, his church flooded, lives changed in an instant.

---

## Quick reference: load-bearing material for **sermon 2 (Hab 1:5-11)**

Platt's main contribution to sermon 2 is **the framework for honest questioning of God** and **the "short-term circumstances poor measurement" axiom**. He treats Habakkuk's complaints not as failures of faith but as the legitimate work of faith pressing into God.

### Platt's central thesis

> **God uses painful experiences to accomplish His sovereign purposes.**

That sentence is the entire book in nine words. The whole sermon develops it.

### Habakkuk's questions, named one by one (Platt)

Platt takes the questions Habakkuk asks and lifts them out as questions every honest believer asks:

1. **Does God hear?** ("How long shall I cry for help, and you will not hear?")
2. **Does God care?** ("Cry out to you 'Violence!' and you will not save?")
3. **Is God good?** (the deepest question — how can God be good with so much evil?)
4. **Is God holy?** ("Why do you make me see iniquity?")
5. **Where is God's power?** ("Destruction and violence are before me")
6. **Where is God's Word?** ("The law is paralyzed")
7. **Will God show that He is just?** ("Mercilessly killing nations forever?")

All of which sum to: **Is God worthy of my trust?**

### Platt on the "you would not believe" answer (Hab 1:5)

> "I've preached at mission conferences and other things where that's like the theme verse, like, 'Look at the nations and watch. Be amazed at what I'm going to do.' Well, the reality is that was not good news for Habakkuk, and he wanted to be a part of no conference that revolved around that verse, because God is saying that He was about to raise up the enemies of the people of God and use them to rout His people and destroy them."

That's a useful corrective. Hab 1:5 is often quoted out of context as a missions verse. Platt names the original meaning: God's "work you would not believe" was a terrifying answer to a desperate prayer.

### Platt's three responses to suffering

1. **Listen to the truth of God** (Hab 2:2 — "write the vision")
2. **Lean on the timing of God** (Hab 2:3 — "if it seems slow, wait for it")
3. **Live with your trust in God** (Hab 2:4 — "the righteous shall live by faith")

### Platt's central axiom for sermon 2

> **"Short-term circumstances provide a poor measurement of the long-term character of God."**

That sentence is portable. Worth absorbing or quoting attributed.

### Platt on faith parallel between salvation and suffering

> "Just as you trust in God for your salvation, trust in God amidst your suffering. Same. It's faith."

> "Just as at the moment of your salvation you thrust yourself upon God and said, 'You alone can do this. I need you. By faith… that's the only way I can be saved' — the same way in your suffering. Thrust yourself upon God."

The Romans 1:17, Galatians 3:11, Hebrews 10:38 quotations of Hab 2:4 — Platt draws the parallel that the same faith that saves us in Christ sustains us in suffering.

### Platt on the temporary/eternal contrast (close)

> "Our suffering is temporary. Cancer is temporary, and tumors are temporary, and trials are temporary, and pain is temporary, and hurt is temporary, and disease: temporary, disaster: temporary. Death itself is temporary. Christ is eternal."

> "So, thrust yourself upon Him. Thrust yourself upon the eternal God who reigns sovereign over all things, and realize your suffering is temporary, and your God is trustworthy."

### Platt's closing summary

> "God's ways in Habakkuk may seem strange and, yes, God's ways in our lives may seem strange, but look to the cross, for there God takes the penalty of our sin to our lives and pours it out on His Son, and takes the pain of His Son to bring us peace. God, the Father on high, wills the crushing death of His Son to bring us life. His pain brings us peace, His death brings us life, and we find salvation in His suffering."

The Christological move is doing the work the text invites. Habakkuk's God uses painful means for sovereign purposes — the cross is the supreme example.

### Platt's personal anchor: Hurricane Katrina

Platt opens the sermon with his own story — five years out from Katrina, watching the news from a shelter in Central Louisiana, seeing the church building where he served on staff under water, his neighborhood gone, "to be a part of a faith family that was much smaller than Brook Hills... and one Sunday, to be together and the next week to have those relationships, in a sense, ripped apart in a way that there are many members of that faith family that we have not seen since."

This is a model of how a preacher uses personal lament-sized loss as an anchor for preaching Habakkuk. The preacher's own funeral last week (the 18-year-old nephew) functions identically in sermon 1. The model is portable — a preacher can speak from personal loss without making the sermon about himself.

---

## What carries forward to sermon 2

1. **The list of seven questions** Platt names is sermon 2's "some of you" list, refracted through Habakkuk. The questions Habakkuk asks ARE the questions the congregation asks.
2. **"Short-term circumstances provide a poor measurement of the long-term character of God"** is a portable, attributable line — could carry the application section.
3. **The corrective on Hab 1:5** — that this verse is usually quoted out of context as a missions verse but was originally a terrifying answer — is a teaching moment worth a sentence in sermon 2.
4. **The "thrust yourself upon God" verb** is the right verb for what trust looks like in Habakkuk-shaped suffering. Stronger than "have faith." Faith is active, throwing one's full weight on God.
5. **The Job parallel** Platt draws (the divine drama Job didn't see) is a useful illustration for sermon 2 — when God says *"I am doing a work,"* the worker doesn't see the whole work. We never do.

## What carries forward to other sermons in the series

- The "listen to truth / lean on timing / live with trust" three-fold response → sermon 4 (Hab 2:2-5)
- The temporary/eternal contrast → sermon 6 (Hab 3:17-19)
- The Christological landing → present in every sermon's gospel call, but especially sermon 6
- The "is God worthy of my trust" question → spine for sermon 1 already preached, also a callback in sermon 3

## What to filter

- Platt's eschatology is broadly evangelical and not strongly dispensational here — usable
- His "imprecatory psalm" framing of Hab 3:16 is brief and defensible
- The Hurricane Katrina personal anchor should NOT be borrowed (it's his story, not yours; you have your own anchor)

## Sermon 2: Freed in Christ (Galatians 3)

**Source:** David Platt, "Freed in Christ," in *David Platt Sermon Archive* (Birmingham, AL: David Platt, 2008), 1767-1775.
**Text:** Galatians 3 (with extensive Habakkuk 2:4 treatment via the Galatians quotation)

A Galatians-series sermon framed as three "mountain peaks" of God's covenant: Abraham (necessity of faith), Moses (futility of the flesh), Christ (price of freedom). Platt's Hab 2:4 treatment lands inside the Abraham peak via Paul's quotation in Gal 3:11.

---

## Quick reference: load-bearing material for **sermon 4 (Hab 2:2-5)**

This is a Platt sermon on Galatians 3 — but the material on Hab 2:4 itself is dense enough that it functions as a working commentary on the verse. Especially valuable for sermon 4's plan to walk through the three NT quotations (Romans 1:17 / Galatians 3:11 / Hebrews 10:38).

### Platt's three-peak structure of redemptive history (Galatians 3)

1. **Abraham — God's covenant of promise: necessity of faith**
   - "By grace alone God blesses His people; through faith alone, God's people receive His blessing"
   - Abraham believed God *before* circumcision (Gen 15 → Gen 17 sequence)
   - Faith is not earning, not meriting — it is *believing Someone*, not just believing something
   - Hab 2:4 is the bridge: Paul quotes it in Gal 3:11; this was the verse that turned Luther on the Scala Sancta
2. **Moses — God's covenant of law: futility of the flesh**
   - Law exposes sin (Rom 3-7 logic compressed into a single move)
   - Law intensifies sin (Gal 3:19; Rom 5:20 — "added so that the trespass might increase")
   - Law shows that we all disobey and all deserve God's wrath
   - "Cursed is everyone who does not continue to do everything written in the Book of the Law" (Gal 3:10 / Deut 27:26)
3. **Christ — God's covenant of fulfillment: price of freedom**
   - Christ takes the curse for us — "He stepped in and said, 'I'll take the curse for you, and I'll take condemnation for you.'"

Portable as a structure for sermon 4: Hab 2:4 is the bridge text between the necessity of faith and the futility of the flesh. Habakkuk anticipates both halves.

### Platt on the Luther / Hab 2:4 / Scala Sancta scene

> *"Luther went to Rome, and he saw this happening, and he joined in... He said his son later said, it was at that moment, when he was on those stairs that Habakkuk 2:4 was brought to his mind, 'The just will live by faith.' Not by climbing stairs to try to get your way to God. So, Luther immediately stopped what he was doing, went back to Wittenberg and the Protestant Reformation was the result. The just will live by faith."*

A familiar Reformation story but well told here. Sermon 4 already plans to land on Hab 2:4 as the Reformation verse — Platt's framing reinforces the "this verse changed the world" weight without overplaying it. Note: the Scala Sancta tradition is Luther family memory (Paul Luther's later report), not contemporary documentation. Platt handles this responsibly without overclaiming.

### Platt on Abraham's obedience as faith working itself out (Gen 22)

> *"This is not somebody who was just justified by faith back in Genesis 12 or Genesis 15. This is the guy who's living by faith in Genesis 22, and it's living a radical faith."*

The point Platt presses: when Gen 22:16 says *"because you have done this... I will surely bless you,"* the obedience is not earning the blessing — it is faith being lived out. Same logic underwrites Hab 2:4: *"the righteous shall live by his faith"* — the righteous live, not earn life, *by* faith. Faith is the mode of the righteous life, not the wage paid for it. Useful for sermon 4's pastoral landing on what living-by-faith looks like in practice.

### Platt on Hebrews 11 as the Hab 2:4 catalogue

> *"People who live by grace through faith live radically different lives than the rest of the world... Why? Because they believe God, and they know they don't need the stuff that we hold onto in this world."*

Hebrews 10:38 quotes Hab 2:4 and then Hebrews 11 is the catalogue of what living-by-faith actually looks like. Platt's instinct is right: sermon 4 should make the move from Hab 2:4 → Heb 10:38 → Heb 11. The "Hall of Faith" is not a list of moral heroes; it is a list of people who *believed God enough to risk everything*.

### Platt on John Bunyan / "Your righteousness is in heaven"

> *"One day, as I was passing into a field, this sentence fell upon my soul: 'Your righteousness is in heaven.' And I thought with all I saw, with the eyes of my soul, Jesus Christ at God's right hand. 'There,' I say, 'was my righteousness.' So that wherever I was, or whatever I was doing, God could not say of me, 'He lacks righteousness,' for that was just before Him... For my righteousness was Jesus Christ Himself."*

Bunyan's *Grace Abounding* moment — directly portable as illustration for sermon 4's treatment of imputed righteousness. The sentence *"Your righteousness is in heaven"* is the exact pastoral counterpart to Piper's "imputation in seed form" argument from Hab 1:13 + Hab 2:4. Bunyan is what it sounds like when the doctrine lands on a soul.

The Bunyan-in-prison material that follows (12 years in jail, wife's miscarriage, "by the faith of Christ I can mount higher than the stars") could function as the closing image for sermon 4's exhortation: this is what living-by-faith looks like under pressure.

### Platt on the law's purpose (Gal 3:10-25)

> *"This verse right here, 'Cursed is everyone who does not continue to do everything written in the Book of the Law,' that should sound to us like an announcement that there are a hundred nuclear warheads headed right for the United States at this moment."*

The illustration is too current-events-flavored to borrow as-is, but the *function* is right: the curse of the law is not background — it is an emergency that demands immediate response.

Platt then quotes the **Westminster Shorter Catechism** Q&A 84:
> *"What does every sin deserve? Every sin deserves God's wrath and curse, both in this life and that which is to come."*

Useful for sermon 4. Westminster Shorter Q84 is confessionally compatible with the 1689 LBCF (the LBCF chapters on sin and justification track this same logic). A catechism quote can carry the doctrinal weight without requiring a historical-theological excursus from the pulpit.

Platt also quotes **Luther** on the law's purpose:
> *"The principal point of the law is to make men not better, but worse. That is to say, to show men their sin, that by the knowledge thereof, they may be humbled, terrified, bruised, and broken. And by this means, may be driven to seek grace."*

This is from Luther's *Galatians* commentary. Portable for sermon 4 as part of the Hab 2:4 → Reformation thread.

### Platt's central pastoral landing

> *"We need to be delivered from a Christianity that says, 'I claim grace,' and indulges in stuff that the world does. That's not grace, and it's not faith, and this is the whole point."*

Sharp line. Sermon 4 will need to make exactly this move: living-by-faith is not a doctrine that lets us coast; it is the doctrine that produces lives like Hebrews 11.

## What carries forward to sermon 4

1. **The three-peak structure (Abraham/Moses/Christ)** as a *background* frame — not as the sermon's own structure (sermon 4 is on Habakkuk, not Galatians), but as the redemptive-historical context for why Hab 2:4 became the Reformation verse.
2. **The Bunyan "Your righteousness is in heaven" illustration** — directly portable as the pastoral landing for the sermon's treatment of imputed righteousness. Pairs perfectly with Piper's Hab 1:13 + Hab 2:4 logic.
3. **The Westminster Shorter Q84 quote** — a tight, confessionally-compatible line on what every sin deserves. Helpful for sermon 4's "what is faith escaping us from?" move.
4. **The Luther-on-law-as-driver-to-grace quote** — frames the law's negative function (exposing sin) as actually merciful: the law drives us to seek grace.
5. **Platt's "law was added so that sin might increase" reading of Gal 3:19 + Rom 5:20** — sermon 4 needs to be careful here; the Reformed reading is that the law *exposes* sin's increase rather than *causes* it, and Platt is closer to the Lutheran "law as accuser" reading. Use Platt's pastoral force but stay closer to the Reformed exegesis.

---

## Filter issues

- **Platt's "Caleb" anecdote** about his son's disobedience is good preaching for him in his pulpit; the move is portable but the content is his (his children, his family). Use the *pattern* (a parent's command exposes a child's heart) without borrowing the family detail.
- **The "hundred nuclear warheads" illustration** is dated and culturally narrow. Skip.
- **Platt on the Judaizers** — solid exegesis but already covered in your Romans series treatment of Gal 2-3. No need to duplicate.
