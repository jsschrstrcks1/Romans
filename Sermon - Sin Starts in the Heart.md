# Sin Starts in the Heart
## Genesis 2:15–17

*[Part of the through-the-Bible series. Follows the Genesis 1–2 sermon on creation and precedes the Genesis 3 sermon on the fall.]*

---

I've been struck this week by the depravity of man.

Did you hear about that attempted abduction in Citrus County? And another one in Hernando? In one of them, the mom fought back and got her little girl away from the would-be kidnapper. In the other, the mom fought the attacker, he dropped his gun, and the thirteen-year-old picked it up and shot him. A thirteen-year-old child, in a parking lot, defending his mother. That's where we are.

There was a hostage situation on Zimmerman not long ago. Ex-military. Former cop. His ex-wife wanted custody — and he decided he wasn't going to allow that. He held the child at gunpoint for eight hours until he fell asleep around three in the morning. The police saved the kid.

A woman I know left her children outside with the neighbors while she went inside to smoke pot. Her little boy ran into the parking lot and was hit by a car. He's okay — but only by the grace of God.

How many suicides this week? How many murders? Rapes? Car thefts? They ran a full manhunt out by 52 and Hays for two men who stole two vehicles — presumed armed. They caught them. Recovered the cars.

And then there was this: a Pasco County sheriff's employee was caught exposing himself to other men. He attempted suicide. Then admitted to being gay and to having AIDS — a disease he had passed to his wife while hiding his secret life from her.

People — the reality of the world today is not what we have been reading about in Genesis.

God made everything perfect. The anthropic principle we talked about a few weeks back — the intricate, staggering balance built into creation so that life could not just survive but flourish — that was the world as God intended it. But what we are living in today has Christians hiding and on the run. Missionaries being killed or deported. A thirteen-year-old having to shoot someone to protect his mother. A little boy hit by a car because his momma wanted to get high. This world is no longer perfect.

Let's open Genesis chapter 2, verses 15 through 17, and see why.

> The LORD God took the man and put him in the garden of Eden to work it and keep it. And the LORD God commanded the man, saying, "You may surely eat of every tree of the garden, but of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil you shall not eat, for in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die."
> — Genesis 2:15–17 (ESV)

---

While you're settling in, let me remind you of something from the previous week. One of the last things we covered in Genesis 2 is that God told Adam there was only one tree he couldn't eat from. One. Out of everything in the garden — every tree that was good for food, everything that was beautiful to the eye, everything God had made and declared very good — there was one tree off limits.

Now — some people argue this tree is metaphorical. A symbol. A literary device. I am convinced otherwise, and here's why: neither the Hebrew text, nor the early Greek translations, nor the English give any indication that this passage is figurative. The text reads as historical narrative. It reads as a literal, actual account of things that happened in a real place. When we come to Scripture in our attempt to understand it, we have to take it as it was intended — and this was intended as a record of things that happened.

Literal tree. Literal fruit.

And that matters for this reason: if it was literal, then the sin didn't begin when they put the fruit in their mouths. The sin began in the heart — the moment they decided to disobey.

That's where sin always begins.

Not with the act. With the decision. With the wanting.

---

Not only does sin start in the heart. Our enemy also knows our weaknesses. He knows where to push, and when, and how hard.

Think about every kid who has ever lived. Tell a child not to touch something, and within seconds you'll find them huddled over it, staring at it, hands hovering. The pull of the forbidden thing is almost physical for kids — and for teenagers, and if we're honest, for adults too. Libraries of books have been written about this. Shakespeare wrote about it. Romeo was probably just an ordinary young man to Juliet — until her father told her to stay away. After that? He's dangerous. He's mysterious. There's a rush just from being near him. The same goes for Romeo. Maybe he never would have been interested without the prohibition. But that command — *stay away* — rewired everything.

We don't know how long Adam and Eve had been in the garden before Genesis 3. We know they walked with God. We know it seems to have been a pattern, a regular thing. And yet — the very next thing we see after the prohibition is Adam and Eve huddled around the tree they know they shouldn't touch.

I have a story about this. I couldn't have been older than four or five. We were at the house on Williams Lane — just around the corner from where I live now. You can actually see it from my parents' dock. In the dining room there was a ramp that led out to a sliding glass door, and I used to love rolling toy cars down it and crashing them into the aluminum base of the slider. Good times.

On one wall of that room was an electrical outlet.

I remember my cousin AJ — hope you don't mind me telling this one, AJ — trying to stick his fingers into that socket. He got scolded, picked up, carried to the living room, and told very clearly that bad things happen when you shake hands with Mr. Electricity. Don't do that.

Next thing we know, he'd found a fork. And he was coming back to find out what was stuck inside.

Fortunately my mom — who swore she had eyes in the back of her head, and I'm still not sure she didn't — saw what was about to happen and intercepted him before any damage was done.

Adam and Eve did this in a major way.

They had a proverbial fork. They were huddled around the socket. And when they pushed the fork in together, instead of just damaging the two of them, it triggered a curse — a shattering of all creation. We talked earlier about how something doesn't seem —
