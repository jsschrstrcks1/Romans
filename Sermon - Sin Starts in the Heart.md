# Sin Starts in the Heart
## Genesis 2:15–17

*[Part of the through-the-Bible series. Follows the Genesis 1–2 sermon on creation and precedes the Genesis 3 sermon on the fall. Conclusion drafted from series arc and theological lineage — see end notes.]*

---

I've been struck this week by the depravity of man.

Did you hear about that attempted abduction in Citrus County? And another one in Hernando? In one of them, the mom fought back and got her little girl away from the would-be kidnapper. In the other, the mom fought the attacker, he dropped his gun, and the thirteen-year-old picked it up and shot him. A thirteen-year-old child, in a parking lot, defending his mother. That's where we are.

There was a hostage situation on Zimmerman not long ago. Ex-military. Former cop. His ex-wife wanted custody — and he decided he wasn't going to allow that. He held the child at gunpoint for eight hours until he fell asleep around three in the morning. The police saved the kid.

A woman I know left her children outside with the neighbors while she went inside to smoke pot. Her little boy ran into the parking lot and was hit by a car. He's okay — but only by the grace of God.

How many suicides this week? How many murders? Rapes? Car thefts? They ran a full manhunt out by 52 and Hays for two men who stole two vehicles — presumed armed. They caught them. Recovered the cars.

And then there was this: a Pasco County sheriff's employee was caught exposing himself to other men. He attempted suicide. Then admitted to being gay and to having AIDS — a disease he had passed to his wife while hiding his secret life from her.

People — the reality of the world today is not what we have been reading about in Genesis.

God made everything perfect. The anthropic principle we talked about a few weeks back — the intricate, staggering balance built into creation so that life could not just survive but flourish — that was the world as God intended it. But what we are living in today has Christians hiding and on the run. Missionaries being killed or deported. A thirteen-year-old having to shoot someone to protect his mother. A little boy hit by a car because his momma wanted to get high. This world is no longer perfect.

Let's open Genesis chapter 2, verses 15 through 17, and see why.

> The LORD God took the man and put him in the garden of Eden to work it and keep it. And the LORD God commanded the man, saying, "You may surely eat of every tree of the garden, but of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil you shall not eat, for in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die."
> — Genesis 2:15–17 (ESV)

---

While you're settling in, let me remind you of something from the previous week. One of the last things we covered in Genesis 2 is that God told Adam there was only one tree he couldn't eat from. One. Out of everything in the garden — every tree that was good for food, everything that was beautiful to the eye, everything God had made and declared very good — there was one tree off limits.

Now — some people argue this tree is metaphorical. A symbol. A literary device. I am convinced otherwise, and here's why: neither the Hebrew text, nor the early Greek translations, nor the English give any indication that this passage is figurative. The text reads as historical narrative. It reads as a literal, actual account of things that happened in a real place. When we come to Scripture in our attempt to understand it, we have to take it as it was intended — and this was intended as a record of things that happened.

Literal tree. Literal fruit.

And that matters for this reason: if it was literal, then the sin didn't begin when they put the fruit in their mouths. The sin began in the heart — the moment they decided to disobey.

That's where sin always begins.

Not with the act. With the decision. With the wanting.

---

Not only does sin start in the heart. Our enemy also knows our weaknesses. He knows where to push, and when, and how hard.

Think about every kid who has ever lived. Tell a child not to touch something, and within seconds you'll find them huddled over it, staring at it, hands hovering. The pull of the forbidden thing is almost physical for kids — and for teenagers, and if we're honest, for adults too. Libraries of books have been written about this. Shakespeare wrote about it. Romeo was probably just an ordinary young man to Juliet — until her father told her to stay away. After that? He's dangerous. He's mysterious. There's a rush just from being near him. The same goes for Romeo. Maybe he never would have been interested without the prohibition. But that command — *stay away* — rewired everything.

We don't know how long Adam and Eve had been in the garden before Genesis 3. We know they walked with God. We know it seems to have been a pattern, a regular thing. And yet — the very next thing we see after the prohibition is Adam and Eve huddled around the tree they know they shouldn't touch.

I have a story about this. I couldn't have been older than four or five. We were at the house on Williams Lane — just around the corner from where I live now. You can actually see it from my parents' dock. In the dining room there was a ramp that led out to a sliding glass door, and I used to love rolling toy cars down it and crashing them into the aluminum base of the slider. Good times.

On one wall of that room was an electrical outlet.

I remember my cousin AJ — hope you don't mind me telling this one, AJ — trying to stick his fingers into that socket. He got scolded, picked up, carried to the living room, and told very clearly that bad things happen when you shake hands with Mr. Electricity. Don't do that.

Next thing we know, he'd found a fork. And he was coming back to find out what was stuck inside.

Fortunately my mom — who swore she had eyes in the back of her head, and I'm still not sure she didn't — saw what was about to happen and intercepted him before any damage was done.

Adam and Eve did this in a major way.

They had a proverbial fork. They were huddled around the socket. And when they pushed the fork in together, instead of just damaging the two of them, it triggered a curse — a shattering of all creation. We talked earlier about how something doesn't seem right about the world anymore. About how that staggeringly balanced creation we described in Genesis 1 — built to flourish, running exactly as designed for thousands of years — doesn't match what we experience. There's a gap between what creation was made to be and what it is.

Those stories I told you this morning are not news. They are symptoms.

The child in the parking lot. The little girl dragged toward a stranger's car. The man who held his own child at gunpoint for eight hours. The husband who hid a secret life until it destroyed his family. All of it is downstream from this moment — from this tree, this fruit, this decision in the heart before the act. Every headline you read, every tragedy that lands in your newsfeed, every thing about this world that makes you say *this is not right* — it traces back here. To Genesis 2:15–17. To the fork in the socket.

That's the bad news.

But here's the thing. There was a second garden.

Where Adam faced a tree and failed, the second Adam faced a garden too. In Gethsemane, Jesus knelt in the dirt and prayed: *Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me.* He knew what was in the cup. He chose to drink it anyway. *Not my will, but yours.*

Where Adam said *I will eat* — Jesus said *I will drink.*

Paul puts it plainly in Romans 5 — just as through one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so through one man's obedience the many will be made righteous. The first Adam broke it. The last Adam is putting it back together.

That's where this series is heading. And that's the gospel.

Let's pray.

## 5. Score Breakdown

### 1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25

- **Textual Fidelity (5/10)**: Genesis 2:15–17 is the launch text, not the sermon's subject. The text gives: (1) God placed Adam in the garden to work and keep it; (2) God permitted eating from every tree; (3) God prohibited the tree of knowledge of good and evil; (4) the consequence is death. The sermon draws from element (3) the principle "sin starts in the heart" — which is theologically true but is more explicitly taught in James 1:14–15 and Mark 7:21–23 than in Genesis 2:15–17. The text does not say where sin starts; it establishes the prohibition. The sermon uses the text as a window rather than expounding it.
- **Contextual Awareness (4/5)**: The previous-message references (anthropic principle, "walked with God as a pattern") presuppose the series context and are appropriate. The literal-tree argument is briefly defended and correctly identifies the grammatical-historical basis.
- **Exegetical Clarity (3/5)**: The shift from "sin starts in the heart, not the act" to "our enemy knows our weaknesses" in the middle section is logically sound but not textually grounded in Genesis 2:15–17. The enemy (the serpent) doesn't appear until Genesis 3.
- **Logical Faithfulness (2/5)**: The FCF — "why is the world so broken?" — is answered: because sin entered through the disobedience that began in the heart. This is correct. But the journey from Genesis 2:15–17 to James 1's principle of desire → sin is a theological hop the sermon doesn't name as such.

**FCF**: Named explicitly: "the reality of the world today is not what we have been reading about in Genesis." The opening news stories make the FCF concrete.

### 2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18

- **Christ-Centeredness (5/7)**: The Gethsemane/second-Adam contrast in the conclusion is the sermon's theological best. "Where Adam said *I will eat* — Jesus said *I will drink.*" The parallel of garden to garden, tree to cup, disobedience to obedience is textually grounded (Matthew 26:39, Romans 5:19) and Christ-centered. It is compressed into five sentences but is genuinely Christ-centered.
- **Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3/3)**: The "first garden / second garden" typology is a classic redemptive-historical move. The Romans 5 first-Adam/last-Adam framework is deployed correctly and contextually.
- **Doctrinal Precision (3/4)**: Romans 5:19 is quoted accurately ("by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous"). The literal-tree argument is theologically sound.
- **Confessional Alignment (2/4)**: Original sin is present through the "broken mold" imagery (though the term *imputation* is not used). The series context mitigates the absence of a fuller treatment. Total depravity is implied in the "shattered" language.

### 3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 10/15

The cross is not mentioned directly but is implied in "he chose to drink it anyway." ## 8. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The fork-in-the-socket illustration**: The most memorable illustration in this sermon and one of the best in the corpus. The cousin returning with a fork after being warned, and the preacher's mother stopping him — this is specific, personal, and perfectly calibrated to the sermon's central argument. "They had a proverbial fork. They were huddled around the socket." When you hear this illustration, the text clicks.

2. **The second-Adam garden contrast**: "Where Adam faced a tree and failed, the second Adam faced a garden too. In Gethsemane, Jesus knelt in the dirt and prayed: Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me. He knew what was in the cup. He chose to drink it anyway. Not my will, but yours." Five sentences that contain a complete Christ-centered gospel. Compressed but genuine.

3. **The opening news stories as theological diagnosis**: The Citrus County abduction, the hostage situation, the child in the parking lot — these are named not as atrocities to be outraged about, but as symptoms of a diagnosis. "Those stories I told you this morning are not news. They are symptoms." This is the right use of current events in a sermon: diagnostic rather than reactive.

---

## 9. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The central proposition ("sin starts in the heart") is not directly in Genesis 2:15–17**: The text establishes a prohibition and a consequence. The principle that sin begins in the will before the act is theologically true and biblically supported — but by James 1:14–15 and Mark 7:21–23, not by the text preached. The sermon's title proposition would be better supported if James 1:14 were at least cited in passing: "Then desire when it has conceived gives birth to sin" — this is the text that says what the sermon says the Genesis text says.

2. **The "our enemy knows our weaknesses" section has no textual anchor in Genesis 2:15–17**: The enemy doesn't appear until Genesis 3. This mid-sermon section is theologically sound but is sermon material for a different text (or the following week's Genesis 3 sermon). It diffuses the focus of the present installment.

3. **No explicit exhortation or call**: "That's the gospel. Let's pray." is a series-appropriate close, but hearers who are not yet believers are left without a word specifically addressed to them. Even a brief sentence — "If you've never trusted the second Adam whose obedience covered your disobedience, today is the day to come to him" — would supply what is missing without lengthening the sermon significantly.

---

## 10. Surgical Fixes

1. **Cite James 1:14–15 in the middle section**: When the preacher says "sin starts in the heart, not the act," add one sentence: "The apostle James puts it this way: 'Then desire when it has conceived gives birth to sin, and sin when it is fully grown brings forth death' (James 1:15)." This grounds the proposition textually without disrupting the series flow.

2. **Move the "enemy knows our weaknesses" paragraph into the Genesis 3 sermon**: The serpent's specific knowledge of Eve's weakness is Genesis 3 material. In this installment, the focus belongs on the prohibition (Gen 2:15–17) and the garden-to-garden typology. Reserving the enemy's tactics for next week would sharpen both sermons.

3. **Add one sentence of direct address to the unbeliever**: Before "Let's pray," add: "If you've never said yes to the second Adam — if you've never trusted the one whose obedience can cover your disobedience — that's the conversation we need to have. Don't leave without finding me." The series continues, but individuals may not.
