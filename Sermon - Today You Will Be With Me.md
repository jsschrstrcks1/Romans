# Today You Will Be With Me
## A Sermon on Luke 23:39–43

**Series**: The Night Is Far Gone
**Type**: Special Message / Interlude

---

## Opening

Two men hang on crosses.

Same crimes — the Greek word is *lēstēs*, the same word used for Barabbas. These weren't petty thieves. They were revolutionaries. Bandits. Likely violent men. Maybe murderers. Maybe part of Barabbas's own crew.

Same sentence. Same nails. Same slow suffocation.

And between them — close enough to touch — hangs another man. Beaten beyond recognition. Mocked by the crowd. Abandoned by his friends. Naked. Bleeding. Dying.

Everything visible says: *This man is finished.*

But one of those criminals sees something no one else can see.

He looks at a dying man — and calls him King.

Where did that come from?

## People Group of the Week

**The Shaikh of Bangladesh** — over 135 million people. Muslim. Historically the ruling and landholding class of Bengal. A frontier unreached group — fewer than 0.1% Christian of any kind. No self-sustaining gospel movement.

Would you pray with me?

And we ask you to let us live like people who believe the gospel is the power of God for salvation — to everyone who believes. For your name's sake. Amen.*

## Scripture Reading: Luke 23:39–43 (ESV)

> One of the criminals who were hanged railed at him, saying, "Are you not the Christ? Save yourself and us!" But the other rebuked him, saying, "Do you not fear God, since you are under the same sentence of condemnation? And we indeed justly, for we are receiving the due reward of our deeds; but this man has done nothing wrong." And he said, "Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom." And he said to him, "Truly, I say to you, today you will be with me in paradise."

---

## I. The Mirror (What the Thief Saw About Himself)

The first thing that comes out of this man's mouth is a rebuke — not to Jesus, but to the other criminal.

*"Do you not fear God, since you are under the same sentence of condemnation?"*

He's not pretending to be innocent. He's not making excuses. He's not blaming his circumstances or his upbringing or the Roman occupation.

*"We indeed justly, for we are receiving the due reward of our deeds."*

This is a man who has looked in the mirror.

This is Romans 1–3 in a single breath.

Paul spends three chapters building the case: Jews and Gentiles alike, religious and irreligious, moral and immoral — "None is righteous, no, not one; no one understands; no one seeks for God" (Romans 3:10–11).

The thief got there in one sentence.

*We are receiving the due reward of our deeds.*

He's not asking for fairness. He knows what fairness would mean. Fairness would mean exactly what he's getting — nails and suffocation and death.

This is where grace always begins: with the truth about ourselves and our sin.

Until you see that the sentence for our crimes is just, mercy makes no sense. Until you stop arguing with the verdict, you'll never cry out for pardon.

The first thief wanted rescue without repentance. *"Save yourself and us!"* Get me off this cross. Fix my circumstances. But don't ask me to admit I belong here.

The second thief wanted something else entirely.

He wanted mercy. And mercy requires honesty.

*We are receiving the due reward of our deeds.*

---

## II. The Theology of a Dying Criminal

Before we move on, slow down and look at what the thief actually *said*.

This man was not a theologian. He wasn't trained. He'd never been to seminary. He probably couldn't quote the prophets or parse the Hebrew Scriptures. He was a violent criminal — hours from death.

And yet listen to what came out of his mouth.

**"Do you not fear God?"**

He has a doctrine of God. God is someone to be feared. God is holy. God is judge. In one question, he shows more theological clarity than many churchgoers ever reach.

**"We are receiving the due reward of our deeds."**

He has a doctrine of sin and justice. He's not making excuses. He's not blaming his circumstances, his upbringing, or Roman oppression. He accepts the verdict. He knows the sentence is fair.

**"But this man has done nothing wrong."**

He has a doctrine of Christ's innocence. While the religious leaders called Jesus a blasphemer, while the crowds jeered, while the soldiers mocked — this criminal confesses: *He is innocent. I am guilty. He is righteous. I am not.*

**"Jesus..."**

He uses His personal name. Not a title. Not "Lord" or "Master" — just *Jesus*. It's intimate. It's a beggar speaking to a king by name.

**"...remember me..."**

This is covenant language. Throughout the Old Testament, God "remembers" His people — not as if He'd forgotten, but as an act of favor and faithfulness. Noah. Abraham. Rachel. Hannah. "Remember me" is the cry of someone casting themselves entirely on mercy.

**"...when you come into your kingdom."**

He has a doctrine of resurrection. A doctrine of Christ's kingship. A doctrine of eschatological hope. He's looking at a dying man and saying: *You have a kingdom. You are coming into it. And I want to be there.*

Do you see what just happened?

A dying criminal — untrained, unschooled, unknown — just confessed:
- God's holiness
- Human guilt
- Divine justice
- Christ's innocence
- Christ's kingship
- Christ's coming reign
- His own need for mercy

That's more theology than most people learn across their entire lifetime. And he got it all right. On a cross. In agony. With no training.

Where did *that* come from?

---

## III. The King (What the Thief Saw About Jesus)

Now look at what he saw — because what he said makes no sense given what he could see.

*"This man has done nothing wrong."*

Look at Jesus in this moment:
- Flesh torn open from the scourging - some of his insides on the outside.
- Blood running down his face from the thorns
- Gasping for breath between the spikes
- Surrounded by mockers, gamblers, executioners
- The disciples nowhere to be found

And feel the weight of that cross.

Crucifixion wasn't just painful — it was designed to be the most shameful death the Roman Empire could devise. It was reserved for slaves, rebels, and the lowest criminals. Roman citizens were legally exempt; it was beneath their dignity to even witness one. Cicero called it *crudelissimum taeterrimumque supplicium* — "the cruelest and most disgusting punishment" (*In Verrem* 2.5.165) that one human could inflict upon another.

Victims were stripped naked — every shred of dignity gone. Displayed at eye level so the crowds could mock, spit, and jeer. Left to lose control of their bodies while strangers watched. Death came slowly — hours, sometimes days — and it came publicly.

The cross was Rome's way of saying: *This person is worthless. Less than worthless.*

And for a Jew, it was even worse. Deuteronomy 21:23 says, "a hanged man is cursed by God." To die on a cross was to die under the curse of God Himself.

Paul understood this. In Philippians 2:8, he writes that Jesus "humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross." That phrase — *even death on a cross* — isn't redundant. It's emphasis. Death wasn't enough shame. It was *that* death. The lowest death. The most degrading death a human being could experience.

Hebrews 12:2 tells us Jesus endured the cross, "despising the shame."

He knew exactly what He was walking into. And He walked into it anyway.

And here's a detail from Matthew that sharpens this scene.

Earlier in the gospel, the mother of James and John comes to Jesus with a request: seat my sons at your right hand and your left when you come into your Kingdom (Matthew 20:21). It's a power play — proximity, influence, the seats closest to the throne.

Jesus answers with a question: *"You do not know what you are asking. Are you able to drink the cup that I am to drink?"* (Matthew 20:22)

They said yes. They had no idea what they were agreeing to.

And Matthew tells us who actually ended up at Jesus' right and left.

*"Then two robbers were crucified with him, one on the right and one on the left."* (Matthew 27:38)

*Lēstai.* The same word used for Barabbas. The same word that describes the men we're studying right now.

Pay attention to this, the seats of honor in the Kingdom weren't thrones. They were crosses. And the men who occupied them weren't apostles or cabinet members. They were criminals.

Everything visible screams: *This man has lost.*

The religious leaders saw a blasphemer getting what he deserved.
The Roman soldiers saw another dead Jew.
The crowd saw entertainment.
The first thief saw a failed messiah.

But the second thief saw a King.

*"Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom."*

When you come. Into your kingdom.

A dying man speaks to a dying man about a coming reign.

This is not wishful thinking. This is not hedging his bets. This is a confession of faith that makes no earthly sense.

Where did he get the categories to even say such a thing?

He didn't get them from the crowd — they were mocking.
He didn't get them from the disciples — they had fled.
He didn't get them from the religious leaders — they were sneering.
He didn't get them from the scene itself — there was nothing kingly about it.

Think about this: no human being, reasoning naturally, would look at that scene and say, "That's my King."

Every piece of visible evidence pointed the other direction:
- Naked — not royalty
- Mocked — not respected
- Dying — not powerful
- Cursed by Jewish law — not blessed
- Abandoned by his followers — not worth following

The crowd drew the logical conclusion: *This man has lost.*
The religious leaders drew the logical conclusion: *Fraud exposed.*
The Roman soldiers drew the logical conclusion: *Another criminal.*
The first thief drew the logical conclusion: *He can't even save himself.*

Every one of them was using their eyes. Every one of them was reasoning from the evidence. And every one of them reached the same verdict: *failure*.

But the second thief looked at the same scene — the same naked, bleeding, dying man — and said: *"When you come into your kingdom..."*

That's not good judgment. That's not careful reasoning. That's not a wise bet.

That's an *impossible* conclusion — one that contradicted every visible fact.

Something else was happening.

Someone was opening his eyes.

---

## IV. The Mystery (Why One and Not the Other)

Here's the question we have to sit with.

Two men. Same crosses. Same crimes. Same proximity to Jesus.

Same opportunity.

One mocks. One worships.

Why?

This is the question Paul wrestles with in Romans 9. Why did God choose Jacob and not Esau? Why did he harden Pharaoh? Why does he have mercy on some and not others?

And Paul's answer is not what we want to hear:

*"So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy."* (Romans 9:16)

The second thief didn't out-think the first one. He didn't have better information. He didn't try harder. He didn't make a wiser choice by sheer willpower.

He saw what no natural eye could see.

And that sight was a gift.

This is what Paul is talking about in Romans 9. This is why he quotes Exodus 33:19 — "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion."

Two men, equally guilty, equally condemned, equally close to Jesus — and one is given eyes to see a King.

That's not luck. That's not coincidence. That's not the triumph of human decision-making.

That's mercy.

And the more Scripture you know, the more you see this pattern everywhere:
- God chose Abraham, not the other nations.
- God chose Isaac, not Ishmael.
- God chose Jacob, not Esau — "though they were not yet born and had done nothing either good or bad" (Romans 9:11).
- God chose David, the youngest, the overlooked.
- And here, God chooses one thief — not the other.

Why? Because that's what mercy is. Mercy isn't getting what you deserve. If both thieves got what they deserved, both would go to hell.

Grace is God giving sight to the blind. And you can't explain why He opens one set of eyes and not another — except to fall on your face and worship.

Because if it depended on us — on our wisdom, our insight, our decision-making under pressure — we'd all be the first thief.

---

## V. The Darkness and the Dawn

There's one more detail Luke gives us. Don't miss it.

*"It was now about the sixth hour, and there was darkness over the whole land until the ninth hour, while the sun's light failed."* (Luke 23:44–45)

The thief's confession happened in literal darkness.

Noon turned to midnight. The sun itself gave out. Creation was groaning under the weight of what was happening on that cross.

And in that darkness — in that midnight moment — a dying criminal saw what no one else could see.

He saw a King.

This is the journey we've been tracing through Romans. The night is far gone. The day is at hand.

Romans 1–3: Midnight. Total darkness. We are all condemned, and most of us don't even know it.

Romans 3–5: The alarm. Justification breaks in. We are declared righteous while it's still dark.

Romans 6–8: Getting dressed. We put on Christ. We cast off the works of darkness.

Romans 9–11: The certainty of dawn. God's promises are sure. His purposes cannot fail.

The thief on the cross lived the whole journey in an afternoon.

He saw the mirror. He saw the King. He received the verdict.

And he went from literal darkness to paradise before sunset.

*"Truly, I say to you, today you will be with me in paradise."* In that one moment, he went from walking in darkness, to walking in light. 

Today.

Not someday. Not eventually. Not after you prove yourself.

Today.

---

## VI. The Verdict

Notice what Jesus doesn't say.

He doesn't say: *"We'll see how you do."*
He doesn't say: *"If you'd had more time, I'd want some evidence."*
He doesn't say: *"Your faith is commendable, but let's wait and see if it's real."*

He speaks a verdict.

*"Today you will be with me in paradise."*

This is Romans 8:1 before Paul ever wrote it down.

*"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."*

The thief contributed nothing.
- No good works
- No track record
- No baptism
- No church membership
- No time to prove his faith was real

And that word *baptism* matters.

Some will tell you that you cannot be saved without being baptized first. That faith alone isn't enough. That you must add water to the equation before God will accept you.

But the thief couldn't get baptized. He was nailed to a cross. He couldn't walk to a river. He couldn't find a minister. He couldn't do *anything* except hang there and believe.

And Jesus didn't say: "I wish I could save you, but you haven't been baptized."
He didn't say: "Your faith is real, but my hands are tied."
He didn't add conditions or requirements or rituals.

He said: *Today. With me. Paradise.*

If baptism were necessary for salvation, the thief would have been lost. And Jesus would have had to tell him so.

He didn't.

This doesn't make baptism unimportant — it's the first act of obedience for every believer, and we should pursue it gladly. But baptism is not the door. Faith is. The thief proves it.

He had nothing but a cry for mercy and eyes that saw a King.

And Jesus said: *Enough. Today. With me. Paradise.*

That's not how religion works. Religion says: prove yourself. Earn it. Demonstrate your sincerity. Show me your fruit.

But grace speaks verdicts to the undeserving.

Grace pronounces the ungodly righteous.

Grace takes a criminal and makes him a citizen of paradise — not in spite of the cross, but because of it.

---

## VII. What This Means for You

Some of you are the first thief.

You want Jesus to fix your problems, but you don't want to admit you deserve them. You want rescue without repentance. You want a Savior who serves your agenda, not a King who demands your allegiance.

That thief died three feet from Jesus and went to hell.

Proximity is not salvation.

Some of you are trying to earn what can only be given.

You think if you just do enough, try harder, clean up your act, then maybe — maybe — God will accept you.

The thief couldn't try harder. He couldn't clean up anything. He was nailed to a cross with hours to live.

And Jesus said: *Today. Paradise.*

If a dying criminal with nothing to offer can receive that verdict, so can you.

Some of you have been Christians for years, and you've forgotten how scandalous this is.

You've domesticated grace. You've made it transactional. You've started to think God accepts you because you've become acceptable.

Go back to the cross.

Look at the thief.

He had nothing. And he got everything.

That's still how it works.

---

## VIII. What the Thief Couldn't Do

Think about what the thief couldn't do.

He couldn't get sober first.

If he'd spent years drinking, years destroying himself — he couldn't go get clean before coming to Jesus. He was nailed to wood. And Jesus took him anyway.

Some of you are fighting that battle right now. You think you need to get your addiction under control before God will accept you. You think you need to prove you've changed, stack up some sober days, show you're serious.

The thief couldn't do any of that. And Jesus said: *Today. Paradise.*

He couldn't fix his marriage first.

Whatever relationships he'd wrecked — whatever vows he'd broken, whatever trust he'd destroyed — he couldn't go home and make it right. He couldn't apologize. Couldn't start over. Couldn't prove he'd be different this time.

Some of you feel like your marriage is beyond repair. Like you've failed too many times. Like God couldn't possibly accept you until you fix what you've broken.

The thief couldn't fix anything. And Jesus said: *Today. Paradise.*

He couldn't reconcile with his family first.

He'd brought shame on everyone who shared his name. His parents. His siblings. Maybe his children. He couldn't write a letter. Couldn't ask forgiveness. Couldn't sit across the table and say the words he should have said years ago.

Some of you are estranged from people you love. Years of silence. Years of pain. And you think you need to repair those relationships before God will take you seriously.

The thief died with all of that unresolved. And Jesus said: *Today. Paradise.*

He couldn't heal his body first.

His body was failing. Hours to live. No recovery possible. No second chance at health.

Some of you are watching your bodies break down. Diagnoses you didn't ask for. Futures you didn't plan. And maybe you've wondered if God is done with you.

The thief's body was destroyed. And Jesus said: *Today. Paradise.*

Do you see the pattern?

Jesus didn't wait for the thief to clean up, fix up, make up, or heal up.

He took him as he was — broken, dying, helpless — and spoke a verdict over him.

That's grace.

And it's still how grace works today.

---

## Closing

The night is far gone.

The sun failed over Golgotha. Darkness covered the land. And in that darkness, sovereign grace opened the eyes of a dying man.

He looked at a crucified carpenter — and saw a King.
He looked at a condemned criminal — and saw a Savior.
He asked for remembrance — and received paradise.

Not because he deserved it.
Not because he earned it.
Not because he figured it out faster than the other thief.

Because God, who is rich in mercy, opened his eyes.

And God can open yours.

If you've never cried out for mercy — today is the day.
If you've been trying to earn what can only be given — stop. Receive.
If you've forgotten how good the good news is — remember.

A thief went from darkness to paradise in a single afternoon.

Because sovereign grace reached down, opened blind eyes, and spoke a verdict that no human religion could ever promise:

*Today. With me. Paradise.*

The night is far gone. The day is at hand.

Come into the light.

---

## Benediction

May the God who opens blind eyes open yours to see the King.
May the Christ who spoke paradise to a thief speak peace to your soul.
And may the Spirit who gave faith to a dying man give you faith to live.

Until the dawn.

Amen.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### Verdict Title
**Sovereign Grace Proclaimed with Pastoral Heat — A Near-Exemplary Gospel Sermon with Minor Applicatory Gaps**

### One-Sentence Summary
A faithful and theologically penetrating opening of Luke 23:39–43 that magnified sovereign grace and the sufficiency of Christ with unusual pastoral warmth, while stopping just short of pressing the full Perkins taxonomy and leaving the exhortation slightly underdeveloped at the close.

---

### Berean Gate: PASS

**Scopus Test**: The sermon's thesis — that sovereign grace alone opens blind eyes to see Christ as King, and that mercy speaks a verdict over the undeserving — demonstrably arises from the passage's own structure and argument. The thief's confession, Christ's response, and the contrast between the two criminals all drive the main point.

**Authority Test**: Pass. The preacher submits to the text's claims, including the uncomfortable ones — election, divine sovereignty over salvation, the doctrine that the first thief "died three feet from Jesus and went to hell." Nothing is softened for comfort.

---

### Fatal Flaw Flags: NONE

No fatal flaws triggered. The gospel is clearly proclaimed. Christ is essential and not merely helpful. Application is differentiated. Exhortation is present. The sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers.

---

### Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 23 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity* — 9/10: The sermon stays tightly in orbit around Luke 23:39–43, returning to the text repeatedly. Every major movement derives from the passage. The Matthew 27:38 / Matthew 20:21 parallel is exegetically legitimate and illuminating. The one modest deduction: the Greek word *lēstēs* is invoked but not unpacked beyond "violent men/revolutionaries" — a brief gloss on the covenant-language dimension of "remember me" appears in the manuscript and is excellent, but the Deuteronomy 21:23 "cursed by God" thread is introduced then not fully returned to in the theology section.
- *Contextual Awareness* — 5/5: The Lukan context (darkness in vv. 44–45), the Matthean cross-reference, Philippians 2:8, Hebrews 12:2, and the Romans series integration are all handled with organic awareness. The connection to the series arc (Romans 1–11) is natural, not forced.
- *Exegetical Clarity* — 5/5: The breakdown of the thief's theology (Section II) is among the most effective in the manuscript — original meaning is explained with clarity accessible to a high schooler and satisfying to a trained elder. The covenant language of "remember me" (Noah, Abraham, Rachel, Hannah) is a genuine exegetical gem.
- *Logical Faithfulness* — 4/5: No eisegesis detected. The sermon discovers rather than imposes. Minor note: the baptism section in Section VI, while pastorally important and doctrinally sound, is slightly tangential to the text's own argument — it reads as a doctrinal aside inserted at a structurally awkward moment, though the argument itself is correct.

**FCF (Fallen Condition Focus)**: Clearly named — human guilt, blindness, and the impossibility of self-generated faith. The FCF answer to "Why does this text exist?" is implicit throughout: because we are all by nature the first thief, and only sovereign mercy distinguishes us.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 17 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness* — 7/7: Christ is not merely helpful — He is the King, the Judge, and the Giver of the verdict. The sermon never lets go of this. The cross as both curse (Deuteronomy 21:23) and glory (Philippians 2:8) is handled well.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* — 3/3: The darkness-to-dawn motif, the Romans series arc, the OT covenant patterns (God who "remembers"), and the Matthean right-hand/left-hand irony all demonstrate genuine redemptive-historical reading. Connection is organic, not bolted on.
- *Doctrinal Precision* — 4/4: Justification by faith alone is clearly articulated through the thief's story. Grace as unmerited verdict is precise. Sovereignty in salvation is stated plainly without apology.
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse* — 3/4: Strong on election, total depravity, and effectual calling. The baptism section is handled correctly (faith precedes, baptism follows) and aligns with 1689 Ch. 29. Minor deduction: the sermon's sovereignty emphasis is clear, but the Covenant of Grace framework — which would have deepened the "remember me" section — remains implicit rather than named.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 14 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel* — 5/5: Justification by faith alone is present and clear. The verdict ("Today. With me. Paradise.") is preached as gospel declaration, not moral instruction.
- *Necessity of Christ* — 5/5: Christ is absolutely essential. The sermon would collapse without Him. The Synagogue Test: this sermon could not survive in a synagogue without offense — it explicitly proclaims that the criminal's only hope was Christ's verdict, not his own merit or religion.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity* — 4/5: The sermon moves toward the cross and from it. The minor deduction: the theological weight of *why* Christ could speak "paradise" — i.e., that He was bearing the curse described in Deuteronomy 21:23, absorbing the condemnation so the thief could be released from it — is hinted at but not explicitly pressed. Penal substitution is implied, not proclaimed. One more sentence at the close of Section III or in the closing would have unlocked the full weight.

**Law/Gospel Dialectic**: The hammer (Section I — "we are receiving the due reward of our deeds") precedes the balm (Section VI — "Today. With me. Paradise.") in proper order. Law is allowed to land before grace relieves it.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 13 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration* — 5/5: Section VII and VIII are strong. The preacher does not merely address the intellect — he corners the heart with concrete scenarios (addiction, marriage, family estrangement, failing body). These are specific and pastoral.
- *Specificity* — 5/5: Section VIII is the strongest in the manuscript for specificity. "You can't get sober first." "You can't fix your marriage first." These are not generic — they are addressed to real people in real pain.
- *Spiritual Differentiation* — 3/5: The sermon addresses three types clearly: the first-thief-type who wants rescue without repentance, the self-righteous who are trying to earn salvation, and the long-time believer who has domesticated grace. This covers approximately three of Perkins' seven categories. The humbled-but-not-yet-believing, the backslider, and the hardened/unteachable receive no direct address. The sermon is strong on the believer/unbeliever binary but does not reach the full Perkins taxonomy.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 6 / 8**

- *Call to Response* — 3/4: The closing section offers a clear triple invitation: cry for mercy, stop trying to earn it, remember. These are direct and unambiguous. However, they are stated more as description than as command — the imperative edge is present but not sharp. "Today is the day" is invitation; "Come to Christ now, on these terms, and receive this verdict" would be exhortation. The distinction is real.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness* — 3/4: The closing benediction ("May the Spirit who gave faith to a dying man give you faith to live") gestures toward life-forward faith, but the sermon stops short of sending the church outward. The thief is a beautiful example of received grace — the sermon could have pressed: "Now you who have received that verdict — go tell others that this grace exists. Live like someone who has been pardoned."

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 8 / 9**

- *Clarity of Outline* — 4/4: Six expository sections plus closing — each with a clear thesis, all traceable. The structure follows the narrative arc of the passage naturally.
- *Progression* — 3/3: Builds from observation (the mirror) to theology to mystery to verdict to application. The movement is logical and increasing in force.
- *Unity* — 1/2: The baptism section in Section VI, while doctrinally sound, creates a momentary unity fracture — it feels like a separate sermon point inserted into the verdict section. A brief acknowledgment that baptism is discussed elsewhere would suffice; as written it slightly interrupts the flow.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 5 / 5**

- *Relevance* — 2/2: All illustrations are native to the text. The Shaikh of Bangladesh prayer moment is unusual for a sermon rubric, but it works — it drives home the universal stakes of "Today you will be with me in paradise."
- *Clarity* — 2/2: The Matthew 20/27 throne-at-the-cross parallel is the strongest illustration in the manuscript. It illuminates without competing.
- *Memorability* — 1/1: "Today. With me. Paradise." will be carried home. So will "You think you need to get sober first."

**8. Sermonic Force — 5 / 5**

- *Reads as Preaching* — 2/2: Direct address, breath-length paragraphs, repetition at pressure points ("Today. With me. Paradise." — repeated four times at strategic moments). This manuscript is written to be preached.
- *Conviction on the Page* — 2/2: The ratio of Scripture-grounded assertion to general statement is high. Filler is nearly absent. The text drives assertion throughout.
- *Cadence and Breath* — 1/1: Sentence length variation is deliberate and effective. The shortest sentences appear at the sermon's climactic moments. "That's mercy." "That's grace." "Today." The manuscript roars at the right moments and earns those moments.

**Tone-text congruence**: The text carries both the weight of condemnation and the shock of mercy. The sermon matches both registers — somber in sections I-IV, erupting in grace in sections V-VI.

3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* — Both convicted and comforted, in proper sequence. The first-thief diagnosis lands before the verdict-over-the-ungodly comfort.

### Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Matthew 20/27 throne inversion** (Section III): The juxtaposition of James and John's throne request with the actual occupants of Christ's right and left is exegetically precise, homiletically memorable, and theologically rich. It is earned by the text and serves the sermon's main point without competing with it.

2. **Section VIII — "What the Thief Couldn't Do"**: The sustained, specific pastoral press — addiction, marriage, family estrangement, physical decline — addresses real human conditions with genuine pastoral intelligence. This is Puritan applicatory work dressed in accessible language.

3. **The "remember me" covenant exegesis** (Section II): The identification of "remember me" as covenant language, anchored in Noah, Abraham, Rachel, and Hannah, is a genuine exegetical contribution that elevates the sermon beyond surface-level application. It teaches the congregation to read the Old Testament in Christ.

---

### Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Penal substitution is implied but not proclaimed** (Section III/VI): The Deuteronomy 21:23 "cursed by God" thread is introduced in Section III with appropriate force — but never closed. The thief's paradise is grounded in Christ's verdict, but the sermon does not explain *why* Christ can pronounce that verdict: because He absorbed the curse on Calvary in the thief's place. The cross-as-substitution is present beneath the surface but never breaks through. One paragraph connecting "cursed by God" (Deuteronomy 21:23) to the mechanism of the thief's pardon would transform a strong gospel sermon into an airtight one.

2. **Perkins taxonomy is underdeveloped** (Section VII): Three hearers are addressed — the first-thief type, the self-righteous striver, and the complacent believer. The backslider, the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, and the hardened receive no direct word. Given the pastoral richness of Section VIII, this is surprising — and fixable with two or three targeted sentences.

3. **The baptism section disrupts the verdict's climax** (Section VI): The doctrinal parenthesis on baptism is sound, but it appears at the moment the sermon is building to its highest peak. The thief's pardon is declared, and then the reader is redirected to a polemical aside before the gospel force can fully land. This section belongs either earlier (after Section II, as a "he contributed nothing" note) or as a footnote. As positioned, it interrupts the sermon's most powerful moment.

---

### Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that his Baptist confessionalism surfaces most explicitly in the one section (baptism) where it is most structurally disruptive. The argument itself is correct; the placement costs the sermon momentum at its peak.

The sermon also does not name the corporate dimension of the verdict — "there is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus" (Romans 8:1) is cited, but the covenant community aspect of paradise (not just "you and Jesus" but the communion of saints) is absent.

---

### Illusion of Strength

Section IV (The Mystery) reads with great theological confidence — and is theologically correct. But the confidence slightly outpaces the pastoral bridge. The preacher asserts sovereign election with appropriate force, then lands on "fall on your face and worship" without quite bridging why that is the only right response for someone who is still asking "but why not me?" A sentence of pastoral sympathy before the doxological conclusion would make the section stronger without softening the doctrine.

---

### Missed Opportunity

The darkness of Luke 23:44–45 (Section V) is used well as a narrative and symbolic frame, but its deepest theological freight — that the darkness at noon is the darkness of divine abandonment, the Father turning his face away from the Son bearing sin — is untouched. That darkness is not atmospheric; it is the weight of wrath. The thief's sight in that darkness is not just ironic — it is miraculous precisely because it occurs in the moment of maximum divine judgment. That connection would have transformed the mystery section and given the penal substitution thread a natural landing point.

---

### Surgical Fixes

1. **Close the Deuteronomy 21:23 thread**: After the "cursed by God" introduction in Section III, add one paragraph in Section VI: "Jesus could say 'Today. With me. Paradise.' because He was absorbing the curse so the thief didn't have to. He was being made a curse for us (Galatians 3:13) so that God's verdict over the thief — and over you — could be pardon rather than condemnation."

2. **Relocate or compress the baptism section**: Move the baptism argument to follow the bulleted list in Section VI ("No good works. No track record...") as a two-sentence note rather than a four-paragraph aside. Or move it entirely to Section II after the thief's theology, framing it as "he was saved without it — not because it doesn't matter, but because what matters is what it points to."

3. **Add two Perkins-category sentences in Section VII**: After addressing the complacent believer, add: "Some of you are almost persuaded. You're not mocking like the first thief. But you haven't cried out yet either. You're standing where the thief stood before grace opened his eyes — and the invitation is still open." This reaches the humbled-but-not-yet-believing.

4. **Add one exhortation sentence to the closing**: After "Come into the light," add: "And then go. Go tell someone else that this verdict exists. Go live as a person who has been pardoned at the cross. That's what it means to carry the light into the dark."

5. **Engage the cosmic darkness in Luke 23:44**: In Section V, add one sentence: "That darkness was not a weather event — it was the face of God turning away from His Son as He bore the sin of the world. The thief's sight was opened at the exact moment God's wrath was heaviest. That's not coincidence. That's sovereign mercy operating at maximum cost."

### Dead Orthodoxy Flag: NOT TRIGGERED

Exposition scores high AND conscience/applicatory force scores high (13/15). The sermon does not merely explain — it searches. No flag triggered.

### Weight of Glory Assessment

This sermon carries genuine eternal weight — God is not manageable in it, the thief is not sentimentalized, and the verdict "Today. With me. Paradise." lands as the most consequential five words in human history. Bonus: +4 of 5. The one point withheld is for the unfinished thread on penal substitution, which leaves the glory slightly incomplete — the cross is present, but its mechanism is not fully named.

### Rubric Fit Note

Luke 23:39–43 is a narrative passage, and the rubric bends slightly in the illustration category — the "illustration" here is the passage itself, which the sermon exposits rather than illustrates in the conventional sense. The rubric handles this well: illustrations serve the passage rather than competing with it, which is the correct relationship. No structural strain noted. The Reformed Baptist framework fits this passage naturally — sovereign grace, election, justification by faith, and the sufficiency of Christ's finished work are all textually native to Luke 23.
