## Processional / Opening

Today, as we join Paul Zinda and Kerry Williams Compton in marriage, we celebrate them as they begin a new family together. Yet we also know that this new branch of the family tree will be strengthened and enriched by the love, traditions, and knowledge of their family roots.

On behalf of the bride and groom I want to thank everyone for coming. A marriage is the most sacred relationship that people can enter into. So I think it is fitting that we begin this ceremony with prayer.

---

## Opening Prayer

Our Father, we come today as family, friends, those who are physically present and those whom we love who are now with you, asking your blessing upon these two lives and this home being established. You have made us so that we are incomplete without the other so that we yearn for someone whom we can love and whose love we can receive.

We are thankful for the love we see here, and even more, for the love we feel from you. May we never take your love for granted. Grant them patience, gentleness, forbearance, and understanding. Protect their home from those forces that would break it apart.

We ask for health, for long life, for the fulfillment of every good dream. May their love continue through life and, finally, blend into life eternal.

In Jesus name we pray, amen.

## Introduction

In the beginning, God saw that it was not good for man to be alone. So out of his wisdom and love, he gave to him a woman, to be his companion in a union so intimate and tender that they were to be regarded no longer as two, but as one flesh. Since this is of God, it must not be looked upon lightly. Therefore, I shall ask you to turn and face one another and join your hands.

## Scripture Reading — 1 Corinthians 13:4-8

*4 Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. 5 It does not dishonor others, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. 6 Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. 7 It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres. 8 Love never fails.*

## Homily

I want you to notice something critical in this passage. Every sin a person can commit comes back around to a single letter. I. I, then, is the antithesis of love.

Love is patient — not when someone feels like it, there are no escape clauses — love is simply patient. Patient when the toothpaste is rolled the wrong way, patient when you're running late and don't have a plan. In all things and all circumstances love is patient. If you find yourself in a situation where you are not feeling patient, stop and consider for a moment whether or not it's because you are putting yourself before your spouse. Is your need to leave in a hurry, getting there as soon as possible, conflicting with their need to have a couple unlikely contingencies planned for? In that moment neither of you are being loving. When conflict arises — and it will — always be quick to ask: am I being loving in this situation?

The same is true of kindness. If you are both being kind in a situation you will also be demonstrating Christlike patience. I know a couple, good friends of mine, who struggled with not dishonoring each other. When things were bad, he would look drawn and be uncharacteristically reserved. She by contrast was talking badly about him to the members of the church where he was pastor. Saying mean and hurtful things simply to make him look bad. Her dishonoring manner was one of the contributing factors in their ongoing struggle. Perhaps if there had been more kindness they would be in a different place right now.

Next look with me at the last set of descriptors. It says love always protects, trusts, hopes and perseveres. In your arguments never say *never* or *always*. Because always and never are usually not true, and they discourage your partner from trying in that area. Always trust your partner to have your best interests at heart. Never lose hope, and in the dark days that may come, always persevere. A friend said sometime back that the only reason he is still married to his wife after forty some years is that she has doggedly persevered. She said the same about her husband. That his stubbornness, and refusal to give up on her, is one of the main reasons they have stayed together as long as they have.

In truth, a marriage is a lot like a triangle. In this triangle God is at the top, and you both are the corners at the bottom. As long as you two are keeping Christ at the center, and growing in your relationship with God, you two will grow closer together. Paul, as man of the house, biblically it is your job to disciple your wife, to read the Bible with her, to pray together. In addition to all the other benefits of prayer, prayer is an important part because in prayer we can bare our soul to the Lord in front of our spouse in ways we may hesitate to do at other times. Prayer then helps keep you close to each other. A professor once said that you have to keep your hand on the pulse of a community lest you lose touch and become ineffectual — the same is true in a marriage. Prayer helps you keep track of each other's pulse — where they are with various situations in life, how they are growing and changing, and what's bothering them. Even if these things are openly discussed in your household it's still vitally important to bring them before the throne of God.

Scripture says it's better to have never made a vow than to make one that you can't keep.

## Ring Words

Let us seal these vows with rings. Rings are symbolic of many things. First they are made of precious metals and as such are valuable. Second, while they have a beginning, once started a ring continues on without end. And so as a sign of your love, and in remembrance of your vows, exchange rings.

## Community Prayer

This next part is a little untraditional at a wedding, but I think is one of the more important things we will do today. Let's all gather around Paul and Kerry, lay hands on them and each take turns praying for them, asking for blessings for them, for protection, long life, happiness, and anything else the Lord places on your heart. Dad — why don't you go ahead and start, and when it seems like everyone is done, I will go ahead and close.

*(Prayer)*

*(Return to your seats)*

### One-Sentence Summary

A warm and practically-minded homily on 1 Corinthians 13 that serves the occasion with pastoral care and memorable illustration, but does not press the theological roots of Christian love deeply enough to give the application lasting weight.

---

### Berean Gate

**Scopus Test**: The homily's central thesis is: love is self-forgetfulness — putting your spouse before yourself is the definition of Christian love. This is a defensible reading of 1 Corinthians 13:4-8. The passage's argument is that love is defined by a series of behaviors, each of which negates self-centeredness. The "I is the antithesis of love" observation is a real insight from the text.

**Authority Test**: The preacher does not editorialize away from the text or soften its claims. He applies the passage directly to marriage without straining it.

**Berean Gate**: Pass — with notation. The main point of the sermon (love is self-forgetfulness) does arise from the passage's structure. However, the Scopus is only partial — the passage in its canonical context (1 Corinthians 12-14, the gift chapters) has an ecclesiological purpose, and the preacher's narrowing of it entirely to marriage is not dishonest but is not contextually transparent. The text does what the preacher says; he could have noted more fully why Paul wrote it.

---

### Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag assessment (homily only)**:

1. No clear gospel presentation — **Partial flag**. The gospel is gestured at ("keeping Christ at the center") but Christ's finished work as the ground of marital love is not proclaimed. The homily gives moral instruction without explaining what makes Christian love different from good-willed secular love. The love described could survive without the cross.
2. Main point drifts from passage's argument — **No flag**. The main point is supported by the text.
3. Christ is absent or unnecessary — **Partial flag**. Christ is mentioned but not necessary to the homily's argument. The triangle illustration introduces Christ as a structural device (the apex), but the homily could function with "God" substituted throughout. The cross is nowhere present. A couple seeking a humanist wedding with theistic language would find little to object to.
4. Application entirely generic — **No flag for a wedding homily**. The advice is specific to marriage — patience, kindness, not dishonoring — and grounded in illustrated examples.
5. No actual exhortation — **Partial flag**. The vows function as the ceremony's exhortation. Within the homily itself, the exhortation is implicit ("always be quick to ask: am I being loving?") but never summoned with prophetic directness.
6. Inaccessible to ordinary hearers — **No flag**. The homily is accessible to everyone in a wedding assembly.

**Score cap**: No full fatal flaw flag triggered. Partial flags on gospel presentation and Christ's necessity lower the Gospel Centrality score substantially but do not alone cap the score.

### Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily of the couple and of good marriage practice. Christ is present but not magnified.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas, warmly presented. The opening prayer carries more of God's presence than the homily.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Informed and encouraged. The dishonoring illustration approaches conviction but does not press it to a point.

---

### Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The "I is the antithesis of love" observation** — This is a genuine and accessible exegetical insight that sets the homily's frame. It is the clearest moment of text-governed thought and the most memorable theological sentence in the homily.
2. **The illustration of the forty-year marriage** — "She has doggedly persevered... his stubbornness and refusal to give up on her" is the warmest and most human moment. It is specific, believable, and carries the sermon's main point into lived reality.
3. **The prayer counsel** — The instruction to pray together as a way of keeping your hand on the "pulse" of the other person is original, practical, and pastorally formed. It gives the couple something to do Monday morning.

---

### Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is absent from the homily's center** — The love of 1 Corinthians 13 is not grounded in the love of God shown at Calvary. The homily commends self-forgetful love but does not tell the couple why it is possible, where its power comes from, or what resource sustains it when the feelings are gone. The cross should appear here. The homily ends with "Scripture says it's better to have never made a vow than to make one that you can't keep" — which lands as warning without the balancing comfort of Christ's grace toward covenant-breakers.
2. **Ephesians 5 is entirely absent** — The New Testament's own theology of Christian marriage grounds it in Christ's covenant love for the church. The triangle illustration gestures toward this but does not name it. A wedding is the single most natural occasion in the pastor's ministry to preach Ephesians 5's "as Christ loved the church" — and it is not here.
3. **The contextual frame of 1 Corinthians 13 is not acknowledged** — Paul wrote this chapter as a corrective within a charismatic controversy, not as a wedding text. The preacher's use of it for marriage is conventional and not dishonest, but acknowledging that Paul's love chapter exceeds its popular use — and then showing why it applies to marriage — would have strengthened the exegetical credibility of the homily substantially.

---

### Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how thin the gospel content is because the ceremony as a whole is Christian in tone, the prayer is gospel-infused, and the couple is known to be believers. The assumption is doing work the homily does not do. A guest who is not a Christian, or a believer in a struggling marriage, would walk away with good advice but not with the grace that makes the advice possible to follow.

---

### Illusion of Strength

The triangle illustration feels theologically grounded — God at the apex, marriage as a function of closeness to God. But the triangle does not explain why: it is a geometric metaphor without theological content. Why does closeness to God produce closeness to each other? The homily does not say. The triangle works as a memory device but not as a theological argument. The illustration carries authority it has not earned.

---

### Missed Opportunity

The closing warning about vows — "better to have never made a vow than to make one that you can't keep" — is the most serious theological statement in the homily and receives exactly one sentence of development, arriving without context or comfort. This is the moment to say: "And this is why you need more than love as a feeling — you need the grace of the One who kept the covenant when we broke it." That sentence would have placed the cross exactly where the homily needs it.

---

### Surgical Fixes

1. **Add a gospel tether after the triangle illustration**: "Paul and Kerry, the only reason Christian marriage can reflect this kind of love is because Christ first loved the church and gave himself for her. The same grace that forgives your sins is the grace that will sustain you when patience runs out. Your love for each other flows from his love for you."
2. **Develop the vow warning with gospel comfort**: The Ecclesiastes citation about vows is theologically serious. Follow it immediately with the comfort: "The good news is that when you fail — and you will — you have an advocate who never fails his covenant."
3. **Name the fallen condition explicitly**: "Every one of us by nature defaults to self. That is what the gospel calls sin. Marriage doesn't cure it — but the grace of God can transform it."
4. **Add one direct address to the guests**: A brief moment acknowledging that others in the room carry their own marriage stories would extend the homily's pastoral reach beyond the couple without lengthening it significantly.
5. **Introduce Ephesians 5:25 as the theological grounding for the husband's discipleship charge**: "Paul, when Scripture says it is your job to disciple your wife, it does so in the same breath as: 'Husbands, love your wives as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her.' That is the standard — and the grace — you are being called to."
