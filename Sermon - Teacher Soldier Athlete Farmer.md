## Opening: The Hulk in the River

Before we start I want to share with you a story I heard from John MacArthur. Supposed to be a true story, it takes place in New Jersey at the mouth of a river that dumps out into the Atlantic Ocean. This old wooden ship — he calls it a hulk — a great big heavy wooden ship's hull was right in the middle of the mouth, right where they intended to build a bridge across the river. So they are working on the bridge pilings, and a team of engineers are working on dealing with the hulk. They tried everything they could think of. They had teams of horses and oxen pulling, they built structures and great big levers and all sorts of things like this. They tried everything they could think of, and before you knew it the bridge had just about caught up to them. One of the junior engineers asked if he could try something, and in desperation they told him sure. So he hired a bunch of barges, and had them go out to the hulk at low tide.

At low tide they tied ropes and chains onto this old wooden hulk, everywhere they could, and then he told the captains to park there. Some of his peers ridiculed him for wasting so much time when time was the thing they were nearly out of, and one of the older engineers stepped in and said, no, there is wisdom here, hush and watch. He might have something.

So the barges waited for six hours until the tide had changed again, and as the water came up, it lifted the barges, which in turn lifted the hulk a number of feet out of the muck and mire. At low tide they re-tied all the barges onto the hulk, and waited again. Again and again they did this until the whole hulk popped up out of the muck, and they were able to haul it out of the way of the bridge. What the machines of man had failed to accomplish the tides had accomplished. MacArthur goes on to say "what we cannot do in the flesh, God can do by the power of His Spirit. And that essentially is what Paul is saying to Timothy here."

I think each of these analogies Paul gives are setting a high bar for us to follow, perhaps one that is too high for us. But is anything too difficult for Jesus? What the machines of man can't do, the tides of God can handle no problem.

There are four analogies that Paul gives Timothy, that I want to really focus on this morning. That of a soldier, teacher, an athlete and a farmer.

## Scripture Reading: 2 Timothy 2:1-7

> You then, my child, be strengthened by the grace that is in Christ Jesus, and what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also. Share in suffering as a good soldier of Christ Jesus. No soldier gets entangled in civilian pursuits, since his aim is to please the one who enlisted him. An athlete is not crowned unless he competes according to the rules. It is the hard-working farmer who ought to have the first share of the crops. Think over what I say, for the Lord will give you understanding in everything.
> — 2 Timothy 2:1-7 (ESV)

The main point of all of this passage today is to remind Timothy what it looks like to be a strong Christian.

---

## The Teacher

The first analogy we're going to talk about is that of the teacher. In verse 2, he refers to Timothy as a teacher. MacArthur writes:

> In verse 2, Paul says, "And the things which you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses" — in other words, that is the truth of God, the revelation of God, which I taught to you; revelation from God attested to by other witnesses, not just my own opinion; the Word of God which I taught to you; these truths — "you entrust to faithful men who will be able to teach others also."

In other words, "You're a teacher. I taught you; you teach others so they can teach others." That's the essence. We have to see ourselves as having received a deposit of truth, as he says in chapter 1, verses 12 to 14. We have received a deposit of truth to be guarded, and at the same time given away — an interesting paradox. Guard its purity and give it to someone else who also will guard its purity. We have received the oracles of God; we have received the truth of God. That treasure is to be kept clean and pure and passed on to the next generation.

And so, he says, "Look, Timothy, you can't quit. You can't bail out. You can't drop the baton. You cannot step out of the line. You are a link in a living chain, and somebody, namely me, gave the truth to you. And there are faithful and competent men waiting for you to give it to them so they can give it to others." That's the living chain of which every believer is a part. And to whom is Timothy to give the truth? Faithful men. That speaks of their spiritual character. They're loyal; they're trustworthy with the truth.

So, a strong Christian is a diligent student of Scripture. A strong Christian is faithful to articulate that Scripture. A strong Christian is loyal to the intent and content of that Scripture and involved in the training of others who can train others still. So, we're teachers. Every believer has received truth to be passed on. Whose purity should be guarded.

## The Soldier

The second metaphor is that of a soldier. MacArthur writes:

> You see, Christianity is not a spectator event. Christianity is not a game; Christianity is a war. It's hard for us to understand that because we live in an environment that while philosophically is hostile to Christianity legally and politically is not. And so, we're not constantly being battered by the system, nor do we have to give our life in standing for the faith.

But nevertheless we are in a war. The forces of good versus the forces of evil. And everyone is a soldier on one side or the other. There are only the two sides, there is no Switzerland in the battle for heaven and hell. There is no middle ground. It's like electrons — they are either positive or negative. No middle ground. So we are all soldiers. If you're not a soldier for Christ, you're a soldier for the enemy of Christ.

We all know stories of warfare for our faith. Places where liberties we take for granted are impinged upon, and most seriously, the fighting that takes place for the souls of those around us. I remember sitting with a guy the week before his marriage. He had been living with this young lady, and the Lord got ahold of him, and he realized what he was doing was wrong, so me and another friend were sitting with him trying to keep him distracted from powerful temptations that he was dealing with. We're in a war. MacArthur shares a story of someone possessed by a demon attacking and beating him up on the floor of his office.

### Fighting Apathy

But this war isn't all flashy and demonic. Sometimes we have to fight our own apathy. We have all been there. I'm tired, I don't feel like doing X, Y and Z. I don't want to go help replace their broken window, even though it would give me a couple hours with people who I haven't seen in a while. I'm tired, I don't feel like doing this, or that. I don't want to vacuum, I don't want to deal with this challenging person, or that one. We all have been there and felt that in one situation or another. But I think we need to think of it in terms of being a soldier in a war. The lost around us are the battleground, so when you are working your way down your mental decision tree, and you ask yourself, do I have the energy for this — could my presence at this activity, awkward conversation, or event, impact someone's eternity? If it's even a maybe, then we have to soldier through it, that's what soldiers do.

Secondly, I think this applies to our brothers and sisters as well. They aren't lost. But, our enemy would love to see Christians sidetracked by anything unrelated to the war. They would love to see Christians get confused and turn their guns on other Christians. The enemy would love to see us hurting instead of helping each other.

### Point the Gun the Right Way

So when we see a brother or sister wandering, help remind them which way to point the gun. I saw a meme this week that showed a guy with a shotgun held in a wildly wrong way. The barrel of the gun was over his shoulder, the stock of the gun was pointed towards the enemy. Basically even though he was pointed towards the enemy, his gun was pointed behind him. We need to gently, lovingly help them live life pointed in the right direction. So when you see your brother or sister struggling, go to them and help. Love on them. Help them get the gun pointed the right way.

Sometimes helping someone means assisting in physical things they can't manage. My parents brought home the world's heaviest couch this past week, and it took four of us to get it up the stairs. Willie came by with his strong back, and professional experience as a furniture mover and helped us make it work. Wes was instrumental too. We needed help, and they came alongside.

One time when I was with World Team I heard a missionary talking about how he was getting old and needy. That he was struggling to do a lot of the things that he had been able to do as a younger person. Anyone ever felt that way? He went on to say his ministry had never been more fruitful. The people he was trying to minister to were now coming to him, working alongside him for an hour or two, and then he'd buy them lunch and minister to them some more. Instead of short conversations, he now had two uninterrupted hours of one on one time with people he was trying to share the gospel with. Even infirmities can be a blessing. I think especially we men get into a mode where we don't want to ask for help. We don't want to admit weakness. We don't want people to think less of us, and yet God has a purpose even in our weakness. Don't miss out on blessings because you're upset about how you got there. A book I love to read, by my favorite author has a line in it that is repeated over and over: Journey before Destination. Don't be so upset by the journey that you miss the purpose of the journey.

### Soldiers Don't Clock Out

Lastly, as MacArthur pointed out, soldiers aren't soldiers from 9-5. Soldiers are soldiers 24/7. Could you imagine being a Ukrainian soldier on the battlefield, and at 5:01, one minute after your shift ends, the Russians come up over the next ridge, and you just sit there and do nothing?

I read about how the Russians are taking children from Ukraine, and making them available for international adoption. How heartbreaking. Families being broken up by war, and their kids maybe being real orphans, or maybe they've just been kidnapped. The dust hasn't even settled long enough to know the truth, and yet these kids are being sent away to new families.

So you're that soldier, and you know they are coming for your family, and maybe will kidnap your kids. But now it's 5:01, one minute after your shift ends. Would you sit there sipping tea, and watch them destroy your home, and take your kids? Certainly not. You wouldn't run up to the enemy commander and say "Mr. General sir, would you mind coming back tomorrow? I'm on break right now you see. It's been a long day of fighting, and I just think I'm going to want a good night's sleep and a shower first." Certainly not. You fight like your life is on the line. Like your kids' lives depend on it. You fight until you drop dead, or of exhaustion in a situation like that. You are not just a soldier from 9 to 5. You're a soldier till the war is over, or you're in glory with God.

Soldiers know what it is to suffer deprivations and hardship. But soldiers know they have a purpose in fighting. That lives are on the line, and if it were just lives on the line, that might be one thing. There are people that have walked away from war over just lives being on the line. But for our battle the stakes are so much higher. Life is short. But eternity is long, and the stakes of our war are eternal.

MacArthur writes:

> This is war, and you expect that you're going to get your share of suffering. You're going to have your wounds and your pain. It's just how it is in war. You need to understand it. Boy, that's important for us to understand. I think there are people running around, purveying the idea that when you become a Christian, everything is just perfect, that all Jesus wants you to do is come to give your life to Him, and He'll take away all anxiety, all difficulty, all trial. I don't hear anybody saying, "Come to Christ and pick up your armor and go to the frontline and fight a lifelong war with the demons of hell." But that's the issue. That's the reality.

Any other soft-pedaling of the truth makes false converts that fall when the first breeze of opposition comes along. We need to be teaching people well, so they have deep and strong roots, so that when the war comes they can not just endure, but be useful along the way.

### Don't Get Entangled

This comes with the warning not to be entangled with the affairs of civilian life. Back to our analogy of the soldier on the front line. Imagine what it would be like — those Russians came up and over the hill, and just then you realize you forgot to pay your cable bill. So you run across the street to the internet cafe, so you can log in and pay your cable bill. No — you have things that are more important in that moment. You have to focus on staying alive. There will be time for the cable bill later. Pay your bills. But don't get so entangled in the mundane and practical things that you sideline yourself from what's most important.

So strong Christian, Paul says you're a soldier. Soldiers can expect their fair share of rough treatment. A soldier is on duty 24/7, a soldier is engaged in battle on the front lines all the time with the enemy of God. So strong Christians don't do anything that would entangle themselves with this world, and make themselves vulnerable. All of these things, we do for the glory of God. Someone read verse 4. We do these things to please our Savior, so that one day when we get to eternity our Savior will look at us and say "well done my good and faithful servant."

This whole line of thinking reminds me of the song "Arlington" by Trace Adkins. I like Adkins' voice, but I don't like a lot of the messages in his songs, and I don't agree with everything even in THIS song. But it still brings tears to my eyes, the thought of a man using his life as a shield for others, and losing his life in that noble task and being told job well done by a grateful commander. That will be our reward one day too. Standing face to face with our Savior, and being told job well done.

## The Athlete

Thirdly — and we will probably go over a bit today, but I think that's OK — the third picture is that of an athlete. Remember back in this time frame the Greeks had their original Olympic games. They ended by decree of the Roman emperor a few hundred years later, but they were still a thing at this point, and might have even been what Paul was thinking about when he referenced athletes in verse 5 competing for the crown. In those games, there were three requirements that every athlete had to meet in order to compete. Firstly they had to be Greek. Second, they had a ten month training period they had to participate in. At the end of which they had to stand before a statue of that pagan god Zeus and swear they had abided by the training regimen to the best of their abilities, or may Zeus strike them down where they stand. And finally, if it was found out they had broken any of the rules, or that he hadn't prepared for ten full months, that person was instantly disqualified and made a pariah of Greek culture for trying to claim honors that weren't rightfully earned.

MacArthur writes:

> But it is inherent in the athletic metaphor that there is a striving to be the best, and that there are the concomitant sacrifices that make that a reality. That's the picture. It's the picture of self-discipline. It's the picture of self-denial and self-sacrifice and tremendous effort. How many times have we seen an athlete run a race and literally, at the very peak of their speed, come across the finish line and then have — having totally exhausted all their human faculties — fall into a faint, and hit the ground, having expended every ounce of available energy? That's the athlete.

And for what prize? The momentary acclaim of men? That crown of leaves the Olympic athletes of old won? Elite athletes work incredibly hard their entire lives to reach the peak of their skill and at the end of the day, the very best they can earn are things that are corruptible, that moth and rust or thief can steal or destroy.

## The Farmer

So we had teacher — who guards the purity of what's been entrusted to us, and passes it on. The soldier that is on the front lines of battle, never looking back, pressing on in the face of all adversity. The elite athlete that trains for this like it's his job, every day. And now we have a farmer. I heard a statistic the other day that 1% of the population of our country feeds the other 99% three meals a day, every day, and many meals that go outside of our country besides. Farmers are hard working, industrious. MacArthur says the word here for farmer is a combination of several words, and carries with it the idea of "working to the point of exhaustion, to wear yourself out, to sweat, to strain."

Each of the other three come with a built-in reward. The teacher has the joy of teaching, and seeing someone learn and grow. The soldier has the adrenaline rush of being in the thick of battle. The athlete has that rush of that moment of achievement, and crowning. But farmers don't really have any of that. I can relate to farming perhaps more than some of the others. Because it's a lot of hard work. Farmers work sun up to sun down. I farmed peppers for a while — we had to pull up rocks, plow the fields, sow the seeds, pull the weeds, kill the pests, harvest the fruit, and repeat. Or for those who farm sheep like I do, you care for those sheep every day, and then they give up and die, with no discernible issue. You feed them, you water them, you lead them beside the still waters, and when they are sick you comfort them, you treat their wounds and illnesses. Frequently at great personal cost. Only to be sad again when it's time for them to go to a new home, or when they just randomly give up on life.

There is no thrill, or built-in reward like the athlete, soldier or teacher has. With farming sometimes it goes the way you hope and sometimes not. Sometimes all your lambs make it to adulthood, sometimes you get a weird gene and all of them are suddenly susceptible to parasites where they hadn't been before, and half of them die. Sometimes weevils kill your peppers, or floods drown them, or birds eat them before you ever have a chance to enjoy them, and sometimes you have good years. Much of life, I think, is spent here in the doldrums of farming.

---

## Closing: Rewards at the Feet of Jesus

So then those of us who God has called to be farmers work hard to the point of exhaustion, like the soldier, like the athlete, like the teacher. But you don't get the instant gratification of the other fields. You have to wait patiently for the rewards that come largely in the next life. Where one day we will be rewarded by God, and we will be able to place those rewards at the feet of Jesus in adoration.

What a day that will be. What a glorious day. Where we can lay the sum total of our life's worth at the feet of Jesus in worship. That is a reward worth striving for.

It won't be easy. But it will be worth it. And it all starts with the gospel.
