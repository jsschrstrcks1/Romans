## The Sons of Korah

Before we read, I want to draw your attention to the top of the passage — just above verse 1, where it says: *"For the director of music. According to gittith. Of the Sons of Korah."*

That's called the superscription. *Super* being above, *script* being the verse. A few things in it are worth noticing.

First — these were given to the directors of music. These weren't dusty, dry passages kept in a book on someone's shelf. They were originally meant to be sung and performed. A lot of people skip the superscriptions, but there's real depth tucked away in them.

I love the Psalms. We talked last week about how thankfulness can guard your heart. The Psalms remind you what you're thankful for.

Second — this psalm was written by the Sons of Korah. Anyone know who they are? Most people have seen the name in the Psalms somewhere, but they go back a long way. Back to the wilderness years, when the Israelites carried the ark of the covenant and set up the tabernacle tent.

The sons of Korah were part of the tribe of Levi but were not eligible to serve as priests — that role belonged to the sons of Aaron. Nevertheless, Moses gave them a job. They were responsible for carrying and caring for much of the treasure that belonged inside the tabernacle — candelabras and all manner of decorated, sacred items. Other Levites could load their items onto carts and let oxen haul them. Not the sons of Korah. Each piece had to be wrapped individually and carried by hand. It was a lot of extra work. Work that required trust.

Fast forward: Solomon's temple is built. The sons of Korah have transitioned — they clean a little, guard the sacred items, watch the doors. Scripture says they were the gatekeepers. Which means they spent most of their time just outside the temple. Privy to what was happening inside. An important role.

When I was in college, a lot of guys paid their way through school with security jobs — sitting in a room, watching monitors, trying to keep people from stealing. One friend caught a man trying to walk out with thirty items of clothing layered underneath a giant winter parka. In Florida. In summer. He left nothing to the imagination. Clear as day.

But more often, the stories were mundane. Stared at a screen. Almost fell asleep. Did fall asleep — don't tell anyone. Did homework and at least got paid for it. That kind of thing.

I imagine the sons of Korah had something of the same experience. Watch the door. Don't let anyone take the treasure. Dust the candle holders. Go home. Day in and day out.

Even in something as stunning as Solomon's temple — where every item represented the very best of what mankind could produce in gold, silver, precious stones, and carved wood — even then, I imagine, elements of it could get old. Like working at Disney. I read a piece about Disney's operational culture. A Disney princess said a day didn't go by where some unruly kid didn't hit, spit on, or vomit on her. It's magic until you work there, and then it loses its shine.

With all of that as backdrop, let's read Psalm 84.

## Psalm 84

Doesn't that backstory add depth to it?

Every day. All day. Watching the door. And yet — verse 1: *How lovely is your dwelling place.* Verse 2: *My soul longs, yes, faints for the courts of the Lord; my heart and flesh sing for joy to the living God.* Verse 10: *Better is one day in your courts than a thousand elsewhere.*

## Taking It for Granted

It's so easy to take the beauty around us for granted.

Ask people who live in the mountains where they'd go if they could go anywhere — they'd say the ocean, or Alaska, or Europe. Ask people at the coast, they'd say the mountains. Ask an Alaskan, they'd say somewhere warm. People always seem to wish they were somewhere else. But not the sons of Korah. Being even in the vicinity of God's presence was so marvelous, so extraordinary, that they would trade thirty years of anything else — any beach, any time in the mountains, thirty years on a cruise ship in the most beautiful place imaginable — for a single day of guard duty outside the courts of God.

They loved their job. But it wasn't really about the job, was it? It wasn't the temple. It wasn't anything to do with where they were. It had everything to do with *who* was there.

## Emotions in Worship

Verse 2 jumps out at me: *My heart and flesh sing for joy.*

We as Baptists tend to be more reserved than a lot of other traditions. There are groups where it's all about the emotional experience — people jumping, flags waving, the whole production. For them, how you feel carries the service. We've watched those movements drift until it's mostly spectacle with little Word. We don't want that. We want Scripture preached faithfully. Substance over production. We'd rather hear from God than get a funny feeling — pizza can do that. We want to grow.

And yet.

There is room for emotion in the worship of God. I think we can swing that pendulum too far the other way, and call it discernment, when really it's just distance.

The Westminster Shorter Catechism says the chief end of man is to glorify God and enjoy him forever. When was the last time any Baptist you knew was so moved by God that he literally cried out?

Have you ever been to a really good concert and sung along? I was at a movie once, and the man behind us had been genuinely moved by the closing song. He didn't care who knew. He sang out loud, off key, as loud as he could, because something had gotten into him. The world does that for movies and concerts. When was the last time you were so moved by God that you wanted to shout?

Now — if our emotions are in the driver's seat, that's too far. If our decisions and our theology are driven by feelings, that's a problem. But honest, open expressions of genuine worship? That's something we could probably grow in.

Scripture is full of it.

Psalm 66:1-2 — *Shout for joy to God, all the earth; sing the glory of his name; give to him glorious praise!*

Ezra 3:11 — *"For he is good, for his steadfast love endures forever toward Israel." And all the people shouted with a great shout when they praised the Lord, because the foundation of the house of the Lord was laid.*

Luke 19:37-38 — *As he was drawing near — already on the way down the Mount of Olives — the whole multitude of his disciples began to rejoice and praise God with a loud voice for all the mighty works that they had seen, saying, "Blessed is the King who comes in the name of the Lord! Peace in heaven and glory in the highest!"*

Psalm 47:1 — *Clap your hands, all peoples! Shout to God with loud songs of joy!*

Isaiah 12:6 — *Shout, and sing for joy, O inhabitant of Zion, for great in your midst is the Holy One of Israel.*

We have so much reason to shout.

## Twelve Things About God in This Passage

David Platt has pointed out at least twelve attributes of God present in Psalm 84. Just quickly —

He is the covenant-keeping Lord. He commands the heavenly armies. He is the *living* God — not a dead god, not a carved one, but a God who is alive and active. He is a *personal* God: *my* King, *my* God. We can actually know him.

Think about what that means. The Lord who keeps his promises, who commands all heavenly hosts, who is living and active and faithful — not just to this generation but back to Jacob — is *my* God.

When I was in college, two of my friends started dating each other. They eventually got married and have been together twelve years. But when they first made it official, both of them couldn't stop bringing it up. My friend was walking around saying "Sarah — you know, she's *my* girlfriend now." Like it was an accomplishment just to be dating her. And she was the same way — she'd just drop into any conversation "well, you know, *my boyfriend* says..."

At the wedding reception he walked around saying *look at this. Look who I get to call mine.* It's that same idea. The church is the bride of Christ. He treasures us. He loved us so much he sent his Son to die for us. Christ loved us enough to go to the cross willingly. That's *my* God.

Back to the list — faithful God, source of light and life, a shield. And then this: verse 11 calls God a *sun*. This is, as far as I know, one of the most direct places in Scripture where God is compared to the sun. That's remarkable given the ancient context — many pagan religions worshiped the sun itself. But the sun warms the earth, encourages life, is necessary for human flourishing. Scientists have documented that prolonged deprivation of sunlight depletes vitamin D and leads to depression. Life *requires* the sun. God is saying — I am that. I am what your life actually runs on.

Verse 5: the man whose strength is in God — his life runs on God the way ours runs on the sun. When we are weak, God provides.

The God of angel armies, who keeps his promises, who is living and moving and faithful not just now but stretching all the way back through time, who provides and protects and is the only true God — and he is *mine.* We can know him personally.

## The Valley of Baca

Notice verse 6 — the valley of Baca.

This is a real place, and the name means "tears." A valley of weeping.

I know most of your stories. I know that being human means going through those valleys. I know some of you listening today have been through extreme hardship.

I'm reminded of a family — I won't name them — who eighteen years ago lost their little girl to a brutal murder. She was one year old. I was just a teenager myself, and I remember crying on my own. I wasn't the mother or the father. I was just a friend of the family. I can't imagine what they carried. Their precious, innocent baby — gone in the worst possible way.

That's what I think of when I think of the valley of Baca.

And yet — God saw them through it. Not around it. Through it.

Sometimes he uses his people to do that. We have someone who joins us remotely — I won't name her either — who's had a hard stretch. Lost her job. Laid off because of the pandemic. Unemployment ran out. Trying to figure out how to pay rent, buy groceries, keep the lights on. And someone else in this little congregation — also anonymous — hears about it and goes out and fills a car with groceries for her.

God is using this small church body in real, practical ways. We have a need over here, and he moves someone over there.

I think of other stories too, that aren't mine to tell. Health crises. Real needs. Quiet, grinding difficulty. I was talking Thursday with a boy who told me his mother had left — packed up and moved to another state, abandoned him and his siblings. This will be their first Christmas without her.

No matter how dark the night gets — no matter how deep the tears in the valley of Baca — God is there. Moving on behalf of his people.

We had to journey to the temple in the Old Testament. In the New Testament, we *are* the temple.

## The Sparrows

Last — verse 3. The sparrows.

David Platt has spoken about this image in a way that's worth sitting with:

> *"The Bible talks about God being omnipresent, dwelling everywhere on earth and in heaven. So when you think tabernacle or temple, don't think the only place where God dwells. Instead, think the place that God shows in the Old Testament to dwell in a particular, powerful way among His people. And if that's the case, this psalmist wanted to be there. He longed for communion with God in the courts of the tabernacle or the temple. He loved that place, because he longed for God's presence. He even looks up into the structure of this place, and he sees sparrows and swallows that have set up nests there. And he says, 'That is best of all.' It's great imagery — the presence of God as the place where the humble find a home. The sparrow used throughout Scripture to describe a humble, lowly, common, seemingly worthless bird. Jesus pointed out in Matthew 10 how two of them are sold for a penny, yet this simple bird finds majestic meaning by having a house in the presence of God. It's really a great description, when you think about it, of the people of God: humble, lowly, common people who find majestic meaning in gathering before God.*
>
> *Donald Grey Barnhouse, one-time pastor of Tenth Presbyterian Church in Philadelphia, compared the church to sparrows like this. He said, 'I look down some little street and see a humble chapel, where a group of simple people worship the Lord in the beauty of holiness, despised and rejected of men, even as was their Lord, and I know that this is the rich reality of spiritual truth. Here are the sparrows who find their nest at the cross of Jesus Christ. Here is worthlessness that finds its worth because the Savior died.'*
>
> *And then — the swallow. A fast-moving bird, back and forth in different directions. And yet, here in the presence of God, that same bird builds a nest and settles.*"

Augustine said it plainly: *"Our hearts are restless until they find their rest in Thee."*

If you're here today and you're a Christian looking for rest — Scripture says cast your cares upon him, because he cares for you.

If you're here and you don't yet have a relationship with Jesus Christ —

## Source Notes — Verify Before Print Use

**Sons of Korah / Kohath clarification:** The shoulder-carrying rule (Numbers 7:9 — "to the sons of Kohath he gave none, because they were charged with the service of the holy things that had to be carried on the shoulder") applies to the **Kohathites** broadly. Korah was a son of Kohath (Numbers 16:1), so his descendants were Kohathites. However, the sons of Korah *specifically* (the survivors of the Numbers 16 rebellion — Numbers 26:11) are referenced in Chronicles primarily as gatekeepers and singers (1 Chronicles 9:19, 26:1), not as the tabernacle carriers. The sermon uses the carrying-by-hand detail as their backstory — this is defensible by their Kohathite lineage but conflates the broader clan with the specific Korah branch. Consider either broadening to "the Kohathites" or noting the lineage connection explicitly.

**Westminster Shorter Catechism:** "The chief end of man is to glorify God and enjoy him forever" is from the **Westminster Shorter Catechism, Question 1** — not the Westminster Confession of Faith. Corrected in the manuscript above.

**Psalm 66:2 (ESV):** ESV reads "give to him glorious praise!" — not "make his praise glorious." The manuscript above uses the ESV wording. Verify the reading being used in the service.

**Psalm 66:1-2, Ezra 3:11, Luke 19:37-38, Psalm 47:1, Isaiah 12:6** — all checked against ESV and match or are corrected above.

**"Only place in Scripture" equating God with the sun:** Psalm 84:11 is indeed a striking and direct usage. However, Malachi 4:2 says "the sun of righteousness shall rise with healing in its wings" (ESV) — a messianic passage also involving sun imagery. The claim is not wrong but should be softened to "one of the most direct" rather than "the only place." Adjusted above.

**Psalm 84:5 — "God is our strength":** ESV reads "Blessed is the man whose strength is in you, in whose heart are the highways to Zion." The text frames strength as residing *in God*, not as God being our strength directly. The distinction is minor but worth noting if preaching it closely.

**David Platt quote:** Extended and embedded. Needs source sermon title, series, or book before print attribution. Possible source: *Secret Church*, a sermon series, or his Psalm 84 expository sermon — unconfirmed. Do not attribute in print without exact citation.

**Donald Grey Barnhouse quote:** Quoted within the Platt passage. Barnhouse (1895–1960) was pastor of Tenth Presbyterian Church, Philadelphia — that detail checks out. The quote itself needs independent source verification (likely from his writings or published sermons). Do not use in print without confirming source.

**Augustine — "Our hearts are restless":** *Confessions*, Book 1, Chapter 1: "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee." Verified. This is one of the most well-attested quotes in Christian literature. Safe to use with attribution to *Confessions* 1.1.
