While you're turning there — our people group of the week: the Dar Hamid of Sudan.

A friend of mine from college — he was in his 80s — was a missionary to Sudan, and traveled all over the place drinking the water and eating the food. It was a harrowing thing in my mind for a person his age to do. But for him, in faith, he would travel all over Sudan sharing the gospel.

People go through all the time selling charms and trinkets promised to ward off spirits or encourage good health. They put more stock in the charms than they do in Islam. ### Protestants, Catholics, and the Word of God

This is an important point of distinction between Protestants and Catholics. We often tell the story of Martin Luther — a Catholic monk — nailing his 95 Theses to the door of the Wittenburg church in Germany. The contents of which started a return to the Word of God. To that point, and even still in the Catholic church, the words of the pope were held in higher regard than the words of God.

Can God speak through a man? Certainly. I believe that happens every week in pulpits around the country. But when the word of a man is elevated to the same extent — or even higher — than the Word of God, that becomes a problem. Who is our ultimate authority? God. And God alone.

Many popes have been well-meaning. But a significant number were not. There was a time that for the right donation, even the papacy was for sale — and so what you end up with are papal traditions and sayings that directly contradict Scripture and contradict each other.

The opinions of popes change, but the Word of God is forever. Unchanging. We can look back across time and compare copies of Scripture from thousands of years ago to the copies we have today, and there are only a handful of differences — mostly a line duplicated, or spelling issues with someone's name. But as you would expect of a document brought into being by divine means, it has been protected by God as well. Not a single one of those differences affects any issue of doctrine or theology. All the things we believe remain untouched by time. Almost as though God is protecting his word.

We do not see that in papal decrees. One day a pope might say the sky is blue, and the next pope might say the sky is violet with pink polka dots. Man is changing and broken; only God is not.

> "Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever."
> — Hebrews 13:8 (ESV)

*[Note: The source manuscript quotes this as "God is the same yesterday, today and forever." The ESV text names Jesus Christ specifically. The theological point stands, but the citation should be used as written.]*

So God's Word is to be preferred over man's. We will get more into that next week.

Almost every single one of Luther's theses — 92 out of 95 — had to do with man adding things to Scripture. The purchasing of indulgences, for instance. For the right check to the church, they believed you could get out of trouble. Planning to cheat on your wife? Make a donation to the church, and they would argue it's excused by God.

Any sin could be excused — for a price.

I used to work in rental management. There was this one particular house — an elderly couple dying of cancer had left everything they owned behind and gone to live with their children out of state. A whole lifetime's worth of things they cared about, tossed away. During the clean-out I came across an indulgence they had purchased that excused them from church for a year. I have no idea what that cost, but I'm sure it was insanely expensive. It makes me angry to think about the church preying upon the elderly dying of cancer — evicted because they can't make rent, but told they're going to hell if they don't buy an indulgence. Worse, that kind of thing is nowhere to be found in Scripture.

The words of popes change all the time — there are Catholic organizations that track exactly this kind of thing. Under one pope they grow more liberal; under another, more conservative. But does God ever change? No.

So how can the ever-changing word of man be held in the same — or higher — regard as what the unchanging Word of God says? It can't be. This is at least part of what spurred Luther to write. He never intended to leave the Catholic church. He just wanted to draw Catholics back toward Scripture. At the end of the day, the Catholics forced him out, and the Lutheran church was born — created by Luther's followers. As Baptists, we trace our heritage back to Luther, among others.

*[Note: The claim that "92 out of 95" theses were about "man adding things to scripture" is directionally true — indulgences were a central concern — but imprecise. The 95 Theses dealt specifically with indulgences and repentance. The broader authority conflict developed later. See source note above.]*

### Man's Condition

This message is mostly centered around Sola Gratia, but it necessitates we look at our own condition first. So let's start there.

What is man capable of? To answer that, I think we need to look at man's condition. We won't be turning there, but I'll summarize Ephesians 2 for you. It says we are dead in sin — followers of the world, followers of the devil, sons of disobedience, subservient to the passions of our flesh, carrying out the desires of the flesh. By nature we are children of wrath. It says we are separated from Christ due to our sin — alienated, hostile, slaves to the law. Think about that. What can a dead body do on its own? Absolutely nothing.

We have horror shows in our culture that prove this. We are fascinated with the idea of the dead rising up and attacking the living — the dead walking down streets, the dead eating people's brains. Millions of dollars each year are made scaring people around this concept. Yet is this not exactly how we are apart from Christ?

Jesus said as much in our passage tonight. Verse 44:

> "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him. And I will raise him up on the last day."
> — John 6:44 (ESV)

And again in verse 65:

> "And he said, 'This is why I told you that no one can come to me unless it is granted him by the Father.'"
> — John 6:65 (ESV)

Apart from God, what we have to offer is what? Zilch. Zero. Zip. Nada. Nothing. Apart from the grace and mercy of God, we are a dead body walking around.

A famous preacher of the Great Awakening said this: "You contribute nothing to your salvation except the sin that made it necessary!" — Jonathan Edwards.

### Grace Already at Work

The only thing we bring to the table is need.

I saw an interview with R.C. Sproul — he's a pastor out of Orlando. He said he had been talking with a man and outright asked him if he loved God as much as he thought he ought to. Now in truth, no one can say yes to this. If you think you do, I challenge you to look squarely into Scripture and find your reflection — because I promise you, what you will see is just how far short we all fall from loving God like we ought.

So we are dead in our trespasses, children of wrath. Apart from Christ we can't do anything good. Because of our choice to sin, we have selected a nature that prevents us from willingly following Christ of our own accord. We can't get away from it, we can't overcome it — we can't escape it even by turning our lives around. We are dead. And what can a dead body do?

But that is where Christ comes in. Before we can even follow him, he has to do a work in us. Before we even have an interest in following Christ, he's already been at work on our hearts. This is the beginning of grace. When governed by our own ambitions we spurn Christ — but he works on our hearts so that we can accept him. Verse 44 makes this clear: no one can come to him unless the Father draws him.

We are, after all, just a dead body walking around. But there is hope in this. If you have been considering following Christ — that means God is doing a work in you, enabling you to even think that way. He is working on you, just as he has worked and is working in me and in all of us who are believers in Jesus Christ. Chipping away at our cold hearts of stone. Gently, for our sake. He could simply slam a hammer into our stony parts and shatter it away — but most of the time that isn't what's best for us. So he chisels and chips. Like Michelangelo: every hammer strike critical, every chip necessary for Christ's masterpiece. Every struggle we experience is a hammer strike, and every pain a chip being sculpted away for the glory of God. It's not just random struggle — it's God making something magnificent. We shattered the image of God in creation through sin, and yet God is slowly restoring that in us, moment by moment. He's not surprised when you struggle. He allowed it to mold you and to shape you.

### The Stakes

Much of the struggle we have chosen for ourselves as a result of choosing to sin. In so doing, we chose to separate ourselves from God. If our earthly life comes to an end and we are still in that same condition — apart from God, apart from a relationship with Christ — what happens to us? If we die separate from God, we remain separate from God into eternity. In short, we choose hell.

### The Danger of Cultural Christianity

Want to hear something scary? Is your relationship with God marked by obedience rooted in faith?

I heard a guy preach recently that being a Christian in the Bible Belt is dangerous. Because going to church is easy. It's easy to bring a dish to a potluck. It's easy to stay late and vacuum — and I am glad some of you do! It's easy to go to Sunday school and Bible study, and it's easy to look real good and still be as lost as a goat. Lost as a tare. Lost as chaff among the wheat.

So is your relationship with God marked by obedience rooted in faith? Because if not — if you are making no movement, if there are no marks of obedience to God in your life — you are not a Christian. You should consider yourself lost and in danger of damnation.

You might think that's hard, or mean-spirited, or cruel. But I think it's more cruel to allow you to choose hell without being warned. I think it's mean-spirited not to share the gospel with people.

### Not Perfect — Just Born Again

Does God expect us to be perfect? No. He provided the cross for that. He expects us to try to meet his standard — to push through, and even though we trip and fall, to keep getting back up and pressing forward time and time again. If we were all perfect and faultless, God wouldn't have had to send his Son to die. But we are anything but perfect, and God loved us anyway. So he sent his Son to die on the cross for us.

---

I have this mental picture. When my sister and I were kids — and indeed with her kids now — they just absolutely love to pile on to you and let you drag them around. Wyley, when he was younger, loved to lay on the floor and hold on to your ankles while you try to walk. Lilly and Violet sit on top of your shoe and wrap their arms and legs about your ankle. My niece Paris loved to jump on my back when I'm not paying attention and wrap her arms about my neck and just dangle there.

One at a time, it's not so bad — you can kind of struggle along. But when ALL of them are hanging on to you, dragging on the floor, sitting on your feet, and hanging from your neck, it becomes really hard to move. Frequently I can't move at all. It's easy to stumble, easy to fall, easy to get tripped up by an extra couple hundred pounds of kids dangling off you.

But is it not the same with sin? Without the work of Christ in your heart you don't even have the desire to try. As Scripture says, the things of the Lord are foolishness to those who don't believe.

> "The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned."
> — 1 Corinthians 2:14 (ESV)

Those who don't even try should consider themselves lost. So many people in the cultural South are Christians not because they follow Christ but because of perceived social benefits.

The Scripture does not say you must be better. Scripture says you must be born again.

### Sola Gratia Defined

And a well-known Baptist preacher wrote this on the topic:

> "Grace alone. The words *sola gratia* mean that human beings have no claim upon God. That is, God owes us nothing except just punishment for our many and very willful sins. Therefore, if he does save sinners — which he does in the case of some but not all — it is only because it pleases him to do it. Indeed, apart from this grace and the regenerating work of the Holy Spirit that flows from it, no one would be saved, since in our lost condition, human beings are not capable of winning, seeking out, or even cooperating with God's grace. By insisting on 'grace alone' the Reformers were denying that human methods, techniques, or strategies in themselves could ever bring anyone to faith. It is grace alone expressed through the supernatural work of the Holy Spirit that brings us to Christ, releasing us from our bondage to sin and raising us from death to spiritual life."

*[Note: Attributed in the source manuscript to "James Petigrew Boyce" as "famous Baptist Preacher of the last century." Attribution is questionable — see source note. The attribution has been softened here pending verification. Do not restore for print or projection without confirming the source.]*

---

So God owes us nothing. Absolutely nothing. What we deserve is eternity separated from him — but those of us who are believers in Jesus Christ, he has chosen to save. Not because of any merit, any height of virtue, or anything we could achieve in our own strength. We are, after all, dead according to Scripture. As Ephesians 2:8–9 says:

> "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast."
> — Ephesians 2:8–9 (ESV)

*[Note: The source manuscript quotes this as a paraphrase. The ESV text is supplied above.]*

When God intercedes on our behalf it is an undeserved blessing. If you ask him and mean it, you will be saved — Scripture says so.

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with concern**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's thesis — that salvation is entirely by God's grace and that human beings contribute nothing to it — does arise from John 6:44 and 6:65, which plainly assert that no one can come to Christ unless the Father draws him. The text's main point (divine initiative in salvation, the total inability of the natural man) is the sermon's main point. Pass.

*Authority Test*: The preacher submits to the text's uncomfortable claim — that natural persons cannot come to God — and does not soften it. He names total depravity plainly and applies it without hedging. Pass.

Concern: The sermon's textual anchor is almost entirely John 6:44 and 6:65. The surrounding verses (43–51, 60–64) — which include Jesus' Bread of Life discourse and his dialogue with the grumbling disciples — receive almost no treatment. The text is used as a launching pad more than a sustained passage. The exposition stays close enough to avoid failing the gate, but the treatment is thin.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the Reformation/Catholic history section (roughly the first third of the sermon) is functioning as a semi-separate sermon on Sola Scriptura that has been prepended to a sermon on Sola Gratia. The section makes the point that man's word (papal decrees) changes while God's Word does not — but this is Sola Scriptura's argument, not Sola Gratia's. The bridging sentence ("Almost every single one of Luther's theses... had to do with man adding things to Scripture") attempts to connect, but the link to grace is indirect. The preacher may not realize how much of the Reformation material is doing no work for the Sola Gratia thesis.

Additionally, the preacher likely does not see that the Billy Sunday section — though charming and energetic — arrives without landing. The quote is vivid, but it arrives in the middle of an application section and then disappears. It produces a surge of momentum that the sermon does not capitalize on.

The doctrinal material is largely correct, but it arrives as quoted authority rather than as exegetical discovery. The impression of depth exceeds the actual depth.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

John 6:51 — "the bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh" — is the theological climax of the assigned passage and is entirely absent from the sermon. This verse bridges the total depravity argument (vv. 44, 65) directly to the atonement. The crowd's grumbling (vv. 60–61) and the departure of many disciples (vv. 66–67, just outside the assigned range) provide the exact illustration the preacher is building toward with the cultural Christianity warning — the self-identified followers who walk away when the cost is revealed. The passage itself is a diagnostic of false discipleship, and the preacher missed it while preaching a sermon on the danger of false discipleship.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

**Fix 1 — Open John 6:51 and build the cross from the passage itself.**
After establishing total depravity from vv. 44 and 65, move to v. 51: "the bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh." This is how Jesus answers the inability problem — not with explanation, but with self-offering. The cross is not just an add-on to Sola Gratia; it is the means of grace. The passage hands the preacher the cross. Take it.

**Fix 2 — Write out the gospel call; remove the "[Gospel]" placeholder.**
The manuscript must contain the exhortation as written text. At minimum: What must the unconverted person believe? What must they repent of? What must they do tonight? The current ending is an invitation without content. Write it.

**Fix 3 — Tighten or relocate the Reformation history section.**
Either (a) compress the Sola Scriptura / papal authority material to two paragraphs as context-setting, or (b) relocate the indulgence illustration to the grace section where it naturally belongs — indulgences are a grace problem, not only a Scripture problem. The first third of the sermon is doing duty it was not assigned.

**Fix 4 — Use John 6:60–65's departing disciples as the cultural Christianity illustration.**
The preacher correctly identifies cultural Christianity as the great danger. The assigned text shows it happening in real time — disciples walking away when grace is hard. "After this many of his disciples turned back and no longer walked with him" (v. 66) is the biblical illustration for "lost as a goat in the Bible Belt." The preacher drove past the very verse that would have grounded the application in the text rather than in anecdote.

**Fix 5 — Build a climactic moment and end there.**
The Billy Sunday quote has the energy for a sermon climax. The Ephesians 2:8–9 citation has the doctrinal weight. Consider: (1) Billy Sunday quote as the passion of witness, (2) Ephesians 2:8–9 as the foundation of that witness, (3) a direct address to the room — believer and unbeliever both named and spoken to — and close. Do not add more material after the climax.
