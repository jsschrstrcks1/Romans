# Missionaries or Imposters
## A Sermon on Missions, Hell, and the Urgency of the Gospel

---

## Opening

Open with me to Revelation 20:11. While you're turning there I want to talk about missions for a minute.

We have been talking about missions lately, about our brothers and sisters in other countries enduring persecution. I've been listening to a lot of sermons on the topic, and one was by the president of our denomination's foreign mission board, the IMB.

He told a story about how he was serving in one of these countries up here on the wall — one of the black ones — in Indonesia. He was on the run, trying to meet up with another underground church. They were camping in the forest, waiting for contact, and after a long and fruitful day of ministry he was sitting there reading a book by a man named Tozer. And he thought to himself, "I bet God is really glad to have me on his team."

Let the arrogance of that sink in for a moment.

God — the one who spoke the universe into existence. The one who hung the stars in the sky. The one who has storehouses of snow. The one who in six days made a universe so complex that we have spent the next five thousand years trying to figure it out and have only scratched the surface. We're talking about the God who holds our molecules together.

A supreme and sovereign, omnipotent, all-knowing, omnipresent, perfectly all-powerful God like that — he doesn't *need* us to accomplish his will.

When I was little, my dad would say, "I brought you into this world, and I can take you out and make another one that looks just like you." While my dad's threat was empty and not exactly practical — that's something that our God could actually do. He could wipe us out and start over. With a word. We serve a mighty and powerful God.

---

## The Knowledge of the Holy

So my friend thought that — "I bet God is really glad to have me on his team" — and then opened his book and read the following. It's a long quote, but I think it's a good one. The book was *The Knowledge of the Holy* by A. W. Tozer. He wrote:

> So, were every man on earth to become atheist, it could not affect God in any way. He is what He is in Himself without regard to any other. To believe in Him adds nothing to His perfections; to doubt Him takes nothing away.
>
> Almighty God, just because He is almighty, needs no support. The picture of a nervous, ingratiating God fawning over men to win their favor is not a pleasant one; yet if we look at the popular conception of God, that is precisely what we see. Twentieth century Christianity has put God on charity. So lofty is our opinion of ourselves that we find it quite easy, not to say enjoyable, to believe that we are necessary to God. But the truth is that God is not greater for our being, nor would He be less if we did not exist. That we do exist is altogether of God's free determination, not by our desert nor by divine necessity.
>
> Probably the hardest thought of all for our natural egotism to entertain is that God does not need our help. We commonly represent Him as a busy, eager, somewhat frustrated Father hurrying about seeking help to carry out His benevolent plan to bring peace and salvation to the world; but, as said the Lady Julian, "I saw truly that God doeth all-thing, be it never so little." The God who worketh all things surely needs no help and no helpers.
>
> I fear that thousands of young persons enter Christian service from no higher motive than to help deliver God from the embarrassing situation His love has gotten Him into and His limited abilities seem unable to get Him out of.
> — A. W. Tozer, *The Knowledge of the Holy*

We are included in God's mission not because he *needs* us, but because he loves us enough to include us in his plan.

Does the God who hung the stars in the sky truly need us? Certainly not.

## I. The God Who Includes Us

Have you ever taken a child fishing? You don't take a kid fishing because you need them to help catch more fish. You take them because you love the child, and you want to do something you enjoy with the child. You don't take a child horseback riding because you *need* them to hold the horse down. You take them horseback riding because you love your kid, and you want to draw them deeper into your world.

That's what God does with us.

He doesn't need our hands. He wants our hearts. And when he invites us into his mission — to carry the gospel to the nations, to share the good news with our neighbors — it isn't because the almighty Creator of the universe is short-staffed. It's because he's a Father who wants his children beside him while he works.

---

## People Group of the Week

Before we go further, let me share some numbers that should break your heart.

Right now there are approximately 3.4 billion people in the world who are utterly and completely unreached. That's over 7,000 people groups. Seven thousand unique cultures, unique languages, unique peoples — and virtually no gospel witness among them.

Someone tell me what it means to be unreached.

Unreached means you could live your entire life — birth to death — and short of the direct intervention of God, you would never hear the gospel. Never have a chance to respond to the gospel. Never even see, much less possess, a Bible to read.

Take the country of Yemen. You've all heard of Yemen. It's one of the nations in black on our map. The entire country of Yemen has over 40 million people. Yet by some estimates you could fit every single Christian in Yemen in this room, and we would still have enough seats for all of us. The most conservative estimates place the number of believers in Yemen between 3,000 and perhaps 16,000 — in a nation of over 40 million. Under Yemeni law, conversion from Islam is a capital offense. Most Christians there practice in secret.

An unreached people group represents a desert, and the missionary an oasis of God's truth.

John Piper says that missions exists because worship doesn't. If we loved God like we should, if we worshiped God like we should, there wouldn't be lost people around us. The prophet Habakkuk gives us a picture of what God is working toward:

> For the earth will be filled with the knowledge of the glory of the LORD as the waters cover the sea.
> — Habakkuk 2:14 (ESV)

God's purpose is that knowledge of him spread across the world like a flooding ocean of truth.

Would you pray with me for Yemen?

*Father, we lift up the people of Yemen — over 40 million souls, the vast majority of whom have never heard the name of Jesus. We pray for the tiny remnant of believers there, practicing their faith in secret, risking their very lives. Strengthen them. Embolden them. Multiply them. Send laborers to Yemen — through radio, through the internet, through dreams and visions, through every means your sovereignty allows. For the glory of your name. Amen.*

## II. Our Own Back Yard

In truth, even global missions are neglected by the average Christian. Even national missions. Even state missions. Let's look in our own backyard.

The Orlando–Kissimmee–Sanford metropolitan area has nearly 3 million people. While surveys say around 18 percent identify as evangelical, we all know there's a difference between checking a box on a survey and truly knowing the Lord. The vast majority of those 3 million people are lost and heading to hell in our backyard.

Millions of people dying without the gospel. In our backyard.

I know people who live in Orlando. Lost people. I imagine most of us know people there too.

Now — it's a massive opportunity, because it draws millions of visitors every year. So does Tampa. Walt Disney World alone drew nearly 50 million visitors last year across its four parks. From places like Yemen. From China. From Indonesia. From Russia and Germany, and France, and England, and India. From each and every one of these countries colored black on our map. From Iraq and Afghanistan.

I want you to think about this for a minute. What do you think the average person in Afghanistan makes in a year? What's their salary? Anyone want to guess?

Around $400 a year. So it's safe to say it's not the average Afghan who's going to Disney for a week. It's the leaders. The wealthy. The people with power and influence and authority to affect actual change in their countries.

Think about this. Before the Taliban takeover in 2021, Ashraf Ghani was the president of Afghanistan. He had two children — Mariam and Tariq. Both were educated in the United States. Afghanistan is now under Taliban rule — one of the most closed and unreached nations on the planet. It is harder than ever to get the gospel into that country.

But the people from places like Afghanistan — the influential ones, the educated ones, the future leaders — they are still coming here. To our universities. To our tourist attractions. To our neighborhoods.

Orlando is an epicenter of Muslim activity. There are dozens of mosques spread around the city and into the suburbs. And nearly 68,000 people a day fly in and out of Tampa International Airport. Why do they call it international? Because flights come from overseas and go overseas.

We have people flying into our own backyard to experience our culture, our beaches, to learn our language, to try American food. Many of these people visit Pasco — and why not? An hour from Busch Gardens. Two hours from Disney.

We pass these people in the grocery stores, at the gas stations, at our local colleges, at Weeki Wachee, and basically everywhere we go. We are passing people that are lost and bound for hell. Will you stand idly by as they march toward an eternity in hell?

I was talking with a pastor a while back who said Islam is the biggest problem facing our country and that he would not share the gospel with a Muslim because they all deserve to go to hell. That is the wrong attitude to have toward these people. Every one of us deserves to go to hell. That's the whole point of the gospel — we are all sinners, every one of us, and yet God in his mercy offers us salvation through Christ.

The best way to address the issue of Islam is to tell them about Jesus. Meet them. Get to know one by name. Ask one to lunch sometime. They are very hospitable people. In general, girls will need to ask girls, and guys will need to ask guys — the mixing of company is frowned upon in their culture, especially American men speaking to Muslim women. But don't miss this opportunity to be involved with God's mission to the world. We live in a time and in a place where the people in the countries most difficult to reach are coming to our back door.

## III. The Reality of Hell (Revelation 20:11-15)

Don't raise your hand, but how many of you have shared the gospel with someone this week?

Statistically speaking, other than the pastor, in a group this size — less than a fraction of one percent of you have.

Hear me: I'm convinced that the American church either doesn't truly believe in the horrors of hell, or else they haven't truly tasted the majesty of God. One or the other. Because if you believed both — really believed — you could not keep silent.

Some of you know exactly who I'm talking about. There's a face in your mind right now. A coworker. A neighbor. A family member you love dearly. And you haven't said a word.

So we're going to talk about hell with the rest of our time. I want you to see the verses — the promises in Scripture — for what will happen to the lost people we know and love.

Let's start with Revelation 20:11-15:

> Then I saw a great white throne and him who was seated on it. From his presence earth and sky fled away, and no place was found for them. And I saw the dead, great and small, standing before the throne, and books were opened. Then another book was opened, which is the book of life. And the dead were judged by what was written in the books, according to what they had done. And the sea gave up the dead who were in it, Death and Hades gave up the dead who were in them, and they were judged, each one of them, according to what they had done. Then Death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire. This is the second death, the lake of fire. And if anyone's name was not found written in the book of life, he was thrown into the lake of fire.
> — Revelation 20:11-15 (ESV)

Everyone whose name is not written in the Lamb's book of life is bound for an eternity in hell. We've talked about this before. I want you to raise your hand if you know someone whose eternity you're unsure of. Keep that person in your mind as we walk through these verses. Picture them experiencing the things we're about to talk about. Imagine the outcome of their life choices, and how it's going to affect them.

We're going to be sprinting through Scripture, because I want you to read these verses — to see them for yourselves.

---

### The Fire That Is Not Quenched

Mark 9:48-49, speaking of hell, says:

> 'where their worm does not die and the fire is not quenched.' For everyone will be salted with fire.
> — Mark 9:48-49 (ESV)

Salt is a preservative. It is a sign of the sealing of a covenant. Salt in a wound burns. Sulfur forms naturally in salt domes. So I think this is speaking in part to the horrors of hell and their permanence. You may have heard of brimstone — in old English, the word meant "the stone that burns." Sulfur, as it happens, burns quite well — with a blue flame. It can take the form of rocks. People have argued that hell smells like burning sulfur.

The reference to worms takes us to the next verse.

---

### The Worm That Does Not Die

Isaiah 66:24 — can someone turn to that passage for me?

> "And they shall go out and look on the dead bodies of the men who have rebelled against me. For their worm shall not die, their fire shall not be quenched, and they shall be an abhorrence to all flesh."
> — Isaiah 66:24 (ESV)

The word for "worm" can be translated as grub, worm, or maggot. Maggot seems the most natural when talking about things that eat rotting, decaying flesh. It's a horrific picture. Are the worms literal? Are they metaphorical for the kinds of horrors that are present? We can't know for certain. What we do know is that this burning, this state of being gnawed upon, is eternal. It never stops. Always consuming but never satisfied.

Do we really want to find out if the worms are literal or metaphorical? Do you want your loved ones to find out? Certainly not.

---

### The Gloom of Utter Darkness

2 Peter 2:17 adds another element to hell:

> These are waterless springs and mists driven by a storm. For them the gloom of utter darkness has been reserved.
> — 2 Peter 2:17 (ESV)

So hell is dark and gloomy. I'll be honest — I never thought it would be bright and cheery. But "utter darkness" makes me think of being deep in a cave. Have you ever been in a cave and had the lights turned off? You can't see your hand in front of your face. It's an oppressive, pure and perfect, palpable blackness. Your eyes will never adjust. It's an imposed blackness — one that has been stored up, reserved, to be dished out for all of eternity.

---

### Weeping and Gnashing of Teeth

So we've talked about the smell, the look, and some of what it feels like to be in hell. But what about what you'll hear? Matthew 24:51 says:

> ...and will cut him in pieces and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.
> — Matthew 24:51 (ESV)

As if the rest of it wasn't bad enough, the only thing you'll be able to hear is the screams of agony of other people, punctuated by the sound of billions of people grinding their teeth all at once.

And Matthew 13:49-50 adds that it will be like being inside a fiery furnace:

> So it will be at the end of the age. The angels will come out and separate the evil from the righteous and throw them into the fiery furnace. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.
> — Matthew 13:49-50 (ESV)

## IV. The Separation That Sends Us (Isaiah 59:2)

Now — if you truly love your lost friends and family, how could you let them go there unwarned?

Isn't it worth risking the relationship? Even for a flicker of a hope that they wouldn't have to spend an eternity in that place?

Everyone apart from God is heading straight to hell. Everyone. That is what we deserve. Spurgeon said it plainly: "Every Christian is either a missionary or an impostor." And in his turn of phrase, "missionary" didn't mean going overseas. It meant sharing the gospel with the lost in our midst.

Isaiah 59:2 speaks to why anyone ends up there:

> But your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear.
> — Isaiah 59:2 (ESV)

Our sin separates us from God. That's the problem. That's why we need the gospel.

---

## The Gospel

And here is the good news — the best news there has ever been:

God, the one who doesn't need us, the one who hung the stars in the sky, the one who holds our molecules together — that God looked down at a world full of rebels and loved us anyway.

> For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life. For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but in order that the world might be saved through him.
> — John 3:16-17 (ESV)

> But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.
> — Romans 5:8 (ESV)

Jesus Christ — fully God and fully man — lived the perfect life that we could not live. He died the death that we deserved to die. He was buried, and on the third day he rose again. And he offers forgiveness, salvation, and eternal life as a free gift to anyone who will repent of their sin and trust in him.

> For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.
> — Romans 6:23 (ESV)

That is the message. That is what 3.4 billion people have never heard. That is what the people in our grocery stores and gas stations and colleges need to hear. That is what your lost friends and family members need to hear before it's too late.

---

## What This Means for You

**If you don't know Christ:** The reality of hell is not meant to scare you into faith. It's meant to show you what you're being rescued *from*. God loves you. He sent his Son for you. Repent and believe the gospel today.

**If you've been silent:** I'm not here to heap guilt on you. I'm here to tell you the truth. If you believe what the Bible says about hell, and you love the people in your life, you cannot stay quiet. Start with one person. One conversation. One invitation to lunch. Ask God for the courage and the opportunity.

**If you think you've done enough:** Remember the Tozer quote. God doesn't need you on his team. He's invited you in because he loves you. Don't let that invitation make you proud. Let it make you humble — and let it make you go.

**If you're afraid:** Of course you are. Sharing the gospel is uncomfortable. It risks rejection. It risks the relationship. But isn't the alternative worse? Isn't an awkward conversation better than watching someone you love walk into an eternity of fire and darkness and weeping?

---

## Closing

3.4 billion people have never heard the gospel. Millions of them are in our own backyard. Tens of thousands fly through our airports every single day. They sit next to us at restaurants. They work alongside us. Their children attend the same schools our children attend. And they are heading for an eternity that should make us weep.

Spurgeon also said:

> "If sinners be damned, at least let them leap to Hell over our dead bodies. And if they perish, let them perish with our arms wrapped about their knees, imploring them to stay. If Hell must be filled, let it be filled in the teeth of our exertions, and let not one go unwarned and unprayed for."
> — Charles Spurgeon

We are included in God's mission not because he needs us. But because he loves us. And if we love him back — if we have truly tasted his majesty, if we truly believe what his Word says about hell — then we cannot keep silent.

Every Christian is either a missionary or an impostor.

Which one are you?

---

## Benediction

May the God who doesn't need us but chose to include us fill you with boldness to speak and compassion to weep for the lost.

May the Christ who endured the cross so that you would never face the fire give you the courage to risk a conversation, a relationship, a moment of discomfort — for the sake of someone's eternity.

And may the Holy Spirit go before you into every grocery store, every gas station, every neighborhood, every encounter — preparing hearts to hear the gospel and preparing your lips to speak it.

Go. Be missionaries. Not imposters.

Amen.
