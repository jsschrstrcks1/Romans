## Opening

I want to start somewhere personal today, because I think most of you are going to meet me there.

This has been a lot of a week. I had a twenty-page paper due last Sunday. This afternoon, another paper. Next weekend is my midterm. On top of seminary, I'm trying to figure out what this church looks like if we're meeting online for months on end. How do we pay bills? How do we make it possible for you to give? There are a hundred things on top of those things — big ones and small ones. You know how life gets.

I don't like talking about myself too much from up here. But I was talking to a pastor friend this week, and he told me something I think is for this room.

He was in his senior year of seminary — which is its own kind of pressure — and had been pastor of a church for about six months. Eight hundred people in it. A little more than half of them wanted him gone. He reminds me of Bob Leko — a bull in a china shop. He'd been pushing the church toward more biblical theology, and the pastoral committee had warned him upfront: this was going to be an uphill battle. The church was weak on the authority of Scripture. They wanted a pastor who was fine with women preaching. They wanted someone comfortable with homosexuality. They wanted someone who would teach that God is loving, and that as long as everyone is sincere, he'll take them all in the end.

Which is not what the Bible teaches. On any of those things.

The church was growing. People were getting saved. Baptisms every few weeks. Lost people were wandering in and getting converted — which, apparently, was upsetting some folks who'd been comfortable for a long time. My friend was getting more anonymous mail in that six months than he'd gotten cumulatively in the rest of his life. You know what anonymous letters are. They're things people are too afraid to say to your face, said meaner than they'd say them to your face.

He's pouring himself out for six months. Writing his dissertation at the same time. Wife and a couple of kids at home. Four mouths to feed. Car payment, seminary payment, on and on. And then things came to a head. He walked in on a conversation about rallying the church to vote him out.

He said he was about broken.

Six months of his life poured into those people, and they wanted to fire him — with everything already on the line. He left church that night and drove home. He was so deep inside his own head that he didn't notice what was happening behind him for several miles. When he got out of his car at his house, a police officer was there. Turns out he had run two red lights and then driven at high speed for five miles without pulling over. The officer was not happy with him. *Preacher, don't you know what kind of trouble that can bring? I ought to have your car towed.* He took my friend's information, went back to his car, and called his sergeant. When the sergeant heard the name, he told the deputy not to worry — that preacher is already in enough trouble. They let him go without even a warning.

He was under a lot of stress.

Can any of you relate to that? Trying to balance work and family? Worried about your kids, your parents, loved ones in the middle of all this coronavirus uncertainty? There is a ton of things that can take root in your brain and crush you. For some of you it's heartache and longing. Some of you feel empty and alone. The stress comes in different shapes, but it's the same weight.

The medicine is not smoking. Not vaping. Not drugs, or a relationship, or a cruise. Those are temporary. Some are sinful. The only thing that will actually help is Jesus.

Open your Bibles to Philippians chapter 4.

## How Sunday Went

My friend got up early the next morning. He was a little upset with God — as you might understand. About eight in the morning, he prayed: *God, if you're never going to talk to me again, I need to hear from you. Right here, right now.*

He got his Bible and a stack of notecards and started reading through the Psalms. Every psalm that felt like it was written for that moment, he copied onto a card. He read straight through — every word of every verse — and filled the whole stack. He felt a little better.

The next day was Sunday. He went to church and preached. He told me the stress was so severe that morning that he sweated through his shirt and through his suit jacket — white salt stains on both pits of the suit when it was over. They baptized fourteen people that Sunday.

And by the end of the service, his hands were shaking. He felt like a dead man walking. Like he'd already been condemned and was just making the walk toward it. He had all his notecards in his pocket — all those psalms he'd written down. But the verse that came to him in that moment wasn't one he'd written down. It wasn't even one that had made the list. It just rose.

*This I know — God is for me.*

That's Psalm 56:9. The rest of the verse says his enemies will turn back when he calls for help. He didn't know that then. He only knew in that moment that peace came over him. He remembered that God was in control. That there is nothing good that can happen to you without God's hand in the middle of it, and nothing bad that can happen to you that God doesn't limit according to his will. Nothing that surprises God.

Long story short — the following Sunday, my friend lost four hundred people. But otherwise it was a great week.

All right. Let's read.

## Scripture Reading

> Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice. Let your reasonableness be known to everyone. The Lord is at hand; do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus.
> — Philippians 4:4–7 (ESV)

## Rejoice

*Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice.*

If you want to add weight to something, you repeat it. Kids do this in English today — if a kid tells you it was a bad bad bad bad bad day, you know something happened. If it was the best most awesomest good day ever, you feel the force of that. Many languages, old and new, use repetition to add weight. The Bible does this too.

Paul is not saying rejoice when you feel like it. He is not saying when you get a free moment, take a beat and rejoice. He is pounding the pulpit. He is demanding it.

And do you know what he's dealing with when he writes this?

Philippians is called a prison epistle. Do you know why? Because Paul wrote it from prison. To even get to prison, he had to survive a shipwreck. The Roman soldiers wanted to put him to death, but God had other plans. They get to Rome, and he's imprisoned. Under Nero. Does anybody remember something about Nero from a few weeks ago? The man who is believed to have set the city of Rome on fire — burning it down, killing more people than they could count, all so his palace could be a little larger and more grand. The man who lit Christians on fire to light the streets of Rome.

Some of the people who would have read this letter had watched a son or daughter, a husband or wife, impaled on a post and burned alive.

To them, Paul says: *Rejoice.*

That's not cruelty. That's not cheerfulness dressed up as theology. It's an anchor. Paul knows something these people need to be reminded of.

There are days when it is harder to choose joy than to keep grieving. I heard it preached once that joy and sorrow travel the same pathways in your brain, and it's up to you to choose which one you feed. You can do nothing and stay down — stressed, depressed, overwhelmed. Or you can choose to rejoice in the Lord.

I don't know exactly what each of you is going through. I can't know it as intimately as you do. But here's what I know: God commands us to rejoice. And I may not know how bad things are — but I know how good God is. I know he has a plan for this. He has a purpose. He is working in you through this. He loves you.

He promises he will never leave you or forsake you. He promises to provide for your needs. He promises that one day he will wipe every tear from your eye. He promises to be your Father in your fatherless days. He promises every believer a home in heaven, paid for by his Son.

Because of him, you have hope. You have a future.

---

## A Word on Stumbling

Some of you will stumble. That happens. Some of you will fall. Some of you will be lured by the empty promises of this world into thinking something temporary can fill a permanent hole.

Someone suggested this week that smoking helps with stress.

Smoking kills your body — the body God made — and it is a sin against him. And it only lasts a moment before the stress comes right back. Same with vaping. It's the same logic as drugs. Instead of taking your emptiness to the Lord and letting him fill it, you try to fill it with something that burns off in twenty minutes and leaves you exactly where you started. Paul writes it plainly — *shall we continue in sin that grace may abound? May it never be.*

You don't lose your salvation because you smoke. But you do grieve God. And you don't find peace that way either.

The only joy that lasts is from the Lord.

---

## Prayer and Supplication

*Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God.*

Who wants to tell me what prayer is?

Talking to God. Right.

Supplication is a harder word. Let me give you a picture of it.

You are a starving beggar. Your kids are starving. Somehow you make your way near the palace — and there, right in front of you, is the king. What do you do? You throw yourself on the ground. You plead with everything you have. You beg for a scrap of bread so your children will not die tonight.

That is supplication. That is the posture Paul is calling you to. Completely emptied of self-sufficiency. On the ground.

In my experience, when people are struggling with sin and can't find peace, they haven't gotten on the ground yet. They are still trying to muscle through it. Still trying to get through it on their own. And you cannot both be struggling and be at peace. You can't. If you want what Paul describes in verse 7, you have to get to verse 6 first. Prayer. Supplication. Thanksgiving. Making your requests known to God.

Until you are willing to humble yourself like that — to actually beg for holiness, to beg for peace — you won't make much progress.

*And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus.*

Guard. That's a military word. Paul is surrounded by soldiers — he's been chained to them around the clock. He knows what a guard looks like. He's saying the peace of God will take up a post at the door of your heart and your mind.

That peace doesn't come from figuring it out. It doesn't come from controlling the situation. It surpasses all understanding — meaning you won't be able to explain why you're at peace. You'll just be at peace. Because God is guarding you.

---

## Guard Your Mind

In counseling, there's a concept about changing your internal dialog. If you're depressed, you have to start telling yourself true things. Start rejoicing in the Lord. Start naming what God has done for you.

Think about Ryker getting the medication his family couldn't afford. Think about the fact that you can walk through those doors on a Sunday morning without fear. Think about God's specific provision in your own life — the times he showed up when you couldn't see how he would.

If you're feeling down today, I want you to leave here with one task: find one thing you can rejoice in the Lord for. Just one. Tomorrow, find a second one. Give it a month. If you're still in a dark place after a month of that, it may be something medical — and there is no shame in going to see a doctor. But try this first.

The other thing that will drag a Christian down is the company they keep. Can you play in the mud without getting dirty? The Christian cannot run with the world without being marked by it. If you surround yourself with people who use drugs, you're more likely to use drugs. If you spend your time with people who drink constantly, you're more likely to develop a drinking problem. You become like the people you're around.

Scripture says a cord of three strands is not easily broken. You and another believer, surrounding someone who is lost — that's safe. You alone, surrounded by people walking away from God, will be pulled down over time.

Maybe this isn't you — maybe it's your kids. Maybe you can see it happening because of the company they keep. If that's the case, you need to pray about creating some space in those relationships. That's not rejection. That's wisdom.

---

## Closing

Normally we close with the gospel. But this morning I want to do something different.

If there is something you need to lay down — sin you've been carrying, anxiety you've been running from, a void you've been filling with everything except the Lord — I want to give you a moment to come to the altar and repent.

And then before we're done, I want us to pray together. Our denomination has called on every Baptist church to spend time this morning in prayer for everyone affected by the coronavirus — and everyone who will be affected. The medical workers who don't know what's coming. Our government. Our friends and loved ones who may get sick. We don't know how bad this is going to get. But we know how big our God is.

And he is able.
