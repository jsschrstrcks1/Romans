CORRECTIONS LOG — READ BEFORE USING THIS FILE IN ANY CONTEXT

1. FLAGGED — Steven Davey quote: source needs verification
   The pastor attributes the following to "the preacher Steven Davey" who "preached on this
   passage": "David is guilty of lust, deception, adultery, conspiracy to murder, hypocrisy,
   lying, abuse of power, and on and on. He had tried to cover his tracks and only dug the pit
   deeper still. David realizes that apart from God's mercy, he doesn't stand a chance."
   Stephen Davey is a real pastor and author (Wisdom International / Colonial Baptist Church,
   Cary, NC). The list of David's sins is accurate to the biblical narrative. However, the
   exact quote and its original sermon/article source have not been confirmed. Do not present
   as a direct quotation in public use without locating the source.

2. FLAGGED — Calvin Latin quote: not verbatim
   The pastor quotes Calvin in Latin: "hominis ingenium perpetuam, ut ita loquar, esse
   idolorum fabricam" and translates it as "the human heart is a vigorous and perpetual
   factory of idols."
   The canonical text from Calvin's Institutes, Book I, Chapter 11, Section 8 reads:
   "Ingenium hominis perpetua, ut ita loquar, idolorum fabrica."
   Discrepancies:
   (a) Word order: Calvin has "Ingenium hominis" (the mind of man); the pastor says
       "hominis ingenium."
   (b) Grammar: Calvin uses nominative "perpetua...fabrica"; the pastor uses accusative
       "perpetuam...fabricam" and adds "esse" — changing the grammatical construction.
   (c) "vigorous" does not appear in Calvin's Latin and is not in standard English
       translations. Battles (1960) translates it: "man's nature, so to speak, is a perpetual
       factory of idols."
   The meaning is substantially preserved. However, this is a paraphrase/memory rendition,
   not verbatim Calvin. In any public attribution, use: Calvin, Institutes I.xi.8, and quote
   from a reliable English translation rather than the pastor's Latin rendition.

3. FLAGGED — "Bank robber" story: source/identity unverified
   The pastor tells a story about a college-age woman who, with friends, decided to rob a bank
   to raise money for the Black Panthers. She was the getaway driver. A police officer was
   killed in the ensuing gunfight. She escaped, was on the FBI Most Wanted list with her photo
   in post offices, then decades later turned herself in, saying: "I was tired of living with
   shame and hiddenness...and guilt." The details (Black Panthers, getaway driver, officer
   killed, FBI Most Wanted, photo in post offices, decades later came forward) are consistent
   with cases from the 1970s radical era. The identity and specific source of the quote have
   not been confirmed. Do not present as a named, sourced account without independent
   verification.

4. VERIFIED — Acts 13:22 quotation
   The pastor quotes: "God raised up David to be their king, of whom he testified and said,
   I have found in David the son of Jesse a man after my heart, who will do all my will."
   ESV Acts 13:22 reads: "And when he had removed him, he raised up David to be their king,
   of whom he testified and said, 'I have found in David the son of Jesse a man after my
   heart, who will do all my will.'"
   The quoted portion matches ESV exactly (the introductory clause is omitted, not misquoted).
   VERIFIED.

5. VERIFIED — Hebrew machah / "blot out"
   The pastor refers to the Hebrew word as "Mah hah" and says it means to wipe away a ledger
   entry as though it never happened. The Hebrew root is מָחָה (machah), from which מְחֵה
   (mekheh) in Psalm 51:1 derives. The transliteration "Mah hah" is a rough approximation;
   the standard transliteration is "machah." The meaning given — to wipe out, erase, blot out
   — is accurate. The ledger illustration is a fair pastoral application. VERIFIED in substance.

6. VERIFIED — Bara / "create in me, out of nothing"
   Psalm 51:10 — "Create in me a clean heart." The pastor notes that the Hebrew word for
   "create" here is the same word used in Genesis 1:1 for God's creation (bara, בָּרָא),
   which carries the sense of creation ex nihilo. This is a standard and accurate
   exegetical observation. VERIFIED.

7. VERIFIED — Hyssop references
   The pastor says hyssop was used to apply blood to the doorposts at Passover (Exodus 12:22)
   and to sprinkle blood in sacrificial rituals. Both are accurate:
   - Exodus 12:22: "Take a bunch of hyssop and dip it in the blood that is in the basin, and
     touch the lintel and the two doorposts with the blood that is in the basin."
   - Leviticus 14:4-6; Numbers 19:18: hyssop used in cleansing rituals involving blood or
     water of purification.
   The typological connection to Christ's atoning work is a standard Reformed reading.
   VERIFIED.

8. NOTE — Dictation artifact: doubled paragraph
   In delivery, the pastor repeated himself before introducing the Steven Davey quote — the
   "Right, if grace is God giving us things we don't deserve" paragraph appears twice in the
   raw dictation. The transcript below includes it once.

INTRODUCTION — EPSTEIN AND THE WEIGHT OF GUILT

Have you guys been following the Jeffrey Epstein and Ghislaine Maxwell saga that has been
playing out in the news the last few years? I read an article this week — they are trying to
get Prince Andrew to talk. Epstein was tied to the who's who of the wealthy around the world.
If they have been described as a billionaire, or had access to levers of power, he was mixed
up with them — including former prime ministers and former presidents. He was basically a pimp.
Bringing in girls as young as 14, coercing them to have sex with whomever. More than three
dozen teenagers pressed into prostitution. I wonder how many of those kids are addicted to
drugs today because of what they went through back then?

It strikes me that Epstein had ordered about a half dozen books on how to force women to be
slaves. It makes me sick to my stomach that such books exist. How to subjugate another human
being.

The whole mess has even cost Bill Gates his marriage. He was apparently a visitor to Epstein's
island. When the news about Epstein broke, Bill Gates' wife left him — rightly so. I've been
praying for restitution for the victims. How awful.

Ultimately, Epstein met his end. It's a common thing to read on social media that Jeffrey
Epstein didn't kill himself. Maybe he did or maybe he didn't. But all I know is I read an
excerpt from his psychological profile that described him as despondent, and an account of a
second attempt earlier in the week. Part of me wonders: if it was suicide, maybe just maybe it
was tied to him getting a clear picture of all the damage he had done to all of these young
women. Maybe he had come to understand he was the villain.

Similarly, I read a story about this woman — she was young, in college, idealistic. She had a
burning need to make a difference in the world. So she and some friends set out to raise money
for the Black Panthers. However, instead of walk-a-thons and bake sales, she and her friends
decided to rob a bank. She would be the getaway driver. The day of the heist, things went
sideways and there ended up being a gunfight that killed a police officer. She got away scot
free. Despite being on the FBI's most wanted list for a time, and having her picture up in the
post office, several decades later — wracked with guilt — she came forward. She said: "I was
tired of living with shame and hiddenness...and guilt." It was time to face up to the truth.
[SOURCE UNVERIFIED: See correction log item 3.]

That is more or less where we find David this morning as he wrote Psalm 51.

PSALM 51:1 — MERCY, GRACE, AND THE THREE REQUESTS

Let's start right there in verse 1. Who can tell me what mercy is?

Right. If grace is God giving us things we don't deserve, mercy is not getting the punishments
we do deserve.

David was very aware of his sins. The preacher Stephen Davey made a list of David's sins when
he preached on this passage. He wrote:
[SOURCE: Stephen Davey, verified as a real pastor (Wisdom International / Colonial Baptist
Church, Cary, NC). Exact source sermon/article not confirmed. See correction log item 1.]

"David is guilty of lust, deception, adultery, conspiracy to murder, hypocrisy, lying, abuse
of power, and on and on. He had tried to cover his tracks and only dug the pit deeper still.
David realizes that apart from God's mercy, he doesn't stand a chance. He's not asking God for
what he deserves. But he dares to ask this: notice the first three requests bound up in this
petition. First, blot out my transgressions. Secondly, wash me thoroughly from my iniquities.
Thirdly, cleanse me from my sin."

Blot out is a verb, and my hermeneutics professor said to always look those up. The idea behind
the Hebrew word machah — "Mah hah" — is like that of when you keep a ledger, and you make a
mistake, and the mistake is wiped away as though it had never happened. [VERIFIED — see
correction log item 5.]

Wash me is talking about laundering dirty clothes. There is an email going around about one of
those new robot vacuums — do you know the one I'm talking about? They turn on when you're not
home, vacuum the whole house, and then go back to their charge station when they're done.

The problem came when the puppy that wasn't yet house-broken somehow got out of the crate.
Puppy does what puppies do, and left present number two on the carpet. Fortunately that was the
worst that the puppy did. The rest of the trouble was due to the robot — which turned on and
did exactly what it was supposed to do. Bounced around the house. The whole house. Every room,
one room at a time. The hallway, the bathroom, the kitchen, and even the laundry room.
Unfortunately it started in the doggie-do room, and in the process spread the puppy's present
around the entire house. The entire house had a thin layer, or tracks, or marks on the walls.
It was an awful thing for them to come home to. He wrote that every table leg, every chair leg,
every toy and toybox, every carpet and rug — apparently there was no fix for the rug it all
started on, which was fairly centrally located. Nothing quite like spending six hours after a
long day at work scrubbing the house and shampooing the carpets. The kind of cleaning that
family had to do is what's in view here when David says "wash me thoroughly." Every crack and
crevice of that house needed a thorough scrubbing — and so do our souls.

John Calvin famously wrote — "hominis ingenium perpetuam, ut ita loquar, esse idolorum fabricam"
— or, the human heart is a vigorous and perpetual factory of idols.
[NOTE: This is a paraphrase/memory rendition of Calvin's Latin, not verbatim. The canonical
text (Institutes I.xi.8) reads: "Ingenium hominis perpetua, ut ita loquar, idolorum fabrica."
Battles translates it: "man's nature, so to speak, is a perpetual factory of idols." The word
"vigorous" is not in Calvin's Latin. See correction log item 2.]

Every shiny thing that isn't God our heart latches onto and lusts after. Those desires have to
be cleansed from our soul — which is David's third request: cleanse me.

Apparently, as messy as the house was, the robot vacuum was worse. The circuits were shorted out
just as if they had been dipped in water. When he found it, the robot was making a sound — the
one that indicates electronics are dying. But he still tried to fix it. He took the whole thing
apart and scrubbed every gear, every circuit, every single piece of its innermost parts. Like
God does for us.

PSALM 51:3-7 — CONFESSION AND HYSSOP

When we get to verse 3, we see David confessing.

[Read vv. 3-7]

He knows the depths of his own sin. He owns it before God. I think children have this tendency
— and even some adults have retained that trait — to try and shirk their own guilt. Stephen
Davey points out that people say things like: "You know how I am! You know my personality. You
know what my childhood was like. You know my parents. You know about my low-paying job and my
terrible working conditions. You know about all those people that work around me — all that
stuff, that's what made me sin!"

But that's the opposite of what David does here. David is boldly declaring: all that stuff —
the lust, deception, adultery, conspiracy to murder, hypocrisy, lying, abuse of power, actual
murder, being a terrible father, and on and on — all that stuff? It was me. David says: these
are my transgressions.

Transgression is a good word. It refers to violating a boundary. I read a story about a man
living in North Korea who was desperate for the freedom to worship God, desperate for a Bible.
Things were so bleak that communist China was looking pretty good. How bad do things have to
be to make China look good? So he decided he was going to sneak across the border into China
so he could have a shot at studying the Bible. He and a group of others planned it out. They
would wait until the gap in the Korean patrols, then wait an additional three minutes, then
make a run for it — because if they waited ten minutes they would encounter the next patrol.
They had to time it perfectly. The guard passes. They wait. As a group they stand up and run.

Except the North Korean army had been tipped off and was waiting in ambush. They opened fire
and shot down — so they thought — everyone. The man who was telling the story said he lay
there with a bullet hole in his leg for most of the night. The Koreans thought he was dead. He
eventually saw an opportunity and fled into China, where he got medical help. Ultimately he
made his way to the United States, where he is a legal citizen and now an author.

In any case, for him to escape the tyranny of Korea, he had to commit a transgression — he had
to cross the Korean and Chinese borders illegally, risking his life in doing so.

David owned his transgressions before God. What was true of David is no less true of us, nor
of our children. Every time we have ever sinned, or will ever sin, we prove that Adam's sin was
— you want the five-dollar word? — was imputed to us. Adam added a defect to the mold you and
I were formed from. That was our inheritance from Adam.

When we do anything other than own our sin before God, as David did, we are spitting in God's
face.

When we say "it's not my fault, it's my circumstances," what we are really saying is: "It's
your fault, God." God made us. God gave us our personality. God chose our parents and allowed
us to have the childhood we had. He blessed you with that job. He gives you opportunities to
reflect him into the dark places we sometimes have to work. He has a reason for that, and that
reason is your sanctification. He wants to make you more holy. He wants you to declare him
among the nations. He has a mission for you, and only you are right for it.

So when we blame the circumstances, we spit in God's face and say: "What you gave me wasn't
good enough. It's not up to my standard." Folks, we aren't up to God's standard — and he still
loves and works with us. I think the problem is us.

David, when he had Bathsheba's husband killed, could have said: "But I wasn't the one who
pulled the trigger. I didn't stick him with the spear." He could have said: "I really am a good
person, even if I did sleep with another man's wife and had her husband killed." So often those
are the kinds of things we say when confronted with our own sin. We equivocate.

Instead what David said was: God. It was me.

Which brings us to verse 7. Someone read that nice and loud.

[Read v. 7]

Verse 7 talks about cleansing with hyssop. That phrase is repeated a number of times in
Scripture, but it wasn't until recently that I had ever thought about what it might mean and
looked it up. Turns out hyssop was a plant with a very particular kind of shape — a shape that
lent itself to being used in some of the more important ceremonies mentioned in Scripture.
[VERIFIED — see correction log item 7.]

Stephen Davey points out that the first use of hyssop was in Egypt — they took a bunch of
hyssop, dipped it in the blood of the lamb, and used it to make a mark over their doorposts.
Later, when animals were to be sacrificed, they would dip hyssop in the blood of the animal
and sprinkle. These of course are references to what God would do for us one day on that old
rugged cross at the top of Golgotha — where he was nailed to the cross and left to die. Where
he defeated our sin for us. Where his death gave us an alternative to hell. Where our ransom
was paid. Once and for all. The righteous for the unrighteous.

PSALM 51:10 — CREATE IN ME A CLEAN HEART

It's such a radical transaction. It's referenced again in verse 10 — "Create in me a clean
heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me." In the Hebrew you could translate this as:
"Create in me, out of nothing, a clean heart." [VERIFIED — see correction log item 6.]

David knew, and throughout every verse has owned, the depths of his sin. He knew the weight
and the cost of what he had done. And he owned it before God in the best ways. David basically
ended up in the right place. He certainly didn't always start there. Certainly started in the
wrong place with Bathsheba and her husband — crimes he would pay a high price for.

Ultimately God would send his Son to die for the sins of the elect. And ultimately David's own
son — his son with Bathsheba — would die as well. David knew intimately the price God would
pay for his sins.

But more than just that: the Bible gives us a reason in the book of Acts. Scripture says: "God
raised up David to be their king, of whom he testified and said, I have found in David the son
of Jesse a man after my heart, who will do all my will." [Acts 13:22 ESV — VERIFIED, see
correction log item 4.]

David, across the arc of his life, in the end had done everything that God asked him to do. I
believe he followed through on his promise in verse 13 as well: "I will teach transgressors
your ways, so that sinners will turn back to you."

David was one who had been forgiven much. He had heavy consequences, sure. But even with those
he was obedient to God in everything. God honored his frank assessment of himself. David knew
he had transgressed. He knew he had done wrong. And instead of trying to hide it, he puts all
his cards on the table and confesses to the Lord. He pleads for forgiveness, and ultimately God
honors that. We know he gets a new heart because God says of David that he is a man after his
own heart.

CLOSE

Sometimes we wander and stray. Sometimes we screw up. But when we do, what should our response
be? I think David had it right. "Have mercy on me, O God, according to your steadfast love;
according to your abundant mercy blot out my transgressions. Wash me thoroughly from my
iniquity, and cleanse me from my sin!"

point in your spiritual life where you've been saved — come talk to me.

If you have accepted Christ but your life looks more like the person described in Romans 1 than
it resembles Christ, and you want to rededicate — come talk to me.

If you're looking for a church to join — come talk to me. One of the things I love about this
church is that when people bare their souls, there is no judgment here. People aren't treated

because we love you.
