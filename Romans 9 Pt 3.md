**Romans 9 Pt 3**

**Sermon: Coffee With Three Witnesses**
*Romans 9:19–33*

**Introduction – The Objection and the Table**
Romans 9 presses into the most humbling truths of God’s sovereignty. After showing that salvation depends not on human will but on God’s mercy, Paul imagines the objection rising in every heart: *“Why does He still find fault? For who can resist His will?”* (v.19).
This is not innocent curiosity. The word Paul uses, *ἀντ**απ**οκρίνῃ* (“to answer back”), is defiance—man arguing with God. And Paul rebukes: *“But who are you, O man, to answer back to God? Will what **is** molded say to its molder, ‘Why have you made me like this?’”* (v.20).
We are clay; He is the Potter. That truth is enough to silence pride. Yet Paul does more. To show that God’s dealings have always been this way, he invites witnesses from Israel’s own history. Imagine three men sitting across the coffee table from you—Hosea, Isaiah, and Jesus. Each has something vital to say.

**I. Hosea’s Voice – “Not My People, Now My People” (vv. 25–26)**
**Hosea’s Story**
Hosea lived out a heartbreaking sermon. His wife, Gomer, was unfaithful. Their children’s names carried God’s verdicts: *Lo-Ruhamah* (“No Mercy”) and *Lo-Ammi* (“Not My People”). Imagine Hosea calling his children through the neighborhood: “No Mercy, supper’s ready!” “Not My People, come inside!” Every name was a billboard of judgment.
But then came God’s promise of reversal: *“Those who were not my people I will call ‘my people,’ and her who was not beloved I will call ‘beloved.’ And in the very place where it was said to them, ‘You are not my people,’ there they will be called sons of the living God.”* (Hos. 2:23; Rom. 9:25–26).
**Word Insight**
Hebrew *רֻחָמָה* (*ruḥāmāh*) — “compassion, tender mercy.”
Greek *ἀγ**απ**ηθήσῃ* (*agapēthēsē*) — “to be loved,” passive voice. Love is poured upon us, not drawn out of us.

**Illustration**
Think of a judge declaring a guilty man “condemned.” Then, stepping down from the bench, he takes the sentence himself and adopts that man as his son. That is the gospel. From “Not My People” to “My beloved child.”
Hosea’s children were renamed. So are we.
**Application**
**Struggling saint:** When the accuser says, “You are unloved,” remember: you have been renamed. Beloved. Child of God.
**Church:** This crushes pride. We were outsiders, but God made us His own. The church is a home for redeemed nobodies.
**Mission:** God delights to call those far off. Hosea’s prophecy is fulfilled every time someone from an unreached people group calls Jesus Lord.

**II. Isaiah’s Voice – “A Remnant Shall Be Saved” (vv. 27–29)**
**Isaiah’s Context**
Isaiah preached when Israel was numerous but rebellious. Assyria threatened, Babylon loomed. Outward numbers meant nothing. God said: *“Though the number of the sons of Israel be as the sand of the sea, only a remnant will be saved.”* (Isa. 10:22).
If God had not preserved a seed, Isaiah warns, they would have become like Sodom and Gomorrah—completely wiped out.
**Word Insight**
Greek *ὑπ**όλειμμ**α* (*hypoleimma*) — “remnant,” that which survives after devastation.
Greek *σπ**έρμ**α* (*sperma*) — “seed, offspring,” showing salvation is rooted in promise, not performance.

**Illustrations**
**Wildfire:** A forest fire scorches acres black. Yet after the smoke clears, a small patch of green remains untouched. That is the remnant—survivors by design, not chance.
**Titanic:** Over 2,000 people sailed, but only a remnant survived in the lifeboats. Numbers didn’t save; the boat did. So too, Christ alone saves.
**Puritan Witness**
John Owen: *“The preservation of a remnant is the fruit of God’s distinguishing grace; it is His mercy, not their merit.”*
**Application**
**Self-assured sinner:** Don’t think proximity to religion saves you. You can sit in church your whole life and still perish. Only Christ saves.
**Weary believer:** When faith feels small and culture mocks truth, take heart. God always preserves His people. You may feel like part of a dwindling remnant, but His mercy will hold you.

**III. Jesus’ Voice – “The Stumbling Stone” (vv. 30–33)**

**The Reversal**
Paul brings the final witness: Christ Himself. *“What shall we say, then? That Gentiles who did not pursue righteousness have attained it… but that Israel who pursued a law that would lead to righteousness **did not succeed**.”*
Why? Because Israel sought it as if by works, not faith. They stumbled over Christ.
**Word Insight**
Greek *π**ροσκόμμ**α* (*proskomma*) — “stumbling block,” something you trip over.
Greek *σκάνδ**α**λον* (*skandalon*) — “offense, snare.”
Christ is cornerstone to some, trip hazard to others. The same stone that supports one crushes another.
**Illustrations**
**Construction site:** The cornerstone is laid. Some builders align with it and the structure stands firm. Others ignore it, stumble over it, and their work collapses. Christ is the cornerstone or the stumbling stone—there is no neutral.
**Simeon’s prophecy:** Holding the infant Christ, Simeon said: *“This child is appointed for the fall and rising of many in Israel.”* (Luke 2:34). Christ divides humanity still.
**Missionary example:** When Hudson Taylor preached in China, some bowed in tears. Others spat in his face. The same Christ—cornerstone to one, stumbling stone to another.
**Application**
**Skeptic:** Your greatest barrier is pride. The cross declares you helpless. Will you stumble or stand? Trip over Christ or trust Him?
**Believer:** Christ promises you will never be put to shame. Accusations may fly, but the verdict of heaven is secure.

**Conclusion – Three Voices, One Gospel**
Imagine the coffee table once more:
Hosea whispers: *“You were not His people, but now you are.”*
Isaiah warns: *“Only a remnant will be saved—but God preserves His own.”*
Jesus declares: *“Believe in Me, and you will never be put to shame.”*
Three voices. One gospel. One Savior.
Spurgeon said: *“The same sun that melts wax hardens clay.”* Christ is the dividing line. To some, He is refuge. To others, offense. But to all who believe, He is precious.
So bow before the Potter. Trust the Stone. Rejoice—you are a vessel of mercy, renamed, preserved, never put to shame.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Faithful Witnesses, Thin Gospel Center — A Structurally Clever Sermon That Stops Short of the Cross**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A creative and textually engaged opening of Romans 9:19–33 that leverages three OT witnesses with pastoral warmth and accessible illustrations while consistently stopping short of driving the gospel home with doctrinal precision and cross-centered force.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with qualification**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis can be stated: God sovereignly calls unlikely people by grace, preserves a remnant, and makes Christ both cornerstone and stumbling stone — demanding a faith response. This arises organically from vv. 25–33.

*Authority Test:* The preacher largely submits to the text's claims, including the hard edges of election and the stumbling stone. However, the sermon softens the severity of Paul's argument. The potter/clay confrontation in vv. 19–21 is introduced and then stepped past rather than preached through. The full weight of vv. 19–21 (which the sermon treats as a brief framing paragraph) is the most demanding passage in the section — and the sermon moves off it quickly. This is not a disqualifying failure, but it is a notable retreat from the text's hardest edge.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No fatal flaws triggered. Score is uncapped.**

However, the following near-flags are noted:
- Gospel presentation is present but thin. Justification by faith is gestured at ("sought it as if by works, not faith") but never articulated clearly enough that an unbeliever would know what to do. The judge-and-adoption illustration is the closest moment, but it is not pressed home.
- Application is differentiated (addressing struggling saint, skeptic, weary believer, self-assured sinner) — this is genuine strength. No generic-application flag.
- Exhortation is present, though it softens at the close rather than sharpening.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 18 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity (7/10):* The sermon covers vv. 25–33 well, with genuine engagement of Hosea and Isaiah in their OT context before applying Paul's use of them. The word studies are functional and not merely decorative. However, vv. 19–24 — Paul's potter/clay argument, including the vessels of wrath fitted for destruction — are introduced in the introduction and then effectively abandoned. These verses carry the theological weight that vv. 25–33 presuppose. Treating them as a framing device rather than preaching through them is a material omission.
- *Contextual Awareness (4/5):* The OT witnesses are set in their historical context (Hosea's marriage, Isaiah's Assyrian threat). Paul's use of these texts as supporting his argument about the inclusion of Gentiles and the remnant of Israel is reasonably tracked.
- *Exegetical Clarity (4/5):* Greek word studies are well chosen — *ἀνταποκρίνῃ*, *hypoleimma*, *proskomma*, *skandalon* — and accessible. They unlock meaning rather than decorate.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5):* The sermon does not commit eisegesis, but the FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is underdeveloped. The passage exists to address the heart that demands explanations from God — the posture of defiance that wants sovereignty on its own terms. That condition is named in the intro but not sustained through the sermon. The sermon moves to hope and mission quickly rather than pressing the sinfulness of the objector first.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 14 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (5/7):* Christ as stumbling stone and cornerstone is textually driven and well-handled. The sermon avoids artificial Christology. However, the sermon's Christ-connection through Hosea and Isaiah is more typological gesture than covenantal development. The "Jesus as third witness" framing risks making Christ equal in weight to the two OT prophets rather than showing how their words find their *telos* in Him.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* The OT-to-NT movement is present but not developed. Why does Paul reach to Hosea for a text about Gentile inclusion? The Gentile/Israel tension — the central argument of Romans 9–11 — is touched but not foregrounded.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4/4):* No errors. Sovereignty, remnant, faith vs. works — all handled accurately. The distinction between seeking righteousness by works vs. faith is stated correctly.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4):* No contradictions of confessional theology. The doctrines of grace are implicitly affirmed. However, a sermon on Romans 9:19–33 in a Reformed context has a natural opportunity to articulate the doctrinal weight of distinguishing grace more precisely — and this sermon stops short of that.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 10 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (3/5):* The judge-adoption illustration is the sermon's clearest gospel moment: "stepping down from the bench, he takes the sentence himself and adopts that man as his son." This is penal substitution in miniature — but it is embedded in the Hosea section, presented as an analogy, and never returned to or pressed home. An unbeliever attending this sermon would know that Christ is necessary and that faith is required, but would not leave with a clear understanding of how the work of Christ effects the salvation described.
- *Necessity of Christ (4/5):* The stumbling stone section establishes Christ as essential — you either stumble over Him or stand on Him. No neutral ground. This is genuine gospel force.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (3/5):* The sermon lacks a sustained movement toward the cross. Each of the three sections has its own application, which creates a fragmented gospel arc rather than a cumulative drive toward Calvary. The conclusion sums up three voices but does not crescendo to the cross as the ground of all three witnesses' promises.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 11 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration (3/5):* The applications are warm and specific enough to reach the heart, but they tend toward comfort rather than penetration. The sermon is better at encouraging the weary saint than cornering the self-deceived one. The "self-assured sinner" address in Point II is the sharpest moment: "You can sit in church your whole life and still perish."
- *Specificity (4/5):* The sermon distinguishes by hearer category — struggling saint, self-assured sinner, skeptic, weary believer, church, missionaries. This is genuine differentiation and above average.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (4/5):* Perkins' taxonomy is partially engaged: the ignorant-but-teachable (skeptic address), the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled (self-assured sinner), the humbled (struggling saint), the believers (weary believer). The backslider is not addressed. The hardened-and-unteachable is only glanced at.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5 / 8**

- *Call to Response (3/4):* The close — "bow before the Potter, trust the Stone" — is a genuine exhortation. The skeptic is directly addressed: "Will you stumble or stand?" These are real calls. But the exhortation is brief and comes after a summary recap, which dilutes its force. The "therefore" moment should be sharper.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The mission application under Hosea is the strongest equipping moment — the church is sent out in light of God's delight in calling those far off. But this is one point among several, not a sustained sending of the body into the world. The conclusion equips for personal assurance more than for corporate witness.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 7 / 9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* The three-witness structure is clear, memorable, and easy to follow. A listener can sketch it on a napkin: Hosea (we are renamed), Isaiah (a remnant is saved), Jesus (stumbling or standing). The coffee-table framing is accessible without being gimmicky.
- *Progression (3/3):* The sermon builds reasonably — from mercy (Hosea) to warning (Isaiah) to decision (Jesus). The progression is not perfectly tightened but it moves forward.
- *Unity (1/2):* The dominant idea — God sovereignly calls, preserves, and confronts through Christ — is present but competes with the three separate witness structures. At moments the sermon feels like three mini-sermons rather than one unified argument.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4 / 5**

- *Relevance (2/2):* Illustrations are well-chosen and drawn from accessible experience: wildfire, Titanic, construction site, Simeon's prophecy.
- *Clarity (2/2):* Each illustration illuminates its point without competing with it. The Titanic illustration (numbers didn't save; the boat did) is particularly clean.
- *Memorability (0/1):* No single illustration is likely to linger the way a great image should. The judge-adoption illustration comes closest but is not developed long enough to become a cornerstone image for the sermon.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3 / 5**

- *Reads as Preaching (1/2):* The manuscript has oral markers — direct address ("Some of you..."-equivalents), short climactic lines at close, the coffee-table device. But the word-insight sections read as study notes more than pulpit speech. They slow the oral rhythm.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The sermon carries warmth but not urgency. The tone-text congruence is uneven: Romans 9 carries the weight of God's sovereignty over human destiny — the sermon's tone is pastoral and warm throughout, but rarely rises to match the text's gravity.
- *Cadence and Breath (1/1):* Sentence variation is present. Conclusion is compressed. Climactic lines land.

### 6. Cognitive Load Assessment
**Score: −1 (Slightly dense)**

The three-witness structure, multiple word studies per section, and differentiated applications create a full sermon. An average listener can follow the main argument. The word studies may be a minor friction point for new believers. The sermon is accessible but dense enough that one fewer concept per section would sharpen the take-home.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* Primarily of Christ — the three-witness structure keeps the focus on what God has done in calling, preserving, and confronting through Christ. This is genuine strength.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Mostly ideas about Him. The sermon is pedagogically rich but does not press the hearer into the presence of God's holiness or grace with enough force to produce an encounter. The potter/clay confrontation is left on the table.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Comforted more than convicted. The weary believer is well served. The self-deceived, the hard-hearted, and the presumptuous churchgoer are addressed but not cornered.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Hearer differentiation is genuine and specific.** Across all three points, the sermon addresses distinct categories — struggling saint, self-assured sinner, skeptic, weary believer, the church corporate, the missionary. This is one of the sermon's best features and above what most expository sermons achieve. The "self-assured sinner" address in Point II is the sharpest moment of applicatory force.

2. **The three-witness structure is exegetically honest and memorable.** Hosea and Isaiah are not ripped from context — their historical situations are briefly but accurately set before Paul's use of them is explained. The structural device does not override the text; it serves it.

3. **The stumbling-stone section carries genuine gospel offense.** "Christ is cornerstone to some, trip hazard to others. The same stone that supports one crushes another." The use of Simeon's prophecy and the Hudson Taylor example give this section both textual depth and historical weight. The binary is clear: no neutral ground.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The hardest passage in the pericope is preached past.** Verses 19–24 — the potter/clay argument, including the vessels of wrath fitted for destruction — are introduced in the introduction as framing and then abandoned. This is the most theologically demanding material in the section, and it is precisely the material that makes vv. 25–33 intelligible. The mercy shown to the "not my people" presupposes the justice shown to the vessels of wrath. A sermon that skips vv. 20–24 in favor of the more comfortable promises in vv. 25–33 has quietly edited the passage.

2. **The gospel is illustrated but not proclaimed.** The judge-adoption illustration in Point I is the clearest articulation of penal substitution in the sermon — but it is an analogy, briefly stated, and never returned to. The sermon would pass the Synagogue Test, but only barely. A devout Jewish listener, familiar with the concept of God's mercy and covenantal calling, could receive most of this sermon without being confronted with the necessity of Christ's blood. The finished work of Christ is assumed more than proclaimed.

3. **The conclusion recaps without crescendo.** The three-voice summary at the end — Hosea whispers, Isaiah warns, Jesus declares — restates the structure without building toward a cross-centered climax. The sermon ends on three separate notes when it should end on one: all three witnesses point to Christ crucified and risen as the ground of the mercy, the preservation, and the non-shame promised. The final lines ("bow before the Potter, trust the Stone") are good but arrive after the sermon has already begun winding down.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears unaware that the coffee-table framing device, while accessible, also subtly domesticates the text. Hosea, Isaiah, and Jesus are rendered as conversational companions offering counsel rather than as divine witnesses bearing the weight of God's sovereign word. The structural conceit risks making Paul's argument — which is urgent, defensive, and theodicy-heavy — feel like a comfortable devotional. The tone never rises to match Romans 9's gravity.

Additionally, the sermon does not appear to recognize that Paul's OT citations in vv. 25–26 are formally a misapplication of Hosea's original context (Hosea addressed Israel, Paul applies to Gentile inclusion) — or if the preacher sees this, he does not address it. A brief acknowledgment of Paul's typological hermeneutic here would strengthen the exegetical credibility.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The word studies (*ἀνταποκρίνῃ*, *hypoleimma*, *proskomma*, etc.) create an impression of exegetical depth throughout the sermon. They are accurate and well-chosen. However, they function primarily as credibility markers — they establish that the preacher has done his homework — without always driving the interpretation in a way the congregation could not have reached without them. The Greek does genuine work in the stumbling-stone section; it is more decorative in the Hosea section. The impression of exegetical rigor may mask the thinner treatment of vv. 19–24.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Paul's use of Hosea is remarkable precisely because Hosea's original text addressed *Israel's* restoration, and Paul applies it to *Gentile* inclusion. This typological/hermeneutical move is the exegetical jewel of the passage — and the sermon passes over it without comment. The theological payoff is enormous: God's mercy to Israel in Hosea becomes the pattern for God's mercy to all who were "not His people." This is the passage that blows the doors off ethnic and national categories of covenant membership. A sermon that captured this would have matched the text's own explosion of grace.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach through vv. 20–24 before reaching vv. 25–33.** The vessels of mercy (v. 23) cannot be fully understood apart from the vessels of wrath (v. 22). Add a brief but honest treatment of God's right to harden — not to shock, but because it is the soil in which the Hosea/Isaiah mercy grows. The mercy is only tender against the background of what it rescues from.

2. **Return to the judge-adoption illustration at the conclusion and name it explicitly.** The penal substitution imagery is your best gospel moment — expand it and make it the culminating note of the sermon rather than a subordinate illustration in Point I. The three witnesses all point here: Hosea's renamed children are renamed because the Judge took the sentence; Isaiah's remnant is preserved because the Seed (sperma) bore the curse; the stumbling stone is also the cornerstone because He was crushed first.

3. **Tighten Point III to function as the sermon's climax and cross-centered crescendo.** Currently the stumbling-stone section has the same structural weight as Points I and II. It should feel heavier — it is the moment of decision, the resolution of the whole argument. Consider reducing Points I and II slightly and expanding Point III to carry the full weight of gospel invitation and warning.

4. **Address Paul's hermeneutical use of Hosea in one sentence.** Something like: "Notice what Paul does — he takes a text written to Israel about Israel's restoration and applies it to the Gentiles. Why? Because the pattern of God's mercy is the same: He calls the uncalled. He loves the unloved. Hosea's promise was bigger than Hosea knew." This would strengthen the exegetical credibility and open the theological wealth the sermon currently leaves closed.

5. **Sharpen the exhortation in the conclusion.** Instead of restating the three voices, end with a single summoning sentence that calls the whole congregation to one response: "Today the Potter is working. Today the Stone is laid. What will you do with Christ?" Maintain the direct second-person address through the final paragraph rather than letting it diffuse into summary.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon carries genuine pastoral warmth and real applicatory differentiation. The fire is lower than it should be given the text, but the sermon is not bloodless. The Conscience score (11/15) is above the trigger threshold.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries the warmth of a shepherd who loves his people and wants them to receive mercy — but it does not yet carry the weight of a man who has stood in the presence of the God whose sovereignty over vessels of wrath and mercy is the ground of all the hope he is offering.

**Weight of Glory Bonus: +2 / 5**

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Romans 9:19–33 is dense didactic-apologetic literature — Paul defending God's justice and sovereignty in the face of implicit objections. The rubric fits this passage well. The heaviest weights (Exposition, Theology, Gospel Centrality) are exactly the right categories to press in a theodicy passage. The one area where the rubric must be applied carefully is Sermonic Force: Romans 9 is not a doxological passage (that comes in 11:33–36) — it is an argument, and a tightly structured sermon-argument is appropriate to the text's own genre. The lower sermonic force score should be understood as a call to match the text's *urgency*, not necessarily its emotional register.
