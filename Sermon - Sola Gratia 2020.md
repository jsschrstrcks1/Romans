### Source Notes

- **Primary sources**: `Other-Sermons/Sermons-txt/sola gracia.txt` (2017 version, John 6) and `Other-Sermons/Sermons-txt/sola gracia 2021.txt` (2021 version, John 6, Dar Hamid). The submission is an intermediate revision: Dar Hamid people group matches the 2021 txt; "503 years ago" dates this to 2020 (the 2021 txt says "five hundred and fourth anniversary"). Primary text was changed from John 6 to Philippians 3 for this delivery, but the body was not fully updated.
- **Estimated date: October 2020** — "503 years ago the end of the month" (1517 + 503 = 2020); Reformation Day (October 31) and pastor appreciation month context confirm October.
- **"Sola Gracia"**: Consistent misspelling across all versions in the archive. Correct Latin is *Sola Gratia* (*gratia* = grace). *Gracia* is Spanish/Italian. Corrected to *Sola Gratia* throughout this document.
- **Primary text inconsistency — significant**: The sermon opens with Philippians 3:1–12 and contains a substantial section on Paul's credentials from that passage. However, the body then refers to "Vs 44" and "Vs 65" as "our passage tonight" — these are John 6 verses ("No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him," John 6:44; "no one can come to me unless it is granted him by the Father," John 6:65). The Philippians 3 opening and Paul's resume section were added in this revision, but the body's John 6 references were not updated. As delivered, this creates a passage conflict. The 2021 txt version reverts to John 6 as the sole primary text. **Recommend resolving: either add John 6 formally as a secondary text, or update "our passage tonight" references to point to the appropriate text.**
- **Billy Sunday — description inaccurate**: The sermon describes Billy Sunday as "a locally famous preacher down in Ybor City." The Billy Sunday whose quote follows (William Ashley Sunday, 1862–1935) was a nationally celebrated revivalist — one of the most prominent American evangelists of the early 20th century, drawing crowds of hundreds of thousands. "Locally famous" significantly understates him. His specific connection to Ybor City (Tampa) is not documented. The quote itself — "I'm against sin. I'll kick it as long as I have a foot..." — is well-documented for the famous Billy Sunday. ✓ The description, not the quote, needs correction.
- **Jonathan Edwards attribution** — "You contribute nothing to your salvation except the sin that made it necessary": Widely attributed to Jonathan Edwards. The original source in Edwards's works has not been definitively identified by researchers; it circulates broadly in secondary sources. Flag for verification before continued use.
- **James Petigru Boyce**:
  - Spelling: "Petigrew" in source — correct spelling is **Petigru** (from the Petigru family name).
  - Century: Described as "the famous Baptist Preacher of the last century." Boyce (1827–1888) is a 19th-century figure; "the last century" would indicate the 20th. Should read "the 19th century" or "the previous century."
  - The quote begins "3. Grace alone." — the number "3." indicates this is item 3 from a numbered list in a Boyce work, most likely *Abstract of Systematic Theology* (1887). Recommend verifying the full citation.
- **"90 of the 95 theses"**: The 2021 txt version says "92 out of 95"; the submission says "90." Both are approximations. Neither has been verified against Luther's 95 Theses.
- **John 6:44 and 6:65**: Both check out against ESV. ✓
- **BFM Article V (God's Purpose of Grace)**: Quoted text matches BFM 2000. ✓
- **Ephesians 2:8–9**: Quoted as a paraphrase without translation label; substance is accurate. ✓
- **Philippians 3:1–12**: Referenced as the opening text; not quoted in the submission (read from congregation's Bibles).

If you've brought your Bible, and I hope you have, please open with me to Philippians 3:1–12. While you're turning there, our people group of the week.

**The Dar Hamid of Sudan.** A friend of mine from college — at 80 years old — was a missionary to Sudan, and traveled all over the place drinking the water and eating the food. It was a harrowing thing in my mind for a person in his 80s to do. But for him, in faith he would travel all over Sudan sharing the gospel. The Dar Hamid is a group of 19 tribes representing about 750,000 people. They are nominally Muslim — most are only Muslim because someone once told them they were. People go through all the time selling charms and trinkets promised to ward off spirits or encourage good health. They put more stock in the charms than they do in Islam. They are a nomadic people with few possessions who trade in wool and leather products. They are difficult to reach because Sudan as a country is hostile to Christians, and nomadic people groups are incredibly suspicious of outsiders. Please pray for opportunities for the gospel.

October is an important month in church history. This week will be the first in a series on the Five Solas of the Reformation, which started 503 years ago at the end of the month — when a man named Martin Luther put hammer to nail and began what became the Protestant Reformation. The hammer strike that was heard around the world.

One of the reasons that he nailed his theses to the door was because the Catholic Church had been preaching that you could buy your way out of trouble. Maybe not buy your way all the way into heaven, but you could buy a better place in purgatory. Before we go any further, I should point out that there is no purgatory mentioned in the Bible. If it's not in the Bible it's not God's word, and shouldn't carry as much weight. As it happens, approximately 90 of the 95 theses were about this issue — adding things to the gospel. Adding a way for people to pay to be crooked.

Want to have an affair? Well the pope decreed that an affair would add so many sin-days to your penance in purgatory, but if you paid enough per day you could get it reduced to zero. Or what if your loved one was in hell — you have a little money; you can get them out, get them upgraded to purgatory, or upgraded from purgatory to heaven. What a way to prey on the grieving. What a way to prey on the sinful. Going to murder someone? Just fine in the eyes of the pope, as long as you pony up the cash. Rome had to build those cathedrals somewhere. In fact, if you ever make it to Europe and wander through those old cathedrals, many of them were paid for through indulgences. People's sin paid for those cathedrals.

Just in case it isn't clear: giving money to the church doesn't save you. Giving your time to the church doesn't save you. Don't get me wrong, I appreciate both. But that isn't what saves you. As a pastor, I can't put you in better or worse standing with God. If anyone says they can, they are lying. The reformers' fight against these false doctrines in particular became known as *Sola Gratia* — by grace alone. It can only be grace alone. Let's look at our passage.

*(Read Philippians 3:1–12.)*

Right away, in my Bible, the heading here says "No confidence in the flesh." The subject headings weren't written by the apostles or necessarily by God, but they are a good indicator of what's coming next in the text.

This is also Paul's résumé. Considering all he's done and been through — including things not listed in the text, like, oh, I don't know, writing half the New Testament — considering all that, wouldn't you expect Paul, if anyone, to have the best shot at earning his way to heaven? And yet what does he say? All those things I did? Garbage. Refuse. Worthless.

He has quite the list of garbage accomplishments. He was born into the nation of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin. He followed all of the laws — even circumcised on the 8th day. He was a Hebrew of Hebrews; in other words, he knew the language and culture. This was in addition to the lingua franca of Rome, in addition to Aramaic, in addition to Greek — on top of all of that he was fluent in Hebrew. He was quite well educated. One of the leading thinkers of his day, Gamaliel counted Paul among his students (Acts 22:3). When it came to the law, he was a Pharisee — a Pharisee's Pharisee. That is to say, he kept the law in such a way that other Pharisees wanted to live up to his example. He was the cream of the crop. The kid all the parents pointed to and said, "Why don't you be more like him?" If anyone had a chance of living up to God's standard on his own, it was Paul. But Paul says that's garbage. He has no confidence in his flesh. All his accomplishments are worthless. One might say — as Isaiah put it — "as filthy rags" compared to knowing Christ.

He explains why in Ephesians. We won't be turning there, but I'll summarize Ephesians 2 for you. It says we are dead in sin, followers of the world, followers of the devil, sons of disobedience, subservient to the passions of our flesh. By nature we are children of wrath — separated from Christ due to our sin, alienated, hostile, slaves to the law. Think about that. What can a dead body do on its own? Absolutely nothing. We have horror shows in our culture that prove this. We are fascinated with this idea of the dead rising up and attacking the living — the dead walking down streets, the dead eating people's brains. Millions of dollars each year are made scaring people based around this concept of the walking dead. Yet is not this exactly how we are apart from Christ?

Jesus said as much in John 6. Verse 44: "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him." Again in verse 65, Jesus says: "This is why I told you that no one can come to me unless it is granted him by the Father."

Apart from God, what do we have to offer? Zilch. Zero. Zip. Nada. Nothing. Apart from the grace and mercy of God we are a dead body walking around. A famous preacher of the Great Awakening said: *"You contribute nothing to your salvation except the sin that made it necessary!"* — Jonathan Edwards. *(Attribution widely circulated; original source in Edwards's works has not been definitively identified — see source notes.)*

The only thing we bring to the table is need. I saw an interview with R.C. Sproul — a pastor out of Orlando. He said he had been talking with a man and outright asked him if he loved God as much as he thought he ought to. In truth, no one can say yes to this. If you think you do, I challenge you to look squarely into scripture and find your reflection — because I promise you what you will see is just how far short we all fall from loving God like we ought.

So we are dead in our trespasses, children of wrath. Apart from Christ we can't do anything good. Because of our choice to sin, we have selected a nature that prevents us from even willingly following Christ of our own choice. We can't get away from it, we can't overcome it — by turning our lives around we can't escape even then. We are dead, and what can a dead body do? Not much.

But that is where Christ comes in. Before we can even follow him, he has to do a work in us. Before we even have an interest in following Christ, he's already been at work on our hearts. This is the beginning of grace. When governed by our own ambitions we spurn Christ, but he works on our hearts so that we can accept him.

As a result of choosing to sin, we also chose to separate ourselves from God. If our earthly life comes to an end and we are still in that condition, what happens to us? If we die separate from God, we remain separate from God into eternity. In short, we choose hell.

Want to hear something scary? Is your relationship with God marked by obedience rooted in faith? I heard a guy preach recently that being a Christian in the Bible Belt is dangerous — because going to church is easy. It's easy to bring a dish to a potluck. It's easy to stay late and vacuum — and I am glad some of you do! It's easy to go to Sunday school and Bible study. It's easy to look real good and still be as lost as a goat. Lost as a tare, lost as chaff among the wheat. So is your relationship with God marked by obedience rooted in faith? Because if not — if you are making no movement, if there are no marks of obedience to God in your life — you are not a Christian, and you should consider yourself lost and in danger of damnation.

You might think that's hard, or mean-spirited, or cruel. But I think it's more cruel to allow you to choose hell without being warned. I think it's mean-spirited *not* to share the gospel with people.

Instead we should follow the example of Billy Sunday. Are you familiar with him? *(Note: Billy Sunday, 1862–1935, was a nationally celebrated revivalist — not "locally famous"; see source notes.)* He would go around sharing the gospel with people, preaching on street corners, and basically anyone who would sit still long enough would hear the gospel. This is what Billy Sunday said:

> "I'm against sin. I'll kick it as long as I have a foot. I'll fight it as long as I have a fist. I'll butt it as long as I have a head. I'll bite it as long as I've got a tooth. And when I'm old and fistless and footless and toothless, I'll gum it till I go home to Glory and it goes home to perdition."

Does God expect us to be perfect? No — he provided the cross for that. He expects us to try to meet his standard. To push through, and even though we trip and fall, to keep getting back up and pressing forward time and time again. If we were all perfect and faultless, God wouldn't have had to send his Son to die. But we are anything but perfect, and God loved us anyway. So he sent his Son to die on the cross for us.

I have this mental picture. When my sister and I were kids — and indeed with her kids now — they absolutely love to pile on you and let you drag them around. Wyley loves to lay on the floor and hold on to your ankles while you try to walk. Lily and Violet sit on top of your shoe and wrap their arms and legs around your ankle. My niece Paris loves to jump on my back when I'm not paying attention and wrap her arms around my neck and just dangle there. One at a time it's not so bad — you can kind of struggle along. But when all of them are hanging on to you, dragging on the floor, sitting on your feet, and hanging from your neck, it becomes really hard to move. I still can. But when I am entangled with those kids, it's hard to push forward. It's easy to stumble, easy to fall, easy to get tripped up by an extra couple hundred pounds of kids dangling off you. Is it not the same with sin? Without the work of Christ in your heart you don't even try. Those that don't even try should consider themselves lost. So many people in the cultural South are Christians not because they follow Christ but because of perceived social benefits.

The Scripture does not say you must be better. Scripture says you must be born again.

**The Baptist Faith and Message says on the topic of grace:**

> **V. God's Purpose of Grace**
> Election is the gracious purpose of God, according to which He regenerates, justifies, sanctifies, and glorifies sinners. It is consistent with the free agency of man, and comprehends all the means in connection with the end. It is the glorious display of God's sovereign goodness, and is infinitely wise, holy, and unchangeable. It excludes boasting and promotes humility.
> All true believers endure to the end. Those whom God has accepted in Christ, and sanctified by His Spirit, will never fall away from the state of grace, but shall persevere to the end.

Finally, the famous Baptist preacher of the 19th century, James Petigru Boyce *(spelled "Petigrew" in source — corrected; described as "last century" in source — Boyce died 1888; see source notes)*, wrote this:

> "Grace alone. The words *sola gratia* mean that human beings have no claim upon God. That is, God owes us nothing except just punishment for our many and very willful sins. Therefore, if he does save sinners, which he does in the case of some but not all, it is only because it pleases him to do it. Indeed, apart from this grace and the regenerating work of the Holy Spirit that flows from it, no one would be saved, since in our lost condition, human beings are not capable of winning, seeking out, or even cooperating with God's grace. By insisting on 'grace alone' the Reformers were denying that human methods, techniques, or strategies in themselves could ever bring anyone to faith. It is grace alone expressed through the supernatural work of the Holy Spirit that brings us to Christ, releasing us from our bondage to sin and raising us from death to spiritual life."
>
> — James Petigru Boyce *(likely from* Abstract of Systematic Theology*, 1887 — verify citation)*

So God owes us nothing. Absolutely nothing. What we deserve is eternity separated from him. But those of us who are believers in Jesus Christ he has chosen to save. Like Ephesians 2:8–9 says: "For it's by grace you have been saved, through faith, and this not of yourself — it's the gift of God, lest any man should boast." When God intercedes on our behalf it is an undeserved blessing. If you ask him and mean it, you will be saved, scripture says.

Gospel.
