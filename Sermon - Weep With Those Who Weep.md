# Weep With Those Who Weep
## A Pastoral Response to Racial Violence and the Dallas Shootings

**Primary Text**: Psalm 22
**Supporting**: Romans 12:15; 2 Corinthians 5:18; Genesis 9:6

**Context**: This was a Wednesday night prayer meeting. The planned text was the Noaic covenant (Genesis 9), but the week's events — the shootings of Alton Sterling and Philando Castile, and the subsequent sniper attack on Dallas police officers (July 7, 2016) — led to setting that aside entirely. This service ended in corporate lament and intercessory prayer using the structure of Psalm 22.

---

## Opening: A Friend Named Kev

Before we start tonight, I want to talk a little bit about a friend of mine. He asked me to call him Kev.

He is a believer in Jesus Christ. He works security for SEBTS. He graduated out of a friend of mine's youth group in Tampa. He attended a Bible college as an undergraduate, and feels called into full-time Christian service.

Twenty-five times in one semester, he was pulled over — commanded to get out of the car, lay on the ground, hands behind your head — while the police officer searched his car, called a drug dog out, checked his pockets, and eventually let him go on his way, now late, to work or school.

Twenty-five times in sixteen weeks. More than once a week.

The semester after that, he was kind enough to give me a ride to school when my car — my old Oldsmobile — had died. He warned me ahead of time that it might happen. That I might have to answer for why I would be in a car with such a man. Such a black man.

This is, unfortunately, what it is like to be black at times in our country.

## Two More Voices

Another guy I went to school with here at Trinity — who just won a Dove Award, and I think a Grammy — was pulled over along with his wife and kids. Held at gunpoint until the other officer could run the plates and prove he didn't have drugs on him.

*[Note: The individual described is believed to be KB (Kevin Burgess), a Christian rapper. Dove Award and Grammy wins are referenced from the pastor's recollection — specific awards and dates not verified.]*

And then there's a friend of a friend. Not hugely famous, but famous enough — nominated, I believe, twenty-eight times for Dove and Grammy awards, with at least one album on top of the Billboard 200 for something like twenty-five weeks. Every album he has released has topped the iTunes chart. He has his own record label.

*[Note: The individual described appears to be Lecrae. His album "Anomaly" debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 in September 2014. The specific nomination count, chart weeks, and Madison Square Garden show referenced below are from the pastor's recollection and have not been independently verified.]*

This fellow was in New York, heading to a sold-out show at Madison Square Garden. He was driving himself through the city in a ten-year-old Honda CRV. He was pulled over — no reason given — ordered at gunpoint out of the car, told to lie facedown on the ground, hands behind his head, legs spread. The officer took a knife and cut open the seats of his car, presumably searching for drugs. Called in a drug dog. Found nothing.

Lecrae has said it wasn't the first time, and it won't be the last.

## Where They Are Now

My buddy Kev told me he was really afraid at first. Then resentful — as anyone would be. I asked him where he was at now. He said: *You know, I've come to think about it like this. If this is what it takes for that officer to feel secure, then let him search me. I have nothing to hide. And besides, in a small way, I can identify with Christ through this persecution.*

KB — the second man I talked to — said he is worried about the impact these things are going to have on his son. His boy is two years old, and has already seen his dad pulled from the car at gunpoint half a dozen times in Atlanta. He says he wants to see this sort of thing die with him, and not be carried on to the next generation.

He tells his son that cops are a shield between us and the bad guys. That cops are heroes who put their lives on the line every day to protect good people from bad people. He tells him that sometimes cops make mistakes, but they are trying hard not to, and when they do, you just listen, and be respectful, and you are going to be ok.

Lecrae says the answer is twofold. Humility — and the gospel.

Because it takes humility to understand suffering that has been imposed on other people. It takes humility to walk a mile in their shoes. It takes humility to say: *I haven't seen that. But if that has been your experience, that is terrible, and we as a society need to change.*

And the gospel is a great equalizer — or should be, for those who truly believe. As believers in Jesus Christ there is no slave, no free, no Jew, no Greek. We are all brothers and sisters. When one hurts, we should all hurt together. And a big section of our family is hurting and being hurt right now.

## This Week

I'm sure you've heard about the two black men who were killed, and the officers who were shot in Dallas. The details are still shaking out, and the full picture isn't yet clear.

A Baptist pastor in Tampa — John O. — was interviewed by the Gospel Coalition this week. He said his seven-year-old daughter came to him in tears, worried for her two-year-old little brother. Worried he would be shot. He said: *Baby, I'm worried too.*

In Dallas, a protest was being held in the wake of these deaths. Rightly or wrongly taken — we don't know that yet. What we do know is that somewhere between one and five individuals were involved in a coordinated sniper attack on police officers. Police say it had been planned three to six months in advance.

*[Note: The pastor described the protest organizer as "a white Muslim man named Hood." Jeff Hood, who helped organize the Dallas march, was a white progressive Christian minister and chaplain — not Muslim. This appears to reflect early, inaccurate reporting from the day of the shooting. His religious identity is complex, but Muslim is not accurate.]*

We also know that when the bullets started flying, rather than taking cover, the police circled around the pedestrians and intentionally stood in the line of fire — shielding these protestors from Micah Johnson, who was targeting them. Eleven officers were shot. Five were killed.

I have a great deal of respect for police officers. These officers in particular.

Even when it became clear that cops were being targeted, they held the line. Think about what that means. Think about who that sounds like. Who else do we know who gave up his life so that others might live?

## What Scripture Says

I know there are good cops and bad cops. Good white folks and bad. Good black folks and bad. And we don't know who is what in most of these situations until a jury has settled it. So what should our response be?

We look to Scripture. And then we pray.

The passage I had planned to go through tonight — the one I'm setting aside — deals with the Noaic covenant. God says that any man or beast that takes the life of a man will have his life taken, because it is wrong to destroy anything that bears the image of God.

*[Note: The pastor references "Genesis 7" here. The Noaic covenant regarding the shedding of blood and the image of God is Genesis 9:6 — "Whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed, for God made man in his own image." Genesis 7 is the flood narrative. The reference in the transcript appears to be a misspoken chapter number.]*

Later in Scripture — and Jesus himself says it — "all who take the sword will perish by the sword" (Matthew 26:52). We are under grace now — things work differently. But apart from Christ, and his work on the cross taking away our sin, wouldn't we still cry out for an eye for an eye? So many are doing so right now, for perceived and real offenses.

But don't forget: in the original creation — before the fall of Adam and Eve — none of this was ever meant to happen.

*[Note: The pastor references "the lion laying next to the lamb" as an image of pre-fall peace. Isaiah 11:6 reads "the wolf shall dwell with the lamb" — the popular "lion and lamb" phrasing is a common conflation of Isaiah 11:6 and 65:25. The pastor is using it in passing as a shorthand image, not as a cited text.]*

God made everything perfect and good, and man was *very* good. It was us who made a mess of it. In the words of the hymn — Stuart Townend's "How Deep the Father's Love for Us" — *it was my sin that held Him there until it was accomplished.* My sin. Your sin. We are all culpable for the state of every broken thing. We are all culpable for the racial mess. We are all culpable for every evil that happens. As my dad likes to say — we are ministers of reconciliation. Scripture says that too, in 2 Corinthians 5:18. In our evangelistic and discipleship efforts, we need to be reaching out not only to people who look like us, but to people who don't. Helping families reconcile. Helping races reconcile. Helping the lost be reconciled to God.

Romans 12:15 gives us another task. *Weep with those who weep.* Guilt and innocence aside for now — there are a lot of people we need to be weeping for. Including Micah Johnson. He was an Afghan war veteran, and clearly we as a country let him down. Any time anyone breaks on that level and responds through murder, society loses. But it is especially heartbreaking when someone who put his life on the line for this country felt he had no other recourse but to murder — and chose to target the very men who, that very night, stood in front of bullets to protect the people he claimed to love.

## A Time to Lament

We as a nation need to spend time in lament.

Open to Psalm 22, if you brought your Bible.

There are a number of types of psalms. This one is a lament. And it has a structure common to all laments: Address — Complaint — Request — Expression of Trust. That is a good model for crying out to God in times of great need.

It is also worth noting that this particular psalm prophesies about Christ.

---

### The Structure of Psalm 22

**The Address** — verse 1:

> *"My God, my God"*

He is praying to someone. That is where lament starts.

**The Complaint** — verses 1–2 and 15:

> *"Why have you forsaken me? Why are you so far from saving me, from the words of my groaning? O my God, I cry by day, but you do not answer, and by night, but I find no rest. . . . my strength is dried up like a potsherd, and my tongue sticks to my jaws; you lay me in the dust of death."* (vv. 1–2, 15 ESV)

*[Note: The pastor quoted v. 15 as "My mouth is dried up like a potsherd" and v. 2 as "I cry out by day." Both reflect NIV wording. ESV text is used above. The core sense is identical; the precise words differ.]*

**The Request** — verses 19–21:

> *"But you, O LORD, do not be far off! O you my help, come quickly to my aid! Deliver my soul from the sword, my precious life from the power of the dog! Save me from the mouth of the lion! You have rescued me from the horns of the wild oxen!"*

**Expressions of Trust** — scattered throughout:

> *"Yet you are holy, enthroned on the praises of Israel. In you our fathers trusted; they trusted, and you delivered them. . . . Yet you are he who took me from the womb; you made me trust you at my mother's breasts. . . . I will tell of your name to my brothers; in the midst of the congregation I will praise you. . . . For he has not despised or abhorred the affliction of the afflicted, and he has not hidden his face from him, but has heard, when he cried to him."* (vv. 3–4, 9, 22, 24 ESV)

*[Note: The pastor quoted v. 24 as "he has not despised or scorned the suffering of the afflicted one... but has listened to his cry for help" — NIV wording. ESV reads "abhorred the affliction" and "has heard, when he cried to him." ESV text used above.]*

This is the model. Address. Complaint. Request. Trust.

### Prayer Guide (Lament Structure)

Pray through each of these with the congregation:

1. The deep racial divide and the tensions tearing at us right now.
2. That we would be given great empathy — the humility to understand what it is like to live in conditions others face by virtue of their skin.
3. For the officers involved in the prior shootings — for truth, and for a fair process.
4. For perfect justice in each situation. We don't know who is guilty of what yet. Pray that the truth comes out, and that it is answered rightly.
5. For people to carry the gospel to the families who have lost someone.
6. That kids can be kids — no matter what neighborhood they grow up in.
7. For opportunities to be ministers of reconciliation — in families, between races, and between God and man.
8. For opportunities to share the gospel with loved ones and strangers.
9. For inroads into the community around us for the gospel.
10. For God to be at work in and through whoever will serve as our youth pastor.
11. For God to increase his love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, and self-control — in us, and in our country.
12. For our president — that God would direct him according to his purposes.
13. For the election — that God would make clear whom we are to seek, and that He is already at work in that outcome.
14. For Larry and Lois.
15. For Jill and Duane as they travel.
16. By name — for those who aren't here tonight.
17. For those participating in the weekly prayer drives.
18. For those recently baptized.
19. For the food.
