# Service — Ann Burdette's Funeral
## Ecclesiastes 3:1–8

---

First of all, thank you for coming. Those of us that knew Granny — that knew Ann — loved her dearly. She was quick to smile, and her smile could light up a room. She loved the Lord, and always made sure her kids made it to church. I had the privilege of being her pastor the last little bit of her life. I'd always ask her how Martha and Sylvia, or Carl and Ashley were doing. She'd smile and say they were doing good, or sometimes she'd say she was proud of them.

The last time I visited with her, her health was struggling. I said, Ann, we're all adults here — we all know that one day we're going to take one last breath, and that might not be too far off for you. So I have to ask: when you take that last breath, do you know where you're going to go?

She responded with more force than I'd seen her respond to anything in a while. She said, *"Yes! I've been waiting to go there and be with Jesus for a long time. I'm ready!"* Then she said, *"I just want all my family to be there too."*

Her last words to me have shaped my preparations for today. If you've brought your Bibles — and I hope you have — open with me to Ecclesiastes 3:1–8. I'm reading from the English Standard Version today.

---

> For everything there is a season, and a time for every matter under heaven: a time to be born, and a time to die; a time to plant, and a time to pluck up what is planted; a time to kill, and a time to heal; a time to break down, and a time to build up; a time to weep, and a time to laugh; a time to mourn, and a time to dance; a time to cast away stones, and a time to gather stones together; a time to embrace, and a time to refrain from embracing; a time to seek, and a time to lose; a time to keep, and a time to cast away; a time to tear, and a time to sew; a time to keep silence, and a time to speak; a time to love, and a time to hate; a time for war, and a time for peace.
> — Ecclesiastes 3:1–8 (ESV)

---

It struck me that there are at least three seasons — or times — we need to talk about today. Granny's time. Our time. And God's time.

---

## Granny's Time

Granny **knew** her time here on earth was wearing thin. She knew her body was wearing out. She may have forgotten that moment to moment, but she knew it in her heart, and she was ready for what comes next.

The Bible talks in a number of places about heaven, and I want to give you a little glimpse of where she is now. Scripture talks about how the streets are paved with gold and the gates are made of pearl. More importantly than that, it talks about how we get a new body. So for Granny — no more struggling to get around. No more walker. No more difficulty eating. No more sickness, or sadness, or pain. I imagine the first thing she did when she got there was go and find her Savior.

Scripture says two things that sometimes people think conflict. It says there will be no sadness in heaven, but it also says God will wipe away every tear that is shed. If there is no sadness — and I believe the Bible when it says that — then these tears being shed are tears of joy. And her Savior is there to wipe them from her face. To give her hugs and say, *"Well done, good and faithful servant."* *(Matthew 25:21)*

Willie mentioned that Ann's life was marked by her efforts to make sure her kids grew up in church, to make sure her kids knew the Lord. And she spent a number of years as a teacher of other people's kids — telling them about Jesus.

For Ann, this new season of her life is one of rejoicing, happiness, and peace. If she were here today, she would tell you not to mourn for her — she's happy. And secondly, she'd say: *I'd better see you here too one day.*

---

## Our Time

Which brings me to our season. Our time.

It's hard losing someone we love. My grandmother passed away a number of years ago, and her death was something that deeply affected me — even though I knew beyond the shadow of a doubt that I would see her again. Her favorite verse and one of Ann's favorite verses were the same:

> Trust in the Lord with all your heart, and do not lean on your own understanding. In all your ways acknowledge him, and he will make straight your paths.
> — Proverbs 3:5–6 (ESV)

That's one of those things that is sometimes easier to say than to live. But I think Ann lived it. She knew what came next. She knew she was going to pass, and she was ready to meet her Savior.

For those of us left behind, it is indeed a sad thing. Sad because we miss them. But for those of us who believe in Jesus Christ as our Lord and Savior, death is not the end. Death is merely *so long — see you again soon.*

Paul wrote:

> O death, where is your victory? O death, where is your sting?
> — 1 Corinthians 15:55 (ESV)

In Jesus we have victory over death. Because we can know — that we know — that we know — we will see Ann again. We can allow hope to begin to fill that void in our hearts that Ann used to fill. We can allow God's love to fill us. We can rest in the hope and the promise of the future: not only that we get to see Ann again, but that we can be with our Savior in heaven. A place where the only tears shed are those of joy.

I asked myself what Ann would want from us at this time of grieving and healing. I think she would want us to celebrate because she's with her heavenly Father. Celebrate because she's not trapped inside a failing body anymore. Celebrate because where she is, we can also go.

---

## God's Time

So we've talked about Granny's time — the season she's experiencing. And briefly about the season we're going through without Granny. Now let's talk about the season God wants us to experience.

Firstly, even in the midst of death, God has good plans for us. There is hope. There is a future. God is directing those steps.

> For I know the plans I have for you, declares the LORD, plans for welfare and not for evil, to give you a future and a hope.
> — Jeremiah 29:11 (ESV)

This was as true for Granny as it is for us.

Even when things seem the most bleak, God has something good in store for us. What does it mean that he has a good plan for us? I think it stems from who God is. Scripture says that God is good. It means simply that God is not evil — he does not love sin. He cannot be corrupted or enticed into sinning. Goodness is as far from evil as the east is from the west.

Divine goodness goes hand in hand with divine justice. God, because he is good, is also just. But he is also kind. One of the ways he shows us his goodness is through his kindness.

Because of our inclination toward sin — because of our love for sin — there is only one thing we deserve from God, and that is an eternity separate from God. If God is good, and good and evil are as far apart as you can get, then to be apart from God is to be stewing in evil. If you want to know what hell is like, imagine the opposite of God. If God is compassionate, in hell will be all the cruelty that our enemy can muster. If God is perfect justice, hell will be all the injustice our enemy can muster. If God is love, hell will contain all the hate our enemy can muster.

It is kindness, then, that God offers us his unconditional love. It is mercy that he gives it to us without our being able to deserve it on our own. It is because of God's goodness that we have breath in our lungs, and light in our eyes, and scent in our noses. It's God's goodness that we have sunrises and sunsets and other beautiful works of God's hands to enjoy. It's God's kindness when we have food on the table and good fellowship with loved ones. It's God's goodness that allowed someone as wonderful as Ann in our lives. It's God's goodness that brought her home to a place where she can walk with ease once more — where she can hug the necks of those she's loved and lost who are in Christ. It's because of God's goodness that we can see Ann again one day in the presence of our Savior Jesus.

---

## The Gospel

I've mentioned Jesus as Savior and sin a couple of times. Let's expand on that a bit.

Sin is anything we do that displeases God. Let's look at a couple of the Ten Commandments. The Ten Commandments are just a beginning. To reach heaven apart from Jesus Christ, there are hundreds of laws that would have to be kept. But let's just look at this small subset that the Bible records for us in Exodus 20.

Let's start with the third commandment, which says, *"You shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain."* It's surprisingly common these days to hear people exclaim the name of our Lord as a curse. My little nieces tried it a few times. I've heard parents use it. It's even reached popularity in internet shorthand — people write *omg* all the time, which stands for *oh my God.* I'm guessing all of us have violated this third commandment. That means each and every one of us are blasphemers.

Let's jump to another one — ever disobeyed your parents? Even once? Disobedient.

Only two commandments in and we're disobedient blasphemers.

What about taking a cookie from the cookie jar when you know you're not supposed to? Or — and maybe this is showing my age — you remember how they used to have those bulk candies for sale at the grocery store? Ever eaten one of those without paying for it? Then we are thieves as well. Blaspheming, disobedient thieves.

Next up is hatred. Ever been so angry with someone you told yourself you hated them? I know I have. You know what the Bible says? *"Everyone who hates his brother is a murderer."* *(1 John 3:15)* Blaspheming, disobedient thieves who have committed murder in their heart. That's only four commandments — I think we get the idea.

When we are compared to the perfect, holy, and righteous standard of God, we fall short. We are incapable of measuring up. The Bible says this:

> ...for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God.
> — Romans 3:23 (ESV)

That's exactly what we have just been talking about. We couldn't even get through four of the commandments without being murderous thieves and disobedient blasphemers. There are 613 commandments in the Old Testament. We couldn't get through four. I'm still human — I've done these things too. These things are just as true of me as they are of you.

> For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast.
> — Ephesians 2:8–9 (ESV)

So basically, there is nothing we can do about it on our own. It's like this. *(visual gospel illustration)*

As a result:

> For the wages of sin is death...
> — Romans 6:23 (ESV)

So we all have this sin — all of us — that we can do nothing about on our own. Though we try to be better, even one single sin is enough to separate us from God. The payment we deserve for a life spent sinning is death. Eternity separated from God. And there's nothing you or I can do about it on our own.

That's some pretty heavy bad news. But the good news that Ann wanted to make sure you heard today was that 2,000 years ago God sent his Son Jesus to pay that price for us on the cross. We deserved death. But he died on our behalf. He was nailed to a cross. I won't get too graphic, but it was arguably the most horrific way man has devised to kill another man. After his time on the cross, he was buried and guarded by Roman soldiers. Three days later he rose again from the dead. *O death, where is your sting?* There were more than five hundred recorded witnesses. People saw him walking through walls and doing things only God can do. *(1 Corinthians 15:6)*

Point is this, people: Jesus is the only one who has been to heaven and come back to tell us how to get there.

> ...because, if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved.
> — Romans 10:9 (ESV)

---

## The Invitation

I'd like you all to close your eyes and bow your heads for a moment. Let me ask you this — by show of hands, with no one looking: how many of you know that you know that you know that when you take your last breath, you'll wake up in heaven with Jesus, with Ann?

If you don't, and you would like to one day go to heaven and be with Jesus, I'm going to invite you to pray this prayer with me. In fact, those of you who are already Christians, I want you to say it out loud as well.

*Dear Jesus, I know I'm a sinner. I know I have fallen short of your standard. I know that what I deserve is death and separation from you — but that you are offering me an alternative. I believe that you died for my sins. I trust your word, and that you rose again three days later. So Jesus, I put my trust in you, that one day when I take my last breath I'll wake up in heaven. Thank you for adopting me as one of your children. Thank you for saving me. In Jesus' name I pray. Amen.*

With every head still bowed and every eye still closed — if you prayed that prayer for the first time and meant it, please raise your hand.

*(Pause.)*

I see your hand. Thank you. You can open your eyes again.

If you prayed that prayer for the first time and meant it today, I know you've made Ann proud. But I also encourage you to talk to your family about the decision you made today, or come talk to me. It's important that you find a church — it doesn't have to be my church, but some church that believes in the Word of God and can help you learn more about the One who died for you.

---

*Closing song. Closing prayer. Thank everyone that came. Pray for their comfort, their peace, their relationship with God.*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Courageous Gospel Invitation in a Difficult Room — Funeral Homily That Goes Where Most Won't**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A faithful funeral homily on Ecclesiastes 3:1–8 that honored the deceased, genuinely comforted the grieving, and courageously moved from pastoral warmth into a clear, textually-grounded gospel presentation and an evangelist's invitation — rare at a graveside.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS — with qualification**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's three-part thesis ("Granny's time, Our time, God's time") is a pastoral structure overlaid on Ecclesiastes 3, not a direct exposition of the passage's own argument. However, the passage's core claim — that there is a divinely appointed time for every matter under heaven, including birth and death — genuinely governs the sermon's movement. The sermon does not contradict the text's main point; it derives a legitimate pastoral application from it.

*Authority Test*: The preacher stands under the text. He does not edit out its hard edges — he adds harder edges in the gospel section that most funeral preachers would soften or omit. **Pass.**

*Rubric Fit Note applies* (see item 17): Ecclesiastes 3:1–8 is wisdom literature operating within the Qohelet framework, and this is a funeral homily — a genre with its own pastoral logic. The rubric must bend slightly here. A funeral sermon that also delivers a full gospel presentation is not a typical expository sermon, and the scoring reflects this contextual awareness.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**None triggered.**

The gospel is explicitly and clearly presented (Section: "The Gospel"). Christ is essential to the sermon's argument. Application is differentiated — the living are addressed in their grief, the unbeliever is addressed evangelistically, and the believer is addressed in terms of hope and reunion. An explicit invitation to respond is given. The sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers — including the unchurched family members present at a funeral.

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 16/25**

The honest qualification: the Qohelet argument (that the time-boundedness of all things under the sun creates existential tension, pointing to eternity, Eccl. 3:11) is not surfaced — the preacher takes the comfort without naming the tension.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Evangelistic courage in a funeral setting.** Most funeral preachers avoid the gospel presentation out of social pressure. This sermon walks directly into it — naming sin explicitly, quoting commandments with memorable applications, and issuing an altar call. Ann's stated dying wish ("I just want all my family to be there too") is used to justify the evangelistic turn. This is pastorally excellent and theologically faithful.

2. **Accessible sin diagnosis.** The commandment sequence (blasphemy → disobedience → theft → hatred/murder) is concrete, accumulative, and culturally grounded. "Blaspheming, disobedient thieves who have committed murder in their heart" is the kind of memorable category that a funeral attendee will carry home. The preacher does not exempt himself: "I'm still human — I've done these things too."

3. **Structural clarity.** "Granny's time, Our time, God's time" is a simple, memorable, funeral-appropriate outline that any listener can track. The progression from memorial to grief to gospel to invitation is purposeful and complete.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Ecclesiastes 3 is used as a comfort text without engaging its actual argument.** The Qohelet preacher uses the "times" poem to drive toward existential tension, not pastoral ease — "He has made everything beautiful in its time. He has also set eternity in the human heart; yet no one can fathom what God has done from beginning to end" (Eccl. 3:11). This verse, sitting immediately after the passage, is the key to everything: God has appointed times, and he has planted in the human heart a longing for eternity that nothing under the sun can satisfy. This is the text's own gospel hinge — and the sermon does not touch it. The preacher takes the comfort of the poem while leaving behind the very verse that would ground that comfort in the text itself.

2. **The "God's Time" section is the weakest.** Jeremiah 29:11 is used in its popular but contextually stripped form (a corporate restoration promise given to exiles reapplied as a personal promise of individual prosperity). The sermon then moves into a description of God's goodness that, while accurate, reads more as a homily on divine attributes than as an application of Ecclesiastes 3. The transition into "The Gospel" section feels like a gear shift rather than a natural development.

3. **Spiritual differentiation is binary: believer vs. unbeliever.** The sermon addresses the grieving Christian (hope of reunion) and the unconverted (gospel invitation), but it does not address the person who has heard this gospel a hundred times and not responded, or the backslider who was once in church with Ann, or the person who is convinced they are a Christian but has never been truly converted. These categories are likely well represented at a family funeral. Addressing them would not require more time — only a sentence or two of direct address.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not recognize that the visual gospel illustration ("*(visual gospel illustration)*") — almost certainly a bridge diagram or similar — does significant load-bearing work in the sermon as delivered, and that its absence from the manuscript creates a gap in the text's argument. The illustration is referenced but not transcribed, which means the connective tissue between "we can do nothing on our own" and "Jesus paid the price" exists in a diagram rather than in words.

The preacher is also likely unaware that Ecclesiastes 3:11 is the theological gold mine immediately adjacent to his text. Using it would have unified the three sections of the sermon under one argument: all times are in God's hands, eternity is written on every human heart, and only the gospel of Christ can satisfy that longing.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The three-section outline ("Granny's time, Our time, God's time") feels more structurally tight than it is. The three sections are not equally weighted by the text — the passage itself makes no such division. The structure is pastoral and memorable, but it is imported onto the text rather than arising from it. The outline works as a funeral outline; it does not work as an exposition of Ecclesiastes 3. The risk is that the preacher credits the text with giving him these categories when the text only supplies the general theme.

Similarly, the Jeremiah 29:11 quotation sounds like a strong textual anchor but is used in its decontextualized popular form. It does not add theological precision — it adds familiarity. That is not the same thing.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Ecclesiastes 3:11 — "He has set eternity in the human heart."** This verse is the key that unlocks everything. The reason the funeral congregation is aching is not merely that they loved Ann — it is that eternity has been written on their hearts, and every death confronts them with the world-sized gap that only God can fill. Ann's own dying declaration ("I've been waiting to go there and be with Jesus for a long time. I'm ready!") is the human voice of Ecclesiastes 3:11 — a soul that found what the text says every human heart is longing for. Using this verse would have: (a) grounded the comfort in the text's own argument, (b) given the gospel section a stronger foundation, and (c) unified the three-part outline under one theological idea.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific)

1. **Add Ecclesiastes 3:11 as the sermon's theological keystone.** After reading the passage, note that the very next verse after the poem says: "He has made everything beautiful in its time. He has also set eternity in the human heart." This is the text's own explanation of why the times matter — and why every human heart aches at a graveside. Ann found the One who satisfies that ache. The sermon can then frame Granny's time, Our time, and God's time as three ways of seeing what it means to live with eternity written on the heart.

2. **Replace or contextualize Jeremiah 29:11.** Either use it with a brief acknowledgment that this is a promise originally given to Israel in exile (which actually strengthens it — God met his exiled people, and he meets us in our grief), or replace it with a verse that more directly addresses the present moment of loss (e.g., Psalm 116:15, "Precious in the sight of the LORD is the death of his saints," or Romans 8:38–39).

3. **Add one sentence addressing the person who has heard the gospel many times but never responded.** After the invitation prayer, one direct sentence: "Some of you have heard the gospel your whole life — you grew up in church alongside Ann — and you've never made it your own. Today is the day." This is where the backslider and the nominal churchgoer live, and they are unaddressed.

4. **Transcribe or summarize the visual gospel illustration in the manuscript.** The parenthetical "*(visual gospel illustration)*" leaves a gap in the written sermon. Even a one-sentence summary of what the diagram shows will make the manuscript self-contained and strengthen the argument from sin to Christ.

5. **Tighten the "God's Time" section.** The attributes of God (goodness, justice, kindness) are theologically accurate but structurally loose. Consider cutting the middle portion (describing what hell looks like by inverting divine attributes) and replacing it with a tighter transition: "God's time for us is this — a God who is good enough to judge our sin, and kind enough to provide a way out." This clears the runway for "The Gospel" section to land cleanly.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This is a funeral homily, not a Sunday expository sermon, and the rubric bends appropriately in two places:

1. **Exposition scoring is adjusted for genre.** A funeral homily is not expected to work through a passage verse-by-verse. The legitimate pastoral use of a text — drawing on its main theme to address the occasion — is a recognized homiletical form. The critique of exposition here is not that the preacher failed to exposit thoroughly, but that he missed the text's own theological peak (Eccl. 3:11) which would have strengthened his pastoral application.

2. **Evangelistic weight is disproportionately high for the occasion — and that is a feature, not a bug.** A funeral homily that issues a full gospel call is unusual and courageous. The rubric's gospel centrality and exhortation categories reward this appropriately. The score does not penalize the preacher for not being a Sunday expository sermon.
