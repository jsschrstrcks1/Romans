# Service — Kim's Funeral
## Psalm 23

---

At the request of the family, we will be reading out of Psalms 23 today. While you are turning there if you've brought your bibles, and I hope you have, I want to talk about the psalm itself. It has a lot of depth, its one we could spend a month of Sundays talking about without fully plumbing its depths. I'm going to try and keep my part short, and still do justice to the psalm.

This passage reminds me a lot of Kim for a lot of reasons. Lets read it:

---

Lets start with **The Lord is my shepherd.** Some of you that know me, know I have a flock of about a dozen sheep. I love having sheep. The little ones are so cute running across the fields in spring. They are just figuring out how to run by then, and their back ends sometimes get away from them, and they end up turned around backwards while they run. It's the cutest thing. But sheep aren't smart when it comes to a lot of things. They need protected from predators. Their instinctual response will get them killed more often than not. Some of them will eat things that can make them sick. Looking at the second verse gives you another insight into sheep. The shepherd makes them lay down by the still waters. Sheep for all their spring time cuteness, don't always know when and where to rest. They have to be guided to safe comfortable places, and made to lay down, sometimes.

That makes me think of Kim a little bit. She was always doing something. Always working. She helped keep the church clean for a while, and on Sunday mornings she would get here about an hour early and help get things started. She was always quick to make coffee for everyone – good coffee too. She'd almost always apologize for it each week also. *Sorry I don't think I got the coffee right this week* she would say. But I never heard anyone complain.

She also would bundle coupons for people. Rolled up and kept together with a rubber band, she kept probably half the church supplied. If Kim liked you, she would do just about anything for you. The one thing I didn't see Kim do much of was rest. Unless it was after 7:30 or so in the evening. Or before about 4 in the morning. Otherwise she was as busy as an ant doing a little of this or a little of that. Getting ready for a garage sale, or doing some house cleaning. She was quick to offer help to anyone she liked.

---

**Vs 3 says that "He restores my soul."** I'm not sure how many people she told about this, but when she was younger she had been a Sunday school teacher. Each week she would prepare a lesson for kids, and would teach them about the Lord. Despite the darker roads she traveled later in life, I believe one day those of us that know Jesus Christ as our Lord and Savior will see Kim again. Coming back to church was a fairly recent thing for Kim. Maybe the last 9 or so months of her life. I know she made a big impact on us here. We will miss her warm smiles, and peanut butter chocolate cake. We will miss her willingness to help, and the love she had for us, and the Lord.

She wasn't one to express her love with words. She would say she loved you through her actions. I doubt there was anyone living she loved more than her son David. She would keep his bed made, and do many of the chores he couldn't do. She'd cook, and clean, and bicker and fight with him. A wise man once told me that love and anger are close to each other. I think that he meant both require passion and strong emotion. That both often go hand in hand, the people you love most, sometimes, you get the most angry with. She loved the Lord also. I remember several times while preaching, I would look out over the crowd, and see Kim mopping the tears from her eyes while we were talking about Jesus. Or the weight of our sin.

---

Kim's story is like the parable that Jesus told.

> "See that you do not despise one of these little ones. For I tell you that in heaven their angels always see the face of my Father who is in heaven. What do you think? If a man has a hundred sheep, and one of them has gone astray, does he not leave the ninety-nine on the mountains and go in search of the one that went astray? And if he finds it, truly, I say to you, he rejoices over it more than over the ninety-nine that never went astray. So it is not the will of my Father who is in heaven that one of these little ones should perish."
> — Matthew 18:10, 12–14 (ESV)

Kim was that one sheep. She came into the fold, and then got lost wandering down some side roads. But God loved her so much, he – like the shepherd in the parable – God pursued her, and brought her back into the fold. Kim couldn't have ever done anything that was so bad that God couldn't save her, there is no path so dark that God can't find you. There is nothing you can do that's so bad, that Jesus can't save you. There is hope for everyone, love for everyone, peace for everyone, security for everyone. Scripture says all who call on the name of the Lord shall be saved.

I've known Kim for probably close to 25 years, and if there is one thing that I KNOW for a fact about Kim its this – that she will want to see all of you in Heaven again one day. Your sister loved you, your aunt loved you, your cousin, or friend, or niece loved you. David, your mom loved you and she wants to see each of you again in heaven one day.

---

**Vs 4 says "even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I shall fear no evil. For you are with me, your rod and staff they comfort me."**

A shepherd's tools are used for a lot of things. The staff – or you may have heard it called a shepherd's crook – is used to pull a sheep back from trouble. If a sheep starts to wander, you grab it with your crook, and pull it back towards you, then you can give it medical attention, or shear the wool off, or take care of whatever the sheep needs. Most sheep tend to run away from help offered. Likewise the rod is used in protection of the sheep. If a hungry predator comes around, you use the rod like a baseball bat, and protect your flock from the predator. The rod and staff are the tools of God's help, and compassion, and care, and security for us. When we are hurting, God uses his staff to pull us close, and comfort us. When we are in danger, God uses his rod to protect us. When we go through these struggles of various kinds, God is there for us, to help us, to care for us, to protect us even from ourselves. To the point he was willing to send his son to take our death on the cross.

---

There are more than two kinds of people in this church today, but there are two kinds that I want to talk about right now.

Firstly, there are those – who like Kim are believers in Jesus Christ, they are part of God's flock of sheep. But they maybe – like Kim – have wandered away. I want you to know today that God is calling you back.

The second kind of person, that I think is here today, is someone who has not yet ever been a part of God's flock. Maybe you don't understand what it all means. Maybe you just know you want to see Kim again one day. God wants you to know this.

---

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Warmth and Gospel Courage at the Graveside — With Exposition That Stays Shallow**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A genuinely pastoral funeral address on Psalm 23 that reaches for the gospel with real courage and personal tenderness, while treating the psalm's own argument as a launching pad rather than a governing text.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**Conditional Pass — with significant caveat.**

*Scopus Test*: The sermon's thesis is roughly: "The Lord is the shepherd who pursues the wandering sheep and brings them home — trust him now." This is not incompatible with Psalm 23, but it is not quite the psalm's own argument either. Psalm 23's main point is the confident trust of a sheep who knows his shepherd — present tense security in the LORD's provision, guidance, and presence through the valley. The sermon tilts this toward the lost sheep parable of Matthew 18 and uses the psalm illustratively rather than expositionally.

*Authority Test*: The preacher submits to the text's comfort and does not contradict it. He does not edit the hard parts — there aren't many hard parts in Psalm 23. He uses the text as a frame, not as the argument's engine.

**Verdict**: The Berean Gate passes — the sermon does not contradict the psalm, and the gospel invitation arises naturally from the shepherd imagery. However, the sermon's weight is carried more by narrative and pastoral instinct than by the text's own logic. This is noted, not condemned — funeral homiletics under grief constraints are a legitimate pastoral genre. See Rubric Fit Note (#17).

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No fatal flaws triggered.** The gospel is clearly presented (Romans 3:23, 6:23, 5:8–9, John 3:16, Romans 10:9). Christ is necessary — his cross and resurrection are the basis for the hope offered. Application, while not elaborated in depth, is differentiated (believers who have wandered / those who have never believed). The congregation is clearly addressed and called to response. Accessibility is high — this is one of the most accessible funeral sermons possible.

**Note**: The gospel section is outlined as a bullet list of references rather than a written-out proclamation. This is a manuscript gap — the evaluator cannot score what is not on the page. The evaluation treats this section as gospel-present but notes the absence of full manuscript text as a limiting factor in scoring Gospel Centrality.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 15/25**

- *Textual Fidelity*: 6/10 — The sermon touches vv. 1–4 selectively. The shepherd illustration (sheep needing rest, needing guidance) is accurate and warmly rendered. But the psalm's own progression — from green pastures to the valley of the shadow to the table prepared before enemies to the house of the LORD — is not followed as an argument. Verse 5 (the table before enemies) and verse 6 (goodness and mercy following all one's days) are entirely absent. The text is used as a frame for memorial, not as the subject of proclamation.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 3/5 — The shepherd metaphor is treated with genuine insight from personal farming experience. The canonical move to Matthew 18 (the lost sheep) is natural and effective. No treatment of the psalm's Davidic authorship or its place in the Psalter.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 3/5 — The rod and staff explanation (v.4) is the strongest exegetical moment — clear, concrete, and pastorally applied. The "shadow of death" receives no unpacking beyond the obvious.
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 3/5 — No outright eisegesis. The problem is under-reading rather than over-reading. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is grief and lostness — this is present but more assumed than named.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness*: 5/7 — Christ is present and necessary. The cross reference in the rod/staff section ("to the point he was willing to send his son to take our death on the cross") is brief but real. The Matthew 18 parable is well-applied. Christ is not decoration.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness*: 2/3 — The move from Psalm 23 to the Good Shepherd motif is implicit but not developed. The connection is natural but understated.
- *Doctrinal Precision*: 3/4 — No theological errors. The gospel verses cited (Romans 3:23, 6:23, 5:8–9) are sound. The concern is vagueness: "there is hope for everyone, love for everyone, peace for everyone, security for everyone" — this universalizing language, without qualification, sounds more like broad grace than particular grace. Not heretical at a funeral context, but imprecise.
- *Confessional Alignment*: 3/4 — No confessional contradictions. The sinner's prayer / raised-hand format leans evangelical-revivalist rather than Reformed Baptist in tone, but this is a practical method, not a theological error.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 10/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel*: 3/5 — The gospel is clearly referenced via the listed verses, and the invitation is explicit. The limitation is that the full manuscript does not show us the actual proclamation — only the reference outline. Scored at mid-range accordingly.
- *Necessity of Christ*: 4/5 — Christ is essential to the sermon's conclusion. The "no path so dark God can't find you" section grounds hope in Christ's atoning work, not mere sentiment.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity*: 3/5 — The cross is mentioned but not dwelt upon. The sermon's emotional gravity is in Kim's story and the family's grief, with the cross as the doctrinal support rather than the sermon's climax.

*Synagogue Test*: A devout non-Christian would be mildly uncomfortable — the gospel invitation is explicit and the necessity of Christ is stated. The test is passed, though not with force. The sermon is Christ-present, not Christ-centered as its dominant key.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10/15**

- *Heart Penetration*: 4/5 — The portrait of Kim — the early-morning church arrival, the coffee apologies, the coupon bundles, the tears during preaching — is genuinely moving and pastorally wise. Grief is honored, not leveraged.
- *Specificity*: 3/5 — The two-category differentiation (wandering believer / unbeliever) is appropriate for the context. It does not press deeply into the specific conscience struggles of the grieving, but for a funeral this is appropriate restraint.
- *Spiritual Differentiation*: 3/5 — Two of Perkins' categories are addressed (the unbeliever, the backslider). The grieving believer is not distinctly addressed — those who are in Christ and simply mourning receive less specific pastoral care. A sentence to the confirmed believer sitting in grief would round this out.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 6/8**

- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness*: 2/4 — The congregation is called to conversion but not particularly equipped for ongoing discipleship, grief, or witness after the service. For a funeral, this category is rightly weighted toward conversion invitation rather than long-term equipping.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline*: 2/4 — The sermon flows somewhat loosely: introduction → sheep illustration + Kim memorial → parable of the lost sheep + gospel appeal → rod/staff → two-kinds-of-people + gospel → sinner's prayer. This is traceable in retrospect but would be hard to sketch in real-time for a grieving listener. The movement from pastoral eulogy to gospel invitation is natural — the architecture governing that movement is not explicit.
- *Progression*: 3/3 — The sermon builds toward the gospel invitation effectively. The emotional arc is sound: remembrance → recognition of need → gospel offer → response.
- *Unity*: 1/2 — The dominant idea is present (the Lord is the shepherd who finds the lost and brings them home), but the eulogy sections and the expositional sections compete rather than integrate.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

- *Relevance*: 2/2 — The sheep-from-personal-farming illustration is exactly right: native, concrete, humble, pastoral. It earns trust immediately.
- *Clarity*: 2/2 — The rod and staff explanation is the sermon's clearest illustrative moment.
- *Memorability*: 0/1 — The lamb-running-backward image is delightful and could be memorable, but it is used for a gentle point rather than a load-bearing truth.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching*: 2/2 — The cadence is conversational and warm. This reads like a man speaking to people he loves. The italicized Kim quotes and the direct address ("David, your mom loved you") are powerful sermonic moments.
- *Conviction on the Page*: 1/2 — The manuscript shows pastoral conviction but not prophetic weight. The assertions are tender rather than declarative.
- *Cadence and Breath*: 0/1 — The manuscript is fairly uniform in register. There is no moment where the sentence structure compresses toward a climax. The emotional peak ("Kim was that one sheep...") deserves a sharper landing.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
More of Christ than myself — the memorial material serves to illustrate Christ's pursuit of the lost, not to produce self-congratulation or sentimentalism. The gospel invitation is real. *Answer: Yes, though the balance tips toward Kim at points.*

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
The sermon's pastoral warmth and the image of Kim weeping during preaching carry genuine weight. God is present as shepherd and rescuer, not merely as theological category. *Answer: Partially yes — the encounter is available but not pressed.*

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Grieving believers would be comforted. The unconverted are convicted toward decision. The wandering believer is gently called back. *Answer: Yes — both comfort and conviction are present, though differentiation is limited.*

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The personal sheep-farming illustration is earned and pastoral.** The image of lambs running with their back ends getting away from them is native to this preacher's life — not a borrowed illustration. It establishes credibility and warmth immediately, and the application to sheep needing a shepherd is not labored.

2. **The gospel invitation is explicit and courageous.** In a mixed funeral congregation — where many will be unchurched family members — the direct call to repentance and faith using gospel texts (Romans 3:23, 5:8–9, John 3:16) takes pastoral backbone. The preacher does not soften the invitation in deference to grief. This is the sermon's best decision.

3. **The portrait of Kim is theologically productive, not merely sentimental.** The detail of Kim mopping tears during preaching about Jesus and the weight of sin is the most powerful line in the manuscript. It serves as evidence of genuine faith, connects the personal to the gospel, and honors the deceased without inflating her. The love-and-anger observation about Kim and David shows pastoral wisdom about human affection.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Psalm 23 is used as a backdrop rather than preached as a text.** Verses 5 and 6 — arguably the psalm's doxological climax — receive zero treatment. "Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life, and I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever" is the verse most naturally suited to a funeral — and it is entirely absent. The sermon is built around the shepherd imagery but does not follow the psalm's own argument to its conclusion.

2. **The gospel section exists only as a bullet-point reference list.** The actual proclamation of the gospel — the most critical moment in a funeral service filled with unconverted hearers — is outlined rather than written. This may indicate a section delivered extemporaneously, which is understandable, but it means the manuscript lacks the very content that most needs sharpening. The cross is mentioned briefly in the rod/staff section but the full substitutionary logic of "Christ died for sinners in our place" is assumed rather than declared.

3. **The "hope for everyone, love for everyone, peace for everyone" language risks universalizing grace without condition.** The statement "there is nothing you can do that's so bad that Jesus can't save you" is true as an expression of God's sufficiency — but without the accompanying call to repentance and faith as the means of receiving that grace, it can land as unconditional assurance to all present, regardless of spiritual state. The very next sentence ("Scripture says all who call on the name of the Lord shall be saved") provides the condition, but the sequence could be tightened.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that Psalm 23 verse 6 is the most naturally resonant funeral verse in the passage — and the one most suited to the very promise he wants to make (Kim is in the house of the LORD forever). The sermon builds toward that promise but never arrives at the verse that makes it explicit. The text itself offers the landing he is reaching for, and he passes over it.

Additionally, the grieving believer — the person who knows Christ, is not wandering, and is simply devastated by the loss of Kim — receives almost no direct address. The sermon addresses the backslider and the unbeliever with pastoral care, but the confirmed Christian sitting in grief is left to apply comfort to themselves.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Kim memorial sections feel like the sermon's heart, and in pastoral terms they are warm and skillful. But they carry more emotional weight than theological weight. A listener could leave the service primarily moved by Kim's story — her faithful service, her wandering, her return — rather than by the grace of the Shepherd who pursued her. The memorial material is excellent but needs to function more explicitly as *evidence of the gospel at work* rather than as the sermon's emotional center of gravity.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Psalm 23:6 — "I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever."** This is the psalm's own declaration of eternal life. The entire funeral service is building toward the question "Will we see Kim again?" — and the text itself answers that question in its final verse. The preacher reaches for this assurance through the Matthew 18 parable and through personal testimony, but bypasses the psalm's own climactic answer. Preaching verse 6 as the sermon's climax would have unified exposition, memorial, and gospel invitation in one textual moment.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Preach verse 6 as the sermon's closing anchor.** "I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever" — this is the text's own answer to the congregation's grief. Build the entire movement toward this verse. Kim's return to the fold, the shepherd's pursuit, the gospel invitation — all can culminate here.

2. **Write out the gospel proclamation in full manuscript form.** The bullet-point reference list for the gospel section is a manuscript gap that requires filling. The most critical moment in a funeral full of unbelievers must be the most carefully prepared. Draft 3–4 sentences per verse, moving clearly from human sin to Christ's substitution to faith as the means of reception.

3. **Add a sentence directly to the grieving believer.** After the two-categories section, add: "And then there are those of you who know the Shepherd — you are in his fold, you are not wandering — and today you are simply devastated. To you the Shepherd says the same thing: I am with you in this valley. My rod and my staff — they comfort you too." This fills the pastoral gap for Perkins' category of confirmed believers in grief.

4. **Qualify the universal grace language with explicit gospel conditions.** After "there is nothing you can do that's so bad that Jesus can't save you," immediately add the call: "But that grace comes only through Christ — through his death in your place, and your trust in him." This prevents the assurance from landing as unconditional.

5. **Compress the sheep illustration and extend the text's argument.** The opening sheep section is long enough to risk losing the congregation before the exposition begins. Trim it by half and use the recovered space to follow the psalm's logic through verse 6, which the sermon currently skips entirely.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** This sermon is not cold orthodoxy — it is warm pastoral care with real gospel urgency. The fire is genuine. The deficit is expositional depth, not pastoral heat.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment
**+3/5** — The eternal weight is genuinely present: a woman found by her Shepherd after years of wandering, now in the house of the LORD — and the invitation extended to her grieving family to follow her there. This is not performative gravity; it is real. The bonus is withheld at full value only because the gospel proclamation itself is underdeveloped in the manuscript, and the psalm's own doxological climax is absent.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
**This rubric strains somewhat against the funeral homily genre.** Psalm 23 is a lament-adjacent psalm of trust — lyric poetry, not didactic discourse. A funeral homily operates under constraints that normal preaching does not: a congregation gathered under acute grief, many of whom are unchurched, with a pastoral and evangelistic dual mandate that must be fulfilled simultaneously and briefly. The rubric's exposition categories penalize the sermon for not following the psalm's argument in full — but the pastoral and evangelistic demands of a funeral are legitimate reasons for selective treatment of the text. The score of 70 should be read in light of this genre context. The surgical fixes above are offered as genuine improvements within the funeral format, not as demands to turn this into a full expository sermon. The preacher made the right call prioritizing gospel invitation over comprehensive exposition. The question is whether both could have been achieved together — and the answer is yes, particularly by preaching verse 6.
