# Prayer Service — Bathing the Decision
### Nehemiah 1

---

## Opening

For those of you who are visiting with us tonight — welcome. This is not how we normally run things on a Wednesday. But I think it's an important thing to do from time to time, and especially when we're facing a big decision as a church.

For those of you who were here last week, you'll remember that someone has come forward offering to pursue the purchase of the building we're meeting in right now — from the Church of God that owns it. You can see their banners up around the room. Pastor Cox's congregation worships here on Sunday mornings, and we want to handle this whole process with generosity toward him and toward his church — at least as generous as we'd want anyone to be with us. Maybe more so.

This is a significant decision, and we're not going to rush it. We've committed to bathing it in prayer for the next sixty-one days — until July 15th. During that time we'll also be doing our homework: figuring out the drainage ditch situation, making sure this is where God wants us to put down roots, doing the due diligence that any responsible decision like this requires.

Last week we looked at Nehemiah's prayer in Nehemiah chapter 1, and as a group we committed to holding this decision in prayer as we work through it. Prayers for wisdom in how we handle things. Wisdom in the process. Wisdom in knowing whether to move forward at all.

---

## Two Years Ago

As I was writing this week, I kept coming back to Ephesians 3:20 —

> *Now to him who is able to do far more abundantly than all that we ask or think, according to the power at work within us.* — Ephesians 3:20 (ESV)

Two years ago, we weren't even thinking about a building. We had a few kids here and there. Now we've had as many as twenty children in a single night.

Which brings me to something Jesus said in Matthew 9:37 —

> *The harvest is plentiful, but the laborers are few.* — Matthew 9:37 (ESV)

Walk outside the doors of this church and talk to ten people. Chances are good that all ten of them have never asked Jesus into their heart. Never made him Lord of their life. Never been born again. These are people who are lost — dying — and bound for hell without a relationship with Jesus Christ.

That's not a small thing. That's not a background concern we can keep on the back burner while we handle the building question. It has to stay in the forefront of our minds as we live and move in this community.

So here's what I'm asking. I want you to commit to praying with us for the next sixty-one days — until July 15th:

- That God would guide our decisions about this location and building.
- That God would guide us in establishing ministries like the food pantry, things that meet people where they actually are.
- That the Lord of the harvest would send us workers, because the harvest is plentiful and the workers are few.

If you're willing to commit to praying with us for these things for the next sixty-one days, I want you to come up front right now. Just come up. I'm asking you to pray with us. That's it.

---

*[Pause for response.]*

## Praying Like Nehemiah

Good. Now — in a few minutes I'm going to split you into two groups, and we're going to spend the rest of the night in prayer. But first I want to walk through Nehemiah's prayer briefly so you understand what I'm looking for. Then if you've got questions, ask them before we start.

The first thing you notice is that Nehemiah had put real effort into this prayer. He had been soaking in Scripture, and he prayed Scripture back to God. This wasn't a flip request — the kind of thing people pray when their team is down in the fourth quarter. He planned this. He prepared.

And there's a structure to it.

---

### First: Adoration

He opens: *"O Lord God of heaven, the great and awesome God."* He's describing God — naming the things he loves and admires about him. His greatness. His power. His faithfulness. That's where we start too.

---

### Second: Confession

From adoration he moves to confession. Nehemiah confesses things he and the nation are guilty of — not in vague, comfortable terms, but specifically. He says he has failed. His family has failed. His people have failed. They have not kept God's commands. He owns it personally, and then he names it nationally.

We can do the same thing. Can't think of a national sin to confess? Start with abortion. Our embrace of sexual immorality — fornication, adultery, homosexuality, or the growing hostility toward the gender God gave people at birth. Those are national sins, and they have names.

Get local. We know this area. Prostitution is here. Drug addiction is here. Alcohol. Theft — they arrested a woman at the Citgo last week for fraud. There is no shortage of things that need to be confessed on behalf of this community.

Nehemiah prayed like this for days. We'll spend a few minutes.

---

### Third: Thanksgiving

From confession we move to thanksgiving. Straightforward — being genuinely thankful for specific things God has done.

Kristina, if you don't mind me using you for a moment — we've been celebrating the fact that you're clean and sober. *[How long has it been now?]* I think that deserves some applause. Praise God for what he's doing in her life. That is worth being thankful for.

But there are a million things. We as Americans have more than we realize. The poorest among us — people living on government assistance — are still among the wealthiest people on earth by global standards. Let that sit for a second. If you live in a place where the poor quietly starve to death under an overpass, $800 a month is a fortune. We've been given an enormous amount. Most of it we take for granted.

I can tell you I'm personally thankful that God is at work in the life of my sister's boyfriend right now. He's being drawn closer to the Lord — in ways and through things no one expected. He doesn't even see it happening yet. But God is always at work. And sometimes, praise God, we get to watch.

That's the spirit. Thank him for what he's done. For what he's doing. For specific things in your own life and family.

---

### Fourth: Requests

Finally, bring your requests. This is where Nehemiah prays something remarkable — he quotes Scripture *back* to God. He prays the promise God had already made:

> *"but if you return to me and keep my commandments and do them, though your outcasts are in the uttermost parts of heaven, from there I will gather them and bring them to the place that I have chosen, to make my name dwell there."* — Nehemiah 1:9 (ESV)

He holds God to his own Word. And then at the end, he makes his specific request: that God would give him favor with the king of Persia.

So this is where we bring our specific requests. I've got a sheet. There are requests in the bulletin. And there are things God has already put on your heart — bring those too.

Pray for guidance on the building. Pray for wisdom in how we use what God has given us — our time, our money, our abilities. Pray for more workers for this harvest.

And then pray for whatever else God puts on your heart. Pray for our nation. Pray for our leaders. Pray for Kenny Duffield, who is grieving the loss of his wife. Pray for David and the SSI situation he's working through. Take all the time you need. We're not worried about the clock tonight. If we end early, we end early. If we run late, we run late.

---

## Group Instructions

*[Split into two groups. Need a secretary for each group.]*

Here's what I want. Start with worship — just tell God what you love about him. Praise him for his attributes, for his faithfulness, for the specific ways you've seen him move. A professor I had in college used to say that any time he was running late, his students should start listing the things they love about God. It was never graded. It was always the best use of the hour.

When everyone has had a chance to pray those things, move into confession. Keep it as specific or as general as the Spirit leads — but don't rush past it.

Then thanksgiving. Be specific. Name things.

Then requests. The sheets, the bulletin, your own heart. Anything God brings to mind.

Secretaries — you with me?

Alright. Let's pray.

---

*[Read Nehemiah 1:4–11 aloud before the groups begin.]*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Pastoral Prayer Guide with a Missing Gospel Center — Nehemiah as Method Without Message**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and practically useful prayer-service guide built around Nehemiah 1 that faithfully organizes a congregational prayer meeting while leaving Christ, the cross, and the gospel largely unpreached.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: Conditional Pass.**
The sermon's main point — that the congregation should pray over this decision using the structure Nehemiah modeled (adoration, confession, thanksgiving, requests) — does arise from Nehemiah 1. The text's own dominant note is structured, Scripture-soaked, intercession born from grief over sin and confidence in God's covenant promises. That theme is present here.

However, the sermon's *functional* main point is the building decision, and Nehemiah is the instructional scaffold for that decision, not the controlling subject. The text is serving the occasion rather than governing it. This is a close call — not a gate failure, but a flag.

**Authority Test: Pass.**
The preacher submits to the text's structure and quotes it fairly. No softening or editorial reframing is detected. The uncomfortable parts (Nehemiah's detailed national confession) are not avoided — they are extended into a pointed list of contemporary sins.

**Ruling: Conditional Pass. Proceed to scoring with noted caveat.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Fatal Flaw #1: No clear gospel presentation.**
Christ's finished work is not proclaimed in this manuscript. His name appears in the evangelistic aside ("never asked Jesus into their heart... never been born again... bound for hell") but the mechanism of salvation — the cross, justification, penal substitution, faith — is entirely absent. The gospel is referenced in passing as background music, never as the melody. Score capped at 69.

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Fatal Flaw #3: Christ is absent or unnecessary.**
Remove every reference to Jesus from this manuscript and the prayer meeting still runs. Nehemiah's prayer structure (adoration, confession, thanksgiving, requests) does not require Christ in this presentation. A Jewish or Muslim prayer service could follow this guide without alteration. Christ is decorative, not load-bearing.

**Note: Fatal Flaw #4 (generic application) is borderline but not fully triggered.** The application is specific to this congregation's situation — the building, the 61 days, the local sins, the specific prayer requests. That specificity partially satisfies the category. However, the application lacks differentiation by hearer type.

**Score cap: 69 maximum.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 16/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10):* 7/10. The four-part prayer structure (adoration, confession, thanksgiving, requests) is drawn faithfully from Nehemiah 1:4–11. The specific inclusion of Nehemiah praying Scripture back to God (v.9) is well-handled and genuinely instructive. However, the text's larger redemptive context — the covenant promise being invoked, the exile and return pattern, Nehemiah's role as a type of intercessory mediator — goes unexplored. The sermon uses the text correctly without inhabiting it deeply.
- *Contextual Awareness (5):* 3/5. Some awareness of context (Nehemiah in Persia, the king's favor as the request), but the broader book structure, the Ezra-Nehemiah canonical unit, and the post-exilic redemptive context are ignored.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5):* 3/5. The structural observations are clear. No Greek or Hebrew work (not expected in a prayer guide). The meaning is accessible. But the Fallen Condition Focus is only partially named: the congregation is in a situation requiring wisdom, but the deeper FCF — that we are prone to act without God, to build without prayer, to treat decisions as management problems rather than covenant moments — is not named.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5):* 3/5. No eisegesis. But the text is functionally instrumentalized: the congregation's building situation governs the sermon, and Nehemiah's prayer pattern is imported to serve that situation rather than letting the text's own burden (grief over exile, zeal for God's dwelling, covenant intercession) govern the message.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 10/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7):* 3/7. Nehemiah 1 does have genuine Christological resonance — the intercessor who identifies with his people's sin and pleads their cause before the throne on the basis of covenant promise is a rich type. None of this is present. The sermon applies Nehemiah's method without connecting it to Christ as the great intercessor, without noting that Christians approach the throne through him, without anchoring the prayer meeting in the new covenant reality. The Christ-references in the sermon are evangelistic asides, not theological anchors.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3):* 1/3. The post-exilic context is noted lightly but not opened. The redemptive-historical movement (exile → return → temple → greater temple in Christ) is untouched.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4):* 3/4. What doctrine is stated is stated correctly. The evangelistic section ("bound for hell without a relationship with Jesus") is orthodox in its claim. No errors present, but doctrinal engagement is minimal.
- *Confessional Alignment (4):* 3/4. No confessional errors. The emphasis on Scripture-prayer, covenant-promise-pleading, and national confession all align well. The absence of any covenant framework is a missed opportunity, not a violation.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Concrete pastoral specificity.** The named individuals, the specific local sins, the $800 comparison, the Kristina moment — this sermon is in the room with real people. This is not generic churchmanship. It is pastoral care rendered in public.
2. **Clear, actionable structure.** The ACTS framework is well-handled and genuinely serves the congregation. A first-time prayer-meeting participant could follow this guide and pray meaningfully. That is a real achievement.
3. **The response call is specific and proportionate.** "Come forward if you're willing to pray for 61 days" is a genuine exhortation with a concrete shape — not vague emotionalism, not high-pressure manipulation. It matches the sermon's burden.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Christ is absent as the ground of prayer.** The entire section on "requests" includes Nehemiah pleading God's covenant promise (v.9) — but the sermon never asks: on what basis does *this* congregation approach the throne? We are not post-exilic Israelites. We approach through a mediator. His name and work are the gospel-hinge that makes Christian prayer different from any other religion's prayer — and it is missing entirely.
2. **The gospel is asserted but not proclaimed.** The section on the harvest and lostness ("bound for hell without a relationship with Jesus") is directionally correct but evangelistically incomplete. An unbeliever in the room hears that they are lost but not how to be found. The cross does not appear. Justification is not named. Faith is not defined. This is an evangelistic aside that does not evangelize.
3. **The confession section names national and local sins but does not press personal conviction.** Abortion, fornication, prostitution, and drug addiction are named — and naming them is right. But the preacher moves through them quickly as a list and then invites the congregation to continue in the groups. There is no moment where the individual hearer is cornered: *you have participated in this culture; you have been silent; you have looked away.* The hammer is waved, not swung.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that Nehemiah's prayer is not primarily a *method* — it is a *theology of intercession*. Nehemiah approaches God on the basis of covenant identity ("thy servants... thy people, whom thou hast redeemed by thy great power," v.10). The prayer is structured not merely by ACTS but by covenantal logic: God made promises; God's people failed; God's promises still stand; therefore ask. That logic is the backbone of the prayer — and it points directly to Christ as the one in whom all covenant promises are Yes and Amen (2 Cor. 1:20). The method was taught; the theology was bypassed.

There is also a blind spot around the prayer meeting format creating a false sense of gospel activity. The congregation will feel they have prayed well, and they will have. But the text's own urgency — that a holy God's purposes for his people cannot be frustrated, that the God who gathered Israel from exile will gather his own — is the *fuel* for that prayer, not the building's drainage ditch. The sermon situates the prayer meeting in the building decision rather than in the sovereign purposes of the God of covenant.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The evangelistic aside ("walk outside... bound for hell") feels like gospel preaching because it names lostness with seriousness. But it is not gospel preaching — it is a diagnostic with no prescription. The urgency of the harvest language (Matthew 9:37) reads as passion, but it creates urgency without providing the gospel content that makes urgency answerable. The congregation is told the fields are white but not what the workers are supposed to say when they get there.

Similarly, the confession section feels like it has prophetic edge because specific sins are named. But the movement from named sin directly to thanksgiving and requests — without Christ as the forgiver of those sins, without the cross as the ground of confidence in God's mercy — means that the conviction created by the confession section is left unresolved. Law without gospel is not edgy faithfulness; it is an incomplete and potentially harmful act.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Nehemiah 1:10 is the most theologically charged verse in the passage: *"They are your servants and your people, whom you have redeemed by your great power and by your strong hand."* Nehemiah grounds his entire prayer in the redemption God has already accomplished. This is the link between Nehemiah's intercession and Christian prayer — we too are a redeemed people, redeemed not by an exodus from Egypt but by the blood of the Lamb. That verse, opened and applied, could have grounded the entire prayer meeting in the gospel. It was quoted as part of the larger block (v.9 is quoted), and verse 10 sits immediately after — but it was not preached. The sermon stopped at the method (v.9 as Scripture-prayer) and never reached the foundation (v.10 as gospel-grounded boldness).

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Add a Christ-and-intercession paragraph between the "Requests" section and the group instructions.** Something like: "Nehemiah could pray this boldly because he knew who he was — a servant of a redeeming God. We come to the throne with even greater boldness, because we come through a greater mediator. Jesus Christ has prayed for you. He still does. His blood is the basis of every request you will make tonight." Two sentences. The prayer meeting changes entirely.

2. **In the confession section, add personal application before the list.** Before naming national and local sins, pause to address the hearer: "Some of you in this room have participated in what you're confessing. Some of you have been silent when you should have spoken. Some of you have been numb to it for so long that the list I'm about to read no longer shocks you. Confess that numbness first." This turns the confession section from a catalogue into a confrontation.

3. **Replace or supplement "ask Jesus into your heart" with one sentence of gospel content.** In the harvest/lostness section, add: "They need to hear that Christ died for sinners, that God raised him from the dead, and that everyone who repents and trusts him will be forgiven and declared righteous before God. That is the message the workers carry." This does not lengthen the sermon; it makes the evangelistic urgency actionable.

4. **Anchor the response call in the gospel, not the building.** The current call to come forward is: "commit to praying about the building and ministries for 61 days." That is a valid call. But it could be extended: "and pray that God would give us the gospel clarity to tell this community what Nehemiah knew — that we are a redeemed people, and there is redemption for them too."

5. **Open Nehemiah 1:10 explicitly.** Even one sentence: "Notice what Nehemiah says in verse 10 — he calls Israel 'your people, whom you have redeemed.' His confidence is not in his own persistence. It is in what God has already done. That is the ground of every prayer we will pray tonight."

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This is a prayer-service guide, not a conventional expository sermon, and the rubric bends in several places:

- **Sermonic force** and **illustration** categories are naturally lighter in a prayer-service context, and the low scores here should be understood accordingly.
- **Structure** scores well precisely because the text's four-part structure *is* the sermon's structure — this is an appropriate and skillful use of the form.
- **Gospel centrality** is not reduced in weight by the format: a prayer meeting guide that does not anchor prayer in the gospel of Jesus Christ has a structural problem regardless of genre.
- The rubric's emphasis on *equipping the church for witness* is especially relevant here — the evangelistic urgency of the sermon is real, but a prayer meeting that prays for workers without equipping those workers with the gospel is praying for something the congregation isn't yet ready to do.

The rubric is reasonably well-fitted to this document, with the understanding that the "sermonic force" and "illustration" categories measure a narrower target in this format. The gospel centrality and Christ-centeredness deficits are genre-independent — they are as serious in a prayer-service guide as in an expository sermon.
