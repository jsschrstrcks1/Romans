# City in Ruins
### Nehemiah 2:9-19

---

## Opening Illustration — The Flaming Mountain

I read a story this week about something happening out in the Midwest. A farmer has 16,000 cattle, and each one produces about nine pounds of manure per day. Naturally, 144,000 pounds of manure a day can't just be left on the ground. Within a few days the entire pasture would be buried up to the tops of the fence posts.

So the farmer bought a tractor with a big bucket and scoop, and he had his men go out and gather the manure up into a pile. Before long he had a pile ninety feet long, fifty feet wide, and fifty feet tall. When he saw how big it was, he had his men start a new pile. Before long there were a number of these enormous piles — he estimated each one contained about 2,000 tons of fertilizer.

And then it rained. Not much. Just enough for mold to begin to grow in the oldest pile. Between the mold and the heat of decomposition, the entire mountain of manure caught fire. Not a roaring, all-consuming fire — a slow, smoky smolder.

The little town downwind was not happy. A restaurant owner called the fire department because people had stopped coming in — nobody could eat through the smell of flaming poop mountain. The fire department went out to the farmer's place, explained that the town was unhappy, and asked if they could help put it out.

The farmer agreed. But he explained that it was going to be harder than they were expecting — the burning pile was only a half mile from the river the town draws their water from. If they used water to put out the fire, the town would face something worse than smoke.

So the fire department called in the forestry service. The forestry service has bulldozers and big tractors with buckets — the plan was to spread the manure out, disrupt the decomposition process, and hopefully kill the fire that way. The farmer agreed. The forestry service showed up.

When they began spreading the manure, giving the fire more oxygen made it burn hotter. And as the tractors moved the pile, the contents of the buckets began to catch fire. Everywhere they moved it, the fire spread — and ultimately caught a second mountain of manure on fire. A second mountain also too close to the river to put out with water.

True story. As far as I know, it's been burning since February. The farmer, the fire department, and the forestry service have not been able to extinguish the blaze. The only thing they've managed to do is make the fire worse — and double the amount of smoke pouring over that little town.

I don't normally tell stories about manure. But this one fits. You'll see why in a moment.

Open with me to Nehemiah chapter 2, beginning at verse 9.

## City in Ruins

So Jerusalem was a city in ruins.

It was a decent-sized city — it took Nehemiah somewhere between one night's survey and three days in the city to size the whole thing up. And you know, there's a difference between reading about something and seeing it for yourself.

Everyone remembers 9/11. I had a chance to go to New York in 2004. While we were there I was able to see Ground Zero for myself. I had never seen the Twin Towers standing in person. I remember that day vividly — everyone around us was either weeping or trying to sell you something. Little mementos, light-up replicas, paintings of the skyline without the towers, all kinds of things.

I bought one that kind of summed up what I figured I'd remember most. It's a small print, up close on the giant hole in the ground left after the towers fell — with a single thing left standing tall. A near-perfect cross that had formed in the heat and chaos of that morning, set on a pillar of concrete, with a flag right behind it. I had read almost everything I could get my hands on about what things were like near Ground Zero in the two years after 9/11. It was one thing to have read about it, heard about it. It was something else entirely to see it for myself. I remember weeping over the loss of life and the sheer scale of the damage.

In the end, after all the carnage, two things remained — our flag, almost hidden behind the cross.

I think it was a similar moment for Nehemiah. Up to this point he had only heard about things from his brother Hanani. Now he sees it for the first time. It had to have been an emotional moment — to see his homeland in tatters.

The text says he went in secret, and that no one knew where he was or what he was doing. I think there are a couple of reasons for that. First, he wanted time to take in the scene without an audience. If he were brought to tears, he didn't want the men he'd come to lead and motivate to see him fall apart. Second, I think he wanted to see it firsthand — to compare the report with reality, to know the actual scope of the task before he ever opened his mouth to the remnant living in the city.

There's a difference between reading a list of destroyed gates and standing in front of them yourself. Seeing what might be salvaged. Seeing what was forever gone. Only then can you honestly speak about the work to the men you came to lead.

Which brings me back to my story. Verse 13 mentions the Dung Gate — the gate where refuse was removed from the city. And the text tells us that the gates of Jerusalem had been destroyed by fire.

Turns out, manure can burn for a long time.

## Life in Ruins

Not all that different from our lives without Christ, is it?

Apart from Christ — what were we like?

**Pride.** An absolute confidence in our own sufficiency. No room for God. In the moment you decide to follow Christ, you have to set that aside. You're saying, in essence: *I am not enough on my own.* Ephesians 2:8-9. Not by works. Not by will. By grace, through faith.

**Greed.** An intense desire to accumulate — wealth, power, comfort, control. What's at the center of greed? *I* want. *I* think I need. *I* deserve. *I* feel my judgment is what matters. In accepting Christ you set that aside and submit to him. You accept that his desires are superior to yours. That he should receive the power, the glory, the first fruits of your wealth and harvest — tithe and offering.

**Lust.** God has a created order for things. Marriage first. Then sex. Lust, outside that covenant — or any desire that overrides your desire to serve God — leads somewhere bad. It leads to fornication. To adultery. And here is what people miss: love and lust cannot both occupy your heart at the same time. When you have sex outside of marriage it's not just you sinning. You're facilitating someone else's sin. If you genuinely loved that person, you would not help them sin.

I could keep going. But let's be direct. How do you think God sees someone so wrapped up in their own pride they have no room for him? Someone consumed with their own power, their own wealth, their own comfort? Someone who not only commits sins against their own body but pulls others down with them?

Turn with me to 1 John 2:15-17.

> *Do not love the world or the things in the world. If anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him. For all that is in the world — the desires of the flesh and the desires of the eyes and pride of life — is not from the Father but is from the world. And the world is passing away along with its desires, but whoever does the will of God abides forever.*

People who love this world — who love greed, who are consumed with themselves, who lust after the flesh or the eye or the pride of life. What does this passage say? The Father is not in them. Such a person is not of the Father but of the world. And the world is passing away. Pretty stark.

A professor once told me that sinners stink to God. I think Scripture backs that up. There are a number of passages that speak of offerings and sacrifices and right living as a *fragrant offering* to the Lord. If good things smell sweet to him, then corrupt living must smell like something else.

In almost every way, then, the best we can do apart from Christ is be like that flaming mountain of manure out in the Midwest. Stinking things up for miles in every direction.

Let that settle in for a moment. Let that sink down.

Without Christ, the best I can offer the Lord is a stinking, smoldering mountain of poo.

## Life Restored by God

Ultimately, Jerusalem was restored. Nehemiah led the people to rebuild. And those who believe in Christ are restored by God.

It struck me that the order of what happens in Nehemiah maps to something important theologically. The term is *Ordo Salutis* — a Latin phrase meaning "the order of salvation." Have you ever wondered what actually happens inside a person as they move from unbelief to faith? Most people haven't thought about it. But Scripture says there is a sequence.

One of the passages I come back to is Romans 8:29-30.

We all know verse 28 — *all things work together for the good.* But have you ever read verse 29?

> *For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son, in order that he might be the firstborn among many brothers. And those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified.*

We won't have time to work through all of that tonight — it's a theme that's likely to come up again as we move through the book. But let's start at the beginning. Foreknowledge.

The ten-dollar theological term is *omniscience*. There are at least four views on how that works, but I'll give you the classical one — the one the early church held, and the one I believe Scripture supports most clearly. God knows everything. Everything that has happened. Everything that will happen. Everything that might happen — in the past, the present, and the future. His knowledge of what might happen allows him to work in the world toward the best possible outcome.

One of the fathers of the church — Augustine of Hippo — wrote the following about 1,600 years ago:

> *"Just as your memory does not force the past to have happened, God's foreknowledge does not force the future to happen. And just as you remember some things that you have done but did not do everything that you remember, God foreknows everything that he causes but does not cause everything that he foreknows... Let us rather confess that nothing in the future is hidden from God's foreknowledge, and that no sin is left unpunished by his justice, for sin is committed by the will, not coerced by God's foreknowledge."*

One of the biggest problems people have is this: they can't wrap their minds around God allowing terrible things to happen. You've heard of the butterfly effect — a butterfly flaps its wings in Africa, and somewhere else a hurricane forms. The same principle extends from now into the future. Maybe that flat tire this morning kept you from causing an accident that would have killed someone who would have cured a disease. Only God can see every thread running forward. He foreknows everything he causes. He does not cause everything he foreknows.

God knows what decisions you'll make. He knew how, without Christ, you would be that stinking, burning mountain. And here is the thing — even before you cared two bits about Christ, he cared enough about you to come to this earth and die on a cross for you.

He knew the wrecked state of your life. Just like Nehemiah knew the wrecked state of Jerusalem's walls. Nehemiah spent time surveying the damage. God has known since before the beginning of time everything each of us would do — right and wrong. Your deepest, darkest regrets. God *knew* about them. And he chose you anyway.

He says it plainly. Romans 5:8 — *while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.*

1 Peter 3:18 — *For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, that he might bring us to God. He was put to death in the flesh but made alive in the spirit.*

Romans 6:23 says what we deserved was death. The wages of the wreckage of our lives was death. But God does not give us what we earned. He gives us what Christ earned. The gift of God is eternal life through Christ Jesus our Lord.

## Source Notes — Verify Before Print Use

**Manure fire story:** Attributed as a true, recent news story from the Midwest. No source URL or news outlet provided. Verify and document before publication or repeat use.

**"Hanani" (2:9-19 context):** Raw draft reads "hannaiah" and "brother in law." Nehemiah 1:2 identifies the source of Nehemiah's report as **Hanani, one of his brothers** — not a brother-in-law. Corrected above.

**Survey timeline:** Raw draft says Nehemiah spent "three days to survey." Nehemiah 2:11 says he was in Jerusalem three days before the survey; the wall survey itself was done in **one night** (2:12-15). Corrected above.

**"Two months" after the disaster:** Raw draft says Nehemiah arrived "about two months" after the city's destruction was reported. Nehemiah 1:1 places Hanani's report in the month of **Kislev** (month 9 of the Jewish calendar). Nehemiah 2:1 places his departure in **Nisan** (month 1 of the following year) — approximately **four months** later. Flagged above with neutral language; verify and correct in final draft.

**Augustine quote:** Widely attributed and theologically consistent with Augustine's thought, but the exact work and chapter need confirmation before pulpit attribution. Possible sources: *On Free Will* (*De Libero Arbitrio*), *City of God* Book V, or his letters on divine foreknowledge. Do not use attributed in print until sourced. Consider citing as "Augustine, *On Free Will*" with a specific chapter if confirmed.

**Romans 8:29-30 (ESV):** The ESV reads: *"For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son, in order that he might be the firstborn among many brothers. And those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified."* Raw draft quoted NASB wording ("become conformed," "brethren"). ESV text used above.

**Romans 5:8 (ESV):** ESV reads "while we were **still** sinners." Raw draft used KJV wording ("yet sinners"). ESV wording used above.

**1 Peter 3:18 (ESV):** ESV reads "that he might bring **us** to God" and "put to death in the **flesh**." Raw draft read "bring **you** to God" and "put to death in the **body**." ESV text used above.

**"Fragrant offering" passages:** The professor's claim that sinners "stink to God" is a reasonable inference from the fragrant-offering passages. Key texts: 2 Corinthians 2:15-16 (the aroma of Christ); Ephesians 5:2 (a fragrant offering and sacrifice); Philippians 4:18 (a fragrant offering, an acceptable sacrifice). Worth having one of these in view if you land on this image again.

**"Ordo Solutis"** in raw draft is a misspelling. Correct Latin: **Ordo Salutis**. Corrected throughout.
