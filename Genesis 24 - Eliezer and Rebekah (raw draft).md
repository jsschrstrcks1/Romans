## Verification Flags

### Confirmed Errors — Must Correct Before Preaching

- ❌ **"Rachel" used throughout**: Wrong name. The woman in Genesis 24 is **Rebekah** (Hebrew: רִבְקָה, *Rivkah*), not Rachel. Rebekah is the daughter of Bethuel, granddaughter of Nahor (Abraham's brother). This is stated plainly in Genesis 24:15: *"Rebekah came out, who was born to Bethuel, son of Milcah, the wife of Nahor, Abraham's brother."* She becomes **Isaac's** wife. Rachel is an entirely different woman — she is Laban's daughter (Rebekah's niece), and she becomes **Jacob's** wife in Genesis 29. Both stories involve a well meeting, which is almost certainly the source of the confusion. Every instance of "Rachel" in this draft must be changed to **Rebekah** before preaching. This includes "vs57 is the first time anyone asks Rachel's opinion" — the passage is accurate (Gen 24:57–58 does ask Rebekah's opinion), but the name must be corrected.

- ❌ **The *betulah* / "belly dancing / palace courtiers / abdominal exercise" claim**: This claim has no basis in Hebrew lexicography, archaeology, or scholarship of any kind and must be removed entirely. The Hebrew word in Genesis 24:16 is **בְּתוּלָה** (*betulah*, Strong's H1330). Every major lexicon — Strong's, Brown-Driver-Briggs, HALOT — defines it as "virgin," from a root meaning "to separate" (a woman separated from men). The word appears 51 times in the Hebrew Bible and is consistently used to mean a sexually inexperienced young woman. There is zero evidence — in any ancient Near Eastern text, any lexicon, any commentary, any archaeological record — for the claim that *betulah* refers to palace courtiers, an exercise regimen, abdominal strengthening, or belly dancing. This is not a case of nuance or scholarly debate; it is a claim with no traceable source in any credible reference work. Do not use it. The verse itself confirms her virginity plainly: Genesis 24:16 (ESV) reads *"The young woman was very attractive in appearance, a virgin, whom no man had known."* The repetition of "a virgin, whom no man had known" is rhetorical emphasis — the text is making itself clear. The sermon's theological point (that Abraham was looking for a woman of character, not just status) can be made from the text itself without any appeal to an unverifiable linguistic claim.

- ❌ **"Eliazar" — misspelling**: The traditional name for this servant (drawn from Genesis 15:2) is **Eliezer** (Hebrew: אֱלִיעֶזֶר, *ʾĔliʿezer*), meaning "God is my help." Genesis 24 never names him — he is referred to throughout as "the servant" or "the oldest servant of his household." The identification with Eliezer of Genesis 15:2 is traditional, not explicit, and is worth noting as such. Correct the spelling throughout.

- ❌ **"Nearly two centuries" of service**: Mathematically impossible. Abraham's total lifespan was **175 years** (Genesis 25:7) — he never approached two centuries himself. Eliezer is first named in Genesis 15:2, when Abraham was approximately 85 years old. The mission of Genesis 24 occurs when Abraham was approximately 140 (Isaac was born when Abraham was 100; Isaac was 40 at his marriage per Genesis 25:20). That gives Eliezer a documented window of service of roughly **55–90 years** — significant, and worth noting, but less than half of "nearly two centuries." Correct to something like: "this servant had watched God move in Abraham's life for decades — perhaps as long as fifty or sixty years."

- ❌ **Camel carrying capacity — "right around 1,000 pounds for 8 hours a day"**: Overstated for a working animal on a sustained journey. The 1,000 lb figure is the documented *maximum* for a Bactrian (two-humped) camel under exceptional conditions — not a standard working load. The dromedary camel (one hump, the type in the biblical Near East) carries significantly less. For sustained multi-day travel, the typical working load is **300–600 lbs**. The sermon's illustration of the treasure's weight and value still works at the lower figure — 10 camels × 300–600 lbs of choice goods is still an extraordinary cargo. Simply use "hundreds of pounds per camel" rather than claiming a specific figure that is inaccurate for the context.

- ❌ **Cultural taboo requiring water to be poured out after a stranger drinks**: No evidence for this custom exists in any ancient Near Eastern source, archaeological record, or commentary tradition. The text of Genesis 24:20 is plain: *"So she quickly emptied her pitcher into the trough and ran back to the well to draw water, and she drew for all his camels."* She emptied the pitcher into the trough as the first step of drawing water for the camels — a purely practical action described by the text itself. No cultural prohibition is stated, implied, or documented. This interpretive gloss should be removed; the text's own explanation is sufficient and more compelling than an unverifiable custom.

### Needs Clarification Before Preaching

- ⚠️ **Camel water consumption — "about 20 gallons in a sitting"**: Plausible but on the low end. Most sources document the range as 20–30+ gallons, with a dehydrated camel potentially drinking up to 53 gallons in a single session. The sermon's math (10 camels × 20 gallons = ~200 gallons) is therefore a conservative estimate. The illustration of Rebekah's enormous labor still holds — even at 20 gallons it is ~200 gallons of water drawn a few gallons at a time. The figure is usable but "up to 30 gallons or more" would be more accurate.

- ⚠️ **Purchase price of Machpelah field — "something like 30 times the normal cost"**: The price paid was 400 shekels of silver (Genesis 23:15–16). The claim that this was approximately 30 times market rate is cited in some commentaries (notably Keil & Delitzsch and some comparative land-price studies based on ancient Near Eastern records) but is not universally agreed upon and the preacher hedges it appropriately with "something like." If used, this should be attributed to commentary tradition rather than stated as established fact.

- ⚠️ **"Most scholars believe the servant was with Abraham from the outset of his journey"**: This is a traditional reading but is not a consensus of modern scholarship — it is an inference based on Genesis 15:2 and the servant's familiarity with the route to Haran. It is a reasonable interpretive tradition, but "most scholars believe" overstates the scholarly consensus. Consider: "tradition holds that this may have been Eliezer, Abraham's steward from decades earlier."

### Substantially Accurate

- ✅ **Rebekah (the woman in Gen 24) is from Haran / Sarah's family**: Confirmed. Genesis 24:10 names the city of Nahor; Genesis 24:15 confirms her family connection to Abraham through Nahor.
- ✅ **Ten camels loaded with goods (Genesis 24:10)**: Confirmed. *"Then the servant took ten of his master's camels and departed, taking all sorts of choice gifts from his master."*
- ✅ **Vs 57–58 — first time Rebekah's opinion is sought**: Accurate. Genesis 24:57–58 (ESV): *"They said, 'Let us call the young woman and ask her.' And they called Rebekah and said to her, 'Will you go with this man?' She said, 'I will go.'"* This is the first direct address to her for her own decision. The observation is valid — just requires the name correction to Rebekah.
- ✅ **Ten camels × 20 gallons = ~200 gallons total**: Math is correct on the conservative figure; actual total may be higher.
- ✅ **1 Corinthians 11:23–26 ESV**: Confirmed accurate. The quoted portion matches ESV exactly. Note: the sermon begins the quotation mid-verse (omitting the opening "For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you"). The quoted portion itself is correct. ESV reads *"This is my body, which is for you"* — not "which is broken for you" (that is KJV/NKJV); the ESV rendering is the correct scholarly translation from the oldest Greek manuscripts.
- ✅ **Romans 3:23**: Previously verified — *"for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."*
- ✅ **Servant motivated by fear of God / "Lord God of my master" language**: Legitimate textual observation — the language "the LORD, the God of my master Abraham" (Genesis 24:12, 27, 42, 48) is noted by commentators as potentially reflecting early faith or respectful deference. The sermon's reading is defensible.
- ✅ **Servant could have absconded with the treasure**: Valid homiletical observation; nothing in the text or historical record contradicts it.
- ✅ **God answers prayer even for those not yet fully believers**: Theologically defensible point drawn from the text; the servant prays and God responds. The observation is consistent with the narrative and with broader biblical themes (cf. Cornelius in Acts 10).
- ✅ **Oath "under the thigh" = verbal contract equivalent**: Correct. Genesis 24:2 records this ancient near eastern oath custom. The preacher wisely chose not to elaborate — that's the right call.

First of all we are jumping ahead a chapter and going straight to gen 24. If you've brought your bible, and I hope you have, go ahead and turn there. While you're turning, I'll catch you up to speed.

After the the events that took place last week, apparently Abrahams wife left him, and is at the very least apart from him when she dies. Whether she was just on a trip, or had left him because they weren't getting along we don't know. In a patriarchical society, I don't think she would have gone on vacation without him, nor was it normal for a woman to leave her husband. But for whatever reason they are separated when she dies. Abraham negotiates for a piece of land – a field with a cave, and purchases it for something like 30 times the normal cost of land in the area. He agrees to pay it. Burys his wife, and that's where we pick back up in chapter 24.
There are two people were going to talk about today. First is the servant, and secondly were going to talk about Rachel. The servant apparently isn't at the beginning of the story a believer in God. Notice how he refers to God as "Lord God of my Master". It could be a turn of phrase to show deference. So as to not sound as though He'd forgotten his place. Posturing was important back then. If you were a servant, you ought not act above your station. So it could be that. But I think he starts out not neccesarily beliving in God. He's basically being asked to go find a wife for his masters son. Most scholars believe that this man was with Abraham at the outset of his journey, and so would have been – perhaps one of the few that knew the way back to where Sarahs family lived.

Abraham loaded him down with treasures. I was curious about how much the servant was bringing with him. We know Abraham was quite wealthy to start with and became exponentially wealthier along his journey. But I was curious to know just how much a typical camel could carry. Turns out its an exceptional amount. A camel can carry right around 1000 pounds for 8hrs a day. Can you imagine unloading 10 camels at a thousand pounds of goods per camel every night before you could have dinner and go to bed?
Further, consider the value of that amount of "choice goods"vs 10. This servant could have easily run off with this tremendous treasure. We know some of it was gold jewelry – as he presents some to Rachel in vs22. He could have taken the money, and run. Gone and been the master of an entourage like Abrahams. 10,000 pounds of gold, and probably bolts of clothing, and everything else that was desirable to the people of the day. I mean this would be like offering her family a Maserati a 5000 sq foot home on the water, and a new Harley for each family member for the bride price. Converted to dollars today, the man was carrying around 200 million dollars in treasures. He could have gone and done anything he wanted for the rest of his life. Ancient police weren't anything like modern police, he probably would have never been caught by the law.

But he was motivated I think, by a fear of God. This man would have seen God open Sarahs womb, would have seen God bless his master time and time again, even when Abrham fumbled and messed up as he is prone to doing, God still blessed his socks off. Abraham seems to be pretty open with his struggles. We talked a few weeks ago about how Powerful God is, and some of the ways in which he acted in Abrahams life. All of these things this servant would have been a first hand witness to.

Would that have been great? I love to see God move in answer to prayers. We've really seen him move here too haven't we. My mom – healed of arthritis. Bob and sherries son healed of leukemia. Brenda's Job. Kurt, man you're a walking miracle. To think where you've been and where you are now. You guys have heard my stories. Money coming in, car getting better gas mileage than it ever had before. The Haitian bathroom – if you haven't heard that one, its funny, ask me about it later. You guys have some stories too don't you? Stories of God's provision?

These I'm sure are some of the things that Abrahams servant saw. While he wasn't ready to make Abrahams God his God, he certainly had seen enough to know that our God is real.

So Eliazar needs to find the right girl for his masters son. He starts with prayer. Don't miss this, because so often God is responsive to the prayers of people that don't even believe in him yet. He asks God to send a girl, that meets a few criteria. She has to be pretty, has to be hospitable in offering him a drink. Not only that but she has to offer to water the camels as well. There was a method here to his system. In the culture of the day, it was wrong to let a stranger drink from the same water as your family. So once she lets him drink from her pitcher, she has to pour out the rest of the water. However his camels would have drank a lot more than the 5 or 6 gallons that she was carrying. Indeeed I looked it up. A single camel drinks about 20 gallons in a sitting. Having been walking across a desert area to get to this city, these camels would have been pretty thirsty, so for her to water them all likely meant pulling up to 200 gallons likely a few gallons at a time. Many girls might have offered him a drink, tossed out the balance of the pitcher of water to his camels and moved on, but she went the extra mile to be nice, and to avoid a culturally awkward moment. This meant a lot of extra work for her, but she did it without complaint. The second half of Eliazars prayer was that she be willing to return with him to the promised land, where Abraham was living. Having read the story, of course you know she passed the first test, she offered him water, and she passed the second test by pulling up a couple hundred gallons of water for his camels, but would she pass the third test? Would she be willing to head off into the desert with a strange but wealthy man in a step of faith similar to what Abraham had to take.

I find it interesting (Vs57) is the first time anyone asks Rachels opinion. Up till the he talks to the parents, and the brother even seems aware. Money has changed hands and in fact lots of rich gifts have been given to Rachel, but no one has asked her if she wants to go.

So they do, and she agrees and they take off towards home.

There are a couple Hebrew words that are interesting in this passage. One of which is the word that they translated virgin. That word kept jumping out to me. I kept wondering how they could know that without being cruel to this young girl. So I did some research, and I found out that the Hebrew word used there doesn't directly translate as virgin. Its true that she was, and we can know that because this other word they used is the name of a group of women, palace courtiers. Unmarried virgins that were participating in a unique exercise regimen that prepared them for marriage, and was designed to strengthen her abdominal muscles. They believe back then that strong abdominal muscles led to successful and safe baby deliveries. So when Abraham sent his servant looking for a virgin, the thing he's really looking for is one of these young women who dance at the palace in this particular way that strengthens their abdominal muscles so she can successfully bear him grandkids. The style of dancing is and the word roughly translates to belly dancing. He sent his servant in search of a belly dancer.

The other word, is the word for thigh, but I don't think we will get into that too much. Its enough to say that ancient cultures had strange customs, and this was the ancient equivalent of a verbal contract sealed with a handshake.

But the important thing here is this. There are two people in our story tonight who responded to the movement of God in their lives.

Eliazar the servant saw God moving In the life of Abraham for nearly two centuries. He saw blessings, and provision, and protection. He saw clearly miraculous things, that only could have happened by the direct hand of God. Maybe some of you can identify with that. You know people, you've seen God move in their life, and its piqued your interest. Maybe that you tonight. Maybe youre just here checking out that God thing that other people are doing. If that is you, I challenge you to ask God to make himself real to you this week. To make his presence known in your life this week. If he isn't real you aren't out anything at all. If he is real, and he is, its time for you to begin a relationship with him.

You see everyone has sinned and fallen short of God's standard. Romans 3:23. Gospel presentation
