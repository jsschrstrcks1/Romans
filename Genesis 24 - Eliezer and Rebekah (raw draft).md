### Needs Clarification Before Preaching

- ⚠️ **Camel water consumption — "about 20 gallons in a sitting"**: Plausible but on the low end. Most sources document the range as 20–30+ gallons, with a dehydrated camel potentially drinking up to 53 gallons in a single session. The sermon's math (10 camels × 20 gallons = ~200 gallons) is therefore a conservative estimate. The illustration of Rebekah's enormous labor still holds — even at 20 gallons it is ~200 gallons of water drawn a few gallons at a time. The figure is usable but "up to 30 gallons or more" would be more accurate.

- ⚠️ **Purchase price of Machpelah field — "something like 30 times the normal cost"**: The price paid was 400 shekels of silver (Genesis 23:15–16). The claim that this was approximately 30 times market rate is cited in some commentaries (notably Keil & Delitzsch and some comparative land-price studies based on ancient Near Eastern records) but is not universally agreed upon and the preacher hedges it appropriately with "something like." If used, this should be attributed to commentary tradition rather than stated as established fact.

- ⚠️ **"Most scholars believe the servant was with Abraham from the outset of his journey"**: This is a traditional reading but is not a consensus of modern scholarship — it is an inference based on Genesis 15:2 and the servant's familiarity with the route to Haran. It is a reasonable interpretive tradition, but "most scholars believe" overstates the scholarly consensus. Consider: "tradition holds that this may have been Eliezer, Abraham's steward from decades earlier."

### Substantially Accurate

- ✅ **Rebekah (the woman in Gen 24) is from Haran / Sarah's family**: Confirmed. Genesis 24:10 names the city of Nahor; Genesis 24:15 confirms her family connection to Abraham through Nahor.
- ✅ **Ten camels loaded with goods (Genesis 24:10)**: Confirmed. *"Then the servant took ten of his master's camels and departed, taking all sorts of choice gifts from his master."*
- ✅ **Vs 57–58 — first time Rebekah's opinion is sought**: Accurate. Genesis 24:57–58 (ESV): *"They said, 'Let us call the young woman and ask her.' And they called Rebekah and said to her, 'Will you go with this man?' She said, 'I will go.'"* This is the first direct address to her for her own decision. The observation is valid — just requires the name correction to Rebekah.
- ✅ **Ten camels × 20 gallons = ~200 gallons total**: Math is correct on the conservative figure; actual total may be higher.
- ✅ **1 Corinthians 11:23–26 ESV**: Confirmed accurate. The quoted portion matches ESV exactly. Note: the sermon begins the quotation mid-verse (omitting the opening "For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you"). The quoted portion itself is correct. ESV reads *"This is my body, which is for you"* — not "which is broken for you" (that is KJV/NKJV); the ESV rendering is the correct scholarly translation from the oldest Greek manuscripts.
- ✅ **Romans 3:23**: Previously verified — *"for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."*
- ✅ **Servant motivated by fear of God / "Lord God of my master" language**: Legitimate textual observation — the language "the LORD, the God of my master Abraham" (Genesis 24:12, 27, 42, 48) is noted by commentators as potentially reflecting early faith or respectful deference. The sermon's reading is defensible.
- ✅ **Servant could have absconded with the treasure**: Valid homiletical observation; nothing in the text or historical record contradicts it.
- ✅ **God answers prayer even for those not yet fully believers**: Theologically defensible point drawn from the text; the servant prays and God responds. The observation is consistent with the narrative and with broader biblical themes (cf. Cornelius in Acts 10).
- ✅ **Oath "under the thigh" = verbal contract equivalent**: Correct. Genesis 24:2 records this ancient near eastern oath custom. The preacher wisely chose not to elaborate — that's the right call.

First of all we are jumping ahead a chapter and going straight to gen 24. If you've brought your bible, and I hope you have, go ahead and turn there. While you're turning, I'll catch you up to speed.

After the the events that took place last week, apparently Abrahams wife left him, and is at the very least apart from him when she dies. Whether she was just on a trip, or had left him because they weren't getting along we don't know. In a patriarchical society, I don't think she would have gone on vacation without him, nor was it normal for a woman to leave her husband. But for whatever reason they are separated when she dies. Abraham negotiates for a piece of land – a field with a cave, and purchases it for something like 30 times the normal cost of land in the area. He agrees to pay it. Burys his wife, and that's where we pick back up in chapter 24.
There are two people were going to talk about today. First is the servant, and secondly were going to talk about Rebekah. The servant apparently isn't at the beginning of the story a believer in God. Notice how he refers to God as "Lord God of my Master". It could be a turn of phrase to show deference. So as to not sound as though He'd forgotten his place. Posturing was important back then. If you were a servant, you ought not act above your station. So it could be that. But I think he starts out not neccesarily beliving in God. He's basically being asked to go find a wife for his masters son. Most scholars believe that this man was with Abraham at the outset of his journey, and so would have been – perhaps one of the few that knew the way back to where Sarahs family lived.

Abraham loaded him down with treasures. I was curious about how much the servant was bringing with him. We know Abraham was quite wealthy to start with and became exponentially wealthier along his journey. But I was curious to know just how much a typical camel could carry. Turns out its an exceptional amount. A camel on a sustained working journey carries several hundred pounds of cargo — conservatively 300 to 600 pounds per animal. **[❌ NOTE: Corrected from "1,000 pounds" — that is the documented maximum under exceptional conditions, not a standard working load for a dromedary (one-humped) camel on a multi-day desert journey.]** Can you imagine unloading 10 camels at a thousand pounds of goods per camel every night before you could have dinner and go to bed?
Further, consider the value of that amount of "choice goods"vs 10. This servant could have easily run off with this tremendous treasure. We know some of it was gold jewelry – as he presents some to Rebekah in vs22. He could have taken the money, and run. Gone and been the master of an entourage like Abrahams. 10,000 pounds of gold, and probably bolts of clothing, and everything else that was desirable to the people of the day. I mean this would be like offering her family a Maserati a 5000 sq foot home on the water, and a new Harley for each family member for the bride price. Converted to dollars today, the man was carrying around 200 million dollars in treasures. He could have gone and done anything he wanted for the rest of his life. Ancient police weren't anything like modern police, he probably would have never been caught by the law.

But he was motivated I think, by a fear of God. This man would have seen God open Sarahs womb, would have seen God bless his master time and time again, even when Abrham fumbled and messed up as he is prone to doing, God still blessed his socks off. Abraham seems to be pretty open with his struggles. We talked a few weeks ago about how Powerful God is, and some of the ways in which he acted in Abrahams life. All of these things this servant would have been a first hand witness to.

Would that have been great? I love to see God move in answer to prayers. We've really seen him move here too haven't we. My mom – healed of arthritis. Bob and sherries son healed of leukemia. Brenda's Job. Kurt, man you're a walking miracle. To think where you've been and where you are now. You guys have heard my stories. Money coming in, car getting better gas mileage than it ever had before. The Haitian bathroom – if you haven't heard that one, its funny, ask me about it later. You guys have some stories too don't you? Stories of God's provision?

These I'm sure are some of the things that Abrahams servant saw. While he wasn't ready to make Abrahams God his God, he certainly had seen enough to know that our God is real.

So Eliezer needs to find the right girl for his masters son. He starts with prayer. Don't miss this, because so often God is responsive to the prayers of people that don't even believe in him yet. He asks God to send a girl, that meets a few criteria. She has to be pretty, has to be hospitable in offering him a drink. Not only that but she has to offer to water the camels as well. There was a method here to his system. **[❌ REMOVED: The "cultural taboo requiring water to be poured out after a stranger drinks" claim has no basis in any ancient Near Eastern source or commentary. Having been walking across a desert area to get to this city, these camels would have been pretty thirsty, so for her to water them all likely meant pulling up to 200 gallons likely a few gallons at a time.
