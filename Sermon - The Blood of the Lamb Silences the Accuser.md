## Opening: Dumb Laws

In my reading this week I came across a website that lists dumb laws from around the world. Some of these laws are real stinkers. For instance it's against the law to flush your toilet after 10pm in Switzerland. Also in Switzerland if you leave your keys in the ignition of your vehicle, and the door of your car open, you can be arrested and taken to jail. In South Korea, if you are a police officer and you are bribed, and you accept the bribe, it's against the law not to report yourself to the police station. In the UK it's perfectly legal to shoot a Scotsman with a bow and arrow Monday through Saturday, but on Sunday you'll go to prison. Just in case you were considering it, you may not hang your bed out your window on Sundays in the UK. Closer to home in Canada, businesses must provide rails for tying up horses; and if you scare a person, and they die — you're automatically guilty of murder. In California it's against the law to walk your elephant without a leash on Thursdays.

## Subject to Authorities

Our passage tonight says we are to be subject to authorities. That's easy when you have a leader you like. Like now — my guess is most of you voted for Trump, most of you are happy he's in office. Most are happy with his actions. I'm really glad he repealed government funding for abortions. I think that's a vile and immoral practice that we as a nation should be ashamed of. Eradication of abortion would be ideal, but short of that we have to push for and be happy with these small victories. But it's easy when we have a president we like. It's much more difficult when the leader seems to be attacking our way of life, attacking the church, attacking Scripture. It's much more difficult to follow the rules of an evil, arbitrary or capricious person. It's easy to follow the leader when he asks you to do something easy. When he starts getting arbitrary it's a lot more difficult. In Argentina for instance, the last number in your license plate determines the days you're allowed to drive. I can hear it now — "Sorry boss, just got my new license plate, can't work on Mondays and Fridays anymore!"

But we know this, don't we? We have had 8 long years under a man who through his capriciousness or mal-intent has pulled our country hard away from the Lord. We as a people seem to care less for holiness, less for helping the needy around us, less about refugees from war-torn countries, less about God in general, and in particular increased apathy and vitriol towards Christ. They want references to Christianity removed from the public square while Muslim religious services were being held in the White House. I'm sure if asked you guys could come up with a handful of things each that I didn't bring up that demonstrate the same thing.

### The Real Problem

Under the last administration we have been at best adrift, and at worst being pushed away from Christ. Our response as a nation should have been to pull towards Christ. But instead we evangelical believers en masse decided it wasn't more of Jesus that we needed, it was a politician. It wasn't more of Christ that our country needed. We didn't turn to evangelism and disciple more people — instead we get on the internet and fight over who ought to lead our country and why. Is that important? Yup. And I understand this is a little like the man who was laying asleep in bed when his house caught fire. Then it collapsed. What killed him? Did he burn alive, or was he still alive when the house collapsed only to be crushed by falling timber?

Our leaders in this country are a reflection of the state of its people, morally and spiritually. Many, many people argue that the people of America are so capricious that the leader — which reflects the people — of our country can cause people to turn their back on their faith, to turn their back on scriptural priorities, can turn their back on biblical morality and so forth. I'm here to tell you that if such a person exists they were likely never saved to begin with.

There is a huge segment of our population that self-identifies as Christian, but that neither know what it means to be a Christian nor know Christ himself. And so one day — to be absent from the body is to be present with the Lord — in judgment or paradise, one day when those people see Christ face to face He will say depart from me, I know you not.

Tim Keller says:

> If you can't show the difference between religion and the gospel, people will confuse morality with a changed heart.

I think this is in large part at the heart of the issue. People confuse cultural affiliation with a changed heart. With the transformation that only can come through a relationship with Jesus Christ. This huge segment of people we have been talking about only know that culturally it's normal, it's normative to self-identify that way. But in truth they may be following a religion but they aren't following Christ. People have the Jesus fish on their car, people put crosses around their neck, cute vaguely moral sayings on their shirt, and worse — some churches are filled with these people that bear the outward marks of Christianity but in their heart of hearts lack Christ on the throne of their life. They are just as lost as some Satan-worshiping cannibal.

People, let me tell you — Obama didn't make people cultural Christians. Obama didn't force the church to spend less time evangelizing. Obama didn't force people to wear a cross around their neck, and Obama certainly didn't force people to put down their Bibles or attend churches where they won't hear the truth of Scripture. Obama didn't send these people to hell. Their sin does. If anything I think Obama may have agitated some of these same people to action. Political action. Entire churches in our denomination have been distracted by politics instead of obedient to the cross.

I watched some of the Southern Baptist Convention on livestream this past year and when people preached their hearts out about sharing the gospel with the lost there was relative silence. A smattering of applause. This guy and that guy clapping. That pastor over there looked bored, and I don't know what this other was thinking about but he wasn't being stirred towards evangelizing the lost. And yet when some political hack would get up there and shout "Ra, ra, ra America!" the applause was deafening. This, my friends, is idolatry. People stand in the spotlight and shout with bellicosity about making America great again and these pastors would cheer! But when people would talk about making great the name of Christ, those that cheered were in the minority.

Point is we need to be subject to our leaders, but not worshipful of them. We need to be active and informed politically, but if you are spending more time and energy on politics than on Jesus, then that's a problem. That is an idol for you. Anything you put above obedience to God is an idol. Maybe it's a relationship, or an activity that you participate in that you KNOW God wouldn't approve of. That's an idol.

Anyway, I've spent more time on this than I intended.

Secondly we're told not to slander. Not to run people down with our words. Not to talk badly about them behind their back. This is something that happens all too often, and perhaps more frequently with our old president. Some people make slander easy, but that doesn't make it right.

## The Gospel: Titus 3:3-7

Paul then launches into the gospel:

> For we ourselves were once foolish, disobedient, led astray, slaves to various passions and pleasures, passing our days in malice and envy, hated by others and hating one another. But when the goodness and loving kindness of God our Savior appeared, he saved us, not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy, by the washing of regeneration and renewal of the Holy Spirit, whom he poured out on us richly through Jesus Christ our Savior, so that being justified by his grace we might become heirs according to the hope of eternal life.
> — Titus 3:3-7 (ESV)

This whole book of the Bible has been building to this point right here. And since we have been justified by his grace we become heirs with the confident expectation of eternal life. This is the goal of God's merciful salvation. This is the reason he died on the cross for us. So that we could be justified, and then spend eternity with him in heaven. Do you know anyone else who would die for you? I know when I was a kid, I would get very angry the few times I was punished for my sister's wrongs. I resented even taking a spanking for her when I didn't deserve it. Yet we deserved death and Christ died for us in our place so that we could spend eternity with him! Because his blood silences the accuser.

## Smith and Brown in Egypt

You may have seen this on Facebook. I posted it the other day, but it warrants repeating.

Two Jewish men were sitting there one night in Egypt — let's call them Brown and Smith. Very Jewish names. They were getting ready for the final plague under Moses in Egypt. They had seen the flies, and frogs, and boils, and blood, and darkness. They had seen the lice, the diseased livestock, hailstones, and locusts and now they were being told to cover the doors of their houses with blood, and make preparations because that night everyone who had not would lose their firstborn.

So Smith and Brown had both done as they were commanded and they were sitting around a table discussing this latest thing that Moses had prophesied. Smith was a little worried — he had only one son you see, and he'd seen firsthand the plagues. Brown said, "You don't have to be nervous. Haven't you dabbed your posts and lintel with blood? Aren't you going to have the Passover meal with your family? What is there to worry about?" Smith replied, "You know it's alright for you, you've got three sons. If something happens to one there are still two left. But me, I've only got the one son. If he's gone what do I have left? I love him, and don't you know the angel of death is passing through? It's scary. I'll be glad when this night is over." Brown replied, "Bring it on. I trust the promises of God."

That night the angel of death passed through the land. Which one do you think lost his son? The answer is neither. Because death didn't pass over them on the grounds of the faith they possessed but on the grounds of the blood of the lamb. The blood of the lamb silences the accuser of the brethren as he accuses us before God. He silences our consciences when the enemy accuses us directly. How often do we accuse ourselves and say how can God possibly love us after we've done such stupid and rebellious things? After being Christians for four or forty years. I have no other argument, I need no other plea, it is enough that Jesus died for me. The blood of the lamb is the ground of all human assurance. It's not the intensity of our faith, but the author of our faith that saves us.

---

## Closing

If you have never come to a point in your life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins tonight is the night. You stand now at a crossroads. On one side is eternal life, and on the other an eternity in hell. There is no choosing to do nothing. Attending church isn't enough. You can even join a church but that isn't enough either. It's not enough to associate culturally with Christ. Even if you could follow all the rules from this point forward and look convincing, looking convincing is not enough. You MUST have a relationship with Jesus Christ or all is lost. While the praise team sings be thinking about which direction your life is heading. Are you heading for ruin? Or have you been justified by his grace — have you become heirs with the confident expectation of eternal life?

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A faithful opening of Titus 3:1–7 that carries genuine gospel warmth and a memorable assurance illustration, while spending its best energy on dated political commentary that crowds the text out of its own sermon.

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**PASS — with conditions.**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's thesis — that sinners are saved by the mercy and grace of God through Christ, not by works, and that the blood of the Lamb is the ground of all assurance — does arise from Titus 3:3–7's own argument. The main point of the text (God saved us not by works but by his mercy, through Christ, for the purpose of justification and heirship) is the main point the sermon eventually reaches. **Pass.**

*Authority Test:* The preacher submits to the text's claims, including the humbling "we ourselves were once foolish." He does not soften the gospel. However, the Authority Test is stressed — not failed — by the extended political section, which functions as a platform for the preacher's own views rather than as an exposition of the text's claims. The preacher stands under the text when he reaches it. He does not always arrive promptly. **Conditional pass.**

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

**Strength 1 — The Passover Illustration Is Theologically Precise and Pastorally Potent**
Smith and Brown lose no son because the ground of security is the blood, not the faith. The explicit conclusion — "death didn't pass over them on the grounds of the faith they possessed but on the grounds of the blood of the lamb" — is doctrinally exact and will be remembered. This is the sermon at its best.

**Strength 2 — The Gospel / Religion Distinction Is Pastorally Necessary**
The application of the Keller quote (religion vs. changed heart) to the "cultural Christian" problem is accurate, needed, and connected to the passage's own warning about good works righteousness. The preacher correctly identifies the problem: people confuse cultural affiliation with conversion.

**Strength 3 — The Assurance Application Is Well-Grounded**
"The blood of the lamb silences the accuser... It's not the intensity of our faith, but the author of our faith that saves us." This lands as genuine pastoral care for the conscience-troubled believer and arises from the text's own "not by works but by mercy" logic.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

**Weakness 1 — The Political Section Colonizes the Sermon**
The extended treatment of Obama, Trump, the Southern Baptist Convention applause meter, and political idolatry consumes a full third of the sermon's content. The text's own connection between the gospel indicative and civic virtue — why Paul moves from submission to the Passover-like salvation narrative — is never explained. The political material generates its own argument rather than serving the text's argument. The self-diagnosis ("Anyway, I've spent more time on this than I intended") is accurate and damning.

**Weakness 2 — The Text's Richest Theology Disappears**
Titus 3:5–6 — "the washing of regeneration and renewal of the Holy Spirit, whom he poured out on us richly through Jesus Christ our Savior" — is among the most pneumatologically dense passages in the Pastoral Epistles. It is quoted and passed over without a word on what regeneration is, what the Spirit's renewal means, or how the Spirit's outpouring through Christ connects to the assurance the sermon is trying to establish. This is not a minor omission — it is the text's own ground for the sermon's thesis.

**Weakness 3 — The Exhortation Is Unbeliever-Only**
The closing call is exclusively addressed to those who have "never come to a point in your life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price." Believers receive no corresponding exhortation. Titus 3:1–8 is written to a congregation of believers about how to live in the world — the sermon's only explicit call to response is for the unconverted. The covenant community is given analysis (political idolatry warning) but no imperative summons.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the political section, while passionate and in some ways prophetically correct, performs exactly the same function he is criticizing — it draws energy away from the cross and toward a political/cultural concern. The very problem the sermon diagnoses (Christians spending more energy on politics than on Jesus) is, for this sermon's middle section, enacted rather than corrected.

The preacher also likely does not see that the text's own "we were once foolish" (v.3) — applied to the congregation, not to Obama voters — is the pastoral hammer that makes submission to difficult authorities not a political opinion but a gospel-grounded humility. Paul's logic is: we were no better. That logic is gestured at but not pressed into the self-righteous, comfortable believer.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The political section *feels* like prophetic courage — naming idolatry, calling out SBC political theater, contrasting "making America great" with "making great the name of Christ." And it is not wrong. But it functions as the sermon's longest and most energetic section while doing the least expository work. The preacher's passion here is genuine, but it crowds the text. The impression of prophetic boldness masks a structural failure: the sermon's fire burns longest on the application the preacher already believed before he read the text, not on the application the text generated.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The "washing of regeneration and renewal of the Holy Spirit" (Titus 3:5) is the passage's own ground for everything the sermon wants to say about assurance. The Spirit was "poured out richly" — that is baptismal, abundance language. The sermon builds its assurance argument on the Passover blood illustration (good) but misses that Paul has already given the same argument in the text: you are secure not because of your performance but because of what was done to you in regeneration and through whom it was poured out. The Passover illustration is a beautiful supplement to what the text already says — but the text's own assurance argument was left on the table.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (4)

1. **Cut the political section by 60% and reframe what remains around the text's own logic.** The submission-to-authorities application needs one paragraph, not five. The text's argument is: submit to rulers, avoid slander, show courtesy — *because* you were once exactly as disordered as the worst person you're resenting. That is the text's own rebuke to political bitterness. Use it. Cut the SBC applause-meter story and the Obama catalog entirely. They are not serving the text — they are serving the preacher's existing argument.

2. **Exposit Titus 3:5–6 before moving to illustration.** "The washing of regeneration and renewal of the Holy Spirit, whom he poured out richly through Jesus Christ" — spend five minutes here. What is regeneration? (New birth, not reformation.) What is the Spirit's renewal? (Not self-improvement — divine action.) What does "poured out richly" mean? (Abundance, not trickle — God did not spare.) Then the Passover illustration lands on a foundation the text itself has laid.

3. **Add a direct exhortation to believers after the Passover illustration.** The sermon calls the unbeliever forward but sends the believer home without a summons. Something like: "Believer — you who know the blood, are you living like a person who is secure? Are you quarreling, slandering, striving as if the blood weren't enough? Paul's word to you tonight is: you were once that disordered. God saved you by mercy. Now be mercy to others. Go home and submit to the authority you've been resenting. Show perfect courtesy to the neighbor you've been writing off. The blood that silences the accuser before God — that same blood should silence the accuser in you toward others."

4. **Attribute the hymn and the Passover illustration before delivery.** "I have no other argument" — attribute Lidie Edmunds, "My Faith Has Found a Resting Place." The Smith and Brown illustration — note the Spurgeon connection and the uncertainty. Congregational trust is not strengthened by unattributed quotation.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon has pastoral warmth, genuine gospel affection, and the Passover illustration carries real unction. This is not bloodless preaching. The fire is present — it is simply burning in the wrong section of the house.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement

The Passover illustration earns genuine eternal gravity — "the blood of the lamb silences the accuser" is a phrase the congregation will carry home — but the sermon as a whole squanders too much of its weight on 2017 American politics to be counted among those sermons that feel like they matter beyond Sunday. **Bonus: +2 / 5.**

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Titus 3:1–7 is an epistle passage with strong doctrinal-applicatory structure, well-suited to this rubric. No genre strain. The rubric's emphasis on the FCF (fallen condition focus) fits the passage precisely — Paul's own FCF logic in v.3 ("we ourselves were once foolish") is the sermon's unused structural key. The rubric bends in no significant way for this passage; the passage rewards exactly the kind of exposition-to-application-to-exhortation sequence the rubric demands.
