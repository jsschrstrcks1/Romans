**The Remnant of Grace**
**Romans 11:1–10 (ESV)**
**Big Idea**
God has not rejected His people. In every age He preserves a remnant **chosen by grace**—and grace, by definition, leaves no room for works or pride.
**Introduction — “Has God Rejected His People?”**
Open your Bible to Romans 11. Paul asks a question that some of his hearers were already whispering:
“Has God rejected His people?” (v. 1).
After all, many of the Jews had stumbled over Christ (9:32–33), while the gospel was now flooding into the Gentile world (10:18–21). Did Israel’s unbelief mean that God’s promises had expired?
Paul’s answer explodes like a thunderclap:
**“By no means!”** (μὴ γένοιτο)
You might remember that phrase from earlier in our series—the most emphatic “no” in Greek. He points to himself as Exhibit A:
“For I myself am an Israelite, a descendant of Abraham, a member of the tribe of Benjamin.” (v. 1)
If God were done with Israel, Paul wouldn’t be an apostle—he’d be an orphan.
Then Paul reaches back to **Elijah**—that weary prophet who once thought the light of faith had gone out in Israel. And through Elijah’s despair, Paul reveals the beating heart of this passage: even in the darkest hours, **God keeps a people for Himself.**
Here’s where we’re headed:
God’s Answer — Not rejected but preserved (vv. 1–2)
Elijah’s Lesson — When we think “none,” God says “some” (vv. 2–4)
The Principle — A remnant now, chosen by grace (vv. 5–6)
The Divide — Earners are hardened; beggars are blessed (vv. 7–10)
The Call — Humble assurance and hopeful witness

**1) God’s Answer: Not Rejected —but Preserved (vv. 1–2)**
“Has God rejected His people? By no means! … God has not rejected His people whom He foreknew.” (vv. 1–2)
The word **“foreknew”** (*προέγνω — proegnō*) does not mean mere foresight, as though God looked ahead and saw who would love Him. It means **fore-love**—the setting of God’s covenant heart upon His people. Amos 3:2 says,
“You only have I known of all the families of the earth.”
That “knowing” is covenantal intimacy. God loves His people even when they are messy, the way a parent loves a baby who spits up. She may be messy, but she’s still beloved. We may be spiritually messy—but God loves us anyway.
Paul’s logic is covenantal: **what God sets His love upon, He keeps.**
Your assurance isn’t anchored in Israel’s faithfulness, or in your own; it’s anchored in God’s.
My dad said in Bible study the other night that trusting us with our own salvation would be like handing a priceless porcelain dish to a toddler in a tantrum—we’d destroy it in seconds. Not out of malice, but because on our best day we are still children before God.
And yet, *“He who did not spare His own Son but gave Him up for us all”* (Romans 8:32) **will not abandon His promises.** God would never sacrifice something as precious as His Son for something so fragile as a temporary salvation. If our salvation was worth Jesus’ death, it is worth His safeguarding. He did not send His Son to die for a lost cause.
That He foreknew us—saw every sin, every failure, every rebellion—and still chose to love us, is one of the marvels of the gospel. Imagine God looking upon someone like you before the foundation of the world, knowing everything you would do, and saying, *“I will die for that one.”* Not because of any good in you, but **in spite** of all the bad. We say that easily, but it was the weight Christ carried to the cross.
**Applications**
**To the struggling saint:** God’s grip preceded your first prayer and will outlast your worst week. *We love because He first loved us* (1 John 4:19).
**To the self-assured sinner:** Being near the covenants is not the same as being **in** Christ. Many go to church their whole lives and never move from head knowledge to heart surrender—missing heaven by eighteen inches.
**To the seeking skeptic:** God’s faithfulness to a stiff-necked people is the very hope of stiff-necked people like us. If there was hope for Paul, there is hope for you. No one is too far gone. No one is beyond God’s reach. *“Ain’t no mountain high enough,”* as the song says—*and the heavens are His footstool after all.*
**2) Elijah’s Lesson: When We Think “None,” God Says “Some” (vv. 2–4)**
Paul continues:
“Do you not know what the Scripture says of Elijah, how he appeals to God against Israel? ‘Lord, they have killed your prophets, they have demolished your altars, and I alone am left, and they seek my life.’ But what is God’s reply to him? ‘I have kept for myself seven thousand men who have not bowed the knee to Baal.’” (Romans 11:2–4 ESV)
After the fire fell on Mount Carmel, Jezebel’s threats drove Elijah into despair. He cried, *“I alone am left.”* But God answered, *“I have kept (κατέλιπον — katelipon) for myself seven thousand who have not bowed to Baal.”*
Two truths rise from that exchange:
**a) God keeps.**
The remnant is not self-made but God-made. The seven thousand may have thought they held themselves upright, but God says, *“I have kept them.”* Preservation is an act of divine sovereignty.
That reminds me of a story a dear friend once told me. Her grandmother married young—she was 16, her husband 18. Not long after, the government called him to war. He was a **code talker**, one of the Navajo men whose language helped safeguard freedom in World War II. He went overseas, leaving his wife alone in the woods, too far from her parents to walk home.
She was not helpless—she was capable and strong—but survival was hard. The only possessions she had were three hunting dogs. The oldest and wisest of the pack somehow recognized her need. When they caught a deer or a rabbit, the matriarch would bring her a share of the food.
What was survivable in the warmth of summer became perilous in the frozen silence of winter. Yet through those long months of 1943 and 1944, those dogs brought her what she needed to live until spring returned.
She thought she had kept herself alive. The dogs thought they had done it. But the One who truly kept her was **God**—the same God who told Elijah, *“I have kept for Myself seven thousand.”*
**b) Hiddenness does not mean absence.**
Elijah couldn’t see those seven thousand. He saw only the darkness. Yet God had them all along.
**Pastoral word:** Our moment often feels like Elijah’s cave—apostasy loud, faithfulness quiet. But God’s arithmetic runs deeper than our headlines. When your eyes report “empty” or “alone,” trust the God who whispers, *“I have kept.”*
Even now, He has His remnant—in every nation, every generation, every neighborhood. We may not see them all, but heaven’s census is never wrong.

**3) The Principle: A Remnant Now, Chosen by Grace (vv. 5–6)**
“So too at the present time there is a remnant (λείμμα — *leimma*), chosen by grace (κατ’ ἐκλογὴν χάριτος — *kat’ eklogēn charitos*). But if it is by grace, it is no longer on the basis of works; otherwise grace would no longer be grace.” (Romans 11:5–6 ESV)
Two anchor words shape this verse:
**ἐκλογή (eklogē) — “election,”** God’s free, sovereign choosing.
**χάρις (charis) — “grace,”** His unearned, undeserved favor in Christ.
Paul slams the door on human boasting. If you add one gram of works to grace, you no longer have grace. As Thomas Watson said, “Grace does not find men worthy; it makes them so.”
Someone once preached, “We are a wounded gazelle, and God’s grace a hungry lion.” That’s true to the heart of Paul’s thought here—grace does not merely help the weak; it consumes the dead and raises them to life. Regeneration precedes fruit; mercy births faith.
**Preach this to your heart:**
Grace isn’t God helping good people finish the climb; grace is God raising the dead. *(Ephesians 2:1–5 ESV — “And you were dead in the trespasses and sins in which you once walked… But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which He loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ — by grace you have been saved.”)*
Grace isn’t leniency; it’s **substitution** — Christ crucified instead of me.
Grace leaves no boasting but in the cross. *(Galatians 6:14 ESV — “But far be it from me to boast except in the cross of our Lord Jesus Christ, by which the world has been crucified to me, and I to the world.”)*
Grace is not a ladder we climb; it’s a lifeline God throws. It does not help the strong achieve; it raises the dead to believe. Every believer is a miracle of mercy.

**4) The Divide: Earners Are Hardened; Beggars Are Blessed (vv. 7–10)**
“What then? Israel failed to obtain what it was seeking. The elect obtained it, but the rest were hardened, as it is written, ‘God gave them a spirit of stupor, eyes that would not see and ears that would not hear, down to this very day.’ And David says, ‘Let their table become a snare and a trap, a stumbling block and a retribution for them; let their eyes be darkened so that they cannot see, and bend their backs forever.’” (Romans 11:7–10 ESV)
Here Paul weaves three Scriptures together to explain the divide between faith and hardness:
**Deuteronomy 29:4 (ESV)**
“But to this day the Lord has not given you a heart to understand or eyes to see or ears to hear.”
**Isaiah 29:10 (ESV)**
“For the Lord has poured out upon you a spirit of deep sleep, and has closed your eyes (the prophets), and covered your heads (the seers).”
**Psalm 69:22–23 (ESV)**
“Let their own table before them become a snare; and when they are at peace, let it become a trap. Let their eyes be darkened, so that they cannot see, and make their loins tremble continually.”
The same sun that softens wax hardens clay. The same gospel that melts one heart can expose and harden another. Those who come to God as earners stumble; those who come as beggars receive.
Charles Spurgeon warned, “Morality can keep you out of jail; only Christ can keep you out of hell.” Paul might add: **privilege without repentance becomes a trap.** If we sit at a gospel-laden table and never eat, that table condemns us.
Jesus Himself said, > “But Jesus said to him, ‘No one who puts his hand to the plow and looks back [to the things left behind] is fit for the kingdom of God.’” (Luke 9:62 AMP)
**Pastoral Cautions**
**Against Presumption:** Proximity to truth is not possession of Christ.
There was a game invented in Japan. You paid a few dollars to enter, and in the center of the room was a large glass box, barely wide enough for a hand to fit through. At the bottom lay a thirty-pound gold bar worth a fortune. The rule was simple: if you could pull it out with one hand without breaking the box, you could keep it. The game was rigged, of course—anyone strong enough to lift it had an arm too large to withdraw it.
Still people lined up for miles, each paying their entry fee, each trying and failing, touching the gold but never owning it. Hundreds of thousands came and left poorer than before—until one old man figured out a way to tilt it to the glass and slide it out. For all the rest, proximity was not possession.
The same is true spiritually. Being near the truth is not the same as being in Christ. You can sit in church, hear the Word, even feel the weight of conviction and still walk away unchanged. As James says, “Even the demons believe — and shudder.” (James 2:19 ESV)
**Against Pride:** If you believe, it was given to you. > *(Philippians 1:29 ESV — “For it has been granted to you that for the sake of Christ you should not only believe in Him but also suffer for His sake.”)* Be low before God and gentle with sinners.
Hardening is not God’s cruelty—it is His justice in response to persistent unbelief. But grace is His mercy to the undeserving. One saves, the other exposes. And if you are in Christ, you owe your soft heart to His sovereign grace.
**5) The Call: Humble Assurance and Hopeful Witness**
**Assurance**
If you are in Christ, it is because grace hunted you down. Think of Saul of Tarsus on the Damascus road (Acts 9). Your failures cannot unwind God’s election; your future rests on Christ’s finished work. “Chosen by grace” is a pillow for a weary soul.
**Humility**
Election is not a trophy; it’s a life ring. The remnant is a rescued people, not a superior people. Any doctrine of grace that produces arrogance has not been learned from the Bible.
**Witness**
Elijah learned there were seven thousand within reach of God he could not see. So do not measure your mission by your eyesight. It’s easy to say, “I’m too old, too sick, too tired.” But if you still draw breath, God still has a purpose. Pray, preach, plead—and trust that God has “many in this city who are My people.” (Acts 18:10 ESV) Some you already know and love; others you have yet to meet with the gospel.
**Applications (Believer • Complacent • Skeptic)**
**To the Believer — The Struggling Saint**
When condemnation hisses, answer with verse 5: “At the present time there is a remnant chosen by grace.” Your place in Christ is not a performance review; it’s a blood-bought adoption. Kill sin, yes—but do it from acceptance, not for it. Christ has already loved you with an eternal love before the foundation of the world.
**To the Complacent — The Self-Assured Sinner**
If you sit weekly at a gospel table but never eat Christ by faith, that table can become your snare (vv. 9–10). Don’t mistake nearness to holy things for new birth. Lay down your works and receive the righteousness of Another.
**To the Skeptic — The Seeking Soul**
Maybe you fear you’ve gone too far. Romans 11 begins with a persecutor-turned-preacher and a nation that resisted God—yet He still says, “I have kept for Myself.” Come to Christ with empty hands. Grace specializes in impossible cases. If there was hope for Paul, there is hope for you. The man who once hunted Christians became the instrument who wrote half the New Testament. No one is too far gone for God.
**Gospel Clarity (v. 6)**
“If by grace, then it is no longer on the basis of works; otherwise grace would no longer be grace.” (Romans 11:6 ESV)
Christ **lived** the law we failed.
Christ **died** the death we earned.
Christ **rose** to give the life we cannot produce.
Receive Him by faith—not faith plus anything. As you said, “Unless Jesus be all to you, He is nothing to you. Jesus only — Jesus all.”
J. C. Ryle reminds us, “Christ is never fully valued until sin is clearly seen.” Let the law show you the need; let Christ be your remedy.

**Closing Exhortation**
Beloved, the remnant exists because grace exists—and grace exists because Christ lives and intercedes. Let that truth make you steady, small, and bold all at once: **steady** in assurance, **small** in humility, **bold** in witness.
A Puritan might pray:
“Oh, make me Thy faithful remnant—
low at the cross, loud with the gospel,
and loyal to the end.”

**Prayer**
Father, thank You that You foreknew people like us. Thank You that You have not rejected Your people whom You foreknew. Thank You for keeping a people for Yourself when our eyes cannot see them. Root us in grace, empty us of pride, and send us with hope. For Jesus’ sake — Amen.
