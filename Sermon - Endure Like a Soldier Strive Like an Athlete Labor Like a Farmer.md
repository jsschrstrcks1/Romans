## People Group of the Week — Arain (Pakistan)

The Arain are a Muslim people of Arab origin who settled in the Punjab region of Pakistan during the early Islamic expansion. More than a million of them. Cultivators. Farmers. A people known for hard work, frugality, and discipline — respected in their communities for exactly those qualities.

And almost entirely without the gospel.

The barrier isn't geography. It's social. When someone from the Arain community becomes a Christian, their family doesn't just disapprove. They are shamed. The whole family. The convert is often cut off — sometimes violently. The cost of following Jesus for an Arain believer is not inconvenience. It is everything.

Pray that God would raise up witnesses among them. That the same endurance Paul is going to call us to this morning would be found in a young Arain believer who has lost his family and still stands. Pray for those workers planting seed they may never see harvested.

## Opening

I want to start with a question.

How many of you, when you came to Christ, were told it would be hard?

Not — how many of you were told it would be *worth it.* I mean the other thing. Did anyone sit across from you before your baptism and actually say: *"I need to warn you. This is going to cost you something"?*

Because there's a version of Christianity being offered in a lot of places right now that sounds like this:

Come to Jesus. Find peace. Life gets better.

And I understand the appeal. The gospel *is* good news. God is a comfort. The Holy Spirit is called the Comforter. I'm not arguing with any of that.

But if that's the whole sermon — if no one ever told you that following Jesus means sharing in suffering — then at some point reality is going to hit you like a wall, and you'll have one of two reactions. Either you'll think: *something is wrong with my faith.* Or you'll think: *something is wrong with Christianity.*

Paul is writing this letter to Timothy from prison. Not a retreat center. Not a comfortable study with good lighting and a cup of coffee. He's writing with chains on his wrists, knowing he is about to die, to a young pastor who is scared and tired and probably wondering what he got himself into.

And what Paul does not say is: *Hang in there, it gets easier.*

He says: *Share in suffering as a good soldier of Christ Jesus.*

That's the word. Suffering. And it is not an accident. It is the summons at the center of this passage.

What I want to do this morning is work through three pictures Paul paints for what a strong Christian looks like. Not a comfortable one. Not a happy one. A *strong* one. A soldier. An athlete. A farmer.

But before we get there, we have to start at verse 1 — because if you miss verse 1, everything that follows becomes a to-do list. And a to-do list will either make you proud or crush you.

---

## The Foundation — Grace Before Command (v. 1)

Paul says in verse 1: *"Be strengthened by the grace that is in Christ Jesus."*

Not your willpower. Not your discipline. Not your track record.

**Grace.** Specifically, grace *in Christ Jesus.* Located grace. Purchased grace. Grace secured at the cross and poured out through union with the Son.

This matters more than anything else I'll say today.

Christ has already done what you could not. He endured suffering perfectly — Hebrews 12:2 says he endured the cross "for the joy that was set before him." He obeyed where you and I fold at the first hard thing — Philippians 2:8, "obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross." He finished it — John 19:30, "It is finished." Not paused. Not pending. Finished.

So Romans 8:1 stands: *"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."*

This passage is not *earn your standing with God.* It is *live from the standing Christ has already secured.*

That changes everything about how you hear what comes next.

## 1. The Focused Soldier (vv. 3–4)

*"Share in suffering as a good soldier of Christ Jesus. No soldier gets entangled in civilian pursuits, since his aim is to please the one who enlisted him."*

Paul doesn't ease into this. He just says it: share in suffering.

And this is not a metaphor he invented. Suffering is the consistent description of the Christian life throughout the New Testament.

Ephesians 6:12 — *"For we do not wrestle against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the cosmic powers over this present darkness, against the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly places."*

1 Peter 5:8 — *"Your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion, seeking someone to devour."*

2 Corinthians 10:4 — *"The weapons of our warfare are not of the flesh but have divine power to destroy strongholds."*

This is not poetic language. This is the honest description of what you walked into the day you said yes to Jesus.

**You are in a war.**

Now, that's hard to feel on a Sunday morning in a comfortable building in the American South. We don't have chains on our wrists. Nobody has dragged us to court for preaching the gospel. I understand that. We are not Timothy in a hostile empire.

But the war is real. The enemy is real. The souls of the people who live on your street, work at your job, sit next to you at Thanksgiving — those are real. And the nature of any war is that you do not get to opt out of the hard parts.

### A. The Danger of Entanglement

Look at verse 4: *"No soldier gets entangled in civilian pursuits."*

That word *entangled* — in the Greek it is ἐμπλέκεται, from the verb ἐμπλέκω. It means to be woven in. Braided together with something until separation is nearly impossible. The picture is not a soldier who checks his phone occasionally. The picture is a soldier whose life has been slowly, completely absorbed into something that has nothing to do with his mission — and he doesn't even notice because it happened gradually.

Jesus uses the same image in Matthew 13:22, talking about the seed sown among thorns: *"the cares of the world and the deceitfulness of riches choke the word, and it proves unfruitful."*

Choke. Entangle. Woven in. Fruitless.

Let me be plain with you for a moment.

I'm not asking about open sin right now — though for some of you, that's exactly where you need to sit and stay. But I'm asking about something more subtle. What has gradually, quietly absorbed you?

Is it the endless scroll — not sinful in itself, just constant, and the moment you stop you feel anxious, and Scripture keeps getting pushed to the margins of the day?

Is it comfort addiction — organizing your whole life around the avoidance of difficulty, inconvenience, and pain?

Is it living from one break to the next, one vacation to the next, one weekend to the next, and the Christian life has become the thing you do *between* the things that actually matter to you?

You are not called to dabble in Christianity between leisure activities. You are called to be a soldier in a war.

### B. The Aim of a Soldier

The end of verse 4: *"since his aim is to please the one who enlisted him."*

You belong to Christ. You were purchased. 1 Corinthians 6:19–20 — *"You are not your own, for you were bought with a price. So glorify God in your body."*

The primary question of every day is no longer *what do I want.* It is *what pleases the One who bought me?*

**To the tired believer:** You feel the cost of following Jesus. The marriage is a war zone. The relationship with your kid is broken. The body is failing. The addiction is a daily fight. You're exhausted, and some mornings you wonder if it's worth it.

Let me tell you something: that weariness is not evidence of weak faith. It's evidence that you're actually in the fight. A soldier who isn't tired probably isn't fighting.

And hear this carefully: you are not fighting *for* Christ's love. You already have it. You are fighting *from* it. There is a difference, and it is the difference between endurance and despair.

**To the comfortable Christian:** If your Christianity costs you nothing — no resistance, no sacrifice, no tension between what you want and what Christ calls you to — then I want to ask you honestly: are you in the war? Or are you watching it from a lawn chair?

And then there are some of you who have known all three of these metaphors since you were twelve years old. Soldier, athlete, farmer — you could preach this text. You've heard it, you've nodded at it, you've filed it away. The question isn't whether you know it. It's whether it has ever actually broken you open. Has the soldier passage ever made you actually put something down? Has the farmer section ever produced anything in your prayer life — any change in how you wait on God? James 1:22 — *"But be doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving yourselves."* Familiarity without obedience is self-deception. Check carefully which one you're carrying.

## 2. The Disciplined Athlete (v. 5)

*"An athlete is not crowned unless he competes according to the rules."*

Now Paul shifts the picture — from *focus* to *discipline.*

In Paul's world, the Greek games were serious business. Citizenship requirements. Ten months of mandatory supervised training. An oath before competing. And if you violated the rules — cut corners in training, cheated in the race — you weren't just stripped of the crown. You were disqualified publicly. Made a pariah. Your name was a warning, not a legacy.

The picture is not a casual jogger on a Saturday morning. The picture is someone whose entire life is shaped around a single goal.

### A. No One Drifts Into This

Here's the thing about athletic discipline that we don't like to say out loud: no one stumbles into it.

I've never met a person who accidentally got up at five in the morning to train. I've never met a Christian who drifted accidentally into deep knowledge of Scripture. There is no such thing as a disciple who grew rich in prayer without deciding to pray.

Hebrews 12:11 says it plainly: *"For the moment all discipline seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained by it."*

Painful at first. Fruitful later.

You don't get around that equation. You go through it.

### B. According to the Rules

*According to the rules.* Paul says it because it matters. You don't get to define Christianity. Not your preferences, not your feelings on a given Tuesday, not the mood of the culture. *"All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness"* — 2 Timothy 3:16. The standard has been set.

Which means some of you are wanting the prize — the deep faith, the fruitful life, the settled soul — while skipping the discipline that produces it. You want growth without the daily Word. You want spiritual depth without the slow, unglamorous, repeated practice of being in God's presence.

That doesn't exist. There is no crowning without competing. That's not law — that's sanctification.

### C. The Gospel Anchor

But I want to make sure you hear this: your discipline is not purchasing anything.

Christ already ran the race perfectly. Hebrews 12:2 — he ran it all the way to the cross and through it, "for the joy that was set before him." You are not running *for* your justification. You are running *because* you have been justified. The posture of a son is different from the posture of a slave trying to earn something.

**To the person who keeps failing:** Hear Philippians 1:6 — *"He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ."* He has not abandoned the project. Get back up. Keep running.

**To the person who is coasting:** I'll say this gently but I need to say it. Coasting in the Christian life is not neutral. The currents of the world are always moving, and if you're not rowing against them, you are drifting downstream. You are not standing still. That's not an accusation — it's an invitation to get back in the race.

## 3. The Patient Farmer (v. 6)

*"It is the hardworking farmer who ought to have the first share of the crops."*

Now we get to the hardest one. And it's hardest not because the burden is heaviest, but because it's the most ordinary.

The soldier gets the adrenaline. Something is clearly at stake. The athlete gets the race, the crowd, the finish line. But the farmer gets none of that. The farmer gets up before the sun. Does what needs to be done. Goes to bed. Gets up and does it again. The seed he plants in the spring won't show anything until summer. The harvest won't come until fall. And he just has to keep working the ground, pulling the weeds, carrying water — for months — with no visible return on the investment.

I can speak to this personally. I've farmed peppers. And I've raised sheep, which is the thing that'll really humble you. You feed them, water them, tend their wounds, sit with them at two in the morning when they're sick — and sometimes they just give up and die for no reason you can figure out. And sometimes the whole flock makes it. And you can't always tell why one year is different from another. Farming is not a system you master. It is a relationship with weather and biology and Providence that you learn to work with rather than control.

James 5:7 — *"See how the farmer waits for the precious fruit of the earth, being patient about it, until it receives the early and the late rains."*

Patient. That's the operative word. And I think patience is where most of us actually come apart — not in some dramatic crisis of faith, not in some spectacular fall, but in the quiet accumulation of unanswered prayers and invisible fruit. And eventually the question settles in: *what's the point?*

You have been praying for your spouse for eight years. You can't see any change.

You've been faithful — church, Bible, raising kids in the Word — and your teenager just told you he doesn't believe any of it.

You've been showing up to serve quietly, with nobody noticing, for years. And you're starting to wonder if it matters.

Here is what the farmer knows: **growth happens underground before it happens above it.**

The seed is real before the stem. The roots are deep before anything is visible. God is doing something in your family, in your own soul, in this church — something you cannot see from where you're standing. The field doesn't look like much in February. You plow it anyway.

Galatians 6:9 — *"And let us not grow weary of doing good, for in due season we will reap, if we do not give up."*

*If we do not give up.* The harvest is certain. But only for the farmer who stayed in the field.

Do not abandon the field.

---

## 4. The Dependent Thinker (v. 7)

*"Think over what I say, for the Lord will give you understanding in everything."*

Don't skip this verse. Paul is tying everything together here with something that looks small and isn't.

He gives Timothy two things that seem like they might pull in opposite directions: *Think* — engage your mind, wrestle with the text, meditate on it. And: *the Lord will give understanding* — this is not self-powered comprehension. This is Spirit-given illumination.

Both are real. Both are necessary.

This is not passive Christianity — sit back and wait for God to download the meaning. Paul says *think.* Wrestle. Engage. Let the passage grind on you.

But it is also not self-sufficient Christianity. 1 Corinthians 2:14 is plain: *"The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned."* Understanding is a gift. You can sit in front of this text all day and generate nothing if the Spirit is not at work.

You bring the thinking. God brings the light.

Which is why the soldier prays before he fights. Why the athlete trains on his knees as well as on the track. Why the farmer knows that he works the ground and God sends the rain — and there is no harvest without both.

Think deeply. Depend fully.

## The Gospel

Before I close, I want to make sure no one leaves this room thinking the sermon was about trying harder.

It was not.

Here is the truth about every person in this room, starting with me:

We are sinners. Not imperfect people in need of coaching. *Sinners.* Romans 3:23 — *"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."* That's not the congregation. That's all of us. That's me.

And God is holy. And judgment is real. And there is no amount of soldiering, disciplined training, or faithful farming that can undo what we owe.

But — Romans 5:8 — *"God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us."*

While we were still in the middle of it. Not when we got our act together. While we were still enemies.

He lived the life we could not live. He obeyed where we fold. He endured to the end where we would have quit at the first hard thing. He took the judgment we deserved.

This is justification by faith — not your performance credited to your account, but His righteousness credited to yours. Freely. By grace alone. Through faith alone. You do not bring a record. You bring empty hands.

And He rose.

He is not in a grave. He is at the right hand of the Father, and He is alive, and He is interceding for you right now.

So when Paul says *share in suffering* — the One who calls you into suffering has suffered infinitely more than you ever will, and He did it for you, in your place. When he says *compete according to the rules* — the One who sets the standard met it completely, so that you stand before God not on your record but on His. When he says *work hard like a farmer* — the One who calls you to labor is the same One who said "I will build my church," and the harvest ultimately belongs to Him.

If you have never trusted Christ — this is the moment. Repent. Believe. The door is open. The debt is paid. The record is clear.

If you are in Christ — you are a soldier whose Commanding Officer already took the hill before you ever got there. Run. Fight. Plow. Not to earn anything. Because you've already been given everything.

---

## Closing

Paul wrote these words from a prison cell. He knew what suffering was in ways most of us will never approach. Beaten, shipwrecked, hungry, betrayed by people he loved, abandoned when it mattered most. He knew the full cost.

And near the end of this same letter — just a few chapters from now — he wrote this:

*"I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith."* — 2 Timothy 4:7

Not: *I had an easy road.*
Not: *I found the balance.*

**I finished.**

That is what I want for you. Not comfort. Not ease. Not a life that raised no eyebrows and cost you nothing.

A life you finished. A faith you kept. A field you worked until the harvest came.

One day you will stand before Christ. And you will not be asked whether you were comfortable. You will not be asked whether you found the right work-life balance. You will be asked whether you were faithful.

By His grace — we will be.

*Soli Deo Gloria.*
