## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Genuine Pastoral Heart, Thin Expositional Frame**

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and personally invested opening of Romans 14:1–21 that successfully applies the weaker-brother principle to concrete congregational life, while leaving the text’s own theological argument largely unexplored and the gospel more assumed than proclaimed.

### 3. Berean Gate: PASS (Qualified)

**Scopus Test**: The sermon’s central thesis — "Consider how your actions affect others and whether they honor God" — does arise from Romans 14, particularly the weaker-brother principle of vv. 13–21. The passage’s own controlling argument (mutual welcome among believers with differing convictions, grounded in the lordship of Christ over each) is partially represented.

**Authority Test**: The preacher submits to the text’s basic claims and does not soften its ethical demands. However, the sermon’s structure is organized around the preacher’s own four questions rather than the text’s own argumentative movement, which means the passage functions more as a reservoir of illustrations than as the governing voice. The gate passes, but narrowly — the sermon’s backbone is topical even if the content is biblical.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

No flags triggered at the cap-level. However, note the following near-misses:

- **Gospel Centrality (borderline)**: The gospel is present in the closing appeal, but it is functional rather than proclamatory — it answers "what must you do to be saved?" without explaining what Christ has done. The finished work of Christ (atonement, justification, resurrection) is not proclaimed; Christ is named as the means of entry to heaven but not as the substance of the good news. This does not trigger the fatal flaw cap but is a significant weakness.
- **Application specificity**: Application is stronger than average — the Rami/hat and Holly/hand stories are genuinely specific. This flag does not trigger.
- **Exhortation**: A closing call is present. Does not trigger.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity (6/10)*: The sermon touches all four sections of Romans 14 (vv. 1–5, 6–9, 10–12, 13–21) and draws the weaker-brother application from the correct locus. However, the passage’s central theological claim — that the lordship of Christ (vv. 7–9) is the grounds for mutual forbearance — receives almost no development. The "it is before his own master that he stands or falls" (v. 4b, cited in the title) is never actually exposited. The distinction between the "strong" and the "weak" in faith — Paul’s careful taxonomy — is flattened into a general call to consider others.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5)*: The connection to the early church’s food-sacrificed-to-idols controversy is mentioned but treated as background detail rather than hermeneutical key. The broader argument of Romans (chapters 12–15 as the ethical outworking of the gospel) is entirely absent. No mention of the specific Roman congregation’s Jew/Gentile tension that animates the passage.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5)*: The canon/measuring-stick point (v. 12) is a reasonable illustration. But key terms — "fully convinced in his own mind" (v. 5), "in honor of the Lord" (v. 6), "the kingdom of God is not food and drink but righteousness and peace and joy in the Holy Spirit" (v. 17) — are either not treated or not explained. The rich v. 17 is entirely absent from the sermon.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5)*: No serious eisegesis. The four-part structure does follow the passage’s general movement. But the import of the dog illustration (Grace checking to see if anyone was watching) slightly misfires as an application of v. 5 — "fully convinced in his own mind" in Paul refers to settled conscience about disputable matters, not the absence of hiding behavior from God. That conflation is not fatal but does misread the text’s emphasis.
- *FCF*: The fallen condition addressed — divided community, self-serving judgment, lack of conscience-driven conviction — is implied but never named precisely.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7)*: Christ appears as Lord of the living and the dead (vv. 7–9 region) and as Savior at the close. The connection between Christ’s lordship and the text’s ethical argument is gesturally present but not developed. The "for none of us lives to himself" foundation is not theologically unpacked.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (1/3)*: No placement of this text within redemptive history. The Acts 10 reference (food declared clean) is a helpful contextual point but is used illustratively rather than theologically.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4/4)*: No serious doctrinal errors. The sermon does not contradict confessional theology. The politics-as-"impostors" note (2 Tim 3:13) is wisely annotated in the manuscript as needing acknowledgment of its limits.
- *Confessional Alignment (2/4)*: The sermon handles Romans 14 ethically but does not engage the covenantal or soteriological dimensions that a full confessional reading would surface. The "kingdom of God" language in v. 17 — rich with redemptive-historical freight — is absent.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 7 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (2/5)*: The closing call names Jesus as Savior and eternal life as the gift. But it frames the gospel primarily as the answer to "why should God let you in?" — which is a judgment-seat framing without the substance of what Christ did. The words "died," "resurrection," "forgiveness," "justification," "blood," and "propitiation" do not appear. An unbeliever would know to put faith in Jesus but not why Christ’s death was necessary.
- *Necessity of Christ (3/5)*: Christ is presented as necessary for salvation in the closing appeal. Within the body of the sermon, however, the ethical argument proceeds largely without Christ as its ground — the case for considering others could largely survive without Jesus as its center of gravity.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5)*: The sermon moves toward a salvation appeal at the close but the cross is not the gravitational center of the sermon’s argument. The Spurgeon quote is the sermon’s most powerful moment — vivid, urgent, gospel-adjacent — but it arrives as a closer rather than as the fruit of cross-centered argument.
- *Synagogue Test*: A devout non-Christian could sit through most of this sermon without offense — the ethical argument about considering others and living for God rather than self is largely available to a morally serious non-Christian. Christ becomes essential only at the closing appeal. This is a concern.
- *Law/Gospel*: The law has force in places (the "will it stand the test before God?" section), but the gospel is not given time and space to relieve it. The balm is thin.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 10 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration (3/5)*: The closing question — "If he were to return tonight..." — presses the conscience genuinely. The "which team are you playing for?" challenge has real force. The earlier sections are more instructive than penetrating.
- *Specificity (5/5)*: This is the sermon’s clearest strength. The Rami/hat story, the Holly/hand story, the cousin Larry story, the Facebook post question — these are specific, congregationally rooted, and they land. This is Puritan "use" in practice, even if not labeled as such.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5)*: The sermon essentially addresses two categories: the believer who needs to examine motives and actions, and the unbeliever who needs to come to Christ. The backslider, the humbled-but-not-yet-believing, the knowledgeable-but-unmoved, and the self-righteous are not distinctly addressed. The Rami/hat story implies a self-righteous hearer — but that confrontation is not pressed home to those who might recognize themselves in the condemning woman.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5 / 8**

- *Call to Response (3/4)*: The closing call is clear and direct. The "which team" challenge is genuinely urgent. The application sections ("consider your actions," "are you doing this unto the Lord?") contain implicit exhortation. What is missing is a sharp "therefore" moment — a precise, text-grounded, "in light of what we have seen, here is what you must now do."
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4)*: The Nik Ripken reference and the gospel-sharing challenge send the church outward, which is a strength. The equipping dimension for ongoing community life (how to practically navigate the strong/weak dynamic) is more gestured at than developed.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6 / 9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4)*: The four-question structure (Am I fully convinced? Am I doing this for the Lord? Will it stand the test? Will I cause others to stumble?) is clear, memorable, and followable. A listener could sketch it.
- *Progression (2/3)*: The sermon builds somewhat toward the closing appeal. However, the dog illustration appears in sections 1 and 2 and then again in section 4, which creates slight repetition without cumulative force.
- *Unity (1/2)*: The sermon contains competing centers: the strong/weak brother principle, the motive-examination principle, the gospel-sharing imperative, and the church-size discussion. The church-size paragraph near the close is a tangent that breaks the sermon’s unity at the moment it should be tightening.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4 / 5**

- *Relevance (2/2)*: Grace the dog, cousin Larry, Rami’s hat, Holly’s hand — all are congregationally native and immediately accessible.
- *Clarity (1/2)*: Most illustrations illuminate their point clearly. The Grace/Opie parallel to the weaker-brother section works well. However, the Grace-checking-the-window illustration is applied to "fully convinced in his own mind" in a way that slightly misreads the verse (see Exposition notes).
- *Memorability (1/1)*: The Spurgeon quote and the Grace-puts-herself-in-the-crate image are both memorable. The cousin Larry story earns its place.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3 / 5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2/2)*: This reads like a man speaking to people he knows, not an essay. The direct address and personal stories give it an oral quality. Sentence variation is present.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2)*: The Spurgeon quote carries the highest conviction density of the sermon. The preacher’s own voice is warm but sometimes diluted by hedging ("honestly that’s between you and God," "I’m pretty sure..."). At the moments that need to thunder — the judgment seat, the two teams — the manuscript pulls slightly short of its own weight.
- *Cadence and Breath (0/1)*: Some good short-sentence pressure at the close ("He KNOWS you."), but the sermon lacks a sustained climactic compression. The church-size paragraph interrupts the cadence at the wrong moment.

### 6. Cognitive Load Modifier: 0

The sermon is accessible. The structure is clear, the illustrations are local and concrete, and the argument can be followed by any attentive hearer. No reduction applied.

**Band: Concerning Gaps** — The sermon explains and illustrates, but does not fully preach. Significant revision needed, primarily in gospel proclamation and expositional depth.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily myself and my motives. Christ appears but does not dominate. The ethical argument runs largely on self-examination rather than Christ-exaltation.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about how to relate to others and to God. The weight of God’s presence is glimpsed in the closing question but not sustained throughout.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** Some conviction in the closing appeal; some comfort in the Spurgeon quote’s urgency for the lost. Primarily, though, the sermon informs and instructs.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Congregational specificity in application**: The Rami/hat and Holly/hand stories are textbook examples of grounded, particular application. The preacher knows his people and speaks to their actual lives. This is the sermon’s best quality and its most practically useful section.
2. **Closing gospel appeal**: The final paragraph — standing before the omniscient God — has genuine urgency and clarity of invitation. The "he KNOWS you" construction is effective. For a congregation that includes unbelievers, this is a real and specific call.
3. **Structural accessibility**: The four-question scaffold is simple, memorable, and traceable. A first-time visitor, a teenager, or a new believer could follow this sermon without difficulty.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is present but not proclaimed**: The closing appeal names Jesus as Savior, but the substance of what Christ accomplished — his death as substitutionary atonement, his resurrection, justification by faith alone — is entirely absent. A listener would know to "believe in Jesus" without knowing what Jesus did or why it was necessary. This is a structural failure in gospel centrality, not a stylistic quibble.
2. **The text’s own theological argument is bypassed**: Romans 14’s controlling claim is that Christ’s lordship — "for to this end Christ died and lived again, that he might be Lord both of the dead and of the living" (v. 9) — is the grounds for mutual forbearance. This verse is the theological center of the passage and it receives no development. The sermon floats on top of the text’s ethical surface without descending into its theological foundation.
3. **The church-size tangent breaks the sermon’s unity**: The paragraph beginning "In a related note there has been some talk about us being small..." is pastorally understandable but hermeneutically unjustified and structurally damaging. It arrives just as the sermon should be tightening toward its conclusion, introduces a new concern, and dissipates the momentum built by the "two teams" challenge.

### 11. Blind Spots

- The preacher likely does not see how thoroughly the sermon’s ethical argument can stand without Christ. The four questions ("Am I convinced? Am I doing this for the Lord? Will it stand? Will I cause stumbling?") are sound, but they could be answered by a serious Muslim or devout Jew as readily as by a Christian. The specifically Christian answer — that it is the lordship of the crucified and risen Christ, and our union with him, that grounds all of this — is the missing load-bearing wall.
- The sermon’s strength in illustration may be masking its weakness in exposition. The stories are so vivid and relatable that they carry the listener through sections that have not actually done the exegetical work. The Grace illustration in particular does the heavy lifting that the text should be doing.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The four-question structure feels like a solid expositional outline — and in one sense it is organized around the passage. But the questions are not Paul’s questions; they are the preacher’s synthesis of Paul. The difference matters: Paul’s argument in Romans 14 is not "ask yourself four questions before you act" — it is "because Christ is Lord of the living and the dead, neither judge your brother nor cause him to stumble." The outline imposes a personal-examination framework on a passage whose primary movement is theological and communal. The structure’s clarity creates the impression of tight text-work when the text is actually being summarized from a distance.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Romans 14:17 — "For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking but of righteousness and peace and joy in the Holy Spirit" — is one of the richest verses in the chapter and is entirely absent from the sermon. This verse reframes every disputable matter in light of the kingdom, and it connects the present ethical argument to the eschatological reality that the church inhabits. It would have given the sermon precisely the theological altitude it currently lacks, and it is native to the text rather than imported. The sermon also passes over v. 9 ("to this end Christ died and lived again, that he might be Lord both of the dead and of the living") — the verse that makes the gospel necessary to the ethical argument rather than merely appended to it.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach verse 9 before the ethics**: Before the four-question structure, preach Romans 14:9 as the theological foundation — "Christ died and rose to be Lord of the living and the dead." Then show how the four questions are not self-improvement exercises but flow from that lordship. This transforms the ethical argument from moralistic into gospel-grounded.
2. **Name what Christ did in the closing appeal**: The closing paragraph is close to excellent. Add two sentences: what Christ did (died in our place, rose from the dead), and why that matters (because that is what reconciles us to the God before whom we stand). "Believe in Jesus" requires "here is what Jesus did and why it was enough."
3. **Cut or relocate the church-size paragraph**: Move it to announcements or a separate pastoral word. Where it sits, it fractures the closing momentum. The "two teams" challenge is strong; let it land without interruption.
4. **Press the Grace/Opie application to the self-righteous hearer**: The condemning-woman stories (Rami’s hat, Holly’s hand) imply a specific class of sinner in the room. Name them directly: "Some of you are the woman who said something to Rami. Some of you know exactly what I’m talking about. The question is not whether you were technically right — the question is whether it was for the Lord." Corner that hearer. The text demands it.
5. **Add Romans 14:17 as a third illustration anchor**: Introduce it at the transition between "will it stand the test?" and "will I cause others to stumble?" — show that what is actually at stake in these disputable matters is the visibility of the kingdom of God: "righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit." This gives the application sections a theological ceiling they currently lack.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered. The sermon is not doctrinally cold — it has genuine warmth, pastoral investment, and personal urgency. The weakness is in expositional depth and gospel proclamation, not in spiritual temperature.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The Spurgeon quote and the closing "he KNOWS you" paragraph carry genuine eternal gravity, but the sermon as a whole does not yet sustain the weight of the text it is opening — the lordship of Christ over the living and the dead deserved a heavier sermon than this one delivered.
