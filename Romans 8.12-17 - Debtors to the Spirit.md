## Opening Illustration: The Mini-Golf Course in Japan

I heard a story this week about a man I'm sort of related to who tried to open a mini-golf course in Japan.

Japan, as you know, has miniature everything — they specialize in miniaturizing technology. They are the people who fit an entire computer inside of a wristwatch. They have hotels where guests sleep in individual pods — capsule hotels — each person climbs into their own narrow sleeping compartment.

*[Note: Japanese capsule hotels (カプセルホテル) consist of individual sleeping pods roughly the size of a twin bed, stacked in rows. They are individual rather than shared — the "four or five people in a dresser" description captures the spirit of their compact scale rather than the literal arrangement.]*

Part of this is due to the urbanization of Japan — more people, less space, more emphasis on efficiency. Missionaries use the word "dehumanization" for this effect, because people's personal bubbles shrink — the natural zone of comfortable space between yourself and another person shrivels up and disappears.

*[Note: In general missiology and urban anthropology, crowding-related reduction of personal space is a documented phenomenon. The term "dehumanization" in missiology usually carries a stronger meaning — stripping people of human dignity. The pastor is using it loosely to refer to the compression of personal space, which is a recognized cultural challenge in dense urban mission contexts.]*

In Paris, if a gap opened between you and the person in front of you in line, the French would assume you were no longer in line and step right in. Similar dynamics exist in Japan. And yet — with all their emphasis on miniaturization — they still played on full-size golf courses.

So this sort-of cousin by marriage decided to take out a loan, move to Japan, and open a mini-golf course. He convinced a bank to lend him a couple of million dollars, packed up his belongings, and went. The property alone cost over a million. Once he purchased it he took out a second loan to ensure he had capital to keep things going for a year or two. Technology and advertising cost most of the second million.

He opened on a day when everyone was on holiday from work — his consultants' recommendation — and a grand total of three people turned out. Five million US dollars, and three people turned out. Two hundred fifty thousand dollars in advertising, and three people turned out.

As he came to find out over the next three years, the Japanese simply didn't care about mini-golf.

The amount of money he owed kept him up at night. He held things together for the first couple of years, spending everything he had — first the loan proceeds, then his personal savings, then his credit cards. He spent the last thousand dollars on a plane ticket back to the United States. He came home broke, in debt beyond any amount he could ever hope to pay back, without the several hundred thousand he had saved before the venture, and had to live with his father for a few years until he could get back on his feet.

This man owed a debt he could never hope to pay. Hold that thought — we'll come back to it.

## Before Christ We Were Debtors to the Flesh

Before Christ, we were debtors to the flesh. Likewise, this man owed money to a US bank and a Japanese bank — millions upon millions of dollars. More than some of us may have earned across an entire lifetime. True of us spiritually as well.

The debt was enormous. Even one sin is enough to separate us from God for eternity, and the cost of reconciliation was beyond our reach. We may as well have owed five hundred trillion dollars, because we were never going to come close to paying it back.

Romans 3:23 — all have sinned. Because of that sin we deserved death. What we deserved was eternal separation from God. What we deserve is hell.

*[Note: Romans 3:23 says "for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." The verse establishes the universality of sin and separation from God's glory, but does not state "we deserved death." That consequence — "the wages of sin is death" — is Romans 6:23. The pastor moves immediately from 3:23 to 6:23 content. Both are true; they are separate verses.]*

Romans 6:23: "the wages of sin is death." Praise God we don't get what we deserve.

**Romans 8:13** echoes this — while we live by the flesh, we will die. Living by the flesh means living for what you want, what you desire, what you feel like. Culture says "if it feels good, do it" or "you only live once" — in almost every instance that philosophy encourages you to live for the flesh. Scripture says, and I believe based on the lives I've seen invested in that philosophy, that the logical end of that way of life is death. Not just physical death — everyone dies physically — but something more, something deeper: eternal separation from God.

**God made a better world for us.** He made it perfect and safe. We took that safe place and ruined it through sin. We broke the natural order of things. We took what he had made in his image and shattered it. We, in essence, rejected him and his plan for us.

When people ask, "If God is good and all-powerful, why didn't he make a world where people don't starve to death, die of AIDS or cancer, where children aren't abused and sold into sexual slavery?" — they are missing one critical point. God *did* create the world to be that way. To be perfect. But we — through Adam, and through every sin we've ever committed and will commit — rejected that world. We rejected perfection and embraced sin and death.

**The trading-down illustrations:**
- Satan convinced Adam and Eve — and convinces us every time we sin — that the broken, fallen, sin-filled world where we reject Christ is better than God's perfect, holy version.
- He got us to trade our Porsche for a Yugo.
- We traded a perfectly trained Grand Prix dressage horse, worth hundreds of thousands, for an untrained, elderly Shetland pony with an attitude and bad ground manners.
- We traded a 747 for a soggy paper airplane.
- We traded the best God had to offer for the worst mankind could conceive.

So when people ask how a good God could create a world with sin and death and heartbreak — the simple answer is that he didn't. He created something perfect. We rejected it and chose this instead.

We deserve death. We ruined something perfect — a reflection of God manifest on the earth. And we continue to reject him every time we do something he wouldn't want us to.

*[Illustration: A "friend" who would wound you, act repentant, restore the friendship, and then deliberately wound you again — who at the end of their friendship stole the pastor's parents' identity and had their utilities disconnected.]*

That is exactly how we treat God when we return to our sin. Caveat: for those who believe in Jesus Christ and are saved, we cannot do something so bad that we lose our salvation — but returning to sin is still a slap in the face of Christ. For those who don't know Christ, there is no way to reconcile yourself on your own.

It is like Monopoly. Everyone has drawn the Chance card: "Go to Jail. Go directly to jail. Do not pass Go. Do not collect $200." Everyone living by the flesh has chosen that card over Boardwalk and Park Place.

## Adopted by the King

Fortunately, God loves you too much to leave you there.

> *"But God demonstrates his own love for us in this: While we were still sinners, Christ died for us."*
> — Romans 5:8 ✓

> *"For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord."*
> — Romans 6:23 ✓

> *"If you confess with your mouth the Lord Jesus Christ, and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*
> — Romans 10:9 ✓

While we deserve death, Jesus Christ bought our freedom by dying on the cross. We deserved hell. He was perfect and didn't deserve any of what he went through — and yet he took our penalty as he hung there dying for you and for me. The only way to have your debt paid is if someone else pays it.

**Back to the mini-golf story.**

He came back to the US in failure, debt to his eyeballs, destitute, feeling broken. He met with his father and explained what happened: "It wasn't like we thought, Dad. I lost it all. I can't pay it back. I'm going to have to declare bankruptcy."

His father said: "Don't fret. I have a plan."

The next day he didn't see his father until that evening. The son had been moping about — feeling bad, knowing it was the result of his own decisions. The least he could do was make his dad supper, and so he did. His dad was a man of orderly habits — came home at a set hour — so the son had dinner ready in anticipation. The dad came home, grateful. They sat down and blessed the food and began eating together.

The son brought it up: "Dad, you said last night you had a plan." Thinking his dad had probably been at the lawyer drawing up bankruptcy papers.

His dad said: "Yes. Your debt is erased. You're free."

The son sat there stunned. "I'm — what?"

"Free. You don't owe any more money."

"You mean I don't owe anything to Bank of America?"

"No — you don't owe any more money. Your credit card is paid. Your loan from the Bank of Japan, your loan from Bank of America. You've been redeemed."

---

It is like that for us too. Verse 15 says we have received a spirit of adoption, so that we can call God *Abba* — which means Father. Not only did he pay our debts, but he adopted us. We are made heirs to his kingdom. Heirs with Christ.

**A few things I want you to notice.**

**Verse 15 — we are adopted.** Think about the process of adoption. There is paperwork, you learn about the child, you show up and meet them, you talk with them — and then you choose. You choose.

There is a doctrine called election. One of the major texts supporting it comes from Romans 8:29–30 — just past today's passage. Most people know Romans 8:28: "all things work together for the good of those who love God." But many are less familiar with 29 and 30.

*[Read Romans 8:29–30]*

Like parents who choose a child to adopt, God too chooses us. That is a very Sunday school thing to say, but let that wash over you for a moment. The King of Kings, Lord of Lords, ruler of all rulers. The omnipotent God of the universe who uses the heavens as a footstool. Who knows you — flaws and all — has chosen you.

He chose Kurt knowing everything about Kurt's past. He chose Kurt in spite of everything he knew Kurt would do — not because of anything inherently special about Kurt. From Ephesians 2:8–9: it was a result of God's grace that he chose Kurt, not as a result of anything Kurt had done or not done.

If you're here tonight, I believe God has you here for a reason. He loves you. He has a plan for you. He has chosen you for a purpose — for something good.

Romans 10:9: if you confess with your mouth and believe in your heart that Jesus is Lord and that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved. Hopeless to hope. *"I once was lost but now I'm found, was blind but now I see"* — as the old hymn says.

*[Note: "Amazing Grace," written by John Newton (1772). ✓]*

## Salvation Secure — Romans 8:31–39

Look at two sections in particular: verse 31 and verses 37–39.

**Verse 31** — "If God is for us, who can be against us?"

The God who in six twenty-four-hour days imagined and created everything we've spent five to ten thousand years trying to understand. The God who uses the heavens as a footstool. The one who loved you so much he sent his son to die for you. That God is on your side.

**Verse 32** — if he didn't spare his own son for you, is there anything you could ask for that he would withhold? Obviously that doesn't apply to things that will hurt you — he still does what is right, especially when we want something that isn't.

**Verses 37–39** — nothing can separate you from him.

Once you are saved, you are signed, sealed, and delivered. The only way to stop that is by being stronger than God — and until you can speak time and the universe into existence, you need to accept the fact that you are not. No creation has ever been stronger than its creator. Especially not when your Creator can imagine the Grand Canyon and speak it into existence.

For those who believe in God: your salvation is secured. You and I deserved to spend eternity in hell. But God had other plans — he chose us, he has a purpose for us, and his purpose cannot be thwarted. Not by life or death, height or depth, rulers or angels or demons, things present or things to come, not by powers nor by anything else in all creation. Nothing will be able to separate you from God.

But all of that starts with accepting a free gift from God.
