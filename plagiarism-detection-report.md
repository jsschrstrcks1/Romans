### 6.1 External Plagiarism Findings

#### ⚠️ ITEM 1: "Sin will keep you from the word, or the word will keep you from sin"

**Appears in:** Hebrews 3 (line 62), Hebrews 1 (line 44)
**Status:** Used without attribution in both sermons as if it were the preacher's own line.
**Actual source:** This is a well-known quote from **John Bunyan** (1628–1688), author of *Pilgrim's Progress*. Reportedly found handwritten in the cover of his Bible: "Either this book will keep you from sin, or sin will keep you from this book." Also widely attributed to **D.L. Moody**. John Piper has cited it attributing it to his mother's inscription in his childhood Bible.
**Verdict:** This is the clearest instance of unattributed external content in the corpus. The quote is famous enough that knowledgeable listeners would recognize it. **Should be attributed to Bunyan (or at minimum acknowledged as a well-known saying) in future use.**

#### ⚠️ ITEM 2: David and Svea Flood missionary story (Galatians 6)

**Appears in:** Galatians 6 — Sowing and Reaping (lines 17–33)
**Status:** Told as a detailed narrative with no source attribution.
**Actual source:** This is a widely published missionary story. The primary source is **Aggie Hurst's** own published account (*Aggie: The Inspiring Story of a Girl without a Country*, Access Publishing). It has been popularized by **Randy Alcorn** through Eternal Perspective Ministries, and retold by countless preachers. The specific details in the sermon (boy selling eggs, Svea's death at 27, white cross, 600 believers, 110,000-person national church) closely match the Alcorn/Hurst version.
**Verdict:** This is not plagiarism in the malicious sense — retelling well-known missionary stories is standard preaching practice. However, a story this detailed and this well-documented should credit its source (e.g., "As told by Aggie Hurst" or "Randy Alcorn shares the story of…"). **Add attribution when reusing.**

#### ⚠️ ITEM 3: Titanic oranges illustration (Galatians 6)

**Appears in:** Galatians 6 — Sowing and Reaping (line 12)
**Status:** Told without source attribution.
**Actual source:** This is a widely circulated sermon illustration found in multiple collections including *10,000 Sermon Illustrations* (source unknown). Charles Swindoll has retold it. A historical kernel may trace to **Major Arthur Peuchen**, a Titanic survivor who left $300,000 in cash and securities to take three oranges (documented in Marshall Everett, ed., *Wreck and Sinking of the Titanic*). Most sermon versions feature an unnamed woman rather than Peuchen.
**Verdict:** Common homiletical illustration with no single "owner." No attribution required, but acknowledging it as a well-known story (e.g., "There's a famous story from the Titanic…") would be good practice.

#### ITEM 4: "Blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church" (IDOP)

**Appears in:** IDOP transcript (line 68)
**Status:** Used without attribution.
**Actual source:** **Tertullian**, *Apologeticus* ch. 50 (c. AD 197).
**Verdict:** This phrase has entered common Christian parlance to the point where attribution is not strictly necessary. However, it is a direct quote from a named author. Noting "as Tertullian said" would be best practice.

## SECTION 7: Conclusion

### No Plagiarism Found

After reading every Romans sermon file (45+ files across root and Other-Sermons/), searching for cross-sermon content reuse, and verifying attributions:

1. **No external plagiarism detected.** The pastor does not present others' words as his own. Quotes are attributed. External sources are cited. Even AI-generated content (ChatGPT definition) is transparently acknowledged.

2. **No unattributed sermon borrowing.** The sermons do not reproduce content from published sermon series by other pastors (MacArthur, Sprgeon, Sproul, etc.) without attribution. The MacArthur and Sproul files in `quotes-and-references/` are clearly labeled as reference material, not passed off as original.

3. **Recycled illustrations are normal.** The reuse of stories (Bob Clark, Chip Hall, Anthony Cox), quotes (Spurgeon "leap to hell," Owen "slight thoughts"), and theological formulations ("hidden with Christ in God") across multiple sermons is standard preaching practice. Preachers draw from a consistent repertoire. This is not plagiarism.

4. **Duplicate files are a housekeeping issue**, not an integrity issue. The Other-Sermons/ directory contains copies of root-level files, and some sermons exist in both raw and polished forms. This is consistent with an active working repository.

### Recommendations

1. **Clean up duplicate files** — Many files in `Other-Sermons/Sermons/` are exact copies of root-level files. Consider whether the Other-Sermons/ copies serve a purpose or should be removed to reduce confusion.

2. **Verify flagged quotes** — The ⚠️ flags already in `Romans 7.md` are good practice. Apply the same rigor to:
   - A.W. Pink wrath quote (specify source work)
   - Martin Luther quotes in `romans 15(2)` (specify source letters/documents)
   - McCheyne quote in `romans 15(2)` (specify source letter)
   - Origen quote in `Romans 13b` (specify commentary)

3. **Track illustration reuse** — The `sermon-map.md` already does this well. Continue updating it as new sermons are added to avoid unintentional over-repetition of the same illustrations (especially Spurgeon "leap to hell" which appears in 12+ sermons).

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Attribution discipline is exceptional**: The report's executive summary states "no external plagiarism detected" across 45+ files — and the evidence supports it. The transparent citation of ChatGPT-generated content (Romans 3 wrath), the correct attribution of Rupertus Meldenius (correcting the common misattribution to Augustine), and the consistent sourcing of Owen, Pink, Calvin, and Edwards all demonstrate integrity that many preaching ministries lack.
2. **Illustration variety**: The corpus contains illustrations from military history (Bannockburn, Gulf War, Navajo Code Talkers), science (Tesla resonance, nuclear fine-tuning), literature (Pilgrim's Progress), contemporary missions (David and Svea Flood, Aggie Hurst), daily life (garlic uncle, gold bar in glass box), and personal experience. This breadth serves a diverse congregation well.
3. **Self-correction culture**: The correction log for "The Measuring Stick" (2 Timothy 3) demonstrates a preacher willing to flag his own errors publicly — the overstated Chicago Statement claim, the inaccurate 1689/Westminster relationship, the Psalm 119 summary errors. This is a rare and valuable form of pulpit integrity.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The Bunyan attribution gap** (Section 6.1, Item 1): "Sin will keep you from the word, or the word will keep you from sin" — used in two sermons without attribution — is the single most important integrity finding in the report. Bunyan's words carry their own authority. Presenting them as the preacher's own diminishes both Bunyan and the preacher, and erodes the congregation's trust if they recognize the source. This must be corrected before either sermon is used again.
2. **Citation drift on heavily-used quotes**: The A.W. Pink wrath quote appears in 6+ sermons but the source work is "not specified in all instances." The Martin Luther quotes in romans 15(2) have "no specific source documents." The McCheyne quote has "no specific letter/sermon cited." Sporadic citation is worse than consistent citation because it implies the quotes are verified when some are not. The preacher should either source them all or acknowledge them as "commonly attributed to."
3. **The David and Svea Flood story** (Section 6.1, Item 2): Told with granular detail — the boy selling eggs, Svea's death at 27, the white cross, 600 believers, 110,000 national church — without naming Aggie Hurst or Randy Alcorn as the sources for that specific telling. Detailed, published missionary stories with traceable authorship deserve attribution. The report is correct that this is not malicious plagiarism, but it is the kind of careless borrowing that trains congregations not to ask where stories come from.

### 11. Blind Spots

The report focuses on *external* attribution — whether the preacher is crediting others' words. It does not assess whether the sermons are *theologically dependent* on sources in a way that is not disclosed. For example, if multiple sermons follow the structural outline of a MacArthur or Lloyd-Jones commentary without acknowledgment, no word-level plagiarism has occurred — but the congregation is receiving another man's theological framework without knowing it. The report's scope is appropriate for its stated purpose, but this deeper dependency is invisible to a word-level audit.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The report's conclusion — "No plagiarism found" — is both accurate and potentially misleading. "No plagiarism" is a floor, not a ceiling. A preacher can be entirely original and entirely unfaithful to the text. The absence of plagiarism says nothing about the presence of exposition, gospel clarity, or pastoral force. The report correctly scopes itself to integrity; the risk is that "clean plagiarism report" becomes a proxy for "good sermons," which it is not.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The report catalogs *what* is reused (Spurgeon quote, 12 sermons; Calvin quote, 10 sermons; Pilgrim's Progress, 7 sermons) but does not ask *whether these reuses are effective*. The question for a preaching ministry is not only "Did you credit Spurgeon?" but "Is the Spurgeon quote doing work each time, or has it become a verbal tic?" The report recommends tracking reuse to avoid "unintentional over-repetition" — which is the right instinct — but the diagnostic question underneath that recommendation is: does the congregation now hear the Spurgeon quote and stop thinking? That would be a sermon-quality failure invisible to a plagiarism audit.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

For the *sermons this report describes*:

1. **Hebrews 3 and Hebrews 1**: Add attribution for the Bunyan/Moody "Sin will keep you from the word" quote before either sermon is used again. The standard form: "As John Bunyan reportedly wrote in his Bible, or as D.L. Moody put it..."
2. **Galatians 6**: Add one sentence attributing the Flood missionary story: "Aggie Hurst tells the story in her memoir — and Randy Alcorn has helped make it widely known — of a missionary family..."
3. **Create a master citation file**: The report already recommends verifying flagged quotes. Extend this to a standing document that records verified source text, page number, and edition for the Pink wrath quote, the Luther quotes in romans 15(2), the McCheyne letter reference, and the Origen commentary. This protects the preacher and serves the congregation.
4. **Audit the Calvin "factory of idols" citation**: It is cited with Institutes I.xi.8 in Romans 1b. Verify that the same precision is added in the 9+ other sermons where it appears without the Institutes reference.
5. **Retire or replace the Spurgeon "leap to hell" quote in lower-priority sermons**: At 12 appearances, this quote has likely lost its power for the regular congregation. Reserve it for sermons where it is uniquely suited and use other material elsewhere.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

The "Thus Says the Lord" rubric does not fit this document. It was designed to evaluate a completed sermon manuscript — a text written to be preached before a congregation from an opened Bible. A plagiarism detection report is a repository integrity audit.
