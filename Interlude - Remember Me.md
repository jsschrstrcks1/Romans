# Remember Me: How Grace Teaches Us to See the Cross Clearly

**A Devotional on Luke 23:39–43**

> "And he said, 'Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom.'"
> — Luke 23:42 (ESV)

---

## Opening: Why Theology Matters in a Moment Like This

There is a quiet lie that floats around Christian circles — especially devotional ones.

It says: *Doctrine is for classrooms. Devotion is for the heart.*

But Scripture never makes that separation.

In fact, the deepest moments of devotion in the Bible are fueled by the clearest moments of theology.

The thief on the cross is often treated as proof that theology doesn't matter:
- "He didn't know much."
- "He just had simple faith."
- "All he did was believe."

But when we slow down — and let good theology guide our reading — we discover something very different.

This man's faith is the product of astonishing theological clarity, given by grace.

That's exactly the kind of clarity *A Journey in Grace* presses us toward: not colder faith, but truer faith — faith that sees God as He actually is.

---

## The Text: Luke 23:39–43

Two men.
Same crosses.
Same pain.
Same proximity to Jesus.

But radically different outcomes.

Why?

---

## Point 1: Grace Teaches Us to See Ourselves Rightly

The first thief speaks with entitlement:

> "Are you not the Christ? Save yourself and us!" (Luke 23:39)

This is not disbelief — it's demand.

He wants rescue without repentance, deliverance without lordship.

The second thief, however, says something theologically loaded:

> "Do you not fear God, since you are under the same sentence of condemnation? And we indeed justly, for we are receiving the due reward of our deeds; but this man has done nothing wrong." (Luke 23:40–41)

This man has:
- A doctrine of God's holiness — *"Do you not fear God?"*
- A doctrine of human sin — *"We indeed justly..."*
- A doctrine of divine justice — *"...receiving the due reward of our deeds"*

That's not shallow faith. That's regenerate sight.

This is Romans 1–3 in a single breath. The mirror Paul holds up for fourteen chapters — "None is righteous, no, not one" (Romans 3:10) — this dying criminal has already seen it. He knows he belongs on that cross. He knows the sentence is just.

Sproul repeatedly emphasizes in *A Journey in Grace* that grace begins by stripping us of self-justification. Until we see that God is just and we are guilty, grace makes no sense.

Good theology doesn't puff us up — it puts us in our place.

And that's exactly where grace meets us.

---

## Point 2: Grace Teaches Us to See Christ Rightly

Now comes the most staggering moment in the passage:

> "This man has done nothing wrong." (Luke 23:41)

Remember where Jesus is:
- Beaten
- Mocked
- Bleeding
- Publicly executed

Everything visible screams failure.

And yet the thief confesses Christ's sinlessness.

This is not emotional optimism.

This is theological precision.

Even more astonishing:

> "When you come into your kingdom..." (Luke 23:42)

A dying man sees a coming reign.

Where did that come from?

Not from the crowd — they were mocking.
Not from the disciples — they had fled.
Not from the religious leaders — they were sneering.

And certainly not from the scene itself. Jesus doesn't look like a king. He looks like a criminal.

But the thief sees what the crowd cannot.

Paul will later write to the Romans: "For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes" (Romans 1:16). The cross looks like weakness. It looks like defeat. But to those with eyes to see, it is the power of God.

This is where good theology matters most.

Without categories like:
- Christ's kingship
- Christ's innocence
- Christ's authority beyond death

...this confession collapses.

Sproul insists that grace does not bypass the mind — it renews it. The Spirit doesn't give us vague religious feelings; He gives us truth-shaped vision.

The thief sees what the crowd cannot because grace has taught him to think rightly about Christ.

---

## Point 3: Grace Teaches Us How to Ask Rightly

Notice what the thief does *not* ask for:
- Relief from pain
- Rescue from consequences
- A second chance at life

He asks for remembrance.

> "Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom." (Luke 23:42)

This is covenant language. Old Testament language. Kingdom language.

He casts himself entirely on Christ's mercy and authority.

This is exactly what *A Journey in Grace* emphasizes: Saving faith is not bargaining with God — it is resting in who God is.

The thief contributes nothing.
Promises nothing.
Negotiates nothing.

Paul puts it this way: "For while we were still weak, at the right time Christ died for the ungodly... God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us" (Romans 5:6, 8).

The thief was ungodly. Christ died for the ungodly. The math works.

And Jesus responds — not as a fellow sufferer, but as a King:

> "Truly, I say to you, today you will be with me in paradise." (Luke 23:43)

No delay.
No uncertainty.
No conditions.

"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus" (Romans 8:1). The thief heard that verdict before Paul ever wrote it down.

Grace does not wait for improvement — it pronounces verdicts.

---

## The Mystery: Why One and Not the Other?

Here is where we must tread carefully — and worshipfully.

Two men. Same crosses. Same proximity to Jesus. Same opportunity.

One mocks. One believes.

Why?

Paul wrestles with this very question in Romans 9:

> "So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy." (Romans 9:16)

The thief didn't out-think the other criminal. He didn't have better information. He didn't make a wiser choice by sheer willpower.

He saw what no human eye could naturally see: a dying man was a reigning King.

That sight was a gift.

Consider: this man may have been part of the same band of insurrectionists as Barabbas. The same Greek word — *lēstēs* — is used for Barabbas in John 18:40 and for the two men crucified with Jesus in Matthew 27:38. These weren't petty thieves. They were likely revolutionaries, bandits, murderers.

And one of them, in humanity's darkest hour, pledges allegiance to a naked, dying man — and calls Him King.

That is not natural.

That is sovereign grace.

---

## The Night and the Dawn

There's one more detail we dare not miss.

Luke tells us: "It was now about the sixth hour, and there was darkness over the whole land until the ninth hour" (Luke 23:44).

The thief's confession happened in literal darkness.

The sun itself had failed. The world had gone black. And in that midnight moment, a dying criminal saw what no one else could see — and staked his eternity on it.

He went from darkness to paradise in a single afternoon.

From midnight to dawn.

This is the journey Paul describes in Romans 13:12 — "The night is far gone; the day is at hand."

For the thief, the night ended that very day. He heard the words no religious system could ever promise: *Today. With me. Paradise.*

---

## Conclusion: Why Good Theology Deepens Devotion

If we strip theology away from this passage, we get a vague moral:

*"It's never too late."*

But when theology is allowed to do its work, we get something far richer:
- We see sovereign grace at work
- We see regeneration before reform
- We see faith as a gift, not a contribution
- We see Christ as King, even on a cross
- We see the mirror of Romans 1–3 in one man's confession
- We see the promise of Romans 8:1 in one sentence from a dying Savior

Good theology doesn't cool our love for Christ — it anchors it.

It keeps us from sentimentalizing grace.
It keeps us from domesticating the cross.
It keeps us from imagining we saved ourselves.

The thief on the cross doesn't minimize doctrine — he proves it.

Because only grace-trained eyes could look at a crucified man and say:

*You are a King. Remember me.*

And only a sovereign Savior could answer:

*You're coming with me.*

---

## Closing Prayer

Lord, teach us to see rightly.
Strip us of shallow thoughts about You.
Give us minds shaped by truth and hearts moved by grace.
May our devotion grow deeper as our theology grows truer.
And may we never look at the cross without seeing the King who reigns from it.
Amen.
