# Remember Me: How Grace Teaches Us to See the Cross Clearly

**A Devotional on Luke 23:39–43**

> "And he said, 'Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom.'"
> — Luke 23:42 (ESV)

---

## Opening: Why Theology Matters in a Moment Like This

There is a quiet lie that floats around Christian circles — especially devotional ones.

It says: *Doctrine is for classrooms. Devotion is for the heart.*

But Scripture never makes that separation.

In fact, the deepest moments of devotion in the Bible are fueled by the clearest moments of theology.

The thief on the cross is often treated as proof that theology doesn't matter:
- "He didn't know much."
- "He just had simple faith."
- "All he did was believe."

But when we slow down — and let good theology guide our reading — we discover something very different.

This man's faith is the product of astonishing theological clarity, given by grace.

That's exactly the kind of clarity *A Journey in Grace* presses us toward: not colder faith, but truer faith — faith that sees God as He actually is.

---

## The Text: Luke 23:39–43

Two men.
Same crosses.
Same pain.
Same proximity to Jesus.

But radically different outcomes.

Why?

---

## Point 1: Grace Teaches Us to See Ourselves Rightly

The first thief speaks with entitlement:

> "Are you not the Christ? Save yourself and us!" (Luke 23:39)

This is not disbelief — it's demand.

He wants rescue without repentance, deliverance without lordship.

The second thief, however, says something theologically loaded:

> "Do you not fear God, since you are under the same sentence of condemnation? And we indeed justly, for we are receiving the due reward of our deeds; but this man has done nothing wrong." (Luke 23:40–41)

This man has:
- A doctrine of God's holiness — *"Do you not fear God?"*
- A doctrine of human sin — *"We indeed justly..."*
- A doctrine of divine justice — *"...receiving the due reward of our deeds"*

That's not shallow faith. That's regenerate sight.

This is Romans 1–3 in a single breath. The mirror Paul holds up for fourteen chapters — "None is righteous, no, not one" (Romans 3:10) — this dying criminal has already seen it. He knows he belongs on that cross. He knows the sentence is just.

Sproul repeatedly emphasizes in *A Journey in Grace* that grace begins by stripping us of self-justification. Until we see that God is just and we are guilty, grace makes no sense.

Good theology doesn't puff us up — it puts us in our place.

And that's exactly where grace meets us.

---

## Point 2: Grace Teaches Us to See Christ Rightly

Now comes the most staggering moment in the passage:

> "This man has done nothing wrong." (Luke 23:41)

Remember where Jesus is:
- Beaten
- Mocked
- Bleeding
- Publicly executed

Everything visible screams failure.

And yet the thief confesses Christ's sinlessness.

This is not emotional optimism.

This is theological precision.

Even more astonishing:

> "When you come into your kingdom..." (Luke 23:42)

A dying man sees a coming reign.

Where did that come from?

Not from the crowd — they were mocking.
Not from the disciples — they had fled.
Not from the religious leaders — they were sneering.

And certainly not from the scene itself. Jesus doesn't look like a king. He looks like a criminal.

But the thief sees what the crowd cannot.

Paul will later write to the Romans: "For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes" (Romans 1:16). The cross looks like weakness. It looks like defeat. But to those with eyes to see, it is the power of God.

This is where good theology matters most.

Without categories like:
- Christ's kingship
- Christ's innocence
- Christ's authority beyond death

...this confession collapses.

Sproul insists that grace does not bypass the mind — it renews it. The Spirit doesn't give us vague religious feelings; He gives us truth-shaped vision.

The thief sees what the crowd cannot because grace has taught him to think rightly about Christ.

---

## Point 3: Grace Teaches Us How to Ask Rightly

Notice what the thief does *not* ask for:
- Relief from pain
- Rescue from consequences
- A second chance at life

He asks for remembrance.

> "Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom." (Luke 23:42)

This is covenant language. Old Testament language. Kingdom language.

He casts himself entirely on Christ's mercy and authority.

This is exactly what *A Journey in Grace* emphasizes: Saving faith is not bargaining with God — it is resting in who God is.

The thief contributes nothing.
Promises nothing.
Negotiates nothing.

Paul puts it this way: "For while we were still weak, at the right time Christ died for the ungodly... God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us" (Romans 5:6, 8).

The thief was ungodly. Christ died for the ungodly. The math works.

And Jesus responds — not as a fellow sufferer, but as a King:

> "Truly, I say to you, today you will be with me in paradise." (Luke 23:43)

No delay.
No uncertainty.
No conditions.

"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus" (Romans 8:1). The thief heard that verdict before Paul ever wrote it down.

Grace does not wait for improvement — it pronounces verdicts.

---

## The Mystery: Why One and Not the Other?

Here is where we must tread carefully — and worshipfully.

Two men. Same crosses. Same proximity to Jesus. Same opportunity.

One mocks. One believes.

Why?

Paul wrestles with this very question in Romans 9:

> "So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy." (Romans 9:16)

The thief didn't out-think the other criminal. He didn't have better information. He didn't make a wiser choice by sheer willpower.

He saw what no human eye could naturally see: a dying man was a reigning King.

That sight was a gift.

Consider: this man may have been part of the same band of insurrectionists as Barabbas. The same Greek word — *lēstēs* — is used for Barabbas in John 18:40 and for the two men crucified with Jesus in Matthew 27:38. These weren't petty thieves. They were likely revolutionaries, bandits, murderers.

And one of them, in humanity's darkest hour, pledges allegiance to a naked, dying man — and calls Him King.

That is not natural.

That is sovereign grace.

---

## The Night and the Dawn

There's one more detail we dare not miss.

Luke tells us: "It was now about the sixth hour, and there was darkness over the whole land until the ninth hour" (Luke 23:44).

The thief's confession happened in literal darkness.

The sun itself had failed. The world had gone black. And in that midnight moment, a dying criminal saw what no one else could see — and staked his eternity on it.

He went from darkness to paradise in a single afternoon.

From midnight to dawn.

This is the journey Paul describes in Romans 13:12 — "The night is far gone; the day is at hand."

For the thief, the night ended that very day. He heard the words no religious system could ever promise: *Today. With me. Paradise.*

---

## Conclusion: Why Good Theology Deepens Devotion

If we strip theology away from this passage, we get a vague moral:

*"It's never too late."*

But when theology is allowed to do its work, we get something far richer:
- We see sovereign grace at work
- We see regeneration before reform
- We see faith as a gift, not a contribution
- We see Christ as King, even on a cross
- We see the mirror of Romans 1–3 in one man's confession
- We see the promise of Romans 8:1 in one sentence from a dying Savior

Good theology doesn't cool our love for Christ — it anchors it.

It keeps us from sentimentalizing grace.
It keeps us from domesticating the cross.
It keeps us from imagining we saved ourselves.

The thief on the cross doesn't minimize doctrine — he proves it.

Because only grace-trained eyes could look at a crucified man and say:

*You are a King. Remember me.*

And only a sovereign Savior could answer:

*You're coming with me.*

---

## Closing Prayer

Lord, teach us to see rightly.
Strip us of shallow thoughts about You.
Give us minds shaped by truth and hearts moved by grace.
May our devotion grow deeper as our theology grows truer.
And may we never look at the cross without seeing the King who reigns from it.
Amen.

A Doctrinally Rich Devotion That Stops Short of the Pulpit

**2. One-Sentence Summary**
A theologically careful meditation on Luke 23:39–43 that illuminates sovereign grace with genuine clarity, but — being a devotional rather than a sermon — never summons the congregation to respond, never presses the conscience with distinguishing force, and ends at instruction rather than exhortation.

**3. Berean Gate**
- **Scopus Test**: PASS. The main thesis — that grace teaches us to see rightly (ourselves, Christ, and how to ask) — arises organically from the passage's own movement through the second thief's confession. The one-sentence summary is demonstrably the text's argument.
- **Authority Test**: PASS. The preacher/author stands under the text. The passage's hard implications (sovereign election, one saved and one not, Romans 9:16) are not softened — they are named and pressed, at least intellectually.

**4. Fatal Flaw Flags**
- **Flag 5 — No actual exhortation**: TRIGGERED. The devotional explains beautifully and applies cognitively, but never issues an imperative. The congregation is never told what they must now believe, repent of, obey, or pursue. The closing prayer is passive — it asks God to do what the preacher should have demanded of the hearer. There is no "therefore" moment.
- **Flag 4 — Application is entirely generic**: BORDERLINE. The application ("Good theology deepens devotion") is sound but remains at the level of principle. No specific hearer category is addressed with particularity. The politically confident, the doubter, the nominally religious, the backslider — none are cornered.

**8. Core Override Questions**
1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* More of Christ. This is one of the piece's genuine strengths. The thief's trajectory from self-justifier to confessor mirrors the reader toward Christ, not self-performance.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Mostly ideas, clearly handled. The doxological conclusion gestures toward encounter, but the devotional's dominant mode is instruction. God is present, but the density of theological commentary keeps Him at arm's length from the affections.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Primarily informed. The piece communicates truth about grace without pressing the reader to ask, *"Have I seen myself as this thief did?"*

**9. Strengths (Top 3)**
1. **Sovereign grace handled with courage**: The "Mystery" section (Romans 9:16, the *lēstēs* note, the refusal to reduce the thief's faith to a wiser choice) is theologically honest and confessionally grounded. Many devotionals on this passage soften this question. This one doesn't.
2. **The three-point structure is genuinely exegetical**: "See yourself rightly, see Christ rightly, ask rightly" is not imposed on the text — it arises from the second thief's own movement through vv. 40–42. This is text-governed structure.
3. **The *lēstēs* / Barabbas connection**: The note that this man may have been a revolutionary-bandit, not a petty thief, using the same Greek word as John 18:40 — this is a genuine exegetical contribution that lands with force. The image of a murderous insurrectionist pledging allegiance to a naked, dying man and calling him King is indelible.

**10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)**
1. **No exhortation, no imperative, no "therefore"**: The conclusion lists six doctrinal benefits of good theology and closes with a prayer. It does not tell the reader what to do. What does the grace-trained sight the thief had require of me? Repent of self-justification? Look at the cross right now and confess Christ's kingship over what I am currently avoiding? The devotional stops at description and never becomes demand.
2. **The conscience is never cornered**: The first thief — who demanded rescue without repentance — is analyzed but never used to search the room. Every audience contains people who want God's benefits without God's lordship. This text gives the preacher/author direct access to that hearer, and the opportunity is left on the table.
3. **The mechanism of forgiveness is absent**: How does the thief go from criminal to paradise? Sovereign grace is rightly named. But *why* can Christ say "today you will be with me in paradise"? The cross as substitution — Christ bearing the condemnation the thief deserved — is the very event happening at that moment. The Romans 8:1 echo at the end ("no condemnation") is used decoratively without explaining why condemnation is removed. The "math" of the atonement is gestured at ("The thief was ungodly. Christ died for the ungodly. The math works.") but not shown.

**11. Blind Spots**
The author is confident in the doctrinal content and has done genuine exegetical work, but appears unaware that a devotional that never issues an imperative has not yet become proclamation. The closing prayer reveals this: asking God to give readers grace-trained eyes is exactly right as petition, but the preacher should also have *demanded* that hearers look at the cross right now with whatever eyes they have and confess what this man confessed. The devotional is satisfied with illuminating truth; it has not yet felt the pastoral urgency to press it home.

**12. Illusion of Strength**
The Sproul references create the illusion of applicatory weight. Because a named authority is invoked to drive each main point, the piece feels like it is pressing the reader. But Sproul's assertions are doing the application work that the author should be doing in first-person address to the congregation. "Sproul insists that grace does not bypass the mind" tells the reader what Sproul thinks. "The Spirit has renewed your mind — or He has not. Which thief are you?" tells the reader what they must face. The former is citation; the latter is preaching.

**13. Missed Opportunity**
The darkness of Luke 23:44 is named and used as image, but the theological weight of it is left undeveloped. This darkness is not merely atmospheric — it is the sign of God's judgment falling on the Son, the hours in which the penalty of hell is being absorbed at the cross. The thief's confession happens *inside* the moment of substitutionary atonement, not merely adjacent to it. The thief hears Christ's promise of paradise while Christ is bearing the curse that would otherwise be the thief's. That simultaneity — paradise promised while hell is being absorbed — is the most explosive moment in the passage and it goes unpreached.

**14. Surgical Fixes**
- After Point 3 ("Grace Teaches Us How to Ask Rightly"), add a direct address to the hearer: *"Some of you are the first thief right now. You want God to intervene — in your marriage, your health, your finances — but you are not confessing that you deserve nothing from him. The first thief's demand is the demand of every unbroken heart."*
- In the conclusion, add a single exhortatory paragraph before the closing prayer: *"So what must you do? Look at the cross. Not at the devotional. Not at Sproul. Look at him. And say what this man said: 'I deserve nothing. You are a king. Remember me.' That is not a prayer to work up. It is a cry to be given. Ask him for the eyes to say it."*
- Develop the penal substitutionary logic in "The Mystery" section — the thief was promised paradise at the very moment the curse he deserved was being absorbed by the man next to him. Make that explicit.
- Remove or abbreviate the Sproul citation-as-application pattern. Let the text do what the book's author is being credited for.
- The closing prayer is beautiful; place it *after* an exhortatory paragraph, not in place of one.

**15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag**
Not triggered (the score pattern does not meet the Dead Orthodoxy threshold — this is not a case of high exposition paired with low gospel; the gospel scores are reduced by absence of mechanism, not by coldness). However, the piece is in a related danger: *warm orthodoxy that stops short of proclamation.* The devotion is not bloodless — the doxological register is genuinely present. But it mistakes theological illumination for pastoral summons.

**16. Weight of Glory Assessment**
The piece carries real doctrinal gravity and genuine doxological warmth, but it does not yet reach the threshold where a man who has been with God would weep — it is a clear candle, not a fire.

**17. Rubric Fit Note**
This rubric is designed for sermon manuscripts and strains noticeably against a devotional/study guide format. Categories 5 (Exhortation & Response) and 8 (Sermonic Force) are structurally disadvantaged by the genre — a devotional is not written to be preached aloud, and it is not obligated to issue imperatives in the same mode a sermon is. The Berean Gate and theological categories apply naturally. The applicatory and exhortatory categories expose real absences, but those absences are partly genre-inherent. If this piece is intended to function as a sermon or be preached from, the surgical fixes above are mandatory. If it is intended to function as a written devotion for private or group study, the threshold for "exhortation" is lower — though even devotional writing benefits from an imperative moment.
