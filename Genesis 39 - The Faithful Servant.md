Last week we were in Genesis 38 — Judah's chapter. And it wasn't a pretty one.

We tracked through his life. He found a wife for his oldest son, Er. God saw how wicked Er was and took him home early. His second son Onan died the same way. So Judah told his daughter-in-law Tamar to go back to her parents' house and wait for the youngest son, Shelah, to grow up. He never followed through.

Tamar figured that out. So she hatched a plan. Judah had a problem with prostitutes, and she apparently knew this. She stationed herself on the road he was traveling, covered her face, and when he propositioned her she agreed — but demanded his signet, cord, and staff as a pledge until the promised goat arrived. He agreed, sent a servant to pay the girl later, and she couldn't be found.

Here's the detail I mentioned last week — the one that stuck with me. When the servant went looking for her, he asked the locals about the *cult prostitute* on the road. Not just a prostitute. A cult prostitute. In Canaan that meant a woman associated with the worship of Baal or Asherah — one of those carved sticks of wood we talked about a year or two ago. By sleeping with her, at least in the eyes of his community, Judah wasn't just breaking his marriage vows. He was worshipping another god. Not the God of Abraham and Isaac. The gods of Canaan. Maybe that's connected to the idols Rachel stole from her father when Jacob's family fled. We don't know for certain. But I think it's safe to say Judah was not walking with the Lord that day.

One commentator put it this way: Joseph had been sold into Egypt with Judah's approval, but Judah was himself a slave — to sin.

That contrast is exactly what the text wants you to see. You wonder why Joseph was elevated above his brothers? He wasn't sleeping with prostitutes. He never voted to sell his brother into slavery. And most importantly — he had a relationship with the one true God.

**Faithful in Service**

Joseph is kind of like Aunt Dottie.

We don't say it enough, but she does so much around here. She stays in contact with people — keeps in touch with folks who've moved out to Ohio, or down to Missouri. She keeps track of who misses and usually sends out a copy of her notes from the sermon. She tracks prayer requests. She watches for changes to the law that can affect us. And the bulletin each week — and on and on the list goes.

That is faithful service. Showing up. Doing the work. Week after week, whether or not anyone notices.

Joseph had that same quality — but with something extra. Everything he put his hand to was being blessed by God. I imagine if Potiphar had put him in charge of the tomatoes, he'd have brought in a bumper crop. If he'd been put in charge of the school, the kids would have gone home with straight A's. If he'd been managing Potiphar's portfolio, he pulled out of Enron before the collapse. Whatever he was responsible for, the Lord blessed it.

So much so that Potiphar eventually put him in charge of everything. Potiphar was no fool. He watched God bless everything Joseph touched, and the logical conclusion was to make Joseph responsible for more things.

That's the reward for faithful service. Not always a promotion. Not always immediate. But faithful work done unto God does not go unnoticed — by God, or by the people he has placed around you.

---

**Faithful in Self-Control**

But Joseph had another quality. Let me read starting in verse 7.

*[Read Genesis 39:7–10.]*

A couple of commentaries I read pointed out that adultery was a serious crime in Egypt at this point in history. Hammurabi's code — the ancient law code from Mesopotamia, roughly contemporary with this period — prescribed death by drowning for both parties caught in adultery. The same commentaries noted, though, that women of wealthy and powerful families were well known for breaking this particular law. Potiphar's wife may have understood that his position protected her.

None of that excuses what she did. This was a daily assault on Joseph's integrity. She wasn't subtle. She wasn't occasional. This went on day after day. Every time Joseph showed up to do his job, she was there with the same proposition.

And Joseph said no.

There were a hundred reasons to say yes. She was his master's wife. Saying no could cost him his life. Saying yes would have been easy. And no one who knew him back home would ever have known.

But Joseph wasn't thinking about who was watching back in Canaan. He was thinking about who was always watching.

*"How then can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?"*

Sin against God. Not just against Potiphar. Against God.

It finally builds to the day she grabs his cloak off his back — and Joseph does the only sensible thing left. He runs. He left the coat and got out of the house.

Now — I've heard it said before that sexual immorality is the one sin in Scripture we are specifically told to flee. There's something to that. But notice what made Joseph run. He hadn't been fleeing temptation. He had stood his ground against temptation for a long time. What made him run was that she put her hands on him and wasn't letting go. At that point there was only one option.

He left the coat.

That's the whole story. That's his answer to three years of daily pressure. He left the coat.

Someone said it well: *Joseph lost his coat, but he kept his character.*

Paul says make no provision for the flesh — don't put yourself in the position where the temptation has a foothold to begin with. And again: *"Flee youthful passions and pursue righteousness, faith, love, and peace."* — 2 Timothy 2:22.

The old preachers were right about the conclusion. If there is someone in your life pulling you toward your spouse's opposite — toward the thing you've been fighting — the answer is not willpower. The answer is distance. Cut off the access. Run toward your husband. Run toward your wife. Most importantly, run toward God.

Joseph could have given in. It would have been easier. But he had been with God too long to throw it away for a moment.

**Faithful in Suffering**

Let me read the last section. Verse 19.

*[Read Genesis 39:19–23.]*

Potiphar had to respond. His wife brought him a story, and she had the coat as evidence. His anger was kindled.

But notice what he does. Egyptian law — and the ancient law codes of the region — prescribed death for adultery. Joseph should have been executed. Instead, Potiphar throws him in jail. Not just any jail — the king's prison, the place where Pharaoh's personal servants were confined.

Scholars debate why. Maybe Potiphar had doubts about his wife's story. Maybe putting Joseph on public trial would have raised questions he didn't want raised. Whatever his reasons, Joseph ends up in a better outcome than the law strictly allowed.

Even what looked like a miscarriage of justice was being steered.

No prison three thousand years ago was particularly nice. Many of them were caves — stone floors, no light, no running water. The intent wasn't reform. It was deterrence. You wanted people to see what prison looked like so they never ended up there.

Joseph lands here having done nothing wrong. He served faithfully. He ran when running was the only thing left to do. And now he's in a cell.

And the text says the Lord was with him. Again. Still.

The keeper of the prison saw what Potiphar had seen. And he did what Potiphar had done — put Joseph in charge of more things. Eventually Joseph is responsible for all the other prisoners. Everything the keeper of the prison was responsible for, Joseph was responsible for.

Same four words, same result: the Lord was with him.

Whatever God was building in Joseph's life, he was building it here. Not despite the prison. Through it.

So let me ask — which of these two men do you most resemble tonight?

Maybe you are like Joseph. You've been walking with God for years. Showing up, doing the work, holding the line. And you find yourself in a season that looks exactly like that prison — doing everything right and still sitting in a cell. I want you to know that the Lord is with you there. He is not confused about your situation. He has not made a mistake. What he is building, you may not be able to see yet.

Or maybe tonight you're more like Judah. A slave to something — lust, money, TV, power, position, authority, the need for people's approval. Maybe it's none of those. It doesn't matter what it is. If it isn't Christ, it will disappoint you. One day you will stand before God and give an account for your life.

Friends — it doesn't matter whether people like you. What matters is whether your name is written in the Lamb's Book of Life.

But there is hope. We deserved what Judah deserved. All of us. A sentence we could not survive. But God sent his Son — who willingly took our death sentence in our place. Because he was fully man and fully God, death had no permanent claim on him. Three days later he walked out of the tomb. Over five hundred people saw him — 1 Corinthians 15:6. He ascended to heaven and is seated at the right hand of the Father.

If you call on him tonight, his death covers yours.

Romans 10:9: *"if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."*

Pray that. Mean it. And you will be saved.
