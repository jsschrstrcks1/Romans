# There and Back Again
## Isaac's Journey to the Brink of Death to Save the World
### Study Notes on Genesis 22

*These are academic study notes, not a preached sermon.*

---

**Main Idea (What was the main point to Abraham?):**
God wanted Abraham to demonstrate his faith in a practical way, and through that to depict what he would allow his own one and only Son to go through.

**Theme (What was the biblical author talking about?):**
Talking in narrative about events in Abraham's life that caused him to exhibit faith to the brink of sacrificing his one and only son, while at the same time modeling events that would happen in the life of Christ.

---

### The Seams

**Genesis 22:1-2 — God Asks for the Sacrifice**

God asks Abraham for a sacrifice — his son, his only son — which mirrors God's own sacrifice for our sins (John 3:16). Rabbi Schwarz quotes the Midrash saying that this is a dig from God at Abraham. Abraham has two sons, but he favors one over the other. In fact, at his wife's request, he ran Hagar and his other son Ishmael off.

The Hebrew word *veha'alehu* has at least two meanings. First, it can be translated "to bring" or "raise up." The second definition can mean "to sacrifice" — more specifically, as I understand it, a burnt offering.

---

**Genesis 22:3-6 — The Three-Day Journey**

Abraham does as he is commanded, seemingly without hesitation — contrary to what he did to save Lot, and his pleading with God for Ishmael. Together with some servants they walk for three days (which mirrors Christ's three days in the tomb — see Hebrews 11:19, "figuratively speaking, he did receive him back") to the mountain ridge Christ would later be sacrificed upon.

- **Isaac carries his own wood**, as our Lord carried the cross.
- **Abraham carried the implements of his son's death**, even as God's sovereignty makes him responsible for Christ's death.
- **The fire** might be symbolic of judgment (hell).
- **The two servants left behind** could mirror our relationship with Christ's sacrifice. All of the details are not known of what happened between and within the Godhead. Parts of it are private — between God and Christ. So it was with Abraham and Isaac. Parts of it the servants were allowed to observe and participate in, but they did not have full-fledged, integral, critical parts in this part of the story.

---

**Genesis 22:7-8 — "God Will Provide Himself a Lamb"**

Isaac could have fought back (as could have Christ). Several scholars — J. Vernon McGee and rabbinic tradition — place his age in his 30s. He was born when Sarah was 90, and in the following chapter (23) she dies at 127.

Isaac calls out to his father, as Christ called out to his Heavenly Father from the cross. Abraham says something that predicts the immediate and distant futures — whether he realizes it or not — regarding atonement:

> "God will provide for himself the lamb for a burnt offering, my son."
> — Genesis 22:8 (ESV)

---

**Genesis 22:9-14 — The Altar, the Ram, the Provision**

Abraham built the altar upon which he was to kill his son, which mirrors God's sovereignty through the crucifixion of Christ (Isaiah 53).

- **The ram was caught in the thicket** — Christ wore a crown of thorns.
- **The ram represented life for Isaac** — without the ram, Isaac would have been dead. The same should be said for believers: without Christ's death, we would receive what we deserve (hell).
- **God provided salvation** in the physical sense for Isaac through the ram, but through his Son — the Lamb — he provided salvation in the eternal sense in the future.
- **Jehovah-Jireh** ("The LORD will provide") is meant to remind us of God's provision for his people.

---

**Genesis 22:15-19 — The Abrahamic Covenant**

The covenant promises:
1. He would be blessed
2. He would have a multitude of descendants
3. Some of his descendants would rise to positions of great earthly power
4. Through his "seed" the world would be blessed

Jesus is a descendant of Abraham and blessed the world with salvation.

**Hebrew note:** "It is worth noting that the root of the Hebrew word *vayashav* is the same as the word for 'repentance,' *teshuva*" (Schwarz, p. 3). *Vayashav* also carries within its semantic range the idea of restoration — of human relations, spiritual relations — as well as repetition (Strong's #07725, שׁוּב *shub*).

---

**Genesis 22:20-24 — News from Haran**

Abraham is told (passive voice — not the angel; the angel's speech ends at v.18) that his brother Nahor has had children back in Haran (Gen 11:31; cf. Gen 24:10). The text names nephews (Nahor's sons) and a grand-niece, Rebekah (Bethuel's daughter, v.23).

**Additional note:** According to Rabbi Schwarz, the word for "returned" in Hebrew can also mean "repent."

#### Corrections Applied
1. ⚠️ **"mirrors Christ's time on the cross"** → corrected to **"mirrors Christ's three days in the tomb"** with Hebrews 11:19 cross-reference. Christ was on the cross ~6 hours, not three days. The three-day journey parallels death-to-resurrection, which is the typological connection Hebrews 11:19 makes explicit.
2. ⚠️ **"dying"** listed as a meaning of Strong's #07725 → removed. *Shub* does not include "dying" in its standard lexical range. Its semantic range includes return, restore, repent, recover, repeat.
3. ⚠️ **"The angel tells Abraham"** (v.20) → corrected. The ESV reads "it was told Abraham" (passive voice, unspecified source). The angel's speech ends at v.18.
4. ⚠️ **"nieces and nephews, and great-nieces, in Ur"** → corrected to **nephews and a grand-niece in Haran**. The text lists Nahor's sons (Abraham's nephews) and Rebekah (Bethuel's daughter = Abraham's grand-niece). No nieces are named in the text. The family is in Haran (Gen 11:31, Gen 24:10), not Ur — Terah's family left Ur and settled in Haran.

#### Notes for Precision
- **Isaac's age**: Calculates to 37 (Sarah 127 minus 90), not vaguely "in his 30s." However, this calculation assumes the Aqedah occurred near Sarah's death — this is midrashic tradition (Seder Olam Rabbah), not explicit Scripture. Genesis 22 does not state Isaac's age. The notes say "30s" which is defensible but should be presented as inference, not fact.
- **J. Vernon McGee** placing Isaac in his 30s: from *Thru the Bible* commentary, widely cited but speculative. Consistent with rabbinic tradition.
- **"Pleading with God for Ishmael"**: Genesis 17:18 is a single-verse plea ("Oh that Ishmael might live before you!"), not extended pleading. The extended pleading is for Sodom (Gen 18:23-33). "Pleading" slightly overstates the Ishmael case but the point — that Abraham argued/negotiated before but not here — is valid.
- **"Mountain ridge Christ would later be sacrificed upon"**: Moriah (Gen 22:2) is identified with the Temple Mount (2 Chr 3:1). Golgotha is nearby on the same ridge system but not the identical spot. This is traditional Christian typological identification, widely held but not proven archaeologically.
- **"Positions of great earthly power"** (covenant promise #3): The ESV text says "your offspring shall possess the gate of his enemies" (Gen 22:17). "Positions of great earthly power" is a paraphrase — accurate in thrust but less precise than the text.
- **Ram / crown of thorns typological connection**: The ram was caught "by his horns" in the thicket (Gen 22:13). The connection to Christ's crown of thorns is a common homiletical observation and valid typology, but should be noted as typological parallel rather than textual assertion.
- **Rabbi Schwarz**: Specific publication unidentified. The Midrashic content cited is consistent with known rabbinic commentary on Genesis 22 (Midrash Rabbah, Rashi). Cannot verify exact citation without the source document.

### 5. Score Breakdown

*Scores not assigned; narrative diagnostic substituted.*

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics** — *Strong foundation; typological method sound.*
The notes track the narrative carefully and segment it at natural seams. The Hebrews 11:19 citation is exactly right — the author of Hebrews himself establishes the typological precedent, which means the notes are not imposing a Christological reading but following one already authorized by the canonical interpreter. The Fact-Check Log within the document shows that the preacher caught and corrected overreach in his own typological claims (e.g., "three days in the tomb" vs. "on the cross"). The distinction between what Scripture asserts and what is "common homiletical observation and valid typology" (on the ram/crown of thorns parallel) is a discipline that will serve a sermon well.

The main weakness at the expositional level is that the Fallen Condition Focus is unnamed. The passage exists to address something in the human condition. The notes identify God's purpose (testing Abraham, prefiguring Christ) and Abraham's response (faith without hesitation), but they do not name what the passage was written to cure — what hole the grace of God is filling for the original audience and the congregation who will hear it preached.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity** — *Theologically careful and confessionally sound.*
Penal substitution is correctly affirmed. The typological connections are handled as types (not allegorical identifications), which preserves both the historical reality of the events and their forward-pointing significance. The Abrahamic covenant is correctly identified as culminating in Christ ("Jesus is a descendant of Abraham and blessed the world with salvation"). The Jehovah-Jireh observation correctly interprets God's provision as a revelation of his character toward his people. No theological slippage.

The one area for development: the notes observe God's sovereignty through the crucifixion (citing Isaiah 53) but do not dwell on what that sovereignty means for the believer. The doctrine is noted; the doxology is absent.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force** — *Present as framework; absent as proclamation.*
The gospel is the structural purpose of the notes — the typological connections all point toward Christ's substitutionary death. But the gospel is framed observationally ("the ram represented life for Isaac... without Christ's death, we would receive what we deserve") rather than proclaimed. A listener could follow the argument and understand the parallelism without ever being confronted with the question of whether they are trusting that Lamb. The cross is the subject of the notes; it has not yet become the gravity of the sermon.

The Synagogue Test would likely pass in the notes' current form — a devout non-Christian could observe the typological parallels with interest and not be offended, because the cross is discussed as historical event and theological framework, not as personal urgency.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force** — *Absent.*
This is the largest gap. The notes observe what Isaac's experience typologically represented but never press the question of what the hearer must do with it. Perkins' taxonomy of hearers is entirely unaddressed. The person who has never trusted the Lamb of God is not spoken to. The believer who needs to see again the certainty of God's provision is not comforted. The backslider who has drifted from the God who provides is not confronted.

**5. Exhortation & Response** — *Absent.*
No call to response is present anywhere in the notes.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow** — *Appropriate for notes; needs reconception for preaching.*
The "seams" structure — following the narrative's own sections — is a reasonable organizational principle for a Genesis sermon. Narrative sermons often track the text's own movement. However, a sermon cannot simply advance through the verses; it must build toward a climax. The notes as structured would produce a survey, not a sermon. The question a sermon on Genesis 22 must answer is: where is the peak of the narrative, and what is the homiletical equivalent of standing at the brink of the knife descending and hearing the angel call out? The notes do not yet identify that moment or build toward it.

**7. Illustration & Imagination** — *Implicit in the typology; needs development.*

That is a genuine insight and it is stated with appropriate restraint. "Whether he realizes it or not" is good theology.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The typological parallels are listed, not developed.** The bullet list structure under each seam ("Isaac carries his own wood — as our Lord carried the cross") is appropriate for notes but deadly for sermons. Each parallel is stated and abandoned. A sermon needs to dwell — to let the Isaac-carrying-wood moment land, to give the congregation time to feel the weight of it, before moving on. Currently the notes produce a catalogue of typology rather than an experience of it.

2. **The Fallen Condition Focus is entirely absent.** These notes do not identify what the passage was written to address in the human condition. Was Genesis 22 written to address doubt about God's faithfulness? Fear in the face of impossible demands? The temptation to withhold the best from God? The question of whether God can be trusted when he asks for everything? None of these are named. Without identifying the FCF, there is no bridge between the exposition and the congregation — and the application, when it comes, will float.

3. **The main idea as stated is God-centered in the wrong direction.** "God wanted Abraham to demonstrate his faith" frames the passage primarily around Abraham's performance — what God wanted him to do. But the passage's climax is not Abraham passing the test; it is God providing the ram. The theological center of gravity in Genesis 22 is divine provision and the nature of substitution, not human faithfulness as demonstration. A sermon built on the stated main idea risks becoming a sermon about Abraham's faith as a model, with Christ as a distant parallel — which is moralism decorated with typology.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The notes are attentive to what Isaac's experience parallels in Christ's but do not ask what Abraham's experience reveals about God the Father. The passage is not only about a son offered — it is about a father who offers. The anguish of Genesis 22:2 ("your son, your only son, whom you love") is not only designed to show Abraham's faith; it is designed to show what God himself bore when he did not spare his own Son. John 3:16 is cited in passing but its weight as commentary on the Father's love is not explored. A sermon on Genesis 22 that only sees the Isaac-Christ parallel has missed half the typology.

Additionally, the notes do not engage what the passage means for assurance. If God provided the ram to spare Isaac, what does that mean for the believer's confidence that God will provide? The "Jehovah-Jireh" note is made but the pastoral consolation — "the God who provided on Moriah is the God who has provided on Calvary, and will provide for you" — is not drawn out.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The typological parallels, when listed in bullet form, can create the impression that the sermon is rich because the comparisons are numerous. But breadth of typology is not depth of preaching. A sermon that observes seven parallels without developing any of them will feel busy but empty. The congregation will leave knowing that Isaac is like Jesus in several ways, but will not have encountered the God who provides — which is the point of the passage. The list creates the appearance of exegetical richness while bypassing the encounter that makes preaching preaching.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The moment the angel calls out in Genesis 22:12 — "Do not lay your hand on the boy or do anything to him, for now I know that you fear God, seeing you have not withheld your son, your only son, from me" — is the hinge of the entire narrative. The knife is raised. The moment of silence before the angel speaks is the silence before provision arrives. That moment is not developed in the notes. A sermon on Genesis 22 should build to that moment, dwell in it, let the hearer feel what Abraham felt in the instant before the ram was seen — and then pivot to the moment God did not stop his own hand, the moment no angel intervened, the moment the knife fell. The contrast between Moriah and Calvary is the heart of the passage, and it is absent from the notes.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (For Preaching Development)

These are conversion tasks — what must happen to transform these notes into a sermon:

- **Restate the main idea around divine provision, not human demonstration.** "God, who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, first showed us what that would cost him on the mountain in Moriah — where he stayed his own hand but did not stay his heart." The sermon's spine is substitution and provision, not faith as demonstration.
- **Name the Fallen Condition Focus.** Before outlining the sermon, answer: what in the human condition does Genesis 22 exist to address? Suggested candidate: our inability to trust God when he asks for everything, our terror that his demands exceed his provision — and his answer: he himself provides the lamb.
- **Develop two or three parallels deeply rather than seven lightly.** Candidates: Isaac carrying the wood / Christ carrying the cross (substitution); the ram in the thicket / the crown of thorns (provision through suffering); Abraham's anguish / the Father's giving of his Son (the cost of love). Cut the servants' observation or develop it into a full illustration of the hiddenness of redemption.
- **Build the sermon toward 22:12-13 as the climax.** The raised knife, the angel's voice, the ram in the thicket — these must be the homiletical peak, not just a seam in the outline.
- **Add the contrast: Moriah vs. Calvary.** "On Moriah, God stopped Abraham's hand. On Calvary, no angel came. The knife fell. The Son bore what Isaac was spared. And it was for you."
- **Add direct address to the doubter and the trusting believer.** "Some of you are on your own three-day journey right now. God has asked for something that feels like everything. And you do not see the ram yet. The name of this mountain is still Jehovah-Jireh — the LORD will provide. He has provided the Lamb. He will provide for you."
- **End in worship.** The sermon on Genesis 22 should leave the congregation wanting to kneel, not just to understand. Jehovah-Jireh is not an academic observation about God's character — it is the cry of a man who came back down the mountain alive, with his son, knowing what God had just shown him. End there.
