We’ve just stood on the heights of Romans 8, hearing Paul thunder: “Nothing can separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord.” But now—without taking a breath—he plunges into a valley of anguish. Romans 9 begins not with triumph, but tears: “I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart” (v. 2). He looks at his people, Israel—given every privilege, every promise, even the Messiah Himself—yet most have rejected Christ. His theology isn’t abstract; it breaks his heart.

Lets open with a word of prayer. **Sovereign Lord, **King of glory, whose throne is everlasting and whose mercy endures forever, we come to You with heavy hearts, grieving with Charlie Kirk’s family and all who mourn the loss of a faithful witness. Their tears cry out like Paul’s for his kinsmen (Romans 9:1–3), and like the souls beneath Your altar who cry, *“How long, O Lord, holy and true, before You judge and avenge our blood?”* (Revelation 6:9–10). Yet we trust Your unchanging purpose.
You are the Judge of all the earth—righteous and fierce in justice—who promises to repay the wicked (Romans 12:19). But You are also the Father who binds up the brokenhearted (Psalm 147:3). Comfort Charlie’s wife, child, and family as they walk through this valley of sorrow. Let them know You as their light and salvation, and calm their fears (Psalm 27:1).
We confess our own struggle: we rejoice in Your sovereign choice and covenant promises (Deuteronomy 7:6–8), yet we wrestle when those we love wander far from You. Teach us to pray earnestly and to trust Your Spirit to call Your elect effectually (John 6:37). Forgive us when we shrink back from proclaiming the gospel, unlike Your servant who spoke with such boldness. Ignite in us the same courage, that we might lift our voices like trumpets for Christ (Isaiah 58:1).
Your Word never fails (Romans 9:6). Your Son has triumphed, Satan has been defeated, and Your chosen ones are secure forever. Until Christ returns, sustain us through our tears and strengthen our trust, so that in every circumstance we may glorify You.
Amen.

Church, we feel that ache this week. Charlie Kirk’s murder Tuesday—a bold gospel voice, a brother in Christ—stuns us, as mockers jeer online at an image-bearer’s death. I felt it at my uncle’s funeral, sharing the gospel with the lost. Life is fragile, short. And yet, we often nod at God’s choice of Israel while crying, ‘Why not my loved ones, Lord?’ Hold that tension. If we’re tempted to answer venom with our own, or sit unmoved by such evil, Scripture calls higher: “In your anger, do not sin” (Eph. 4:26).
But we grieve, not as those without hope (1 Thess. 4:13). Paul’s sorrow didn’t undo his trust in God’s promises, nor should ours. When Israel rejects their Messiah, when evil seems to triumph, when blood spills on a campus, God’s word has not failed. It cannot fail. His justice will not be mocked. His mercy will not be thwarted. His grace relentlessly pursues those He has chosen, as we’ll see in Jacob’s election today. Jesus remains our Savior and King.
As Spurgeon said, “God’s election is always of grace… according to the sovereign will of God.” Let’s hold Paul’s tears and God’s trust together as we see three truths:
Paul’s anguish for the lost (vv. 1–5).
God’s word has not, cannot, will not fail (vv. 6–9).
Election is God’s purpose, not man’s works (vv. 10–13).
Good Christians wrestle differently with these truths, and we’ll see them in glory. But our goal is Scripture’s truth, not our preferences. If God’s Word never confronts us, we’re worshiping ourselves, not Him. So, church, let’s bow our hearts to God’s Word, trusting His higher ways, as we read Romans 9:1–13 to see His sovereign grace.
**Point I: Paul’s Anguish for the Lost (Romans 9:1–5)**
Paul begins not with debate, but with tears. Verse 1: *“I am speaking the truth in Christ—I am not lying; my conscience bears me witness in the Holy Spirit—that I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart.”*
This is a triple oath—Christ, conscience, Spirit—all called as witnesses. Why such solemnity? Because what he says in verse 3 is staggering: *“For I could wish that I myself were accursed and cut off from Christ for the sake of my brothers, my kinsmen according to the flesh.”*
Paul says he would trade places with Israel if it meant their salvation. He echoes Moses’ plea in Exodus 32:32, *“Blot me out of your book,”* and points ahead to Christ, who actually became accursed for sinners (Galatians 3:13).
A pastor shared this week: If the Holocaust happened today, he’d volunteer to go, sharing the gospel with captors and captives, hoping some be saved. That echoes Paul’s heart—wishing himself accursed for the lost (v. 3). Do we have such faith, like Christ dragging His cross up that terrible hill (Gal. 3:13) to drink down the cup of God’s wrath? Too often, we hesitate to cross the street for the gospel.
**Theology that weeps**

That’s the kind of theology Paul has here. Theology that weeps. Fresh off Romans 8’s soaring assurance, his high view of God’s sovereignty doesn’t harden him—it breaks him. His doctrine leads to tears, not arguments.
Spurgeon said: *“Brethren, if sinners will be damned, at least let them leap to hell over our bodies. And if they will perish, let them perish with our arms about their knees, imploring them to stay.”*

Bunyan said: *“The godly man’s sorrow for the lost is a reflection of God’s own heart, yet His **decrees remain unchangeable.”* What does your level of sorrow for the lost say about your heart? Does it reflect the heart of Christ?
And remember what Jesus Himself demanded of His disciples: *“If anyone comes to me and does not hate his own father and mother and wife and children and brothers and sisters, yes, and even his own life, he cannot be my disciple”* (Luke 14:26). Or as Matthew records it: *“Whoever loves father or mother more than me is not worthy of me, and whoever loves son or daughter more than me is not worthy of me”* (Matt. 10:37). Not literal hatred, but love in comparison. Our devotion to Christ must be so supreme that all other loves seem small beside it. Paul understood this. His supreme love for Christ overflowed into anguish for the lost—even his enemies.

*And maybe today you carry that sorrow** that anguish** in a very personal way. **A number** of** you saw the video of Charlie Kirk’s death, and it pierced you. You can’t unsee it. It left you with deep pain, regrets, even fear. Brother, sister, hear me: that grief is not foreign to Christ. He Himself wept at the tomb of Lazarus (John 11:35). He is the Man of Sorrows who carried our griefs (Isa. 53:4). Do not feel ashamed for being shaken. Paul knew “unceasing anguish.” And yet, Christ invites you to bring your trembling heart to Him, because He holds even this in His nail-pierced hands.*
**Israel’s privileges**
Israel had every advantage—*“**to them belong the adoption, the covenants, the law, the worship, the promises, the patriarchs, and from their race… the Christ, who is God over all, blessed forever”* (vv. 4–5). They were God’s sons and daughters (Exodus 4:22), chosen not for merit but because of His great love (Deuteronomy 7:6–8).
And Israel’s advantages mirror our own. As believers today, we hold immense privileges: the full Scriptures in our hands, the presence of the Spirit, the fellowship of the church. Yet privilege is no guarantee of salvation. Outward covenant blessings mean nothing without Christ Himself.
**Why it matters**
**Doctrine must break our hearts.** If our grasp of God’s sovereign election does not move us to anguish for the lost, we have missed Paul’s point.
**Privilege is not salvation.** We see God’s choice of Israel and nod, but our hearts ache when our children, our spouses, our neighbors resist Christ. Paul’s anguish teaches us to trust God’s grace even there. So pray earnestly, trusting His call reaches whom He wills (Eph. 1:4–5). He chose us in Christ before the world began, and His call reaches whom He wills. Being born into a Christian family or surrounded by church culture doesn’t save without Christ. We must teach our children the gospel, and we must live in a way that reflects the gospel. They must hear it not only from our mouths but also see it in our lives. What is your life preaching to your children right now about the worth of Christ?
**Christ is the fulfillment.** Reject Him, and you forfeit the promises; receive Him, and you gain all of them. As Paul said in Romans 2:28–29, *“No one is a Jew who is merely one outwardly… but inwardly, by the Spirit.”*
**Tie to earlier sermons**
In Romans 6 we asked, *“Whose banner flies over your life?”* Israel had God’s banner but lived under sin’s. And in Romans 5 we heard, *“While we were still sinners, Christ died for us.”* That heightens Paul’s sorrow here—Israel spurned the very Christ who bore wrath in their place.
**Application

**So I ask: Who breaks your heart? Your wandering child? Your unbelieving spouse? Your neighbor unmoved by the gospel? This week we’ve felt it in the murder of Charlie Kirk—a gospel voice silenced, mocked online. Like Paul, perhaps we’d trade places if we could. But we rest in the Judge who says, *“Vengeance is mine”* (Rom. 12:19). Comfort your heart: as the 1689 Confession says, God’s covenant of grace *“promises to give unto all those that are ordained unto eternal life, His Holy Spirit, to make them willing and able to believe”* (2LBCF 7.2). Your tears may join Paul’s, but God’s purpose stands firm, even in the teeth of rebellion.
And here’s the comfort Jesus Himself gives us: *“All that the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never cast out… this is the will of him who sent me, that I should lose nothing of all that he has given me, but raise it up on the last day”* (John 6:37, 39). John repeats it because God wants us to remember it. He wants it to sink deep into our bones: not one of His own will ever be lost. Paul’s anguish is real, and ours is too—but so is Paul’s hope. The salvation of our loved ones is never a hopeless situation. Because the Son of God never loses those the Father has given Him. The story’s end is written: Christ triumphs, Satan falls, and His elect are secure forever.
**Point II: God’s Word Has Not Failed, is not failing, will never fail. (Romans 9:6–9)**
Paul’s anguish for Israel’s unbelief raises a piercing question: *If God chose Israel, gave them covenants and promises, yet they reject Christ, has His word failed?*
Verse 6 answers with unshakable clarity: *“But it is not as though the word of God has failed. For not all who are descended from Israel belong to Israel.”*
This is the hinge of Romans 9. From the very beginning, God’s promises never rested on ethnic descent but on His sovereign purpose. There has always been a distinction between the children of the flesh and the children of the promise.
**Isaac and Ishmael (vv. 7–9)**
Paul proves this from Abraham’s family: *“Not all are children of Abraham because they are his offspring, but ‘Through Isaac shall your offspring be named.’ This means that it is not the children of the flesh who are the children of God, but the children of the promise are counted as offspring. For this is what the promise said: ‘About this time next year I will return, and Sarah shall have a son’”* (vv. 7–9).
Isaac’s birth was humanly impossible. Sarah was barren, Abraham was old. Yet God spoke, and life came (Gen. 21:12). This is salvation’s pattern: God’s word creates His people where no life should be. As Isaiah prophesied, *“My word… shall not return to me empty, but it shall accomplish what I purpose”* (Isa. 55:11).
So it is with us. As Paul says in Galatians 4:28: *“Now you, brothers, like Isaac, are children of promise.”* Not by flesh, not by will, but by God’s word that calls life from the dead.

**Monergism, not synergism**
Here’s Paul’s point: salvation is not man reaching up to God, but God reaching down to man. This is monergism—God alone saving—not synergism, where man cooperates. Isaac’s life came solely from God’s promise, not from human effort. So does our new birth.
Our 1689 Confession affirms: *“Those predestinated unto life, God… hath chosen in Christ unto everlasting glory, out of His mere free grace and love, without any other thing in the creature as a condition”* (2LBCF 3:5).
Ephesians 2 spells it out: *“You were dead in the trespasses and sins… but God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ”* (vv. 1, 4–5). Dead men don’t cooperate. Dead men don’t choose. God speaks, and the dead live. Paul says in Romans 4:16: *“The promise rests on grace and is guaranteed.”* If salvation rested on us, it would fail every time. Because it rests on Him, it cannot fail.

Martyn Lloyd-Jones said, *“God’s promise never fails, for it rests not on human lineage but on His divine choice.”* John Piper adds, *“The children of promise are those whom God has chosen to receive His mercy, not those who claim it by birthright.”* And John Owen reminds us, *“The greatest sorrow… is not to believe that he loves you.”* Paul grieves Israel’s unbelief, but he never doubts God’s word.

**Christ Himself Guarantees It**
Jesus declared in John 6: *“All that the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never cast out… This is the will of him who sent me, that I should lose nothing of all that he has given me, but raise it up on the last day”* (vv. 37, 39).
The Father chooses. The Son secures. The Spirit makes alive. That’s why God’s word cannot fail. Not one sheep is lost. Not one promise is broken.

*“And here is the wonder: Christ’s death was not a vague potential. He laid down His life for His sheep (John 10:11). He gave Himself for the church, to present her holy and blameless (Eph. 5:25–27). The atonement is definite — not limited in its power, but particular in its design. He died to **actually save** the very ones the Father chose. Election in eternity past and atonement at the cross are two sides of the same coin — the **Father chooses, the Son redeems, the Spirit applies. That’s why Paul can say with such confidence: God’s word has not **failed, and** never will.”** God’s will has never failed. Is not failing, will never fail, and indeed Cannot Fail. Because God himself is not/will not/cannot fail. *
**A Picture of Promise**
Picture a barren desert, lifeless and cracked. God speaks, and suddenly a stream flows, a garden blooms. That’s Isaac’s birth from Sarah’s womb. That’s salvation.
I remember a man—steeped in sin, no hope in sight. Everything you can name that is wrong, he was an expert at. Yet one day at the foot of the cross God’s word pierced his heart, and today he attends church every time the doors are open. Today his life is wildly different. Today he weeps over his sin. That’s monergism. That’s God creating life where none should be. And if you belong to Christ, that’s your story too.
Charles Spurgeon said: *“God’s promises are like the stars; the darker the night, the brighter they shine.”* Israel’s unbelief casts a shadow, but God’s word shines brighter still. *“The word of our God will stand forever”* (Isa. 40:8).
**Why It Matters**
Some of you grieve—Charlie Kirk’s murder, or perhaps a child far from Christ, or a spouse unmoved by the gospel. Hear Paul: God’s word has not failed. The God who spoke life into Sarah’s barren womb can speak life into the angriest rebel’s heart (Eph. 2:1, 4–5). Some of you know someone like that—resistant, hardened, seemingly unreachable. But remember, we all began the same way: dead in our trespasses and sins, by nature children of wrath. Yet God made us alive. And despite their fiercest rebellions, even the vilest apostasy and the boldest insurrection against heaven, Jesus will not lose one the Father has given Him (John 6:37). What looks like chaos and failure is not chaos at all, but victory in Christ. Because His victory is sure, let us not be silent. To honor Charlie’s legacy—and Christ’s glory—proclaim the gospel with fire in your bones (Isa. 58:1).
Privilege doesn’t save. Ishmael was Abraham’s son, but only Isaac was counted. Growing up in church, knowing Bible stories, or having Christian parents doesn’t make you a child of God. *“By grace you have been saved through faith… not of yourselves; it is the gift of God, not a result of works”* (Eph. 2:8–9). Parents, teach your children the gospel by word and life. Live as though God’s promises are true. You must be born again.
If you trust Christ, you are a child of promise, *“like Isaac”* (Gal. 4:28), not by accident but by God’s call. The God who gave Sarah a son gave you new life. As our Confession says, *“God hath decreed… all things, whatsoever comes to pass”* (2LBCF 3:1). And His decree guarantees your salvation until the last day.
**Application**
So church, trust God’s unfailing word. Grieve for the lost—your wandering child, your unbelieving friend, Charlie’s silenced voice—but don’t despair. His promise holds. His word cannot fail. Share the gospel boldly, for the God who called Isaac calls His elect today. Pray earnestly for those you love, resting in His sovereign purpose.
**Point III: Election Is God’s Purpose, Not Man’s Works (Romans 9:10–13)**
Paul has shown that God’s word never fails, running through the children of promise, not the children of flesh (vv. 6–9). But now he presses deeper, silencing every objection to God’s sovereign choice. Someone might say, *“Of course Isaac was **chosen—Ishmael** was Hagar’s son, not Sarah’s.”*
So Paul turns to Jacob and Esau: *“And not only so, but also when Rebekah had conceived children by one man, our forefather Isaac, though they were not yet born and had done nothing either good or bad—in order that God’s purpose of election might continue, not because of works but because of him who calls—she was told, ‘The older will serve the younger.’ As it is written, ‘Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated’”* (vv. 10–13).
**Election Before Birth**
Jacob and Esau shared the same parents, same womb, same heritage—no distinction in lineage. And yet before they were born, before they had done anything good or bad, God declared: *“The older will serve the younger.”*
Why? Paul answers: *“In order that God’s purpose of election might continue, not because of works but because of him who calls.”*
This is unconditional election. God’s choice is not based on merit, works, or even foreseen faith. It rests on His sovereign will. As Paul says elsewhere: *“He chose us in him before the foundation of the world… in love he predestined us”* (Eph. 1:4–5).
John MacArthur explains: *“God’s choice is not based on what we do but on His eternal purpose, fixed before time began.”*

**God’s Freedom in Love**
Paul quotes Malachi: *“Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated”* (Mal. 1:2–3). These are strong words—not the language of human emotion, but of covenant. God set His redeeming love on Jacob and passed over Esau.
Here’s where we must tread carefully. God did not inject unbelief into Esau’s heart. Esau, like all of us, was already a sinner by nature and choice. Reprobation is not God actively working sin into a man’s life; it is God leaving a sinner to himself. That’s terrifying, but different from the active mercy He shows Jacob.
Deuteronomy 7 reminds us of this principle: *“It was not because you were more in number than any other people that the LORD set his love on you and chose you… but it is because the LORD loves you”* (Deut. 7:7–8). Election is God’s freedom to love whom He will.
Charles Spurgeon put it like this: *“God’s election is always of grace; it is not according to the will of man, nor according to the works of man, but according to the sovereign will of God, who hath mercy on whom He will have mercy.”*

**Monergism, Not Synergism**
Our 1689 Confession declares: *“Those predestinated unto life, God… hath chosen in Christ… out of His mere free grace, without any foresight of faith, or good works… as conditions”* (2LBCF 3:5).
Paul echoes the same in 2 Timothy 1:9: *“God saved us… not because of our works but because of his own purpose and grace, which he gave us in Christ Jesus before the ages began.”*
Or as John Owen wrote: *“Election is the fountain of all saving good, from which **floweth** all grace.”*
Jacob was not better than Esau—he was a deceiver, a schemer. God chose him to prove that His saving love rests on grace, not merit.
**A Tapestry of Grace**
Picture Rebekah’s womb as a stage. Two sons wrestle inside her, but before they draw breath, God has already written their stories. Not because of their works, but because of His call.
Think of a tapestry, woven before time. Each thread—your life, your salvation—was chosen by God, not because of or for your merit but because of and for His purpose. I recall a young woman in our church, lost in rebellion, her life tangled in sin. Yet God called her before time, weaving her into His family. That’s election’s beauty—His grace overcomes our impossibility, threading us into His eternal plan.

**It Matters Because**
**First.** Election humbles us but also steadies us. If God chose you before you did anything good or bad, then your failures cannot undo His choice. Parents, grieving a wandering child—pray in hope. God’s election is not bound by human stubbornness. He can break through the hardest heart. Bunyan saw this as comfort: *“God’s choice of Jacob was a declaration of His freedom to love whom He will, not bound by human merit.”* And when we ache over Charlie Kirk’s silenced voice, let it stir us to boldness: like him, proclaim Christ with fire in your bones (Isa. 58:1).
**Second.** Election crushes pride. If you think God chose you because you’re better than others, think again. Jacob was a schemer. The point of his story is to strip away every illusion of merit. If you rest on church attendance, family heritage, or Bible knowledge, you are standing on sand. Salvation is *“by grace you have been saved through faith… not of yourselves”* (Eph. 2:8–9). Parents, live as if you believe this, teaching your children the gospel by word and example.
**Third.** If you are trusting Christ today, it is because God set His love on you before time. Your name is written in the Lamb’s Book of Life. And Jesus Himself assures you: *“My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand”* (John 10:27–28). The God who chose Jacob will keep you forever.
**Closing Cadence**
So church, trust God’s electing purpose. Grieve for the lost—your wandering child, your unbelieving friend, Charlie Kirk’s silenced voice—but do not despair. His purpose stands. His word cannot fail. Share the gospel boldly, for the God who called Jacob still calls His elect today. Pray earnestly, resting in His sovereign grace.
**Conclusion: From Anguish to Assurance to the Cross**
So where does all of this leave us? Paul’s tears are real. Our tears are real. Israel’s unbelief, a nation’s rage, a family’s grief, a prodigal’s defiance—none of it is theoretical. But stand where Paul stands: on the granite of God’s unfailing word. *“It is not as though the word of God has failed.”* It cannot fail. It will not fail. Why? Because the Father’s purpose stands, the Son’s purchase stands, and the Spirit’s power stands.
Lift your eyes now to the hill called Golgotha. Romans 9 has taken us into the mystery of God’s choosing, but the thunderhead of that mystery erupts at the cross. Here is where the Father’s eternal love becomes visible, where promise becomes blood-bought reality. Here is where the Son was “made a curse” for us (Gal. 3:13), where He bore the wrath that should have drowned Jacob—and us. Here is where definite grace was definitively given: not a vague possibility, but a finished purchase. *“The good Shepherd lays down His life for the sheep”* (John 10:11). He *“loved the church and gave Himself up for her, to present her holy and blameless”* (Eph. 5:25–27). The atonement is not limited in power; it is particular in design—powerful enough to save any, designed to surely save the many the Father gave Him.
Do you hear the cords pulling together? The Father chooses—not because of works, but because of Him who calls. The Son dies—not to make salvation a maybe, but to make it ours. The Spirit comes—not to wait on dead men to rise, but to speak life where there was none. And what God begins, God completes. *“Those whom He predestined He also called, and those whom He called He also justified, and those whom He justified He also glorified”* (Rom. 8:30). That is why Paul can weep without despair, grieve without surrender, ache without letting go: the chain holds because Christ holds it.
And now, the gospel comes to you—not as a puzzle to solve, but as a Savior to trust. Election never cancels invitation; it guarantees its success. Christ is preached to all, sincerely offered to all, gladly received by any who come. *“All that the **Father** gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never cast out”* (John 6:37). Do you fear you are too far gone—an angriest rebel, a vilest apostate, a bold insurrectionist against heaven? Look to the cross. The same voice that called Isaac out of barrenness can call you out of death. The same blood that washed Jacob clean can wash you—today.
Believer, take this to heart. Your adoption does not hang on the thin thread of your performance but on the steel cable of Christ’s finished work. When your sins accuse, point them to the cross. When your fears whisper, “What if I fall away?”, answer with the voice of your Shepherd: *“I give them eternal life, and they will never perish”* (John 10:28). When you watch a loved one wander and your prayers feel small, remember Sarah’s womb—remember the empty tomb. The God who raises the dead writes the last line.
Skeptic, seeker, sinner—hear this invitation. You are not asked to clean yourself first; you are summoned to come. Lay down your arms of rebellion. Bring your guilt, your shame, your sin, your self-salvation projects, and cast them on the crucified and risen Christ. He will not cast you out. Turn from your sin, trust in the Lord Jesus, be reconciled to God. Today.
Church, what do we do now? We do what Paul did. We take our tears into the throne room. We plead for souls with the confidence that God’s word cannot fail. We preach Christ with urgency—not because we can force life, but because God loves to speak life through His gospel. We love our enemies, we pray for those who persecute us, we refuse to repay venom with venom, and we lift high the name that saves. We honor the memory of those taken from us too soon—not by fury, but by faithfulness; not by silence, but by a trumpet blast of grace.
And we worship. Because this story—your story, my story, the church’s story—ends not in ashes but in glory. The Lion of Judah who became the Lamb slain has triumphed. The banner over the ages is not “Man’s will be done,” but “Worthy is the Lamb who was slain.” …The Father’s purpose stands. The Son’s purchase stands. The Spirit’s power stands. And at the center of it all stands a cross and an empty tomb.
So come to the cross. Come again to the cross. Come weary and heavy-laden; come guilty and afraid; come joyful and bold. Come, and find that underneath are the everlasting arms. Come, and hear the verdict already announced over every child of promise: *No condemnation. No separation. No defeat.* Not now. Not ever.
And let us pray with the words of saints who have gone before us, from *Streams in the Desert*:
“O Lord, I thank You that all my life is in Your hands—woven by Your design, not mine. Help me to rest in Your promises, even when my heart is heavy with anguish for those who resist You. Teach me to trust that not one word of Yours has ever failed, nor ever will. When all looks barren, breathe life. When I despair, remind me that You are faithful, and Your purposes stand. Keep me from murmuring against Your will, and give me grace to say in every circumstance: ‘Even so, Father, for so it seemed good in Thy sight.’”
Amen.
