## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
A Pastorally Grounded Survey with a Hollow Center and an Absent Gospel

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A conversational and discernment-minded opening of Acts 2 that handles the tongues debate with pastoral care and rightly warns against charismatic excess, while failing to preach the gospel, ground the main point in the text's own argument, or press the conscience toward Christ.

### 3. Berean Gate
**Result: Marginal Pass — with serious reservations.**

**Scopus Test:** The sermon's stated aim is to cover "the ministry of, the gifts of, and proof of the Holy Spirit in our lives." This is a topical survey, not an exposition of Acts 2's own argument. The text (Acts 2:1–9, 36–40) has a clear thrust: the outpouring of the Spirit at Pentecost as the fulfillment of Christ's promise and the inauguration of the new covenant church, culminating in Peter's gospel call in vv. 36–40 — "Repent and be baptized… for the forgiveness of your sins." The sermon never arrives at Peter's sermon. It treats vv. 36–40 as a reference without exposition. The main point of the sermon (discernment about charismatic gifts and spiritual growth markers) is not the main point of the text. **The Scopus Test is a failure.** However, since the text is read and engaged — not merely referenced — a formal gate-halt is withheld in favor of a score-cap warning. The sermon drifts significantly from the passage's own climax.

**Authority Test:** The preacher handles the text fairly where he engages it. He does not soften uncomfortable parts. But he largely ignores the text's own confrontational weight (vv. 36–40: "Let all the house of Israel know for certain that God has made him both Lord and Christ, this Jesus whom you crucified"). **Partial pass.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Score is capped at 69.**

**Flag 1: No clear gospel presentation.** The sermon ends with a one-word heading: "Gospel." Nothing follows. There is no actual gospel proclamation. The finished work of Christ — his substitutionary death, resurrection, justification by faith — is not articulated. The congregation is not told how to be saved. This is not a minor gap; it is the central failure of the sermon.

**Flag 3: Christ is largely absent and unnecessary to the sermon's argument.** The discussion of spiritual growth markers (prayer, Bible reading, humility) and the extensive treatment of Todd Bentley could survive largely intact without Jesus. Christ is mentioned in passing ("God, after paying our sin price, after securing our salvation") but never proclaimed.

**Flag 5: No actual exhortation.** The sermon closes without calling the congregation to believe, repent, obey, or pursue anything in particular. The self-examination questions are good, but they function as diagnostics, not summons. There is no "therefore" moment.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is entirely absent.** The manuscript ends with the single word "Gospel." — apparently a placeholder that was never filled. Acts 2:36–40 is one of the most explicit gospel proclamations in Scripture: Christ is Lord and Messiah; his hearers crucified him; they must repent and be baptized for the forgiveness of sins; the promise is for them and their children. The sermon selected this text and then abandoned it at the exact point where the text speaks with greatest force. This is the fatal failure.

2. **The sermon has no exhortation.** The growth-marker questions are diagnostics. They tell the congregation where they are. They do not tell the congregation what to do. There is no "therefore." There is no moment where the preacher looks the congregation in the eye and says, "Repent. Believe. Go. Do this." The sermon examines but never summons.

3. **The main point of the sermon is not the main point of the text.** Acts 2:1–40 is not primarily about the tongues debate or about discerning charismatic excess. It is about the fulfillment of Joel's prophecy, the exaltation of the risen Christ, and the inauguration of the new covenant church through the outpouring of the Spirit — climaxing in the invitation to repent and receive the Spirit. The sermon treats the text as a launching pad for a topical survey on pneumatology and discernment. The text's own argument is not preached.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher does not appear to see that Acts 2:36–40 — listed in his own Scripture reference — contains the most direct gospel call in the New Testament ("Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins"). He referenced the passage; he never preached it. This may reflect a tendency to use Acts 2 as a pneumatological proof-text rather than as evangelical proclamation. The text is a missionary text, an evangelistic text, a repentance text — and the sermon treated it as a doctrinal resource for an internal church debate about charismatic gifts.

Additionally, the preacher may not recognize that his extensive Todd Bentley section — which is practically useful — is doing the theological work that the text itself should be doing: showing what the true Spirit looks like vs. counterfeits. The text itself provides this contrast (the Spirit produces repentance and faith, not theatrical violence). But the contrast is made via YouTube anecdote rather than from the text's own categories.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The **discernment section** feels like the sermon's strongest moment. The preacher speaks with obvious conviction, the congregation is clearly engaged, and the Spurgeon quote lands memorably. But this section's strength is rhetorical, not expository. It derives its force from outrage at Todd Bentley, not from the weight of the text. The congregation may leave talking about Todd Bentley rather than about the lordship of Christ or the call to repentance. The section is not wrong — it is misweighted and textually unmoored.

Similarly, the **self-examination questions** feel like pastoral depth. But self-examination in the absence of the gospel produces introspection, not faith. The Puritan tradition that produced this mode of conscience-searching always embedded the diagnostic questions within a gospel frame: you are examined *in order to be driven to Christ*, not merely to measure your spiritual progress. Without that frame, the self-examination questions risk producing either pride (in those who answer "yes") or despair (in those who answer "no").

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Acts 2:36–40 is the entire missed opportunity.** Peter does not conclude Pentecost with growth-marker checklists or discernment warnings. He concludes with a verdict on history ("God has made him both Lord and Christ, this Jesus whom you crucified") and an invitation to the conscience ("cut to the heart… what shall we do?"). This is precisely the heart-penetration and exhortation that the rubric calls for — and the text provides it ready-made. The preacher selected this passage and then stopped before reaching its own climax.

The phrase "cut to the heart" (v. 37) is itself a remarkable opening for the conscience-and-application section. The crowd's question — "Brothers, what shall we do?" — is the exhortation prompt the preacher never reached. Peter's answer — repent, be baptized, receive the Spirit, be saved from this crooked generation — is the gospel and the call to response combined in four verses. All of this was in the preacher's stated text. None of it was preached.

1. **Preach vv. 36–40.** This is not optional. The sermon selected these verses and owes the congregation their content. Specifically: "God has made him both Lord and Christ, this Jesus whom you crucified" (v. 36) is a gospel declaration and a conscience-confrontation in one sentence. Preach it as such. The call to repentance in v. 38 is the exhortation the sermon currently lacks entirely.

2. **Replace "Gospel." with an actual gospel presentation.** Even two to three minutes of clear articulation: Christ died for sin, rose from the dead, and calls all people to repent and believe. This is not an add-on — it is the text's own conclusion.

3. **Restructure the Todd Bentley section as a contrast drawn from the text.** Rather than "here is what the Spirit does NOT do (Bentley), and here is what we inferred the Spirit does do," let the text supply both sides. Acts 2's Spirit produces repentance, faith, baptism, community, and proclamation. Any spirit that produces theatrical violence, self-aggrandizement, and neglect of the Word fails the Acts 2 test — and *the text itself is the criterion*, not YouTube.

4. **Convert the self-examination questions into gospel-driven diagnostic questions.** After each diagnostic question, point to Christ as the answer: "Are you more humble? That humility is the work of the Spirit in a believer who is regularly returning to the cross — not measuring themselves against others, but measuring themselves against Christ and finding that they are still in need of grace." The diagnostic without the gospel produces moralism.

5. **State one central thesis from the text.** Something like: "The gift of the Spirit at Pentecost is the seal of the new covenant, the fulfillment of Christ's promise, and the call to the church — and to every sinner — to repent and receive what God has freely given." Then preach *that*, using the tongues debate and the discernment section as supporting material.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Acts 2 is narrative-historical material with an embedded sermon (Peter's Pentecost address), which the rubric handles well. The narrative sections are appropriately treated as historical-literal, not as allegory or type — the rubric's emphasis on textual fidelity is well-suited here. The embedded sermon (vv. 14–40, with the preacher's selection of vv. 36–40) is itself an example of what this rubric demands: exposition, gospel proclamation, conscience-confrontation, and exhortation. The rubric does not strain against this genre — in fact, Acts 2 is almost a worked example of what the rubric calls for. The sermon's failure to reach the text's own climax is therefore a particularly sharp irony: the text itself models the very thing the rubric is looking for.
