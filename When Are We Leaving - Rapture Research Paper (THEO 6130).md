A wise man once said in the context of Muslim ministry that words are simply vessels into which we pour meaning. In the interest of clarity then we will start by examining the word for Rapture. You may have heard it argued that the word Rapture does not appear in the Greek New Testament and that is true – there are no English words in the autographs. Further, there are many theological terms like rapture, that we use today, that are not used explicitly in the New Testament. Trinity for instance is never used. Neither is Great Commission. Yet all of these things are describing real biblical truths. Both the Trinity and Great commission can be seen in some of the same verses! Rapture is also a biblical doctrine. In fact according to Hultberg we get the word rapture from the Latin word Rapio – which means to seize or violently carry off. Rapio as it happens is actually in the Septuagint, which is the Latin translation of the New Testament. In the original Greek, the word for rapture can be found in 1 Thessalonians 4:16-17. The word is ἁρπάζω, like in Latin, in Greek the word translates: "'snatch, seize', i.e. take suddenly and vehemently, or take away". In the same paragraph, it suggests that within the range of the word is the idea that one is drug away as by a wild animal, or as one might come to recover ones lost property, to take possession of something as a thief might, or else to remove through force. Those are apt images. As C.S. Lewis wrote of his supposition of God – named Aslan in the Chronicles of Narnia: God is not safe, but he is good.

## Why is the Rapture Worth Studying?

The rapture is important because it is a distinct possibility that it is something we will experience. We should know of the rapture because God thought it important enough to mention. We should study the rapture because there will invariably be Christians that are curious about the passages in question – and it is important that we be prepared to answer the tougher questions. Questions regarding the Eschaton generally are more difficult. Finally and perhaps, most importantly we study the eschatology in general and the rapture in particular because it is in part a way for us to learn more about Christ. Philip Schaff said: Jesus Christ is the beginning, the middle, and the end of all. In the Gospels he walks in human form upon the earth, and accomplishes the work of redemption (…) he founds the church, and fills and guides it by his Spirit. And at last, (…) he comes again in glory, and with his bride, the church of the saints, reigns forever upon the new earth in the city of God". That is to say that by fully neglecting the topic of eschatology we are in a sense cherry picking aspects of Christ's ministry choosing to study the easy parts while ignoring that which can be confusing. Studying Christ's Second coming then, is a part of getting to know our Savior. A woman – a painter – was preparing to die. In her planning for what was to be done regarding her estate – especially her paintings she said that she pours so much of herself out her art that she wanted all of them to go to those who loved her. Christ then in His second coming has poured some of Himself into what we read in scriptures on this topic. So then we who love Christ, should study these hints of who He is spattered throughout the complicated imagery heavy with symbolism so that we can have a more complete picture of the God-Man who died for us. As the scriptures say in 2 Corinthians 5:21 "For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God." It was for our sake that Christ died, and worse – was separated from His Father for a time - if for no other reason than this we must study eschatology.

As with any topic in the realm of eschatology, there is an incredibly wide variety of views on the subject. Wider than the scope of this paper, so in the interest of brevity and succinctness we will pre-suppose a pre-millennial position and only, explore the mainstream rapture positions within this eschatological framework. There are at least four, though we will focus on three: Pre-Wrath (previously called Mid-Tribulation), Pre-Tribulation, and Post Tribulation. As is the case with anyone who writes on a question of theology this author has certain pre-conceptions that he will try to set aside in an effort to earnestly and honestly evaluate the three positions previously mentioned in light of scripture, and then having read the scripture we will examine the writings of a number of great minds who have studied this deeply and scoured the scriptures for clues as to the timing of this event.

The Greek Lemma αρπαζω which you will remember is the Greek word we translate as rapture is actually found 30 times in the Greek New Testament (Novum Testamentum Graece 28th). Most of these refer to snatching, plundering, seizing, taking, and dragging. However there are a few we should zero in on; those that translate the word "caught up" and one other in Acts where Philip is Harpazo (translated carried) from one place to another. First is 2nd Corinthians 12:1-10. Paul is speaking of a friend who had been "Harpazo" into the third heaven for a time. His friend was forbidden by God to speak of his experiences. This of course calls to mind the recent blockbuster "Heaven is for Real". The movie is tripe, but some of the protagonists experiences may offer insight onto the prohibition of speaking about heavenly journeys. Jeremy Werdt the four year old child around which the story centers dies and goes to "heaven". While there he sees many friends and family members including people he had never met. He then returned to his body and relayed his experiences to his parents. Eventually his mother realized her mother wasn't there. So she asked the boy if he had seen her mother. The boy responded in the negative and then went back to sleep. How heartbreaking would it be to learn that your parent was forever separated from God in Hell? How might that affect an individual's faith? Knowing their seemingly devout parent went to hell. Further lets consider another angle, as wonderful as heaven has been described as being, wouldn't it be depressing to have tasted the real thing, and then be forced back to earth to live in a fallen state, amongst fallen individuals! It might trigger a depression in the one who was brought back! Certainly may trigger anger, or disappointment. I've always wondered how Lazarus and others fared after their resurrections. Having been three days gone, and now forced back into their earthly, fallen tent would be at a minimum disappointing, and at most incredibly depressing. Moving on, the next verse is 1st Thessalonians 4:17. This passage is speaking of things to come; it seems clearly prophetic. It begins by speaking of those who had died prior to Christ calling them "those who are asleep" in the ESV translation. It would appear that Paul is trying to comfort the Thessalonians who are wondering about their loved ones; they seem to be asking if their beloved dead will be a part of what is to come, or if they are they will be left out. Paul responds that the dead will rise first, but quite quickly thereafter, the believers in Christ will be brought up to the clouds to be with their loved ones, and most importantly; to be with Christ. He concludes in verses 17 and 18 saying that once this happens we will be with Him (the Lord) always, and that this is a source of encouragement to the church. Next is the Olivet Discourse – That is Mathew 24-25. This is a huge passage, nearly a hundred verses, and one of the longest monologs of Christ. Further some object to this passage being used as a description of the rapture (especially 24:40ff). However their proof was unconvincing, though I will cede that the greek word used in the passage is not Harpazo. The author of the book of Mathew certainly knew the word Harpazo as he uses it 5 times across his Gospel; and yet at this point he uses a different word entirely. Clearly, Christ is speaking of end times. Clearly, we have people being taken, and yet Mathew chooses a different word. Perhaps – since all his earlier uses were of thieves and brigands he wanted to make a distinction between the two actions. In Mathew, Harpazo is used as a description of Wolves (MT 7:15), described as an action of the violent (11:12), described as an action that might be taken against a strongman (12:29), used as a description of an action that Satan himself may make (13:19), and immediately prior he uses Harpazo as a description of an action taken by greedy men. It seems clear that Mathew wanted to make a distinction between his earlier uses of the word in conjunction with evil, and what God would do. Finally we see in Revelation 12:1-6 an abbreviated Gospel. It speaks of the Birth of Christ, and how Christ was "Harpazo" before the great red dragon (an allusion to Satan) had a chance to swipe the babe. Most of the time the word is used, God is the one doing the actions. However, we gain further insight into this in the story of Philip. Acts says the Pneuma (Greek word for Sprit) of the Lord carried (Harpazo) Philip. The physical act of the "Harpazo" of an individual then seems to be a part of Pneumatology. Perhaps all three parts of the God-Head are capable of this act – however we only have record of the Holy Spirit acting in this way. We mortals (and Christ) are the recipients of this action. Ultimately we are "Harpazo-ed" for our own benefit; and the post-tribulation camp might argue that we are also raptured (or harpazo-ed) to be a part of the victorious and triumphal re-entry to the world.

The basic discussion regarding the timing of the rapture circles around when an individual sees God's wrath being poured out on the earth. For the pre-tribulation camp, wrath is the entirety of the tribulation. For the pre-wrath position, the first part of the tribulation signifies Satan's wrath, while the second part signifies God's wrath. We are to be spared from the infinitely terrible wrath of God say the adherents of a pre-wrath rapture but not from the wrath of the devil. The third camp – post-tribulation rapture – believes that there is a difference between the wrath of God and the tribulation. There are even two separate words in Greek to help delineate when one is experiencing the wrath of God, and the tribulation. When the latter is used, Romans 5 says that we are made to be more like Christ through perseverance. Further if God considers himself to be pouring out wrath on the people of earth He is more than capable of applying it selectively (such as at the first Passover in Egypt). Thus God spared His children from his wrath.

All three positions have their issues. Pre-tribulation & Pre-wrath raptures both struggle with Luke 17. The language used seems to indicate that Jesus believed the day the rapture occurs is also the day God begins to pour out his wrath on earth. Christ points to two examples - Noah and Lot. We all know both stories well. One is clearer than perhaps the other is. With Lot - as he is fleeing the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah, God begins to rain down his punishment. The punishment seems immediate based upon the idea that while they were still fleeing lots wife looked back at their hometown and was turned to a pillar of salt. With Noah, the situation is a little less clear. We see in Luke 17 that the rain started the day Noah entered the ark. Genesis supports this but immediately prior, it mentions a seven-day period between God speaking to Noah and the arrival of the flood. The pre-tribulational camp seems to believe that the representatives of every species on earth, and supplies needed to survive, and household belongings and so forth were all able to pack themselves into the ark in the first day of the seven-day window. Whereas the pre-wrath view would believe that, the animals and people would have been moving into the ark over a seven-day period. The final day of this time Noah and his family moved in, mind locked the door. Then and only then was the door locked, and did the floods come. It is interesting to note at this point, that the Sumerian flood legend also supports the seven-day window, though it seems to indicate that the rains began on the first day, and it was not until the 7th day that the "cult-centers" (cities) were covered deeply by water. The Pre-Trib camp would posit that the loading of the ark was done in the time leading up to the start of the seven days, and the pre-wrath camp would argue for the water coming at whatever point Noah and family boards the ark and God seals the door. The biggest issue I see with the post tribulational rapture camp is that it is hard to believe God would rapture everyone into the clouds – briefly – then head back down to the earth. It is understandably ceremonial, but not implicitly necessary. There are other ways God could have arranged this meeting of resurrected souls, and living saints. Further, once back on the ground, what does one do with the disembodied spirit of ones loved one for the millennial kingdom period? Will they sleep? Will they eat? Will they just drift around ones bedroom while said individual sleeps? So many questions and so few answers to be found in the text of scripture on this point. We may not know until such a time as we meet Christ in the air. However, at this point I will fuse together the pre-tribulational and pre-wrath raptures agreeing that both are more or less the same on the essentials, differing only on the definition of wrath, and then we really cannot know God's definition this side of heaven. For the rest of the paper, we will focus on the pre-tribulation, and post tribulation positions. George Eldon Ladd has it right when he posits: "The Rapture of the Church before the Tribulation is an assumption; it is not taught in the Olivet Discourse". While Rapture is seen there, its timing is not spelled out. For that matter, its precise timing is not mentioned anywhere. 2 Thessalonians 2 talks about the man of lawlessness being restrained by a man for a time. This lawless man is brought on the scene by Satan himself, and will deceive many. This calls to mind men like Joel Osteen, and others who preach a different Gospel. In 2nd Timothy 3:13, Paul; having just spoken of the end times, and given admonitions regarding suffering that was both already and not yet – advises Timothy to be ware, because while these persecutions are occurring, there are evil men and imposters going around deceiving and being deceived. While only God knows whether the Osteens are truly saved or not; it is safe to say that we can compare what he teaches with the truth of the Gospel. If what he teaches is other than this Gospel truth, then he is quite possibly one of the evil men or imposters. He is but one of many in our current generation. In Miami Florida, a man claimed to be Jesus, and now claims to be Satan. He has developed a considerable following. Others have gone before these men founding the Jehovah's Witnesses, Mormonism and Islam. All founded by imposters. More recently, the Stone-Campbell movement twisted the Gospel adding to Baptism to the requirements for salvation. This has given some a false sense of security when it comes to salvation. Simply be dipped and believe and you are saved. This is little better than our own traditions simple sinners prayer. While it is certainly better than nothing, it is not the sinners prayer that saves. Nor does Baptism save. God saves, and he saves the elect, or said another way those whom he has given the ability to believe. When the rapture comes, many will be surprised. Later at the judgment, many will say "Lord Lord" I did this or that in your name, and He will look them in the eye and send them to Hell. It is an unfortunate situation, yet one we as ministers must consider in our pastoring. The idea of living like we are leaving, or living with the expectation of Christ's return at any moment is the best possible route for any believer to take. We know for a fact that the great tribulation is coming. We know that we will be spared some of it. We know that we richly deserve anything that may come as part of the tribulation (And worse), and yet God is gracious. He loves us. God promises to spare us from His righteous wrath. 1st Thessalonians says regarding the Day of the Lord that people will be surprised, but we shouldn't be. That it will come like a thief in the night. That this day will be heralded by birth pains. Some women are in labor for days; the signs of the times will come well in advance of the actual events. 5:8 says: "But since we belong to the day, let us be sober, having put on the breastplate of faith and love, and for a helmet the hope of salvation. 9For God has not destined us for wrath, but to obtain salvation through our Lord Jesus Christ, 10who died for us so that whether we are awake or asleep we might live with him. 11Therefore encourage one another and build one another up". In light of this coming, and the fact we can identify the birth pangs, we ought to be paying attention. We ought to be sharing the Gospel with people. We ought to be warning those in our pews that if they are complacent perhaps they need to double check the status of their salvation. If they have arrived at a place where they think they are sinless, they need to address their own arrogance and pride. That too was a description of people in the end times. This doctrine is a weakness of the Post Tribulation camp. If we know when the tribulation starts, we can track its progress, and thus know roughly, when the Rapture will come. Christ says no man shall know the day or hour. While the idea that we can tell when the rapture will occur has caused scores of date setters sleepless nights and much shame (such as the person who wrote "88 reasons why Jesus will return in 88", and had the hubris to write its sequel the following year – which contained 89). Christ does say we can know the season. He says in the Olivet Discourse seems to suggest that we will be able to know when the time is upon us, like we can know a fig tree is about to bear fruit. That does not mean we will necessarily know days, months, or even years in advance. Nevertheless, in the end, when the time is upon us – we will see the early buds of the fig tree of tribulation, we as a church will collectively feel the pangs of labor. This knowledge should intensify our yearning for the souls of the Lost. The Prince of Preachers Charles Spurgeon said in one of his sermons that: "Oh, my brothers and sisters in Christ, if sinners will be damned, at least let them leap to hell over our bodies; and if they will perish, let them perish with our arms about their knees, imploring them to stay, and not madly to destroy themselves. If hell must be filled, at least let it be filled in the teeth of our exertions, and let not one go there unwarned and unprayed for". Whenever the rapture, this fervency should be the hallmark of our church, and of our faith; that we believed earnestly in our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ, that we prayed fervently, that we proclaimed the Gospel frequently and loved the souls of the lost deeply so that no one can go to hell without being warned and prayed for. If this is true now, how much more so would it be if we knew we were in the season of the rapture?

Another difficulty with Pre-Tribulationism is that in the book that deals most extensively with the great Tribulation, in the parts that speak most extensively about this period of time (chs 8-16) we see absolutely no reference to Rapture. Out side of Chapters 8-16 there are very few places that one could construe to be speaking of the rapture. Ladd Suggests that most Pre-Tribulationists would point to Revelation 4:1, saying that the place where God points to John and the open door saying come up here, is in fact the rapture and that John simple omits it. However he (Ladd) says, that one must take entirely too much for granted for this to be the case. If John saw millions of people disappearing off the earth, wouldn't he have made note of it? Another scripture held by some to be the point at which the elect are raptured at the close of chapter 3, before the start of chapter 4 of revelation. Yet again, Ladd bats them down saying "To find a pretribulation rapture in the book which says most about the Great Tribulation, one must take it for granted". Put simply "The Word of God nowhere says that the Rapture will precede the Tribulation". Such an important event seems like it should have been more thoroughly recorded; especially if the Rapture were to be at the beginning of the Tribulation. Yet all of the passages we've studied that use the word "Harpazo", and indeed none of the other passages (such as 1 cor 15:51ff, or Philippians 3:20-21) that we have not looked at mention that the rapture will trigger the start of the tribulation, or that it will occur before the tribulation, or any other fact that could possibly be construed to indicate that the rapture would occur before the Tribulation. The single solitary best argument that they have is that God will spare us from His wrath. Yet even that argument is failing. Several individuals that I spoke with posited that the un-countable multitude in Revelation 7:9 is speaking largely of jews. However ignoring the rest of the verse for a moment, we know that elsewhere in scripture John was able to identify a group of 200 million in God's army at Meggido (Rev 9:16). IF John can accurately recognize 200,000,000 individuals in the valley, then surely a number that is too high to count must be exponentially bigger than 200 million. Looking back at the context once more, we see that this innumerable multitude consists of representatives of every tribe, tongue, and nation. This host of people likely numbered into the billions. Reading on in chapter 7 of Revelation, we see that these individuals are ones coming "out of the great tribulation" and that had been "washed clean in the blood" (vs 14). Thus it is conceivable that this huge number of people worshiping God in His throne room (imagine the size of the throne room that can hold a billion people) are those believers who either survived the tribulation, or else were martyred the dead in Christ may or may not be included in this number. In either case, the number is going to be huge. At this point in world history, we have around six billion people. The IMB estimates that there are around 720 million believers as of 11:19pm, on December 1st 2014. Around 7m believers are added to the church each year. Within the next 30 years, it is conceivable that there will be a billion believers on our planet. More Lord willing. If that innumerable host of Rev 7 also includes all those believers who have passed on knowing Christ, then those present in the throne room would be billions upon billions. It is highly unlikely that a billion Jews have ever existed, even counting those who are not fully Jewish that have assimilated into other countries. Even counting those exterminated by Hitler, we would not be anywhere near 100 million, much less a billion. Nevertheless, it is possible that all of those present are those who were raptured at the end of the tribulation. What a glorious moment that will be – seeing individuals from every tribe, tongue, and nation worshipping God in unison. It will be like the promise keepers march on DC a number of years ago, only with hundreds, perhaps thousands of times more people. How glorious will it be to sit there in our glorified bodies able to tolerate not only the glory of God, but God himself. To see the face of Christ, to see the holes in His hands and weep for joy at our salvation, and at the anguish we caused our Savior.

The next passage we will examine is Revelation 2:10b which says: "ten days you will have tribulation. Be faithful unto death, and I will give you the crown of life". The context here is a letter to the church of Smyrna. These are believers, and they are told that they will experience tribulation. This seems to me to be yet another nail in the coffin of the pre-tribulation position. If the pre-tribulation position is that believers won't experience the events of revelation because God won't subject them to Tribulation, than this verse is the death knell. Their position is a little more nuanced of course, and so their position persists.

At this point it is fair to point out that the authors position has changed dramatically through the course of this assignment. As an undergraduate the theology professor he studied under simply dismissed the possibility of a post tribulation rapture as silly; arguing instead for a progressive dispensational pre-millennial, pre-tribulation position. As one could imagine under such teaching the majority of the class graduated having assume the same pre-tribulational position. Yet now – having read more thoroughly on the topic and having heard better treatments on all the positions the winner seems to be that of the Historic Pre-millenialists – that is to say those who adhere to the post tribulation rapture. While I may not agree with them (or anyone) on every point, they seem to have the scriptural view on most of the passages, and have fewer liabilities than other views. However Douglas Moo writes: "Because this conviction is founded upon Logic, inferences, and legitimately debated points of exegesis, I cannot, indeed must not allow this conviction to represent any kind of barrier to full relationships with others who hold differing convictions on this point. May our discussions on this point enhance (…) our common expectation of 'the blessed hope (…)'". This author's belief then is as follows: We deserve punishment for our sin, and that may come by way of the great tribulation should we live so long. God will protect us from His wrath, though that may not extend to every aspect of every punishment he pours out upon the earth during the tribulation. Certainly, we as believers will be targets of the wrath of the anti-christ throughout. Scripture is unclear regarding anything beyond the fact that there will be a rapture (though not everyone agrees). IF God sees the great tribulation as being part or parcel of his wrath then we will not experience it. IF as some suggest, God's wrath is only poured out at a certain point than we will be spared that wrath by the rapture. IF as I believe God's wrath is represented by an eternity separated from Him, and not in the tribulation at all, then we will not be raptured until the end of the tribulation at the moment of Christ's glorious return. One cannot be dogmatic on an issue where scripture is not perfectly clear and where we depend upon the logic of fallen man to determine what God intends through this mystery, shrouded in mist, and locked in an enigma. At best we can make educated guesses. At best we make inferences. At best we see a glimmer of the work yet to be done by the One who died for us at Calvary. In the end, perhaps that is the best we can do.
