# Service — Denise's Friend
## A Graveside Message on Psalm 139:13–16

**Text**: Psalm 139:13–16 (ESV)
**Type**: Memorial Service

---

We are gathered here today to honor the memory of _________________________.

He was a friend to one we dearly love. Scripture says to weep with those who weep, and mourn with those who mourn. Today, Denise — we mourn along with you, and with his family, friends, and coworkers who have been left behind.

---

I'm reminded today of Psalm 139, verses 13 through 16, which says:

> "For you formed my inward parts;
>     you knitted me together in my mother's womb.
> I praise you, for I am fearfully and wonderfully made.
> Wonderful are your works;
>     my soul knows it very well.
> My frame was not hidden from you,
> when I was being made in secret,
>     intricately woven in the depths of the earth.
> Your eyes saw my unformed substance;
> in your book were written, every one of them,
>     the days that were formed for me,
>     when as yet there was none of them."

---

While we may not have much or many in this life, we have a God who intentionally knit us together in our mother's womb. A God that makes no mistakes. A God that promises to save even those who feel like they are alone in this world — if they turn to Christ.

You can never be too alone for Christ.
You can never be too lost in sin for Christ.
You can never be so bad that Christ rejects you.

He made you the way you are, and he doesn't make any mistakes.

---

My sincere hope is that one day we will get to meet Denise's friend _________________________ in glory with Christ.

God makes no mistakes. We are all made the way we are for a reason. None are beyond his reach. None are outside of his will. We all have a role to play in the overarching story of history, and we can trust God to get us there — and get us through it. To do what's best for not only us, but for those around us, and for the world along the way.

Even when we can't understand, we still have hope, because of who God is. Because he loved us enough to send his Son to die for us on the top of that lonely hill.

---

For Christ, and for us, death is not permanent.

Christ rose again three days later and now sits at the right hand of the Father. For those of us who die in Christ, we are going to join him in glory. For those who don't know Christ, eternity is going to look a lot different. But God is gracious, and just, loving, merciful, and perfectly holy in everything he chooses to do.

Even in taking to their eternal home those we love, God is still loving, and merciful, and holy, and just. We can rest in that. God has a purpose even in this — a good one. Good for _________________________ and good for us too.

---

Let's close in prayer, and then release the birds.

---
## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Tender Pastoral Word with a Hollow Gospel Center and Absent Exhortation**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A gentle graveside address drawn from Psalm 139:13–16 that offers genuine comfort in God's sovereignty and creative care, but fails to proclaim the gospel with clarity, lets "Christ" function as a vague hope-word rather than the crucified and risen Lord, and closes without summoning the living to any specific response.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**Pass — with qualification.**

The sermon's main point — that the God who knit this man together in the womb is the same God who holds his death in sovereign purpose — does arise from the passage's emphasis on God's comprehensive, intentional knowledge and formation of the individual. The preacher submits to the text's declaration rather than rewriting it.

However, the passage's own emotional movement (praise, wonder, the psalmist's awe at being known) is almost entirely displaced by a pastoral reassurance framework. The sermon uses the text as a launching pad for comfort rather than staying in close orbit around its argument. This does not fail the gate outright, but it strains the Scopus Test.

**Scopus Test**: Barely passes. The thesis — "The God who knit us together makes no mistakes, and we can trust him even in death" — is derivable from the text, if not the sharpest reading of it.

**Authority Test**: Passes. There is no editing out of difficult material; the passage itself is not difficult in this direction.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Two fatal flaw flags are present. Score capped at 69.**

**Flag 1 — No clear gospel presentation.** Christ's finished work is gestured at but never proclaimed. "He loved us enough to send his Son to die for us on the top of that lonely hill" is a single clause, untethered to substitution, justification, or the meaning of the cross. The resurrection is mentioned, but its saving significance is not explained. An unbeliever present at this graveside would not know *why* Christ's death matters, what they must believe, or how to receive what Christ accomplished.

**Flag 5 — No actual exhortation.** The sermon explains and offers comfort, but it never calls the living to believe, repent, or turn to Christ. The phrase "if they turn to Christ" (paragraph 3) is a conditional statement, not a summons. No one is told what turning to Christ means, what stands in the way of it, or what the cost of not turning is. The sermon ends by releasing birds. The congregation is comforted but never summoned.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 14/25**

- *Textual Fidelity*: 6/10. The text's content (divine formation, God's sovereign knowledge of days) is present, but the sermon's argument is driven more by pastoral context than by the passage's own logic. The verbs of Psalm 139 — *formed*, *knitted*, *woven*, *saw* — receive almost no development. The phrase "fearfully and wonderfully made" is implied but not unpacked.
- *Contextual Awareness*: 2/5. No awareness of the broader psalm (the surrounding confession of God's omniscience and omnipresence in vv. 1–12, the imprecatory close in vv. 19–24, or the psalmist's own wrestling). The passage is treated as a standalone comfort text, which it can be — but the full weight of what it means that God *knows* in this way is not surfaced.
- *Exegetical Clarity*: 3/5. The key phrase "the days that were formed for me" is used effectively for the sovereignty-in-death point. Otherwise, the text is read and applied but not opened. No Fallen Condition Focus is named — the sermon does not identify the specific human condition this passage addresses (grief, the fear of divine absence, the terror that death is the final word).
- *Logical Faithfulness*: 3/5. No eisegesis, but also insufficient exegetical depth to demonstrate that the sermon's conclusions were *discovered* rather than assumed.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 10/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness*: 3/7. Christ appears — but as a name that provides comfort, not as the person whose specific work accomplishes anything theologically defined. The cross is mentioned in one clause. The resurrection is stated. But neither is connected to what the congregation needs to believe about them. The Christ-connection is organic in trajectory (this psalm drives toward a Redeemer who knows the individual fully) but that connection is not made.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness*: 1/3. No redemptive-historical placement. Where does this psalm sit in the story of God's saving purposes? How does the God who knit the psalmist together become the God who sends his Son to die? This is the hinge that would make the comfort genuinely Christian rather than generally theistic.
- *Doctrinal Precision*: 3/4. What is present is not wrong. The affirmations about God (loving, merciful, holy, just) are orthodox. But the sentence "For those of us who die in Christ" is doing enormous theological work without explanation — what does "in Christ" mean? How does one come to be "in Christ"? This is assumed, not proclaimed.
- *Confessional Alignment*: 3/4. Nothing contradicts confessional theology. The sovereignty of God in death and the particularity of his knowledge are consistent with a Reformed understanding of providence. No red flags.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 6/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel*: 1/5. The cross is mentioned in a single dependent clause: "loved us enough to send his Son to die for us on the top of that lonely hill." This is the shape of the gospel without its substance. There is no mention of sin as the problem requiring Christ's death, no mention of substitution or propitiation, no mention of faith as the instrument of receiving what Christ accomplished.
- *Necessity of Christ*: 2/5. Christ is present, but barely necessary. The sermon's primary comfort — God makes no mistakes, he knit us together, he is sovereign — does not require the cross to function. The same sermon could be preached by a theist who believes in divine providence without the specific atoning work of Christ. This is the most significant structural weakness.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity*: 3/5. The sermon does gesture toward the cross and resurrection, and the brief mention of different eternal outcomes for those "in Christ" versus those who are not shows some awareness that the cross matters ultimately. But the sermon's center of gravity is theological comfort, not the cross.

*Synagogue Test*: A devout Jewish mourner who believes in God's providence, God's sovereign knowledge of individuals, and God's purpose in death could sit through the majority of this sermon without offense. The cross and resurrection are mentioned, but not in a way that makes Christ's specific work a scandal or a necessity. This is a mild flag.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 7/15**

- *Heart Penetration*: 3/5. The opening is pastorally sensitive — it names the grief, weeps with the weeping, and honors the specific relationship. The threefold "You can never be too alone... too lost in sin... so bad that Christ rejects you" is the strongest moment of heart-address in the sermon. It touches a real fear.
- *Specificity*: 2/5. The sermon knows the setting (graveside, specific loss, specific friend) but the application remains general. "We can rest in that" is a conclusion, not a specific direction. What does resting in this look like for Denise, today, at this grave?
- *Spiritual Differentiation*: 2/5. The sermon addresses primarily the grieving believer and the potentially unknown spiritual state of the deceased. It does not distinguish between the grieving believer, the unbelieving mourner, the self-sufficient present, or the person who may have brought their own guilt or shame to this graveside. Perkins' taxonomy is largely unused — the congregation is addressed primarily as a unit of mourners, not as individuals in varying spiritual conditions.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 2/8**

- *Call to Response*: 1/4. The one conditional statement ("if they turn to Christ") acknowledges that a response is possible, but never calls for one. The congregation is comforted, not summoned. No one is told: "This is what you must do."
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness*: 1/4. The sermon ends at the graveside. There is no sending. There is comfort for the moment but no equipping for the life ahead — for Denise's grief, for the unbeliever standing at the grave, for the believer who will leave and need to carry this hope into the week.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline*: 2/4. The sermon moves naturally: opening (we mourn together) → text → God's character in creation → Christ (briefly) → eternal outcomes → comfort → close. The progression is intuitive for the pastoral context. But the sermon has no traceable thesis structure — it meanders toward comfort rather than building toward it.
- *Progression*: 3/3. For the brevity and occasion, the sermon does move. It does not wander badly. The movement from creation to incarnation to eternity is correct in direction.
- *Unity*: 1/2. There is one primary idea (God is sovereign and good even in death), but it competes slightly with a secondary idea about divine accessibility ("you can never be too lost") that is not fully integrated with the Psalm 139 text.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 2/5**

- *Relevance*: 1/2. "The top of that lonely hill" is evocative but understated. No illustrations that connect the congregation's lived experience to the truth.
- *Clarity*: 1/2. No illustrations that genuinely illuminate the point rather than merely gesture at it.
- *Memorability*: 0/1. There is no image, story, or phrase that will be carried home distinctly.

**8. Sermonic Force — 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching*: 2/2. The short, direct sentences have oral quality. "You can never be too alone for Christ. / You can never be too lost in sin for Christ. / You can never be so bad that Christ rejects you." — this reads and preaches well. The voice is appropriate to the occasion.
- *Conviction on the Page*: 1/2. The manuscript is gentle when it should sometimes be firm. The eternal-outcomes sentence ("eternity is going to look a lot different") needed to be stronger and more specific — it is softened before it lands.
- *Cadence and Breath*: 0/1. The sermon does not build or compress toward a climax. It concludes quietly, which may be contextually appropriate, but the rubric asks whether that quietness was earned or merely defaulted to.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Primarily neither — it directs attention to God's character and sovereignty, which is appropriate. Christ appears but is not magnified specifically. The congregation is left with "God is good" more than "Christ is sufficient and necessary."

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Partially. The affirmations of God's character are true. But the God encountered here is somewhat abstract — his goodness is asserted more than demonstrated from the text or proclaimed through the cross.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Comforted — the pastoral warmth is genuine. Not convicted. Barely informed theologically. The comfort is real but thin, because it is not grounded in the specific comfort of the gospel — the substitutionary death and bodily resurrection of Christ, received through faith.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Pastoral attunement to the occasion.** The opening is right — naming the grief, honoring the specific relationship, weeping with those who weep before speaking a word of truth. This is the correct posture for a graveside service, and the sermon demonstrates it.

2. **The threefold declaration of Christ's accessibility** ("You can never be too alone... too lost in sin... so bad that Christ rejects you") is the strongest moment in the manuscript. It addresses a real fear — that this man's apparent aloneness or lostness disqualifies him from grace — and it answers it with gospel instinct, even if not with full gospel precision.

3. **The sovereignty framing is theologically correct.** Using Psalm 139:16 ("the days that were formed for me") to address the question of whether this death was outside God's purpose is a legitimate and pastorally important move. It answers the grief-question that every mourner brings: "Was this a mistake?"

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is gestured at, not proclaimed.** The single clause "loved us enough to send his Son to die for us on the top of that lonely hill" is the entirety of the atonement's presence in this sermon. There is no explanation of *why* the death was necessary (sin and its penalty), what the death accomplished (substitution, propitiation, justification), or how a person receives its benefit (faith, repentance, union with Christ). At a graveside where unbelievers are almost certainly present, this is a critical failure.

2. **No exhortation reaches the living.** The sermon ends with a prayer and bird release. No one present — including Denise, including any unbelieving friend of the deceased — is told what to do with what they have just heard. "If they turn to Christ" implies a response without calling for one. The preacher had the congregation's full attention at the most spiritually receptive moment many of them may experience in years. The summons never came.

3. **The text is under-opened.** Psalm 139:13–16 is one of the most profound passages in Scripture on divine knowledge, creation, and the value of the individual human life. The verbs alone — *formed*, *knitted*, *woven*, *saw* — carry enormous homiletical potential. The sermon reads the text aloud and then departs from it. The congregation leaves having heard the text, not having understood it.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the comfort being offered is structurally available to monotheists who do not hold to the specific atoning work of Christ. The sermon's comfort rests on God's character (loving, merciful, just, sovereign) in a way that does not require the incarnation or the cross to function. This is not intentional — the preacher clearly believes in Christ — but the sermon's architecture is theistic comfort with a Christian veneer, rather than specifically Christian comfort rooted in the cross.

Additionally, the preacher may not recognize the evangelistic opportunity at a graveside service. Death concentrates the mind. Unbelievers attend funerals who would never enter a church on a Sunday. The sermon addresses Denise and the family primarily as believers being comforted, missing the open door to the spiritually unmoored.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sentence "For those of us who die in Christ, we are going to join him in glory. For those who don't know Christ, eternity is going to look a lot different" feels like it addresses the eternal stakes. It does — in shape. But it is the softest possible version of the contrast. "Eternity is going to look a lot different" is not a warning; it is an almost pleasant-sounding vagueness. The sermon gives the impression of having addressed eternal accountability without actually doing so. The contrast is present on the page but absent in force.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The passage itself contains one of the most powerful arguments for why an individual's life — including the life of Denise's friend, whose circumstances we are not told but who appears to have died in a difficult context (the reference to being "alone in this world" and "too lost in sin" suggests a troubled life) — mattered to God before a single day was lived. The language of "intricately woven" and "your eyes saw my unformed substance" is almost unbearably personal. The sermon could have stood at that language and let it do what it was written to do: overwhelm the mourner with the specific, intentional, pre-existent love of God for this particular person. Instead, it moves quickly to doctrinal comfort-statements that are true but abstract.

Furthermore, the passage's own movement from God's knowledge of the individual *life* to God's foreordaining of the individual *days* provides a natural and exegetically grounded bridge to the question every mourner brings: "Why did he die when he did?" The sermon uses this (correctly), but too briefly to let it carry the weight the text is capable of bearing.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the gospel center with one clear paragraph.** After "He made you the way you are, and he doesn't make any mistakes," add a paragraph that explains *why the cross was necessary* and *what it accomplished*: "The same God who knit [name] together also knew that he was born into a world broken by sin — and that sin separates every person from God. So the Son of God came. He lived the life none of us lived. He died in the place of sinners. And he rose again — which means death does not have the final word. For everyone who trusts in him, his death counts as theirs, and his resurrection is the guarantee of theirs." This is four sentences. A graveside service can hold four sentences.

2. **Add a direct summons to the living.** Before the closing prayer, add: "If you are here today and you do not know Christ — if you have been living as if death is the end, or as if God does not see you — today is the day to turn. The same God who saw [name] in his mother's womb sees you now. The same Christ who died for sinners died for you. Don't leave this graveside without settling that." This is the moment the sermon has been building toward and currently misses entirely.

3. **Stay in the text longer.** Slow down at the verbs of Psalm 139:13–16. What does it mean that God *knitted* this man together? What does the word "intricately woven" tell us about God's relationship to individual human life? Let the text do its pastoral work before explaining its theological implications. One minute of dwelling on the language of the passage would deepen everything that follows.

4. **Sharpen the eternal-contrast sentence.** "Eternity is going to look a lot different" must be stronger. Either develop it with enough specificity to be a genuine warning, or remove it and let the positive gospel statement stand. As written, it raises a specter without naming it, which creates unease without offering anything to do with it.

5. **Give Denise a specific word.** The service is in part for her. At some point, the sermon should speak directly to Denise about what she is to do with her grief, her hope, her friendship with this man's memory. The Puritan "uses" pattern — conviction, direction, consolation — would serve here: what does this text say to the mourner specifically?

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon is not doctrinally cold — it is doctrinally thin. Dead orthodoxy occurs when theology is right and fire is absent; here, the theology is itself underdeveloped, so the category does not quite apply. The pastoral warmth is genuine. The problem is insufficient gospel substance, not bloodless correctness.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries a real pastoral tenderness and a genuine instinct toward eternal hope, but it does not yet carry the weight of the eternal — the congregation is comforted at the level of God's general goodness rather than shaken and steadied by the specific weight of Christ's cross and empty tomb.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This is a graveside service address, not a full expository sermon, and the rubric acknowledges that genre matters. The brevity is appropriate; the occasion shapes what is possible. However, genre does not reduce the obligation to proclaim the gospel with clarity — it may actually heighten it, since the congregation includes mourners who may be at their most spiritually open. The rubric's categories apply with full force to the gospel-centrality and exhortation dimensions, even if the structure and illustration categories must be weighted with more grace given the pastoral context. The rubric does not bend here so much as it identifies that even a five-minute graveside address must do the irreducible work of proclaiming Christ.
