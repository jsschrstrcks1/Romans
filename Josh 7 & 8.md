Joshua 7–8 — Leaving God Out / Achan and Ai
*(Raw draft — unpolished oral transcript; processed March 2026)*
*(Verification flags at bottom — review before preaching again)*

---

**Primary Text**: Joshua 7:4-13; Joshua 8:1, 27
**Supporting Texts**: Deuteronomy 8:17-18, Psalm 106 (referenced), Isaiah 64:6 (allusion)
**Themes**: Prayer as first step, self-reliance vs. dependence on God, consequences of hidden sin, fear of God, "God's work done God's way," accountability
**Quoted**: Charles Spurgeon, Jonathan Edwards, John MacArthur, Hudson Taylor (two quotes)
**Illustrations**: Lake Placid FL (lightbulbs/clown college), Sopchoppy FL (worm gruntin'), Yanomami people of Brazil, Hudson Taylor biography, mouth cancer / church division anecdote

I was reading about small towns this week, we are home to some relatively famous small towns. One of which named lake placid produces somewhere between 90 and 98% of all the caladium bulbs of a certain type in the entire world. *(Corrected — the crop is caladium plant bulbs, not lightbulbs; Lake Placid is the "Caladium Capital of the World." Source: Farm Flavor / Caladium Festival historical records.)* Population 2000. That same town is known as the Clown Capital of the USA and is home to Toby's Clown School, a legitimate clown training school operating since 1993. *(Corrected — "98% of all the clowns in the world" is unverifiable hyperbole; Lake Placid does hold the Clown Capital designation. Source: Atlas Obscura / Toby's Clown Foundation.)*

I didn't know that was a thing, but I suppose if you can do it as a profession there is a college somewhere that teaches it.

Another small town is called sopchoppy. Ever heard of it? Its another small town of about 200 people. They have in their back yard a spectacular state park that has a lazy river that's supposed to be one of the best in the state, a boat ramp, and all sorts of wonderful things. They are also the "worm gruntin" – not grunting with a g, gruntin, capital of the world. Apparently they have an annual festival and people from all over the south gather and do their best to trick worms into come up to the surface. The winner gets some kind of prize. People can really devote their lives to foolish things can't they.

Any way so Moses has died, and Joshua has taken over at this point. They have defeated Jericho you remember the battle for Jericho, they prayed for direction, and God told them to march around it so many times, and on a certain day to march around it more times than normal playing their instruments and so forth. On that day the whole city collapsed except for the house of Rahab who helped the spies.

In the grand scheme of things Jericho was a large city. In todays scale, it would be like New York, or Dallas Texas, or Miami. It was a significant city, and there were few in the area that could compare.

By contrast the city of AI in our passage tonight was like the town of sopchoppy. Not very many people, that were not very prepared for battle. Yet the Lord was with them, and they won a great victory against the Israelites. Were going to start reading in vs 4 here in a minute, but before that I want to point out a thing they apparently neglected in vs 2. Joshua gathers men to send as spies, sends them in vs 2, takes their report in vs 3, develops a battle plan and initiates it. What do you notice they neglected to do? God's people never consulted God, even before going to war.

This defeat is huge! It would be like the mighty military of the united states and all the branches of our armed forces, including the new space force, being defeated at the hands of the Yanomami people of brazil. *(Spelling corrected — transcript had "yaonami"; standard spelling is "Yanomami.")* They are a small group – about 35000 people in total. *(See flags — "Yanomami" is the standard spelling; population figure ~35,000–38,000 is approximately correct.)* They fight with spears of wood, move in hand made dug out canoes, they hunt with bow and arrow. Their clothes – those that wear them – are made from animals they've killed. They wouldn't know what to do with a gun if you gave them one. They've never seen a missle, or an airplane, or a bomb. they are technologically inferior, inferior in numbers, inferior in training, and arms, and support. Just like the city of AI, and yet somehow the city of AI manages to route all 3000 of the better armed, stronger, physically fit Israelites.

Alright, lets stop there, and read the passage. Joshua 7:4-13

I touched on something a moment ago, and want to bring it up again. It goes back to something we talked about last week. Remember the verse we closed with last week? "Beware lest you say in your heart, 'My power and the might of my hand have gotten me this wealth'" *(Corrected March 2026 — ESV Deut 8:17 reads "this wealth," not "this power and wealth.")* But what does God say? "You shall remember the LORD your God, for it is he who gives you power to get wealth, that he may confirm his covenant that he swore to your fathers." *(Corrected March 2026 — ESV Deut 8:18 reads "power to get wealth," not "power and wealth." Also "LORD" is all-caps in ESV; the spoken transcript had lowercase "Lord.")* It strikes me that the nation had already forgotten this warning from God. Without prayer they sent out spies, without prayer they accepted the reports of the spies, without prayer they act on the recommendation of the spies and without prayer they send men out. Notice the trend?

I think its quite probable that they had come to depend upon their own two hands, rather than the God that holds the universe together. This is the same lesson God had to teach their parents. It strikes me that a lot of the lessons these peoples parents learned leading up to the desert, had to be re-learned by their kids. Even circumcision. You might remember that Moses had the nation stop for a time, and circumcise all the men. Once they had healed up, they started marching again. You might think something like that would be uhh memorable, and that they wouldn't forget God wanted them to circumcise their kids. It was part of moses law. Yet in the early chapters of Joshua, Joshua has to command them all to stop, take a couple days, and be circumcised. So this lesson of coming to depend upon God had to be re-taught.

I think this is an American short coming as well. We have this thing – I heard it jokingly called sola bootstrapsa by one of my professors – but it's a thing that we all believe deep down, that we are good enough, strong enough, work hard enough, smart enough, and capable enough to do and accomplish, and overcome anything we set our minds to. The problem with that sentiment is it leaves something major out of the equation.

Just like the Israelites, when we think that way, when we wrap ourselves with self aggrandizement, and unmitigated arrogance, we are leaving God out. Over and over, the Israelites have to learn this lesson, over and over I think we as a people have to learn this lesson as well.

Because only with Christ are we ever enough, Only with Christ are we ever capable enough, only with Christ are we ever smart enough, or able to work hard enough, only with Christ can we do, and accomplish, and overcome enough. Apart from Christ the best, most righteous we can be, scripture says, is like filthy rags. *(Isaiah 64:6 allusion — ESV reads "polluted garment"; the Hebrew refers to menstrual cloths. See note in Colossians 2 draft.)*

Apart from Christ, our best efforts can result in horrible defeat. Apart from Christ, our wisest men can give horrible advice.

And I want to be clear about something — I'm not just saying "add Jesus to your plans and you'll win." That's not what I mean. Christ didn't come to be a strategy upgrade. He came to bear what Achan deserved. That herem — that devoted-to-destruction judgment — that should have been ours. Our sin earns it just as surely as Achan's did. "Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us." *(Galatians 3:13)* He was cut off from the camp, outside the gate — and scripture uses that language deliberately: "Jesus also suffered outside the gate in order to sanctify the people through his own blood." *(Hebrews 13:12)* Same geography, same logic. Achan was dragged outside the camp and destroyed. Jesus walked outside the gate voluntarily, to bear what we deserved. That's the only reason any of us can stand on the right side of that line.

Charles Spurgeon wrote: "While others are congratulating themselves, I have to sit humbly at the foot of the cross and marvel that I'm saved at all." *(Verified March 2026 — confirmed genuine but paraphrase; original: "lie humbly at the foot of Christ's Cross… for I know that I am saved." Source: "One Aspect of Christ's Death," MTP Vol. 52, Sermon #2986, Oct 14 1875. Cite as paraphrase in print.)* I think that is the best posture we can take. A quote widely circulated under Jonathan Edwards' name reads: "We contribute nothing to our salvation except the sin that makes it necessary." *(Flag March 2026 — misattribution; no trace in Edwards' primary works. Most likely source: William Temple, Nature, Man and God, Macmillan, 1934, p. 403: "All is of God; the only thing of my very own which I can contribute, to my own redemption is the sin from which I need to be redeemed." Theologically sound; do not cite as Edwards. Either attribute to Temple or use without attribution.)*

Leaving God out of the plans was only the first of their sins.

The second sin listed a little further than our passage tonight in vs 20, was committed by a single man, and yet God held the entire fellowship accountable. You see after Jericho, God told them not to hang onto certain things. Don't marry their women, don't keep their idols, their clothes, their money, none of it. Destroy it all God had commanded them. Yet this one man – achan – kept a pile of Gold, and silver, and a cloak that ought to have been destroyed. One Hebrew scholar points out that the phrase devoted things – from vs 13, carries with it a lot of meaning. You have heard of double entendre – when someone says one thing but means two? This is kind of like that. Devoted things here means three things – it means anything God told them to destroy, secondly, it means things that were used in pagan worship. The attractive cloak, may well have been something used in pagan worship. Finally, the specific words used here in this grouping are frequently used to refer to sexual sins. *(Note — verify before reprising: the first two senses are well-supported. The "sexual sin" sense is not a documented lexical meaning of Hebrew* herem *in Joshua 7. The connection runs through the Arabic cognate* haram/harim *["forbidden," "women's enclosure"], a real etymological relationship but not a distinct sense of the Hebrew term itself. If using in print, present this as a cognate observation and contextual illustration, not as a third definition of the word.)*

The people in the region called themselves canaanites. Canaanites worshiped who? Baal and asheroth? They had a gross fertility ritual, that involved sex with prostitutes trying to manipulate God into bring forth life from the ground. Their highest festival took place in april, and several scholars feel like april was when Jericho fell. *(Note — spring/April timing is solidly supported by the biblical text: the Jordan crossing was Abib 10 [Josh 4:19], Passover on Abib 14 [Josh 5:10-12], flax drying on Rahab's roof [Josh 2:6 — a spring harvest marker], and burned barley grain found in excavations [Bryant Wood / Associates for Biblical Research, biblearchaeology.org]. These are textual and archaeological facts, not a scholar's opinion. However, the specific claim that "several scholars" link Jericho's fall to a Baal/Asherah spring festival is overstated — no mainstream named scholarly source has been located making this argument; Baal's spring association in the Ugaritic texts is real, but connecting it specifically to Jericho's timing as a festival counterpoint is a theological inference, not a documented scholarly consensus. Present the April timing as textually grounded; present the Baal festival connection as the preacher's own plausible observation, not "several scholars.")*

The cloak then, and the silver and gold, may well have been things used in the sexual worship of baal. Further, these festivals involved the ritualistic sacrifice of children – Psalm 106 fills us in on this. The Canaanite religion was hopelessly filthy, and disgusting in every single way. Adults having sex with prostitutes while children were being killed. I don't really even want to go further. I learned more than I wanted to about the worship of baal and asheroth this week. There were uhm lets just call it scatological and hematological elements to their sick and twisted, vile disgusting, evil, abhorrent sinful worship of baal. It was kind of like watching a train wreck.

The canaanites that worshiped baal and asheroth were an absolute filthy contrast to the true and holy God Yahweh.

That such a filthy vile religion attracted a follower of Yahweh is surprising and disgusting, and shocking. I think the vile nature of it is in part what leads God to call for the complete destruction of Jericho, and then later is part of why God has such a strong reaction to one of his people keeping these "devoted" items.

Its as though God had drawn a line in the sand, and on the one side were people that were earnestly, sincerely following God, if in a bumbling way. On the other side, were the canaanites. If there was a thing they could do that was the exact opposite of what God wanted, they seemed to embrace it. By keeping those devoted items, achan had crossed the line from the side of God's people, to the vile side of those living in absolute rebellion against God.

There have been a few times in my life, where I've seen situations where I felt like God took someone home rather than see them cause more harm. I knew a guy that caused a couple of churches and a bunch of families great harm, division, and heart ache using his mouth. He ended up getting mouth cancer suddenly, and died shortly there after. I won't say that God killed the man, but when you look at all the circumstances, its possible!

Thirdly I notice they didn't fully fear God. John MacArthur has taught, in a spirit faithful to Romans 3:18: "The problem with people is they don't fear God. They're not afraid of God. They're afraid they might lose their bank account. Afraid they might lose their health. They're afraid they might lose their fitness. They're afraid they might lose their marriage partner, children. The one they really should fear is God. And when there's no fear of Him before their eyes they are in eternal danger." *(Flag March 2026 — unverifiable as verbatim quotation; no exact source found in Grace to You archive. The sentiment is authentically MacArthur's — his sermon "Humanity on Trial" (gty.org/sermons/80-343) on Romans 3:9–20 treats Rom 3:18 as the capstone of Paul's indictment, and the passage "no fear of God before their eyes" is central to his teaching. Treat as paraphrase of his teaching, not a direct quotation, in any print context.)*

If the people of God, had maintained an appropriate fear of God, then they never would have found themselves in this position.

I want to jump ahead to chapter 8. The nation has repented, and realized their mistakes. They finally decide to seek God's input. Vs 1 God tells them not to be afraid, that he's with them, that the city of AI has been given into their hand. God also lays out their plan. Take ALL your soldiers, not just some. Kill the king, and keep the cities resources. Oh and by the way, Attack it from behind.

One quote I love from Baptist history is by a man named Hudson Taylor. Have you ever heard of Hudson Taylor? He was always one of my favorite missionaries. At 21 he felt God leading him to be a missionary to china. So he packed everything up, and left. His first few years there were met with difficulty and disappointment. The field was hard and unfertile, the few other missionaries present were in his words, worldly, and unengaged. Most received a stipend from their sending agency, but also worked as interpreters for business men.

Hudson Taylor by contrast bought local clothing, dressed like the locals, had a haircut like the locals, and basically tried to be like the locals to the extent that he was able. He eschewed the company of foreign nationals – the English speaking business men his peers spent their time with in favor of actual Chinese people.

He quickly decided to move inland. He bought up, and imported bibles, and took off for the Chinese inland. About that time, his missionary sending agency ran out of funds, and he decided that if God had him there, God could sustain him there. So he left the Methodists and became an independent missionary.

That surely required a lot of faith. Many people would have returned home. But he redoubled his efforts.

He wrote: "Can all the Christians of England sit still with folded arms while these multitudes [in China] are perishing—perishing for lack of knowledge—for lack of that knowledge which England possesses so richly?" *(Verified March 2026 — confirmed from China: Its Spiritual Need and Claims, 1865. Sermon had omitted "of England"; restored here. Taylor was directly addressing English Christians. Source is authentic.)*

He kept serving. Now married, with a child, the second opium war broke out, and trade was shut down with England. He found himself in pretty dire straits. The Chinese economy went from one of the largest in the world to one greatly diminished. Meanwhile illegal opium flooded in from the west – especially the UK, but we americans were not innocent. The ancestors of FDR, and also John Kerry made much of their family fortunes through the illegal smuggling of opium into China. *(Corrected — "state sponsored" removed: the British East India Company's trade was state-sponsored; American merchants including Warren Delano Jr. [FDR's grandfather] and the Forbes family [Kerry's maternal line] were private actors, not U.S. government agents. The U.S. Treaty of Wangxia, 1844, explicitly disavowed American opium smuggling. Also corrected: "into the heart of China" overstated — the trade operated at coastal ports: Canton, Macao, Hong Kong, Lintin Island. The core claim — both families documented in the opium trade — is accurate. Sources: National Archives, American Heritage, Wikipedia/Warren Delano Jr., Wikipedia/Forbes family.)*

Now with 4 kids, and a burgeoning missions organization under his command he pressed on. He had as much or more western medical knowledge than did anyone else in the country at the time, his medical practice served at its peak 200 people each day – with only Hudson Taylor to doctor them.

He began to pray for help. God sent 76 missionaries. He prayed for more help, and God sent 102 more missionaries. By 1900 his organization was the largest single Protestant mission in China, accounting for roughly a third of all missionaries there. *(Verified March 2026 — 76 confirmed; "106" corrected to 102 per CIM/OMF records. "Nearly half" overstated — CIM held ~37% of Protestant missionaries ca. 1900.)* Each year more and more missionaries were brought into China even after Taylor had to retire due to his health. His life inspired many to travel to one of the more difficult missions locations in the world, so that they might share the gospel. As a result now, today, there are something like 200,000,000 believers in Jesus Christ in China. *(See flags — 200 million figure varies widely by source; Pew, ChinaSource, and Open Doors all give different numbers. Flag as approximate.)*

One of my favorite quotes of Taylor was that "God's work, done in God's way will never lack God's supply." *(Verified March 2026 — well-attested. The documented form includes "in": "done in God's way." The full attested text also opens "Depend on it." and closes with a note about God being too wise to frustrate His purposes for lack of funds. Wording corrected here; use full form in print if quoting formally.)*

The Israelites found this to be true at Ai as well, vs 27 of chapter 8 says "Only the livestock and the spoil of that city Israel took as their plunder, according to the word of the LORD that he had commanded Joshua. So Joshua burned Ai and made it forever a heap of ruins, as it is to this day." *(Corrected March 2026 — ESV reads "Ai" not "AI"; "LORD" all-caps; "a heap of ruins" not "a ruin." Transcript had lowercase "lord" and "AI.")*

When they finally were doing God's work, God's way, the victory was complete and total.

I couldn't help but think about all the struggles people have that they try to fight in secret. I wonder what would have happened if Achan would have spoken up and said hey, I need help. Help keep me accountable. Or even if he had just mentioned his interest in the forbidden items. He likely would have never caused the terrible loss at Ai.

How many times in our lives, would a little accountability keep us from falling head long into trouble? Next time you feel the pull of temptation call someone, and ask them to help keep you accountable. Then pick up your bible, and spend some more time with your savior.

But it all starts with a relationship with God.

So let me tell you what that means. It doesn't mean a vague spiritual openness. It doesn't mean showing up on Sunday. It means repentance and faith in Jesus Christ — the true Joshua, the one whose name is the same, who did what Israel's Joshua only pointed to. He entered where we cannot, bore the herem we earned, and brings His people into rest. Don't wait until you're Achan with a pile of forbidden things buried under your tent. Repent now. Bring it to the light. And if you've never trusted Christ — not in your effort, not in your accountability partner, not in how hard you work — but in Him alone, His death in your place, His righteousness counted as yours — that is where it starts. That is the only crossing that matters.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date**: 2026-03-21
**Sermon**: Josh 7 & 8 — Leaving God Out / Achan and Ai
**Primary Text**: Joshua 7:4-13; Joshua 8:1, 27

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**The Strongest Manuscript in the Series — Gospel Nerve Restored, Exegetical Depth Emerging**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A substantially revised oral transcript that demonstrates genuine expositional growth — the herem/Christ typology, the true Joshua close, and the Galatians 3:13 / Hebrews 13:12 paragraph represent the most theologically grounded preaching in the Joshua series, though the sermon's middle section (the Hudson Taylor biographical detour) disrupts structural momentum and the conscience work remains underdeveloped for believers.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test**: PASS. The sermon's thesis — "Leaving God out leads to defeat; doing God's work in God's way through Christ brings victory" — demonstrably arises from Joshua 7:2-5 (prayer neglected → defeat at Ai) and Joshua 8:1, 27 (God's plan followed → complete victory). The herem/gospel parallel is the sermon's crowning expositional achievement.

**Authority Test**: PASS. The preacher submits to the text's hard material — the communal accountability for Achan's sin, the severity of herem judgment — and does not soften it. The gospel close (Christ bearing the herem we earned) is exegetically grounded in Hebrews 13:12 and is precisely the kind of submission to the text's uncomfortable implications that the rubric rewards.

**Berean Gate**: PASS — the sermon is genuinely text-driven, especially in its revised sections.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

No fatal flaw flags are triggered. This is the first sermon in this Joshua/evaluation batch to clear the Berean Gate with a clean pass and avoid all fatal flag conditions.

The gospel is proclaimed (Galatians 3:13, Hebrews 13:12, the true Joshua close). Christ is necessary to the sermon's argument. Application, while somewhat underdeveloped for believers, is not entirely generic. Exhortation is present and text-derived. The sermon is accessible to ordinary hearers.

**No score cap applied.**

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics (25 pts) — 18/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10)*: 7/10. The sermon derives its argument from the text: the neglect of prayer in Joshua 7:2 → defeat at Ai → herem violation → communal accountability → repentance → God's plan followed in Joshua 8 → complete victory. The herem/Christ parallel (Achan outside the camp; Christ outside the gate) is exegetically grounded in Heb. 13:11-12. The text's own climax at 8:27 is identified and quoted. Solid. Loses points for the Hudson Taylor section, which occupies substantial space not derived from any text at all.
- *Contextual Awareness (5)*: 4/5. The Jericho-Achan-Ai-circumcision sequence is well-narrated. The Deuteronomy 8:17-18 callback from the previous sermon is an effective expositional bridge.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5)*: 4/5. The herem vocabulary is explained carefully (three senses, with appropriate qualifications in the verification notes). The "outside the gate" parallel is explicitly tied to Heb. 13:12. The American sola bootstrapsa observation is culturally acute. Loses one point because the Hebrew herem analysis is in the notes but the in-text explanation somewhat overstates the sexual-sin lexical sense (acknowledged in flags).
- *Logical Faithfulness (5)*: 3/5. The sermon's central argument is exegetically faithful. The Hudson Taylor section (approximately 600 words) introduces a third thread — the history of China missions — that is not derived from the passage and disrupts the logical development.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity (18 pts) — 15/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7)*: 6/7. The Gal. 3:13 / Heb. 13:12 paragraph is the most theologically precise Christ-connection in the entire batch of sermons being evaluated. "Achan was dragged outside the camp and destroyed. Jesus walked outside the gate voluntarily, to bear what we deserved." This is organic, covenantal, and exegetically earned. The true Joshua close (Heb. 4:8) is equally strong.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3)*: 3/3. Full marks. The sermon places the Joshua narrative correctly in the redemptive-historical storyline, identifies the typological function of the herem, and connects it to the New Testament's own exegesis (Heb. 13:12; Heb. 4:8; Acts 7:45).
- *Doctrinal Precision (4)*: 3/4. Justification, repentance, and faith are clearly stated. The Spurgeon paraphrase is appropriately framed. ### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The herem/Christ paragraph**: "Achan was dragged outside the camp and destroyed. Jesus walked outside the gate voluntarily, to bear what we deserved." (Gal. 3:13; Heb. 13:12) — this is exegetically grounded, organically derived from the text's own vocabulary, and proclaimed with real force. 2. **The true Joshua close**: "He is the true Joshua, the one whose name is the same, who did what Israel's Joshua only pointed to. He entered where we cannot, bore the herem we earned, and brings His people into rest." This is Reformed Baptist preaching at its best — typological, christological, pastoral, and precise.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The Hudson Taylor section is too long**: At roughly 600 words covering Taylor's biography from age 21 to CIM's 1900 dominance, this section is a full sub-sermon grafted into the middle of an exposition. "God's work done in God's way will never lack God's supply" is a beautiful quote that connects to the passage — but it does not require five paragraphs of biography to deploy. The section should be compressed to a single paragraph or illustration.
2. **Conscience work for believers is underdeveloped**: The sermon identifies hidden sin and self-reliance as the twin dangers from the text, but it does not press believers with specificity. "What is buried under your tent right now?" is stated and then not pursued. The knowledgeable-but-unhumbled hearer — the church member who knows the doctrine and has not yet been broken by it — is not cornered.
3. **Penal substitution is present but not named**: The herem/Christ paragraph articulates the logic of penal substitution — Christ bore the curse we earned, outside the gate — without naming it. For a Baptist congregation that should be building a theological vocabulary, naming the doctrine and briefly explaining it strengthens rather than obscures the point.

The result is that the sermon's most powerful moment comes after a plateau, not at the peak.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The biographical richness of the Hudson Taylor section creates an impression of expositional depth — it looks like the kind of thing a well-read preacher does. But connecting Joshua 8:27 to Hudson Taylor's China mission via the "God's work done God's way" quote is not exposition of the text; it is thematic association. The real exposition — the herem/Christ paragraph, the true Joshua close — is done. The Taylor section is a strong illustration in search of a less crowded sermon.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Joshua 7:19-21 — Achan's confession: "I saw... I coveted... I took... I hid them." This is the most psychologically precise description of how sin works in the entire conquest narrative: see, covet, take, hide. The sermon notes the hiding but does not walk through the sequence. That four-step anatomy of temptation is native to the text, universally recognizable, and would press the conscience more specifically than the general accountability application.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Cut the Hudson Taylor section from five paragraphs to one**: Keep the quote ("God's work done in God's way will never lack God's supply"), one sentence of biography, and move immediately to the Ai victory and the herem/Christ paragraph. This recovers the climactic momentum.
2. **Preach Achan's four-step sequence**: "I saw, I coveted, I took, I hid — that is how sin always works. What have you seen this week? What have you coveted? What have you taken? What are you hiding?" This gives the conscience application teeth and textual roots.
3. **Name penal substitution**: After the herem/Christ paragraph, add one sentence: "This is what theologians call penal substitution — the penalty was real, the substitute was Christ, and the only thing you bring to that exchange is the sin that made it necessary."
4. **Address the knowledgeable-but-unhumbled directly**: "Some of you know all of this. You've heard it before. You can say it back to me. But the doctrine has not yet broken you — you have not yet stood at the gate with Achan and recognized yourself. That is the most dangerous place to be." This is Perkins' third category and this text demands it.
5. **Strengthen the "what is buried under your tent" moment**: Follow the image with specific contemporary equivalents — not lists, but one or two concrete scenarios pressed with pastoral directness. Then call for immediate action: "Name it. Confess it. Tonight. Not next Sunday."
