“The Gospel Chain: How Shall They Hear?”

Romans 10:14–21
Series: The Righteousness of God Revealed (Romans 9–11)
Introduction — The Nearness of Salvation and the Need for Proclamation

If you’ve brought your Bible—and I hope you have—open with me to Romans 10, beginning in verse 14.

Last week we rejoiced in one of the simplest and most sweeping invitations in Scripture:

“Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved.” (Romans 10:13)

Then Paul presses four urgent questions:
•	How will they call on Him in whom they have not believed?
•	How will they believe in Him of whom they have never heard?
•	How will they hear without someone preaching?
•	And how will they preach unless they are sent?

The gospel is not self-transmitting. It travels from mouth → ear → heart → heaven through the voices God sends. Grace is sovereign—yet grace sends.

Before we open the text, let’s pray for our People Group of the Week:

The Tuareg Berbers of North Africa—nearly two million desert nomads; fewer than one-hundredth of one percent follow Christ. Most have never met a believer. Pray that Christ would reveal Himself through Scripture, through dreams, and through sent servants; pray courage for the few who already carry His name across the dunes.

(Pause for prayer.)
I. The Chain of Salvation (vv. 14–15a) — Grace Ordains Both Ends and Means

Paul lays out five links:
1.	They must call on the Lord to be saved.
2.	They cannot call unless they believe.
3.	They cannot believe unless they hear.
4.	They cannot hear unless someone preaches.
5.	They cannot preach unless they are sent.

This is God’s ordained method. The same Lord who decrees whom He will save decrees how they will be saved—through proclamation.

The word for “sent” is ἀποστέλλω (apostellō)—to be commissioned with authority.
No Christian is self-appointed, and every Christian is sent.
Some cross oceans; all must cross streets.

Your voice is a link in someone’s chain—but the chain itself rests in God’s hands.
Even when our obedience falters, His plan does not.
He has already accounted for our weakness and woven even our failures into His design.
If we refuse, He will raise another messenger—yet what joy to be the one He uses.

Spurgeon said, “If sinners be damned, at least let them leap to hell over our bodies; let not one go unwarned or unprayed for.” That’s not hyperbole—that’s the logic of Romans 10.

(Transition: Having seen God’s method, Paul shows the beauty of those who carry it.)
II. The Beauty of the Messenger (v. 15b) — Obedience Over Glamour

We’ve been in the New Testament; let’s look back to the Old.
One of my sisters’ best friends growing up hated feet—dirty, smelly, stinky things that they are.
She about cried the first time she heard this verse.

Paul borrows from Isaiah 52:7:

“How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him who brings good news,
who publishes peace, who brings good news of happiness,
who publishes salvation, who says to Zion, ‘Your God reigns!’”

You think today’s feet are bad—dusty sneakers and flip-flops. In Isaiah’s day, the messenger came running over the mountains from exile, shouting, “Your God reigns!” His feet were cut, cracked, and caked with dust—but they were beautiful, because they carried the message of peace.

God measures beauty differently. He calls obedience beautiful.
He delights in worn-out messengers who keep running because they’ve been sent by the King.

Today the Lord isn’t looking for polished rhetoric; He’s looking for faithful obedience.
We don’t have to be great orators or people who always know the right words.
We just have to go—and trust the God who sends us to speak through us.

Remember Moses. He wasn’t eloquent; he tried every excuse. But God said,
“Who has made man’s mouth? … Now go, and I will be with your mouth and teach you what you shall speak.” (Exod. 4:11–12)

Moses went toe to toe with the most powerful man on earth—not because of his strength, but because of his weakness.
God filled his mouth with the right words, filled his heart with courage, and used him as His hands and feet to set His people free.

And the same God calls us in 2025 to do the same kind of work—to set people free from the bondage of sin.
Not by our eloquence, not by our power, but by the Spirit who raises the dead.

When we are obedient, the limitless God works through limited people.
Where we are finite, He is infinite.
Where we are weak, He is strong.
He gives us the privilege of stepping into what He is already doing.

And that is a beautiful thing.

The most beautiful feet on earth are those that carry the gospel—into refugee camps and prison blocks, into a cannibal’s hut or the house next door.
When grace takes hold of a heart, it not only redeems the soul—it recruits the feet.

(Pause.)
III. The Tragedy of Unbelief (vv. 16–18) — Hearing Without Heeding

Paul now turns from the beauty of obedience to the heartbreak of unbelief.

“But they have not all obeyed the gospel.” (v. 16)

He quotes Isaiah 53:1:

“Lord, who has believed what he has heard from us?”

Isaiah preached the Suffering Servant—the Christ who would be “pierced for our transgressions” and “crushed for our iniquities.” Yet his own people refused to believe. Paul says, “That’s still happening.” The gospel has been proclaimed, but not everyone obeys it.

The tragedy is not that people haven’t heard—it’s that they won’t believe.
The same sun that melts wax hardens clay.
The same Word that softens some hearts exposes and hardens others.

Verse 17 is the heartbeat of the passage:

“So faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ.”

Faith doesn’t come by osmosis or emotion.
It doesn’t rise from within; it comes down from above—through a message.
Saving faith is born when the Word of Christ is heard, believed, and obeyed.

That means no other path brings saving faith.
Dreams may stir curiosity—as we’ve seen in Muslim-majority nations.
Creation may testify—as it did to me this past week when I saw God’s fingerprints across the heavens.
Conscience may accuse—as we drink deeply from our own wells of shame.
But saving faith comes only through the proclaimed Word of Christ.

Then Paul asks, anticipating our objection:

“But I ask, have they not heard?” (v. 18)

And he answers with Psalm 19:

“Their voice has gone out to all the earth,
and their words to the ends of the world.”

Clarification: In context, Psalm 19 is creation’s voice declaring God’s glory.
Paul borrows that creation language to say the gospel has now gone out with similar reach—from Jerusalem outward—so that unbelief cannot plead ignorance.
Creation leaves us without excuse; the gospel leaves us without delay.
We are not promised tomorrow.

(Pause.)
IV. The Persistence of Grace (vv. 19–21) — Mercy That Refuses to Quit

Having shown us the tragedy of unbelief, Paul now turns our eyes to the mercy of God that keeps pursuing in spite of it.

“But I ask, did Israel not understand?” (v. 19)

They did. They had the Law, the prophets, the promises—all pointing to Christ. But they missed Him. And yet, God was not caught off guard.
Their rejection became the doorway for the nations.

Paul quotes Deuteronomy 32:21:

“I will make you jealous of those who are not a nation;
with a foolish nation I will make you angry.”

Then from Isaiah 65:1–2:

“I was found by those who did not seek Me;
I showed Myself to those who did not ask for Me.
All day long I have held out My hands to a disobedient and contrary people.”

This is grace refusing to quit.
When Israel turned inward, God turned outward.
When His chosen people hardened their hearts, He opened His heart to the Gentiles.
Grace overflowed its banks.

That’s us—the Gentiles. Grace has reached far past Israel’s borders, not because God abandoned His people, but because He is using our salvation to provoke their jealousy and draw them home again.
Even their unbelief cannot undo His promises.

History tells the same story. When the early believers huddled in Jerusalem, content to keep the gospel within their circle, God permitted the death of Stephen—and persecution scattered the church like seed.
What looked like tragedy was divine strategy.
Through blood and tears, the gospel spread to Judea, to Samaria, and to the ends of the earth.

God’s mission cannot be stopped.
Our failures cannot frustrate it.
Our silence cannot silence Him.

Grace is not fragile; it is ferocious.
It presses through rejection, persecution, and apathy until the nations rejoice and the remnant returns.

Verse 21 gives us one of the tenderest portraits in Scripture:

“All day long I have held out My hands to a disobedient and contrary people.”

Hands pierced, yet still extended.
Hands once stretched on a cross, now stretched in invitation.
That’s the patience of God—the same patience that waited for you.
The same patience that urges us now: Go. Speak. Obey.

Because grace doesn’t give up when rejected; it widens the circle.
It draws more people into the story of redemption.
It takes the gospel to the ends of the earth.

(Pause.)V. The Call of the Church — The Chain Must Not Break Here

Romans 10 ends where the Great Commission begins.
If faith comes by hearing and hearing through the word of Christ, then the sending of the church is not a project—it is our very purpose.

God has chosen to use redeemed mouths to proclaim His redeeming mercy.
The same Spirit who raised Christ from the dead now empowers those He sends.
The question is never if we are sent, but how faithfully we will go.

A. Three Pastoral Appeals

1) To the Believer
You are already sent. You may never board a plane, but you can cross the street.
The Spirit who raised Jesus from the tomb lives within you—and He has already factored in your weakness.
He intends to use you, warts and all, to make Christ known.
He does not need your perfection; He delights in your obedience.

2) To the Complacent
The unreached are not unreachable.
God delights to use ordinary people with open Bibles.
The question isn’t should I be involved? The real question is, why haven’t I started already?
The command to “Go” brooks no excuses. To delay is to disobey.

3) To the Skeptic
Those same outstretched hands of verse 21 reach toward you even now.
You have heard the Word of Christ; will you call upon His name?
He stands ready to forgive, ready to save.
His yoke is easy, and His burden is light.

B. The “Go” Charge

From Genesis to Revelation, God commands His people to Go.
“Go therefore and make disciples of all nations” (Matthew 28:18–20).
“You will be My witnesses in Jerusalem, Judea, Samaria, and to the end of the earth” (Acts 1:8).

“Go” isn’t a suggestion; it’s a royal command from the sovereign King of Kings and Lord of Lords who purchased us with His blood.
The Greek construction carries the force: Go now, and keep going until you can go no more.
There is no “too old,” no “too infirm,” no “too late.”

Where do we go? All nations.
The ones we like, and the ones we’d rather avoid.
Those eager to hear, and those likely to resist.
To animists in Africa; to Muslims in the Middle East; to the cannibals of the global south; even to those who may greet us with spears or take our lives or the lives of our children for Christ’s sake. We are commanded to go.

Each name represents souls—image-bearers—within reach of your dinner table.

“I’m not healthy enough to go to China.” Are you healthy enough to go out for dinner? Then you can reach China at the Chinese restaurant where the staff must be kind as you speak of Jesus.
“I can’t go to India.” Then drive to the gas station on Hudson Avenue—you can meet India behind the counter.
The nations have come to us.
“Every name you just heard? That’s a soul. That’s a neighbor. That’s a gas station attendant. That’s a restaurant full of employees. That’s your favorite waiter or waitress. All hell-bound—won’t you go?”
Up the street at the Chinese restaurant, we order food almost every week.
The woman who answers the phone knows us. She knows our voice. She knows our face.
She even knows when something isn’t right.
One night we sent someone else to pick up our order, and she refused to release it—she called us herself and said, “You are NOT Greg Baker. This is his food.”
She defended forty dollars’ worth of dinner with more care than I have shown for her eternal soul.
She has shown more love for me over takeout than I have shown her over eternity.
She knows us, and yet—we have never once shared the gospel with her.
I too need to go.
Ezekiel 33 says the watchman who sees danger and does not warn will be held to account (Ezek. 33:6–7). Paul could say, “I am innocent of the blood of all” (Acts 20:26). Brothers and sisters—won’t you go?
I have this picture in my mind of the lost marching blindfolded toward a cliff—step after step, unaware of the edge before them.
Our task is to share the gospel and remove the blindfold.
How can we who know the danger not take part in God’s mission to save the world?
How can we remain silent when eternity hangs in the balance?  Charles Spurgeon once pleaded:
“If sinners be damned, let them leap to hell over our bodies. If they perish, let them perish with our arms about their knees, imploring them to stay. Let hell surround us in this life, rather than them for eternity in the next—unquenchable fire, undying worm, endless anguish, eternal wrath. Let these terrors drive us to weep, agonize, plead, and even die for the lost. The infinite justice of the Eternal should be the motive that stirs our souls to action.”
Spurgeon: “If sinners be damned, at least let them leap to hell over our bodies; let not one go unwarned or unprayed for.”
May it be said of this church: that We made it hard for our neighbors, our community, and the nations to go to hell.
D. God’s Sovereign Chain

When we keep silent, the chain feels like it falters—but in truth, the chain belongs to God.
He forges every link: the sending, the preaching, the hearing, the believing, the calling.
Our obedience is the hammer in His hand; His eternal counsel is the anvil that will never bend.

When Jonah fled, God sent a storm.
When Stephen fell, God sent a scattering.
When one voice was silenced, the Spirit raised ten more.
The chain held firm. The gospel always finds a way.

So let’s not be found idle while the King’s work goes on without us.
Let it be said of this church: We were not the missing link in God’s mission; we were the faithful ones He used.
Conclusion — The Sound That Saves
Paul began this chapter with a heart burdened for his kinsmen and ends with God’s hands open to the world.
The gospel chain—sending, preaching, hearing, believing, calling—rests securely in the hands of the sovereign God who forged it.
He will not let it break.
Yet He invites us to take our place in it, to feel its weight, and to join its song.
“How beautiful are the feet of those who bring good news.”
Beautiful—not because they’re admired, but because they’re obedient.
Dusty feet that cross boundaries. Weary feet that climb stairs to share the gospel.
Hands that hold Scripture in another language. Lips that speak the name above every name. Church—the Lord who saved you is the Lord who sends you.
The same grace that called you now commissions you.
The same power that raised Jesus now moves through you.

So Go—to the corner and to the ends of the earth.
Go have dinner at the restaurant, but Go with the gospel. Go to the gas station, and take the gospel to India. Go to the grocery store, and give a tract to the girl at the counter. Go wherever God tells you. Go—until every ear has heard, every knee has bowed, and every tongue confesses that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father.

Let the church be found faithful; let the chain not break with us.
May our obedience be the echo of Isaiah’s cry, “Here am I, send me.”
And may our lives bear the dust of beautiful feet that carried the gospel of peace.
