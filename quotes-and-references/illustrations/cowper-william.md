## The Story

William Cowper (1731–1800) is remembered as one of England's finest hymn writers and poets. He is less remembered for what God brought him through before he wrote a single line.

In 1763, facing a legal examination for a government position he had been appointed to but was terrified to occupy, Cowper descended into a total mental breakdown. He attempted to end his life four times:

1. **Poison** — He obtained laudanum, drank it, and expected to die. A friend arrived unexpectedly, recognized what had happened, and got a physician there in time to reverse the effects.
2. **The Thames** — He ordered a coach to take him to the river to drown himself. The coachman, seeing what he intended, held him back and returned him home.
3. **His knife** — He fell on his knife to end his life. The blade snapped, leaving him barely scratched.
4. **A rope** — He went into his barn, fashioned a noose, and hanged himself. He had not tied the rope correctly; it strangled rather than killed him. A friend arrived and cut him down before he died.

Afterward, a friend told him to read his Bible. He opened to the third chapter of Romans and, for the first time, truly met God.

⚠️ **Passage verification note:** Multiple biographers cite Romans 3:25 as the specific verse. The primary source is Cowper's own spiritual autobiography, *Adelphi* (written c. 1767, published 1816). *Adelphi* has not been directly consulted in preparing this entry. Before citing a specific verse from the pulpit, verify against *Adelphi* or James King, *William Cowper: A Biography* (Duke University Press, 1986). "The third chapter of Romans" is defensible; "Romans 3:25" specifically should be confirmed from a primary source.

Years later, Cowper — alongside John Newton — wrote the *Olney Hymns* (1779). One of those hymns was this:

## Hymn Text: "God Moves in a Mysterious Way"

*William Cowper, Olney Hymns (1779), Hymn 35*

> God moves in a mysterious way
> His wonders to perform;
> He plants His footsteps in the sea
> And rides upon the storm.
>
> Deep in unfathomable mines
> Of never-failing skill
> He treasures up His bright designs
> And works His sovereign will.
>
> Ye fearful saints, fresh courage take;
> The clouds ye so much dread
> Are big with mercy, and shall break
> In blessings on your head.
>
> Judge not the Lord by feeble sense,
> But trust Him for His grace;
> Behind a frowning providence
> He hides a smiling face.
>
> His purposes will ripen fast,
> Unfolding every hour;
> The bud may have a bitter taste,
> But sweet will be the flower.
>
> Blind unbelief is sure to err
> And scan His work in vain;
> God is His own interpreter,
> And He will make it plain.

**Stanzas most used in preaching:** 1 and 4 ("Judge not the Lord by feeble sense…")
**Stanzas quoted in `ex 6-7.md`:** 1 and 2 ("Deep in unfathomable mines…")

## Application Notes

This illustration works in any sermon addressing:
- **Discouragement / depression among believers** — Cowper was a Christian when this happened. The slough does not sort by faith level.
- **God's hidden providence** — God was moving in every one of those four failures. The rope that didn't break correctly was not chance.
- **Redemption through suffering** — What looked like catastrophe became the frame for some of the most enduring hymns in the English language.
- **The hiddenness of grace before conversion** — God was present before Cowper knew His name. This is the gospel in miniature.
- **Transition from despair to worship** — From suicide attempts to "God is His own interpreter, and He will make it plain." Few biographical arcs demonstrate this more starkly.
