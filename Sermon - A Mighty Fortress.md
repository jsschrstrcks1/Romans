# A Mighty Fortress
## Psalm 46 (with 2 Chronicles 20 and Reformation history)

---

We're going to be in Psalm 46 today — if you've brought your Bible, and I hope you have. I love the story behind our psalm. If you decide you want to look it up later, it's in 2 Chronicles 20.

The long and short of it is this: Jehoshaphat was a pretty good king compared to many at this time. He ruled over Judah. He is remembered for being vigilant in regards to idols in the country — he sought them out and destroyed them. He sent the priests out to instruct everyone on the proper worship of the Lord and the Lord's laws.

He did make some missteps though. You remember Ahab and Jezebel — the king and queen Elijah worked hard against? Well, that idolatrous pair had a daughter, and she married Jehoshaphat. His head was briefly turned by the idolatry of his wife, and his mission against idols was affected by this. But several of the Lord's prophets who were also ministering at this time interacted with him — especially Jehu — and he repented and once more returned to the Lord.

In the midst of all this, the Lord's enemies banded together and surrounded Judah. Israel and Judah were not on good terms — they were competing nations. The main difference between the two was that while Judah was smaller, they were following God; Israel was led by those who followed the Baals. Israel was antagonistic towards Judah and tried to gain an upper hand financially on a number of occasions. Kings talks about Jehoshaphat's fleet of ships sinking, and Israel basically twisting Judah's arm into allowing them to ferry their goods home at usurious rates.

So Israel was no ally at this point. Beyond the Jordan, in the country now named Jordan, a little north into Syria, and a little south and east into modern-day Saudi Arabia, were the Moabites, the Ammonites, and the Meunites. Jordan has a city called Amman — Syria has a city called Moab. [Note: ancient Moab was located east of the Dead Sea in what is now modern Jordan, not Syria; this geographic claim appears to be an error. The Meunites were also likely from the region of modern Jordan, not Saudi Arabia — exact location uncertain.] Anyway, these were largely kin to Abraham. Moab and Ben-Ammi were Lot's children through his daughters' incestuous relationship (Genesis 19:36–38). Over time they developed a pretty antagonistic relationship with the descendants of Abraham — they were quarrelsome, and both the Ammonites and Moabites hired someone to curse the nation of Israel.

Anyway — all these enemies to the north-east, east, and south-east, all united, and ready to destroy them.

Jehoshaphat wasn't stupid. He saw the threat. He knew there were far more who wanted to see Judah fall than there were humans who would fight for Judah. But he also knew he had a Protector — a Helper, a sovereign King of Kings and Lord of Lords — who was fighting for him.

So in 2 Chronicles 20, Jehoshaphat cries out to God, pleading for help. He humbles himself. He admits he can't do it alone, and pleads with God for his mercy and strength to get through this unwanted battle. He gathered all the people together, said all these things, and reminded them of the things the Lord had brought them through.

At that point, Jahaziel — son of Zechariah — delivered a message from God: [Note: the sermon names "the prophet Zechariah," but 2 Chronicles 20:14 identifies the prophet as Jahaziel son of Zechariah; it was Jahaziel who delivered the word, not Zechariah his father.]

> "Listen, all Judah and inhabitants of Jerusalem and King Jehoshaphat: Thus says the LORD to you, 'Do not be afraid and do not be dismayed at this great horde, for the battle is not yours but God's. Tomorrow go down against them. Behold, they will come up by the ascent of Ziz. You will find them at the end of the valley, east of the wilderness of Jeruel. You will not need to fight in this battle. Stand firm, hold your position, and see the salvation of the LORD on your behalf, O Judah and Jerusalem. Do not be afraid and do not be dismayed. Tomorrow go out against them, and the LORD will be with you.'"
> — 2 Chronicles 20:15–17, ESV

[Note: the sermon quoted this passage in NIV; converted to ESV above.]

At that moment the nation broke out in songs of worship. Those words were in the last decade or so turned back to music and made popular by Chris Tomlin — the song goes, "Give thanks to the Lord, our God and King, his love endures forever" (drawn from 2 Chronicles 20:21 and Psalm 136). In typical Chris Tomlin style he blended in a number of other lines of worship from Scripture.

After worship, the army of Judah got up the next day and marched where God told them to go — worshipping all the way — and what did they find? The bodies of their enemies everywhere, all over the ground. So many that they were sure no man from the deployed armies of any of these nations had survived.

So they turned to bringing home the amassed treasure of the three armies — and found there was more than they could carry home in one trip. It took three days of all the army of Judah hauling treasure back to Jerusalem to retrieve it all.

On the fourth day, once their chores were done, they went back to worshipping the Lord and called the place "valley of worship" in Hebrew.

That is the backstory behind our psalm tonight. In the overflow of relief, and joy, and thanksgiving for the Lord's provision, they wrote this psalm. Let's read it — Psalm 46.

---

Another interesting fact: this passage was the passage that inspired Martin Luther to write "A Mighty Fortress Is Our God."

It was written during the Protestant Reformation. You might remember we talked about this — how the Catholic Church had become quite corrupt. It had taken to selling forgiveness of sins, calling them indulgences. If you knew you were going to have an affair on your wife next week, you could go to the Catholic Church, offer them enough money, and they would give you a piece of paper saying your sins were covered.

I actually found one of these once upon a time — it dated back to the 1970s and according to its inscription entitled the bearer to get out of church for a calendar year without it being seen as a sin by the Catholic Church.

In any event, on what we now call Halloween — and what was and should still be called Reformation Day — Martin Luther wrote about the unbiblical nature of the indulgences and some other things in a document called the 95 Theses. He mailed a copy off to Rome and nailed a second copy to the front door of his church. This is seen as the start of the Protestant Reformation.

Going forward, he was faced with death threats, threats to be cast out of the community, and all sorts of other frightening things. There were murders and imprisonments. It was an intense time. Martin Luther was one of the few men who have lived with the strength of will to endure and succeed through that period. He was certainly outnumbered and surrounded — enemies on every side — but he had God on his side. As a result, the ball of reform started rolling, one that ultimately led to the Southern Baptists.

In hindsight, there were basically five pillars of the Reformation: Scripture alone, Faith alone, Grace alone, through Christ alone, to the glory of God alone.

These five things became the rallying cry of not only Luther, but shortly thereafter John Calvin and a number of others. The longer the Reformation went on, the more people realized just how far the church had strayed from Scripture. That redoubled their interest in Scripture and their focus on keeping God's word at the center of their thinking. They realized quickly that Scripture indicates that faith alone leads to salvation — that it doesn't require a priest as intermediary, that God's grace alone is sufficient to save anyone. That Christ was the only way for all of that to happen, and that not only was this done for the glory of God, but we should do everything we do for God's glory.

These things were widely accepted, and a number of groups splintered off from Rome — the Church of England, the Lutherans, and a number of others who realized that what Luther taught was true and that the Bible needed to be their source, not Catholic tradition.

Ultimately that led to one of Luther's companions — a man named Balthasar Hubmaier — to re-evaluate other things, like baptism. [Note: the sermon calls him "Balthazar Hubmair" and describes him as Luther's "chief lieutenant." Hubmaier was actually more closely associated with Zwingli's reform circle in Zurich than with Luther; describing him as Luther's lieutenant is historically imprecise. He was martyred in Vienna in 1528.] At this point, baptism was in pragmatic terms a way to keep track of who had what kids — functionally a form of census, even among the Reformers.

But what does the Bible say about baptism?

Exactly.

So Hubmaier said, "We're not done reforming yet — we still have areas in conflict with Scripture. We need to return to the practice of believers' baptism." Luther said, "You know, you're right — but I think that might be too much change." So Hubmaier and a group of his friends baptized each other. They ended up being called the Anabaptists. Lots of other groups were subsequently labeled Anabaptists as well — it became the pejorative thrown at anyone who believed the Reformers hadn't gone far enough. After a few years this group splintered, and part of them went to Switzerland — one of whom was Idelette de Bure, the widow who married the French theologian John Calvin. They discipled a number of people, and through further splintering, more travel, and growth, a group of people whose theological pedigree included Luther, Calvin, Hubmaier, and the Anglican Church of England all got on a boat and set sail for a new world — united in at least five things: Scripture alone, Faith alone, Grace alone, through Christ alone, for the glory of God alone.

Once they arrived there was more growing and splintering, but ultimately those men — and a few others — are responsible for the Protestant church in all its nearly 29,000 denominations today. [Note: the 29,000 figure is a commonly cited approximate; estimates vary widely by methodology.]

But it was a dark time — especially during the period when Luther spoke up. There had easily been over a hundred martyrs in the hundred or so years before Luther for speaking up against the Catholic Church. [Note: approximate pastoral figure; cannot be verified precisely.]

So when Luther penned those heartfelt words — a paraphrase of Psalm 46 — he too was surrounded by enemies on pretty much all sides. Outnumbered. Outgunned. He was left with little else he could do except, like Jehoshaphat, go to the Lord in prayer. God gave Luther a hymn, and he worshipped God.

Ultimately I think it worked out pretty well for Luther. Hubmaier's name is all but lost today — he was martyred — but he's still an important part of our own history.

---

Which brings us to today. I know you guys. I know what most of you are going through.

Maybe you feel completely surrounded right now — like Jehoshaphat, enemies on every side, trying to drag you down. Or like Luther, with a monumental, overwhelming, and completely vital task ahead of you. Or maybe it's not you — maybe you're watching your kids struggle, or your family struggle. That's a hard place to be. It can be depressing. It can make one's heart ache, especially when you're talking about the struggles of the people you love.

But what do we know? Pastor Bob always says, "You and God are the majority." When you're surrounded by enemies like Luther was — people who wanted him dead or imprisoned — having God on your side is more powerful than the armies of the world. More powerful than any circumstance, any medical trouble, any situation you can imagine. Having God on your side means the battle is won.

Maybe not always the way or the time frame we would expect. But always in the way that's best for us and the people around us in the long run.

God is so good to us. But it all starts with a relationship with God.
