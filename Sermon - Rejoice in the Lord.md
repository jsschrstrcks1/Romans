## Rejoice in the Lord (Philippians 3:1)

The Greek root word is *chairo* (χαίρω), which means to be in a state of happiness and well-being — to rejoice, to be glad. *(Corrected March 2026 — prior definition "because of what you hear" is not part of the lexical meaning; that was a contextual application, not the word itself. BDAG: "to be in a state of happiness and well-being, rejoice, be glad.")*

Second clause: "To write the same things to you is no trouble to me and is safe for you." Paul says repetition is not laziness — it is safety. The congregation needs to hear the command to rejoice again and again because circumstances will drown it out.

- Rejoice in my persecution — Paul knew there was a chance he would die (1:21). Ultimately Paul would be killed in the wake of the fire, and at the order of Nero.
- Rejoice in what you hear
- Rejoice that their beloved friends' needs are being met
- Rejoice that many are hearing the truth of the gospel

### How often do we let circumstances get us down?

- Our health
- Our jobs
- Our kids
- Our loved ones' health

Paul says Rejoice — rejoice in the gospel, rejoice in the Bible:

> Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice.
> — Philippians 4:4 (ESV)

> Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds, for you know that the testing of your faith produces steadfastness. And let steadfastness have its full effect, that you may be perfect and complete, lacking in nothing.
> — James 1:2-4 (ESV)

## God's Sovereignty Allows Us Rest

If God is in control, what right have we to fret and fuss?

> And which of you by being anxious can add a single hour to his span of life?
> — Matthew 6:27 (ESV)

> Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God.
> — Philippians 4:6 (ESV)

A dear friend of mine once exclaimed "How can God be fully sovereign over the earth, if bad things happen to children?"

- We talked about abortion last week. God will take care of His own.
- Sin was allowed as a possibility, or else Man couldn't have really had free will.
- When Man sinned, he polluted mankind for the rest of eternity. Hudson Beach is periodically closed due to "pollution" — what that means is that someone's septic tank has ruptured and taints the water around the beach for weeks. No one can swim in that without also being tainted. Adam unwittingly ensured that everyone who would be born subsequent to them would have a similar taint.
- It is this taint, this evil, that Man embraced through Adam that causes little kids to be hurt. God in His sovereignty takes these little ones who have been marred by violence, or abuse, or death, and uses them to achieve a good purpose. What our enemy means for evil, God means for good.
- God uses our lives, our stories, our pain, struggles, grief, our cancer, our shingles, our messed up lungs, and failing knees to make an orchestra of redemption to reach the world around us.
- God does not cause sin, but He is capable of turning evil into good in a person's life.
- The suffering God asks us to go through is less than spending an eternity in hell.
- Further consider the cost of your sin: your sin caused greater suffering for Christ than anything he will ask you or anyone else to do. He has led by example. He has suffered more for your sake than you will be asked to suffer for Him. Probably none of us will ever be asked to endure a beating with a Roman scourge. *(Corrected March 2026 — "cat of nine tails" is an 18th-century British naval instrument. The Roman flagrum (or flagellum) was the instrument used on Jesus — a short whip with multiple leather thongs embedded with bone, metal, or stone. Replaced with "Roman scourge" for accuracy.)* While some of us may be called to die for our Lord, no one who believes in Jesus Christ as their Lord and Savior will have to endure separation from God as Christ did on the cross.

## Joshua and Caleb: The Evil Report

Remember the story of Joshua and Caleb? Moses and the Israelites had been wandering for 40 years. About two years in, they reach the promised land and decide to send spies in. 12 spies sent out, and 10 returned afraid their God couldn't keep his promise. Only Joshua and Caleb believed God could do it. As a result God was angry and they spent the next 38 years wandering the desert till they had all died out.

Thomas Watson writes: *(Corrected March 2026 — previously attributed to "D. L. Moody." Moody compiled this quote in his anthology One Thousand and One Thoughts from My Library (1898), p. 314, but the words belong to Thomas Watson, the English Puritan (c. 1620–1686). Watson is the author; Moody is the compiler.)*

> It honors religion, it proclaims to the world we serve a good Master. Cheerfulness is a friend to grace: it puts the heart in tune to praise God. Uncheerful Christians, like the spies, bring an evil report of the good land; others suspect there is something unpleasant in religion, that they who profess it hang their harps upon the willows, and walk so dejectedly. Be serious, yet cheerful. Rejoice in the Lord always.
> — Thomas Watson, compiled in D. L. Moody, *One Thousand and One Thoughts from My Library* (1898), p. 314

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
Pastoral Warmth Without Exegetical Traction — A Heart in the Right Place, Missing the Text's Own Argument

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A well-intentioned encouragement to rejoice through hardship that draws from Philippians 3:1 as a launching point but never returns to the passage's own argument, leaving the congregation with biblical sentiment rather than biblical preaching.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**FAIL — Conditional.**

*Scopus Test:* The stated text is Philippians 3:1 ("Finally, my brothers, rejoice in the Lord"). The sermon's thesis — "God's sovereignty permits and sustains rejoicing through hardship" — is not unreasonable as a *topic*, but it is not the main point Philippians 3:1 is making within its own argument. Paul's command in 3:1 functions as a hinge before his sharp warning against false teachers (the "dogs," the "mutilators of the flesh" in 3:2). The context is not primarily suffering-through-hardship; it is the protection of gospel joy against doctrinal corruption. The sermon uses Phil. 3:1 as a title verse and then departs entirely into sovereign providence, Old Testament narratives, and application lists. There is no sustained engagement with the passage or its argument.

*Authority Test:* The preacher does not stand above the text as an editor — the tone is humble. However, the sermon does not stand under the text either, because it never truly engages the text's own argument. It uses the verse as a launchpad.

**Assessment:** The sermon does not fully satisfy the Scopus Test. The main point of the sermon does not demonstrably arise from the main point of Philippians 3:1 in its context. However, because these are *sermon notes*, not a full transcript, and because the direction is not *contrary* to biblical truth, a full gate-stop is not warranted. The evaluation proceeds — but the score will reflect the exposition gap substantially. The preacher must return to the text.

This preacher is talking *to* his people, not at them.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The text is a title, not a governing argument.** Philippians 3:1 is cited once, Greek word defined, second clause noted — and then the sermon departs and never returns. The congregation receives a theologically themed talk about sovereignty and rejoicing, not an opening of what Paul is actually saying and why he is saying it to the Philippians. The critical context — the coming warning against the "dogs," the contrast between confidence in the flesh and confidence in Christ (3:3–11) — gives Phil. 3:1 its urgency and meaning, and it is entirely absent.

2. **The gospel is present but not proclaimed as the ground of joy.** The sermon asserts that God is good and sovereign, and therefore we can rejoice. This is true but incomplete. The distinctively Christian ground of rejoicing — that we are justified, that nothing can separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus, that the worst thing that can happen to us has already been absorbed by Christ — is only hinted at (Heb. 12:1–3 closing). The mechanism of salvation is never stated. Why can *I*, a sinner, rejoice? Because Christ bore my separation from God. This must be explicit, not assumed.

3. **Application is a generic list, not a conscience-searching press.** "Be honest with your struggles. Wear your faith on your sleeve. Deny your own wants." These are genuine Christian disciplines, but they are stated without grounding, without distinction among hearers, and without exhortation. No one is singled out, cornered, comforted with precision, or summoned to a specific act.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears to be unaware that the text he has chosen has a more specific and sharp argument than he is using. Philippians 3:1 is not primarily a text about sovereign providence — it is a pastoral warning protecting gospel joy from doctrinal threat. The preacher's genuine strength is pastoral comfort; this may be causing him to read Phil. 3:1 as a comfort text when it is actually a watchfulness text. The result is a sermon that is tonally kind but exegetically distant from what Paul is doing.

Additionally, the sermon moves through the problem of evil and free will territory — a genuinely hard doctrinal area — without apparent awareness that the classical Reformed answer (the compatibility of divine decree and human responsibility without libertarian free will) differs from the free-will theodicy implied by the phrase "Sin was allowed as a possibility, or else Man couldn't have really had free will." This may not reflect the preacher's full theology, but in notes that reach the congregation, it warrants more care.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The sovereignty section *feels* strong because it covers familiar ground with familiar examples — Elijah, Jonah, Abraham, Job. The congregation likely finds this section encouraging. But the illustrations function as an anthology of sovereignty proofs rather than as cumulative exegetical argument. Each example demonstrates sovereignty in isolation; none of them connects to how sovereignty specifically *grounds* rejoicing in Christ. The section is theologically true but structurally decorative — it does not build toward anything. Strength is assumed from the volume of examples rather than from depth of argument.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The richest missed opportunity is in the *telos* of rejoicing in Philippians. Paul does not ground rejoicing in sovereign providence alone — he grounds it in knowing Christ, in the surpassing worth of knowing Him (3:8), in being found in Him with a righteousness not his own (3:9). The sermon stays at "God is sovereign," which is true, but misses Paul's personal, relational, and Christological ground of rejoicing. Phil. 3:1 anticipates Phil. 3:7–11. The sermon never makes that move. The difference between "rejoice because God controls things" and "rejoice because you are in Christ, who is your righteousness and your life" is the difference between theism and the gospel.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Actionable)

1. **Anchor the sermon in Phil. 3:1's context.** Before moving to the sovereignty section, spend two to three minutes in 3:2–3. "Paul is about to warn these people against the 'dogs.' Why does he say 'rejoice' first? Because he knows that false doctrine kills joy. He is inoculating their joy *before* warning them of the threat to it. Our joy must be located in Christ, not in circumstances — because if it is in circumstances, the false teachers can take it." This grounds the command in the text's own logic.

2. **State the gospel explicitly as the ground of rejoicing.** In the sovereignty section or the application, add one paragraph: "You can rejoice not merely because God controls your circumstances — but because your standing before God is secure. You are justified. Not because of what you have done — but because Christ bore what you deserved. That is the final ground of your joy. Nothing can take it."

3. **Add differentiated application.** Address at least three categories of hearers: (a) the grieving believer who is tempted to despair — comfort them with the sovereignty and the Hebrews 12 passage; (b) the comfortable believer who has never learned to rejoice *through* hardship — warn them that untested faith is shallow; (c) the unbeliever present who does not have access to this joy yet — tell them how to enter it, through Christ.

4. **Remove or restructure the free-will theodicy section.** The "sin was allowed as a possibility" argument requires more careful handling for a Reformed congregation. Either state the Reformed position clearly (God ordains all things including the fall, while remaining unstained by evil — Joseph: "You meant it for evil, God meant it for good") or remove the free-will framing entirely. As written, it may confuse more than it helps.

5. **Give the exhortation a clear "therefore."** End with a summons, not a list. "Therefore — today, this week — when your circumstances scream at you that God has forgotten you, you have a text to stand on. You have a Savior who proved God's love at the cross. You have a sovereign God who uses your suffering as an instrument of redemption. So rejoice. Not because everything is fine. But because He is."
