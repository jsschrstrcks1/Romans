# Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Sermon:** Psalm 14 (untitled)
**Evaluated:** 2026-03-22

---

## 1. Verdict Title

**Pastoral Heart, Prophetic Instinct — But Exposition Never Arrives**

---

## 2. One-Sentence Summary

A sermon with genuine pastoral fire and courageous confrontation of idolatrous priorities that never actually opens Psalm 14 — the text is announced, the prayer is prayed, and then the preacher preaches from his week rather than from the Word.

---

## 3. Berean Gate

**FAIL — Scopus Test and Authority Test both fail.**

The Scopus Test: The sermon’s thesis — "the American church has its priorities wrong; we should be sharing the gospel instead of fighting for our rights" — does not arise from Psalm 14’s own argument. Psalm 14 is a judicial declaration that universal human depravity makes the righteous vulnerable and that God alone will deliver. That argument never becomes the sermon’s argument.

The Authority Test: The preacher does not stand under Psalm 14 as a herald. He stands over it, using the opening verse (nábal) as a springboard for an extended rebuke of political idolatry — which is itself a legitimate homiletical concern, but it is not what Psalm 14 is saying. Verses 3–7 receive no treatment whatsoever. The text’s own climax ("Oh that salvation for Israel would come out of Zion!") is not touched.

**This sermon is not text-driven. The main point of the sermon does not arise from the main point of the passage. No score is assigned to exposition. The sermon must be rebuilt from the text, not decorated with it.**

---

## 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

Score is **capped at 69**.

- **Flag 2: Main point does not equal text’s point.** The sermon uses Psalm 14:1 as a doorway and never returns. The passage’s actual argument — universal depravity, God’s search for understanding, the oppression of the righteous, and the eschatological cry for Zion’s salvation — is absent from the preaching.
- **Flag 5: No actual exhortation.** The sermon closes with "For those of you that don’t know the Lord, this all starts with a relationship with God. Gospel." — a placeholder, not an exhortation. The word "Gospel" appears as a stage direction, not a proclamation. The congregation was never summoned to respond to anything specific.
- **Flag 1: No clear gospel presentation.** The gospel is literally marked as "Gospel." in the manuscript — a note to self, not a sermon. Christ’s finished work, justification by faith, the ground of forgiveness — none of it is proclaimed. Kenny Duffield’s story is evangelistically touching but is not a gospel presentation.

## 5. Score Breakdown

### 1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 7/25

- **Textual Fidelity: 2/10.** Psalm 14:1 receives genuine lexical work (nábal from BDB). Verses 2–6 receive a summary gloss. Verse 7 is untouched. The sermon departs the text after the opening paragraph and does not return. The passage’s argument — divine search, universal indictment, oppression of the righteous, eschatological longing — is nowhere preached.
- **Contextual Awareness: 2/5.** No canonical context. No awareness that Psalm 14 = Psalm 53. No awareness of the psalm’s placement or original use. No redemptive-historical horizon established.
- **Exegetical Clarity: 2/5.** The nábal work is the sermon’s strongest exegetical moment — the BDB citation is responsible. But it is immediately bent into a contemporary political application rather than into the text’s own uses.
- **Logical Faithfulness: 1/5.** The leap from "nábal means morally bankrupt, not just atheist" to "therefore we shouldn’t fight for our rights" is not a textual argument — it is an application imported from elsewhere. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is partially named (sin twists our desires to lesser things) but is not grounded in the text’s own diagnosis.

### 2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 11/18

- **Christ-Centeredness: 3/7.** Christ appears — the cross is mentioned in the Paul/Nero section, Jesus’ submission to Pilate is referenced — but the Christological connections are illustrative, not structural. Christ is not shown to be the answer to Psalm 14’s problem (universal depravity, the absence of the one who does good). The connection is real but not developed.
- **Redemptive-Historical Awareness: 1/3.** None. The psalm’s own cry for Zion’s salvation (v.7) — a direct messianic horizon — goes unpreached. This is a significant miss.
- **Doctrinal Precision: 4/4.** This is the sermon’s strongest theological section. Total depravity is handled with care: Ephesians 2, Isaiah, Calvin’s "factory of idols," the Damascus Road, Paul’s sovereign regeneration. The monergism here is clear, confessionally sound, and well-illustrated (Kenny Duffield). The free will treatment is precise.
- **Confessional Alignment: 3/4.** The doctrines of grace are well-represented. No confessional contradictions. The 1689 Pulse is satisfied in the depravity section. The absence of a covenant-of-grace framing for the psalm’s eschatological cry is a missed opportunity, not a contradiction.

### 3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 5/15

- **Clarity of the Gospel: 1/5.** The gospel is a placeholder at the end ("Gospel."). There is no proclamation of justification by faith, no explanation of the cross, no call to believe. Kenny Duffield’s conversion is told — but that is testimony, not gospel declaration.
- **Necessity of Christ: 2/5.** Christ appears in the sermon — his submission to Pilate, his meekness, his execution. But he is used as a model of rights-renunciation, not as the only ground of salvation. A devout non-Christian could absorb this section without being scandalized.
- **Cross-Centered Gravity: 2/5.** The sermon references the cross but does not preach it. The cross appears as a historical example, not as the declaration of God’s righteousness in the forgiveness of the ungodly. The Synagogue Test is partially triggered — this sermon could survive in a setting that honors Jesus as a model of humble submission.

### 4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9/15

- **Heart Penetration: 4/5.** The sermon genuinely searches the conscience on priority idolatry. The "raise your hand" diagnostics are effective — "who of you has shared the gospel vs. had a political argument this week?" is a sharp Puritan-style diagnostic that corners the congregation. This is the sermon’s best pastoral moment.
- **Specificity: 3/5.** The masks/politics application is very specific to the moment (summer 2020, COVID). It will land powerfully on its original congregation. But there is no application to other hearers — grief, backsliding, doubt, unbelief. The specificity is narrow.
- **Spiritual Differentiation: 2/5.** The sermon addresses primarily the politically agitated Christian. The unbeliever is gestured to at the end ("For those of you that don’t know the Lord...") but is not actually spoken to. The humbled, the backslider, the doubting, the knowledgeable-but-unmoved — none are distinctly addressed. Perkins’ taxonomy is largely collapsed into one hearer type.

### 5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8

- **Call to Response: 1/4.** "Gospel." is not a call to response. The diagnostics (raise your hand) create conscience pressure, but the sermon does not cross the line from conviction to specific summons. What must I now do? Share the gospel — but with whom, how, starting where? The imperative is named in the abstract but never delivered as a concrete command.
- **Equipping for Holiness & Witness: 2/4.** The Great Commission is cited as God’s priority. But the congregation receives no equipping for gospel witness — no guidance on how to share the gospel, no example of what to say, no encouragement for specific situations. The call to pray for the Tamil is given; the call to witness locally is gestured at but not scaffolded.

### 6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9

- **Clarity of Outline: 2/4.** No discernible outline. The sermon moves: personal announcement → missions moment → lexical observation → political rebuke (COVID masks) → Paul/Nero → rights → hero video → depravity → free will → Kenny Duffield → priorities → placeholder gospel. The movement has pastoral logic but no textual spine.
- **Progression: 2/3.** The sermon builds emotionally — from illustration to rebuke to diagnostic question to testimony to close. But it never builds *textually*. Each section advances the preacher’s concern, not the passage’s argument.
- **Unity: 1/2.** The dominant idea is "get your priorities right — the gospel matters more than politics." This is coherent. But it is not one idea from one text. It is one idea from the preacher’s week.

### 7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5

- **Relevance: 2/2.** The fake watch, the pyrite, the boy on the train tracks, Kenny Duffield’s conversion — all are drawn from the congregation’s relational world. These are not generic illustrations.
- **Clarity: 1/2.** The watch/pyrite illustration is vivid but overlong — and then the pyrite research tangent undermines the illustration’s force. The hero video is the most effective: "would you jump in front of the train to save your child — would you share the gospel?" is the strongest moment of the sermon.
- **Memorability: 1/1.** Kenny Duffield will be remembered. The train track diagnostic will be remembered.

### 8. Sermonic Force — 4/5

This is oral, personal, and direct. The preacher addresses the congregation by name, refers to shared history, and asks direct questions. The manuscript reads like it was spoken, not written. The "Show me!" challenge regarding Paul and civil disobedience has genuine rhetorical heat. The introduction (the niece’s birthday trip) is warm and pastoral even if it does not serve the text.

## 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Doctrinal Precision on Total Depravity.** The section on nábal, Ephesians 2, Calvin’s factory of idols, and the Damascus Road (Acts 9) is the most textually grounded and theologically careful portion of the sermon. The preacher knows his Reformed anthropology and delivers it accessibly.

2. **The Train Track Diagnostic.** "Show of hands — would you jump in front of the train to save your child? Now — who of you shared the gospel this week?" This is a genuine Puritan-style use — it corners the conscience without warning, distinguishes between what we say we value and what we actually do, and does not offer an easy escape. This moment is worth preserving and expanding.

3. **Courage on Political Idolatry.** Preaching this to a congregation during a COVID summer with masks and elections imminent required pastoral nerve. The Paul/Nero section — "Show me one place where Paul recommends we stand up for our God given rights" — is direct, honest, and grounded in real biblical pattern even if it is not the argument of Psalm 14.

## 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **Psalm 14 is never preached.** After the nábal note, the text disappears. Verses 2–6 are summarized in one paragraph. Verse 7 — the psalm’s climax ("Oh that salvation for Israel would come out of Zion!") — receives zero treatment. This is not a sermon on Psalm 14. It is a sermon on political idolatry that opens in Psalm 14.

2. **The Gospel Placeholder.** The sermon ends with: "For those of you that don’t know the Lord, this all starts with a relationship with God. Gospel." This is a stage direction. A word — "Gospel" — is not a gospel proclamation. The very thing the sermon argues the church is neglecting is the thing the sermon itself neglects. This is the sermon’s most serious failure.

3. **No Exhortation.** The sermon diagnoses the problem (wrong priorities) and gestures at the solution (share the gospel) but never crosses from analysis to command. "Share the gospel more than you argue about politics" is implied, never commanded. There is no "therefore" moment — no specific, concrete summons that constitutes a genuine call to response.

## 11. Blind Spots

The preacher does not appear to see that the sermon’s own urgency about gospel proclamation is undercut by the sermon’s own failure to proclaim the gospel. He tells the congregation that the church is neglecting the gospel — and then models that neglect at the close. The irony is not a gotcha; it is a diagnostic. The preacher is convicted by the truth he is preaching, but conviction in the preacher is not the same as proclamation for the congregation.

The preacher also does not see that the pastoral material (the niece, the masks, the missions moment) has displaced the text. The text is present as an occasion for the sermon — it is not the source of the sermon. This is a structural habit that will produce weak exposition over time regardless of the pastoral strength of the applications.

---

## 12. Illusion of Strength

The doctrinal section on total depravity *felt* like exposition — it used Scripture (Ephesians 2, Isaiah, Genesis), it cited Calvin, it was exegetically engaged. But it was not exposition of Psalm 14. It was doctrinal instruction imported from elsewhere to support a point the preacher already wanted to make. The lexical work on nábal felt like rigorous hermeneutics — and in isolation, it was responsible. But it became a springboard rather than a structural root. The sermon *felt* text-driven because it began in the text. It was not.

---

## 13. Missed Opportunity

Psalm 14:7 is the sermon’s unpreached climax: "Oh that salvation for Israel would come out of Zion! When the LORD restores the fortunes of his people, let Jacob rejoice, let Israel be glad." This is the eschatological cry that gives the entire psalm its gospel shape. The psalm diagnoses universal depravity not as an anthropological observation but as the backdrop for the necessity of divine rescue. That rescue — Zion’s salvation, God restoring the fortunes of his people — is exactly what Christ accomplished. The preacher had a legitimate, organic, non-artificial path to Christ built into the passage itself. He walked past it.

The political application was topical and courageous. But the text itself was offering something more durable: the answer to "if everyone is morally bankrupt, what hope remains?" The answer is in verse 7, and it is Christ.

---

## 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild the sermon around Psalm 14’s own argument.** The structure is there in the text: (a) universal indictment — v.1–3; (b) divine search — v.2; (c) oppression of the righteous — v.4–6; (d) eschatological hope — v.7. The political application can remain, but it must emerge from the text’s own categories, not replace them.

2. **Preach Verse 7.** The sermon’s most powerful move is available in the text and costs nothing: "The whole psalm presses us toward one question: if there is none who does good, not even one — where does salvation come from? The psalm answers its own question. *Oh, that salvation would come out of Zion.* It did. It has. His name is Jesus Christ." This is not a Jesus jump — it is the psalm’s own eschatological argument.

3. **Replace "Gospel." with the gospel.** Write out justification by faith in two to three sentences. Who Christ is, what he did, what is required. The missions moment for the Tamil — "to follow Jesus is to risk your life" — creates the perfect transition: "That is what the gospel costs in Sri Lanka. What does it cost you? Let me tell you what it gives you." Then proclaim it.

4. **Issue a clear exhortation.** End the sermon with a specific command: "This week — not someday, this week — share the gospel with one person. Not an argument about masks. The gospel. If you don’t know how, stay after and I will show you. If you are afraid, the cross is the cure for your fear." That is a "therefore" moment.

5. **Cut the pyrite YouTube tangent.** "I spent a good hour researching whether pyrite could be used in an alloy" — this is charming but it bleeds time and credibility from the illustration. The fake watch story lands. The pyrite tangent lands on the preacher’s YouTube history. Cut it.

## 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon is not bloodless — it has pastoral heat and genuine courage. The failure here is not dead orthodoxy but textual neglect: a warm, courageous sermon that is not actually a sermon on this text. The fire is present. The textual faithfulness is not.
