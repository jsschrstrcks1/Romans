## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Warm Pastoral Appeal That Mistakes the Map for the Treasure**

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A genuine and personally engaging call to Bible reading from Psalm 1 that captures the pastoral heart of its preacher, but reduces a text about the blessedness of the righteous man and the ruin of the wicked into a motivational talk about personal devotional habits, leaving the gospel unannounced and the text’s own weight largely unexplored.

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: FAIL (borderline pass on proximity, fail on precision)**

The sermon’s thesis is: "Read your Bible more, because neglecting it leads to ruin." Psalm 1’s thesis is closer to: "The man who meditates on God’s law is blessed and will stand in judgment; the wicked are chaff who will perish." These overlap, but are not the same claim. Psalm 1 is not primarily a motivational text about devotional habits — it is a covenant declaration about two ways, two destinies, and the Lord’s knowing of the righteous. The sermon misidentifies the primary genre (wisdom/covenant declaration) as a self-improvement text and thereby misses the eschatological and Christological depth that is the psalm’s actual burden.

The sermon does remain loosely in contact with the text throughout, so the gate is not slammed shut. But the main point of the sermon — "you should read your Bible" — is not the main point of the psalm, which is "the Lord knows the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked will perish." The sermon passes the gate on a generous reading but with a significant reductionism flag.

**Authority Test: Partial Pass**

The preacher does not stand above the text as an editor who softens it — he is not heterodox. But he does effectively reframe the text’s covenantal and eschatological declarations into pragmatic personal advice, which is a subtle form of editorial reduction.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 2 triggered (partially):** The main point of the sermon ("read your Bible more") does not fully equal the text’s main point ("two ways, two destinies, the Lord knows the righteous").

**Flag 1 triggered:** The gospel — the finished work of Christ — is absent. There is a mention of Christ dying for us (line 80: "God loved us enough to send his son to die for us"), but it appears as a subordinate clause in passing, not as a proclamation. There is no announcement of justification, no presentation of Christ as the fulfillment of what Psalm 1 pictures, no call to faith. The sermon ends with a commitment card, not with the cross. The gospel is not proclaimed; it is assumed.

**Flag 4 triggered (partially):** Application is partially specific (the commitment card, the scripture memorization program) but does not differentiate hearers. The backslider, the unbeliever, the self-righteous, and the doubter are all addressed identically: "read your Bible."

**Flag 5 triggered (partially):** The sermon does exhort — the commitment card is a call to response. But the response it calls for is a behavioral commitment to devotional discipline rather than repentance toward God and faith in Christ.

*Score is capped at 69 due to fatal flaw flags (gospel absence is the clearest trigger).*

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 12/25**
- *Textual Fidelity (5/10):* The sermon touches each verse at some level, but systematically reduces covenantal declarations to motivational bullet points. The "12 warnings" reframe is creative but hermeneutically misleading — Psalm 1 is not primarily a warning list, but a declaration of two ways. Verse 6 ("the Lord knows the way of the righteous") — the psalm’s theological climax — receives no treatment. The eschatological dimension ("will not stand in the judgment," "the way of the wicked will perish") is mentioned but not developed.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5):* No awareness of Psalm 1 as the gateway psalm, the Torah-meditation psalm, its role as the introduction to the entire Psalter, or its connection to the Deuteronomic blessing/curse framework. No redemptive-historical placement.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5):* The preacher is clear and accessible but offers no exegetical transparency — no word study, no structural observation, no awareness of the psalm’s Hebrew poetry or parallel structure.
- *Logical Faithfulness (2/5):* The Absalom illustration is applied to "walking in the counsel of the wicked" but Absalom’s story is about military strategy, not covenant faithfulness — the connection is strained. The eisegesis creeps in when the sermon maps the psalm’s "counsel of the wicked" almost entirely onto personal lifestyle choices rather than the covenantal framework.
- *Fallen Condition Focus:* Partially identified (human tendency to neglect God’s word) but articulated as a motivational problem rather than a sin of covenant unfaithfulness.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 10/18**
- *Christ-Centeredness (3/7):* Christ appears briefly as the motivation for reading the Bible ("he loved us enough to send his son"), but this is not organic Christology arising from the text. The messianic/typological connection of Psalm 1 to Christ — the one man who perfectly delighted in the law, who stood in the judgment and was declared righteous, who is the fulfillment of the "blessed man" — is entirely absent. This is the organic connection the text actually invites.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (1/3):* None. The psalm is read as a timeless self-help principle with no placement in the story of redemption.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4):* No outright error, but the loose use of "the word of God" (conflating "Bible reading" with the relational/covenantal concept of Torah meditation) and the absence of sin/grace categories in the application are concerning.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4):* No contradictions of confessional theology. But the soteriological silence is a gap — the sermon’s response mechanism is behavioral commitment, not faith.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 4/15**
- *Clarity of the Gospel (1/5):* The gospel is not clearly articulated. There is no presentation of sin, wrath, the substitutionary work of Christ, or justification by faith.
- *Necessity of Christ (1/5):* Christ is mentioned in passing as motivation, not as the essential ground of anything the sermon is arguing. The sermon could survive almost intact with Christ removed.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5):* The sermon moves toward behavioral commitment, not toward the cross. The Bob Clark story is the most gospel-proximate moment, but even there the emphasis is on transformation and enthusiastic preaching, not on Christ’s finished work.
- *Synagogue Test:* A devout Jew who loves the Torah could sit through 95% of this sermon and not be offended by the necessity of Christ’s blood. The sermon fails the synagogue test.
- *Law/Gospel Dialectic:* The law hammers inconsistently (the "12 warnings" section) but the gospel never arrives as the balm. The sermon produces conviction about Bible neglect but offers behavioral remedy rather than grace.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 9/15**
- *Heart Penetration (3/5):* The personal illustrations (the friend who fell away, the commitment card, the Bob Clark story) create genuine pastoral warmth and do press on the conscience at some level. The cruise ship illustration is vivid. "Are you drinking deeply from the well of living water?" is a genuine probe.
- *Specificity (4/5):* This is a relative strength. The sermon is specific to this congregation — the Bible collection under the altar, the memorization program, the commitment card. These are concrete, local, pastoral touches.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon addresses one category of hearer: the Christian who is not reading their Bible. The unbeliever is never addressed. The grief-stricken, the doubter, the self-righteous, the backslider — all are collapsed into one address. Perkins’ taxonomy is almost entirely ignored.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5/8**
- *Call to Response (3/4):* The commitment card is a genuine call to response. The direct challenge ("tell God why his word isn’t important enough") is sharp and pastoral. This is one of the sermon’s genuine strengths.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The memorization program equips for personal holiness. There is a passing mention of the Khedi Buzurg and the need to pray for the unreached, which is the only witness-oriented moment. The body is not sent out so much as challenged to practice private discipline.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 5/9**
- *Clarity of Outline (2/4):* The sermon does not have a clearly traceable outline. It moves from Stetzer → Bible ownership statistics → Heraclitus/Spurgeon → cruise ship illustration → Psalm 1 text → "12 warnings" → Absalom → the fallen friend → scoffer definition → banana tree → Bob Clark story → commitment card. The progression is pastoral and intuitive but not clearly structured. A listener could not sketch it on a napkin.
- *Progression (2/3):* There is momentum toward the commitment card, which gives the sermon a landing point. But several sections (the political commentary on pedophilia/Epstein/Clinton/Trump, the stoic philosophers, the cruise ship detail) are long digressions that slow the progression.
- *Unity (1/2):* The sermon has one general topic (read your Bible) but multiple competing sub-arguments that never fully cohere around a single sharp thesis.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Myself. The entire sermon orbits around the listener’s devotional practice. Christ appears as motivation but not as subject.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Genuine pastoral intimacy.** The Bob Clark story, the friend who fell away, the pen pal story — the preacher knows his people and draws from real pastoral experience. This is a genuine strength that should be preserved and sharpened in revision.
2. **The banana tree illustration** (lines 102–108) is vivid, locally grounded, and theologically sound in its contour (the Christian apart from God withers). It earns its place and will be remembered.
3. **The commitment card as response mechanism** (lines 114–128) is specific, actionable, and congregationally concrete. The challenge to "tell God why his word isn’t important enough" is the sharpest exhortational moment in the sermon.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is absent.** The sermon ends with a behavioral commitment to scripture memorization, not with the cross. At no point does the preacher announce who Christ is, what he accomplished, or why that is the reason any of this matters. The entire motivational apparatus — love letter, banquet table, living water — is deployed without the announcement that Jesus is the Living Word, the fulfillment of what Psalm 1 pictures, and the only ground on which any of us can stand in judgment. This is the most serious failure.

2. **The text’s own climax (v. 6) receives no treatment.** "The Lord knows the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked will perish" is the theological payload of the psalm — the covenantal declaration of divine omniscience and eschatological destiny. The sermon never explains what it means for the Lord to "know" the way of the righteous (covenantal intimacy, not mere awareness), never presses the eschatological force of "will perish," and never connects this to the gospel hope that only the man who is righteous in Christ can stand in the judgment.

3. **The political commentary section** (lines 68–73: abortion, homosexuality, pedophilia, Epstein, Clinton, Trump) is a substantial homiletical error. It is theologically disconnected from the text’s actual argument, politically inflammatory in a way that risks the congregation hearing politics rather than scripture, and it disrupts the pastoral cadence of what is otherwise a sermon with genuine warmth. Whatever the preacher intended, this section reads as a digression that will distract, offend, and divide without advancing the text’s claim.

### 11. Blind Spots

- **The preacher likely does not see that he has replaced the text’s subject (God’s covenantal blessing and judgment) with a secondary application (personal Bible reading).** The text is not primarily "about Bible reading" — it is about the two ways, the two destinies, and the Lord’s sovereign knowledge of the righteous. Bible reading/Torah meditation is the means, not the subject.
- **The psalm’s Christological depth is invisible to the sermon.** The "blessed man" of Psalm 1 is not primarily you or me — it is the one Man who perfectly delighted in the law and stood in the judgment. Jesus is the tree planted by the rivers of water. This is the organic connection that transforms Psalm 1 from a self-improvement text into gospel.
- **The preacher likely does not see how close the sermon is to moralism.** "Read your Bible and you will flourish; neglect it and you will wither" — without the gospel ground — is functionally a works principle. It is not wrong, but it is incomplete in a way that misleads.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal illustration density creates the impression of a deeply pastoral and applied sermon — and the preacher’s pastoral gifts are real. But the cumulative effect of the illustrations is to keep the sermon at the level of motivation and experience rather than lifting the congregation to encounter the God of the text. The warmth is genuine; the theological weight it should be carrying is not there. The sermon feels applied because it is full of stories about real people. But application without the indicative of the gospel is exhortation without ground.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**Psalm 1 as the gateway to the Psalter — and to Christ.** The psalm functions as the introduction to the entire book of Psalms, and many Psalms scholars (including Calvin, Mays, and Kidner) note that the "blessed man" of Psalm 1 finds its ultimate referent in the coming King — read alongside Psalm 2 ("his anointed") the two psalms together paint a portrait that the New Testament applies to Christ. The preacher had a remarkable opportunity to show how Jesus is the only man who perfectly walked in the way of the righteous, the only one whose leaf never withered, the only one who stood in the judgment — and then died for those of us who are by nature among the chaff. This is not eisegesis; it is the organic redemptive-historical movement the psalm itself invites. It was entirely missed.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild the central thesis from v. 6, not from "read your Bible."** Restate the main point as: "God knows the way of the righteous — and in Christ, that way is open to you." Everything else (the Bible reading, the meditation, the bearing of fruit) flows from that ground.

2. **Add a Christological movement after working through the two ways.** Something like: "Who is this blessed man who perfectly delights in the law and never withers? It is not me. It is not you. It is the one man who walked perfectly in the way of the righteous — Jesus Christ. And here is the gospel: the chaff that we are by nature, God received as righteous in him. Because he stood in our judgment, we can stand in his blessing."

3. **Remove the political commentary section entirely** (lines 68–73). Replace with a single clear statement about what happens when a society collectively turns from the counsel of God — and return immediately to the psalm.

4. **Address the unbeliever and the doubter directly.** After the gospel movement, turn to the person who does not yet know Christ: "If you are here today and you do not yet know this man — you are like the chaff. Not as a moral judgment, but as a covenantal reality. The only way to be planted by the rivers of water is to be united to the one who is the water of life."

5. **End with Christ, not with a commitment card.** The commitment card is a fine pastoral tool — but it should come *after* the gospel, not *instead of* it. Let the response be: "Because Christ has done this for you, now commit to feasting on the word that tells you of him." The indicative must precede the imperative.

6. **Treat v. 6 as the sermon’s climax.** Structure the sermon so that everything moves toward: "The Lord *knows* the way of the righteous." Unpack the covenantal intimacy of that word "knows" (yada — relational, not merely cognitive), and press its weight: in the judgment, the Lord will say "I know you" to those in Christ, and "I never knew you" to those who are not.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 1 is a wisdom psalm with strong covenantal and eschatological dimensions, and its Torah-meditation theme does stress the applicatory categories toward devotional life — which is appropriate. However, the rubric’s insistence on gospel centrality and Christological grounding is *not* in tension with this psalm; it is required by it, since the "blessed man" of Psalm 1 is fulfilled Christologically in the New Testament’s own reading (cf. Matthew 5:3ff, the Beatitudes as the Great Sermon’s answer to the Psalm 1 portrait). The rubric fits this text well and does not need to bend here.
