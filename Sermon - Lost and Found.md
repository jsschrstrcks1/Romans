## The Lost Sheep (Luke 15:1–7)

A shepherd in this period was personally responsible for every animal in his care. If one turned up missing — taken by a predator, fallen into a crevice, wandered off — the shepherd was expected to account for it. A sheep was worth roughly a day's wages. Not a catastrophic loss, but a loss.

Having kept sheep myself, I can tell you they are not cute and cuddly. They are, however, distinct.

We had two sheep named Razzle and Frazzle.

If you get near Razzle, his entire aim in life is skull-bash. He runs straight at you and hits whatever he can reach — your knees, your hips, your backside, as my uncle can attest. He doesn't give a gentle tap. He puts his whole body into it the way a yearling ram would with another ram. He's dangerous. We learned to keep glasses of cold water or a running hose nearby so we could douse him before he hurt someone.

Frazzle trusts us, sort of, sometimes — mostly when we have food and don't try to touch her. This became a problem one afternoon when she escaped and my dad had a cast on his arm. We spent a good while chasing her through the garden. Eventually we grabbed a rope — when she'd try to run we'd lift it and catch her across the chest, then cross our ends to hold her in place. We got her halter back on. She immediately wiggled out of it and went tearing across the tomato garden, wise to the rope trick by now.

My point is this: the shepherd was not merely trying to recover a financial loss. He was trying to restore a relationship with an animal he had known its entire life. Something with a distinct personality. Something with a face he recognized. The closest modern analog is losing a dog or a cat — an animal you've cared for for years.

So it is with Christ. When one of his sheep is lost, he has come — it's Luke 19:10 — to seek and to save the lost. When we finally caught Frazzle for the third and final time, we rejoiced. Not for the same reasons Christ rejoices over a recovered sheep. But the rejoicing was real — out of breath, soaked with sweat, and rejoicing.

### The Greek Word

Before we go further, I want you to look at a specific word in this text. Some of your translations are going to say the ninety-nine were left in the *wilderness.* Can someone read verse 4 for me?

When I hear wilderness, I think of the unmanaged parts of Starkey Park. The Amazon rainforest. The jungles of Vietnam. Impassable places, loaded with things that want to eat you. My mind always went to the ninety-nine being in more danger than the one that wandered off.

But Jesus chose his words carefully. If we had been listening in Greek, we would have understood immediately that this was not that kind of wilderness.

The word is *ἔρημος* — eremos. It means to be in a state of isolation or desolation. Unfrequented. Empty. Quiet. The ESV actually renders it "open country" — which is closer to what Jesus meant. Sheep are frequently left in *ἔρημος* throughout the New Testament, and based on how it's used, this was a place suitable for sustaining them. Not jungle. Open field.

If my sheep are any indicator, the ninety-nine were standing in a quiet, empty pasture heavily laden with grass, munching away to their hearts' content.

This calls to mind the opening of Psalm 23:

> The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want. He makes me lie down in green pastures. He leads me beside still waters. He restores my soul. He leads me in paths of righteousness for his name's sake.
> — Psalm 23:1–3 (ESV)

There's something worth noticing in that passage. *He makes me lie down.* The shepherds made the sheep stop eating — because sheep aren't smart enough to stop on their own. In a river delta or any patch of especially lush grass, the shepherd had to intervene. A sheep that overeats fresh grass can develop founder or bloat. Both were once fatal. The good shepherd protects his sheep from their own stupidity.

Many of you have been that sheep. God has sought you and protected you when you were doing things you now look back on as foolish. We've heard each other's testimonies in this room. Most or all of us have been there — drinking, or drugs, or living for anything and everything but the Lord. He sought us. He brought us to himself.

### Jesus Seeks the Lost

This first parable represents the salvation of someone who was lost. And here's the point: it is Jesus who takes the initiative. He is the one doing the seeking.

Calvinism gets a bad reputation from people who argue that because Jesus seeks the lost, they don't have to. I attended a church like that for years. We all know someone who is an overbalanced Calvinist. And yet — on this specific point — Calvinism is right: Jesus seeks the lost. He came to earth born into humble circumstances, laid in a feed trough surrounded by barn animals. He lived by meager means. Never owned a home. Never had a retirement account or health insurance. He died a death he did not deserve to save a people who could not save themselves.

Further — apart from God moving in our hearts, we don't even have an interest in the things of the Lord. Paul writes it plainly:

> The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned.
> — 1 Corinthians 2:14 (ESV)

The natural person — the one for whom the Holy Spirit is not illuminating the things of God — finds the gospel foolish. This is not new doctrine. It's in our Baptist Faith and Message. And it stands in direct contrast to the Pharisees who criticized Jesus in this very chapter. They generally believed they shouldn't proselytize outsiders to their sects. Jesus walked straight past that — ate with sinners, welcomed them, told stories about a God who goes out searching for what's lost.

The high point of this first parable is twofold: the return of the sheep, and the rejoicing that follows. Jesus is saying — in parable and by direct statement — that it pleases God to seek and receive sinners. Which was exactly what the Pharisees had been criticizing him for at the beginning of the chapter.

## The Lost Coin (Luke 15:8–10)

When a woman was married in the time of Christ, she was given ten silver coins — drachmas — which she wore as a headdress. The custom of giving jewelry as a dowry or bride gift is still common in eastern cultures today. For a woman from a good family, the amount given was enough that she could start her life over if her husband divorced her — a form of prenuptial provision.

I want to tell you about a woman I met when I was in the UK.

Her name was Nadja. The Slavic spelling — drops the vowels, replaces them with a J. In her culture, when someone converts to Christianity it's customary to take a new name, and all she needed to do was adopt the Slavic spelling. Nadja means hope.

She was Bengali by birth, Pakistani by nationality. I met her and her husband at a dinner party. The host brought up arranged marriages, and called on our new friends to explain the custom. She told us that her parents and her husband's hadn't known each other well, but both families had good businesses in related fields. Through marriage the families would draw together, creating greater benefit for everyone. She met her husband three times before the wedding. Each time she was given the chance to break the engagement. She believed in her father's judgment, and thought the man was good-looking. She married him.

The bride price was 100,000 British pounds. Close to 200,000 American dollars at the time. One condition: she had to wear every piece of jewelry to the wedding. She obeyed. Every earring, broach, necklace, tennis bracelet, ring. Jeweled hairpieces. Buttons made of rubies. A necklace nearly a half-inch in diameter of solid gold. She said she felt like a jewel walking down the aisle.

The higher the bride price, the more valued the woman feels she is. 100,000 pounds is quite high — especially when you consider that in some parts of Africa, the going rate is a bag of feed or a goat. Poor women from modest households attract men of modest means. The woman in Jesus' story was also of modest means.

The closest modern analog to losing one of those ten coins is losing your wedding ring.

I was working with my brother-in-law for a property management company. At the end of a stagnant, smelly canal there was a busted dock and what seemed to be sandbags underwater. They needed to come out. He and I took turns with the unpleasant jobs, and this one fell to him. He tried to grip the bag from above the water. Couldn't get it. So he went under — into the foul, muck-black water — and grabbed it. Even then, neither of us could haul it out. We improvised with a four-by-four and the truck. Left it on the ground to dry. Even dried out, it was too heavy to load. I ended up driving to some family land, tying the bag to a tree, and driving out from under it.

The following morning I got a frantic call. *Did you find my wedding ring anywhere?* He hadn't noticed it was gone until he was home. We drove back to the work site and searched everything. No luck. Which meant one of us was going back in the canal.

He climbed back in. Gloves, mask, snorkel. He put his gloves on — and the ring was inside the glove. Had been the whole time.

A single drachma was worth about a day's wages. The headdress signified she was married and would offer some buffer if disaster came. She'd lost part of something that signified her womanhood. Part of what safeguarded her future. A gift from someone she loved.

She was motivated.

The coin represents us. We are something of value — the church is, as Paul writes, betrothed to Christ. Selected by God the Father, pursued and purchased by God the Son, indwelt by God the Holy Spirit. The Father's bride price was the vanquishing of sin and death. Our bride price was salvation. Freedom from sin. Eternity in heaven. The indwelling of the Holy Spirit.

We lowly sinners are a messy lot. Of poor stock from poor families. We deserved hell and we were given heaven. We deserved death and we were given life. Praise God we didn't get what we deserved. Instead of a bag of feed — eternal life. Instead of a groom of humble means — the church is betrothed to the King of Kings and Lord of Lords. Instead of eternity in hell — eternal life.

### The Coin That Fell Through No Fault of Its Own

One more thing — and it matters.

The coin was not lost through its own doing.

In the first story, the sheep wandered off. That's on the sheep. But the coin didn't go anywhere by choice. Something happened to it. It fell. Maybe the string broke. Maybe the clasp gave way. The coin had nothing to do with it.

This brings to mind a friend of mine whose first experience in church was a public shaming. Over wearing a hat. If wearing a hat brings one more soul into heaven, then let us all wear hats with abandon.

Another friend of mine struggled with a medical condition that made her hands ache. She was young — maybe twenty-one or twenty-two. One Sunday a storm was bearing down, and the pressure had her hands especially painful. Her younger sister happened to be visiting that day, which was unusual — she lived several hours away. During the service, the pain was bringing tears to my friend's eyes. Her sister, moved with compassion, began quietly rubbing her sister's hands — low in the pew, discreetly, so as not to distract anyone. At the end of the service, an older woman walked up to the two of them and announced that God had told her to tell these girls they were a horrible distraction, and that shame on them for behaving so ungodly in church.

That was the last time either of them attended that church.

Where in Scripture does it say a non-believer can't wear a hat? Where does it say a pastor must wear a suit and tie to the pulpit? It doesn't. Those are constructs of culture, not of Christ. I'm not saying come to church in torn pajamas or dressed immodestly. But I am saying that people should be able to come as they are and expect to find no judgment for having done so.

When I was at Jasmine, I felt like we were good about that. We had a man show up once — drunk, sick. We didn't say a word. Just loved him. As I understand it, he eventually sobered up. I don't know his eternal state. But as evangelistic as we were in that church, it wouldn't surprise me to learn he came to faith through the experience.

I met a woman not long ago who had attended a church where the pastor preached in blue jeans. She was appalled — felt it was cavalier, disrespectful to God. A dear friend and former pastor was dining with us, and he asked her: *What did Jesus wear to preach?* She had no answer.

Jesus preached in clothes that were culturally relevant to his day. May have even been behind the fashion curve.

I preach in blue jeans nearly every time I preach. Not out of disrespect for God. Out of concern for the lost. If blue jeans make someone off the street feel comfortable enough to stay through a sermon and hear the gospel — I'll wear blue jeans every Sunday for the rest of my life. Likewise, if a tie is required for the gospel in your context, wear a tie. It's not what a man wears that defiles him. It's what comes from his heart.

Seeking to honor God is not sinful. It becomes sinful when we project our own hang-ups onto other people. You want to wear a tie to honor God? Good. You do well. But let your brother wear his blue jeans, and let him honor God in his own way. Don't be the woman who drove those two girls out of church. Don't be the one who causes the coin to fall from the headdress.

God receives sinners — whatever their state, their dress, their past. Far better to be the one on your knees searching for what's lost.

## The Prodigal Son (Luke 15:11–32)

### Lost by Choice

The son was lost by his own willfulness.

The sheep didn't know better — as our pastor says, sheep are dumb, and the sheep was lost because of its own lack of understanding. The coin was lost because of outside forces acting on it. The son was lost because he chose it.

It's interesting that the father isn't described as going out to search for him. Sometimes, when our children are willful, we have to let them experience the logical outcome of their choices. I believe the father was doing exactly that. Allowing his son's willfulness to run its course. Knowing what would happen. Waiting.

The son's willfulness is quite apparent. Some scholars say that in this culture, asking for your inheritance early was tantamount to telling your father he was worth more to you dead than alive. That you valued his money over your relationship with him.

By Hebrew law, the older brother received two-thirds of the estate. The younger brother's share was therefore one-third — not half, as we often hear preached. Even so: who among you today could write a check for one-third of your home, your retirement, your car, your investments, without feeling it? I couldn't. Even the wealthy would have felt that kind of liquidation for a long time.

The son, meanwhile, was having the time of his life. The ESV says he "squandered" his inheritance in "reckless living." Here's something from the Greek text worth noticing: the concept of waste is actually stated twice in the same verse — two different Greek words, one right after the other. In the same way that a kid who had a really really really bad day is trying to add force with each repetition, the text is pressing a point: this wasn't simple negligence. It was willful, wanton, deliberate waste.

Then a famine struck.

This collapsed him from living like a prince in a foreign land to scrambling for any job he could find. I think of the days leading up to Hitler taking power in Germany — it's said that a woman would fill a wheelbarrow full of cash and take it to the bakery to buy a single loaf of bread. She'd park it outside alongside other wheelbarrows full of cash, go in, select her bread, come back out — and find the money dumped on the ground and the wheelbarrow stolen. The inflation was so severe that the wheelbarrow was worth more than the currency it carried.

We don't know the exact circumstances of the famine or the young man's fall. We know he fell hard. He ended up feeding pigs carob pods. In that culture, the carob pod was known roughly as a *pod of life*. That resonance is not accidental — this formerly wealthy young man was sitting with the pigs, facing death, watching animals eat the pod of life. Pods he was not permitted to touch.

Finally he came to his senses and headed home.

---

### The Father Was Watching

The trip home was not short. Scripture says the son had gone to a far-off place. And in the Greek of Luke 15:20, two words are used together to describe his distance at the moment of return — the English phrase "still a long way off" collapses both of them, but the original presses the point: he was far. Further than a glance out a kitchen window would find him.

In Sunday school I always had this image of the father looking out across the field and catching a glimpse. But the language seems to say more than that.

How, then, did the father see him coming from so far away?

It's reasonable — and many commentators suggest this — that the father had men out looking. That he had never fully stopped watching. That when his son came over the horizon, someone came running with word. This is how the father could see him when he was still a long way off.

This is like our heavenly Father. While we are running away, he is still watching. Still prepared to receive us the moment we turn back.

### The Older Brother

The older brother has received a good deal of attention, and rightly so.

He was angry. And he may have been justified in his anger — but as Scripture says, we are to forgive a repentant man as many times as he comes to us having repented. I heard a sermon not long ago on this subject. I didn't agree with all of it — or perhaps much of it. But a couple of points stuck with me.

The preacher argued that forgiveness is twofold. First, there's the external proclamation — releasing the person from the debt of what they've done. Second, there's the internal — letting go of the anger in your own heart, whether that anger was justified or not. He made a case from Scripture — Luke 17, if you want to look it up later — that the first part isn't always required. But the second part is.

I know this body has been through some deep trauma. I know there are those here who still feel it when it comes up. I want to be gentle, brothers and sisters. I love you. I don't want to compound pain with guilt.

But at some point, the pain has to be given to the Lord.

That doesn't mean the next time it surfaces you won't feel the wound — you may. God doesn't always take pain away suddenly. But each time the memory comes back, ask yourself: Am I focusing on the sinful I — on how I was hurt, how I was wronged, how I was affected? Or am I focusing on God in the midst of it?

Being wrapped in how you were hurt, how you were wronged, how you were affected — that's sinful. That's what it is. John Piper writes that God is most glorified in us when we are most satisfied in him. *[See end notes on this quotation.]* When those feelings resurface — seek God. Ask him the hard questions. Ask him *why.* Read his word. He may show you the answers. And if he doesn't, the struggle to find them will draw you closer to him and loosen the grip of the pain.

God will defend his church. God judges those who teach his word more harshly than he judges others. Those who hurt his children will be dealt with — in the appropriate manner, in God's timing. We can rest in that.

We don't want to be like the older brother.

Because it wasn't only selfish pride that he was guilty of. He was also guilty of severing fellowship — with his brother and with his father. Look at verse 28.

*[Have someone read verses 28 and 31–32.]*

He sat outside and sulked like a child. This past week I was walking through my parents' house and found my nephew in the bathroom standing at the toilet opening and closing the lid. Up, down. Up, down. Then flushing. Up, down. Up, down. Flush. I asked him what he was doing, and why he was playing with the toilet instead of getting his bathing suit on.

He didn't understand I wasn't scolding him. He got defensive. *I'm not playing with the toilet.* I asked what he would call it. He said with considerably more attitude: *I AM NOT PLAYING WITH IT.* He started to stomp off. I called him back. *What were you trying to do?* Still convinced I wasn't listening, he declared again he wasn't playing. I rephrased it. *Why were you doing what you were doing with the potty instead of getting your bathing suit on?* He marched into the living room, climbed into a box, and pouted.

I gave him a few minutes. Called him over. Then it came out: he'd used the bathroom and it hadn't flushed right. It was stinky. So he'd been checking and closing, checking and closing, hoping it would clear. He didn't have a full picture of the situation — like any five-year-old.

Like the older brother.

The father's reply is exactly right. Someone read verses 31 and 32 for me.

Such is the case with anyone who returns to Christ. Apart from him, we were dead. But when we are found — the heavens rejoice. Notice: at the end of each of the three parables, there is rejoicing. Every single time. That is what happens in heaven when someone is saved.

## Three Ways to Read It

Before we close, I want to share something that came across my desk this week.

Focus groups in various parts of the world were asked to read the parable of the Prodigal Son and then interpret it. The results were different enough to stop you in your tracks.

In the United States, 94% of the focus group missed the detail in the text that the young man was a famine victim. For Western capitalists, his sin was squandering his money.

In St. Petersburg, Russia — with a group of seminarians — 84% mentioned the famine, while only 34% mentioned him squandering his money. For the Eastern readers, shaped by a socialist background, his real sin was putting a price tag on his family. He was less wicked than foolish. They acknowledged he was stupid for wasting his money — but the really dumb thing was leaving home. Famines come. Disasters come. You need your family and your village.

A Bible school in Tanzania came up with something neither of the other groups had noticed: *No one gave him anything to eat.* They couldn't get past it. Tanzanians know famine. They know what it is to leave home for work and find yourself at the mercy of strangers. For them, what was determinative was the cruelty of the locals who let him starve.

What accounts for those different readings? Culture. Life experience.

Western readers tended to see the parable as a story of reform. The climax, for them, is when the boy "comes to himself" in the pig pen. Eastern readers tended to see it as a story of recovery — reading it in light of the parables that precede it. Did the sheep leave the fold out of greed? Did the lost coin see itself as less shiny than the others and take off? No. So for them, the climax is the father's joy: *what was lost has been found.* Tanzanian readers saw it as a story of contrast — Jesus answering the Pharisees who criticized him for eating with sinners and outcasts. The inhospitable people who wouldn't feed the prodigal are the Pharisees. The father's house, where sinners have a place at the table, is the kingdom.

— Mark Allan Powell, *What Do They Hear?: Bridging the Gap Between Pulpit and Pew* (Abingdon Press, 2007)
