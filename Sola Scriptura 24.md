Scripture Alone in the 1500’s
It is important to note that Martin Luther did not set out initially to split the church but to reform it from within. After he nailed the 95 theses to the door of the church in Wittenburg his teachings came under attack from the Roman Catholic Church
In 1519 he traveled to Leipzig to debate with Johann Eck - a prominent Catholic apologist
It was during this debate that the ideal of Scriptural authority and the authority of the church came in to direct conflict. Eck accused Luther of espousing the teachings of John Hus
Luther tried to refuse this moniker because Hus had been condemned as a heretic 100 years prior
Luther reviewed what Hus had actually taught and realized that he was more like Hus and that Eck was correct. Eck challenged Luther as to whether he was the only person who had any knowledge - “I answer that God once spoke through the mouth of an ass. I will tell you straight what I think. I am a Christian theologian, and I am bound, not only to assert, but to defend the truth with my blood and death. I believe freely and will be a slave to the authority of no one, whether council, university or pope”. Luther was forced to choose between the authority of the catholic church and Scripture - he chose Scripture.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Evaluation Date:** 2026-03-21
**Sermon:** Sola Scriptura
**Primary Text:** 2 Timothy 3:16; Romans 10:14; Psalm 119:105; Hebrews 1:1–2

---

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Passionate Defense of Biblical Authority That Runs Out of Scripture Before It Reaches the Gospel**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A historically grounded, apologetically energetic case for Sola Scriptura that marshals Luther, Calvin, the Reformation, and manuscript evidence with real conviction, while failing to open or exposit the cited texts and reducing the gospel to a single bracketed word at the very end.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test:** CONDITIONAL PASS — The sermon’s thesis (Scripture alone is our ultimate authority) is a legitimate doctrinal claim; the cited texts (2 Tim 3:16; Ps 119:105; Heb 1:1–2) do support it. However, none of these texts are actually opened and exposited. The sermon treats them as proof-texts and reference points, not as texts whose internal argument drives the sermon. The main point is asserted from tradition and history, not derived from a passage.

**Authority Test:** PASS — The preacher does not stand above the text as editor. He argues for Scripture’s authority without softening uncomfortable claims. The treatment of the UMC, female ordination, and theological liberalism is direct.

**Ruling:** The Berean Gate is conditionally passed, but the sermon is better described as a topical apologetic lecture *about* Scripture than a sermon *from* Scripture. The texts are cited but not opened.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Fatal Flaw #1: No clear gospel presentation.**

The word "Gospel." appears as the final word of the manuscript — a stage note, not a proclamation. There is no articulation of Christ’s finished work, no call to faith, no presentation of justification. The manuscript closes with a rhetorical question about whether God is worth knowing, followed only by the bracketed word "Gospel." as a reminder to preach it. This reminder, apparently never filled in, means the gospel is entirely absent from the written sermon.

**Score capped at 69.**

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Fatal Flaw #4: Application is entirely generic.**

The application ("we need to continue to be a bastion of Scriptural authority") is institutional and abstract. No specific call is made to any category of hearer. The self-righteous, the doubting, the wavering believer, the unbeliever — none are addressed with differentiated application.

**No additional flags triggered** (the sermon is accessible, broadly structured, and Christ is at least referenced).

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 10/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (3/10):* Four texts are cited but none are opened. The sermon offers no exegetical engagement with 2 Timothy 3:16, Psalm 119:105, Hebrews 1:1–2, or Romans 10:14. These verses appear as footnotes to a historical and apologetic argument, not as the engine of the sermon.
- *Contextual Awareness (2/5):* The Second Helvetic Confession, Luther’s Leipzig debate, and Calvin’s distinction are helpfully placed. Basic historical context is present.
- *Exegetical Clarity (2/5):* No Greek engagement. No unpacking of what the texts actually say. The Zwingli and Luther quotes do more exegetical work than the cited Scripture references.
- *Logical Faithfulness (3/5):* No eisegesis per se, but the texts are not given room to drive the argument. The sermon could be preached with the texts entirely omitted and nothing of substance would change.
- *FCF:* Partially identified — the sermon targets the misbelief that experience or tradition can supplement or override Scripture. This is implicit but coherent.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7):* Christ is mentioned in passing — the Bible is described as God’s preserved message, and there are brief references to knowing God through it. But Christ’s person and work are not the focus. The sermon defends the vehicle (Scripture) without arriving at the cargo (Christ).
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (2/3):* The Reformation narrative provides a partial redemptive-historical frame. The point about the Pharisees and oral tradition is helpfully made.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4/4):* The treatment of Sola Scriptura vs. Roman Catholic and JW views is accurate and careful. The distinction between the authority of the preacher’s office and the authority of the Word is well made.
- *Confessional Alignment (3/4):* Strong. The Second Helvetic Confession is quoted directly and accurately. The Reformed doctrine of the preached Word is stated with care.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 3/15**

This is the sermon’s deepest wound. The gospel is a stage note. The law/gospel dynamic is entirely absent. There is no conviction of sin, no presentation of Christ’s atoning work, no call to faith. The sermon defends the book without opening the book to its central message. The Synagogue Test is triggered: a devout Jew or thoughtful Muslim could sit through this entire sermon and find much to affirm — the authority of a divinely preserved book, the need to submit to it, the failures of liberalism — without being confronted by the scandal of the cross.

- *Clarity of the Gospel (0/5):* Absent.
- *Necessity of Christ (1/5):* Christ is mentioned once or twice in passing but is not essential to the sermon’s argument.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5):* The sermon does not orbit morality; it orbits apologetics. But the cross is not the destination.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 5/15**

- *Heart Penetration (2/5):* The appeal to personal Bible reading at the close has warmth. The rhetorical questions ("Isn’t he worth getting to know?") have some affective force.
- *Specificity (1/5):* The application is almost entirely institutional ("our church and personal lives"). No specific situation, no specific sinner, no specific struggle is addressed.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon implicitly addresses the "knowledgeable but unhumbled" — those who claim the Bible is authoritative but fail to submit to it. That’s one of Perkins’ seven. The others are untouched.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 3/8**

- *Call to Response (1/4):* The closest thing to a call is: "We need to continue to be a bastion of Scriptural authority." This is a posture description, not an exhortation. There is no "therefore do this now."
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The sermon does equip for theological discernment — it names specific errors (Rob Bell, Bill Johnson, the UMC) and explains why they are wrong. This is genuinely useful.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (2/4):* The sermon has discernible movements: historical problem → Reformation history → what Sola Scriptura is and is not → what the Bible says about itself → today’s challenge. But the outline is not stated and must be reconstructed by the listener.
- *Progression (3/3):* The historical movement from the Reformation to today’s challenge is well-paced and builds logically.
- *Unity (1/2):* The sermon broadly maintains its thesis, but the manuscript evidence section (30,000 manuscripts, Nostradamus comparison) introduces a second argument (reliability of Scripture) that sits alongside rather than within the Sola Scriptura argument.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 3/5**

- *Relevance (1/2):* The Logos software mention is well-placed and congregationally immediate. The Nostradamus/manuscript comparison is memorable.
- *Clarity (1/2):* Most illustrations clarify, though the Nostradamus tangent runs long.
- *Memorability (1/1):* The 30,000-manuscript argument is the kind of thing people carry home.

**8. Sermonic Force — 4/5**

This is the sermon’s quiet strength. It reads as preaching — direct address, rhetorical questions, oral cadence, personal warmth. The lines "Isn’t he worth getting to know?" and "Isn’t a God that did all this so we could know him, isn’t he worth a bit of our time?" have genuine warmth and oral force. The voice is pastoral and conversational throughout.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* Neither, primarily — it makes the listener think about the authority of a book. Christ is not the destination.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* Ideas — specifically, ideas about why the Bible should be trusted. The encounter with the living God through His Word does not occur.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* Informed, and helpfully so. The information is accurate and useful. But neither conviction of sin nor comfort of the gospel is present.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Reformation narrative is well-told and historically accurate.** The Leipzig debate, Luther’s choice between church and Scripture, Calvin’s contrast with Rome — these are presented clearly and with genuine understanding of what was at stake. The Second Helvetic Confession quote is apt and well-placed.
2. **The contemporary threat is named with courage and specificity.** Rob Bell, Bill Johnson, and the UMC are named and their errors are identified concretely. The preacher does not retreat into vagueness when naming theological failure.
3. **The manuscript evidence section is unexpectedly compelling.** The 30,000-manuscript comparison with Nostradamus is the kind of illustration that lodges in memory and gives congregants a tool for defending biblical reliability in everyday conversation.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is a stage note.** The word "Gospel." appears at the end of the manuscript as a reminder — apparently never developed. This is not a minor gap; it is the absence of the sermon’s only essential content. Everything the sermon defends (the authority of Scripture) exists to deliver the gospel. The defense of the vehicle without delivery of the cargo is a fundamental homiletical failure.
2. **None of the cited texts are opened.** The sermon cites 2 Timothy 3:16, Romans 10:14, Psalm 119:105, and Hebrews 1:1–2 as section headings or footnotes — but none of them are read, explained, or exposited. A sermon that defends the authority of Scripture while declining to open Scripture to the congregation is a profound irony.
3. **Application is abstract and institutional.** "We need to continue to be a bastion of Scriptural authority" is an ecclesial posture statement, not a personal call. No individual hearer is cornered. The self-satisfied Bible-reader, the wavering believer, the skeptic, and the unbeliever are all addressed identically — and none are addressed searchingly.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears not to see the irony: a sermon defending the authority and sufficiency of Scripture that itself does not open Scripture. The sermon argues for the primacy of the Word over tradition, experience, and human authority — while the sermon itself is structured around historical and apologetic arguments rather than the unfolding of a text. The argument is self-undermining in practice, even if sound in theory.

Additionally, the preacher may not see that the Barna statistics and cultural analysis, while interesting, function as extended throat-clearing. The congregation likely needs less sociological framing and more textual encounter.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The historical fluency feels like exposition but is not. The preacher moves confidently through Reformation history, quotes Luther, Calvin, Zwingli, and the Second Helvetic Confession — and it reads like a sermon that has done its homework. But this is apologetic and historical competence, not exegetical engagement with a text. The congregation may feel they have received a Scripture-based sermon when they have received a lecture about Scripture.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The texts cited — especially 2 Timothy 3:16 and Hebrews 1:1–2 — are themselves high-octane gospel passages. Hebrews 1:1–2 is an opening statement of Christ as the full and final Word of God. "In these last days he has spoken to us by his Son" — this is the very point the sermon is trying to make, and it is never opened. A careful reading of Hebrews 1:1–2 would have given the sermon both its apologetic argument and its gospel center simultaneously.

2 Timothy 3:16–17 not only asserts that "all Scripture is God-breathed" but concludes that its purpose is to make the man of God "complete, equipped for every good work" — a telos that is christological and sanctifying. This was the natural place to bridge from the doctrine of Scripture to life in Christ.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Choose one text and open it.** The sermon needs to be rebuilt around a primary passage — Hebrews 1:1–2 or 2 Timothy 3:16–17 are natural candidates. Let the argument of the text drive the sermon’s argument, and let the Reformation history serve as illustration and context, not as the main movement.
2. **Develop the gospel section.** The stage note "Gospel." must become a full proclamation. Suggested placement: at the conclusion of the section on Scripture’s self-testimony, transition to: "And what does this book, which God has so miraculously preserved, say? It says you are a sinner who cannot save yourself. It says Christ died in your place. It says all who call upon His name will be saved." The entire sermon’s defense of Scripture builds toward this delivery.
3. **Add differentiated application.** At minimum, distinguish: (a) the believer who trusts Scripture but does not read it — "You say you trust the Bible. When did you last open it?"; (b) the person who says the Bible is outdated — press the conscience with the specific claim; (c) the unbeliever in the room — invite them to bring their doubt to the text, not to tradition.
4. **Tighten the manuscript section.** The Nostradamus illustration works but the section runs overlong. One pass with a hard word count target (cut by 40%) would sharpen its force.
5. **Open with the Berean Gate text.** Acts 17:11 or 2 Timothy 3:16 as the opening text would ground the entire lecture in Scripture from the first moment — modeling what the sermon is arguing for.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This is a topical doctrinal sermon on the doctrine of Scripture, not an expository sermon on a single passage. The rubric was designed for expository preaching and bends moderately here. The Exposition & Hermeneutics category in particular scores low not because the preacher mishandled a text, but because no text was substantially handled. The topical format is legitimate in certain contexts (a confessional series, a membership class, a special doctrinal occasion), but even topical sermons must open their texts — and this one does not. The rubric’s low exposition score is therefore a diagnosis of the format, not merely of execution. The gospel-centrality and application failures, however, apply regardless of format.
