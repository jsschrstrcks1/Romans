# For Such a Time as This

**Passage:** Esther 4:10–17
**Series:** Reading Through the Bible
**Key verse (opening):** Joshua 1:9

> *"Have I not commanded you? Be strong and courageous. Do not be frightened, and do not be dismayed, for the Lord your God is with you wherever you go."*
> — Joshua 1:9 (ESV)

---

In our reading through the Bible this week — which I hope you've been doing with us — we have been in part reading through the book of Esther. In keeping with my promise to preach out of what we are reading, we are going to be in the book of Esther today, chapter 4.

While you're turning there, here is our people group of the week.

**The Uyghur.** They are a Turkic people group and speak a language that is a mix of eight separate languages including Russian, Arabic, Chinese, and Mongolian. They are a people on the precipice of disaster. There are nearly 13 million of them, almost all of whom are trapped in internment camps where their people are being brainwashed, forced to speak Mandarin, and compelled to reject their religion, culture, and politics. There are some outside of China — those who have escaped to nearby countries. To our knowledge, there are less than one one-hundredth of a percent who have accepted Jesus Christ as Savior. Tens of millions of people, lost and headed for hell, with missionaries not even allowed to talk to them.

Who's willing to pray for the Uyghur?

In our reading this week, some things about Esther struck me that hadn't really fully struck me before. Everyone always talks about good and godly Esther obeying God, saving her people. But as is the case with every person God uses — and that is every person in the Bible — the truth is much more multifaceted and messy.

But before we get to Esther herself, let's talk about how the situation in the palace came to be.

It all started with Queen Vashti. Queen Vashti was the queen of the Medes and Persians, wife of the king. She is described by Jewish historians as being vain and wicked. Her husband, King Ahasuerus, thought she was quite attractive. She was his wife, and he ordered her to parade in front of his friends wearing her crown. Based on Vashti's reaction, I think it's safe to say he wanted her to walk around in only her crown. The king and his buddies were at the tail end of a 180-day drunken banquet. Safe to say his decision-making skills were not fully intact — and so when his wife, when Queen Vashti refused, one of his likely also-drunk advisors convinced him to divorce her and banish her from the kingdom. When he sobered up, he realized it was a stupid mistake. But as the king, his word was law and completely irrevocable — even by him. So his advisors told him to hold what people frequently call a beauty pageant, but that's short-changing the truth also. He gathered up all the beautiful women in the land and ordered them into his bedroom for the night. If what they did pleased him, and he remembered their name, then they would be queen. If not, they stayed as his concubines — kept, as well as possible, but never truly free. They were each given basically anything they wanted — servants, extravagant beauty treatments, clothing, jewelry, and choice foods. But they couldn't leave the palace. Far less than ideal. You're basically taken at the whim of the king. In other respects it would have been an easy and soft life for the times, but that doesn't change what it was.

So it's Esther's turn to go spend the night with the king. We aren't told that she argued, fought, or tried to hide. We aren't told she tried to maintain her dignity, or her virginity for that matter. We aren't told what she did. But whatever she did do made the king very happy, and he decides to marry her.

Shortly thereafter, Esther's cousin and adopted father Mordecai uncovered and exposed a plot to kill the king — a thing he was never fully honored for. But that didn't seem to bother Mordecai.

One day one of the king's drinking buddies — I mean advisors — Haman, earned favor with the king, and the king declared that everyone had to bow to Haman as he was coming and going. Mordecai refused. Because who is the only one worthy of worship? God.

This really agitated Haman, and he committed himself to causing genocide because of his bruised pride. At this point, all the Jews we know about are in captivity in Persia. I think this is probably the pettiest thing that has ever led to an attempt at genocide. Either way, the king — perhaps drunk again — agreed to Haman's plot without knowing it would affect his wife and the man who had just saved his life.

And that's where we pick up chapter four.

There are people with all kinds of theories, and there are scores of conflicting rabbinic traditions. But one thing I found interesting is that Esther is held up as being one of the seven most beautiful women of the world, and that the king was as captivated by her the first night as he was on their last night. Rabbinic tradition also holds, based on calculations in the Seder Olam Rabbah, that Esther may have been as old as 74 or 75 when the king married her — though other traditions place her at 40. We don't really know. Scripture doesn't say. It does call her a virgin. Her first night with her would-be husband, her first experience of what was only meant to be tasted in marriage, was to some extent forced upon her. Only after all of this — a process that may have taken four to five years — only after this was King Ahasuerus able to set aside his feelings for Vashti, and he had the statues and paintings of her taken down from the palace. Esther had made a big impression.

I and my young women will also fast in the same way. Then I will go to the king, though it is against the law, and if I perish, I perish." Mordecai then went away and did everything as Esther had ordered him.

---

I had a professor point out one time that King Ahasuerus hasn't changed. He's still prone to drunken excess. He's still going to expect Esther to do whatever he expects. And yet we don't see Esther standing up to him or putting a stop to it. She may not have had the strength. Indeed, even when her entire race and her beloved father Mordecai's life are in peril, her initial reaction is basically fear and timidity. She says: if I approach the king uninvited and he doesn't receive me favorably, I'm dead. Her concern was completely valid. Despite the favor the king had shown Esther, he still hadn't sent for her in thirty days — that's Esther 4:11. So if she walks in and the king is annoyed by her presence, she's dead.

I love Mordecai's response. "Our people will be saved one way or the other. But either way you'll probably die. And who knows — maybe this is exactly what God is doing in the midst of all this."

I'm not sure that's the motivational speech I'd give to soldiers going into battle. *Either way, whatever happens today, you're probably going to die.* But it was exactly what Esther needed to hear to have the courage to put her life on the line for her people.

We see Esther's response in verse 16. Can someone read that loudly for me?

*"Go, gather all the Jews to be found in Susa, and hold a fast on my behalf... Then I will go to the king, though it is against the law, and if I perish, I perish."* — Esther 4:16 (ESV)

Go. Fast. Pray. And I'll do what I have to do. If I perish, I perish.

---

It strikes me that each and every one of us in this room are in the positions we are in for a reason.

Some of us are retired. Some of us have been entrusted with raising the next generation. Some of us are musicians, and business people, and a dozen other things. Some of us have been given extra opportunities to talk to medical professionals of different kinds. Some of us work for or with people who would never darken the doorway of a church. Maybe you are where you are so God can use you to reach those people. Who knows, as Mordecai says — "whether you have not come to the kingdom for such a time as this?"

Life is fragile. We don't know how much of it we have. But maybe the lost people who are in your life are in your life because God wants you to tell them about His Son. Esther had to risk her life to save God's people. We only have to risk opening our mouths to see God save people.

---

Esther wasn't perfect. No one is. But when Esther realized she had nothing to lose and everything to gain, she did three things. She started with prayer, spoke with boldness, and was obedient to the promptings of God.

Prayer is such an important thing, and yet we neglect it all too often — until we're in the middle of a storm. God wants our prayers before, during, and after. He wants our worship before, during, and after a trial. He wants us to read his Word before, during, and after the storm. He wants us to not forsake the assembly of believers before, during, and after the storm.

Esther may not have been perfect, but I think she's relatable. Who hasn't been to a point in their life where they were faced with a task they didn't feel equipped for? Who hasn't had shortcomings? Who isn't flawed? Yet God used Esther to save her people, and God uses us today. Even with our shortcomings, God desired us as a people enough that He sent His Son to die for us.

---

Another thing about Esther. She didn't choose the king — the king chose her. She didn't work to be chosen queen; the king bestowed it upon her. Likewise, we are incapable of ever being good enough to enter heaven on our own. Believing God exists isn't enough. Scripture says even the demons believe — and shudder (James 2:19, ESV). Belief in the existence of a God is not enough. You have to have a relationship with God to be saved.

---

Ultimately, Haman died for his own sins — hanged on the very gallows he had built to execute Mordecai. He got what we all deserve for our rebellion: death. *(Note: ESV renders this "hanged on the gallows" — Esther 7:10 — though the Hebrew word and Persian practice both suggest what many scholars translate as impalement on a stake.)*

Esther was willing to die for her people, even though they had done nothing to deserve her sacrifice. Yet Haman ended up losing in the end. As you read ahead in the coming week, you'll see Haman caught in his own trap — executed on the very pole he had erected to impale Mordecai. Instead of Mordecai dying, Haman died. Instead of Haman continuing in his position of power and authority, Mordecai was given possession of Haman's estate, his wealth, his titles, and his honors.

Apart from Christ, what we truly deserve is eternal separation from God. But God loved us so much that He sent His Son to die on the cross for us.

---

This whole book is a picture of so many things. There is so much deep symbolism we could spend an hour just talking about it. But let's just point to the big picture.

When Adam and Eve sinned in the garden and introduced sin into the world, do you remember what God said to the serpent? As part of his curse — to eat dirt and crawl on his belly — God said at the end: the offspring of the woman will bruise your head, and you will bruise his heel (Genesis 3:15, ESV). This is but one of many Old Testament prophecies regarding Christ. Though Satan would strike at the heel of the Son of God, Christ would crush the serpent's head. Though the enemy would put Christ's earthly body in the tomb, in so doing, he would for all time defeat sin and death and hell and Satan himself. We see this grand narrative acted out in the lives of Esther and Haman as well. Haman tried to kill God's people. What happened? Haman was the one who ended up dead.

---

One last tradition before we close. It is said in some ancient Jewish sources — Midrash Esther Rabbah among them — that as Esther heard the news of what was to befall her people, and then prepared and went to the king, she recited Psalm 22. You remember Psalm 22 — the one that talks about being encircled by enemies, surrounded by those who mock. The one that prophesies about Christ being surrounded, mocked, and weak, and nailed to the cross.

Esther laid her life on the line for her people, saving them from certain death. Christ laid down His life for His people, saving them from eternal death.

Esther may have had her failings, but Christ did not fail. He lived a perfect life. Esther was able to survive. Christ died — and by that death, secured the salvation of everyone who would ever believe. But Christ didn't stay dead. Scripture says three days after His crucifixion He rose again and was seen — history records — by more than 500 witnesses (1 Corinthians 15:6).
