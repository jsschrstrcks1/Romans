### Critical correction: The Noushad case

The original manuscript states Noushad's eye was gouged out. **This is incorrect.** According to Human Rights Watch documentation of the case:

> Puthan Veettil Abd ul-Latif Noushad, an Indian migrant worker, was sentenced to have his right eye gouged out following a prison altercation in which the other man was blinded. The sentence was ultimately **not carried out** because the victim forgave him.

## Sermon Body

The law shows our unrighteousness. Our unrighteousness shows God's righteousness. When we compare our own failings to the perfection of God, his wrath is proved to be just.

When we violate the law of the land, we deserve the prescribed punishment — even when that law is strange to our ears, even when the punishment seems disproportionate to our sensibilities.

In some countries, theft is answered with the loss of a hand. In Malaysia and Singapore, judicial caning is carried out for offenses ranging from vandalism to certain crimes of violence. And the procedure is not casual. The prisoner is examined by a doctor first — caning cannot proceed without medical clearance. He is strapped in a bent position, with protective padding securing his lower back and kidneys, so that only the designated area is exposed. The number of strokes is fixed by the judge. If the prisoner becomes medically unfit mid-punishment, the remaining strokes cannot simply be administered later — the court may instead impose additional imprisonment in their place.

The law demands its due.

In certain countries operating under traditional Islamic jurisprudence, *qisas* — retributive justice, eye for eye, tooth for tooth — is applied literally. Saudi Arabia is among the only countries in the world where court-ordered retributive blinding is a legally sanctioned punishment. In 2005, Human Rights Watch documented the case of an Indian migrant worker named Noushad — sentenced to have his right eye surgically removed after he blinded another man during a prison fight he did not start. He had tried to defend himself; in the struggle, the other man was blinded. The court ordered Noushad's eye removed in retribution. He sat under that sentence. And here is a detail the headlines often miss: in the end, the sentence was not carried out. The victim forgave him. Under qisas, the victim's family has the right to accept compensation in place of physical punishment — and in this case, they did. Noushad kept his eye.

The principle behind qisas is ancient. It appears in Exodus 21, Leviticus 24, and Deuteronomy 19. It is not arbitrary cruelty — it is a formal legal expression of proportionality. The punishment fits the crime. God himself embedded this framework into the law of Moses, not because pain is virtuous, but because sin has a cost, and justice demands it be paid.

In parts of rural India, village councils have in documented cases ordered punishment against an entire family for what one member did. In 2015 in Baghpat, Uttar Pradesh, a khap panchayat — an unelected village council — ordered that two Dalit sisters, ages fifteen and twenty-three, be raped and paraded naked through their village. Their crime? Their brother had eloped with a woman from a higher caste. The case reached India's Supreme Court. Amnesty International documented it. The pattern is not isolated — similar abuses have been documented across Uttar Pradesh, Haryana, Punjab, and Rajasthan. These councils are not acting within Indian law — the Indian legal system condemns them — but they act with impunity, and people suffer under them.

Aren't you glad God's laws always make sense?

---

When we violate God's law, do we deserve punishment any less? Certainly not.

God's punishments make sense. God's punishments are just. Yet God is love. Toward the end of our chapter (v. 23) it says: *"for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."* The law of scripture is simple. Jesus said: *"You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength"* (Mark 12:30, ESV), and love your neighbor as yourself. He said that on these two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets (Matthew 22:40).

God's laws are simple and straightforward. His punishments are eternal.

---

The second half of verse 5: Paul asks, *"What shall we say? That God is unrighteous to inflict wrath on us?"* And then the thunderous answer: *"By no means!"* (Romans 3:5, ESV).

Paul is fighting against two extremes through these verses and pointing out that the truth stands in the middle. On one hand we have **antinomianism** — the idea that one can sin freely, with abandon, without any thought to consequence or heavenly chastisement. On the other hand is **legalism** — the belief that our works are what save us. The Romans had a penchant for the former; the Jewish believers had a tendency toward the other.

The truth: we are forgiven — but to quote Paul directly: *"Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means!"* (Romans 6:1–2, ESV). And from verse 8 of our passage: *"And why not do evil that good may come?"* — Paul identifies that charge as slander, and says the condemnation of those who reason that way is just.

Both legalism and antinomianism are rampant in today's church. I know a man — met him on a missions trip — who endeavors to keep the entirety of the Old Testament law. The dietary restrictions especially. He absolutely refuses to eat bacon. Can you imagine that? He abides by his interpretation of every OT law. On the other hand, there are believers in today's church who argue that since they have accepted Christ they are "signed, sealed, and delivered" — which is absolutely true — and so in light of this they revisit their pet sins, feel little or no guilt, and have no worries about whether they will be in heaven one day.

Folks, our sin should deeply grieve us. As we come to know the Lord better and more deeply, our sin should cause us deep shame. That shame — and I'm not talking about guilt here. Shame is deeper. Shame is stronger. Shame can only be lifted by blood — by the blood of the Lamb. The shame we feel should drive us to Christ. It should drive us to repentance. It should drive us closer to the Lord.

Shall we continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means.

---

You know, at this juncture I want to be careful. I think introspection is healthy and good, especially when the stakes are eternal. It is good to ask yourself whether you are truly saved once in a while — especially if, after you check your soul, you find that you do not yet know Jesus as your Savior. I would rather you go through this uncomfortable process than miss heaven — like we discussed last week — by eighteen inches.

At the same time, there are many beautiful Christians out there who do have a deep and abiding struggle with the realities of sin and the question of their salvation. Becoming a Christian is permanent. Once you accept Christ, it can't be undone, given back, or lost. Salvation is sort of like a tattoo on your soul.

I was watching TV the other day and this show came on called *Tattoo Nightmares*. People — usually people who had been drinking — would get tattoos from artists who were new, or who didn't yet know what they were doing, or sometimes from someone being outright malicious. This one girl desperately wanted a tattoo and begged and pleaded with her parents to get her one. They finally relented — but they had already made a private arrangement with the parlor: make the tattoo ugly, and the process miserably painful, so that she will never want to get another one. For this they agreed to pay an extra five hundred dollars. The little daisy she had envisioned on her shoulder ended up looking something like a bloody maw. The artist pushed hard enough that it caused scarring and distortion. What was meant to be a cute little daisy left the girl traumatized and feeling betrayed. As an adult, she had tried laser removal and a number of other things said to remove tattoos. But the scarring was still there. She came onto this show to tell her story — and if she was lucky — to have the tattoo and scar covered up by a new tattoo, so she could try to move past the betrayal and anger she felt every time she looked in the mirror. Ultimately they were able to cover it up. The ugly thing was gone — covered by something beautiful, something that reminded her of her grandmother.

It's like this for us too. We through sin have tattooed our soul. We sin and sin and sin, and color it darker. We know that sin separates us from God, and so each new sin may as well be like getting another death flower tattooed upon it. Over the course of our lives, the wages of that sin is death. The father of lies tricks us into justifying what we do — and that is antinomianism. On the other hand, some of us he tricks into thinking: if we just do a little more, if we just follow the law a little better, then we will be saved. The truth though, as Paul says, is in the middle. It's all about the relationship with Jesus. If you have one — if he's paid the price for your sins — then no matter what else happens, the "death flower" tattoo has been permanently removed from your soul.

Whether you are an effective servant of the Lord is another story. I read a book a while back — I've forgotten the title, though I could find it — but it said: *"Those who have been most effective for the Lord are those who least resemble the world."* I believe that is true. But don't think I'm talking about legalism here. I'm not trying to get you to set up travel tents every few miles on Sundays. Or to give up bacon.

It all comes back to motivation. If you're trying to do enough good to offset the bad, or trying to do enough good to buy a place in heaven, you're doing it wrong. We ought to be doing good things from a place of love and gratitude for our salvation. Not out of a sense of debt, but out of love. These two themes will come back several times in our study — pay attention to them when they appear in the coming weeks.

In an effort to cleanse the conscience apart from Christ, they sacrifice their holiness on an altar to themselves. They persuade themselves it's acceptable to sin freely because God's saved them from all their sins. It's true that for someone who is truly saved, all sins are forgiven. But it's also true that the truly saved cannot rest long in their sin. God doesn't let them. He renews our conscience and helps keep us from growing calluses over our hearts. He actively draws us away from the things that seek to put dark flower tattoos on our soul. He changes our desires, our wants, our ambitions to be more in line with what he wants.

In that sense, being a Christian is like being a piece of metal near a magnet. The more we read the Bible, pray to God, and so forth — the more our metal is exposed to God's magnet. A piece of metal exposed to a magnet becomes a magnet itself. The longer it's exposed, and the stronger the magnet it's exposed to, the stronger a magnet the piece of metal becomes. The metal can't help it — these changes are outside the control of the metal. Apart from God, the hunk of metal is useless. But once magnetized, it has a purpose.

Like that metal, once we are saved we have a purpose. Not out of obligation but out of love. Not to earn or manipulate God into giving us what we want — but because we have a reverent fear of him. Because we truly believe he went to the cross for our sins. Because we know what he did for each and every one of us. Because we love him for it.

---

As you know, the only thing we truly deserve is to spend an eternity in hell apart from Christ. None of us are righteous — not one (v. 10). *"Their mouth is full of curses and bitterness"* (v. 14, ESV). *"In their paths are ruin and misery"* (v. 16, ESV). The best we can do on our own is filthy rags. And yet God's word is written on our hearts — so most people have a general idea of right and wrong, even if they don't follow God's word for God's reasons.

That's something that drives postmodern atheists wild. Nearly every sane person shares the same basic moral instincts about what is right and what is wrong. Pretty much every culture believes that murder is wrong. Pretty much every culture believes that if it doesn't belong to you, you shouldn't take it. These absolutes have to come from somewhere. The atheist argues there is nothing beyond the narrative — nothing supernatural, nothing deeper than what a human can hear, taste, smell, touch, and see. But if there is something embedded in mankind — all mankind — something that for the most part transcends race, country, and creed, something that transcends the human experience itself — then how can they continue to believe there is nothing out there beyond themselves?

I truly believe it takes more faith to be an atheist than it does to be a Christian. Life and the world just make more sense to me with a Creator God who is actively involved.

## Closing

You know, as the praise team comes up here to sing and close the service, I want you to be thinking about your own soul tonight. Is it like that girl's shoulder — hideously scarred and made ugly by sin? Or have you come to a point in your spiritual life where you've asked Jesus to take your sin, and tattoo a masterpiece on your soul?

If you've never done that — come talk to me.

If you are looking for a church home — come talk to me.

Maybe tonight you feel like you've fallen into the sin of antinomianism, and you've forgotten why you fell in love with Christ in the first place, and you want to rededicate — if so, come talk to me.

Don't miss heaven by eighteen inches.
