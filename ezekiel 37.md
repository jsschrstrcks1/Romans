# Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

## 1. Verdict Title
**A Sermon That Knows Where It's Going but Never Gets There: Total Depravity Preached, the Gospel Gestured At**

## 2. One-Sentence Summary
A sermon on Ezekiel 37 that handles the doctrine of total depravity with genuine clarity and useful illustration but fails to open the passage exegetically, collapses before the gospel is actually proclaimed, and sends the congregation home with a framework where they needed a feast.

## 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: Borderline Pass.**
The sermon's thesis — that humanity is spiritually dead apart from God, and that God must do the work of bringing dead bones to life — is consistent with Ezekiel 37's vision of the valley of dry bones. The text's main point is God's sovereign revivification of the dead (both nationally for Israel and spiritually as a picture of new birth). The sermon lands near this, though it arrives via a doctrinal introduction (total depravity) rather than via the text itself.

**Authority Test: Pass.**
The preacher does not soften the text's claims. God's initiative, man's deadness, and the necessity of divine work are all affirmed. No uncomfortable passages are quietly edited.

**Verdict: The Berean Gate passes. The main point of the sermon (dead men need God to breathe life into them) is the main point of the text. The sermon does not contradict the text — but it largely ignores the passage's structure, details, and redemptive-historical setting.**

## 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 1 — TRIGGERED: No clear gospel presentation.**
The sermon ends with the word "Gospel." in isolation — a placeholder, not a proclamation. No substitutionary atonement, no justification by faith, no call to repent and trust Christ. The preacher says "God sent his son Jesus to pay our sin debt" in a single sentence and stops. This is a gospel gesture, not a gospel announcement. A person who has never heard the gospel could leave this sermon knowing they are spiritually dead without knowing what to do about it.

**Flag 5 — TRIGGERED: No actual exhortation.**
"Perhaps you are here today and you realize you have never truly committed your life to Christ" is an observation, not a call. The congregation is told they can be changed; they are not told what to believe, repent of, or do. The gospel section that should complete the sermon is marked only with the word "Gospel." — which appears to be a note to the preacher to preach the gospel, not the gospel itself.

The preacher likely does not see that **the note "Gospel." at the end of the manuscript reveals the sermon was never finished.** It appears to be a writing prompt left in a draft — the place where the gospel was going to be inserted and never was. ## 12. Illusion of Strength

**The doctrinal depth of the total depravity section creates an illusion of sermonic completeness.** The teaching is accurate, illustrated, and even catechetically useful. A congregation may feel they've received a solid sermon because they learned something true and important. But total depravity without the gospel is not a Christian sermon — it is half of a law-gospel dialectic with the second half missing. The hammer was swung. The balm was never opened.

**The St. Louis block party story feels like a gospel conclusion but isn't.** It ends with a man praying to receive Christ — which creates an emotional sense of gospel resolution. But the congregation was not told to pray to receive Christ. The story shows conversion; it does not call for it.

## 13. Missed Opportunity

**Ezekiel 37 is one of the most powerful pictures of regeneration and new covenant fulfillment in the entire Old Testament — and it was barely touched.** The two-stage revivification (bones → flesh → breath) maps precisely onto the new birth: moral reformation without the Spirit produces a well-dressed corpse, not a living soul. The sermon had this text in hand and used it as a visual. The entire second half of the sermon could have been built on verse 14: "I will put my Spirit in you, and you shall live." That is the gospel in Ezekiel's idiom. The redemptive-historical thread from Ezekiel 36:26-27 (the new heart, the new Spirit) to Pentecost to the new birth in John 3 was available and untouched.

## 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Preach the gospel before the sermon ends.** In the final section, after "God loves us too much to leave us that way," add a gospel paragraph: Christ came, died as the substitute for dead sinners, rose from the dead, and now calls the dead to live. Faith is not willing your own resurrection — it is hearing the herald and being made alive by God who works through the Word. Then call: "Believe on the Lord Jesus Christ and you will be saved."
2. **Exhort, don't observe.** Replace "Perhaps you are here today and you realize you have never truly committed your life to Christ" with a direct summons: "You are the valley of dry bones. God is commanding you to live. The voice of God comes to you through the gospel right now — respond to it. Repent of your sin. Trust in Christ. That is what it means for the breath to enter you."
3. **Open Ezekiel 37 verse by verse, at minimum through v. 14.** The two-stage revival is the sermon's backbone and it is currently unpreached. Trace the movement: bones to flesh is reformation without the Spirit (religion without regeneration); breath is the Spirit's work alone. This is the text doing its own application.
4. **Connect to Ezekiel 36:26-27 as the interpretive frame.** Chapter 37 is the vision; chapter 36 is the promise that explains it — God will put his Spirit within you. This is the new covenant in preview. Showing the congregation that the bones passage sits inside God's new covenant promise elevates the text's power dramatically.
5. **Choose two illustrations, not three.** The glasses, the Dalmatian, and the bleach all illustrate the same truth. The Dalmatian does the most theological work; the bleach is the most memorable. Keep both. Cut the glasses, or use it only as a one-sentence setup before moving to the more developed illustrations.
6. **Differentiate hearers.** After "we've all been dry bones," address three groups: (a) the person who has already been made alive — "You are not bones; worship the God who breathed into you." (b) The person who knows the truth intellectually but has never responded — "You are the corpse with flesh on it: the body is formed, the breath has not come. The Spirit is offered to you today." (c) The person who doesn't know what they are — "The fact that you can hear this sermon and feel uneasy is the first sign God may be working in you."

## 17. Rubric Fit Note

Ezekiel 37 is apocalyptic vision embedded in prophetic narrative — a genre that the rubric's gospel centrality and exhortation categories can stress, since the text's "application" is primarily doxological and covenantal rather than immediately prescriptive. However, that genre caveat does not explain or excuse the omissions here. The text explicitly warrants a gospel proclamation (the promise of the Spirit, new life, covenant restoration), an exhortation grounded in the promise (God will do this for dead sinners — receive it), and a redemptive-historical grounding (Pentecost, new birth, new covenant). The rubric is not straining against this passage. The passage was straining under a sermon that never fully arrived.
