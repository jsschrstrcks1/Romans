- **"39 lashes from the cat of nine tails"** — two distinct historical concerns:
  1. *39 lashes*: The "forty lashes minus one" comes from Jewish law (Deut 25:3) as applied to Paul's floggings (2 Cor 11:24). Jesus was flogged by Romans (Mt 27:26; Mk 15:15; Jn 19:1), who were not bound by Jewish flogging limits. The Gospels give no number for the lashes Jesus received. The "39 lashes" applied to Jesus is a popular tradition, not a scriptural claim.
  2. *Cat of nine tails*: The Roman flagrum/flagellum had multiple leather thongs (often tipped with bone or metal), but the term "cat of nine tails" refers specifically to a British naval/penal instrument and is historically anachronistic for the Roman instrument. The Roman flagellum typically had 2–3 thongs, not nine. The intent — a multi-thonged, brutal whip — is directionally accurate, but the terminology is imprecise.
  - **Recommendation**: The emotional force of the passage does not depend on either specific claim. Consider softening to "every lash of the Roman whip" or similar.
- **"Earth took 7 days to complete"**: Minor imprecision — Genesis records 6 days of creation work with God resting on day 7. The sermon uses "7 days" loosely to mean "the creation week." Not a theological error; noting for completeness.

Thanksgiving is one of the better holidays I think, right up there with Christmas and Easter. In listening to people preach this week it struck me that one of the purposes of mankind was to give thanksgiving to God. We ought to be thankful for our very existence. God didn't have to create us. Had he not decided to, we would never have had the opportunity to see a pretty sunrise or set. We'd never have the opportunity to worship God, to choose. We never would have even come into being.

Further, we should be thankful for his continued patience with us. When I was a kid my mom would on occasion say, "I brought you into this world, I can take you out, and make another one that looks just like you." While that was not in the scope of my mother's power — not really — it was within the scope of God's power. He created us perfect, in a perfect world, and as soon as he asked us not to do something, we ran right over and messed things up just like any kid does when told not to take a cookie from the jar. At that moment, he could have — and would have been just in doing so — obliterated mankind and the earth and started fresh. What's a week's work to a God of infinite time? He could have made us differently so we didn't screw up so badly so quickly, but he chose to continue working with us even though we had taken a sledgehammer to his perfect creation, shattering his reflection that he had so painstakingly imbued everything with.

You ever spend a bunch of time working on something? Trying to make something nice and special, only to have a kid come along and trash it before you had a chance to gift it or put it on display? That's basically what we did to God.

He would have been within his rights to destroy us; instead he died for us. For this we should be thankful.

We should be thankful for every nail hammered into the hands of Christ, thankful for each of the 39 lashes Christ took from the cat of nine tails, thankful for Christ's substitutionary atonement for our sins.

He has done so much for us, we owe him so much gratitude and thanks. Even then he didn't stop.

Did you ever stop to think that earth took seven days to complete — Earth, our solar system, our universe, our galaxy, and there are millions of them out there. Every scenic vista, every sunset, every mountain, all implicitly or explicitly created that first week.

God has been working on heaven as far as we know ever since. Earth is pretty amazing. Just imagine what our God could create with a year. Or ten years. Now ten thousand years. What a place heaven will be. What a wonderful God we serve.

When Paul commands believers to stop sinning, he also commands believers to put thanksgiving in its place. Paul writes: "Let there be no filthiness nor foolish talk nor crude joking, which are out of place, but instead let there be thanksgiving" (Ephesians 5:4).

Paul knows that we are always acting. We either act with sinful, thankless hearts which leads to foolish talk or crude jokes, or we act with thankful hearts and thereby please God with our speech. John Piper explains:

> The key to unlocking a heart of gratitude and overcoming bitterness and ugliness and disrespect and violence is a strong belief in God, the Creator and Sustainer and Provider and Hope-giver. If we do not believe we are deeply indebted to God for all we have or hope to have, then the very spring of gratitude has gone dry.
>
> — John Piper, "Violence, Ugliness, and Thanksgiving" *(quote to be verified against original source)*

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title

**A Warm Devotional With a Gospel Gesture: Theologically Earnest but Homiletically Undeveloped**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A brief Thanksgiving address that moves from creation gratitude through Christ's substitutionary atonement to Paul's command in Ephesians 5:4, but lacks expository depth, structural development, and any sustained applicatory or exhortative force — functioning as a devotional opening to corporate prayer rather than a preached sermon.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**PASS — barely, with significant qualification.**

The Scopus Test passes at the lowest threshold: the sermon uses Ephesians 5:4 as its scriptural anchor ("let there be thanksgiving"), and the sermon's theme is thanksgiving. However, the main point of the sermon is not derived from the main point of Ephesians 5:4. Paul's command in that verse arises from a passage about replacing the deeds of darkness (5:3–4) with the character of the light-walking saint — a command embedded in a larger ethical argument about sexual immorality, covetousness, and speech. The sermon takes "let there be thanksgiving" as a theme word and builds a topical message around it, using the text as a landing pad rather than an engine. The sermon is topically about thanksgiving; it is not exegetically about Ephesians 5:4.

The Authority Test passes: the preacher does not soften or contradict the text. But the text is not truly under exposition — it is cited near the midpoint as support for a conclusion already underway.

Gate verdict: **Conditional pass.** The sermon is not contrary to the text, but it does not arise from the text's own argument. The rubric's mercy here lies in the fact that this is a brief holiday devotional, not a full expository address. The gate is not slammed — but the sermon is noted as topical with a text, not text-driven.

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**No fatal flaws triggered by strict criteria**, with the following observations:

- **Flag #1 (No clear gospel presentation)**: The gospel is present and explicit — "He died for us," "Christ's substitutionary atonement for our sins," "every nail hammered into the hands of Christ." This is more than a placeholder. The flag does not trigger.
- **Flag #4 (Application entirely generic)**: The application is entirely generic. There is no differentiated address, no specific congregation, no particular sinner cornered. The call is universally "be thankful" without any specificity about what that looks like in concrete life. This approaches Flag #4 but the flag is reserved for complete absence; this is near-absence.
- **Flag #5 (No actual exhortation)**: The sermon does not exhort. The prayer prompt at the close ("Let's spend some time thanking God for...") is a pastoral gathering move, not an exhortation. The congregation is never summoned to a specific response or called to repent of ingratitude. This is the closest the sermon comes to a fatal flaw without triggering the cap.
- **Flag #6 (Inaccessible)**: The opposite concern applies — the sermon is maximally accessible, to the point of lacking depth.

**Score is NOT capped.** However, the near-miss on Flags #4 and #5, combined with the topical rather than expository nature of the sermon, severely limits the score's ceiling.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 8 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity* — 3/10: The sermon uses Ephesians 5:4 as a prooftext for a theme established before the verse appears. The verse is cited but not expounded. Its context (the ethical argument of Ephesians 5:3–14, the walk of light, the contrast with Gentile darkness) is entirely absent. Paul's imperative is quoted but not explained in its own terms — why does Paul connect thanksgiving to holy speech? What does it mean to put thanksgiving "in its place" as a replacement for specific sins? These questions, which the text raises, are left untouched.
- *Contextual Awareness* — 1/5: No engagement with the surrounding text, the letter's argument, or the redemptive-historical context of Ephesians. The verse is floating.
- *Exegetical Clarity* — 2/5: The preacher correctly notes Paul's implicit logic — that we act with either thankful or thankless hearts, and speech flows from that. This is a legitimate expository insight. But it arrives at the end of a topical development, not as the fruit of exegetical work.
- *Logical Faithfulness* — 2/5: No eisegesis in the strict sense, but the sermon imports a gratitude theme from creation and atonement, then uses the verse to affirm it, rather than deriving the theme from the verse. This is proof-texting in the benign sense — using Scripture to confirm a conclusion rather than to generate it.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 12 / 18**

- *Christ-Centeredness* — 5/7: "He died for us." "Every nail hammered into the hands of Christ." "Substitutionary atonement for our sins." This is genuine, organic, and appropriately central for a Thanksgiving address. The Christ-reference is not decorative — it is the pivot of the sermon's argument: we should be grateful because God became human and died rather than destroy us. This is the sermon's strongest theological moment.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness* — 2/3: The sermon moves from creation (days 1–7) through the Fall (Adam and Eve, the cookie-jar metaphor) to Christ's death. This is a compressed redemptive-historical arc. It is not developed, but it is present and correctly ordered.
- *Doctrinal Precision* — 3/4: "Substitutionary atonement" is correctly named. The creation narrative is handled without error (minor imprecision on "seven days" noted in source, not a theological problem). The statement "He would have been within his rights to destroy us; instead he died for us" is a theologically compact and accurate statement of grace.
- *Confessional Alignment* — 2/4: The sermon does not contradict 1689 confessional theology. The substitutionary atonement reference is genuinely confessional. However, justification by faith is absent, the ordo salutis is not engaged, and the soteriological precision that would fully satisfy the Confessional Alignment criterion is not present. The atonement is named; its application to the believing sinner is not.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 8 / 15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel* — 2/5: The gospel is present but compressed. Substitutionary atonement is named. Justification by faith is absent. The gospel is stated as a fact (he died for us) but not as a proclamation (here is how you lay hold of it). An unbeliever in the congregation hears that Christ died for us but does not hear how that death becomes theirs by faith.
- *Necessity of Christ* — 4/5: Christ is essential to this sermon's argument. The move from "he had the right to destroy us" to "instead he died for us" is the heart of the sermon. Remove Christ, and the sermon collapses. This is genuine, not decorative.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity* — 2/5: The cross is mentioned but not dwelt upon. "Every nail" and "every lash" has emotional register but is brief — a sentence rather than a sustained proclamation. The sermon moves past the cross quickly to reach heaven and Ephesians 5:4.

*Synagogue Test*: A devout non-Christian would be offended by the necessity of Christ's blood ("instead he died for us," "substitutionary atonement"). The cross is a scandal here, not a decoration. The gospel test is passed, even if the gospel clarity is not full.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 3 / 15**

- *Heart Penetration* — 1/5: The cookie-jar metaphor is warm and relatable, but it domesticates the Fall rather than pressing it into the conscience. The sermon tells the congregation they should be grateful; it does not corner them in their ingratitude or press on specific forms of thanklessness. No moment in the manuscript exposes the heart.
- *Specificity* — 1/5: The application is entirely general. "Be thankful for your existence. Be thankful for God's patience. Be thankful for Christ's death. Be thankful for creation and heaven." No concrete, congregation-specific application. No one is addressed differently from anyone else.

1. **The exposition is structurally absent.** The primary text, Ephesians 5:4, receives one paragraph near the end of a pre-built topical address. Paul's argument — the context of 5:3–4, the ethical logic of putting thanksgiving in the place of sinful speech, the walk of light in 5:8–14 — is entirely unexplored. 2. **The conscience is never pressed.** No hearer is cornered. The sermon does not ask: "In what specific way are you being unthankful right now, and what does God say about it?" The sermon is a warm reminder, not a pastoral search.

3. **The sermon does not have an ending.** The incomplete sentence ("Let's spend some time thanking God for —") confirms that the written portion ends without a conclusion. A sermon without a landing is a sermon that never arrived. The transition to extemporaneous prayer means the written manuscript never achieves a sermonic close. Whatever force the sermon built is dissipated rather than directed.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not perceive how much theological and homiletical weight this sermon's brevity is carrying without the structural support to bear it. The move from creation to Fall to cross to eschatology to Paul's letter is the skeleton of an excellent sermon — but compressed to four paragraphs, none of these moments have room to develop. The preacher may believe the congregation's existing knowledge fills in the gaps. That assumption is dangerous in a Thanksgiving service, which likely includes guests, nominal Christians, and the spiritually indifferent whose knowledge does not fill those gaps.

Additionally, the historical inaccuracies (39 lashes, cat of nine tails) are flagged in the source notes but not resolved in the manuscript. These details, preached with emotional weight ("every lash"), will be repeated by congregation members as fact. The sermon's apologetic credibility depends on their accuracy.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The warmth and accessibility of the sermon create an illusion of pastoral effectiveness. The cookie-jar analogy produces smiles; the mother's joke produces recognition; the heaven passage produces comfort. The congregation likely feels well-served. But warmth is not the same as depth, and recognition is not the same as conviction. The sermon is easy to sit through — which is not the same as hard to forget. The approachability of the illustrations masks the fact that the sermon never requires anything of the congregation, never corners them, and never sends them anywhere except to a prayer circle.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Ephesians 5:4 is embedded in one of Paul's most direct ethical passages. The surrounding verses (5:3–7) connect covetousness, sexual immorality, and crude speech to idolatry — paralleling Romans 1 almost directly. The insight that thanksgiving is the antidote not just to ingratitude but to idolatry (a thankless heart that grabs what God has not given) is latent in the text. A sermon that opened this connection — "Paul is not just telling you to be polite; he is telling you that thanksgiving is the weapon that breaks the power of greed and lust and contemptuous speech" — would have given the congregation a tool for holiness, not just a reason for gratitude. That move was available in the text and was not taken.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the exposition from Ephesians 5:4 outward.** The current sermon uses the verse as a landing pad. Reverse the architecture: start with the verse, explain Paul's logic (why does thanksgiving replace filthiness and crude joking — what is the connection?), then use creation, atonement, and eschatology as the grounds for the thanksgiving Paul commands. The topical material is good; it needs to be in service of the text rather than decorated with it.

2. **Develop the atonement section.** "Every nail... every lash... substitutionary atonement" is one sentence. This is the theological heart of the sermon and deserves a paragraph — what substitutionary atonement means (Christ taking the punishment we deserved), why that is grounds for thanksgiving (the wrath that would have destroyed us fell on him instead), and how the believer lays hold of it (by faith). This single expansion would substantially lift the Gospel Centrality score and provide the comfort the sermon lacks.

3. **Add one moment of direct conscience address.** After the Piper quote or before the prayer, add a direct address to the congregation's specific ingratitude: "Some of you walked in here today frustrated about something God has not given you, and you haven't stopped to list what he has given you. Some of you said something cutting this week — a crude joke at someone's expense — and you didn't think of it as a thanklessness problem. But Paul does. A thankful heart doesn't produce that speech." This is one paragraph — specific, differentiated, conscience-pressing.

4. **Resolve the historical inaccuracies before reuse.** Replace "39 lashes from the cat of nine tails" with "every stroke of the Roman whip" or similar. The emotional force is identical; the accuracy is restored. This is a one-sentence fix with zero cost to the sermon's impact.

5. **Write an ending.** The sermon currently closes with an incomplete sentence. Before the prayer prompt, write a single closing sentence that lands the main idea: "So this Thanksgiving, your gratitude is not just a feeling — it is a testimony that God is God, that Christ is enough, and that we are a people who have been given more than we could ever deserve." The prayer then becomes the corporate expression of a completed sermon, not its substitute.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Ephesians 5:4 is a single-verse imperative embedded in a larger ethical passage — not an expository sermon unit on its own. The rubric's exposition categories are designed for a passage, not a verse used as a theme text. The rubric does not bend here so much as it reveals: when a sermon takes a verse as a theme rather than a text, the exposition scores reflect that accurately. The rubric is not straining against the genre — it is correctly identifying that a topical devotional with a text is a different homiletical form than an expository sermon, and that this sermon is operating as the former while being evaluated as the latter. That gap is the sermon's structural issue, not the rubric's.
