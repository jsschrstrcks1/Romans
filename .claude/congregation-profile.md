# Congregation Profile

Know who you're preaching to. Every sermon must land on real people, not theoretical Christians.

## Spiritual Spectrum in the Room

- **Seeking skeptics** — still figuring out what they believe; need intellectual honesty and a glimpse of beauty, not clichés
- **New believers** — need to know this is normal Christianity, not the advanced class; give them hope that growth is real
- **Long-time Christians** — may be coasting; need a wake-up call without condescension; familiarity is not faithfulness

## Real Burdens Being Carried

- **Struggling marriages** — love feels impossible; some are showing up to a war zone every day
- **Broken family relationships** — estranged from parents, children, siblings; carrying years of unresolved pain
- **Addiction** — current and former; some are white-knuckling it through every weekend; some relapsed last month
- **Health crises** — diagnoses stealing their future one day at a time; bodies breaking down; suffering that isn't about sin

## Pastoral Implications

- Don't preach to the person who has it together. They're not in the room.
- The sermon must have weight *and* hope. Heavy without hopeful is crushing. Hopeful without heavy is cheap.
- Acknowledge real suffering without turning the pulpit into a therapy session.
- Give the addict who keeps falling a reason to get back up — not shame, but Christ.
- Speak to the broken marriage without pretending love is easy.
- Remember that some suffering isn't moral failure — it's living in a fallen world.

## Political / Cultural Context

- A politically confident room
- A conservative congregation
- People who are certain they see clearly

This means:
- They need the text to challenge them, not just confirm them
- Guard against letting political agreement substitute for spiritual obedience
- Preach the Word, not the mood of the room
