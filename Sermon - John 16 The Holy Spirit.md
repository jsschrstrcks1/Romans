# John 16 — The Holy Spirit

## The Context

Jesus knew and had been dropping hints all along that he was going to leave them — he was going to die.

But this is the moment that it suddenly comes crashing home for them. They are startled, and shocked, saddened.

Remember when Pastor Bob stepped down at Jasmine? I don't think there was a dry eye in there. Everyone loves Bob, enjoys and grows from his teachings. He has been there for us — just speaking for my immediate family on bunches of occasions. At the hospital, at home when mediators were needed, and for a bunch of other things. He is our friend, we love him, and the thought of losing him at that moment in Jasmine brought everyone as far as I know to tears. That moment of realization. Like in the cartoons when the coyote or others would have an idea a light bulb would turn on. For the disciples this is that moment. All the weird things he had been saying about leaving them came together in a flash, and this brought them to a very emotional point — vs 6 "sorrow has filled your heart."

Not only that, but in the first part of the chapter Jesus warns them — vs 2 — that they are going to be put out of the synagogues, that whoever kills them will think he is offering service to God.

> They will put you out of the synagogues. Indeed, the hour is coming when whoever kills you will think he is offering service to God.
> — John 16:2

Jesus the ever patient teacher used this opportunity to talk about the Holy Spirit.

Picture this with me, the 12 disciples, 12 men. Sitting around perhaps at a meal weeping because they knew Christ was going to be leaving. Though I doubt they had a flicker of an idea as to how.

In this moment of emotion, Christ starts to teach them about the Holy Spirit. Which I think is the main thing we are going to talk about tonight. It won't be comprehensive, but we can get our feet wet.

## The Characteristics

In this passage Jesus refers to the Holy Spirit as one who would come later and take his place, one who would be a Helper, one who would convict the world concerning sin, concerning righteousness, and concerning judgment. The KJV calls the Spirit "the Comforter" — same Greek word, *parakletos*. Helper, Comforter, Advocate — it's all the same title. The ESV translates it "Helper."

> Nevertheless, I tell you the truth: it is to your advantage that I go away, for if I do not go away, the Helper will not come to you. But if I go, I will send him to you.
> — John 16:7

> And when he comes, he will convict the world concerning sin and righteousness and judgment: concerning sin, because they do not believe in me; concerning righteousness, because I go to the Father, and you will see me no longer; concerning judgment, because the ruler of this world is judged.
> — John 16:8–11

Lets start with "come later." This would have been re-assuring. Jesus wasn't just abandoning them. There was a plan in place that was bigger than they, that come what may, even when Christ left, that they were not abandoned. This goes hand in hand with "one who would take his place."

> When the Spirit of truth comes, he will guide you into all the truth, for he will not speak on his own authority, but whatever he hears he will speak, and he will declare to you the things that are to come. He will glorify me, for he will take what is mine and declare it to you.
> — John 16:13–14

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Pastoral warmth and accessible voice.** The opening illustration is the sermon's strongest moment. The preacher knows his congregation, names a shared grief, and uses it to build a bridge to the disciples' emotional experience. This is genuine pastoral instinct. The congregation will lean in.

2. **Textual hospitality.** The passage is quoted generously and the Greek title *parakletos* is introduced accessibly — not as a show of learning, but as a genuine attempt to help the congregation hear the word's range. This is exposition serving the people, not the preacher.

3. **Structural intentionality.** The three-part framework (Context / Characteristics / Command) shows a preacher who thinks in outline and has a sense of where he is going. If "The Command" section is developed with specificity and exhortation, the structure will serve the sermon well.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is entirely absent.** The Spirit's coming is not connected to Christ's atoning death. John 16:7 says "it is to your advantage that I go away" — but why? Because the cross must happen. Because Christ must bear the sin of the world, be raised, and ascend before the Spirit is poured out on all flesh. This is not a background detail — it is the theological engine of the entire passage. The sermon currently treats Jesus' departure as a pastoral transition rather than as the culmination of the redemptive mission.

2. **The Spirit's convicting work (vv. 8–11) is quoted but never opened.** This is the heart of the passage's argument. What does it mean that the Spirit convicts the world of sin "because they do not believe in me"? Of righteousness "because I go to the Father"? Of judgment "because the ruler of this world is judged"? These three convictions are the text's most urgent teaching — and they are named, then abandoned. The sermon moves on without doing the exegetical work that would make these truths land.

3. **No hearer is addressed.** The congregation is a passive audience throughout. No one is pressed. The unbeliever is not confronted. The grieving believer is not comforted from the text. The discouraged Christian who feels abandoned by God is not shown the Spirit's present work. The sermon speaks *about* hearers rather than *to* them.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the Pastor Bob illustration, while emotionally effective, may be subtly working *against* the text. Pastor Bob stepped down and left — but Jesus did not merely "step down." He died, was raised, and ascended, and his departure was not a loss but a victory. The Spirit came not despite Jesus leaving but *because of* what Jesus accomplished by leaving. If the illustration establishes the emotional frame as "grief over a beloved leader's absence," it must be explicitly corrected — otherwise the congregation will receive the Spirit's coming as a consolation prize rather than as the triumphant gift of the exalted Christ.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The warmth and naturalness of the preacher's voice creates the impression of pastoral effectiveness. The sermon *feels* relational and accessible. But accessibility without theological substance is not pastoral — it is pleasant. The congregation will feel cared for without being confronted, comforted without being grounded, and informed without being changed. The warmth is real. It is not a substitute for the Word's sharp edge.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The three-part conviction of John 16:8–11 is one of the most theologically precise descriptions of the Spirit's evangelistic work in all of Scripture. "Concerning sin, because they do not believe in me" — this is a singular, clarifying statement: the fundamental sin is unbelief in Christ, not merely moral failure. "Concerning righteousness, because I go to the Father" — the Spirit convicts of righteousness by pointing to the vindicated, ascended Christ as the only source of standing before God. "Concerning judgment, because the ruler of this world is judged" — the cross was not a defeat but the decisive judgment of Satan. Each phrase is a sermon within a sermon. The preacher quotes them and passes by.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5 Specific Actions)

1. **Complete "The Command" section with a specific, text-driven exhortation.** The structure is good. The sermon needs a third movement that tells the congregation what they must now do: trust the Spirit's convicting work, yield to his guiding work, and rest in the assurance that Christ's departure was not abandonment but provision. Give them one clear command arising from the text.

2. **Connect Jesus' departure to the cross explicitly.** After the Pastor Bob illustration, add a pivot: "But here is where the analogy breaks down — Jesus didn't step down. He went to a cross. He went to carry the sin of the world. And the Spirit didn't come to fill a vacancy. The Spirit came as the promised gift of a risen, ascended, victorious King." This turns the illustration from a weakness into a strength.

3. **Open John 16:8–11 with pastoral specificity.** Take each of the three convictions and apply them to a specific hearer. "If you are in this room tonight and you feel like a basically good person who doesn't need Christ — the Spirit is at work in this text calling that a lie. The sin that condemns is not merely stealing or lying. It is unbelief in Jesus Christ." Press the text into concrete conscience.

4. **Add direct address at multiple points.** The sermon currently speaks in the third person about the disciples. Shift to second person for the application: "Some of you tonight feel exactly like those disciples — you feel abandoned, you feel like God has gone quiet, you feel like the Jesus of Sunday morning is very far away on Tuesday night. John 16 was written for you."

5. **Name the gospel.** Before the conclusion, state plainly: Jesus went to the cross to bear the punishment for sin, rose from the dead in vindication, and ascended so that the Spirit could be poured out on all who believe. The Spirit's coming is the fruit of the cross. If a hearer wants the Spirit's help, they must first receive the Christ whose death purchased that help.
