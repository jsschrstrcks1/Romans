# Thanksgiving Communion Service

**Occasion**: Thanksgiving Service
**Scripture**: 1 Corinthians 11:23–26; Matthew 26:26–29; Mark 14:22–25; Luke 22:17–20; 1 Corinthians 10:16–17; John 6:26–27, 35, 47–58

---

### Source Notes

- **Primary source**: `Other-Sermons/Sermons/Thanksgiving Service.doc` (no `.txt` version in archive)
- **All ESV quotations verified** — all eight passages check out against the ESV text. No corrections needed.
- **John 6:26–27, 35**: The ellipsis skips vv. 28–34. The omission is intentional and transparent in the source ("…."). Noted here for completeness.
- **BFM Article VII — Baptism and the Lord's Supper** (not quoted in service; supplied for reference):
  > "Christian baptism is the immersion of a believer in water in the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. It is an act of obedience symbolizing the believer's faith in a crucified, buried, and risen Saviour, the believer's death to sin, the burial of the old life, and the resurrection to walk in newness of life in Christ Jesus... The Lord's Supper is a symbolic act of obedience whereby members of the church, through partaking of the bread and the fruit of the vine, memorialize the death of the Redeemer and anticipate His second coming."
  > — Baptist Faith and Message 2000, Article VII (excerpt)
- **Format note**: This is a special Thanksgiving service, not a regular sermon. It is structured around congregational sharing, prayer, and scripture reading rather than exposition. The passages are given without commentary as a reading for the communion service.

### Congregational Thanksgiving

I want to take a few minutes and go around the room. I'd like everyone to give something they are thankful for. I'll start.

Thankful for family. For this church — for you people. Thankful that my sin debt has been paid on the cross. Thankful we live in a country where we can gather like this without fear of being carted off to jail. Thankful we can own a Bible without the same concern. Thankful for my goofball dog that keeps me on my toes.

*[Congregational sharing — going around the room.]*

All great things to be giving thanks for! Who would like to open us with prayer and give thanks to the Lord for all the things that were said here tonight, and whatever else God puts on your heart?

*[Prayer.]*

---

### A Brief Word from Scripture

I'm going to try to keep it short tonight. I want tonight to be about fellowship and thanksgiving. But I still want to give you a little bit from Scripture. Since we will also be taking communion tonight, it seemed like a good time to talk a bit more in depth about that. We'll dive back into Romans next week. But it seems appropriate on Thanksgiving to look at the thing that gives us the best reason to be thankful.

Go ahead and open your Bibles.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title

**"Scripture Without Exposition: A Reverent Gathering That Is Not a Sermon"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A Thanksgiving communion service structured around congregational sharing and Scripture reading that gathers the saints and honors the Lord's Table, but contains no expository proclamation and therefore cannot be evaluated as a sermon by this rubric's standards.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**The Berean Gate does not apply in the standard sense — and that is itself the diagnostic finding.**

There is no sermon here to pass or fail the gate. The service does not advance a thesis derived from a text. No single passage governs a developed argument. The preacher explicitly states his intent: "I'm going to try to keep it short tonight. I want tonight to be about fellowship and thanksgiving." What follows is a curated Scripture reading for a communion service — liturgically appropriate, pastorally warm, scripturally rich — but not preaching.

The Scopus Test cannot be applied: there is no thesis sentence to evaluate. The Authority Test cannot be applied: the preacher makes no argument to stand under or above. He simply reads the Word and administers the ordinance.

**Berean Gate: NOT APPLICABLE. This is a liturgical service, not a sermon.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Fatal Flaw evaluation suspended.** The rubric's fatal flaw system presupposes a sermon — an act of proclamation with a thesis, argument, application, and exhortation. This service contains none of those structural elements. Flagging it for the absence of exhortation or gospel presentation would be like flagging a hymn sing for lacking an outline.

That said, three observations are offered for pastoral awareness:
- The John 6 passage (vv. 53–58) is read without any orienting comment. For a congregation that may include new or confused believers, this is the most eucharistically contested text in the New Testament, and a brief interpretive word distinguishing spiritual feeding from sacramental consumption would serve well.
- The Lord's Supper is administered without any self-examination exhortation (cf. 1 Cor. 11:27–29 is conspicuously absent from the readings).
- No invitation or closing word is recorded.

**No score cap triggered. No fatal flaws formally flagged.**

### 8. Core Override Questions

These are answered in the spirit of the liturgical service, not as a sermon evaluation:

1. **Does this service make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Yes, of Christ. The Scripture selections are Christ-centered: his body given, his blood poured, himself as living bread. The brief pastoral word ("the thing that gives us the best reason to be thankful") points toward Christ explicitly.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** The ordinance and the Word together create genuine encounter conditions. Whether encounter occurred depends on what the Spirit did — not what the manuscript shows.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** The service is structured for comfort and remembrance, not conviction. That is appropriate for a Thanksgiving communion service. Conviction is not the purpose of the Lord's Table; memorial and proclamation ("you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes") are.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

**1. Scriptural richness and canonical breadth.** The selection spans four institution accounts (Matthew, Mark, Luke, 1 Corinthians), a participation text (1 Corinthians 10), and John's Bread of Life discourse. This is not random — it builds from the historical institution to the theological meaning to the eternal significance. The progression is liturgically coherent and pastorally rich.

**2. Pastoral accessibility and warmth.** The format — congregational sharing, communal prayer, brief word, Scripture, ordinance — models the kind of church gathering that nurtures belonging. The preacher's personal thanksgiving ("my goofball dog") humanizes leadership without trivializing the occasion. The transition ("the thing that gives us the best reason to be thankful") is well-placed.

**3. Textual fidelity and transparency.** The source note acknowledges the ellipsis in John 6:26–35 explicitly ("The omission is intentional and transparent"). ESV quotations are verified. The BFM Article VII is supplied for doctrinal reference even though not read aloud. This is a model of textual integrity.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

**1. The absence of 1 Corinthians 11:27–29 is a significant pastoral omission.** The service reads 11:23–26 but stops before the warning: "Whoever, therefore, eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord. Let a person examine himself, then, and so eat of the bread and drink of the cup." In a Baptist congregation administering the ordinance to a mixed gathering on Thanksgiving, this omission removes the self-examination text that Paul deliberately attached to the institution narrative. It is not a fatal flaw in a rubric sense, but it is a pastoral gap.

**2. John 6:53–58 is read without interpretive orientation.** "Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you" is among the hardest texts in the Gospels. Read in a Baptist communion service without comment, it risks either confusing new believers (is this transubstantiation?) or passing by unnoticed. A single clarifying sentence — even one line distinguishing spiritual reception from physical consumption — would serve the congregation.

**3. No closing word, benediction, or commissioning is recorded.** The service ends with "*[Communion administered.]*" — which may reflect a transcription limitation rather than an actual gap. But as the document stands, there is no recorded closing that ties the Thanksgiving theme, the ordinance, and the congregation's response into a unified end. A service that opens with shared thanksgiving deserves a close that seals it.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how much theological freight John 6 carries in a Baptist context. Read without comment, vv. 53–58 can land very differently on a spectrum of theological backgrounds — from the Zwinglian memorialism that most Baptist pews assume, to the more robustly spiritual-presence language that the text itself supports. The preacher's silence is probably not negligence — it is probably trust in the Spirit and in the congregation's familiarity. But for a mixed or growing congregation, the silence creates interpretive risk.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The selection of John 6 *feels* theologically deep — and the texts themselves are deep. But without a single orienting word, the depth becomes decorative rather than instructive. The illusion: "We read the hardest communion texts in the New Testament, therefore we did the theological work." The reality: the congregation was given the texts to read, not the tools to receive them.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The Thanksgiving frame is genuinely beautiful and underused. The preacher notes that communion is "the thing that gives us the best reason to be thankful" — which is an excellent thesis. But it is stated and immediately abandoned. A brief two-paragraph meditation on eucharistia (the very word means thanksgiving — it is the root of "Eucharist"), connecting the Thanksgiving occasion to the ordinance's own name, would have given this service a theological unity that it currently lacks. The word "thanksgiving" and the Lord's Supper practically beg to be fused — and the preacher gestures at the fusion without completing it.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Add 1 Corinthians 11:27–29 to the reading.** Read it soberly, without comment, before administering the ordinance. Let Paul's own self-examination call do the work. This requires zero additional preparation and fills a genuine pastoral gap.

2. **Add one orienting sentence before John 6:53–58.** Something like: "Jesus is speaking here of the spiritual reality that communion signifies — feeding on him by faith." This discharges the interpretive responsibility without adding length.

3. **Develop the eucharistia bridge.** Two sentences connecting the word "thanksgiving" to the Greek word behind the ordinance's name would give this service a thematic spine. "The word 'Eucharist' — the name Christians have given this meal throughout history — simply means 'thanksgiving.' Tonight we do what the church has always done: we give thanks by remembering."

4. **Add a closing benediction or commissioning word.** Even a brief sentence — or a spoken benediction from Numbers 6 or 2 Corinthians 13 — would close the service as a unified act of worship rather than leaving it end mid-action.

5. **If this service is repeated annually, consider a brief teaching insert on the Baptist understanding of the Lord's Supper** — memorial and proclamation, not sacramental transformation — that can be read as part of the service. It need not be long. Even one paragraph from the BFM (which is already cited in the source notes) read aloud would serve the congregation's doctrinal formation.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** Dead orthodoxy requires a sermon that is correct but bloodless. This is not a sermon at all. The warmth of the congregational sharing, the simplicity of the format, and the centrality of the ordinance suggest pastoral warmth, not cold orthodoxy. No flag.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The service carries genuine weight in its simplicity — the gathered saints sharing what they are thankful for, then sitting together at the Lord's Table with the words of institution read over them, is a picture of the church doing exactly what the church was built to do; the Weight of Glory here is not in the preacher's words but in the ordinance itself.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

**This passage and service are not well-served by this framework, and the rubric bends significantly here.** The Thus Says the Lord rubric is purpose-built for expository sermon manuscripts. A Thanksgiving communion service is a different genre entirely — it is liturgical, corporate, and ordinance-centered. The rubric's categories (exposition, applicatory force, exhortation, sermonic force) have no natural home in a service structured around reading and remembrance.

The evaluation above does not score the service because scoring it would be a category error. What is offered instead is a pastoral diagnostic: observations about what the service does and does not accomplish on its own terms, not on the sermon's terms.

**Recommendation**: When archiving this service, tag it as a liturgical service rather than a sermon. The rubric should not fire on future services of this type. If a brief homily is developed for future Thanksgiving communion services, that homily should be evaluated as a sermon — and the evaluator should note that even a five-minute meditation at the Lord's Table carries the full weight of proclamation responsibility.
