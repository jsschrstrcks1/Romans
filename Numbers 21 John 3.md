NUMBERS 21:5-9 / 1 CORINTHIANS 10:9 / JOHN 3:13-21 — Look and Live
A typological sermon: the bronze serpent in the wilderness as a type of Christ lifted up.

---

I had a hard time this week preparing for tonight. I don't know if I was just being hard-headed, or wasn't listening, or God was allowing me to feel a little bit of stress before guiding me to tonight's passage. I normally know where I feel like God is leading me Sunday morning, and start preparing Sunday, and by Friday night I'm generally pretty happy with it — and then spend Saturday committing parts of it to memory. Not word-for-word memorization, you understand, but the main points. Anyway, despite spending hours in front of the computer this week, despite a half-dozen false starts, by Friday night I didn't have a single word written down on paper. It was a bit stressful, but I'm happy with where I feel like the Lord led me.

If you've brought your Bibles, and I hope you have, please open with me to Numbers 21:5. We're going to spend some time in three passages tonight, so be prepared to jump around a little bit.

While you're turning to Numbers 21, let me catch you up on where we are in Israel's history. This is after Joseph saved his people in Egypt. After they grew quite numerous and became a threat to Pharaoh. After they were subjugated as slaves. Then of course you remember Moses — raised in the palace, commits murder, runs to the desert, marries, has kids, and is led of God to go back and confront Pharaoh. Through the course of Moses arguing for Israel's freedom, God sends ten plagues — you remember the bugs, the boils, the blood, and ultimately the death of every firstborn child of every parent who didn't submit to God's edict and obey his instructions. This finally convinced Pharaoh to let them go. He chased them to the sea, and God parted the sea, and the Hebrew people escaped across it.

I was reading something recently about a place where thousands of chariots, people, and horse bones were supposedly found. I love it when science proves Scripture. Science doesn't have to prove Scripture for me to believe Scripture, but I do believe science done right will ultimately prove Scripture. [Note: The claim of chariot wheels, human bones, and horse bones found in the Red Sea or Gulf of Aqaba (associated with Nuweiba Beach) originates primarily with Ron Wyatt, an amateur archaeologist who made these claims beginning in the late 1970s. Wyatt was not a trained archaeologist. These claims have not been published in any peer-reviewed archaeology or Egyptology journal, and the mainstream scholarly consensus uniformly rejects them. Underwater photographs cited as evidence have been identified by marine biologists and geologists as natural coral formations, not artifacts. No peer-reviewed institution has confirmed the discovery. The pastor's sentiment — that rightly done science ultimately supports Scripture — is theologically sound; this particular claim, however, does not meet that standard and should not be cited as archaeological confirmation of the Exodus crossing.]

Anyway — where we pick up tonight, Israel has been wandering in the desert for a while. They are grumbling, malcontent, and Scripture says they were putting the name of Christ to the test. [Note: 1 Corinthians 10:9 — Paul makes this identification explicit; see below.]

Read with me what happens in Numbers 21:5-9.

    And the people spoke against God and against Moses, "Why have you brought us up out of Egypt to die in the wilderness? For there is no food and no water, and we loathe this worthless food." Then the LORD sent fiery serpents among the people, and they bit the people, so that many people of Israel died. And the people came to Moses and said, "We have sinned, for we have spoken against the LORD and against you. Pray to the LORD, that he take away the serpents from us." So Moses prayed for the people. And the LORD said to Moses, "Make a fiery serpent and set it on a pole, and everyone who is bitten, when he sees it, shall live." So Moses made a bronze serpent and set it on a pole. And if a serpent bit anyone, he would look at the bronze serpent and live.
    — Numbers 21:5-9, ESV

[Note: The ESV renders the food "worthless food" (v. 5). The NIV renders it "miserable food." The Hebrew is לֶּחֶם הַקְּלֹקֵל (leḥem haqqeloqel), something contemptible or worthless. Both translations capture the sense of the people's contempt for the manna God had provided.]

So right off the bat — for those that aren't familiar, what did they mean by "worthless food"? That's right: manna. They had been drinking from a rock and eating manna that God had made specially for them. It was a miracle that each day they had something to eat. A miracle each day that God didn't just wipe them out and start over. I think if I had to deal with as much griping and complaining, I might have done that. But thank God that I am not God, amen?

They kind of remind me of kids. You know how kids can be — maybe a little too much sugar, maybe they get a little overtired. They start to be rank, as my mom calls it. They throw fits, they cry. I happen to know of a girl who would smash her head into the floor when she didn't get her way. Her parents were mortified and would cave. Until the day her face met terrazzo. My understanding is that was the last time she ever tried to get her way by smashing her head into things.

The Israelites were at that point. They were griping and complaining. They were basically spitting on a gift God had given them. Imagine: every day you wake up hungry, and every day the ground is covered with food that nourishes you. Food that keeps you alive.

I read one commentator who said metaphorically the bread was the body of Christ, and the stone that produced water was the blood. [Note: This is not merely a commentator's interpretation — it is Paul's own identification in 1 Corinthians 10:3-4 (ESV): "and all ate the same spiritual food, and all drank the same spiritual drink. For they drank from the spiritual Rock that followed them, and the Rock was Christ." The pastor presents this as a commentator's insight, but the observation originates in Scripture itself.] And this was why it was so offensive when Moses disobeyed God and struck the rock rather than speaking to it as God commanded. [Note: Numbers 20:11-12. God commanded Moses to speak to the rock; instead Moses struck it twice. God responded that Moses would not lead Israel into the promised land because of this act.] Likewise the Israelite people rejected a free gift from God — and if that reading is correct, rejected something symbolic of God's Son himself.

Anyway — like the girl with the terrazzo floor, God put something in the path of the nation that forced them to stop.

That's when the snakes came.

I don't know about you, but I don't like snakes very much. This past summer when Hermine came through and I was evacuating my house, I came running out not watching where I was going and stepped on a four-foot snake — longer than my stairs. I don't know who was most startled. We both took off in different directions. I haven't seen it around since, which I am happy about.

Unlike my snake encounter, the Israelites encountered venomous snakes. These snakes bit everyone. Some translations use the word "fiery," which is a more literal translation but perhaps less immediately helpful. Scholars believe "fiery" was a description of the sensation experienced by those bitten — one description I found compared it to the feeling of fire pouring into an arm and moving through the body. So both words are apt. And as terrifying as snakes that were literally on fire would be, I'm glad they were ordinary venomous snakes.

We don't know the exact number of people, but probably most of them were bitten — certainly enough that they realized they had been foolish. They went to Moses, Moses prayed, and God told them to erect a bronze serpent on a pole. All who looked upon it would be healed. Which is a remarkable account if that is all we are told. But there are two other passages that inform this story that we need to look at.

Let me read 1 Corinthians 10:9 — but first a little context. Paul is writing about various things the Israelites had been brought through, and he says that these things took place as examples for us, that we might not desire evil as they did. [Note: 1 Corinthians 10:6-9 (ESV): "Now these things took place as examples for us, that we might not desire evil as they did. Do not be idolaters as some of them were; as it is written, 'The people sat down to eat and drink and rose up to play.' We must not indulge in sexual immorality as some of them did, and twenty-three thousand fell in a single day. We must not put Christ to the test, as some of them did and were destroyed by serpents." The passage header for tonight lists 1 Corinthians 10:5-9; the pastor reads verse 9 specifically.]

Ready? Verse 9:

    We must not put Christ to the test, as some of them did and were destroyed by serpents.
    — 1 Corinthians 10:9, ESV

That should catch your attention. We're given a detail left out of the Numbers passage. All the Israelites had to do was look up at the snake and they would be healed — instantly, fully, totally, perfectly. But yet some obviously didn't.

I read a letter this week by a man named Newton. He had been a slave trader, but gave his heart to the Lord and became a Christian — and later became an ally of William Wilberforce, the man who was instrumental in bringing slavery to an end in the British Empire. [Note: John Newton (1725-1807) — slave trader, converted to evangelical Christianity, became an Anglican clergyman, and in his later years became an outspoken abolitionist. He wrote "Thoughts Upon the African Slave Trade" (1788), providing moral and historical authority to Wilberforce's campaign. William Wilberforce (1759-1833) led the parliamentary effort that resulted in the Slave Trade Act of 1807 and the Slavery Abolition Act 1833.] Newton eventually became a pastor and wrote a number of hymns, some of which we still sing today. Perhaps you've heard of that obscure gem, "Amazing Grace." [Note: Newton wrote "Amazing Grace" around 1772; it was published in the Olney Hymns collection in 1779.]

Anyway, as part of his pastoral ministry Newton mentored a few other pastors. One of them — quite successful in his own right, who week in and week out saw lives being changed — wrote to Newton and said he knew that God could forgive sins; he'd been seeing lives transformed. But he didn't know if his own sins could be completely blotted out. Newton responded by preaching the gospel to him.

He said this:

    When the brazen serpent was erected in the wilderness, to cure those who must otherwise have died, the benefit was not restrained to those who had been bitten by the fiery serpent but once or a few times. The worst case amongst the people was relieved as soon and as certainly as the very slightest. The remedy was universally proposed to every person. The application was easy; it was only, look and live. But if a man had spent all his time in measuring or counting his wounds, instead of looking to the ordinance of God, he might have died, though the means of life were within his view. The sense of the evil of sin is given to quicken application to Christ, and not to discourage our approach. The Scripture has concluded all under sin, and as such we are all condemned already. But the Gospel proclaims a free pardon to everyone who, with the eye of his mind, looks for life to him who hung upon the cross.
    — John Newton [Note: This quote is attributed to John Newton and is stylistically consistent with his pastoral correspondence. The specific letter and collection have not been identified and verified. To be cited formally, the source letter should be located — likely in "The Works of John Newton" (6 vols.) or "Letters of John Newton" (Banner of Truth). The quote is presented here as delivered, with this verification flag noted.]

Think about that. The person bitten once by the serpents was just as easily, fully, and perfectly healed by looking at the bronze serpent as the man bitten three thousand times. Such is the grace of God. There is nothing you can do that is so bad that God cannot forgive you. As Jonah found out, there is no place so far you can run that God cannot find and forgive you. There are no depths of depravity you can sink to that God cannot lift you out of. No matter what you have done, no matter how fast you can run — you cannot outrun the grace of God. Because he is always waiting right there for you to look up and be healed.

Finally, that brings us to John 3. Turn there with me if you've brought your Bibles. OK, reading from the ESV starting in verse 13: [Note: The passage header lists John 3:13-21; the pastor begins reading at verse 14. Verse 13 (ESV): "No one has ascended into heaven except he who descended from heaven, the Son of Man." The reading picks up at the hinge verse.]

    And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in him may have eternal life.

    "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life. For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but in order that the world might be saved through him. Whoever believes in him is not condemned, but whoever does not believe is condemned already, because he has not believed in the name of the only Son of God. And this is the judgment: the light has come into the world, and people loved the darkness rather than the light because their works were evil. For everyone who does wicked things hates the light and does not come to the light, lest his works should be exposed. But whoever does what is true comes to the light, so that it may be clearly seen that his works have been carried out in God."
    — John 3:14-21, ESV

This, I think, is the answer to why some Israelites were lost. Verse 19 says "people loved the darkness rather than the light." I think in that moment their stubborn pride believed it was better to die than to look at the snake and continue to live in the same circumstances. People would rather die than be saved by Christ. People would rather continue to wallow in their own filth and depravity than truly give themselves over to Christ. And as is so often the case, the same is true today.

I firmly believe the people that end up in hell end up there by their own choice — just like the people who refused to look at the snake chose to die. They would be miserable in heaven and closer to happiness in hell. They run from the light. They run from Christ. They run from anything resembling godliness, so in the presence of God very God their selfish pride would be crushed like a bug underfoot.

People — if you are still drawing air into your lungs, there is still time. We have all sinned and as a result we fall short of the glory of God, Romans tells us. [Note: Romans 3:23 (ESV): "for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."] Not only do we fall short — we earn a one-way ticket to hell for all of eternity, and that is the best that man can accomplish on his own. An eternity apart from God. An eternity apart from the One who loved us so much that he sent his Son to die for us.

I heard about a boat sinking off the Skyway Bridge this week. One of the things they do when someone is in distress in the water is toss them a life ring — round, floats, with a rope tied to it. But no matter how good the throw, no matter how well the ring floats, no matter how perfect the rope, no matter how strong the back of the person holding it — if you choose not to grab the life ring, and you're under a bridge like that, you are going to drown.

It's like that for us too. We messed up, our boat is sinking, and God threw us a life ring. For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have everlasting life. As Moses lifted the serpent, so the Son of Man had to be lifted up. All who believe in Christ shall be saved.

It's that simple. Romans 10:9 says: confess with your mouth the Lord Jesus Christ, and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, and you will be saved. [Note: Romans 10:9 (ESV): "because, if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved." The pastor's version is a close paraphrase; the ESV phrasing is "that Jesus is Lord," not "the Lord Jesus Christ."]

As the praise team comes up to sing a final song, I want you to ask yourself this: Have you come to a point in your spiritual life where you have confessed with your mouth and believed in your heart? With your whole heart? Turning your back on the darkness and embracing the light?

If not, talk to me after the service.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

**Date:** 2026-03-21
**Sermon:** Numbers 21:5-9 / 1 Corinthians 10:9 / John 3:13-21 — "Look and Live"
**Evaluator:** Thus Says the Lord Rubric (Reformed Baptist)

---

### 1. Verdict Title

**A Genuine Typological Gospel Sermon with Pastoral Warmth and Exegetical Thinness**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary

A faithful opening of the bronze serpent type that warmly connects Numbers 21 to John 3 and proclaims the sufficiency of Christ's atoning work, while lacking exegetical depth, differentiated application, and structural tightness.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Pass.**

- **Scopus Test**: Pass. The sermon's thesis — "Look to Christ and live, just as Israel looked at the bronze serpent and lived" — arises directly from Jesus' own typological identification in John 3:14-15. The main point of the sermon matches the main point of the passage cluster.
- **Authority Test**: Pass. The preacher submits to the text. No uncomfortable portions are softened or omitted. The condemnation of John 3:19-20 is engaged directly and applied seriously.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**None triggered. Score is uncapped.**

Mild concerns noted (see weaknesses), but no flag-level failure:
- Gospel is present and clear — Christ's substitutionary death is implied through John 3 and the life-ring metaphor, though penal substitution is not explicitly named.
- Application is thin but not entirely generic — the Newton letter and the call to personal decision provide pastoral specificity.
- Exhortation is present, though gentle.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics (25 pts) — 18/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10)*: **7/10** — The typological connection between Numbers 21 and John 3 is legitimate and scripturally grounded (Jesus makes the connection himself in 3:14). Paul's identification of the Israelites' sin as putting Christ to the test (1 Cor 10:9) is correctly noted. However, the exposition stays at the surface of the typological comparison without pressing into the deeper exegetical logic of John 3:14-21 — why "must" the Son of Man be lifted up? What is the nature of belief here versus mere intellectual assent? The darker half of John 3 (condemnation already present, love of darkness) receives a paragraph but not the exegetical weight it deserves.
- *Contextual Awareness (5)*: **3/5** — The Exodus wilderness context is helpfully sketched. However, the Nicodemus dialogue context of John 3 is entirely absent — the sermon treats vv. 14-21 as a standalone passage, losing the "born again" backdrop that gives the text its full force.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5)*: **4/5** — The "fiery" serpent etymology is handled well. The manna/spiritual food connection is noted (though attributed to "a commentator" when it should be attributed to Paul himself in 1 Cor 10:3-4). No original language work.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5)*: **4/5** — No eisegesis detected. The flow from judgment → remedy → refusal to look is logically faithful.
- **FCF (Fallen Condition Focus)**: Present but not named explicitly. The fallen condition is pride and self-destruction — people who prefer to die in their sin rather than look to Christ. This is implied in the analysis of John 3:19 but never named as *the* human problem driving the text.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity (18 pts) — 13/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7)*: **6/7** — The Christological connection is organic and scripturally warranted. The sermon doesn't manufacture a Christ-connection — Jesus makes it himself. However, the nature of what Christ's "being lifted up" accomplishes (penal substitution, propitiation, satisfaction of divine wrath) is never specified. The sermon says what happened but not why it accomplishes salvation.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3)*: **2/3** — The bronze serpent as type is correctly identified. No broader canonical movement (Covenant of Works/Grace, the problem of God's holiness requiring an answer to sin) is developed.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4)*: **2/4** — "Confess with your mouth and believe in your heart" is used as the gospel formula, but the nature of belief is left thin. The sermon implies universal offer ("no matter what you have done") but without clear teaching on what saving faith entails versus mere assent. The Romans 10:9 citation is positive.
- *Confessional Alignment (4)*: **3/4** — No confessional contradictions. The sermon is evangelical and broadly orthodox. The sovereignty of God is not addressed — the choice framing ("you choose hell by refusing to look") is understandable in context but slightly tilts toward decisionism without the counterweight of divine drawing.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force (15 pts) — 11/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (5)*: **4/5** — The gospel is present: sin, judgment, Christ lifted up, believe and live. Justification by faith is not formally articulated (the word "forgiveness" is used, not "righteousness imputed"), but the substance of the gospel offer is clear.
- *Necessity of Christ (5)*: **4/5** — Christ is essential to this sermon. The typology only works if Jesus is the antitype who actually accomplishes what the bronze serpent symbolized.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (5)*: **3/5** — The sermon moves toward the cross but doesn't land there with full weight. The Newton letter is the strongest moment — the universality of the remedy regardless of the number of wounds. But what happened on the cross (substitution, propitiation, satisfaction) is not stated. The "life ring" metaphor softens the atonement into rescue imagery without distinguishing Christ from any generic savior.
- *Synagogue Test*: The sermon would cause offense at the necessity of Christ. The cross is presented as the only remedy, not as one option. Passes.
- *Law/Gospel Dialectic*: Present but thin. The law (God sending the serpents as judgment for sin) functions as the hammer. The gospel (look and live) is the balm. The order is correct. The law section could land harder before relief is given.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force (15 pts) — 8/15**

- *Heart Penetration (5)*: **4/5** — The Newton letter is the pastoral highpoint. The observation that people "would rather die than be saved" (John 3:19) is a genuine confrontation of the heart. The snake encounter anecdote humanizes the preacher effectively.
- *Specificity (5)*: **2/5** — Application is largely generic. The closing call ("have you confessed with your mouth and believed in your heart?") is addressed to a generic hearer. The "people — if you are still drawing air" address is earnest but lacks the surgical specificity of distinguishing the hardened church-goer from the genuine seeker from the backslider.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (5)*: **2/5** — Only one category of hearer is meaningfully addressed: the unconverted. The believer struggling with the sufficiency of Christ's atonement (the Newton letter's original addressee) is gestured at but not developed. The self-righteous, the backslider, the humbled-but-not-yet-believing receive no specific address. Perkins' taxonomy is essentially untouched.

**5. Exhortation & Response (8 pts) — 5/8**

- *Call to Response (4)*: **3/4** — The closing call is genuine: "Have you confessed with your mouth and believed in your heart?" The invitation to talk after the service is a real response mechanism. However, the call is entirely conversion-focused — no summons for the already-believing congregation.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (4)*: **2/4** — The sermon does not equip the church for holiness or witness. It concludes at the point of individual conversion decision with no commissioning dimension.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow (9 pts) — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (4)*: **3/4** — The three-text structure (Numbers → 1 Corinthians → John) is traceable and logical. However, the sermon has no memorable formal outline — it proceeds as a flowing narrative rather than a structured argument.
- *Progression (3)*: **2/3** — The sermon builds toward the gospel invitation as intended. The long introduction (prep story, Red Sea digression) delays the arrival at the text too long.
- *Unity (2)*: **1/2** — The Red Sea chariot-wheel digression (acknowledged as unverified archaeology) is the most significant structural problem. It consumes real estate and introduces uncertainty where confidence is needed.

**7. Illustration & Imagination (5 pts) — 4/5**

- *Relevance (2)*: **2/2** — The hurricane snake story, the life-ring off the Skyway Bridge, and the head-smashing child are all drawn from the congregation's actual world. Native and effective.
- *Clarity (2)*: **2/2** — Each illustration illuminates its point cleanly without competing with it.
- *Memorability (1)*: **0/1** — None of the illustrations is specifically native to this text. The life-ring metaphor is a classic evangelistic illustration that could appear in any gospel sermon. Nothing here is distinctively attached to the bronze serpent image in a way that will be remembered next week.

**8. Sermonic Force (5 pts) — 4/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (2)*: **2/2** — This reads like a man speaking, not an essay. Oral cadence, personal asides, and conversational transitions are natural and unforced.
- *Conviction on the Page (2)*: **1/2** — Conviction is present in the Newton letter citation and the "people, if you are still drawing air" section. However, the manuscript is inconsistent in force — the long introduction and Red Sea digression dilute the intensity that the closing section achieves.
- *Cadence and Breath (1)*: **1/1** — Good sentence variation. Short declarative sentences at key moments ("That's when the snakes came.").

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. *Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?* **Christ** — the typological movement is consistently toward Jesus as the antitype of the serpent. The Newton letter redirects away from self-measurement toward Christ's sufficiency.
2. *Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?* **Mostly ideas, with one genuine encounter** — the Newton letter creates a moment of pastoral weight. The rest of the sermon is explanation rather than encounter.
3. *Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?* **Primarily informed, with a genuine moment of comfort** — the "no matter how many wounds" application from Newton is the sermon's pastoral peak.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The Newton Letter** — The citation of Newton's pastoral letter to his mentee ("if a man had spent all his time measuring or counting his wounds") is the sermon's high point. It personalizes the typology with pastoral precision — the sufficiency of the remedy regardless of the severity of the sin. This is the kind of illustration that is native to the text.

2. **Organic Typological Movement** — The preacher rightly lets Jesus make the typological connection (John 3:14) rather than imposing it. The sermon follows the redemptive-historical trajectory that Scripture itself establishes, and the inclusion of 1 Corinthians 10:9 (Paul's own retrospective identification of the Israelites' sin) strengthens the exegetical legitimacy.

3. **Oral Authenticity and Preacherly Presence** — The manuscript reads as the record of a man who speaks from conviction. The personal snake story, the hurricane evacuation detail, the conversational asides — these produce a preacher who is present to his congregation, not performing.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **No Treatment of Why the Cross Saves** — The sermon tells us Jesus was "lifted up" and that belief saves, but never explains what happened in the atonement. The bronze serpent type demands an antitype explanation: why does looking at the serpent save? Because God appointed it as the means of healing. And why does looking to Christ save? Because God appointed the cross as the place where divine judgment was exhausted in Christ's body. The sermon stops at the surface of the type without pressing into the antitype's logic. The congregation leaves knowing *what* (look to Christ) but not *why* the looking accomplishes anything.

2. **Application Addresses Only the Unconverted** — The entire application section is directed at people who have not yet believed. The congregation of believers — who presumably constitute most of the room — receive no differentiated address. What does this text say to the Christian who is faithfully looking? To the one who has believed but doubts the sufficiency of Christ's work for their particular sins? The Newton letter opens this door (his mentee was a believing pastor), but the sermon doesn't walk through it.

3. **The Red Sea Digression Undermines the Sermon's Credibility** — The citation of unverified archaeology (chariot wheels, bones at Nuweiba) introduces a false note early in the sermon. The preacher's note ("I love it when science proves Scripture; science doesn't have to prove Scripture for me to believe it") is theologically sound, but citing a claim that is not verified — and that the sermon's own notes identify as Ron Wyatt's rejected claims — weakens the sermon's authority precisely when it is trying to establish trust before the text. Cut it or replace it with something verifiable.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

- **The Nicodemus Context of John 3** is entirely absent. John 3:14-21 is spoken to a religious expert who knows the Scriptures but has not been born again. The sermon addresses the unconverted generally but misses the opportunity to press the person who is doctrinally knowledgeable but spiritually unchanged — exactly who Nicodemus represents.
- **The Nature of "Looking"** — The sermon treats "looking" as a simple act of belief, but the typology invites deeper exploration. What does it mean to look? Why does John 3 use the language of "eternal life" rather than just "healing"? The sermon doesn't press into the Johannine theology of belief (which is not mere assent but entrusting).
- **The preacher doesn't see that the Newton letter is the sermon's best moment** — it receives one paragraph and then the sermon moves on. It deserves to be the organizing center, not a supporting anecdote.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The life-ring metaphor at the sermon's close *feels* climactic and emotionally resonant, and it is a serviceable evangelistic illustration. But it subtly shifts the atonement from substitution ("Christ took what you deserved") to rescue ("Christ threw you a rope"). The feeling of strength at the close may mask the fact that the most important doctrinal content — what the cross actually accomplished — was never stated. The congregation may leave feeling they've heard the gospel when they've heard a gospel-adjacent illustration.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**John 3:19 — "People loved the darkness rather than the light."** The preacher notes this verse correctly as the answer to why some Israelites did not look at the serpent. But the verse contains a stunning diagnosis of natural human depravity — the unregenerate heart actively prefers its own destruction over the light. This is not stubbornness or pride alone; it is a description of what Paul in Romans 1 calls the exchange of truth for a lie, the suppression of what is known. A brief treatment here would have prepared the sermon for a much stronger gospel appeal: *you can't save yourself by looking because your nature doesn't want to look. That is why the Spirit must draw you — and the fact that you are hearing this sermon suggests he is.*

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Cut the Red Sea archaeology digression** (paragraph 3, the Ron Wyatt material). Replace with one sentence: "They escaped across the sea — miraculous preservation from the very beginning." The digression introduces doubt where the text needs confidence.

2. **Name the atonement in the John 3 section** — After reading John 3:14-15, add: "Why does the serpent heal everyone who looks? Because God *appointed* it — his word made it the instrument of life. And why does looking to Christ save? Because God appointed the cross as the place where the judgment you and I deserve was exhausted. Christ didn't just hang there as a symbol — he bore the wrath of God in your place. *That* is why the looking saves."

3. **Add a paragraph for the believer after the Newton letter** — "If you are already a Christian, the Newton letter is still for you. You may know that God can forgive sins — you've seen it in others — but you wonder if your particular history of wounds, your accumulated failures, your besetting sin, is too much. Newton's answer: the remedy does not change by the severity of the wound. Look to Christ. Again. Now."

4. **Differentiate hearers at the close** — Before the final invitation, add two sentences distinguishing the believer from the unconverted: "Some of you have looked — and you're still here, still looking. Keep looking. And some of you have never looked — tonight is the night."

5. **Move the Newton letter to the structural center** — It should not be an anecdote supporting the main point. It *is* the main point. Structure the sermon around it: the problem (wounds from sin), the remedy (the appointed means of healing), the obstacle (measuring wounds instead of looking), and the invitation (look now, regardless of count).

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered.** The sermon has genuine evangelical warmth and pastoral fire. It is not bloodless. The concern is thinness of exposition, not absence of passion.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries genuine gospel weight — the Newton letter in particular achieves a moment of real pastoral gravity — but the full eternal stakes of John 3:16-21 (condemnation versus eternal life, the love of God that sent the Son) are gestured at rather than proclaimed with the force the text deserves.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

This is a typological-narrative sermon drawing on three texts. The rubric fits well. The category of "Exposition & Hermeneutics" appropriately presses for the kind of exegetical development that typological sermons require: not just identifying the type, but unpacking what the antitype accomplishes and why. The sermon's greatest gap — the absence of an explanation of the atonement's mechanism — is correctly surfaced by the rubric's Christ-Centeredness and Gospel Centrality categories.
