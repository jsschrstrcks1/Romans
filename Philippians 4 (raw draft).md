Good morning and welcome to Lakeview Fellowship. We're going to be in Philippians chapter 4 today, and while you're turning there I want to talk about our people group of the week.

**[People group segment]**

This week our people group is the Mo-Jer-Hai of China. They are a relatively small people group — only about 7,000 known members. They are completely unreached and unengaged. Which means that as things stand today, they have no opportunity to hear the gospel. To the best of our knowledge, this people group — isolated by the Hunan mountains — has never left the mountains, much less the country. So they will need to be reached in China.

Their culture centers around singing and dancing. Stories are told and important occasions marked by these things. That may be a great foothold for the gospel — music as a bridge.

Please pray for them. Pray that they would hear of Christ and that their hearts would be moved to pursue Him as a group. Pray for a people movement to Christ.

Who will pray for the Mo-Jer-Hai of China?

**[Introduction — the divided world]**

In my reading this week I came across this passage, and the first few verses made me think about our world today. People at odds with each other. Bickering and fighting.

An acquaintance of mine called his granddaughter a communist on Facebook — said she didn't deserve the right to vote. We are a nation deeply divided right from left. But your politics are less important than your alignment with Scripture. Do we believe what the Bible says? Do we live that out daily? Does our belief in Scripture result in our sharing the gospel? If so, that's wonderful. But don't let your politics get in the way of the gospel.

Like that grandfather. His granddaughter was highly offended by being called a communist. She believed something slightly different from her grandfather on one secondary issue — and rather than argue with Scripture or facts, he went straight to name calling. A family divided. She was offended and likely downgraded her opinion of his opinions. She smarted off, he didn't improve, and now we have two people at odds with each other who are both — essentially — on the same side of the aisle. One who loves Christ. And a granddaughter that's still searching.

Think back, if you can, to a time before you were a believer in Christ. If someone called you names you found insulting, would you have cared to hear about their Jesus?

---

Secondly — aren't you glad that when you have a disagreement with someone, you weren't addressed in Scripture by Paul saying, basically, "Hey — cut it out"? I know I'm glad my arguments aren't recorded in Scripture. This would have been a fairly major dispute to be addressed by Paul directly. And fairly major punishment, too — as these letters from Paul were copied and spread around. Every church wanted copies. These two names would have become fairly well-known for their argument, and for Paul's address of it.

---

It strikes me that we have major divisions in this country right now, and the response of many Christians in the church has been similar to what that grandfather said to his granddaughter. Rather than reasoning and pleading with people — sharing the gospel, with their eternal souls at stake — many in the church are defaulting to the modern equivalent of "nuh-uh, you're stupid." Communist. Socialist. Whatever.

Does it matter whether they know what their position has been historically called? Does it even matter if they really are communists? What matters is the gospel.

The solution to communism is the gospel. The solution to sin in this world is the gospel. See a societal ill that needs to be addressed? Share the gospel. Because when someone is a believer in Jesus Christ, the Holy Spirit indwells them, the Scripture washes them, God and Jesus lead them — and who, when truly following Christ, could stand in support of things like sex trafficking? No one. The gospel washes us. It saves us. It connects us to the only true power to change for good that we can rely upon.

---

**[Eternal perspective]**

As I mentioned last week, we are eternal beings having a temporal experience. This world and its hundred years of life will soon be past. In the words of C.T. Studd: *"This life twill soon be past, only what's done for Christ will last."*

**[NOTE: The widely-known form of this poem is “Only one life, ‘twill soon be past, / only what’s done for Christ will last.” The draft drops the opening line “Only one life,” and reads “twill” for “‘twill.” Correct before preaching. ⚠️ Primary source in Studd’s published writings unconfirmed — attribution widely held but treat as unverified.]**

For the believer, this world is the worst thing we will ever experience. For the lost, however, this is as good as it gets. That should break our hearts. There are good things in this life — love, family, and so on. But even those things are dim shadows of what will come in heaven for eternity. Even now those things are tainted by our sinful hearts. But one day — already and at once not-yet and for eternity — that taint of sin will be fully removed from our perspective and we will be rejoicing in the presence of our Savior forever. Loving fully.

One day of eternity standing guard over the gates is better than a thousand elsewhere. One day when we get to heaven, our broken bodies will be made whole. David — no more wheelchair, pal. A friend of mine who had shoulder replacement surgery and may need a new knee — when he gets to heaven, those scars of this world will be gone. No stroke damage, no heart damage, none of that. My grandmother, when she took her last breath about ten years ago and went to heaven — she no longer has breathing trouble. No more issues from the cancer or COPD. She's been reunited with the loved ones who went before her in Christ, and even better than all of that — she's sitting in the presence of God.

No more sin. No more scars from this world. No more pain. Just God and His glory forever.

I love the words of the old hymn — *"When we've been there ten thousand years, bright shining as the sun — we've no less days to sing God's praise than when we've first begun."*

That will be just as true for those of us who believe in Jesus Christ. But for now — for the next maybe 20, 40, 100 years — we're stuck here. And as you know, things aren't quite right in the world we live in.

---

**[The Fall]**

Adam and Eve were in the garden, and it was perfect — with no sin. A perfect reflection of our Savior. But Adam and Eve, like all kids pretty much, went straight to the one thing they were told not to do, and did it. Which introduced sin into the world, and in so doing shattered the image of God in creation. Instead of us each being a mirror perfectly reflecting God, we are each a broken shard of glass from the original mirror. As we grow closer to the Lord — studying God's Word, spending time in fellowship and worship and evangelism — we better reflect God. Though it will never be perfect until one day we are in heaven with Him.

So as a result we are left in this sinful, fallen world. Dave has to deal with wheelchairs. Some of you have had to deal with cancer, or stroke, or heart attack. Sandspurs, and so on. All part of what happened in the garden. The division we have today between Republicans and Democrats — I attribute that to the fall as well. Grandfathers calling their granddaughters communists? Caused by the fall. As a Christian he should know better. But anyway.

We have all this sin, and consequences of sin, and pain and heartache in the world today — and we know it's not going to get much better in this life.

---

**[What do we do?]**

So what do we do? What do we do when our choice for president isn't elected and everyone is shouting doom and gloom from the rooftops? On top of that there are actually scary elements. How hard will the new administration be on Christians? Our church secretary, under Obama, was audited eight consecutive years in a row — presumably merely for working at the church. Everything came out fine; she's above board. But still. What about freedom of speech? What about freedom of religion? What if this, what if that? What if America becomes a "communist country"?

All are scary thoughts. All are a result of sin. And you know what Scripture says? We read last week that we are to be respectful and obedient to our leadership. And this week we're reading that we need to guard our hearts — with gladness. Our verse tonight tells us to rejoice. Twice: rejoice. Be focused on what is good. Be happy for the things you can be happy for. Find joy in the little things, and rejoice for them.

---

**[Catahoula puppy illustration]**

I had a situation this week where I was getting pretty frustrated with my Catahoula puppy. He wanted me to get up at 5am, and I didn't want to. I wanted to get up closer to 7. So I scolded him and went back to bed.

At 7 when I got up and went to take him out, I lifted his blanket off of his crate — and he had been standing there ready and waiting, and as soon as I pulled the blanket back, he hiked his leg and peed. It was hard to find something to be joyful about while I was on my knees cleaning up his urine. It was hard to find something to rejoice in as I carried his crate — dripping with urine — through the rest of the house. It was hard to find something to rejoice in when I realized he had managed to pee on a bookcase full of books too.

But I settled on the fact that he's a good guard dog. That he's a good companion. That he tries hard to be obedient and genuinely wants to be a good dog. That he's smart and capable — more than most dogs. He's genuinely a good dog in so many ways; he's just still a puppy in some others. So I am quite thankful for Cooper, even though that means sometimes I'm on my knees cleaning up his urine off my books, or the floor, or his crate.

There are things we can find to rejoice in — if we're looking.

---

**[Grandfather's broken hip illustration]**

Another time my grandfather was in the hospital with a broken hip. He'd fallen by the fish-cleaning table, dragged himself clear up to the house and up the stairs. And my grandmother — Grandma Fran — was absolutely adamant that he toughen up and walk it off. So to his credit, and Grandma's chagrin, she made him get up and walk on his broken hip. Ended up having surgery. Ended up spending a few weeks in the hospital, then rehab.

How miserable, right? But even in the midst of that there were things to rejoice in. Because he was in the hospital, we ended up meeting his roommate — who at that point was lost. We shared the gospel with him. And my grandfather's roommate ended up being saved. God had planned it so that we would be there at the appointed time and place, so that my grandfather's roommate would hear the gospel and be saved. For my grandfather, it was worth it. It was something to rejoice in.

---

When we make a habit of finding things to rejoice in rather than complain about, and when we bring our requests to God — what does our passage say will happen? Someone read verse 7.

*"And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."* (Philippians 4:7, ESV)

Worthy rewards for sure.

---

**[Whatever is true — the list]**

As we turn to the final part of the chapter, I want to read a quote from a preacher named Samuel Sey. He's a Southern Baptist from Ghana who now lives and preaches in Ghana. He wrote: *"If the word of God isn't the loudest thing in my life, Satan's whispers will be all I hear."*

**[FLAG: Verify Samuel Sey quote — exact wording, attribution, and whether "Southern Baptist from Ghana" is an accurate description.]**

I think that's important to keep in mind as we read this last section, because there is a wonderful list of things we can focus on.

---

**Whatever is true.** What is true? Tell me something true.

"True" is a term used in carpentry. A piece of wood, or a joint, or an angle is *true* if it conforms to the drawings one hundred percent. If it's supposed to be exactly four inches away — at the top, the middle, and the bottom — and it is, then it's true.

Our Bible is our drawings for life. It's our master plan. Our instruction manual and love letter from God. It is more true than anything else you can read — and helps you become true as well.

Unlike other things in this world: if you hear something on the news, maybe it's true, maybe it's not. Maybe it's spin. Maybe it's designed to inflame your passions so they can sell advertising. With the Bible, you don't have that. It says what it says because God wanted it to say so. What we have is accurate and true across the centuries — and we can prove it.

---

**Whatever is noble.** Noble in English means possessing high values or morality. In Greek the word is *semnos* — whatever is worthy of extra respect, or honor. Things of great dignity.

**[FLAG: Verify that the Greek behind "whatever is honorable/noble" in Philippians 4:8 ESV is semnos — confirm before using from pulpit.]**

---

**Whatever is right.** And I caution you here — it's a small step from focusing on whatever is right to focusing on whatever is unjust. Focusing on what is right is different from focusing on injustices. Focus on what is just. Ways in which God is moving that are just.

---

**Whatever is pure.** This is the same word Paul uses when he commends Timothy to keep himself pure. Keep above the muck and mire of the world. Separate yourself from sin.

---

**Whatever is lovely.** This is a neat word — it's the only time it's used in the New Testament. However, in the Greek translation of the Hebrew Old Testament it appears twice. Once in Song of Solomon, and a second time in Psalm 84:

*"How lovely is your dwelling place, O LORD of hosts! My soul longs, yes, faints for the courts of the LORD; my heart and flesh sing for joy to the living God."* (Psalm 84:1–2, ESV)

Where it says "how lovely is your dwelling place" — it's talking about the place where God is. At that time it was the temple. Today, where is God? He's in our hearts. And He's in heaven.

Looking forward to the glories of heaven helps quite a bit when things are hard.

**[FLAG: Verify that "lovely" (prosphilēs) in Philippians 4:8 is indeed a NT hapax legomenon, and that its two LXX occurrences are Song of Solomon and Psalm 84:1. Confirm LXX spelling and ESV accuracy of Psalm 84:1–2 quote above.]**

---

My friend Bill just had a total shoulder replacement. He's in quite a bit of pain. In fact right after church I have to run up there — I'll be helping him change the dressing on the surgery site, and whatever other little things he needs. He said his shoulder being operated on is one of the most painful things he's ever been through. But he's willing to go through it for a couple of reasons. For one — the pain only lasts for a little while. And for another — he knows that after the painful part, having full use of his arm again is worth pain for a little while.

Just like his shoulder, people — the pain this world exposes us to is only for a while. It only lasts a brief flicker in the scope of eternity. For those of us who are believers, this world is the worst thing we will ever experience — and then on to glory. For the lost, this world is as good as it gets.

---

**Whatever is worthy of praise.** What is worthy of praise? Who is worthy of praise? Only God. Focus on God. Keep your eyes on Him. Let the world around you fade away.

If you are truly focused on God, the ways in which life is unfair will start to dim. Your neighbor stealing your grandfather's tools doesn't sting so badly when your eyes are on God. The lady at the state office misunderstanding your needs and misrepresenting them to the government — when our eyes are on God, those things fade out. When the man you dislike most is elected president and threatens your livelihood. When your leg — Dottie — nearly kills you, and you come to the conclusion: "If God wants a dead saint, then so be it."

When we let our own wants and desires become subservient to Christ and His desires for us — these other issues that seem so important and so terrible, they fade into the background. Second to God, where they should be. Life becomes so much more bearable.

---

It takes a minute to get there — I get that. You've heard me talk about the struggles I've had with my thieving neighbors. It's hard to set aside my rights. I get that.

In the meantime, if you can't quite rejoice, at least be reasonable. Paul says: *let your reasonableness be known by all.* That grandfather who called his granddaughter a communist — is he being reasonable? (Psst: she's not a communist.) Live your life in a way that opens lines of communication with the lost, rather than closes them. Be reasonable. Be ready with the gospel.

It all comes back to setting aside yourself in favor of what God wants.

Charles Spurgeon once said: *"You will never glory in God till first of all God has killed your glorying in yourself."*

**[FLAG: Verify Spurgeon quote — exact wording and source before using from pulpit.]**

I think he would agree that when we get overly upset about thieving neighbors, it falls under that category. When we are upset about the direction our country is heading and have rebellious thoughts — that falls under that category. We want to be comfortable, we want to be successful, we want to be prosperous, we want to live in what is — in my opinion — the greatest nation in the world. We want all of these things. But when the rubber meets the road, all too often it's all about "I." What Spurgeon calls "glorying in yourself."

Christian — there is only One we should glory in, and that is Jesus Christ. If you have more faith, or hope, or trust in anything besides Jesus, then you have an idol issue. We can even make politics an idol — and many have done so today. Like at that Jericho march. Glorying in yourself has to go. And you have to replace it with: *whatever is true, whatever is noble, whatever is right, whatever is pure — if it is lovely, excellent, admirable, or praiseworthy — think on these things.*
