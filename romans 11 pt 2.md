**FULL SERMON MANUSCRIPT — Romans 11:11–32**

**Grafted by Grace: Mercy That Humbles, Mercy That Sends**
*(Single-spaced, full prose, balanced paragraphs, includes all slide cues)*

**Introduction — When Mercy Runs Ahead of Us**

If you’ve brought your Bibles—and I hope you have—open with me to Romans chapter 11. Last week we saw the remnant of grace—that God always keeps a people for Himself, even when human eyes see only ruin. But that raises the next question: If so many in Israel rejected their Messiah, is their story over? Has their stumbling become a fall beyond repair?

It almost sounds like one of those old black-and-white serial announcers: “Is this it? Is this the end? Find out next week!” Except, of course, we already have the conclusion written and held in our hands. So Paul answers once again with his favorite thunderclap: “By no means!” (μὴ γένοιτο — mē genoito). Absolutely not. May it never be.

What looks like collapse to us is choreography to God. Israel’s stumbling opened a door—not only for Gentile salvation, but for Israel’s future awakening. Romans 11:11–24 is Paul saying, “Look deeper—God’s mercy is running a wider race than you can see.”

Today we walk with Paul through three movements: (1) A Stumble Allowed—salvation to the nations; (2) Arousing Jealousy—Gentile salvation as divine provocation; and (3) A Warning to the Wild Branches—humility or being cut off. And we will end where Paul ends: mission fueled by humility; worship fueled by wonder. *(next slide)*

**1) A Stumble Allowed — Salvation to the Nations (vv.11–12)**

Paul begins with a question: “So I ask, did they stumble in order that they might fall?” (v.11). In other words: Is Israel’s rejection of Christ the end of the story? Did their stumble become a fatal fall? And Paul answers with that unmistakable Greek thunderclap: “By no means!”—μὴ γένοιτο. Absolutely not.

The stumble was real, but it was not ruin. It was permitted, but not purposeless. It was tragic, but not terminal. God bent their trespass into a doorway of mercy so that the gospel might run into the Gentile world with unstoppable force. “Through their trespass salvation has come to the Gentiles…” (v.11). This is not Paul softening the blow; it is Paul revealing the plan. Israel’s loss became the world’s gain.

And then Paul says something shocking: “…so as to make Israel jealous.” God intends for the salvation of the nations to awaken Israel from slumber—to stir them, provoke them, draw them to consider the Messiah they once rejected.

Then Paul lifts our eyes even higher: “If their trespass means riches for the world, how much more will their full inclusion mean?” (v.12). If God used their unbelief to bless the nations, what will He do when their eyes are opened?

Israel’s stumble brought mercy to the world. Israel’s restoration will bring mercy in a measure we cannot imagine. *(next slide)*

Paul’s “By no means!” reminds us that God’s plans are never undone by human failure. *(next slide)*

*(This is where μὴ γένοιτο appears in your slides.)*

**Pastoral Applications:** To the believer: Your failures are never bigger than God’s sovereignty. He wastes nothing—not one tear, not one drop of blood. To the complacent: Don’t assume proximity to truth is enough. God honors repentance, not heritage. To the skeptic: If God can turn national unbelief into global salvation, He can take your story—even its broken places—and make it a testimony of grace.

*(next slide)*

Now look at the map—how the gospel moved from Jerusalem outward to the nations. Israel’s stumble opened this door. Grace ran outward to the world.

**2) Arousing Jealousy — Divine Provocation (vv.13–16)**

“Now I am speaking to you Gentiles,” Paul says (v.13). He turns directly to us because the mystery of God’s plan is not merely that Israel stumbled and Gentiles entered. It is that Gentile mercy is meant to make Israel wake up.

Paul says, “I magnify my ministry in order somehow to make my fellow Jews jealous, and thus save some of them” (vv.13–14). This is not ego—it is evangelistic strategy. Paul wants Israel to see Gentile joy in their Messiah and say, “We want Him too.”

This is holy jealousy—a longing for what is spiritually good, true, and beautiful. As Thomas Manton said, “The conversion of one soul is the provocation of another. Grace is contagious.”

And yet, if we are honest, we hear about Israel’s hardness and say to ourselves, “I could never be like that. I’d never resist God like they did.” Paul is setting us up—because we are never more blind than when we trust our own eyesight.

Paul uses the image of grafting: wild olive branches inserted into a cultivated root. *(next slide)*

Look at the grafting image. We are not the root; we were grafted in. Everything nourishing us comes from promises God made long before we were born.

“If the root is holy, so are the branches” (v.16). The church is not a new plant. It is a wild branch grafted into an ancient covenant root—Abraham, Isaac, Jacob. And if the root is holy, the branches should be too.

There is no room for Gentile pride. What do we have that we were not given? And again: people do not envy a faith they cannot see. If our tone, holiness, or speech hasn’t changed, we cannot provoke anyone to Christ.

*(next slide)*

**3) A Warning to the Wild Branches — Humility or Being Cut Off (vv.17–24)**

Paul now turns the spotlight onto us. Up to now, we’ve been nodding at Israel’s failure like spectators. “I’d never do that… I’d never reject God like they did…” But Paul leans in and says: Be very careful. You are becoming exactly like them.

“Do not be arrogant toward the branches” (v.18). Do not assume their sins are not your sins. Do not imagine you’re immune to the very unbelief you condemn. Because the moment a Christian begins saying, “I would never…” they are already halfway to doing exactly that.

The Pharisee said, “I thank you God that I am not like that man,” and in that very moment proved he was exactly like him.

Paul cuts through our pride with his strongest Greek negation—μὴ γένοιτο—“Absolutely not!” It is his spiritual emergency brake. His “Stop before you destroy yourself.”

“They were broken off because of unbelief, but you stand fast through faith. So do not become proud, but fear” (v.20).

Here is where the warning becomes painfully practical: Israel said “No” with their actions; we often say “No” with our mouths—no to the commands of God, no to holiness, no to sharing the gospel, no to bearing the cross He calls us to carry; and don’t you dare think you don’t.

They say the average American only prays when life gets hard—when the pressures rise, when the walls close in. But the hardships we resist are often the very tools God uses to chip away at our hardness, to draw us to Himself, to prepare us for His purposes. Yet our instinctive cry is, “Stop doing that, God. It hurts.” We may not say it out loud, but the heart says, “Leave me alone. Don’t touch that area. Don’t expose that sin. Don’t refine me there.” We cling to the very hardness He is trying to break. We protect the very sins He is trying to free us from. And sometimes, if we are honest, our hearts whisper, “But God—that’s my favorite sin. Can’t I keep just that one?”

And here comes the spark.

**Holiness always touches the mouth.** You cannot provoke holy jealousy if the world hears nothing holy in how you speak. You cannot display a changed life if your speech has not changed. You cannot belong to the Holy One and speak the language of Babylon.

*(next slide)*

Now look at the graphic on the screen—the rotten fruit vs the good fruit. Corrupt speech versus holy speech. This is what Paul addresses head-on in Ephesians 4:29.

**Ephesians 4:29 — Speech That Reveals the Heart**

Paul writes, “Let no corrupting talk come out of your mouths, but only such as is good for building up…” The word “corrupting” means rotten, decaying, foul. Rotten words do not belong in redeemed mouths.

So let’s be plain: Does dropping the F-bomb reflect a heart made new? Do crude words—the “other words” we all know—fit the mouth of someone who claims Jesus as Lord? Paul’s answer is a clear, unflinching **No**.

But he goes deeper. Corrupt speech is not only cussing; it includes cutting speech—cutting someone because you’re disappointed, cutting with sarcasm you later excuse as “just kidding,” cutting with anger you justify by saying, “They deserved it.” No. Stop doing that.

The Greek includes speech that wounds: mockery, sexual innuendo, flirting with someone who is not your spouse, words that darken another person’s imagination. This is not neutral. It corrupts them and you.

And even “neutral” speech fails this command. If your words aren’t building up, they are breaking down. Neutral speech is passive rebellion. Destructive speech is active rebellion. Both fall short of Christ.

Scripture says life and death are in the power of the tongue. James says the tongue can burn down a forest. Paul says when we speak without restraint, we are not venting—we are resisting the Holy Spirit.

A filthy mouth reveals a filthy heart. And the excuse “Everyone I know talks this way” will not stand before the Christ who redeemed you. You are called to be different. To sound different. To speak like people made alive.

Then Paul turns positive: speak “only what builds up.” Christian speech steadies the wavering, strengthens the weary, comforts the hurting, corrects gently, and lifts the cross high. Every conversation is a brick or a hammer. It builds or destroys.

So ask yourself: If your speech at work, at home, online, or in the car sounds exactly like everyone else’s, have you truly been made new? Are you walking with Christ—or resisting Him with the very mouth He redeemed?

When your mouth carries grace instead of decay, people catch a faint but real taste of Jesus—the One who is full of grace and truth.

*(next slide)*

**4) A Mystery Revealed — Mercy for All Peoples (vv.25–32)**

Paul now reveals what he calls a mystery—not a puzzle to solve but a plan once hidden and now unveiled in Christ. “Lest you be wise in your own sight… I do not want you to be unaware of this mystery” (v.25).

He begins with a partial hardening on Israel: not total, not permanent, not purposeless. Then he speaks of the fullness of the Gentiles—there is a sovereignly appointed harvest from among the nations, and when it is complete, another movement begins.

“And in this way all Israel will be saved” (v.26). This refers to a future, large-scale turning of Jewish people to their Messiah. Paul quotes Scripture: “The Deliverer will come from Zion… this will be my covenant with them when I take away their sins” (vv.26–27). Israel rejected their Messiah, but the Messiah did not reject Israel.

To see the depth of this mercy, look at Isaiah 53. *(next slide)*

There on the screen is the Suffering Servant. Isaiah says He “bore our griefs, carried our sorrows,” was “pierced for our transgressions,” “crushed for our iniquities,” and that “by His wounds we are healed.” He was despised, rejected, silent under suffering, killed with criminals, buried with the rich, and raised in triumph. His suffering was not failure but salvation. His wounds heal. His death gives life. His sacrifice makes many righteous.

Israel’s final turning to Christ is not sentimental nationalism—it is repentance before the Servant they pierced.

Paul continues: “As regards the gospel, they are enemies for your sake; but as regards election, they are beloved for the sake of their forefathers.” Israel is beloved not because they are faithful, but because God is. “For the gifts and the calling of God are irrevocable” (v.29). Irrevocable. Unchangeable. Unbreakable.

“For God has consigned all to disobedience, that He may have mercy on all” (v.32). Jew and Gentile share the same problem—disobedience. And share the same solution—mercy. God closed every door except grace so that the only way in is Christ.

**Conclusion — Worship, Healing, and the Beginning of the Romans Road**

We have watched a stumble allowed, a jealousy aroused, a warning given, and a mystery revealed. We saw Israel stumble and God open a door to the nations. We saw Gentiles enter and God promise to awaken Israel. We saw arrogance exposed in our own lips and hearts. And then we saw mercy—mercy for Israel, mercy for the nations, mercy for you.

And behind it all stands Jesus Christ, the Suffering Servant of Isaiah 53, the Lamb who takes away the sin of the world. The One who bore our griefs, carried our sorrows, was pierced for our transgressions, crushed for our iniquities, silent for our salvation, buried for our transgressions, and raised for our justification.

It is no wonder Paul ends this chapter not with debate, but doxology: “Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are His judgments and how inscrutable His ways! For from Him and through Him and to Him are all things. To Him be glory forever. Amen.” *(next slide)*

This is where I want to leave you today: low before the greatness of God, lifted by His mercy, steadied by His faithfulness, ready to walk with Him.

Because if mercy is the story of Romans 11, then the next question is simple: How does that mercy reach you personally? How does a holy God take rebellious people—Jew or Gentile, religious or irreligious, sinners with mouths that wound and hearts that wander—and make them righteous?

How does He forgive?
How does He save?
How does He make dead hearts alive?

The answer begins on a road.
A Roman road.
And if you will walk with me a little farther, Scripture will show you every step of the way.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Faithful Scope, Genuine Pastoral Heat — Derailed by an Unannounced Detour into Ephesians**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A textually anchored and pastorally warm opening of Romans 11:11–32 that exposits the mercy-and-humility logic of Paul's argument with genuine force, but undermines its own coherence by grafting a full Ephesians 4:29 sub-sermon onto the passage without exegetical warrant, displacing the doxological climax that Paul himself provides.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**Pass — with a significant structural caveat.**

- **Scopus Test**: The sermon's main thesis — that God's mercy humbles the proud and fuels mission — does arise demonstrably from Romans 11:11–32. The argument tracks Paul's actual logic: stumble, jealousy, warning, mystery, and doxology.
- **Authority Test**: The preacher submits to the text's uncomfortable claims (the warning to Gentile pride in vv.17–21 is pressed hard). The sermon does not soften Paul's severity.
- **Caveat**: The extended Ephesians 4:29 section (the entire "holiness always touches the mouth" passage) is not derived from the Romans 11 text. It is imported as an application bridge that the text itself does not build. This is not technically eisegesis of the Romans passage, but it does constitute a significant departure from the stated text for a full section of the sermon. The Berean Gate passes because the main point is textually grounded — but this caveat is load-bearing for the scoring below.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**No fatal flaws triggered. Score is uncapped.**

However, note the following near-miss warnings:
- The gospel presentation is real but arrives late and leans heavily on Isaiah 53 rather than being woven into the exposition of Paul's own argument. For a sermon on Romans 11, the gospel could be more organically textual.
- The Ephesians 4:29 departure risks making the application section feel disconnected from the text's actual applicatory logic.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Mostly Christ, but the Ephesians 4:29 section temporarily redirects attention to the hearer's speech patterns. The conclusion recovers Christward orientation well.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Partially. The doxology in the conclusion and the Isaiah 53 passage have genuine weight. The middle sections are more informational. The sermon earns moments of encounter but does not sustain them throughout.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Both convicted and informed — the warning to pride lands, and the mercy-for-all conclusion genuinely comforts. The sermon is not merely informational. However, the shift to Ephesians 4:29 introduces a section that is more morally corrective than gospel-driven, tipping toward instruction over encounter.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The warning to Gentile pride is textually grounded and pastorally courageous.** The Pharisee comparison and the "I would never" indictment in the vv.17–24 section are exactly what Paul intends and land with appropriate discomfort. The sermon does not soften the severity of the warning.

2. **The redemptive-historical framework is handled with genuine competence.** The "ancient covenant root" language, the Abraham-to-church grafting logic, and the treatment of Israel's future turning as repentance before the Servant they pierced are all theologically sound and congregationally accessible.

3. **"God closed every door except grace so that the only way in is Christ"** is one of the finest summary lines in the manuscript — clear, memorable, doxological. The conclusion's movement toward Paul's own doxology (11:33–36) is the right instinct and lands well.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The Ephesians 4:29 section is an unannounced textual departure that fractures the sermon's unity.** Beginning with "Holiness always touches the mouth," the sermon leaves Romans 11 and does not return substantively until the conclusion. This is not application derived from the text — it is a second sermon embedded in the first. The connection ("you cannot provoke holy jealousy if your speech has not changed") is inferentially plausible, but the extended Ephesians 4:29 exposition exceeds what that inferential bridge can support. A listener may leave thinking Romans 11 was primarily about profanity.

2. **The gospel is deferred rather than proclaimed.** The conclusion raises the questions ("How does He forgive? How does He save?") but deliberately does not answer them, pivoting to a future Romans Road sermon. For the present congregation in the present service, this means the gospel is gestured at but not given. Romans 11:26–27 itself contains the covenantal gospel promise Paul quotes — the Deliverer coming from Zion to remove sins — and this is not fully preached as the sermon's own gospel moment. The Isaiah 53 passage is invoked but functions more as backdrop than proclamation.

3. **The indicative/imperative order is inverted in the application section.** The exhortations (stop proud speech, stop corrupt words, ask if you've really changed) come before the indicatives (you are grafted in by grace, God's gifts are irrevocable, mercy is the final word) are fully secured. This creates a momentary moralistic register in the middle of the sermon where the hearer is pressed to change without first being grounded in the grace that makes change possible.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears to not see how substantially the Ephesians 4:29 section displaces the text. From the inside, it likely feels like vivid application of the "humility" theme — and the content is sound. But from the outside, it reads as a sermon within a sermon, one the preacher was perhaps more eager to preach than the Romans 11 material warranted. The homiletical instinct is pastoral and right; the execution imports material the text does not require.

Additionally, the sermon's treatment of "all Israel will be saved" (v.26) is brief — it names the view but does not press its pastoral and missiological implications with the force Paul does. The preacher does not appear to notice how much Paul himself lingers here as the emotional and theological climax of the entire pericope.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Ephesians 4:29 section feels like the sermon's most "practical" and "direct" moment — the preacher names specific sins (profanity, sarcasm, sexual innuendo), speaks plainly, and generates energy. It *feels* like the sermon's strength. But this strength is illusive: it comes from the preacher's own moral conviction and the familiarity of the application, not from the authority of the text being preached. Romans 11 does not say "stop cussing." The power in that section is the preacher's, not the text's. The sermon's actual strength — the mercy-humility-mission framework of Romans 11 itself — is quieter, and the preacher may unconsciously feel that Romans 11 needs the Ephesians 4:29 section to become "practical enough." It does not. The passage itself has ample applicatory pressure.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The greatest missed opportunity is Paul's own climax: **verses 28–32**, especially "For God has consigned all to disobedience, that He may have mercy on all" (v.32). This is one of the most staggering sentences in the New Testament — the universal solidarity of human disobedience and the universal offer of divine mercy in Christ. The sermon names it but does not stay with it. The doxology of vv.33–36, which the sermon rightly invokes, only lands with its full weight if v.32 has first pressed its nail into the congregation. The preacher hurries past the passage's own theological climax to get to the conclusion.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (4)

1. **Restructure or compress the Ephesians 4:29 section back into the Romans 11 warning.** The speech application is legitimate as one concrete illustration of the "do not become arrogant" command. But it should be one paragraph inside the warning section, not a full sub-exposition with its own Greek word studies. Cut or compress to: one pointed application of holy speech as the visible marker of the humility Paul commands, without opening Ephesians 4:29 as a separate text.

2. **Preach v.32 as the sermon's climax, not the doxology.** The doxology is the right ending, but the congregation needs to feel the weight of "God has consigned all to disobedience" before the wonder of "that He may have mercy on all" can release. Spend two to three minutes in v.32 naming the shared disobedience of Jew and Gentile, naming the hearer's own disobedience, and then letting the mercy thunder. The doxology will carry more force.

3. **Reverse the indicative/imperative order in the application.** Before calling the congregation to holy speech and humble mission, anchor the call in the indicative: you were a wild branch, you were grafted in by grace, you are held by roots you did not grow. From that position of grace-received, the exhortations to humility and holy living carry gospel weight rather than moral pressure.

4. **Answer the gospel questions in the conclusion rather than deferring them.** "How does He forgive? How does He save? ### 17. Rubric Fit Note
Romans 11:11–32 is Pauline epistolary argumentation with covenantal-historical scope — it is among the most rubric-native texts imaginable for this framework. The categories of exposition, gospel centrality, conscience application, and doxological force map directly onto Paul's own rhetorical structure. The rubric does not strain here. If anything, this passage is more demanding than most under this rubric because Paul himself provides the applicatory moves (warning, mystery, mercy), the gospel content (Isaiah 53, covenantal removal of sins), and the worship response (the doxology). A sermon on this text that is evaluated low is not being penalized by an ill-fitting rubric — it is being held to what the passage itself demands.
