## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**A Sincere Pastoral Urgency Resting on a Shallow Exegetical Foundation**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A heartfelt and evangelistically earnest opening of Psalm 2 that captured the psalm's sovereignty theme and pressed unbelievers toward the cross, while leaving the text's own royal-covenantal argument largely unexplored and the believing congregation underserved.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: PASS (marginal)**
The sermon's dominant idea — the nations rage against God in vain, and Christ holds sovereign authority — does arise from the psalm. The thesis is not alien to the passage. However, the sermon never states the main point as a single governing sentence; it is felt more than argued.

**Authority Test: PASS (with concerns)**
The preacher submits to the text's claims, including the wrath of God and the terror of judgment. He does not soften what is hard. There is, however, a significant portion of the sermon (America in Revelation, geopolitical speculation) where the preacher launches away from the text and does not return. The text's own climax — "Kiss the Son" (v. 12) and "Blessed are all who take refuge in him" — receives minimal treatment compared to the time spent on geopolitical commentary.

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 13 / 25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10 pts): 6/10* — The sermon touches the psalm's content but does not work through it systematically. Verses 7–9 (the Messianic decree, the Son's inheritance of the nations, the rod of iron) receive almost no exposition. The "Kiss the Son" of v. 12 — the psalm's own summoning imperative — is used only as an implicit backdrop for the altar call, not exposited. The Messianic heart of the psalm (vv. 7–9 as a divine decree spoken to the Son) is the theological center, and the sermon does not open it.
- *Contextual Awareness (5 pts): 3/5* — The typological / prophetic "mountain behind the mountain" illustration is helpful and accurate. The psalm's place as gateway to the Psalter is noted. The redemptive-historical movement from David's kingship to Christ's universal lordship is gestured at but not developed.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5 pts): 2/5* — No Greek or Hebrew is engaged (appropriate for a psalm). But the psalm's structural argument — rebellion (vv. 1–3), divine derision (vv. 4–6), the decree (vv. 7–9), the summons (vv. 10–12) — is not traced for the congregation. A listener could not reconstruct the psalm's own four-part logic from this sermon.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5 pts): 2/5* — The geopolitical digression (America absent from Revelation, Russia mentioned in Revelation as settled fact, coming persecution) is eisegetical expansion. These claims are not native to Psalm 2's argument, and the "Russia in Revelation" claim is contested (correctly flagged in the verification notes). The text is used as a launching pad for prophetic speculation, not as the governing authority throughout.

**Fallen Condition Focus**: Named implicitly (rebellion against God) but not named with surgical precision. The psalm's FCF is the creature's insane revolt against the sovereign Creator, which is both cosmic and personal. ### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Evangelistic urgency and a real invitation** — The closing call is specific, differentiated (unsaved / baptism / membership), and pastoral. "Come talk to me tonight" is not a vague appeal; it is a named response with real categories. This is more than many sermons deliver.

2. **Unashamed proclamation of divine wrath** — The Edwards quotes are well-chosen and land with force. The ant-under-the-shoe image is memorable and theologically precise. The preacher does not soften God's judgment.

3. **Genuine Christological orientation** — The sermon does not treat Psalm 2 merely as a historical text about David. The typological move is real, and Christ is treated as the necessary focus. The sermon cannot survive without Jesus, which satisfies the Necessity of Christ criterion.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The psalm's Messianic decree (vv. 7–9) is the theological heart — and the sermon bypasses it.** "You are my Son; today I have begotten you" is the ground on which Christ's universal lordship rests. Acts 13:33 and Hebrews 1:5 both apply this verse to Christ's resurrection and enthronement. This is the psalm's own gospel announcement, and it receives almost no development. The sermon preaches around the text's center rather than from it.

2. **The geopolitical digression (America in Revelation, Russia as settled interpretation) is eisegesis and speculation.** This section pulls the sermon away from the text for an extended period and makes a contested exegetical claim (Russia in Revelation) as though it were certain. The psalm does not teach American exceptionalism or dispensational geopolitics, and the sermon imports an interpretive framework foreign to the text's own argument.

3. **The gospel relieves the law too late and too thinly.** The Hammer falls for most of the sermon (wrath, hell, futile rebellion). The Balm arrives only at the altar call, and when it does, it lacks the theological weight needed to match the gravity of what preceded it. The cross is named but not explained. Why does Christ's death satisfy the wrath just described? The sermon creates the need but does not fully supply the answer.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see how much the sermon is shaped by an assumed dispensational framework (America in prophecy, Russia in Revelation, persecution escalation). These are presented as natural extensions of the text rather than as one interpretive tradition among others. A congregation formed by this sermon will assume this framework is the straightforward reading of Psalm 2 when in fact the psalm says nothing about modern geopolitics.

The preacher also likely does not see that the believers in the room — the majority of any congregation on any given Sunday — are left without a word. What does "Kiss the Son" mean for someone who has already kissed him? What does the believer do with v. 12b — "Blessed are all who take refuge in him"? This is the psalm's pastoral invitation to the frightened saint, and it goes entirely unexpreached.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The Edwards quotes feel like doctrinal muscle, and they are genuinely useful. But they mask the fact that the sermon's own exposition of God's wrath is thin — the preacher is borrowing Edwards' force rather than generating it from the text itself. The sermon feels like it has preached wrath because it quoted Edwards on wrath. But the psalm's own language of God "laughing" and "holding in derision" (v. 4) — a startling and irreverent image of divine sovereignty — is walked past without unpacking. The borrowed authority of Edwards partially conceals an underdeveloped native exegesis.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 12b: "Blessed are all who take refuge in him." This is the psalm's final word, and it is pure comfort. After all the wrath, the derision, the rod of iron — the psalm ends not with condemnation but with a beatitude. The terrified, the trembling, the newly convicted — "blessed." This is the psalm completing its own gospel arc. The sermon misses it almost entirely. It would have been the perfect capstone: you've heard the wrath, you've felt the weight — now hear the beatitude. Take refuge. Be blessed.

The connection to Acts 13:33 (Paul citing Psalm 2:7 as fulfilled in Christ's resurrection) was also available and would have grounded the Christological reading in the New Testament's own exegesis rather than leaving it as the preacher's typological observation.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **State the main point of the sermon in one sentence before reading the text.** Something like: "Psalm 2 shows us that the world's rebellion against God is not only futile — it is insane — and that the only refuge from the coming wrath is to take shelter in the Son God has installed as King." Then return to that sentence at each transition.

2. **Develop vv. 7–9 before moving to application.** The Messianic decree is the psalm's pivot. Spend four minutes here: God the Father speaks to the Son, promises him the nations as his inheritance, and announces his authority to rule them with a rod of iron. This is the ground of everything that follows. Without it, the call to "Kiss the Son" hangs in the air without its root.

3. **Cut or relocate the geopolitical material.** The America/Russia/Revelation section is not from the psalm. If it serves a pastoral purpose (equipping the congregation not to despair at cultural decline), frame it explicitly as application and keep it brief. Do not present the Russia interpretation as certain. The psalm itself — "Why do the nations rage?" — is sufficient to address cultural hostility without importing prophetic speculation.

4. **Add a direct address to the believing hearer at the end of the wrath section.** Something like: "Some of you are already in Christ, and you're sitting here wondering if that matters when the world goes dark. Hear the psalm's last word: 'Blessed are all who take refuge in him.' The one who kisses the Son now — who trusts Christ now — is not standing under that rod of iron. You are standing under the blessing."

5. **Develop the cross connection before the invitation.** When the invitation comes, tell the congregation *why* taking refuge in Christ works. The wrath is real. Christ absorbed it. The cross is not just the mechanism of forgiveness — it is the proof that God takes his own wrath seriously and the demonstration that he takes his love for sinners seriously at the same time. The invitation will be stronger if it is preceded by a two-minute proclamation of what Christ accomplished.

6. **Tighten the introduction.** The $5 book anecdote is charming but takes too long. The congregation's attention is most available in the first 90 seconds. Consider opening with the psalm's first line as a diagnostic question: "Why do the nations rage? Look at your news feed tonight. Is that not exactly what you see?"

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 2 is a royal-covenantal psalm with prophetic and doxological dimensions. The rubric fits it well. The "Fallen Condition Focus" category is slightly strained in that the psalm's FCF is partly cosmic (national rebellion against God) rather than purely individual, but the rubric accommodates this with only minor bending. The homiletical challenge unique to this genre — how to move from an ancient royal court context through typological fulfillment to pastoral application without either flattening the typology or making the text foreign — is not fully solved in this sermon, and the rubric correctly surfaces that gap.
