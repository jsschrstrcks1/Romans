Psalm 2:1-12 — Royal and Prophetic Psalm
*(Raw draft — unpolished; evening/night service)*
*(Verification flags at bottom — review before preaching again)*

---

We're taking a week off from Genesis, so if you've brought your Bible — and I hope you have — we will be in Psalm 2 tonight. If you open your Bible to the middle, you'll likely be pretty close to the Psalms. The Psalms is a book of Hebrew poetry in the Old Testament. Some of them are prophetic — that is, they speak about the future. Some are songs of sorrow or praises to God. The first two psalms are considered to be the gateway to the Psalter. In fact I scored a free book on the subject in my Old Testament class. A professor was talking about a subject and said he was pretty sure the idea was his — but promised five dollars to anyone that could find another author with the same thought. A quick Google search later and he promised to bring me the five dollars the following class. After class I mentioned I'd rather have a digital copy of one of his books. He sent me a draft copy of a book he had been working on called *Psalms 1 and 2: The Gateway to the Psalter*. I've read a fair bit of it. It was recently published and he sells it for a hundred and twenty dollars a copy — I was pretty happy to trade five dollars for it.

Anyway, we're going to be looking at Psalm 2 tonight. It is a royal psalm, meaning it's talking about David — but it's also prophetic, pointing to Jesus. Prophetic passages tend to work like a double entendre. Have you ever seen something like that? You say one thing but it has two meanings. Or if you're traveling through the mountains, you have the mountain right in front of you, but a bigger mountain just beyond it that you can still see. It's like that here. The immediate context may have been David, but in the longer term it's pointing directly to Christ.

Let's start in verse 1 through the end.

---

Let's start with those first three verses. It seems to me that is an apt description of the world around us right now. Nations raging. China aggressively claiming land in the South China Sea, preparing herself for war. Russia and Crimea. Afghanistan, Iraq, and Syria. There are more than fifty wars going on around the world right now — official declared wars, a country against another country, or two factions within a country. That sounds a lot like nations raging to me.

But I think this goes a step deeper. Around the world there are countries where it would be illegal to wear a shirt talking about Jesus. Where it's illegal to own a Bible, to talk about Jesus. Illegal to share the life-saving truth of the Gospel — for fear of execution, or worse, prison and torture of you and your family. *(Note: Exact country count should be verified against current Open Doors or VOM data before reprising this sermon. The mid-2010s figure was approximately 50 countries at extreme or very high persecution; by 2024 that number had risen to 60.)* The world we live in is hostile to the Gospel. Hostile to the name of Jesus Christ. Hostile to the Word of God. If you've seen the documentary *The Insanity of God*, or read the book or its follow-up, you know the sorts of things that are going on the world over. The first Sunday in November is the International Day of Prayer for the Persecuted Church, and before we close tonight we're going to spend some time praying for the international church. Scripture says in Hebrews 13:3: *"Remember those who are in prison, as though in prison with them, and those who are mistreated, since you also are in the body."* It's so easy to sit here comfortable in the West — with Bibles on the shelf collecting dust and a dozen television channels talking about God — and forget what our brothers and sisters are going through around the world. While we watch our kids bicker over the Xbox, it's easy to forget there are people ripping Bibles apart so they can add a few more pages to their congregation's precious collection of Scripture. People being threatened, manipulated, and tortured to get them to forsake the one true God. It's so easy to drive to church on Sunday, while in much of the world, people have to sneak in and meet in the dark for safety. We are so blessed in this country.

Kenny recently read *The Insanity of God*, and I have ordered a copy of the DVD — it should be here in December at some point. I'll let you know in advance. It's PG-13 but it's heartbreaking and challenging at the same time. Well worth watching. When it comes in, I'll let Dottie know and we'll plan on running a little late that Saturday night.

---

So the nations are raging — against each other, but mostly against the church. Against the name of Jesus. We shouldn't be surprised. Jesus said there would be rejection. Paul said the name of Jesus is foolishness to those who don't believe. John said that apart from a work of Christ, a lost person cannot comprehend the things of the Lord. So we shouldn't be surprised when lost people are elected to lead our country. That sort of thing is coming.

People, we haven't looked at Revelation yet — but when you do, you'll notice something striking. Russia is mentioned, the nations of the Middle East are mentioned — but there is not one single reference to America or to the West in the entirety of the book that talks about end times. *(Note: The identification of Russia in Revelation, typically via Gog and Magog, is a widely held pre-millennial interpretive position but is contested by other evangelical scholars. It is accurate to present as one view, not as settled fact.)* So when that time comes, America is not a major player. That could be due to a number of things. But the point is: to get from here to there, a collapse must take place. Things are going to get worse before they get better. This persecution we see now is but a spark. One day gasoline will be poured on that spark. But praise God — he will be there to take care of us.

---

Now let's look at God's response. Verses 4 through 6.

There are a few places in Scripture that describe God as a potter and you and I as his creation. If you made a pot, and this created object — that can't do anything important on its own — started telling you it was going to plot your demise, I think you might laugh too. Or closer to home: if your three-year-old came up to you and said he was going to bring you to ruin. Imagine little Augie or Violet walking up here and saying that. You'd laugh — because at least in part you know they lack the ability to follow through. They lack full comprehension of everything that would be required to bring you to total ruin. And many things — like your faith in God — even if they were bigger, they simply couldn't destroy.

I like God's response here. When you were a kid and you were in trouble, what did your parents use to discipline you? A wooden spoon? My parents tried to make us go bring back switches from outside. But we caught on pretty quick and brought back little twigs that would break too easily. That just got us more trouble. We were trying to undermine mom's planned punishment — but all it brought us was a worse one.

Our God is big, and his wrath is a terrible thing. Jonathan Edwards, in his famous sermon *Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God*, wrote:

> "There is no Want of Power in God to cast wicked Men into Hell at any Moment. Mens Hands can't be strong when God rises up: The strongest have no Power to resist him, nor can any deliver out of his Hands. They are as great Heaps of light Chaff before the Whirlwind; or large Quantities of dry Stubble before devouring Flames. We find it easy to tread on and crush a Worm that we see crawling on the Earth; so 'tis easy for us to cut or singe a slender Thread that any Thing hangs by; thus easy is it for God when he pleases to cast his Enemies down to Hell. What are we, that we should think to stand before him, at whose Rebuke the Earth trembles, and before whom the Rocks are thrown down?"

Their struggle against God is so incredibly futile.

We're like ants running to and from a hill. Our strongest resistance to God is what an ant can muster against my shoe. It may try — but the results are terminal. For us the results are eternal. In seeking to rage against God, people are choosing to spend an eternity separate from him. They are choosing an eternity in Hell. An eternity as the ant under the shoe. Unlike the ant that finds some solace in death — for people, Hell is forever.

---

If you have never come to a point in your spiritual life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sin, do so tonight. We are not promised tomorrow. Apart from Christ, in our sin, we are no better than those who rage against God — no stronger than the ant under the shoe. No less lost, and intentionally headed for ruin.

Let me ask you this a different way. If something happened right now, and you and I were standing in front of the gates of Heaven, and God walked up to you and said, *"Why should I let you into my heaven?"* — what would you say? Could you answer that? If not, come talk to me tonight. If you can answer it — and you said anything other than "because Jesus Christ is my Lord and Savior, because I've confessed with my mouth the Lord Jesus Christ and believed in my heart that God raised him from the dead" — then come talk to me.

Maybe you've done that, and you're ready to follow through in believer's baptism. Come talk to me.

Maybe you're ready to join the church and have a say in our direction. Come talk to me.

---

## Scripture Text — Psalm 2:1-12 (ESV)

*(LORD corrected in vv. 2, 7, 11 — these use YHWH in Hebrew. V. 4 uses Adonai, so "Lord" is correct there.)*

> Why do the nations rage
> and the peoples plot in vain?
> 2 The kings of the earth set themselves,
> and the rulers take counsel together,
> against the **LORD** and against his Anointed, saying,
> 3 "Let us burst their bonds apart
> and cast away their cords from us."
> 4 He who sits in the heavens laughs;
> the Lord holds them in derision.
> 5 Then he will speak to them in his wrath,
> and terrify them in his fury, saying,
> 6 "As for me, I have set my King
> on Zion, my holy hill."
> 7 I will tell of the decree:
> The **LORD** said to me, "You are my Son;
> today I have begotten you.
> 8 Ask of me, and I will make the nations your heritage,
> and the ends of the earth your possession.
> 9 You shall break them with a rod of iron
> and dash them in pieces like a potter's vessel."
> 10 Now therefore, O kings, be wise;
> be warned, O rulers of the earth.
> 11 Serve the **LORD** with fear,
> and rejoice with trembling.
> 12 Kiss the Son,
> lest he be angry, and you perish in the way,
> for his wrath is quickly kindled.
> Blessed are all who take refuge in him.
