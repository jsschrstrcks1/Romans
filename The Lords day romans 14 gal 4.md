### People Group of the Week: Meo of Pakistan

The Meo people of Pakistan number over a million, and yet not one single believer is known among them today. The Meo follow Muslim practices and customs, but until the partition of 1947 they heavily mixed their beliefs with Hindu teachings. At that time they had a resurgence of Islamic traditions.

Even though they consider themselves to be from the well-respected Rajput community, some consider the Meo to be of a lower caste. Though Pakistani society tries to eliminate caste because of its Hindu roots, it still affects people in this Muslim country.

**Ministry Obstacles**: The Meo people have a long history as Hindus, but their identity is in Islam. They live in Pakistan, a closed Muslim country where the gospel is not welcome. Christians can be heavily persecuted there, and Muslim neighbors can intimidate Meo people who might look outside of Islam for spiritual answers. Please pray for opportunities for the gospel.

Let's read the Baptist Faith and Message statement for the week:

### Which Day?

This is a question that has been asked of me a fair bit lately. There are certain denominations for whom meeting any other day than Saturday is sacrosanct — they believe there is something right and pure and holy in maintaining the day of rest the Jews would have observed, and that to do otherwise is to be stepping into sin.

Keeping the Sabbath, as you may have heard it called, is a concept that goes all the way back to the beginning. The Triune God worked for six days and took the seventh day off. Does an omnipotent God need to take a break? I'm sure it takes a lot of power to speak the universe and everything in it into existence — but it's not like Jesus needed a nap.

I need naps sometimes. We spent this past week at Indian Rocks Beach — we had a great time. Thank you for allowing and enabling me to go. I went for a walk pretty much every morning, followed by a swim, and then later enjoyed the wonders of an afternoon nap. My favorite day was Tuesday when we went down to Caspersen Beach and played in the surf and collected fossilized shark's teeth. By the time we got home we were all exhausted and needed a nap. But I am just a mere man — not supernatural, never all-powerful. I went and played for a few hours and after that I was ready for a break.

Does God need that? Certainly not. So if an omnipotent, eternal, all-powerful God rested for a day when he didn't need it — why would he rest? He was setting an example for us, because we aren't omnipotent or eternal. We're weak and poor and broken. Right from the start, the Sabbath — the day of rest, or as we observe it today, Sunday — is for us. Not us for Sundays.

> "And he said to them, 'The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath.'"
> — Mark 2:27 (ESV)

### Romans 14:1–12

Let's open God's Word. Romans 14:1–12.

This whole chapter is about subjects that Scripture doesn't say much about. Some things have explicit condemnation in both the Old and New Testaments. But some things are only condemned in the Old Testament, or weren't even addressed because they were outside the scope of the technology of the day. If someone asked where the Bible says we have to obey speed limits when driving a car — obviously there were no cars when the Bible was written. So we have to listen to the counsel of Scripture where it is less explicit. This passage gives general guidelines about what to do when a thing is not expressly prohibited — things like the freedom to eat certain foods, or the freedom regarding which day to meet for church. I'm glad we have freedom to eat certain foods, because I really like seafood.

But what is Paul saying? He's asking us not to condemn anyone on either side of the argument. Those who are hung up on the idea of church being on a specific day or time — Paul calls these the weaker brothers. The people Paul was addressing seem to be tied up in a form of legalism. Then and now, not much has changed. Then and now the root was the same: taking things not in Scripture as seriously as Scripture itself.

Anything extrabiblical — and we all know extrabiblical means something that's not in Scripture — anything extrabiblical leads you to trouble. We as Baptists have done this. There was a time that being a Baptist meant you must always wear a tie, or that dancing was a sin. Now, there could be times when you should wear a tie, and there are dances that could be sinful. But is it always sinful to skip the tie? Certainly not. Is it always sinful to dance? No. Yet that was the battleground for a long time in our denomination. We were adding to Scripture.

I read a description of such people that said: "They were not yet convinced that God's grace in Christ had completely freed them from following the law. Still, both groups were to fully welcome the other."

*[Note: Source unknown — reads as a commentary or study Bible note. Attribution needed before print use.]*

People who get tangled up in legalism are no less saved than you or I. They just have a weaker faith. They are still growing, still developing. You wouldn't expect a six-year-old to grasp the same things a twenty-year-old could grasp easily. Everyone is on their own journey and matures at a different pace.

Paul applies that same idea in our passage this morning. As one Bible teacher puts it: "Some suggest that Roman Christians were still following cultural religious ideas, which included a belief that some days were lucky while others were not. It is more likely, however, that Paul is thinking about Jewish feast days and Sabbath days."

### Galatians 4:8–20

This discussion reminds me of Galatians 4, starting in verse 8. Paul writes:

> Formerly, when you did not know God, you were enslaved to those that by nature are not gods. But now that you have come to know God, or rather to be known by God, how can you turn back again to the weak and worthless elementary principles of the world, whose slaves you want to be once more? You observe days and months and seasons and years! I am afraid I may have labored over you in vain.
>
> Brothers, I entreat you, become as I am, for I also have become as you are. You did me no wrong. You know it was because of a bodily ailment that I preached the gospel to you at first, and though my condition was a trial to you, you did not scorn or despise me, but received me as an angel of God, as Christ Jesus. What then has become of your blessedness? For I testify to you that, if possible, you would have gouged out your eyes and given them to me. Have I then become your enemy by telling you the truth? They make much of you, but for no good purpose. They want to shut you out, that you may make much of them. It is always good to be made much of for a good purpose, and not only when I am present with you, my little children, for whom I am again in the anguish of childbirth until Christ is formed in you! I wish I could be present with you now and change my tone, for I am perplexed about you.
>
> — Galatians 4:8–20 (ESV)

Paul goes on to condemn them for their legalism and the hypocrisy that flows from it — their observance of seasons and days and months and years. Paul seemed to feel the Galatians were trusting their religious rule-keeping to save them instead of trusting God's grace through faith in Christ.

John Owen wrote of the futility of legalism for salvation: "Those who seek to keep down sin without the aid of the Spirit labor in vain. They will combat without victory."

*[Note: Attributed to John Owen; consistent with* Of the Mortification of Sin in Believers *(1656). Exact wording needs verification before print use.]*

Legalism is a battle that can never be won. You can never hope to live up to the standard the law sets — and that's why we have the law: so that we know just how far we fall short, so we have an accurate picture of who and where we are.

I saw a quote circulating on social media this week: "As a blind man is oblivious to the sun, so a lost man is oblivious to the full tally of his sin and to the perfect glory of God."

*[Note: No attribution in source; circulating online without identified author. Do not attribute to a specific person.]*

As we grow closer to the Lord, one of the benefits is that we gain a clearer picture of who we are compared to who God is. Said another way: the closer we become to God, the more sin we are able to see in ourselves. The more we see of God — his glory, his perfection — the more acutely aware of our own shortcomings we become.

When I was in Europe, we went to lots of different museums and saw all sorts of art. Everywhere you go, especially in Paris, someone has some kind of art on display — the latest song they've recorded, paintings on street corners, old masters.

We went into one museum that was full of Rembrandts. Rembrandt is not one of my personal favorite painters, but he is an old master and his work is revered by many. I saw one picture that was on loan to whatever museum we were at — a doctor giving an anatomy lesson. He had started to dissect a human and was describing and naming the muscles as he went in this corpse's arm.

*[Note: The described work matches Rembrandt's* The Anatomy Lesson of Dr. Nicolaes Tulp *(1632), Mauritshuis, The Hague. The sermon notes it was on loan; the exact painting cannot be confirmed from the description alone.]*

I don't know about you, but the best of my ability to paint on display next to a Rembrandt would be ridiculous. Rembrandt can render the individual textures one would see in the musculature of an arm. I can paint a stick figure with big red drops of blood.

Likewise is legalism to grace. God's grace is Rembrandt. Our best efforts at legalism are stick figure caricatures of holiness at best. Even those of us who keep the most rules the best — their righteousness is as rags. There is only one thing that saves us, and it is the sinless God-Man Jesus Christ.

Based on what we've read so far — do you think God is bothered by which day we meet on? Do you think he would have told Paul to write that in either Romans or Galatians if it were a serious issue?

I agree.

### Antinomianism: The Other Ditch

A related issue we need to discuss is antinomianism. Does anyone know what that means? A lot of people think it's the opposite of legalism, but in truth — according to Sinclair Ferguson — they are both the opposite of grace. Grace is the middle point on the spectrum between legalism and lawlessness. God's grace in Christ, in our union with Christ, is the antidote to both.

Ferguson continues: "The antinomian is by nature a person with a legalistic heart. He or she becomes an antinomian in reaction. But this implies only a different view of law, not a more biblical one."

*[Note: Both Ferguson quotations are consistent with* The Whole Christ *(2016, Crossway). Exact wording and page numbers need verification before print use.]*

Antinomianism is, in brief, the idea that the Christian can do anything he or she wants — whether Scripture prohibits it or not — because Christ has paid the price for ALL our sins. Antinomians can cheat on their wives — why not? Christ forgave it all! They can cheat on their taxes — who cares that Jesus said to give to Caesar what is Caesar's? And they base it all on passages like Romans 14. Free grace indeed. But is that what Paul was saying? Reject legalism and embrace moral anarchy?

Certainly not.

> "What shall we say then? Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means! How can we who died to sin still live in it?"
> — Romans 6:1–2 (ESV)

*[Note: The source manuscript attributes this to "Romans 12." The correct reference is Romans 6:1–2.]*

---

The middle point between legalism and lawless antinomianism is grace. We can't keep the whole law — that's impossible, and not expected. But we can abide by those things God convicts us of and pulls us away from.

Some of these things will be the same for everyone. Sexual sin, for instance — sex has a very specific set of prescriptions: inside marriage only, only with your one spouse, of the opposite gender. Not animals, not children, not the same sex. Twenty years ago that wouldn't have had to be explained, but our culture's convictions are changing and have abandoned their scriptural underpinnings.

Some things — like eating seafood or pork — are not terrible things to abstain from. They aren't going to save you, and abstaining from them isn't honoring God, but skipping bacon won't send you to hell. If someone feels they have to abstain, that's between them and God. I knew missionaries serving in sensitive contexts where a people who hated pork would just open your fridge and freezer and go through your meat. Those missionaries may have different convictions than we do, and that's okay. But does God prohibit pork? Certainly not. Nor is he especially concerned with whether we meet on Friday or Tuesday or Saturday or Sunday. Scripture says so. The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath.

We keep Sunday as a Protestant church because we believe Jesus was resurrected on a Sunday, and our meeting together is in celebration and remembrance of Christ's death on the cross, burial in the tomb, and resurrection from the grave. Through all of that we have the chance at a relationship with God — grace that covers our sins.

Shall we continue in sin that grace may abound? May it never be. *(Romans 6:1–2)*

But we can only work on things as God reveals them to us and aids us in our victory over them — or all of it is futile. If you're an alcoholic, that doesn't give you license to pick up the bottle again because you're feeling strong today. If you're prone to desiring a thing above all else, it's worth your while to put it down and run away from it. Your relationship with God is far more important than your next drink or your next hit of drugs — and if it isn't more important to you, then you are still lost and in need of a relationship with God.

A famous preacher once wrote:

> "This Antinomian principle, that it is needless for a man, perfectly justified by faith, to endeavour to keep the law, and do good works, is a glaring evidence that legality is so engrained in man's corrupt nature, that until a man truly come to Christ, by faith, the legal disposition will still be reigning in him; let him turn himself into what shape, or be of what principles he will in religion; though he run into Antinomianism he will carry along with him his legal spirit, which will always be a slavish and unholy spirit."

And: "None embrace one extreme whose hearts have not been steeped in the other."
