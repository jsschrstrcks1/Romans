## Sermon

I watched a presentation by a deceived group this week. I do not recommend them. In the past, when we've talked about the problems with various groups, what has been the main problem that started each group off on the wrong track? Anyone remember?

Yup, that's right. This group was no different. They were a little sneakier with their wording though, and so that is why we are going to discuss them today. One of the very first things this particular podcast had to say was that "I believe there are two ways we can know truth: the first is the Bible, and the second is science." So far so good, right? I mean, we know ultimate truth comes from God through Scripture, but science has led to some amazing things. The cure for cancer, or other crazy diseases. Science has led to television, the internet, and putting men on the moon. Science has led to some amazing things. So at first blush what this woman said sounds good.

But in listening to the podcast, she made it clear that she sees herself as the arbiter of truth, and Scripture as merely one source, and science as a co-equal source. So when there seemingly is a conflict between science and Scripture, it's up to her to decide what is truth or not.

See the issue? What's true doesn't depend on our deeming it so. For thousands of years science taught that the earth was flat. Scripture says clearly that the earth has a circular shape — "he who sits above the circle of the earth," as Isaiah 40:22 puts it. Whether or not some individual hundreds of years ago believed it to be true or not didn't change the fact that the earth was actually ball-shaped. Where we got into trouble was when we as a people began to believe things outside of Scripture, over Scripture itself.

We all on the same page? We don't have to believe a thing for it to be the truth. It is still the truth even if we don't recognize it as truth. We cannot be the arbiters of truth — only God can. Only God can decide what is true or not, and God has revealed all the truth he wants us to have in his Word.

But science — I love and enjoy science, and reading about scientific things. I love the advances science has brought. I especially love air conditioning and heat. I love the way science brought us the internet, and now we have greater access to information and to the science being conducted around the world. I have an acquaintance who is using science to cure a genetic disease carried by dalmatians that makes them die of kidney stones. Science is amazing. Science makes a huge difference in our world. But science is flawed because it is conducted by humans, and humans are flawed.

Consider with me the dietary sciences for a moment. First butter was bad, and margarine was good. Then margarine was bad, and butter was good — but only a little bit, because we don't want to clog your arteries. Now they say it's sugars that make the arteries hard. In other words, it wasn't the butter that was bad for you — it's the bread.

Back and forth it goes. This is good. No, this is bad. No, it's good. Then someone comes along and says — actually, it's neither. Science, if it could be done by people with no emotions, completely logically, impartially, without flaws, would be a much better thing. Even then, under ideal conditions it has its limitations. Science can't speak to many aspects of life. Science can't speak to morality. Science can't speak to history. Though born out of philosophy and religion — man's quest to know God — science can't speak to philosophy. Which means science can't handle religion either. Science can't tell you who won the Battle of Waterloo. It can't tell you if two people lived a thousand years ago, or what food they liked. An archaeologist once told me that archaeology is the process of gathering sticks and rocks and making up stories about them. Archaeology is called a science. But if it really is just making up stories, then it's more an art.

All that said. The lady from the podcast went on to argue a couple of points from science. The main thrust of her argument was that science must be right and the Bible must be wrong in Genesis because she trusts science over Scripture. The science involved was called radiocarbon dating. Before we get into that, let's read the Scriptures. **Genesis 1:26–31.**

*(See scripture block above.)*

The entirety of her argument is a house of cards built on a single piece of science that she trusts over Scripture. That science is radiocarbon dating, or carbon-14 dating.

It's sort of like opening a refrigerator and checking out the milk to see when someone went grocery shopping last. If the milk isn't bad, it was probably within a week or two. If it's started to curdle, it's been longer. And if it's also growing mold, it's been a really long time.

In radiocarbon dating, they look at one of several different kinds of molecule, and then use the amount of radiation it is emitting to judge its age. Kind of like the battery meter on your phone. If it's new, the radiation will be fully charged. If it's not, then it will be weaker. Eventually it has no radiation at all.

Carbon is one of the elements they look at. The radiation in carbon has a half-life of just under 6,000 years. A half-life of 6,000 years means that carbon, after 6,000 years, is half as radioactive as it was to start with. After 12,000 years it's one-quarter as radioactive. After 18,000 years it's one-eighth. See the pattern? So it turns out that after about 60,000 years, carbon no longer has any detectable levels of radiation. Yet if you have ever watched the History or Discovery Channel, you'll have heard them say they tested this rock, or that sample, and determined by carbon-14 dating that the rock or thing is hundreds of millions of years old.

Turns out, rather than using science they are guessing. In the void of facts, they guess. They have a mathematical equation, predicated on the idea that the earth is billions of years old, that they use to guess.

Back to archaeology for a moment. Let's say the rock they are radiocarbon dating was next to a fossil from the Cretaceous period. They believe then that all the things they find around that fossil are going to date back to the Cretaceous period. The rock that they found near the fossil — they now believe it to be that old. So the first radiocarbon dating guys come in and say: oh, it's a Cretaceous rock? Well then it must be 100 million years old. So let's see how much radiation that rock gives off. Oh, only a tiny fraction of radiation. That means we should be able to expect all rocks and fossils with carbon-14 in them that are 100 million years old to have that same amount of radiation. So even though it doesn't really line up with what we would expect for radiation half-life, let's find some math that bridges the gap between these sets of facts. Basically — they guessed. They did it again with other fossil layers. There are more problems than just that they guessed, though.

Let's look at the radioactive decay to start with. For one thing, based on what we know now of radiation, it decays at a fairly fixed rate. There are a few ways to slow that down. If a biological organism consumes a radioactive element, it for some reason slows down the rate of decay. Depending on what radioactive thing was eaten, it will probably kill the creature quickly, and then resume decay at normal speeds. Also, if you have a billion-dollar laboratory, you can speed it up a fraction. Atoms are sort of like a planet being circled by a bunch of moons. Each moon is an electron, and if you have the ability to hit an electron with another electron you can make one break orbit, resulting in a slight bit of radiation decay. It's incredibly complicated, and if you don't do it right you end up with a nuclear explosion. Sunlight also has a slight speed-up effect on decay. But again, if we're talking about carbon-14 — with a half-life of 6,000 years — bombard it with solar radiation and the half-life becomes 5,600 years instead of 6,000. Which is precious little change when you're trying to get to millions and billions of years. In practice, what this means is carbon on the surface of the earth can be reliably tested to about 56,000 years, and carbon under the surface of the earth can be tested to about 60,000. Not 600,000. Not 6 billion. 60,000.

Let me ask you this, though. If the earth came to be as the result of a Big Bang roughly 14 billion years ago — as the mainstream scientific account would have it — wouldn't all sources of carbon have the same level of radiation? Being a newly formed carbon molecule, you would expect it to be fully charged. With a lifespan of only 60,000 years under ideal circumstances, if the world were that old, wouldn't you expect to find all carbon completely depleted? Yet it's not. To date we have been unable to slow the decay of carbon enough to account for this discrepancy even when we duplicate things as exactly as humanly possible.

Secondly, and perhaps most importantly, if all the carbon just on earth were to be fully radioactive tomorrow, there would be so much radiation that life on earth would be impossible. Everyone would die.

If life and our planet were the random happening of an impersonal universe, none of this makes sense.

If, however, an intelligent creative being orchestrated everything, then these things make sense. An organized, intelligent being isn't going to create life that can't survive in an overly radioactive environment. He's going to create a world that has an appropriate level of radiation.

Anyway, the podcast lady's second argument is based on the first. Because she believes the carbon-14 results over Scripture, she believes that what they see in genetics confirms the evolutionary timeline rather than what Scripture says. Based on her argument, in order for there to have been just two people who started it all, mankind would have had to be on the earth longer than a million years, which she says just isn't possible. However, if the carbon dates are wrong, the rest of it falls apart. If everything happened in the last 5,000–10,000 years, it's all highly possible. Far more likely, too.

I read an article a while back about a paleontologist — a scientist who studies dinosaur bones — Dr. Mary Schweitzer, working at North Carolina State University, who accidentally came across soft tissue in a *Tyrannosaurus rex* bone: flexible blood vessels, cell-like structures, still present after all this time.

It throws everything else science has said about the age of the earth into question. Because if that dinosaur died 68 million years ago, it is chemically implausible for soft tissue to still be intact. The prevailing scientific view before this find was that organic molecules in fossils should not survive beyond roughly 100,000 years under normal conditions. The find was published in *Science* in 2005 and the scientific community spent years trying to explain it. Yet the evolutionary timeline requires us to believe a bone sitting outside for tens of millions of years still has uncorrupted tissue inside. Impossible.

There are too many things that science cannot explain using its current paradigm — a paradigm that assumes blind natural forces assembled life and existence.

At the end of the day, Scripture is not a science textbook. But where and when it has spoken to issues of science, it has been accurate. Scripture was never meant to unlock all the secrets of the mechanics of the universe. God left that for us to figure out, and science is our tool for doing so. It's great at what it does. But when we elevate it — or our understanding of it — above God's Word to us, then we have trouble.

Scripture's goal, and the original goal of science, was so that we could know God better. So that we could have a relationship with him. So we could better understand the God who created us and the universe we live in.

All of that starts with a relationship with God, and that starts with the Gospel.
