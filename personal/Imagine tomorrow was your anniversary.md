### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **The anniversary illustration is genuinely well-chosen.** The roses-and-duty scenario is accessible, emotionally immediate, and does illuminate the distinction between obligatory and passionate worship. 2. **The theological instinct behind the fragment is sound.** The underlying burden — that worship should flow from genuine love shaped by the knowledge of God's grace, not from duty — is a genuinely pastoral concern and a worthy subject for a sermon. The problem is that it is not yet a sermon.

3. **The Packer quotations are well-chosen.** Both selections from *Knowing God* are substantive and doctrinally rich. ### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **There is no Scripture passage.** This is not a minor gap — it is a categorical absence. A sermon without a text is not a sermon. The most urgent need before anything else is the identification of a governing passage from which the whole argument about love for God and worship flows.

2. **The drowning-captain illustration is theologically misleading.** The preacher says that if a captain orders a life ring thrown to you, you are grateful but do not automatically love the captain. This is then applied to loving Christ. But this analogy distorts the gospel: in the gospel, the captain does not merely order the ring thrown — he jumps in and drowns in your place, and his death is what pulls you to shore. The nature of the love we owe Christ is not merely the gratitude of a rescued passenger; it is the awe of one who was loved with a dying love (Romans 5:8, Galatians 2:20). The illustration undersells the cross and inadvertently makes love for Christ seem like a natural development of familiarity over time rather than the Spirit-wrought response to substitutionary grace.

3. **The fragment ends without arriving anywhere.** The two Packer quotations appear at the end with no framing, no development, and no connection to a conclusion. The reader (or listener) does not know what they are meant to do with them. There is no landing.

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher may not see that the illustration's logic — “love grows with time and effort” — sits in tension with the Reformed doctrine that genuine love for God is a fruit of regeneration and is given, not cultivated from neutral ground. The passage hints at synergism (make an effort, spend time, and love will grow) in a way that could mislead the person who has no regenerate love for God to begin with. The Packer quotations on grace and perseverance actually correct this tension — but because they are not developed, the tension remains unresolved.

There is also a blind spot around the difference between a sermon illustration and a sermon. The illustration is the strongest piece of writing in the fragment, and it may give the impression that the sermon is further along than it is.

### 14. Surgical Fixes (5)

1. **Assign a governing text before anything else.** The most natural candidates for this sermon's thesis (love God out of passion, not obligation) include: 1 John 4:19 (“We love because he first loved us”), Deuteronomy 6:4-5 (the Shema), John 21:15-17 (Jesus and Peter on love), or Revelation 2:4 (the church at Ephesus that lost its first love). Choose one. Build everything from it. The illustration and the Packer quotes then become servants of the text.

2. **Replace the drowning-captain illustration with a cross-centered one.** The captain analogy undersells the gospel. Consider developing the idea differently: the rescuer did not merely order help — he took your place in the water. That is why the love owed is not merely gratitude but worshipful awe. The Cross is not a favor; it is the Son of God absorbing the wrath you deserved. Love that flows from that knowledge is categorically different from affection that grows through familiarity.

3. **Develop the Packer grace quotation in the preacher's own voice.** Do not quote and move on. Take the Packer definition of grace and preach it: What does it mean that God moved heaven and earth? What did it cost? What did we deserve? What did Christ bear? Only when the congregation feels the weight of what grace actually is will the call to love-not-obligation land with force.

4. **Add differentiated address.** The fragment speaks to one type of person: the church attender going through the motions. But who else is in the room? The person who genuinely does not love God and is afraid to admit it. The new believer whose love is real but small. The person who has grown cold. The person who mistakes emotion for love. Each needs a different word.

5. **End with an exhortation, not a quotation.** The sermon (once built) should end with the preacher's own voice issuing a direct call: “This week, do not merely perform worship. Pursue God. Open the Word to know him. Pray not to fulfill a duty but to be with your Savior. Come to the Table not out of habit but because you know what it cost.” The Packer quote is excellent supporting material — it should not carry the close.
