# A Bibliography for Teaching Missions in the Local Church
## Twenty-Five Annotated Entries

*Compiled for a local church missions class. Per the assignment guidelines, dual-purpose books — those with value in both foreign and domestic missions — have been given priority in selection and annotation. See End Notes for flagged bibliographic concerns.*

---

### 1.
Greenway, Roger S., ed. *Guidelines for Urban Church Planting*. Grand Rapids: Baker Book House, 1976. 76 pp.

This concise volume examines five church planting models drawn from three continents and multiple denominational traditions, distilling the transferable principles that account for their effectiveness across vastly different cultural settings. Case studies include the Free Methodist Church in São Paulo, the Mennonite Brethren in Osaka, the Assemblies of God in Nairobi, the Christian and Missionary Alliance in Lima, and the Christian Reformed Board of Foreign Missions in Mexico City and Africa. Greenway's central argument — that the city represents the primary missionary frontier of the modern era — was prescient in 1976 and has only grown more urgent. Its cross-denominational, cross-continental breadth makes it equally applicable for those pursuing urban church planting in North American cities as for those working internationally.

---

### 2.
Snyder, Howard A. *The Problem of Wineskins: Church Structure in a Technological Age*. Downers Grove, Illinois: Inter-Varsity Press, 1975. 214 pp.

Snyder's argument — that the church's uncritical embrace of technology can impede rather than advance the gospel — takes on fresh relevance in every generation, regardless of the specific technologies under discussion. Though the 1975 context anchors the book's examples, the underlying questions Snyder raises about the relationship between method and message remain pressing. His analysis carries particular weight in missionary contexts involving animist or pre-technological worldviews, where technological mediation may erect barriers rather than bridges. The book applies equally well to domestic ministry, making it a genuinely dual-purpose resource for a missions class that aims to develop thoughtful, culturally attentive practitioners.

---

### 3.
Richardson, Don. *Peace Child*. Glendale, California: Regal Books, 1974. 287 pp.

*Peace Child* stands as one of the most celebrated missionary narratives of the twentieth century. Don Richardson and his wife Carol recount their years among the Sawi people of Irian Jaya — a cannibalistic tribe among whom treachery was a cultural virtue — and their search for a bridge through which the gospel could travel into the Sawi worldview. That bridge proved to be the indigenous custom of the "peace child," an act of sacrifice by which warring tribes would seal peace — a cultural analogy of extraordinary depth and power. Richardson later developed this discovery into the broader missiological concept of "redemptive analogies." This book is essential reading for any Christian engaged in cross-cultural ministry, demonstrating both the depth of human fallenness and the faithfulness of God to prepare the ground for the gospel in every culture.

---

### 4.
Miller, William M. *A Christian's Response to Islam*. Nutley, New Jersey: Presbyterian and Reformed Publishing Co., 1976. 178 pp.

Written by a man who spent more than four decades serving as a missionary among Muslim peoples, this volume offers both a reliable general overview of Islamic belief and practice and a clear-eyed comparison with Christian doctrine. Miller's longevity in the field lends his analysis a pastoral and practical depth that more academic treatments of Islam rarely achieve. His closing argument — that Western missionaries should not continue to bypass Muslim-majority nations in favor of more receptive fields — carries the weight of firsthand conviction and decades of personal cost. Given the growing presence of Muslim communities in North American cities, this book serves a meaningful dual purpose: preparing students for missionary service in Muslim-majority countries while equipping local churches to engage Muslim neighbors at home.

---

### 5.
Hasler, Richard A. *Journey with David Brainerd*. Morgan, Pennsylvania: Soli Deo Gloria, 2002. 120 pp.

David Brainerd's journal has shaped the missionary vision of the Western church for nearly three centuries, having deeply influenced figures from John Wesley to Jim Elliot. This volume draws substantially from those journal entries, making Brainerd's spiritual intensity and perseverance accessible to modern readers without requiring engagement with the full journal. For a local church missions class, the biographical and devotional dimensions of this book serve a function that theological argument alone cannot: before students can understand the theology of missions, they often need to encounter the fire of it in a human life. Brainerd's story — marked by physical suffering, relentless faith, and remarkable fruit among the Native American peoples of New Jersey and Pennsylvania — provides exactly that encounter.

---

### 6.
Johnstone, Patrick. *Operation World*. 5th ed. Grand Rapids: Zondervan Publishing House, 1993. 662 pp.

No missions library is complete without *Operation World*. Organized by country, this comprehensive prayer guide equips believers to intercede with specificity and purpose for every nation on earth, providing demographic and religious statistics, a summary of each country's Christian witness and gospel need, and focused prayer points. For a local church missions class, this volume is ideally suited for opening each session in prayer — moving students from abstract concern for "the world" to concrete, name-bearing intercession for specific peoples and places. For those sensing a missionary call, the country profiles serve as an initial compass, helping to clarify and confirm the direction of God's leading toward specific nations or peoples.

---

### 7.
Yohannan, K. P. *Revolution in World Missions*. Carrollton, Texas: Gospel for Asia, 2007. 232 pp.

K. P. Yohannan's impassioned case for the indigenous missionary movement has made this one of the most widely read missions books of the past generation. His argument — that the most effective and cost-efficient use of Western missionary resources is the support of national workers already embedded within their own cultures — is both statistically credible and theologically grounded. Instructors should use this book with intentionality: its strong emphasis on indigenous missionaries over foreign workers may discourage some students from pursuing a calling to which God has genuinely summoned them. Used carefully, however, it functions as a valuable foil, ensuring that those who do pursue foreign missionary service do so out of authentic calling rather than romantic idealism. It also opens an important conversation about the ways every believer can participate in global missions through financial support of national workers.

---

### 8.
Akin, Daniel L. *10 Who Changed the World*. Nashville: B&H Publishing Group, 2012. 188 pp.

Daniel Akin profiles ten individuals whom God used in extraordinary ways across the history of Christian missions — among them Adoniram and Ann Judson, Jim Elliot, Lottie Moon, and William Carey — drawing out the theological convictions and personal qualities that mark their legacies. Written in an accessible, pastoral style suited to a local church context, this volume resists hagiography while honoring genuine heroism. For a missions class, biographical accounts carry a power that theological argument alone cannot: they place a human face on the Great Commission and make the call to missions feel not merely possible but urgent. *10 Who Changed the World* accomplishes this with efficiency and grace.

---

### 9.
Hale, Thomas. *On Being a Missionary*. Pasadena, California: William Carey Library, 1995. 422 pp.

Drawing on the testimony of more than one hundred career missionaries alongside his own decades of field experience in Nepal, Thomas Hale provides a comprehensive, realistic, and deeply pastoral guide to the full arc of missionary life — from the initial call through the daily realities of sustained cross-cultural service. *On Being a Missionary* is honest about the difficulties without being discouraging to those who are genuinely called, and gently realistic about the gap between missionary romanticism and field reality. For a local church missions class, this book provides essential practical grounding, balancing the inspirational energy of biographical reading with a clear-eyed understanding of what long-term missionary service actually demands.

---

### 10.
Garrison, David. *Church Planting Movements: How God Is Redeeming a Lost World*. Bangalore, India: WIGTake Resources, 2004. 355 pp.

David Garrison's foundational study of rapid, reproducing church planting movements around the world makes the case that the Great Commission is fulfilled not through isolated conversions but through self-sustaining, multiplying communities of disciples. His research, drawn from movements across multiple continents and religions, identifies the common characteristics that account for explosive growth and long-term durability. The implications extend well beyond foreign missions: any missional strategy — domestic or international — that does not build multiplication into its DNA from the outset is unlikely to produce lasting Kingdom impact. This book is essential for anyone thinking seriously about church planting as a vehicle for gospel advance, and serves equally well in a local or international missions context.

---

### 11.
Platt, David. *Radical Together: Unleashing the People of God for the Purpose of God*. Colorado Springs: Multnomah Books, 2011. 166 pp.

Where Platt's earlier *Radical* challenged individual believers to surrender personal comfort to Kingdom priorities, *Radical Together* extends that challenge to the local church as a body. Platt argues that a congregation fully surrendered to Christ will necessarily be a congregation on mission — locally, nationally, and globally — and that the church's tendency to prioritize institutional comfort and internal programming over costly, outward-facing engagement with a lost world is a form of corporate disobedience. This book functions well in a missions class as a call to communal obedience, reframing missions not as the special interest of spiritually ambitious individuals but as the baseline identity of any church that takes the Lordship of Christ seriously.

---

### 12.
Rankin, Jerry. *Empowering Kingdom Growth to the Ends of the Earth*. Richmond, Virginia: International Mission Board, 2005. 207 pp.

Writing from his tenure as president of the International Mission Board of the Southern Baptist Convention, Jerry Rankin traces the history of Southern Baptist missionary effort, surveys its current scope and methodology, and challenges individual believers to engage personally in the ongoing work of Kingdom expansion. Though rooted in a Southern Baptist institutional perspective, the book's core argument — that every believer has a role to play in the missionary enterprise — transcends denominational boundaries. For a local church missions class, it offers both a model of organized, sustained missionary effort across generations and a compelling personal challenge: the Great Commission is not a denominational program but a command issued to the whole church.

---

### 13.
Elliot, Elisabeth. *A Chance to Die: The Life and Legacy of Amy Carmichael*. Tarrytown, New York: Fleming H. Revell Co., 1987. 382 pp.

Elisabeth Elliot — herself one of the defining missionary figures of the twentieth century — brings to Amy Carmichael's story both biographical skill and the authority of personal experience. Carmichael's fifty-three years of uninterrupted service in India, her founding of the Dohnavur Fellowship, and her rescue of children from temple prostitution constitute one of the most remarkable missionary legacies in the history of the church. That her story is told by Elisabeth Elliot — whose own missionary journey included the martyrdom of her husband Jim Elliot at the hands of the Waorani people, and her subsequent return to serve among that same tribe — adds another layer of grace and gravity to the reading. This book will encourage every student in a missions class, with particular resonance for women who may be questioning whether there is a place for them in the work of global mission.

---

### 14.
Piper, John. *Don't Waste Your Life*. Wheaton, Illinois: Crossway, 2003. 191 pp.

Piper's challenge to meaningless living is rooted in his conviction that God is most glorified when his people are most fully satisfied in him — and that a life oriented around that satisfaction will inevitably be a life poured out in service to others and the advance of the gospel. The book functions less as a missions manual than as a philosophical and theological framework for why missions matters at all: if eternity is real, if souls are at stake, then the accumulation of personal comfort and earthly security for its own sake is not merely unwise but tragic. For a missions class, *Don't Waste Your Life* serves as a clarifying lens, pressing students to ask the right prior question before they discuss strategy or calling: *Why does any of this matter?*

---

### 15.
Finley, Bob. *Reformation in Foreign Missions*. Charlottesville, Virginia: Christian Aid Mission, 2010. 285 pp.

Bob Finley's provocative argument — that foreign missionaries should ideally be deployed only to nations whose primary language they already speak — functions best in a missions class as a well-reasoned foil rather than a final word. While the absolute version of his thesis creates significant practical problems (full language acquisition typically requires field immersion, and for many of the world's languages the resources for pre-field study are simply unavailable), Finley's underlying concern is sound: missionaries who arrive inadequately prepared linguistically impose a significant burden on themselves and on the communities they serve. This book generates productive discussion about language preparation, stewardship of missionary resources, and the realistic expectations churches should set for those they commission and send.

---

### 16.
Piper, John. *Let the Nations Be Glad: The Supremacy of God in Missions*. 3rd ed. Grand Rapids: Baker Academic, 2010. 279 pp.

*Let the Nations Be Glad* has established itself as the most theologically rigorous and personally compelling introductory text in evangelical missiology. Piper's central argument — that missions exists because worship does not, and that the goal of all missionary labor is the universal, joyful praise of God among every people — reframes the entire enterprise of cross-cultural gospel work in properly doxological terms. The book addresses prayer, suffering, the exclusivity of Christ, and the strategic priority of reaching unreached peoples with equal seriousness and depth. For a local church missions class, this volume sets the theological foundation upon which everything else in the curriculum should be built, ensuring that students understand not merely what the church is called to do in missions but why God has staked his glory upon it.

---

### 17.
Zaka, Anees, and Bruce McDowell. *Muslims and Christians at the Table: Promoting Biblical Understanding Among North American Muslims*. Phillipsburg, New Jersey: P&R Publishing, 1999. 325 pp.

Anees Zaka and Bruce McDowell offer a practically grounded, culturally informed approach to Muslim evangelism centered on the hospitality and relational values that are foundational to Middle Eastern and South Asian cultures. Zaka's firsthand account of using shared meals and sustained relational trust-building as the primary vehicle for gospel witness — an approach that resulted in conversions reaching even to mosque leadership — lends the book credibility that purely theoretical treatments of Muslim evangelism rarely achieve. The methodology is readily transferable to domestic contexts, making this an excellent resource for churches with Muslim neighbors, coworkers, or community members. It pairs naturally with Zaka and Coleman's *The Truth About Islam* for a more complete treatment of both theological content and relational approach.

---

### 18.
Moreau, A. Scott. *Contextualization in World Missions: Mapping and Assessing Evangelical Models*. Grand Rapids: Kregel Academic, 2012. 430 pp.

Contextualization — the careful process of expressing the gospel and forming the local church in ways that are genuinely indigenous to the host culture rather than artifacts of the missionary's home culture — is one of the defining missiological challenges of our era. Moreau's comprehensive treatment surveys the major theoretical frameworks, examines historical case studies across multiple traditions, and provides practical guidance for navigating the complex questions that arise when the gospel crosses cultural boundaries. For students preparing for international missions, this book provides the conceptual tools needed to avoid both uncritical cultural accommodation and the unnecessary imposition of foreign cultural forms on the emerging church. Its principles apply with equal force to cross-cultural ministry in increasingly diverse North American contexts.

---

### 19.
Elmer, Duane. *Cross-Cultural Connections: Stepping Out and Fitting In Around the World*. Downers Grove, Illinois: IVP Academic, 2002. 215 pp.

Elmer's accessible and practical guide to cross-cultural communication and adjustment addresses the human realities that derail many missionaries before they ever reach their primary work. From the mechanics of culture shock to the subtleties of interpersonal communication across cultural lines, *Cross-Cultural Connections* prepares readers to navigate the relational and psychological challenges of life in a foreign context with grace and self-awareness. Importantly, the principles Elmer develops are not limited to overseas service: any believer working with immigrant communities, international students, or ethnically diverse neighbors will find this book equally applicable. It deserves a prominent place in the curriculum as a bridge between theoretical missiology and the practical demands of cross-cultural life.

---

### 20.
Hesselgrave, David J. *Communicating Christ Cross-Culturally: An Introduction to Missionary Communication*. 2nd ed. Grand Rapids: Zondervan Publishing House, 1991. 672 pp.

Hesselgrave's major work on cross-cultural communication remains one of the most thorough treatments of the subject available. Organizing his analysis around seven dimensions of culture — worldview, cognitive process, linguistic form, behavioral pattern, social structure, media influence, and motivational resources — Hesselgrave equips the reader to understand not merely *what* the gospel is but *how* it must be communicated differently across different cultural contexts. The book's considerable length reflects genuine depth rather than redundancy: each dimension receives the careful, nuanced treatment that the complexity of cross-cultural communication demands. For a missions class, it is best used selectively, with instructors assigning key chapters alongside shorter and more accessible texts, reserving the full volume for those pursuing advanced preparation for the field.

---

### 21.
Zaka, Anees, and Diane Coleman. *The Truth About Islam: The Noble Qur'an's Teachings in Light of the Holy Bible*. Phillipsburg, New Jersey: P&R Publishing, 2004. 196 pp.

Zaka and Coleman provide a systematic comparison of Islamic and Christian teaching across key theological categories, drawing directly from the Qur'an and the Bible to identify both genuine points of contact and fundamental divergences. The book is designed to equip Christians for substantive, respectful engagement with Muslim friends and neighbors — not polemical debate, but honest theological dialogue that takes Islamic belief seriously while bearing faithful witness to the claims of Christ. It serves as a natural companion to *Muslims and Christians at the Table*, providing the theological content that undergirds the relational approach Zaka describes in that volume. For a local church missions class, this book prepares students for an increasingly important form of domestic cross-cultural witness.

---

### 22.
Koning, Otto. *The Pineapple Story*. Oak Brook, Illinois: Institute in Basic Life Principles, 2012. 40 pp.

Otto Koning's account of his years as a missionary in New Guinea — and the unexpected way in which a dispute over stolen pineapples became the instrument God used to break his pride and unlock the gospel among the Dani people — is one of the most disarming and memorable missionary stories in the evangelical tradition. Brief enough to be read in a single sitting, it is nonetheless a story that leaves a lasting impression, illustrating the sovereignty of God, the necessity of personal surrender, and the surprising ways the Spirit moves in cross-cultural contexts. For a missions class, it functions particularly well as a devotional or supplementary reading — a story that reaches the heart in ways that more analytical texts do not, and that students are likely to carry with them long after the class has ended.

---

### 23.
Malphurs, Aubrey. *Planting Growing Churches for the Twenty-First Century: A Comprehensive Guide for New Churches and Those Desiring Renewal*. 2nd ed. Grand Rapids: Baker Book House, 1998. 417 pp.

Malphurs provides a comprehensive, methodologically grounded introduction to church planting that is accessible to both the academic student and the ministry practitioner. Building on the conviction that evangelism without discipleship, and discipleship without a community to receive new believers, produces incomplete and short-lived results, he maps the planting process from initial vision through established congregation with characteristic thoroughness. As a medium-level introduction, this book is well-suited to a local church missions class, providing practical frameworks without overwhelming less academically inclined students. It pairs effectively with Hesselgrave's *Planting Churches Cross-Culturally* for students pursuing international or cross-cultural applications of church planting methodology.

---

### 24.
Hesselgrave, David J. *Planting Churches Cross-Culturally: North America and Beyond*. 2nd ed. Grand Rapids: Baker Book House, 2000. 462 pp.

Hesselgrave applies the analytical rigor of his communication work to the specific task of establishing gospel-rooted communities across cultural boundaries. Organizing the church planting process around what he identifies as the "Pauline Cycle" — ten sequential phases drawn from the missionary pattern of the Apostle Paul in Acts and the Epistles — Hesselgrave grounds his methodology explicitly in Scripture while engaging seriously with the practical and cultural complexities of field work. The subtitle's inclusion of *North America and Beyond* signals the book's intentional dual application, making it directly relevant for both domestic cross-cultural church planting and international missions. This is an essential text for anyone considering serious missionary church planting work in any cultural context.

---

### 25.
Conn, Harvie M., ed. *Planting and Growing Urban Churches: From Dream to Reality*. Grand Rapids: Baker Book House, 1997. 271 pp.

Harvie Conn brings together contributions from multiple urban ministry practitioners to address one of the most demanding and strategically significant contexts for church planting: the city. As global urbanization continues to concentrate the world's population in metropolitan centers, the principles Conn develops here take on increasing urgency — internationally, where cities represent the primary missionary frontier, and domestically, where urban neighborhoods are often the most underserved and ethnically diverse communities in a region. This volume complements Roger Greenway's *Guidelines for Urban Church Planting* and provides a fuller, more contemporary treatment of urban ministry strategy. For students who may find themselves planting in or near a city — an increasingly probable scenario — this book is essential reading.

## End Notes — Flagged Concerns

*The following issues were identified during preparation of this bibliography and should be resolved before final submission or classroom use.*

---

**#2 — Title spelling.**
The title should be *The Problem of Wineskins* (one word), not *Wine Skins*. Verify against the title page of the physical copy.

**#7 — Gospel for Asia (publisher and organizational credibility).**
K. P. Yohannan's *Revolution in World Missions* is published by Gospel for Asia, the organization Yohannan founded. In 2015, Gospel for Asia was removed from the Evangelical Council for Financial Accountability (ECFA) following an audit that revealed significant financial irregularities, including the diversion of donor funds designated for overseas work. Multiple lawsuits followed, and several were settled. The book's missiological argument about indigenous missionaries is independent of these institutional concerns, but instructors should be aware of the publisher's history and may wish to address it transparently with students, particularly those who may be moved to give financially to Gospel for Asia as a result of the book.

**#15 — Author name spelling.**
"Bob Finlay" is almost certainly a misspelling. The founder of Christian Aid Mission and author of this title is Bob **Finley**. Verify against the title page before final submission.

**#20 and #24 — Author name spelling.**
"Hesslegrave" (as used in the source notes) is a misspelling. The correct spelling is David J. **Hesselgrave**. Corrected throughout this bibliography.

**#22 — Bill Gothard / Institute in Basic Life Principles.**
The 2012 IBLP edition of *The Pineapple Story* lists Bill Gothard as a compiler or co-author alongside Otto Koning. Gothard resigned from the Institute in Basic Life Principles in 2014 following credible accusations of sexual harassment and abuse involving multiple women, subsequently documented by investigative reporting and a formal organizational review. Including Gothard's name on a church bibliography warrants pastoral consideration. The story itself belongs to Otto Koning and predates the institutional scandal; instructors may wish to locate an alternative edition or print source that does not carry Gothard's name, or to address the concern directly if using the IBLP edition.

**#23 — Title verification.**
The title may be *Planting Growing Churches for the Twenty-First Century* (without "and") — verify against the title page of the physical copy.

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Genuine missiological breadth.** The bibliography ranges from doxological theology (Piper's *Let the Nations Be Glad*) to field anthropology (Richardson's *Peace Child*) to practical communications (Hesselgrave) to biographical inspiration (Elliot on Carmichael). The breadth serves a class well.
2. **Responsible annotation of controversies.** The End Notes section is commendably honest: the Gospel for Asia financial scandal (entry #7), the Bill Gothard/IBLP concern (entry #22), and multiple spelling corrections are flagged with pastoral candor rather than swept aside. This kind of institutional integrity is not common in bibliographies compiled under deadline.
3. **Dual-purpose framing.** The compiler's consistent attention to domestic applicability of international missions resources — noting which books serve both foreign and home contexts — reflects sound pedagogical instinct for a local church setting where most students will never leave their zip code.

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

These are offered not as homiletical critique but as bibliographic observations relevant to the document's stated purpose:

1. **No confessionally Reformed primary text on the theology of missions.** *Let the Nations Be Glad* is present and correctly identified as the theological anchor, but there is no book engaging the biblical-covenantal grounding of the Great Commission — no Bavinck, no Bosch (*Transforming Mission*), no treatment of the Abrahamic covenant as the missionary charter of Scripture. The list is broadly evangelical rather than distinctively Reformed in its theological architecture.
2. **Indigenous church leadership and post-colonial missions are underrepresented.** Garrison's *Church Planting Movements* touches this, but the bibliography is weighted toward Western sending and strategy, with limited attention to majority-world ecclesiology or the missiological challenges arising from colonial-era mission history. For a class aiming to produce thoughtful practitioners, this gap matters.
3. **No text on missionary prayer or spiritual warfare.** Johnstone's *Operation World* is present as a prayer tool, but the bibliography does not include a text treating the theology and practice of prayer in missionary labor — a significant omission for a class preparing believers for costly gospel work.

### 11. Blind Spots

The compiler appears unaware of the degree to which the list's theological center of gravity is American evangelical (primarily Southern Baptist and parachurch) rather than Reformed Baptist or confessionally Reformed. Given that this bibliography is compiled for a local church context, the absence of a confessional theological anchor — one that ties the missionary mandate to covenant theology and the glory of God in redemptive history — means students may leave the class with excellent missiological knowledge but an insufficiently deep theological root system for sustaining long-term missionary conviction.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The End Notes section, which is genuinely strong, may create the impression that the bibliography's content has been thoroughly vetted for theological integrity. It has been vetted for bibliographic accuracy and organizational credibility — but not for theological coherence or confessional alignment. The presence of careful institutional warnings (Gothard, GFA) should not be mistaken for theological curation.

### 13. Missed Opportunity

The bibliography includes no text engaging the relationship between domestic church health and missionary sending — the question of whether a church must be spiritually vital before it can send well. Piper's *Radical Together* gestures at this, but the gap between local church discipleship and missionary commission is the most important practical question for a local church missions class, and it receives no dedicated treatment.

### 14. Surgical Fixes

- Add one confessionally Reformed treatment of the biblical theology of missions (e.g., Andreas Köstenberger and Peter O'Brien's *Salvation to the Ends of the Earth*, or Walter Kaiser's *Mission in the Old Testament*) to anchor the list theologically.
- Consider replacing or supplementing entry #7 (Yohannan) with a title that addresses indigenous church leadership from a majority-world theological perspective rather than a Western fundraising appeal.
- Add a brief prefatory paragraph to the bibliography identifying its organizing theological conviction — that missions exists for the glory of God among all nations — so that the annotated entries cohere around a stated center rather than a tacit one.
- Resolve the Bill Gothard concern in entry #22 before classroom distribution: identify an alternative edition or note that the IBLP edition should not be purchased new.

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

Not triggered — this is not a sermon and the diagnostic does not apply. However, a parallel concern applies to the bibliography as curriculum: a missions class built around this list could produce students who are knowledgeable about missions strategy, emotionally moved by missionary biography, and practically equipped for cross-cultural work — without ever being theologically undone by the weight of the Great Commission and the glory of God. Information without doxological gravity is its own form of dead orthodoxy.

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The bibliography carries genuine freight — Piper, Richardson, Brainerd, Carmichael — but the freight is distributed across twenty-five entries rather than concentrated in a single proclamation, and no document that is fundamentally a reading list can substitute for a sermon that makes God weighty in a room.
