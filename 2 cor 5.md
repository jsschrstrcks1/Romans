2. **The cross is assumed, not proclaimed**: The Synagogue Test is quietly triggered. The sermon could be heard by a devout Jewish moralist as a call to moral transformation and sincere religiosity without the cross functioning as the necessary ground. The "new creation" is presented as a moral-behavioral category ("your life changes, you share the gospel") rather than as the result of Christ's substitutionary death and vindication in resurrection. The great exchange of verse 21 — "God made him who had no sin to be sin for us, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God" — is the passage's own climax and the sermon's greatest missed opportunity.

3. **No ambassadorial or corporate dimension**: The passage's entire second movement (5:18–6:2) is about the ministry of reconciliation entrusted to the church as ambassadors. The sermon addresses the congregation only as individuals needing to examine their personal salvation, never as the community of reconcilers called to proclaim "be reconciled to God" to the world. The "equipping for holiness and witness" dimension is absent at precisely the point where the text provides it most explicitly.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears not to see that the passage he chose is one of the most theologically and evangelistically loaded texts in all of Paul — and that its richness would have served his evangelistic burden far more powerfully than the Washer/Spurgeon illustrations he reached for. The text itself provides everything he is trying to say, in sharper form: the "now is the acceptable time" urgency of 6:2 is more arresting than the sheep/goat comparison; the "God was reconciling the world to himself in Christ" of 5:19 is more awe-inducing than the hamburger analogy; the "be reconciled to God" of 5:20 is the most direct altar call in the Bible. The preacher bypassed his own passage's power.

There also appears to be a blind spot around assurance. The sermon presses relentlessly on "are you really saved?" without providing the positive assurance that 5:17–21 itself grounds — not in behavioral transformation (which can be faked, as the "goat at church" illustration shows), but in the objective accomplished work of Christ to which the believer is united. The sermon leaves genuinely converted believers without pastoral comfort.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The illustrative density creates an impression of sermonic richness and pastoral depth. The congregation is emotionally engaged throughout. But the engagement is driven primarily by the stories — the pig, Kenny, Kurt, the livestock auction — and not by the text being opened. This is a significant illusion: a sermon can feel powerful and pastoral precisely because its illustrations are vivid and its preacher is genuine, while the Word itself has not been opened. The congregation goes home remembering the pig, not the passage. This is not a criticism of illustrations as such — it is a warning about substituting illustrative momentum for expository substance.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**2 Corinthians 5:21** is the single greatest missed opportunity in the sermon. "God made him who had no sin to be sin for us, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God." This verse is:
- The most compressed statement of penal substitution in the New Testament
- The direct ground for the "new creation" of verse 17 — you are a new creation *because* the great exchange has occurred
- The answer to the self-examination question the sermon raises — you know you are truly saved not primarily because your behavior has changed, but because you are clothed in the righteousness of Christ

The sermon raises the question "are you truly saved?" and answers it almost entirely with behavioral evidence (does your life change, do you hate sin, do you share the gospel). This is not wrong, but it is incomplete — and potentially crushing to believers in genuine struggles. Verse 21 would have grounded assurance in something more stable than introspection: the objective righteousness of Christ imputed to the sinner. The preacher walked past it.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (4)

1. **Build the entire sermon from 5:18–21, not from 5:17 alone.** The "new creation" of v.17 is the doorway, but it leads into the grand hall of reconciliation theology in vv.18–21. Open that hall. Show the congregation that they are new creations because God was in Christ reconciling the world — not because their behavior changed, but because the exchange of v.21 has occurred. Ground the self-examination exhortation in this objective reality, not primarily in behavioral transformation.

2. **Preach verse 21 explicitly.** The pig illustration and the Kenny/Kurt stories are powerful illustrations of new creation. They should be capped with verse 21 as the theological ground: "This is not just what happened to Kenny and Kurt — this is what happened because God made Christ, who knew no sin, to become sin for them, so that they could become the righteousness of God in him." The illustration without the ground leaves the impression that new creation is about moral improvement, not substitutionary exchange.

3. **Add the ambassadorial call as a second movement.** After pressing self-examination ("are you truly a new creation?"), the sermon's natural second movement — given the passage — should be: "And if you are, then you are an ambassador. God is making his appeal through you. Be reconciled to God is not just what you said at conversion; it is what you are sent to say to your neighbors." This would give the sermon a two-fold structure (be reconciled / now go and call others to be reconciled) that matches the passage and equips the church rather than only examining it.

4. **Preach 6:1–2 as the altar call, not as homework.** The sermon sends the congregation to Matthew 25 as homework rather than using 6:2 — "now is the acceptable time, now is the day of salvation" — which is the passage's own urgent altar call. "I am telling you to look at Matthew 25 this week" is a pedagogical move. "Now is the day of salvation" is a proclamation. Use the text's own urgency.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** The sermon is not doctrinally inert. It has pastoral heat, genuine evangelistic burden, and real fire. The weakness is expository, not affective. This is not dead orthodoxy — it is a living heart not yet disciplined by deep textual engagement.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
The sermon carries real pastoral heat and genuine love for souls, but it does not yet carry the eternal gravity of the passage it inhabits — because the passage's own weight (the reconciliation of a cosmos to a holy God through the death of his Son) has not been opened; the sermon burns with the preacher's fire rather than with the text's.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This passage (2 Corinthians 5:16–6:3) is an epistle text with a clear propositional argument, theological clauses, and an explicit imperative — it is well-served by this rubric's expository framework. The passage does not strain the rubric; the sermon strains the passage. The rubric applies without modification. The genre is straightforward Pauline argumentation, and the rubric's exposition and gospel centrality categories are precisely what this text demands and what the sermon most needs.
