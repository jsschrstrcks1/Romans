# The Price of Integrity

I had an experience in the grocery store this week that triggered my thinking on this subject. I was at the grocery store – and tucked in behind the powdered Gatorade was an envelope. Curiosity got the better of me, and I opened the envelope to find it contained 3 100$ bills. I instantly started spending it in my mind. Motorcycle endorsement 175$, get grace spayed, 125. OH OH OR I could get two new tires to replace two of my four bald ones. Praise God for his provision. But then I thought, surely someone is looking for this cash. If I lost that amount of money I'd be in a panic. That's probably pretty close to someones entire pay check. So I walked around for a little bit trying to figure out what to do. I finally decided to keep the amount secret but leave my name and number at customer service, and if anyone came looking for lost money and could name the amount and size of the bills, I'd happily give it back. So I head over to the counter, and get In line. Customer service at winn dixie is also the line for the lotto. I waited and waited, and while I'm waiting I notice this girl in front of me carrying a toddler in each arm is crying. The kids were clean and well maintained in new clothes, but she was wearing faded, stained clothing that had clearly been given to her by an older, larger woman. I thought to my self, oh no – its probably hers. Sure enough when she got to the customer service counter she asked if anyone had turned in a little white envelope with a small heart on it. She went on to say that it contained 300 dollars cash that her mother had given her for groceries. I pulled the envelope out and sure enough it had a little hand drawn heart on the back. She continued saying that her fiancé and the father of her children had just been incarcerated for the next year, and that was all the cash she had to make ends meet, it was grocery money, the balance of her rent, and had to cover diapers for the next few weeks too until she could find a job. I pulled the envelope out of my pocket and handed it to her. As useful as it would have been, as tight as things are financially, my integrity was worth more than 300$.

A friend of mine at seminary had a story about integrity too. He and his wife were on a budget, and they wanted a porch swing for cheap date nights. They watched and saved, waited for the right sale at the right time. Scrimped and saved. Finally had about a hundred dollars and the swings went on sale at lowes. They had strict decoration rules at their place – only a single wreath, no lights on the outside of the buildings, so they'd have to set it up and take it down every time they wanted to use it. He gets to the checkout lane. Lady tried to teach him how to scam lowes out of 18 cents. The box only came with 10 bolts, needed 12. He thanked her profusely, and put a quarter on the counter and started off towards hardware to find the two bolts he needed. She stopped him immediately. He was confused. She said no no no, take it home. Put it together, and tomorrow when you're short two bolts come back in, demand to speak to the manager, and he will give you two free bolts. Whats more she said, you really need to stain this thing if its going to last, and so here's what you do! Go buy the biggest container of stain that we offer. Buy the largest container of sealer that we offer. VERY VERY carefully open the lid, and then stain, and seal your swing. Put the lid back on, and return them both to the store. No one will know the difference and you'll get your 25$ back on the pair.

25$ was a pretty decent amount of money, and could mean an extra date night! He paused for a moment, thanked the woman for her kindness and left. He never did return the stain and sealer though, he remembered his fathers words that had been pounded into him nearly all his life with the amount changing to meet the situation. His father would say "your integrity is worth more than 25$."

I was aquainted with a guy a number of years ago, who had more discretionary spending money than he knew what to do with. His father was stinking rich, and this kid liked to throw the fact he had money in peoples faces. I met him through Jeffery Whalen if that tells you anything about this self professed homosexual teenager. He liked to tell us just how much money he had, and what sort of exotic things they'd be doing, or eating, or seeing. Who knows how much money this kid actually had, or whether he did any of these things he claimed. The point is one evening this kid brought up the cost of a mans integrity. He said for the right amount of money I can buy any man and make him do what I want. Being a teenager myself I said that would be impossible. He said ok, fine. I'll give you a million dollars to drink out of that pond. Not believing he had that kid of money I declined. He didn't try again. The next time I saw him, he offered me 5 million dollars to lick jeff's foot. I refused. I never saw the kid again. But I think if I had he would have tried to offer me more money to do something else ridiculous and gross. Honestly if he had had the cash in a briefcase I probably would have drank a single sip of the water in the pond. I just didn't believe that even if his dad did have the kind of money he claimed, that he'd have access to, and be able to give away that amount of money on something so foolish. I figured if I drank the pond water, I'd end up with a weird parasite for my trouble, and no money. I doubt I would lick anyones foot for any amount of money. But let me ask you this – what is the price of your integrity?

## Integrity Defined

Everyone has heard the word integrity. Especially leading up to an election. So and so has it, so and so doesn't. we toss it around like crazy but did you know that it is one of the most commonly looked up words in the English language? Websters dictionary says integrity means being whole, undivided and morally upright. For our purposes we are going to define it as "doing the right thing regardless of the cost". If the only thing you take from tonight is that you need to be more moral, than I've failed. Yes morality is the end result but it needs to begin with salvation – we need God to transform us. We need him to renew our hearts and minds. He gives us a desire for righteousness. He helps us place a priority on holiness and purity. We want to be whole, undivided and morally upright because of Christ!

The ruse worked, and Joshua and the others were deceived. It would seem that at least one of the Gibeonites had some measure of integrity because somehow in their three days together word got out that the Gibeonites were their next door neighbors and inhabited part of the land they were promised by God. The Gibeonites were a part of the hivites and God had promised in Joshua 3:10 (can someone read that?) to drive them out.

But don't miss this, right here is where Joshua made his first mistake with these people and not for the first time. It was his critical mistake when they fought the city of Ai as well. Then and now he went into the situation without praying to God. People, let me just put it out there if you ever go to war, make sure you pray FIRST. Likewise Joshua should have prayed before entering into diplomacy with these deceitful men. Because they didn't seek God, because they didn't pray these gibeonites were able to take advantage of the nation of Israel.

We aren't told why Joshua didn't pray, but here is a possible reason – perhaps Joshua thought he had it all under control. That is Arrogance in the extreme. However in effect they have placed themselves on the throne of their lives – on the throne that belongs to God. Pray always, pray without ceasing. We need to be people of prayer. Praying for the lost, weeping over the lost… When is the last time we've wept for the lost?

You guys have heard the stories of how God has done miracles in my own life. Cars that should have run out of gas continuing to work, money landing in my mailbox when I needed it for a missions trip. I have to tell the story again I don't think all of you have heard it. (Haiti Missions trip).

Every few years they would do another blood test and find that she still had the rheumatoid. It was severe enough it was making her fingers twist. It caused near constant pain. ## Joshua's Integrity

Its interesting to me the position Joshua finds himself in. He went from conquering hero, to defender of a people that God had told Joshua to drive out of the land of Israel. I can almost hear the Israelites now: But Joshua, we were supposed to defeat them in battle, look – lets just kill them here and now and be done with it. It will restored the intended Order. But Joshua had given his word by Jehovah God, and rightly refused to break it. In fact vs 26 says that Joshua delivered them out of the hand of the people of Israel. We can't say for certain what that means, but it sounds to me like they had people out for blood – perhaps even to the point where it was easier to move all the gibeonites out of the camp and back to their home, than it was to control his own people.

## What Happened to the Gibeonites?

Some of you might be wondering what happened to the Gibeonites. I looked them up in some of my books and found out that the gibeonites aren't mentioned much more in scripture. The next reference is in the days of King David. During David's reign a famine came upon the land, and when David sought the Lord about it, God told him it was because Saul had put Gibeonites to death, violating Joshua's oath. Seven of Saul's descendants were given over to the Gibeonites as a result. After that, no one is really sure what became of the Gibeonites – but – there are many that speculate that they are the Nethinim or those selected to serve in and clean the temple. Modern day experts say they may well have had some of the earliest sophisticated water systems in their city. Archaeologists uncovered massive underground wine cellars at the ancient site of Gibeon. Further they speculate that some of the gibeonites may have later migrated and mingled with other populations, though the details are lost to history. Its all speculation – we don't really know. But there were some interesting correlations.

In vs 23 we see that the Gibeonites are cursed – they are told they will never be more than cutters of wood and drawers of water for the house of God. In fact the tabernacle would be in Gibeon until Solomons temple much much later.

Ultimately though it seems their fear of the Lord saved their lives. Even though they would spend the rest of their lives as servants, and indeed so would many of their descendents, that was better than death.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Heart, Hermeneutical Hazard: A Character Sermon That Uses Joshua 9 Rather Than Preaching It**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm, personally transparent talk on integrity that draws from three vivid personal stories but treats Joshua 9 as illustration rather than text, leaving the gospel as an appendix rather than the spine.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result

**FAIL — Conditional Pass with Major Concern**

*Scopus Test:* The stated thesis — "your integrity is worth more than [any price]" — does not arise from Joshua 9:1–27. The main point of Joshua 9 is the costly faithfulness of Joshua's oath-keeping under God's name, and the redemptive irony that deceiving pagans were preserved in the covenant community. The sermon's main point is a morality maxim illustrated by three personal anecdotes, with Joshua 9 brought in as a supporting case study. These are not the same thing.

*Authority Test:* The preacher does not stand under Joshua 9 as a herald of its claims. The text is surveyed, not submitted to. Hard exegetical questions — why did God honor the oath despite the deception? What does the Gibeonites' incorporation into Israel's worship life say about grace and covenant? — are not pressed. The text's own surprise and scandal are passed over.

The sermon passes a loose gate — Christ and grace appear — but the main point is not the text's main point. This is noted prominently below and caps the score accordingly.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**Flag 3 — TRIGGERED (Marginal):** Christ is present but not necessary to the sermon's argument. The integrity framework is established entirely from personal anecdotes and dictionary definition before Christ appears. The sermon would largely survive intact without the Ephesians 2 movement. Christ is added, not organically demanded.

**Flag 4 — TRIGGERED:** Application is generic. "Be a person of integrity," "pray more," "accept Christ as your savior" — none of these are differentiated for the specific hearers. No one in the room is cornered by the text. The closest moment is the altar-call-style invitation, but it is undifferentiated and disconnected from the text's actual claims.

**Flag 2 — PARTIAL:** The main point of the sermon does not equal the text's main point (see Berean Gate). This is the structural foundation of the score cap.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Mostly myself. The moral framework is established through three stories about personal choices under financial pressure. Christ is introduced to improve the moral project, not to displace it.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Partially. The pastor's genuine warmth and personal transparency create authentic moments. But the encounter is with the preacher's experience, not with God through the text.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Informed about integrity, and mildly emotionally moved by the grocery story. Not specifically convicted by the text, and not comforted with gospel grace in proportion to how the sermon presses moral demand.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Genuine Pastoral Voice and Transparency.** The grocery store envelope story is a real, specific, morally weighty moment told with authenticity. It establishes the theme with force and earns immediate congregational trust. This is the sermon at its best — personal, grounded, and honest.

2. **The Gospel Is Present.** Despite the structural weaknesses, Ephesians 2:4–9 is quoted fully and accurately. The preacher explicitly says, "If the only thing you take from tonight is that you need to be more moral, then I've failed." That sentence is theologically alert — the preacher knows the moralism trap, even if he has not fully escaped it.

3. **The Altar Call Has Real Pastoral Heat.** The closing prayer — "don't let them have a moment's peace until they yield their lives to you" — is not a polished formula. It carries the weight of a man who actually wants people to be saved. That is not nothing.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The Text Is Never Preached.** Joshua 9:1–27 is surveyed, summarized, and used as a case study in integrity — but it is not preached. The theological center of the passage (oath-keeping under God's name at personal cost, the redemptive irony of Gentile inclusion through covenant, the mercy embedded in a curse) is never opened. The passage exists to support a pre-existing theme rather than to generate one.

2. **The Gospel Is an Appendix, Not the Spine.** The Ephesians 2 section arrives after the sermon's argument is essentially complete. Christ is not shown to be the only solution to the problem the sermon identifies; He is presented as an upgrade to moral motivation. The statement "we need God to transform us" in the "Integrity Defined" section is the right instinct — but it is dropped immediately and not developed. The gospel is asserted, not argued.

3. **The Prayer Sub-Sermon Derails the Center.** The section on prayer (triggered by Joshua 9:14 — "they did not inquire of the LORD") metastasizes into a separate sermon on prayer, personal miracle stories, and a testimony about the preacher's mother's healing. This is the sermon's most structurally damaging section. It breaks momentum, introduces a second competing thesis, and consumes real estate that should belong to the text.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that the sermon's moral framework is fully operational before Christ enters it — meaning Christ is decorating a structure that was built without Him. The three opening stories teach integrity as personal virtue achievable through willpower and good values. When Christ arrives, He is presented as the divine enabler of what the stories already modeled. This is moralism with a gospel finish coat, not gospel-generated ethics.

There is also likely unawareness of how much the personal stories dominate the sermon's emotional and intellectual weight relative to the text. The preacher is more present in this sermon than Joshua 9 is.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal anecdotes feel like strengths and the congregation likely responds well to them — and in terms of engagement, they are effective. But they create an illusion of applied preaching that masks the absence of text-derived application. The congregation leaves having thought about integrity through the preacher's stories rather than having been searched by the text of Joshua 9. The warmth of the stories substitutes for the weight of the Word.

Similarly, the full quotation of Ephesians 2:4–9 creates the impression of robust gospel content, but the text is quoted rather than preached — it arrives and departs without being driven into the conscience.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Joshua 9 contains one of the Old Testament's most striking anticipations of the gospel: deceiving outsiders, deserving death under the covenant ban, are preserved by an oath sworn in God's name — and are incorporated into the worship life of Israel, becoming servants at the very tabernacle they feared. This is Rahab's logic: faith expressed in imperfect ways, covenant inclusion despite unworthiness, grace operating inside the very curse. The preacher touches this ("I kind of see myself in the Gibeonite") but then apologizes for the comparison and moves on. That is the sermon. That is where Joshua 9 is preachable with full gospel force, and it was abandoned.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the main point from the text.** The thesis should be: "When God's name is on the line, covenant faithfulness demands more than convenience — and grace sometimes arrives wearing a curse." The personal integrity stories can still open the sermon, but they must be redirected to serve this textual point, not replace it.

2. **Preach the Gibeonite-as-sinner analogy fully or drop it.** The preacher himself says "don't poke at that too much because it will fall apart." That is a signal that the typological move is unfinished. Either develop the organic connection (outsiders incorporated into covenant worship through mercy — a genuine type of Gentile salvation) with theological care, or remove the comparison entirely. An analogy the preacher himself warns against is not load-bearing.

3. **Move the gospel from appendix to spine.** The "Integrity Defined" section already gestures in the right direction: "it needs to begin with salvation." Follow that instinct. Show that the only person with perfect integrity is Christ — that His oath-keeping to the Father and His covenant faithfulness to us is the ground of our transformation, not just its enabler. The Ephesians 2 section should not arrive after the sermon's argument; it should be the argument's destination.

4. **Cut the prayer sub-sermon and the miracle testimonies.** The section from "But don't miss this" through the Haiti missions trip and the arthritis healing is a sermon within a sermon. This material may be preachable — but not in this sermon, and not here. Remove it. The space it frees should go to deeper engagement with Joshua 9 and differentiated application.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Joshua 9 is narrative literature, and the rubric's exposition categories apply with some genre adjustment. Narrative preaching legitimately works through dramatic development rather than propositional argument, and the preacher is doing narrative instinctively — moving through the story, noting key observations. The rubric does not penalize narrative form. What it penalizes is the use of narrative as backdrop for a pre-existing theme rather than as the carrier of the text's own theological argument. The genre does not strain the rubric here — the rubric is identifying a genuine hermeneutical problem, not a genre mismatch.
