# Judges 1–2 — Humpty Dumpty
## *The Canaanite Creep and the Faithfulness of God*

*[Raw sermon notes — dictation transcript with light cleanup of repeat artifacts. Verify flagged Scripture references before preaching.]*

---

We are going to be in the book of Judges this morning as we continue on our march through the Old Testament, heading toward the New.

In my prep this week I kept thinking about Humpty Dumpty. Humpty Dumpty sat on a wall, Humpty Dumpty had a great fall — all the king's horses and all the king's men couldn't put Humpty back together again.

In my reading on Judges a lot of the commentators pointed out just how dark the book is. In Genesis we saw the fall – Humpty falling off the wall, and now we are seeing all the king's horses and all the king's men trying to put things back together again, and yet there is so much sin creeping into the lives of God's people. So much moral decline week after week after week. One commentator said that Israel is succumbing to the Canaanite creep. That is the slow creep of sin into the hearts and minds of the Israelites. When the Bible says man is desperately sinful. I'm reminded of Ecclesiastes 7:29 – "God made man upright, but they have sought out many schemes." Or Romans 3:23 – anyone know that one by heart? "For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." Or 2 Chronicles 6:36 – "there is no one who does not sin." Or Micah 7:2–4:

> "The godly has perished from the earth, and there is no one upright among mankind; they all lie in wait for blood, and each hunts the other with a net. Their hands are on what is evil, to do it well; the prince and the judge ask for a bribe, and the great man utters the evil desire of his soul; thus they weave it together. The best of them is like a brier, the most upright of them a thorn hedge."

These are hard words – and this only scratches the surface of what scripture says about the depths of the evil within each and every one of us. Or you could say, the extent of the damage to Humpty.

Mark 7:21–23:

> "For from within, out of the heart of man, come evil thoughts, sexual immorality, theft, murder, adultery, coveting, wickedness, deceit, sensuality, envy, slander, pride, foolishness. All these evil things come from within, and they defile a person."

Verse after verse after verse talks about the depths to which we are slaves to sin. Genesis 6:5 says every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually. And in Genesis 8:21, the Lord says the intention of man's heart is evil from his youth.

The conclusion of the wisest man that ever lived – Solomon – in Proverbs 28:26: *"Whoever trusts in his own mind is a fool."*

Jesus said everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin — John 8:34. Peter says likewise that we are slaves to the corruption.

Paul writes to Timothy: "God may perhaps grant them repentance leading to a knowledge of the truth, and they may come to their senses and escape from the snare of the devil, after being captured by him to do his will." – 2 Timothy 2:25–26.

And that doesn't even take into account the entire book of Ephesians that talks about these things.

Apart from God granting someone repentance – a thing we don't even seem to be capable of without God's help – we are ensnared and slaves to sin and the devil. It is through that kind of lens I think that we have to view Judges. Judges charts the moral decline of the people of God.

---

Just last week we looked at the covenant they made to follow God, and not the useless sticks of the peoples of the land they had conquered. Already in the early parts of Judges we see them falling away.

In the first three chapters we see Israel has already dropped the ball. God through Joshua had commanded them to finish taking the land. Judges 1:27 is talking about Manasseh and five other tribes allowing some of the Canaanites to remain in the land. Even if they are just slaves, they allowed the poison to persist in their midst.

I had a funny thing happen to me one time. I had an interaction with a publisher. I found a couple of paragraphs exactly blatantly copied – it was plagiarism. Word for word. Unashamedly copied text. I was shocked. This publisher, in my mind, had to this point had a sterling reputation. I figured they would want to know right away what had happened. They could at least put in a footnote indicating where the text came from. Any way, I found an email to the editorial group and let them know what I had found.

Their response was: it's just a little plagiarism.

I was shocked. A little plagiarism can get me kicked out of seminary. A little plagiarism has ruined reputations, ruined careers, ruined publishing companies. I didn't respond right away. It didn't come to me until later, but isn't that basically what the Israelites did? They allowed a little sin in the camp, and what were the consequences? Another pastor friend said allowing a little plagiarism is like only watching a little pornography, or getting a little drunk, only doing a little heroin, or being ok with just a little wife beating.

Allowing a little Canaanites into the country had ramifications for years to come.

When you read Judges this week, did you notice which city was captured? Jerusalem. Taken from the Canaanites. And yet six of the twelve tribes allowed the peoples of the land to remain.

Look at what God said would happen because of it. Let's read 2:1–6. Notice verse 3 with me: *"they shall become thorns in your sides, and their gods shall be a snare to you."*

God said it. And it happened exactly as He said. Thousands of years of consequences trace back to this chapter — to six tribes who thought a little compromise wouldn't hurt.

If they had honored their covenant with the Lord — held up their side of the deal — what would the world look like today?

If you had lived every moment of your life for the Lord, what would your life look like today?

---

The passage declares something loud and clear about our God that we can't afford to miss. It's a thing Haddon Robinson pointed out. It's a thing you've heard me mention before. Even in the face of the full scale of Israel's rebellion against God – and that's what disobedience and sin are – God is still faithful.

Those of you that have read Judges, there is a phrase that comes up over and over – anyone remember it? There are two. "Israel again did what was evil in the sight of the Lord." This phrase is repeated seven times across the book of Judges. Just when you think they have it figured out they forsake the Lord for the Baals and Asheroth again. Which is the second phrase that is repeated throughout.

It strikes me that God has thoroughly defeated Baal and his consort Asherah. Remember we talked about the depths of the followers of that old dead stick a while back. Child sacrifice, worshiping an idol through sex, and poop, and blood… Yet God was still faithful. He punished them, sure. But when they returned to him he always came to the rescue. Praise the Lord he does so for us today as well. We may not worship Baal, but is there anyone here who would say they worship God fully, wholly and without error? If you raise your hand you're arrogant and have so fallen short.

**God was faithful even when Israel was unfaithful.**

**God is faithful to us today even when we are unfaithful.**

**God is better to us than we deserve.**

---

When it comes to your heart, I think everyone has a deep dark corner they try to hold in reserve. At least in my experience more people do than don't. Solomon said vanity of vanities, all is vanity, nothing is new under the sun. People are in large part the same yesterday, today and tomorrow apart from the direct intervention of God. So when we read about people turning from God for sex back then, we can know that it's able to happen still today. I had a chat with a guy a while back. He'd come on and off for a while, and he asked me honestly, straight forward, what the Bible says about sex outside of marriage. I told him what it says from a number of verses in the old and new testament. How it's something true Christians ought not do outside of marriage.

After that I never saw the man again. His priority was sex over God. When he had to choose between the two he chose his idol.

So what will you do? Who will you serve?

I saw a picture a while back – a meme – it was a photo of a guy riding down the road on his bicycle. The second frame had a picture of a guy with a stick. He's arguing with himself, he knows he shouldn't do it but he really wants to, and the third frame shows the bike wrecked, the man faceplanted on the ground, and the caption says "I thought just a little wouldn't hurt."

Christ wants all of you. Not just most of you. He gave everything he had – even his very life – for all of you – every part, the good the bad and the ugly. He wants all the good stuff, all the shady stuff and all the dark recesses of your soul. So that He can put Humpty back together again. We have failed to do so in our own power in every way. But where we fail, God succeeds. Where earthly kings and armies fail, God succeeds. That's a promise we can cling to when nothing else in the world makes sense. That God is faithful even in the midst of our unfaithfulness.

That doesn't mean there won't be consequences. The nation of Israel has had 2600 years of consequences.

The Israelites wept, and offered sacrifice to cover their sins. Basically they repented when they heard all of this. I think grieving over our sin and repenting is an appropriate response. Certainly it's the first part of the gospel.

**Flagged — resolve before preaching:**

1. **Genesis reference** — The sermon blends two verses: "every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil" is **Genesis 6:5**; "from his youth" is **Genesis 8:21**. These should not be presented as a single verse. Either cite both separately or choose one.

2. **Proverbs 28:26** — Sermon says "trusts his own *heart* is a fool." The **ESV says *mind***, not heart: *"Whoever trusts in his own mind is a fool."* The KJV says "heart." Decide which translation you're using and be consistent. If you quote it as "heart," note you are using the KJV rendering.

3. **John 8:34** — Sermon says "commits a sin." **ESV says *practices***: *"everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin."* KJV says "committeth." Same as above — decide which translation and note it.

4. **Haddon Robinson attribution** — No specific quote is attributed; the reference is vague ("a thing Haddon Robinson pointed out"). Since no words are placed in Robinson's mouth, this is lower risk — but if pressed, the point about God's faithfulness amid Israel's unfaithfulness is a standard theme in expository preaching literature. Acceptable as-is, but be prepared to be asked for a source.

5. **"Seven times" in Judges** — The "evil in the sight of the LORD" cycle phrase appears in **3:7, 3:12, 4:1, 6:1, 10:6, 13:1** and with a variant in 8:33. Approximately seven — confirm your count before stating it as a precise figure from the pulpit.

**Rhetorical figure — not a verifiable historical claim:**
- "2600 years of consequences" — a rhetorical approximation from the fall of Jerusalem (~586 BC). Not a claim requiring footnoting, but worth noting that the modern Middle East conflict has deeply complex causes beyond this single historical thread.
