# Faithful and God-Fearing
## A Sermon on Nehemiah 7:1–5

---

## Context

Last week they finished the wall. This week they finished the gate. This has been a long time coming.

From the first day of Nehemiah's ministry to the celebration that's coming next week — that's roughly thirteen years, according to many commentators. The actual construction took only fifty-two days. The rest was planning, gathering materials, purchasing enslaved people back from their enemies and their creditors — basically all the prep work. Thirteen years of his life, and then fifty-two days to put it all up.

Now that everything is finally done, Nehemiah gathers three groups together — the gatekeepers, the singers, and the Levites. He's not done. The wall is up. The gates are hung. And immediately he turns to worship.

---

## The Singers

Something about the singers caught my eye.

They weren't just a Jewish institution. According to the text, the singers were part of a royal order from the King of Persia. That's worth sitting with.

*[Note: See end notes on the timeline of Isaiah, Daniel, and Cyrus. The "fifty years" reference in the original notes needs adjustment before preaching.]*

Think about the background. Not long before this moment — in the generations that preceded Nehemiah — the stories of Daniel and Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego were being written. Men and boys, taken into exile, put in positions of power in a foreign empire, and then tested on whether they would bend.

Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refused to worship Nebuchadnezzar, and were thrown into the furnace. But they came out unscathed. And something remarkable happened in that furnace — a fourth figure appeared with them, one whose appearance Nebuchadnezzar himself described as being like a son of the gods. They walked through the fire, and not one hair on their heads was singed, and they didn't even smell of smoke.

Then there was Daniel. The king's advisors were jealous of him and manipulated Darius into issuing a decree they knew would put Daniel in an impossible position. Knowing full well what it would cost him, Daniel went home, opened his windows toward Jerusalem, and prayed — as he always had. He was thrown into the lions' den. The king spent a sleepless night. When he came running to the den at dawn, Daniel called out to him from inside. God had sent his angel, and the lions had not touched him.

Cyrus knew these stories. *[See end notes on the Cyrus succession question.]* The Persian king who issued the decree allowing the Jews to return to their land — God had spoken about Cyrus by name in Isaiah, a full century and a half before Cyrus was born:

> Thus says the LORD to his anointed, to Cyrus,
> whose right hand I have grasped,
> to subdue nations before him
> and to loose the belts of kings,
> to open doors before him
> that gates may not be closed:
> I will go before you
> and level the exalted places,
> I will break in pieces the doors of bronze
> and cut through the bars of iron,
> I will give you the treasures of darkness
> and the hoards in secret places,
> that you may know that it is I, the LORD,
> the God of Israel, who call you by your name.
> For the sake of my servant Jacob,
> and Israel my chosen...
> — Isaiah 45:1–4 (ESV)

God set out to accomplish something through Cyrus — and when God sets out to do something, it is going to happen. It's not concrete, but it's fair to say Cyrus had some awareness of who this God was. He may have known the stories. He may have come to fear the God of Israel.

There is also an ancient tradition — held in various Christian communities — that the Magi who followed the star to Bethlehem came from this region of Persia, and that a thread of reverence for the God of Israel had been preserved there going back to the days of the exile. It's a tradition, not an established historical fact. But it isn't a strange idea. God's fingerprints were all over that empire.

Xerxes, who was Cyrus's grandson through his mother Atossa, would have grown up with these stories in the background. *[See end notes on the Xerxes-Cyrus relationship.]* Certainly he had the sense to put a supremely capable follower of God in charge of his treasury in Nehemiah. He was at least wise enough — and had enough respect for what his forebears had seen — to follow their example. Re-instituting worship through song as a royal decree from Xerxes says something about where Xerxes' heart was.

---

## Worship Before Revival

Proper worship is a prerequisite for revival.

I won't get too deep into it this week — if you've read ahead in Nehemiah, you know a revival is coming in Jerusalem. But notice how it starts. It starts with obedience. It starts with rebuilding the wall. And then, the very first thing Nehemiah does when the work is done is re-institute public worship — through singing, through the priests, through the Levites.

It would be easy at this point for the people to take their eyes off God. They have a huge city, surrounded by a massive wall, with strong gates hung. They've gone from being relatively weak to being a physically capable people — as anyone would be after building an enormous wall in fifty-two days. They went from being peasants and farmers to people familiar with weapons. They were probably sitting there feeling pretty secure behind their wall, feeling like they had accomplished something.

That's exactly when the danger starts. Some of you are riding a victory right now. Be careful.

God gives his people a victory. But if his people start taking the credit instead of giving God the glory — not only is that sin, but more sins follow from it. If you've ever been on a missions trip, you know: the two biggest periods of spiritual attack are right before you go and right after you come back. He attacks us in our insecurity. He attacks us in our success. He will attack you in the middle of the field too.

Our enemy doesn't want you telling people about Jesus. So he tries to knock you down before it happens. But what's even worse to him than you sharing the gospel is you coming home and inspiring someone else to. When you start invading his territory and encouraging others to do the same, he takes notice.

The answer is what Nehemiah prescribes here: sustained, intentional worship. Read God's word. Pray. Gather in fellowship with other believers. Be armed and ready when the attacks come — because they will come.

---

## The Gatekeepers

It struck me what kind of men Nehemiah chose for the positions he needed to fill.

Look at verse 2:

> I gave my brother Hanani and Hananiah the governor of the castle charge over Jerusalem, for he was a more faithful and God-fearing man than many.
> — Nehemiah 7:2 (ESV)

Two criteria. That's it.

Be faithful. Fear God.

Faithful means showing up. It means people who demonstrate their commitment with their presence. Showing up matters more than people think. People who follow through on what they say they'll do. This is an area where younger people today often struggle. Dr. Alvin Reid, a professor of evangelism, has observed that people today can care deeply about something — sex trafficking, hunger, the lost — and never move beyond posting about it. They care about everything and do nothing. *[See end notes on this quotation.]*

There's a story I heard on the radio that the Great Wall of China was breached by invaders only a handful of times, and each time it was through bribery rather than force. I can't verify the exact number. But the principle holds: a wall is only as strong as the people committed to guarding it. Faithfulness. That's the quality.

And God-fearing. Not people-fearing. Not performance-driven. Not concerned with what everyone else thinks of them. Fearing God — which, as we just saw in Daniel and Shadrach and the rest, is what produces faithfulness under pressure in the first place. Those men feared God more than they feared Nebuchadnezzar and Darius. That's why they survived. That's why God used them.

Even Jonah — who didn't want to be faithful, who ran from the assignment — eventually developed faithfulness along the way. When he finally arrived in Nineveh, he delivered the message God gave him, and an entire city repented.

So we've talked about when our enemy attacks — and that the defense is faithfulness and the fear of God. Reading his word. Praying. Gathering. God tends to choose people like that to use.

---

## The Genealogy

Here's the thing that I find most interesting about this chapter.

The very first thing God calls Nehemiah to do once the task is finished is begin a genealogy. Anyone who could prove their ancestry was in. Anyone who could not — was out.

And of all the people who showed up at the gate, look at who was there. Look with me at verses 61–64:

> The following came up from Tel-melah, Tel-harsha, Cherub, Addon, and Immer, but they could not prove their fathers' houses nor their descent, whether they belonged to Israel: the sons of Delaiah, the sons of Tobiah, the sons of Nekoda, 642. Also, of the priests: the sons of Hobaiah, the sons of Hakkoz, the sons of Barzillai (who had taken a wife from the daughters of Barzillai the Gileadite and was called by their name). These sought their registration among those enrolled in the genealogies, but it was not found there, so they were excluded from the priesthood as unclean.
> — Nehemiah 7:61–64 (ESV)

Tobiah's sons. Remember Tobiah? The man who mocked Nehemiah. Who hatched plots against him. Who tried to smear his name and did everything he could think of to destroy the project. Insults, rumors, schemes.

But when the wall went up — when safety was to be found inside it — where did Tobiah send his children?

Tobiah knew. He knew God was real. He knew God was with Nehemiah. He knew God was with Israel. He watched it all and couldn't stop it. And still his children were excluded.

There were, and there still are, two possible outcomes.

On one hand, you can trace your lineage back — for them, through Israel; for us, spiritually, through Jesus Christ. Or you can find yourself in the same camp as the sons of Tobiah: you know enough to come to the gate, but you can't get in.

Nehemiah knew he didn't need to fear Tobiah. He knew there was only one he had to fear, and it was the Lord. Jesus said it plainly in Luke 12:5:

> But I will warn you whom to fear: fear him who, after he has killed, has authority to cast into hell. Yes, I tell you, fear him!
> — Luke 12:5 (ESV)

There is no way to change your genetics. If you were born Asian, you're Asian until you die. If you were born Jewish, you're ethnically Jewish until you die. Whatever ethnicity you were born into, that's yours until the end. Which meant the people excluded in Nehemiah's day had no way to change it. The exclusion was permanent.

But praise God — he made a way for our status to be changed. Because apart from that, because of our sin, we would be eternally excluded from heaven.

**"50 years since the days of Isaiah and Daniel" — flagged:**
- The notes say "its only been about 50 years since the days of Isaiah and Daniel." This requires correction before preaching. Isaiah's ministry ended approximately 700 BC; Nehemiah's ministry began approximately 444 BC — roughly 256 years. Daniel's ministry ended approximately 535 BC; Nehemiah's is approximately 91 years later. Neither is 50 years. If the intended reference is "50 years since the end of the Babylonian captivity," the math is still off (Cyrus's decree was 538 BC; Nehemiah arrives 444 BC = ~94 years). The sentence has been recast in the manuscript to avoid the specific number; the pastor should supply the intended timeframe.

**"Nebuchadnezzar was succeeded by Darius who was succeeded by Cyrus" — flagged:**
- This succession as stated is historically inaccurate. Nebuchadnezzar (d. 562 BC) was succeeded by Amel-Marduk, then Neriglissar, Labashi-Marduk, and Nabonidus/Belshazzar. Cyrus the Great (r. 559–530 BC) conquered Babylon in 539 BC. "Darius the Mede" appears in Daniel 5–6 immediately after the fall of Babylon; his precise historical identity is debated among scholars. The manuscript does not repeat the succession claim; it refers to the relevant biblical narratives (Daniel 6, Daniel 3) without tracing the king list. Do not preach the succession as stated in the notes.

**Xerxes and Cyrus — corrected:**
- Notes say "Xerxes would have been the grand or great grandson of Cyrus." Xerxes I (r. 486–465 BC) was the son of Darius I the Great and Atossa, who was the daughter of Cyrus. This makes Xerxes the **grandson** of Cyrus through his mother Atossa. "Grandson" is the accurate term for the matrilineal connection; "great-grandson" is not supported. Corrected to "grandson through his mother Atossa" in the manuscript.

**"Three wisemen from Persia" tradition:**
- This is an ancient tradition in the church (associated with early Christian commentary on Matthew 2), not an established historical fact. Presented in the manuscript as a tradition.

**Dr. Alvin Reid quotation:**
- Dr. Alvin Reid is a real person — professor of evangelism at Southeastern Baptist Theological Seminary (now retired). The specific quotation "they care about everything but do nothing" is referenced in the manuscript as an observation attributed to him. The exact wording and source could not be verified. If quoting directly from the pulpit, identify the source as specifically as possible (book title, talk, date) or frame it as a paraphrase.

**Great Wall of China — unverified:**
- The claim that "invaders breached the Great Wall only four times, and all four times through bribery" is a popular anecdote but not verifiable from standard historical sources. The Wall was built and rebuilt over many dynasties; "breached" is difficult to define consistently. The manuscript preserves the illustration's point while flagging the number as unverified.
