Chapter 21

Did Christ Make an Atonement for Sin?

A Reformed Baptist Study for Men Walking in Truth and Grace

⸻

LEADER'S NOTE

This chapter follows a path of careful thinking. Walk through it slowly. The men need time with each verse — not just to hear the words read aloud, but to understand what each one actually says.

When a hard word comes up — like "atonement" or "propitiation" — stop and explain it in plain terms before moving on. These doctrines belong to everyone in the room, not just to men who went to seminary.

By the end of the session, the argument should feel earned — not just announced. The goal is not to impress anyone with theology. The goal is for every man to walk out understanding why the cross is better news than he thought.

Note: the chapter ends with nine hard passages still unresolved. That is by design. Do not skip ahead to resolve them. Let the question sit. It will be answered next week.

⸻

What We've Covered So Far

In Chapter 18 we looked at the hardest objection to God's election: Is it fair? Is God unjust to choose some and not others?

The answer we found was this: Every person ever born deserves God's judgment. No one has any right to demand grace. Grace, by its very definition, cannot be earned or demanded. So when God saves some from a group that deserves nothing — that is not unfair. It is mercy.

In Chapter 20, Ira and Terry considered three ways to understand who Christ died for. Two of those were easy to rule out. Now we come to the hardest one.

Chapter 21 – Did Christ Make an Atonement for Sin?

Summary:

In the days after the car accident, Ira spends time with his friend Todd. As they talk through grief and Scripture, something becomes clear: Todd is not a saved man. He has been preaching. He has been going through the motions of the Christian life for years. But he does not know Christ.

Thursday evening, Todd breaks. He runs out of the room, crying. Ira does not chase him — he leaves him in God's hands.

Two hours later, Todd comes back. His face is different. God saved him while he was alone.

Todd says something worth remembering: *"I want you to teach me everything you've been learning. I'm tired of living a false, surface life and preaching false, surface sermons."*

That is what this chapter is about. Stopping at the surface is not safe. Todd's story and the theology of this chapter are the same warning.

The following Friday, Ira and Terry sit down to work through Possibility 3 — the hardest option on the atonement.

⸻

POSSIBILITY 3: WHAT IT CLAIMS

Here is Possibility 3 in plain words:

*Christ died for all people — the elect and the non-elect alike. His death was the same for both groups. The only difference is what people do with it. Those who believe are saved. Those who reject it are not.*

This view sounds reasonable at first. It matches the way many people talk about the cross. But Ira has been thinking hard about it — and he has found a problem.

The problem is this: it depends on what the word *atonement* actually means.

⸻

WHAT IS "ATONEMENT"?

Atonement is a big word. Here is what it means.

Think of a courtroom. A man is on trial. He owes a debt he cannot pay — the penalty for his crimes. Someone walks in and says: *I will pay every debt this man owes. I will take his punishment. He goes free because I stand in his place.*

That is atonement. It means:
- The penalty was actually paid — not just offered
- The debt was cleared — not just reduced
- The substitute stood in the guilty person's place — not alongside them

This is not a theory. This is what Scripture says.

**Isaiah 53:10–11** reads:

*"Yet it was the will of the LORD to crush him; he has put him to grief; when his soul makes an offering for guilt, he shall see his offspring; he shall prolong his days; the will of the LORD shall prosper in his hand. Out of the anguish of his soul he shall see and be satisfied; by his knowledge shall the righteous one, my servant, make many to be accounted righteous, and he shall bear their iniquities."*

Read that slowly. Three things stand out.

First: *"his soul makes an offering for guilt."* This is not a symbolic gesture. It is a guilt offering — the kind of payment that actually removes guilt. Under the Old Testament law, a guilt offering had to be exact. It had to satisfy the requirement. Christ's death was that kind of offering.

Second: *"he shall see and be satisfied."* The servant — Christ — looks at the result of His death and is satisfied. Why would He be satisfied if the work was incomplete? He is satisfied because it worked. The debt was paid. The people He died for are freed.

Third: *"he shall bear their iniquities."* He bore them. Past tense in its effect. The iniquities were laid on Him, and He carried them. That is substitution — one person bearing what another deserved.

**Hebrews 9:12** says:

*"He entered once for all into the holy places, not by means of the blood of goats and calves but by means of his own blood, thus securing an eternal redemption."*

The word is *securing.* Not *offering.* Not *making available.* Securing. The redemption is already accomplished. It is not waiting on something else to make it real.

**Romans 3:25–26** says God put forward Christ as a *propitiation* — which means a satisfaction of His justice. God's anger against sin was not pushed aside or ignored. It was absorbed. Taken. Satisfied in full.

*"It was to show his righteousness at the present time, so that he might be just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus."*

God is both just — He punished sin — and the justifier — He acquitted the sinner. Both at the same time. Because the penalty fell on His Son.

**2 Corinthians 5:21** puts it directly:

*"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."*

Christ took our sin. We receive His righteousness. That is the exchange at the cross. That is what atonement means.

⸻

NOW ASK THE QUESTION

If Christ made that kind of atonement — actually bearing sin, actually satisfying God's justice, actually securing redemption — for every single person who has ever lived...

Then how is anyone in hell?

If your debt is paid, you are free. A court cannot punish a man whose penalty has already been served. To punish a man whose debt is paid would be unjust. It would be demanding payment twice.

**Proverbs 17:15** says:

*"He who justifies the wicked and he who condemns the righteous are both alike an abomination to the LORD."*

God calls it an abomination to condemn someone who is righteous before Him. If Christ fully paid the penalty for a man's sin, that man is righteous before God. Condemning him would be an abomination — which God cannot do.

So if Possibility 3 is true — if Christ made a full, complete atonement for every person — then every person must be saved. But every person is not saved. Therefore, Possibility 3 cannot be true.

That is Ira's argument. It is not complicated. It is simply following the logic.

⸻

THE ESCAPE THAT DOESN'T WORK

Someone might try to escape by saying: maybe Christ's death was not that kind of atonement. Maybe it was just an *offer* — something available to all who take it. It was not a completed payment; it was a potential payment.

This sounds like a solution. But it creates a bigger problem.

If Christ's death was only a potential payment — not an actual one — then the elect are also saved by a potential payment. Their sin was not actually borne. Their debt was not actually paid. God did not actually satisfy His justice against their sin. They activated an offer by choosing to believe.

But then what is the ground of **Romans 8:1?**

*"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."*

Paul does not say: *"There is now a good chance of no condemnation."* He says: *no condemnation.* Not reduced. Not conditional. None.

That sentence requires that something actually happened on the cross — that the penalty was actually paid, the guilt actually borne, the debt actually cleared.

A cross that only made salvation possible cannot produce the sentence: *no condemnation.*

And **John 19:30** — the last words of Christ before He died:

*"It is finished."*

Not: *"It is available."* Not: *"It is offered."* Finished. The word in Greek is *tetelestai* — the same word written on a bill of debt when the payment was complete. Stamped: paid in full.

If the cross was a completed work — then it completed something for someone. It did not complete something for everyone and leave them all in hell.

⸻

Ira's Conclusion

Christ's atoning death was for the elect. Not because God's love is small. But because the cross actually accomplished what it set out to do — and Possibility 3 requires it to have accomplished nothing certain for anyone.

Terry agrees. Then she asks the right question: *"How do you explain all these other verses?"*

She names nine passages that seem to say Christ died for everyone:

* Christ is the Savior of the world (John 4:42; 1 John 4:14)
* Christ is the Savior of all people (1 Timothy 4:10)
* God so loved the *world* (John 3:16)
* Christ is the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the *world* (John 1:29)
* God was reconciling the *world* to Himself (2 Corinthians 5:19)
* God desires *all people* to be saved (1 Timothy 2:3–6)
* Christ gave Himself as a ransom for *all* (1 Timothy 2:3–6)
* Christ tasted death for *everyone* (Hebrews 2:9)
* Christ is the propitiation for the sins of the *whole world* (1 John 2:2)

They agree to come back to those next Friday. The chapter ends with Ira driving back to campus, drifting off to sleep with the question still open — and still thinking about that thousand-seat sanctuary at First Baptist Church.

Connection to Our Romans Series:

Romans 3:25–26 is the key text. Read it again slowly:

*"Whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith... so that he might be just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus."*

God is both just — He punished sin — and the justifier — He freed the sinner. Both. At the same time. Because Christ absorbed the wrath. That is what propitiation means: the wrath was taken. Not deferred. Not offered to be taken. Taken.

And Romans 8:1 is where all of this lands for those who are in Christ:

*"There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."*

The *therefore* in that verse reaches back through all of Romans 1–7. It is the conclusion of the whole argument: because God's justice was satisfied at the cross, those who are in Christ stand with nothing against them. Zero. That is what a completed atonement produces.

UNPACKING THE ARGUMENT — STEP BY STEP

For groups that want to work through the logic more slowly, here it is in plain steps.

**Step 1: What does atonement mean?**
Atonement means a complete, substitutionary payment. Christ stood in the place of others, bore their sin, and satisfied God's justice on their behalf. Not partially. Not provisionally. Completely.

**Step 2: What would it mean for atonement to be made for every person?**
Every person would have their sin completely paid for. God's justice would be fully satisfied on their behalf. They would be righteous before God — their debt cleared.

**Step 3: What does the Bible say happens to people whose debt is cleared?**
Romans 8:1 — no condemnation. Proverbs 17:15 — to condemn them would be an abomination.

**Step 4: Are all people saved?**
No. Some are condemned. Some are in hell.

**Step 5: What does that mean?**
Either the atonement was not made for every person — or God is condemning people whose penalty was already paid. The second option accuses God of injustice. Therefore the first option must be true.

**Step 6: What about the nine passages that say "world" and "all"?**
That is the question for next week. But whatever they mean — they cannot mean that Christ made a complete, substitutionary atonement for every person without exception. Because that path leads to a God who is unjust.

Reflection Questions – Chapter 21

1. **Isaiah 53:11** says the servant "shall see and be satisfied." In your own words, what does it mean that Christ looked at the result of His death and was satisfied? What would have left Him unsatisfied?
2. **Hebrews 9:12** says Christ entered the holy places "securing" an eternal redemption — not just offering one. What is the difference between *securing* something and *making it available*? Which one produces more confidence?
3. **Proverbs 17:15** says condemning a righteous person is an abomination to God. How does Ira use this verse to argue against Possibility 3? Put the argument in your own words.
4. Think about Ira's big question: If Christ paid the full penalty for every person's sin, why would any person be condemned? Walk through the logic yourself. Where does it take you?
5. Todd had been living on the surface of the Christian life — going through the motions. Why is that not safe? What in this chapter connects to Todd's story?

Group Discussion – Chapter 21

* **Read Isaiah 53:10–12 together.** Go through it one verse at a time. What does "an offering for guilt" mean? What does "he shall see and be satisfied" mean? What does "he bore the sin of many" tell us about who the atonement was for?

* **Read Hebrews 9:12.** The verse says Christ "secured" an eternal redemption. Not offered — secured. Discuss: If the redemption is already secured, what does that mean for those for whom it was secured? Can something that is secured become unsecured?

* **Read Romans 3:25–26.** Paul says God is both "just" and "the justifier." How can God be both at the same time? How does the word "propitiation" (absorbing God's wrath) make both possible?

* **Work through Ira's argument together.** Here is the dilemma: either Christ's death was a complete payment for every person — in which case every person should stand under Romans 8:1 — or it was not a complete payment for everyone. Which option do you land on, and why?

* **Terry's question:** She names nine passages that seem to point the other direction. What is the honest posture when you believe the argument is correct, but you don't yet have all the answers? Can a man hold a firm conclusion while still having open questions?

* Todd was saved after running out alone. Ira did not follow him. What does that tell you about what Ira believed about how God saves people? How does that connect to the doctrine of election we studied in Chapter 18?

Closing Reflection — What This Chapter Teaches

Two men came to the end of something in this chapter.

Todd came to the end of a surface faith. All the motion was there — the ministry, the sermons, the Bible reading. But there was no foundation under it. When grief hit, there was nothing. He ran, and found that the only place left to go was to God. And God was there.

Ira came to the end of Possibility 3. Not because he found a clever argument. Because he took one word — atonement — and followed it where it goes. If atonement means what Isaiah 53 and Hebrews 9 say it means, then the cross either saved the people it was for, or it saved no one at all.

He chose the first. And underneath that choice is the sentence that holds everything: *It is finished.*
