### Opening — More Blessed Than We Know

This passage hit me hard this week, because I think it's speaking directly to where we are as a nation right now.

So let me start with a question.

I found a website this week that lets you enter your annual income and see how you rank against the rest of the world — every country, every income level, all of it plotted out. And I went in and entered the kind of number that represents someone on Social Security disability here in America. The average runs somewhere between $700 and $1,200 a month. I picked a round number in the middle — $10,000 for the year.

Now tell me — where do you think that puts a person? Globally speaking. Do you think someone collecting disability in America is in the poorest 10% of the world? The poorest 30%? Somewhere in the middle?

I want you to actually think about that before I tell you.

Would you believe they are in the top 10% of earners on the entire planet?

The least wealthy Americans — the ones who cannot work, who are dependent on a government check — are wealthier than 90% of the rest of the world. That is a staggering thing to sit with for a moment. It blew my mind when I saw it.

And if you want another way to feel the gap: a person on disability in America earns several times what the average doctor earns in some of the world's developing nations. A doctor. Someone who gave years of their life to schooling, who has a medical degree, who sees patients every day — in some parts of the world, that person earns a fraction of what we hand out monthly to those who can't work. We are a blessed nation.

---

We are blessed in other ways too.

Think about peace. We have enjoyed nearly two decades of relative peace on our own soil. Think about what is happening right now in Ukraine. In Israel. In Syria. In Mexico. Countries where the news doesn't cover whether traffic is heavy on the interstate — it covers which neighborhoods got bombed last night. There are places in the world where checking an active-warfare report is just part of the morning routine. Praise God that is not our morning routine.

Think about gas. I read a report out of Haiti where the most recent tax increases brought fuel costs to a point where the average Haitian has to spend roughly two and a half days' wages to buy a single gallon of gas. I live practically across the street from the church and I would burn more gas in a week than I'd earn in a day — and I have to think about that in terms of dollar amounts, not in terms of whether my family will eat. In Haiti, those two questions are connected.

Think about food. A missionary friend who spent time in China told me that America is one of the very few countries in the world wealthy enough that we do not think of insects as a food source. And that is genuinely unusual — there is a word for eating insects now, entomophagy, because the practice is so common globally. Roughly two billion people — about a quarter of the world's population — intentionally eat insects as part of their diet. In about 80% of the world's nations, insects are on the menu.

Meanwhile, California produces around 13% of all US agricultural cash receipts. And that is just one state. The Central Valley — the most productive agricultural region in the world — feeds not just America but enormous portions of the globe, and that is before you count the corn belt, the wheat belt, or the fertile farmland across the South and Midwest. We are drowning in food. We throw away roughly 200 pounds of it per person per year, or about one-third of total world food production — food valued at approximately one trillion dollars annually — in the trash. We turn our noses up at a bruised banana. We throw out lettuce because it started to wilt. In most of the world, you find an orange with some mold on it, you cut the mold off and you call yourself fortunate. Here we throw the whole thing away.

We are so blessed.

And we haven't even gotten to religious liberty. My friend Kurt Albrecht has a shirt that says: *This shirt is illegal in 53 countries.* Flip it over and the back reads Romans 1:16 — *"For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes."* There are roughly fifty countries on the face of the earth where possessing a Bible is against the law, restricted, or actively dangerous. We have more than one copy — most of us have several — in our own language. Some of us have them gathering dust on a shelf. We are incredibly blessed.

I don't think I need to convince you.

### The Bridge — Israel Was Blessed Too

The Israelites were blessed, too.

When they walked out of Egypt, they didn't leave empty-handed. The Egyptians — the same people who had worked them as slaves for generations — paid them to leave. Gold, silver, livestock. This was a world where wealth was counted in animals, and they walked out of slavery loaded with both. There are parts of the world today where that's still the standard. A missionary friend of mine served in Africa as a single man, and he told me that fathers would regularly approach him and try to trade their daughters for a few sheep, some pigs, a bag or two of grain. He said some of the local men he worked with could travel to a poor village with two bags of grain and come home with a wife. Women, aren't we glad we don't operate that way here.

Not only had God given Israel material blessing — He had given them manna. Every morning. They never had to wonder where the next meal was coming from. They never had to worry about running out. They had been slaves. Their parents had known the whip. But this generation had never worn chains. They grew up free, fed, protected — and whenever they followed the Lord, militarily invincible. Nations around them were in awe of them. Nobody wanted to pick that fight.

They were blessed.

### Part 1 — The Pattern of Ingratitude

Here is the thing about Israel. Knowing everything God had done for them, they had a pattern.

The pattern was: God does something extraordinary. Israel doubts. God does something more extraordinary. Israel complains. God provides in a way no one could have imagined. Israel whines that things were better before.

Follow the thread with me.

They were still in Egypt when Moses arrived. God's man was among them, working toward their freedom, and their response was: *God has clearly forgotten about us.* So God moves in a series of signs and wonders — plagues that nobody in the ancient world could miss — and not only gets them out but gets the Egyptians to pay them a parting gift. They walk out of slavery rich.

Then they get to the Red Sea with Pharaoh's entire army behind them. And the response? *Oh, Moses. You've brought us out here to die.* So God splits the sea, walls of water on both sides, they walk across on dry ground, and Pharaoh's cavalry follows them in and drowns. They watch it happen with their own eyes.

Then they realize two million people can't last long without water in the desert. Rather than pray, they complain. *God, why would you free us from slavery just to kill us of thirst?* So God brings water out of a rock. Literal water. From a rock.

Then the manna starts falling every morning. And that's not good enough either. *The food choices in Egypt were better. At least we had meat there. Sure, there were whips, but we had meat.* So God sends quail. More meat than they knew what to do with.

Finally they get to the promised land. Spies go in. Most of them come back terrified. The reasoning is something like: God defeated the world's greatest superpower at the time, He made food fall from the sky, He produced water from solid rock — but these particular people in Canaan? He probably can't handle them.

That made God more than a little angry.

So He waited. Forty years in the wilderness. And still — we saw it last week — Achan was in his tent hiding items devoted to the powerless sticks the Canaanites called gods. Items devoted to the gods of the people God had just destroyed.

The conquest that followed was remarkable precisely because you don't read what you'd expect to read. You don't read about men losing limbs, or casualties, or battles gone wrong. You read about victories — battle after battle orchestrated by God in ways that were obviously, undeniably supernatural. The battle of Jericho happens during the spring season, when the Canaanite fertility cult was at its height — and God has Joshua march around the walls with trumpets and shouts, the city falls, and every idol in it is destroyed. If Baal were real, wouldn't he have had something to say about that? Being a piece of wood, he said nothing.

Over and over. City after city. Nation after nation. The God who brought water from a rock proving to everyone within earshot that the sticks the Canaanites worshiped were exactly that — sticks.

And they still end up here, in Joshua 24, where God essentially has to say it plainly: *Are you going to worship that dead stick — the one we've seen defeated and destroyed a hundred times — or are you going to worship me?*

Choose this day who you will serve.

### Part 2 — God Has Done the Same for Us

He has done the same in our lives. Hasn't He.

How many times has God moved in your life — moved for your benefit, despite your faithfulness being far from what it should have been? We talked this morning about all the ways we are blessed just by virtue of living where we live. Nobody is going to arrest you for owning a Bible. Nobody is going to shoot you on the way to church. Nobody is going to lock up your spouse because you are a Christian — these things happen, right now, daily, around the world. And yet here we are, complaining about traffic.

He has also moved in ways specific to this room.

My mom had rheumatoid arthritis for years. If you look at her hands you can still see the damage it left — the unnatural angles it forced her fingers into. One Sunday, Pastor Bob gave an invitation for healing prayer. She went forward. She went in for her next routine arthritis check, and they told her that while they could see the physical damage the disease had left behind, her bloodwork no longer showed any sign of arthritis. God had healed her.

I could talk about Irma — but I mention her all the time, you know the story. God has moved for Bob's kids. Rob would not be here today without direct intervention. The way He has looked after Julie and those kids in New York.

And some of you in this room — He has freed you from slave masters, too. We have two men in this congregation for whom alcohol suddenly came to taste like lighter fluid. That was not a natural occurrence. God did it. Others have been freed from dependency on drugs, legal and otherwise. Captive. And then free.

He freed us. He fed us. He kept us from starving.

### Part 3 — But There Are Idols in the Camp

Yet here we are.

I want to be careful here, because I think on balance this church does better than most. But I want to ask you some honest questions — and you don't need to raise your hands.

How many of you read your Bible every day this week? How many prayed the way you know you ought to pray? How many of you, in every sphere of your life — at work, at home, in how you spend your money and your time — how many of you are a reflection of Christ in all of it?

Now, by show of hands: how many of you have a TV show you watch regularly? Put your hands down. Don't raise them for this next one — but how many of you got your TV show in this week without getting any time in your Bible or in prayer?

I'm not throwing stones. It is the easiest thing in the world to fall into. But what we make time for is what we actually love. And if the show always gets its hour and the Lord often doesn't — what does that say?

There are other idols. Status. Money. Relationships. Substances. Stuff. Cars. The list is long, and it's personal to every person in the room. But every one of them is the same thing Israel was bowing to: a powerless stick.

And they're losing to it. Because unlike the Canaanite idols, which at least had no real power to offer, our idols do give us something — a temporary feeling, a temporary comfort, a brief satisfaction. But that's all they are. Temporary. They don't deliver. They don't free. They don't heal. They don't split seas. They don't make water come from rocks. They are no better, in the end, than a powerless stick.

---

### Close — Choose This Day

So here we are.

Maybe you've been away. Maybe the sticks have had your attention for a season, and you know it, and something tonight is telling you it's time to come back to the things that matter. Tonight is the night to put them down.

Or maybe you have never truly been a follower of Jesus. Maybe you've been in church, been around the church, know the language — but you have never crossed the line. Jesus said it plainly in John 14:6: *"I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."* Not one of the ways. Not a good option. *The* way.

Tonight is the night to turn to Christ for the first time, or to turn back to Him.

The God who has been faithful over and over and over — to Israel, to this country, to this congregation, to you personally — that God is still in the business of freeing slaves, healing the sick, and making water come from rocks.

But as for me and my house, we will serve the Lord.

As the music begins — if you need to make a decision tonight, come find me.

## Fact-Check Flags Before Print or Repeat

**1. Kyrgyzstan doctor salary / "45x"**
REMOVE the "45x" figure. Verification finds no support for this ratio. Current data for public-sector doctors in Kyrgyzstan runs approximately $3,000-$3,200/year; the 2017 base-salary low was ~$864/year. At either figure, $10,000 is 3-12x, not 45x. The manuscript uses "several times" instead, which is accurate. If a more specific figure is desired, "roughly 10 times" (using the 2017 base rate) is the upper defensible range.

**2. "60 billion tons" of food waste**
This is almost certainly a unit error — should be **pounds, not tons**. World total food production is roughly 9-10 billion tons per year; 60 billion tons of US food waste alone would be 6x total world production, which is physically impossible. The EPA baseline figure for US food waste is approximately 66-80 million tons (130-160 billion pounds) annually. The "60 billion" figure may refer to pounds in a specific sector estimate. Manuscript does not use this figure; uses only the verified "200 lbs/person/year" and "1/3 of world production" figures.

**3. California county / "13% of world food"**
CORRECTED. "13%" is California's share of US agricultural cash receipts — not world food production. No single county produces 13% of world food. Fresno County is the most productive single agricultural county in the US ($8-9 billion/year), and the Central Valley as a whole produces enormous quantities, but the world-share claim is not supported. Manuscript reframes as California's share of US production.

**4. "80% of the world's population" eats insects**
CORRECTED. The accurate figure is approximately **2 billion people** (roughly 25% of world population). The "80%" refers to the percentage of the world's *nations* in which entomophagy is practiced, not the percentage of people. Manuscript uses "roughly two billion people — about a quarter of the world's population."

**5. "$3 trillion" food waste value**
CORRECTED to approximately **$1 trillion** (global market value, FAO/UN). The $2.6 trillion figure exists but includes environmental and social costs. $3 trillion does not match any standard published source. Manuscript uses "approximately one trillion dollars."

**6. World income percentile**
The "top 10%" claim is defensible using nominal dollar comparison (top 10-15%). Using PPP adjustment it is closer to top 20-30%. Manuscript uses "top 10%" framed clearly as a website estimate, which is accurate by the most common non-PPP methodology.

**7. Bible access restrictions**
The "50 countries" / "Kurt's shirt says 53" figures are consistent with Open Doors, VOM, and similar organizations. Magnitude is confirmed. The specific three-category breakdown (50 illegal / 40 restricted / 13 sub-national areas) from the notes could not be traced to a single published source. Manuscript uses Kurt's shirt as the reference point (53 countries) without the specific breakdown.
