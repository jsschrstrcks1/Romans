# Psalm 1 — Blessed Is the Man

**Text: Psalm 1** (full)

---

If you've brought your Bible with you today — and I hope you have — go ahead and find Psalm 1. There's one in the pew ahead of you if you need it. Psalms is roughly in the middle of your Bible. Work your way toward the front from there. If you hit Proverbs, come back a little. If you landed in Chronicles, Ezra, Nehemiah, Esther, or Job, keep going.

While you're turning there, let me tell you about our village of the week.

---

**Khedi Buzurg**

Eighty-five percent of everyone in the Khedi Buzurg is involved in agriculture in some way — a major way or a minor one. The very old and very young are excused, but most everyone else, including children and much of the elderly, works because the family needs to eat to survive. It's a fairly small village — around two thousand people in three hundred or so houses. There are mosques. There are Hindu temples. But there are no churches that I could find. In fact, they are classified as unreached and unengaged, which means that to the best of anyone's knowledge there are no believers among them — and no missionaries working in their midst either.

Let's pray.

---

I read an article this week by Ed Stetzer, the former Executive Director of LifeWay Research, about Bible reading in North America. He started with a question: how many Bibles do you own?

Let's start there. Raise your hand if you have a Bible. More than two? More than five? More than ten? More than fifty? More than a hundred? That's not really a fair question for me to ask — I have what amounts to a collection of Bible translations, about a hundred and twenty-nine right now. I didn't set out to do that. But here we are.

According to LifeWay Research, nine in ten American homes have at least one Bible. But nearly one in five regular churchgoers say they never read it. Among those who call themselves Christians, only a fraction read more than a passage here or there around the holidays.

If the Bible is God's love letter to us — if it is the sum total of His special revelation to us about Himself — and we claim to love and worship God, shouldn't we be reading His Word?

I talked to someone once who said, *"But pastor, I read the Bible once."*

Once.

My answer to that was something the ancient Greek philosopher Heraclitus observed — that you cannot step into the same river twice, because the waters are always different, and so is the man who steps in. The river is the river. But the waters keep moving. And you keep changing.

Our Bible doesn't change. But we do. Your understanding of life changes. What God has revealed to you changes. Because we are always in a state of change — no matter how much anyone hates change, they are still always changing — what you take away from Scripture always changes too. People have spent their whole lives reading the Bible over and over and never fully plumbed its depths, because each time they try, their measuring stick has changed because they have changed.

Charles Spurgeon put it this way:

> "Many books in my library are now behind and beneath me. They were good in their way once, and so were the clothes I wore when I was ten years old; but I have outgrown them. Nobody ever outgrows Scripture; the book widens and deepens with our years."

Think of it like a string of light bulbs coming on in a perfectly dark room. Imagine slowing it down and watching in slow motion. The first bulb lights and we can see a little — the bulb itself, the rudiments of the room. The second lights and we notice there's a whole string of them. The third, and there's a table. The fourth, and there's something on the table. And on it goes until the room is fully lit and a feast is laid out in front of us.

Close your eyes after the first bulb and you'll never see the banquet.

The Bible is like the dining room on a cruise ship. Every single night we went back to that main dining room, they had something amazing waiting — cheese plates, crackers, escargot, shrimp cocktail, lobster bisque. A dozen appetizers. Then a beautiful steak, or fish, or lamb, or sometimes multiples of all three. One night they served us snapper — the best snapper I've ever eaten. Moist, flavorful, a little sweet, with a crispy skin. Perfectly cooked. I didn't know fish could have such a fantastic flavor. Another night, the perfect steak. The crispy goat cheese salad. Crème brûlée. Cheesecake. Chocolate everything. The food of kings, hot and ready and waiting.

But what if instead of eating, we'd sat there with our eyes closed and our noses pinched shut, breathing through our mouths, drinking a glass of water, ignoring all of it. Wouldn't that be absurd? You'd leave dinner hungry, undernourished, and probably a little grouchy. You wouldn't have the fuel to get through the week.

This is exactly what the Christian does who ignores their Bible. They do it to their own detriment, and to the disappointment of God.

Psalm 1 gives a list of stern warnings about what happens when we neglect God's Word. Open your Bible there with me.

---

*[READ: Psalm 1:1–6 — ESV]*

> 1 Blessed is the man
>     who walks not in the counsel of the wicked,
> nor stands in the way of sinners,
>     nor sits in the seat of scoffers;
> 2 but his delight is in the law of the Lord,
>     and on his law he meditates day and night.
> 3 He is like a tree
>     planted by streams of water
> that yields its fruit in its season,
>     and its leaf does not wither.
> In all that he does, he prospers.
> 4 The wicked are not so,
>     but are like chaff that the wind drives away.
> 5 Therefore the wicked will not stand in the judgment,
>     nor sinners in the congregation of the righteous;
> 6 for the Lord knows the way of the righteous,
>     but the way of the wicked will perish.

---

I count twelve warnings in this psalm for the person who neglects the Word of God.

They walk in the counsel of the wicked (v. 1). They stand in the way of sinners (v. 1). They sit in the seat of scoffers (v. 1). They do not delight in the law of the Lord (v. 2). They do not meditate on the law day and night (v. 2). They are not planted by streams of water (v. 3). They do not yield fruit in their season (v. 3). Their leaf withers (v. 3). They do not prosper (v. 3). They are like chaff the wind drives away (v. 4). They will not stand in the judgment (v. 5). They will perish (v. 6).

Let's go through a few of these.

---

**Walking in the counsel of the wicked**

What happens when you walk in the counsel of the wicked? Anyone have a story they're brave enough to share?

I think about Absalom. He had turned against his own father — David, God's ordained king of Israel — and was chasing him with an army, trying to overthrow him, trying to kill him. David, who wrote many of the psalms. David, whose son Solomon would be counted the wisest man who ever lived. David, for whom God still had enormous plans.

After one particular battle, David had been defeated. His troops were demoralized. He was on the run. Absalom was then given competing advice. His most trusted counselor, Ahithophel, urged him to strike immediately — don't let David regroup. A second man, Hushai — who was secretly loyal to David — urged delay: gather all Israel first, make sure the force is overwhelming, then march.

Ahithophel's advice was the militarily sound move. But Scripture says God confused Absalom so that he would reject it. Second Samuel 17:14: *"For the Lord had ordained to defeat the good counsel of Ahithophel, so that the Lord might bring harm upon Absalom."*

Absalom chose Hushai's advice. His momentum stopped. His rebellion collapsed. We read about King Solomon in Scripture, not King Absalom. Everything Absalom had been planning turned to rust and ruin.

When we neglect the Word of God and stop walking in the counsel of God, we end up walking in the counsel of the wicked. We make choices for the wrong reasons. We take advice from the wrong places. And we end up like Absalom — our plans undone, our worst fears realized, the consequences stretching out for years.

---

**Sitting in the seat of scoffers**

I also read an article this week by Denny Burk that outlined what it means to be a scoffer. A scoffer speaks with derision and contempt. He aims that contempt at other people. He is proud and unteachable — the opposite of wise. He amplifies other people's failures. And ultimately he reaches his doom.

Proverbs 19:29 says, *"Condemnation is ready for scoffers, and beating for the backs of fools."* And Isaiah wrote that scoffers will find their end: *"for the ruthless shall come to nothing and the scoffer cease, and all who watch to do evil shall be cut off"* (Isaiah 29:20).

The Roman Stoic philosopher Seneca wrote — and he was passing along an old proverb when he did — *"Speech is the index of the mind."* What comes out of your mouth tells us who you are.

A scoffer uses his mouth to sin. Slander. Gossip. Lying. Flattery. Pride. The quick tongue. Cursing.

So let me ask you a few questions.

Can you receive correction? Are your words marked by strict adherence to the truth? Do you talk about people negatively behind their backs? Do your words build up or tear down? Have you torn anyone down this week? Have you used your mouth to talk yourself up? Have you made foolish choices?

If so, maybe you need more time in Scripture and less time watching television, following football, working, or whatever else you've decided matters more than reading God's Word. It's hard at first. Your mind will wander. It's like any muscle — you have to use it to get better.

---

**Not delighting in the law of the Lord**

I think this single failure — the failure of verse 2, not delighting in the law of the Lord — is the root of most of what we see going wrong in the world.

Killing unborn children is something people are willing to do when they don't know or don't care that God says we are made in His image, and the life of an image-bearer is not to be taken lightly.

Homosexuality is embraced when people don't know or don't care that God has a specific design for mankind and calls us to follow it.

The exploitation of children is only possible where people have made themselves numb to what Scripture says about human dignity and the worth of the weak. We've watched this unfold publicly — Epstein, and the powerful men and women documented in his orbit, who used access and wealth to exploit young women, some of them minors, who were lured with promises of careers and told once they were trapped that they had no choice. The Clintons, Prince Andrew — these are people whose connections to Epstein are documented in flight logs and court proceedings.

Elisabeth Elliot wrote — this is from her book *A Lamp unto My Feet* —

> "The Word of God I think of as a straight edge, which shows up our own crookedness. We can't really tell how crooked our thinking is until we line it up with the straight edge of Scripture."

Every societal illness traces back to that. People who have stopped lining their lives up against the straight edge of God's Word.

---

**The tree planted by water**

As we bring things to a close, look at the illustration in verse 3. Will someone read verses 1 through 3 for me?

> 1 Blessed is the man
>     who walks not in the counsel of the wicked,
> nor stands in the way of sinners,
>     nor sits in the seat of scoffers;
> 2 but his delight is in the law of the Lord,
>     and on his law he meditates day and night.
> 3 He is like a tree
>     planted by streams of water
> that yields its fruit in its season,
>     and its leaf does not wither.
> In all that he does, he prospers.

My grandparents had a stand of banana trees. The bananas were a little different from what you'd find at the grocery store — a little sweet, a little like apple mixed with banana. Pretty good. One day the neighbors cut one down — a young plant, just coming up. I was upset about it. But I thought maybe I could save it. I stuck it in a trash can full of water and left it sit for about a week. Then I dug a hole and planted it by the canal.

It struggled. It had been severed from its roots and sat in a trash can for a week. It needed constant attention because it had no way to feed itself properly. If a strong wind had come, it would have gone over. If a drought had come, it would have withered and died. So I tended it daily. And slowly, over time, it re-grew roots and sent them down deep. When they finally reached the canal, the tree exploded with growth. It drank up the nutrient-rich water and grew and produced fruit and produced baby banana trees.

For a banana tree, its best life is when its roots are deep into soil that carries what it needs. In the right spot, that tree grew like crazy.

Cut off from its roots? Left in the trash can? Dead.

Christian — apart from God, apart from His Word, apart from the church — you are like that banana tree that's been cut down. Can a cut tree grow? No. Can it heal itself? No. What can it do? Wither, and dry out, and be blown away by the wind.

Don't raise your hands. But I want you to think about the last month.

Have you been sinking your roots down deep into Scripture? Have you been sitting at the dinner table on the cruise ship? Have you been allowing your soul to be nourished by the Word of God? Or are you going through life with your eyes closed and your nose pinched shut — surrounded by the best food there is, and still starving?

---

Normally we talk about the gospel at the end of the service. But today I want to ask you to commit to reading your Bible through with us over the next year. I have a reading plan — see Dottie for a copy, and we'll put the week's reading in the bulletin. It's not overwhelming. Four chapters most weeks, sometimes parts of chapters. I'll be preaching from the current reading or the week ahead. We'll all be moving through it together.

Will you commit to that?

I've printed up a commitment card. It's up here at the front. As the praise team comes up, I want you to seek the Lord. If you decide not to sign it, pray and honestly tell God why His Word isn't important enough to warrant your time right now. If you can't bring yourself to pray that prayer — then come sign your name.

---
## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**"A Warm Invitation to Bible Reading That Never Becomes a Sermon on Psalm 1"**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A winsome, accessible call to consistent Bible reading that draws loosely from Psalm 1 but consistently imposes a thesis — "read your Bible more" — onto a text whose actual argument concerns the two ways, the character of the blessed man, and the certainty of divine judgment.

---

### 3. Berean Gate

**Scopus Test: FAIL**

The sermon's thesis — "you should read your Bible regularly" — does not arise from Psalm 1's own argument. Psalm 1 is not a motivational sermon about Bible reading habits. It is a wisdom poem that opens the Psalter with a binary vision of human existence: the blessed man who meditates on the Torah and the wicked man who will not stand in judgment. Its scopus is the contrast between the two ways and the certainty of God's discriminating knowledge ("the Lord knows the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked will perish," v. 6). The sermon treats the psalm as a list of consequences for neglecting Bible reading. That is not what the text is doing.

**Authority Test: PARTIAL PASS / PARTIAL FAIL**

The preacher does not soften the text's warnings — chaff, perishing, not standing in judgment are named. But the text's hard edge is functionally redirected: these warnings become warnings about not reading your Bible, rather than about the nature of the wicked who stand under divine judgment. The psalm's binary judgment of persons (righteous vs. wicked) is softened into a spectrum of believers who read more or less. The Authority Test exposes the substitution.

**GATE STATUS: FAIL on Scopus. Evaluation continues with score cap at 69 per Fatal Flaw #2.**

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Fatal Flaw #2: Main point does not equal text's point.**
The sermon's controlling idea is "read your Bible." Psalm 1's controlling idea is the character, blessing, and security of the man who delights in the Torah, set against the perishing of the wicked — a portrait of two destinies governed by God's discriminating knowledge. These are not the same claim.

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Fatal Flaw #1: No clear gospel presentation.**
The gospel is explicitly deferred: "Normally we talk about the gospel at the end of the service. But today I want to ask you to commit to reading your Bible through with us." The cross is never presented. Justification is absent. Christ is present only by implication. No listener could understand how to be saved from this sermon.

**FLAG TRIGGERED — Fatal Flaw #3: Christ is absent or unnecessary.**
The sermon would survive intact without Jesus. The call to Bible reading could be given by a devout Jewish rabbi or a thoughtful secular humanist who believed in self-improvement through reading. Christ's person and work are not integrated into the argument at any point.

**Score is capped at 69.**

### 5. Score Breakdown

**1. Exposition & Hermeneutics — 12/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (10 pts): 5/10.* The preacher reads the text, identifies the images (tree, water, chaff), and names consequences from the passage. But the controlling interpretive move — "I count twelve warnings for the person who neglects the Word of God" — is hermeneutically strained. Psalm 1 is not structured as a warning list for the neglectful Christian; it is a contrast portrait of two types of persons. The sermon imports a thesis and mines the psalm for material rather than deriving the sermon from the psalm's own argument.
- *Contextual Awareness (5 pts): 2/5.* There is no acknowledgment of Psalm 1's canonical function as the gateway psalm to the entire Psalter, no reference to the Torah's meaning in its original sense (the whole instruction of God, not merely a reading program), and no placement within the Psalter's structure or redemptive-historical context.
- *Exegetical Clarity (5 pts): 3/5.* The banana tree illustration does genuine work for verse 3. The progression of "walking, standing, sitting" is noted in the twelve-warnings list but not expounded with any depth. No Hebrew engagement. The meditation ("hagah") language — with its sense of murmuring, ruminating, incessant turning over — is passed over entirely.
- *Logical Faithfulness (5 pts): 2/5.* The interpretive reframe from "description of the righteous/wicked" to "warning list for neglectful readers" is eisegetical. The FCF (Fallen Condition Focus) is implicitly "we don't read our Bibles enough," which, while a real condition, is not the condition Psalm 1 was written to address. Psalm 1 was written to call the reader to a fundamental orientation — delight in and meditation on the Torah as the posture of the blessed man — not merely to increase devotional frequency.

**2. Theology & Doctrinal Integrity — 9/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (7 pts): 2/7.* Christ is absent. No organic movement to him, no typological awareness that "the blessed man" points toward and finds its fullness in the one man who perfectly delighted in the law and never walked in the counsel of the wicked. The sermon does not attempt Christological connection.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (3 pts): 1/3.* No placement of Psalm 1 within the story of redemption. Torah as the covenantal instruction of Yahweh to Israel, now fulfilled and illumined by Christ, is untouched.
- *Doctrinal Precision (4 pts): 3/4.* What doctrine is present is not sloppy. The warnings about judgment in verse 5 are acknowledged. No errors, but minimal precision.
- *Confessional Alignment (4 pts): 3/4.* Nothing contradicts confessional theology. The sermon simply does not engage the doctrinal terrain deeply enough to surface either alignment or deviation.

**3. Gospel Centrality & Force — 2/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (5 pts): 0/5.* The gospel is explicitly deferred and never delivered. This is a fatal failure.
- *Necessity of Christ (5 pts): 1/5.* One point is granted for the mention that "normally we talk about the gospel," signaling the preacher's awareness of its importance, but Christ is not proclaimed as necessary to the sermon's argument.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (5 pts): 1/5.* The sermon moves toward a commitment card and a reading plan — not toward the cross.

The Synagogue Test: A devout Jew or a practicing Muslim could sit through this sermon without offense. The cross is absent. The sermon passes through a Jewish congregation undisturbed.

**4. Conscience & Applicatory Force — 8/15**

- *Heart Penetration (5 pts): 3/5.* The closing section — "Don't raise your hands. But I want you to think about the last month" — is a genuine conscience move. The self-diagnostic questions about the tongue (scoffer section) are specific and searching. The commitment card device is pastoral and has real pressure.
- *Specificity (5 pts): 3/5.* The Epstein reference, the abortion and homosexuality examples, the television/football competition — these land in real life. Some of these are more catalog than application (the list of societal ills in the "not delighting" section reads as commentary rather than searching address).
- *Spiritual Differentiation (5 pts): 2/5.* The sermon addresses primarily the already-believing, somewhat-neglectful churchgoer. It does not address the unbeliever, the backslider with a serious reason for absence, the hardened scoffer who is present, or the believer in genuine spiritual desolation. Perkins' taxonomy is largely untouched beyond categories 3 and 5.

**5. Exhortation & Response — 5/8**

- *Call to Response (4 pts): 3/4.* The commitment card is a concrete ask. The closing exhortation is pastoral and specific: sign the card or pray and honestly tell God why His Word doesn't warrant your time. That second option — pray it if you won't sign — is quietly brilliant pastoral pressure.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (4 pts): 2/4.* The reading plan equips for personal holiness. The sermon does not send the congregation out for witness, does not connect Bible reading to mission, and does not address the corporate or communal dimension of the church's life in the Word.

**6. Structure & Logical Flow — 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (4 pts): 3/4.* The sermon is easy to follow: introduction (Bible reading problem) → text reading → select themes from the twelve "warnings" → tree illustration → commitment call. It is not formally outlined but is practically navigable.
- *Progression (3 pts): 2/3.* The sermon builds toward the commitment call with genuine pastoral intention. The middle section (walking, sitting, the scoffers, the societal ills) is somewhat episodic — it visits themes rather than developing a sustained argument.
- *Unity (2 pts): 1/2.* The thesis of "read your Bible" holds the surface together, but the detour through Epstein, the political figures, and the societal catalog introduces material that competes with rather than advances the central appeal.

**7. Illustration & Imagination — 4/5**

- *Relevance (2 pts): 2/2.* The banana tree illustration is native to this congregation and this text. The cruise ship dining room is memorable and accessible. The Heraclitus river metaphor is elegantly used without being labored.
- *Clarity (2 pts): 1/2.* The banana tree does genuine illuminating work for verse 3. The cruise ship illustration runs slightly long and begins to accumulate menu items past the point of illustration and into the territory of food memoir.
- *Memorability (1 pt): 1/1.* The banana tree and the light bulbs coming on in the dark room will be carried home.

This is the sermon's genuine strength. The manuscript reads as spoken, not written. Sentence variation is natural. The direct address is earned, not decorative: "Don't raise your hands. But I want you to think about the last month." The Spurgeon quote is well-placed and integrates smoothly. The voice is pastoral, warm, and unhurried. The closing commitment device has real homiletical craft. The cadence fits the intimacy of a congregation the preacher knows.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?** Primarily myself and my reading habits. Christ is absent from the argument.
2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?** Ideas about the benefits of Bible reading. The sermon does not produce an encounter with the living God.
3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?** The closing conscience section approaches conviction. But the sermon's dominant register is motivational — the preacher is recruiting readers, not summoning sinners before a holy God.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Sermonic voice and craft.** The manuscript reads as genuine preaching, not an essay. The commitment card device is a shrewd pastoral instrument — the option to "pray and honestly tell God why His Word isn't worth your time" is a genuine conscience trap, and a well-constructed one.
2. **The banana tree illustration.** Native to the text, native to the congregation, earned by personal narrative, and theologically precise for verse 3 — rootedness, nourishment, explosive fruit-bearing when the roots reach the water.
3. **The conscience questions in the scoffer section.** "Can you receive correction? Are your words marked by strict adherence to the truth? Do you talk about people negatively behind their backs?" These are Perkins-adjacent in their searching particularity and land in real congregational life.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is not present.** The preacher announces its deferral in the second-to-last paragraph. This is not a secondary failure — it is the primary failure. The text ends at verse 6 with "the way of the wicked will perish." The sermon does not ask: how then does any man escape perishing? Christ is the answer. His absence is catastrophic.
2. **The sermon's thesis is not the text's thesis.** Psalm 1 does not say "read your Bible more." It presents two types of men — the blessed man who meditates on Torah and the wicked who will not stand in judgment — and declares that the Lord knows the way of the righteous. The sermon's opening move ("I count twelve warnings for the person who neglects the Word of God") imposes an alien structure on the text. This is not exposition; it is sermon decoration with Scripture.
3. **The political catalog in the "not delighting" section is a liability.** Naming the Clintons and Prince Andrew from the pulpit by name in connection with documented Epstein associations introduces political specificity that (a) competes with the text's argument, (b) risks alienating hearers along partisan lines before the gospel can reach them, and (c) does not arise from anything in Psalm 1. The psalm names no political figures. The sermon does.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher likely does not see that this sermon, for all its warmth, teaches a kind of functional Pelagianism: the blessed life is available to those who discipline themselves to read Scripture. The psalm's portrait of the blessed man is not primarily a moral achievement program — it is, in its canonical context and redemptive-historical trajectory, a portrait that finds its only perfect embodiment in Christ. The man who never walked in the counsel of the wicked, never stood in the way of sinners, never sat with scoffers, and whose delight was perfectly in the Torah — that man is Jesus. The congregation needs to see that before they are sent to their reading plans, or the reading plan will become another form of self-justification.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The commitment card section feels like a strong close. It has the structure of a response, the mechanics of an invitation, and the pastoral nerve of a real ask. But it is an invitation to a Bible-reading program, not an invitation to Christ. The emotional and pastoral weight of the close masks the fact that the gospel has not been preached. A person could sign that card, read through the Bible, and still be damned — and the sermon gives them no category for why that is true, because Christ has not been proclaimed as the one without whom all reading is merely religious self-improvement.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

Verse 6 is the theological climax of the psalm and the sermon barely touches it: "the Lord *knows* the way of the righteous." The Hebrew "knows" (yada) carries covenantal weight — this is not mere cognitive awareness but intimate relational knowledge, election-language. The blessed man is not blessed because he reads more; he is blessed because the Lord knows him. This is the psalm's own gospel move, and it leads directly to Christ — the one whom the Father knows and has known from before the foundation of the world, and through whom sinners become known by God (Galatians 4:9). The sermon walks up to the door and never opens it.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Rebuild the thesis from verse 6 down.** The sermon's main point should be: "The blessed life is not first a discipline — it is first a relationship. The Lord knows the way of the righteous. The question is not 'how much do you read?' but 'does God know you?'" Then the reading plan becomes the fruit of that relationship, not the root of it.
2. **Preach Christ from the "blessed man" portrait.** The man who perfectly embodied Psalm 1 is Jesus. Name him. Show how the psalm points beyond every reader's failure to the one who succeeded. Then the invitation is not "read more" but "come to the one in whom this psalm is fulfilled, and you will find yourself, by union with him, becoming the man Psalm 1 describes."
3. **Remove or radically trim the political names.** The Epstein illustration can make its point without naming the Clintons and Prince Andrew. The pulpit is not the place for political name-checks that fracture the congregation before the gospel can unify them.
4. **Add a direct address to the self-righteous reader.** The person who reads their Bible every day and is still in the seat of the scoffer is not addressed. The text's own tension — you can know the Law and still be among the wicked — should corner the diligent attender who has head-knowledge without heart-transformation.
5. **Restore the gospel before the commitment call.** The reading plan is a good pastoral tool. But it must be preceded by the good news that Christ is the fulfillment of the Torah, that through faith in him sinners are declared righteous, and that the Spirit he gives is the one who creates the very delight in the law that verse 2 describes. Without this, the commitment card is a works program.
6. **Expound verse 3 more carefully.** The banana tree illustration is the best moment in the sermon. It earns a longer stay. The phrase "yields its fruit in its season" — the patient, unhurried, unforced nature of genuine spiritual growth — is worth a full movement of development. What does fruit in its season look like for the man who meditates on the law? Name it concretely.

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag

**Not triggered** — the sermon is not doctrinally cold but pastorally misdirected. The problem is not dead orthodoxy but insufficient orthodoxy. The warmth is genuine; the theological architecture is incomplete.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Assessment

The sermon carries the warmth of a pastor who loves his people and wants them in the Word, but it does not yet carry the weight of a herald who has stood before God and been sent with a message — the eternal gravity is present in the commitment call but absent from the proclamation itself.

**Bonus: +1/5** — pastoral heart is evident; theological weight is not yet achieved.

### 17. Rubric Fit Note

Psalm 1 is wisdom literature functioning as a canonical introduction to the Psalter. The rubric's categories apply appropriately, with one adjustment: the "Fallen Condition Focus" for wisdom literature is sometimes more anthropological than explicitly soteriological — wisdom psalms diagnose the human condition through the two-ways framework. The rubric handles this well. However, the Christ-centeredness category (Category 2) is particularly important here because Psalm 1 is one of the psalms where organic Christological connection is not forced but genuinely textually warranted — the "blessed man" portrait finds its fulfillment in Christ in ways that are not artificial but arise from the psalm's own canonical and redemptive-historical logic. The rubric does not strain here; it is the sermon that declines the invitation the text extends.
