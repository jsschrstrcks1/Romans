### 3. Berean Gate

**CONDITIONAL PASS — significantly strained.**

This is a topical sermon rather than an expository sermon. The stated purpose is to teach hamartiology (the doctrine of sin), and all the texts deployed support that purpose. No clear text governs the sermon; Colossians 3:1–11 is cited but not read at the outset (it is referenced mid-paragraph). Galatians 5:19–21 is quoted. John 6:44 is the closing text.

**Scopus Test**: The sermon does not have a single text from which its argument arises. The main point (sin is weighty, deadly, and must drive us to God) is true and scripturally supported, but it is not the main point of any one text — it is a doctrinal summary assembled from multiple texts.

**Authority Test**: The preacher does not soften or edit the hard claims about sin. The texts are allowed to say what they say. The preacher does not stand above the texts — but neither does he stand under a single governing text as a herald.

Given that the rubric is designed for expository preaching, this sermon strains it. The Berean Gate passes on the grounds that the sermon is *topical teaching from Scripture* rather than eisegesis, and every claim is scripturally grounded. But the rubric fit is significantly reduced.

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The breadth of Scripture cited — Colossians, Galatians, Hebrews, James, John — creates the impression of a rich, text-dense sermon. In fact, each text is cited in a single sentence and moved past. ### 13. Missed Opportunity

The sermon is prompted by a conversation about antinomianism — someone arguing that because Christ died for all sin, sin has no weight. Romans 6:1–2 is the precise text Paul uses to answer exactly this question: "Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means!" This is quoted at the outset but then abandoned. Romans 6:1–14 could have served as the governing text for the entire sermon — Paul himself asks the antinomian's question and then demolishes it with the theology of union with Christ (precisely where Galatians 2:20 also lands). That connection between Romans 6 and Galatians 2:20 was present and unused.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes

1. **Choose a governing text**: Either Colossians 3:1–11 (where the sermon appears to begin) or Romans 6:1–14 (which Paul wrote in direct answer to the antinomian argument that prompted the sermon). Preach that text; let all other passages serve it.
2. **Write the conclusion**: The "Lets close in prayer" line needs to be replaced with a full gospel proclamation — the balm that follows the hammer. The congregation has been shown what sin is and what it costs. They must now be told where relief is found and what they must do.
3. **Develop the gospel proportionally to the law**: If the sermon hammers sin for ten minutes, the gospel must receive proportional time and weight. "His dying breath has brought me life" is a hymn reference, not a proclaimed gospel.
4. **Add differentiated exhortation**: At minimum — one sentence to the one trapped in antinomianism, one sentence to the one crushed by their sin, one sentence to the believer enduring painful sanctification, and one invitation to the seeker. These are the actual categories of hearers in this room.
5. **Move the John 6:44 section to the sermon's theological climax**: Before the gospel call, not after it. "God must draw you — and he is drawing you right now. Will you come?"
