# Suffering Is Not Outside of God's Plan

**Exodus 2:23-25**

---

One of the commentaries I read this week asked an interesting question. If God is good, how could he allow the Israelites to become slaves? It's an interesting question, and it stuck with me — because so many people have asked similar questions. You heard a story about someone asking this just the other week. My cousin, whose husband was diagnosed with MS shortly after he became a Christian, who just last year fell and broke his leg, and a year later his leg remains unhealed — who just a few weeks ago was told she might have breast cancer. In her anguish she asked why? Why, if God is good, why if God loves me would he let me go through this? She said this is too much to bear. I think if we're honest we've all been there, and if you're too young of a believer to have been through something like this, know that one day — unless the Lord returns — you too will enter into the crucible.

Turn with me back to Exodus 2. We will be in vv. 23-25 tonight.

---

While God's people were living in comfort, at least early on, the enemies of Israel in the promised land were fighting with each other and wiping each other out. The Hittites, the Amorites, the Perizzites, the Hivites, the Canaanites, and the Jebusites — and countless other groups — were attacking each other and killing each other. One group would come in and destroy the next, and they would be destroyed by a third. Even Egypt fought in the promised land at one point. Chaos and fighting marked the area for nearly half a millennium.

While all of this was going on, the Israelites were growing in number. Early on they were favored by Pharaoh — they were happy, healthy, and the 75 who moved into Egypt became at least two million. Scripture says 600,000 men. So there would have been at least that many women. If every couple has at least one child, we're suddenly at 1.8 million people. 75 people wouldn't have been able to stand against the Hittites on their own. 75 people wouldn't have been able to stand against the Amorites. And certainly — on their own — could not have handled the army of Egypt. By living in Egypt they learned a fair bit of the country's knowledge — certainly how they build things, how to make bricks. They were able to grow strong while centuries of fighting would have weakened all of the surrounding enemies. Ultimately we know God destroys the army of Egypt as well. So in spite of the slavery, the people of Israel left with more people, with stronger backs, than they could have imagined when they entered Egypt.

They were stronger spiritually too. Remember we talked a few months ago about how Jacob had tolerated idols in his house, and how he thought he was participating in idol worship? Read with me:

> *And the people of Israel groaned because of their slavery and cried out for help. Their cry for rescue from slavery came up to God. And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob. God saw the people of Israel—and God knew.*
> — Exodus 2:23-25

The commentary points out that when things are going well, people often lose their awareness of their need for God's help. Maybe people grow overconfident. People seem to grow confident in their own ability to face the trials and challenges of life and eternity. But I'm here to tell you — we are not strong enough on our own, and in our own power, to face everything that life can throw at us. Much less eternity. Sin has left a crimson stain upon our soul that we are incapable of washing off on our own. Apart from Christ that stain remains and marks us as living in rebellion against God. So I think God allowed them to feel this helplessness so that they could be reminded, as a people, of their dependence upon the one true God — to drive home their need for a savior, and ultimately to draw them as a people closer to God.

I think oftentimes we are allowed to go through hard times for much the same reasons. I see at least three reasons here.

---

**For one, God was seeking to protect his people.** That may sound funny, considering they spent 430 years as slaves. But it is no less true. If they had remained in the promised land rather than traveling to Egypt — apart from the direct intervention of God — they would have been destroyed many times over, or brought into subjugation by a far less merciful master. Then when the Jebusites, or the Midianites, were wiped out, the Israelites might have been as well.

Their time in Egypt was like the time a baby chick spends in an egg. It's warm and comfortable at first. But by the end they are constricted, and drowning is a very real risk for a baby chick. They eventually start breathing in the egg, and if their beak is pointed the wrong direction they die. If they don't start breaking out of their shell — an arduous process — they die. But the process of breaking out of their shell causes them to strengthen certain muscles that don't develop otherwise. If you help a chick out of its shell it can cause them to spend the rest of their lives lame and weak. But by having to fight their way out, they are able to thrive. God allows us to go through hard times because sometimes the difficult thing we're going through is less painful than — or certainly more beneficial to us and our growth than — the alternative.

---

**Secondly, God allows us to go through hard times to strengthen us.** The Israelites were physically much stronger when they left Egypt. They would have been carrying the ancient equivalent of concrete blocks all day. As a people they would have been quite a bit tougher. They were subject to cruel conditions. They may have been hungry at times, and certainly as the number of bricks they were ordered to make in a day were increased — as we will see down the line — they likely would have been deprived of sleep. They were being pushed beyond what they thought they could endure, and were being purified like gold in a crucible.

The crucible in the Marine Corps is something marines use in their training. It's the final test before they let you become a marine. It's a 54-hour arduous test of mental, physical, and moral endurance. They force the future marines to endure a variety of situations, including combat tests and so forth. They are deprived of sleep and made to march 45 miles in those 54 hours. They are given minimal food, and pushed — I imagine — beyond what they would have considered to be their limits before boot camp. But the product that is produced by marine boot camp is stronger, tougher, more confident. People are broken down and rebuilt into marines fit for service in the Marine Corps. So this second reason God allows us to go through hard times is so that we too can go through the crucible and come out more fit for service in the kingdom.

Most of us here in this room have been through a crucible. Christina — your recovery was a crucible experience. Do you think if the conditions hadn't been so severe, the costs so high, that you would have ever chosen to be clean for 19 — coming up on 20 — months?

I spent a little time in the fire myself. One year we went through a flood and lost pretty much everything we owned. Our home — everything. That same year we had a funeral seemingly every week. A little girl who had lived in our home for a time was murdered. My 9-year-old cousin was in a freak accident and died. My only biological grandfather died. My mother was plagued with illness. Financially things were never quite so tight as then. We shared a house with someone, which ratcheted up the stress levels.

Years later I was a night nurse for my grandmother as she passed. It was isolating and exhausting. The first few weeks I felt trapped — it was a terrible feeling. I desperately wanted to go home and sleep in my own bed, to sleep through the night, and to see my friends during the day. It was in some ways what I imagine claustrophobia would feel like. It took me a month to get someone to cover for me, and in a single night she almost died. She didn't get her medicine — and she had this thing about the baby monitor in her room. She felt like people being able to hear her sleep was creepy. But when she would wake up at 3am and need medicine, you couldn't hear her call for help without it. So in order for her to maintain her dignity and yet meet her need, I had hidden it in her room. The helper that came put the baby monitor on her nightstand, and she felt betrayed and spied upon. I made a show of taking it out of her room and put it back while she was napping.

But the point is that God lets us go through these crucibles for specific reasons. I think I spent more time, more frequently, in the Word while I was taking care of Grandma at night than I had to that point in my life. It was a time of growth for me personally and spiritually. It pushed me to my limit emotionally, and during the day when I was out and about I felt like a zombie. But God used it to work in me, to grow me, to refine me.

God lets hard things happen. God lets bad things happen to good people to produce better people. In the fire of the crucible God burns away imperfections in your life. Maybe it's in the words you say — lots of people curse and think nothing of it. Maybe it's addiction, or malice, or relationships where God is not kept at the center and he has to rip that away. Maybe he's teaching you to stand up for yourself, or that you think of yourself too much. So often our prayers are about wanting to feel good, or to be more comfortable. But God very frequently chooses to bless us in the midst of heartache and trial — and comfort is frequently a false friend.

I think we need to spend less time asking why, and instead ask where. Instead of "poor me — why, why, why is God letting me go through these trials?" Ask God to help you not miss the blessing. We should spend some time praising God that he loves us enough not to leave us where we are.

---

I love how the passage ends. Read the passage with me again.

God heard. God saw. And God knew.

A lot of people have said 2016 has been a tough year. We've certainly had our share of tragedy, and injury, and pain. But no matter what's going on, we can take this to the bank: God hears our cries, he sees our pain, and he knows what we're going through. He doesn't ask us to go through anything that he didn't ask his Son to go through. His Son was born into poverty, experienced shame, humiliation, betrayal. He experienced injustice at the hands of his own people and an occupying army. He was ultimately beaten, nailed to a cross, and hung there till he died.

God hears. God sees. God knows what you're going through — and he will be there for you in his timing, in his perfect way, in accordance with his perfect plan for our lives.

But it all starts with accepting Christ as your savior. Confessing that you're too weak on your own, and giving God the throne of your life. If you've never come to a point in your spiritual life where you've asked Jesus to pay the price for your sins, then tonight's the night. Don't let another night go by. We aren't promised tomorrow. Come talk to me.

Let's close in prayer.

## Thus Says the Lord — Sermon Evaluation

### 1. Verdict Title
**Pastoral Warmth Without Theological Spine: A Comfort Sermon That Stops Short of the Gospel**

---

### 2. One-Sentence Summary
A warm and personally vulnerable opening of Exodus 2:23-25 that named real suffering with genuine pastoral care, but traded theological precision for inspirational encouragement and reduced the gospel to a closing altar call appendage rather than the sermon's load-bearing center.

---

### 3. Berean Gate Result
**PASS (narrow)**

*Scopus Test:* The sermon's main point — that God allows suffering for his people's protection, strengthening, and ultimately their good — is derivable from Exodus 2:23-25, particularly the fourfold divine movement: God heard, God remembered, God saw, God knew. The main point is not contradicted by the passage.

*Authority Test:* The preacher largely submits to the text's claims, though the text's covenantal and redemptive-historical weight is substantially flattened into therapeutic categories. The sermon passes the gate but walks close to its edge — the passage itself is about covenant faithfulness, the groaning of a people under judgment, and God's sovereign remembrance; the sermon treats these as background for a general theology of personal suffering.

---

### 4. Fatal Flaw Flags
**Flag Triggered: No Clear Gospel Presentation (Flag #1)**

The gospel appears only in the final paragraph as a closing appeal ("it all starts with accepting Christ as your savior"). The finished work of Christ — penal substitution, justification, the atonement — is never proclaimed as the substance of the sermon. The reference to Christ's suffering in the conclusion functions as an analogy for human suffering (he went through it too), not as the ground of salvation. An attentive unbeliever could receive the sermon's comfort without understanding *why* Christ's cross is necessary.

**Score is capped at 69.**

*Note: Flag #4 (Generic Application) is borderline.* The personal illustrations are vivid and specific, but the application framework (God allows suffering to protect, strengthen, and refine you) remains general enough to survive in most Christian traditions and requires no specifically Reformed or gospel-grounded framework to receive.

---

### 5. Score Breakdown

**Category 1 — Exposition & Hermeneutics: 14/25**

- *Textual Fidelity (6/10):* The sermon's three-point structure (protection, strengthening, growth) is loosely anchored to the passage but is not derived exegetically from the text. Exodus 2:23-25 is primarily about God's covenant memory and sovereign initiation of redemption — the passage does not itself supply the three-point framework the preacher uses. The text is read twice but not opened.
- *Contextual Awareness (3/5):* The preacher does situate the passage within the Exodus narrative and connects it to the broader Pentateuch (Jacob's idolatry, the enemies in Canaan). This is a genuine strength. However, the redemptive-historical weight of the passage — as the pivotal hinge where God breaks his silence and moves toward the Exodus — is not communicated.
- *Exegetical Clarity (3/5):* The fourfold divine response (heard, remembered, saw, knew) is noted briefly at the end but not exegetically unpacked. "God remembered his covenant" — the most theologically loaded phrase in the passage — receives no treatment. What does it mean for God to "remember"? What covenant? On what grounds does God respond?
- *Logical Faithfulness (2/5):* The Fallen Condition Focus is present — human helplessness and dependence on God — but it is articulated in general terms (we can't wash off our own sin) rather than arising from careful exegesis of this specific text. There is mild eisegesis in the application of the chick illustration and the Marine Corps crucible to Israel's slavery.

**Category 2 — Theology & Doctrinal Integrity: 11/18**

- *Christ-Centeredness (4/7):* Christ is present in the conclusion but functions primarily as a sympathy figure — he suffered too — rather than as the one in whom the covenant God swore to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob finds its fulfillment. The connection between "God remembered his covenant" and the New Covenant inaugurated in Christ's blood is the organic Christological connection this text offers, and it is not taken.
- *Redemptive-Historical Awareness (1/3):* The sermon notes the Exodus as preparation for entering the promised land but does not place the passage within the broader story of redemption — the Exodus as typological of the greater Exodus accomplished by Christ (Luke 9:31). This is a significant missed opportunity given the text.
- *Doctrinal Precision (3/4):* There are no significant doctrinal errors. The brief statement about sin leaving "a crimson stain upon our soul" is evocative. However, the nature of sin and the basis of salvation are stated in vague terms ("confessing that you're too weak on your own, and giving God the throne of your life") that fall short of precise gospel articulation.
- *Confessional Alignment — 1689 Pulse (3/4):* No confessional contradictions are present. The sermon's providence theology is broadly compatible. However, the covenant of grace — directly invoked by the text's own language ("God remembered his covenant") — is not developed.

**Category 3 — Gospel Centrality & Force: 7/15**

- *Clarity of the Gospel (2/5):* The gospel is reduced to the closing appeal. Justification by faith is not articulated. "Accepting Christ as your savior" and "giving God the throne of your life" are common evangelical shorthand but do not constitute a clear proclamation of what Christ accomplished, why it is needed, or how one is united to him.
- *Necessity of Christ (3/5):* Christ is presented as necessary but primarily as an example (he suffered too) and as the object of personal commitment. His necessity as the covenant-fulfilling redeemer — the one to whom "God remembered his covenant" ultimately points — is not established.
- *Cross-Centered Gravity (2/5):* The sermon moves toward comfort, not toward the cross. The mention of Christ's death is brief and analogical. The cross is not presented as the ground of hope for those who are suffering; rather, suffering itself becomes the vehicle of growth, with Christ's cross appearing as a final addendum.

*Synagogue Test:* A devout Jew could receive most of this sermon without offense. The three-point theology of providential suffering — protection, strengthening, refinement — is compatible with Jewish theology. The closing appeal would exclude such a listener, but the bulk of the sermon's comfort is cross-free.

**Category 4 — Conscience & Applicatory Force: 9/15**

- *Heart Penetration (3/5):* The personal illustrations (the cousin with MS and cancer, the flood year, the grandmother's night care) give the sermon genuine pastoral warmth and establish real emotional contact with suffering hearers. This is the sermon's strongest practical feature.
- *Specificity (4/5):* The illustrations are notably specific and real. The named congregant (Christina) and the direct address about her recovery, the personal flood year, the grandmother with the baby monitor — these are specific. The application points, however, remain general after the illustrations.
- *Spiritual Differentiation (2/5):* The sermon largely addresses one category of hearer: the suffering believer who needs to be reminded that God is present. The unbeliever is addressed only at the close. The self-righteous, the hardened, the theologically comfortable, the doubter wrestling with theodicy — these are not directly addressed. The woman who asked "why?" in the introduction is never directly answered from the text.

**Category 5 — Exhortation & Response: 5/8**

- *Call to Response (3/4):* The closing invitation is clear: come to Christ, don't let another night go by. For the exhortation to unbelievers, this is adequate. For the believers who form most of the congregation, the exhortation is limited to "ask where, not why" and "praise God that he loves us enough not to leave us where we are." These are directional but lack the imperative weight of true exhortation.
- *Equipping for Holiness & Witness (2/4):* The sermon equips hearers to reframe suffering cognitively ("ask where, not why") but does not equip them for specific acts of holiness, witness, or corporate responsibility in suffering.

**Category 6 — Structure & Logical Flow: 6/9**

- *Clarity of Outline (3/4):* The three-point structure (protection, strengthening, growth/refinement) is clear and accessible. A listener could reproduce it.
- *Progression (2/3):* The sermon moves logically from problem (why does God allow suffering?) to answer (three reasons) to comfort (God hears, sees, knows) to invitation. However, the narrative background in paragraph 2 (about the wars in Canaan and Israel's growth in Egypt) is placed before the text is read, which creates an expository awkwardness.
- *Unity (1/2):* The sermon has two competing centers: the theodicy question ("why does God allow suffering?") and the comfort of divine omniscience ("God hears, sees, knows"). These are related but not the same. The fourfold divine response at the end deserves to be the sermon's organizational spine, not its closing paragraph.

**Category 7 — Illustration & Imagination: 4/5**

- *Relevance (2/2):* The illustrations are from the preacher's own congregation and life — a cancer diagnosis, an addiction recovery, a flood, a dying grandmother. These are as relevant as illustrations get.
- *Clarity (1/2):* The chick-in-the-egg illustration is creative but slightly over-extended. The Marine Corps crucible is effective. The grandmother story, while touching, goes long enough that the applicatory point is slightly blurred.
- *Memorability (1/1):* "God hears, God sees, God knows" will be remembered. The chick illustration and the grandmother story will stay with hearers.

**Category 8 — Sermonic Force: 3/5**

- *Reads as Preaching (1/2):* The sermon has oral markers and conversational warmth. The direct address to Christina by name is a genuine sermonic moment. However, large portions read as pastoral conversation rather than proclamation — there is insufficient imperative weight, insufficient textual assertion.
- *Conviction on the Page (1/2):* The sermon's most assertive moments are in the personal illustrations. Textual grounding density is low — the passage is cited twice but not driven home as the authority for the sermon's claims.
- *Cadence and Breath (1/1):* The sentence variation is natural, the closing ("God hears. God sees. God knows.") has genuine cadence and force.

### 8. Core Override Questions

1. **Does this sermon make me think more of Christ — or myself?**
Primarily myself and my suffering. Christ appears but does not dominate. The sermon's gravitational center is the hearer's experience of suffering, not the person and work of Christ.

2. **Did I encounter God, or just ideas about Him?**
Partially. The ending — "God hears. God sees. God knows." — carries genuine weight, and the personal illustrations create real emotional presence. But God remains somewhat distant as the sovereign arranger of suffering rather than the one who enters suffering in Christ.

3. **Was I comforted, convicted, or merely informed?**
Comforted, partially. The theodicy framework is informative. Conviction is largely absent. A comfortable believer with no current suffering would leave mostly informed.

---

### 9. Strengths (Top 3)

1. **Personal vulnerability that earns pastoral authority.** The preacher's own crucible stories — the flood year with multiple deaths, the night nursing of the dying grandmother — are not manipulative emotion but earned testimony. They give the sermon's comfort real weight. The detail of the hidden baby monitor is the kind of specific, human particularity that makes a sermon stay.

2. **Contextual historical awareness.** The opening background on the wars in Canaan, the enemies who destroyed each other, and the Israelites' growth to two million people is genuinely illuminating. It is more than most preachers provide for this passage and shows real preparation.

3. **The closing cadence: "God hears. God sees. God knows."** This is the sermon's strongest moment. It has the compression and weight of genuine proclamation. If the entire sermon were built toward this landing, it would be a powerful conclusion.

---

### 10. Critical Weaknesses (Top 3)

1. **The gospel is appended rather than proclaimed.** The final paragraph contains the sermon's only clear gospel content, and even there the content is thin ("confessing that you're too weak on your own, giving God the throne of your life"). The passage itself — "God remembered his covenant" — is a direct opening to covenantal gospel proclamation: the same God who swore to Abraham now fulfills that oath in Christ. That connection is never made. The cross must be the answer to suffering, not merely an analogy for it.

2. **The text's own theological center — covenant remembrance — is untouched.** The phrase "God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob" is the most theologically dense phrase in the passage. It is cited but never opened. What does divine remembrance mean? (It is not that God forgot; it is that the time of covenantal action has arrived.) What covenant? Why these three names? This phrase is the hinge on which the entire Exodus swings, and the sermon passes over it to get to three general points about suffering.

3. **The theodicy question raised in the introduction is never answered from the text.** The sermon opens with the cousin's anguished "why?" — and the text itself does not primarily answer the "why" of suffering. Exodus 2:23-25 answers a different question: "Has God seen? Does God care? Will God act?" The sermon's framework promises to answer "why" but delivers "what God uses it for," which is not the same thing. The mismatch between the opening question and the text's actual answer is never acknowledged.

---

### 11. Blind Spots

The preacher appears not to see that the theology of suffering being preached — God allows suffering to protect, strengthen, and refine us — is functionally compatible with Job's three friends. The friends were wrong not because their principle was false (God does allow suffering) but because they applied it mechanically and missed the larger covenantal drama. A sermon on suffering from Exodus 2 has access to something Job's friends did not: the covenant-remembering God who answers groaning not with explanation but with action — the Exodus, and ultimately the cross. That larger answer is not present here.

The preacher also appears unaware that "give God the throne of your life" as a gospel formula subtly centers the hearer's decision rather than God's covenantal initiative — which is precisely what this passage is about: God acting first, before Israel even knows to expect it.

---

### 12. Illusion of Strength

The personal illustrations feel like the sermon's strongest sections, and in terms of pastoral connection they are. But they create an illusion that the sermon has arrived somewhere exegetically when it has not. After each illustration, the listener feels the truth has been established — but the truth has been illustrated before it has been derived from the text. The illustrations are doing theological work the exposition never did, which means they are carrying weight the structure cannot support. A listener who found the illustrations moving might not notice that the text itself was never opened.

---

### 13. Missed Opportunity

**"God remembered his covenant."** This is the sermon. Not the three supporting reasons for suffering, but the staggering claim that when a people groaned in slavery, the eternal God acted on the basis of a promise made to men who had been dead for 400 years — because his covenant does not expire. That is the comfort this text offers. And it points directly and organically to the New Covenant — the covenant of which Christ said "this cup is the new covenant in my blood, shed for you." The people groaned; God remembered; God sent a redeemer. The church groans; God remembers; God has already sent his Son. The arc from Exodus 2 to the Lord's Supper is not an artificial leap — it is the passage's own trajectory. The sermon never takes it.

---

### 14. Surgical Fixes (3–5)

1. **Rebuild the sermon around "God remembered his covenant" as the central claim.** The three-point structure (protection, strengthening, refinement) can remain as subordinate supporting observations, but the load-bearing thesis should be: *When his people cry, the covenant-keeping God hears, remembers, and moves — and the greatest proof is the cross.* This immediately integrates the gospel as the sermon's center rather than its appendix.

2. **Open "God remembered his covenant" exegetically before the illustrations.** Spend two minutes on what divine "remembrance" means in the Hebrew idiom — not retrieval of forgotten information, but covenantal initiation of promised action. Name the Abrahamic covenant. This gives the illustrations something to illustrate rather than carrying the theological weight themselves.

3. **Answer the introduction's question with the text's actual answer.** The cousin asked "why?" — but this text doesn't answer "why." It answers "who": the God who sees, hears, and knows. Acknowledge this explicitly: "I don't think this passage actually answers the 'why' question — but it answers something better: who is there when you are in the fire?" This is more honest, more pastoral, and more text-governed.

4. **Integrate the gospel into the body of the sermon, not only the close.** When making the case that God acted on behalf of helpless people who could not save themselves, draw the explicit gospel parallel: Christ did not merely suffer alongside us; he suffered *for* us, bearing the wrath that our sin deserves, so that God's covenant promise of rescue would be fully kept. This can be done in two sentences and transforms the theological center of the sermon.

5. **Add direct exhortation to the suffering believer in the body.** The sermon addresses the unbeliever in the close and the general congregation through illustrations, but the person most clearly in view — the one in the fire right now — is never directly exhorted. Consider a direct address: "To the one in the crucible tonight: your suffering does not mean God has forgotten you. It may mean the opposite. God remembered his covenant — and he has not forgotten yours."

---

### 15. Dead Orthodoxy Flag
**Not triggered.** This sermon does not have the profile of cold orthodoxy — it has pastoral warmth, personal vulnerability, and genuine congregational care. The weakness is in the opposite direction: pastoral warmth that has not been fully disciplined by theological precision and textual grounding. The fire is present; the word needs to sharpen it.

---

### 16. Weight of Glory Statement
**+3/5** — The closing triad ("God hears. God sees. God knows.") carries genuine eternal weight, and the personal illustrations give the sermon's comfort the texture of real life; but the sermon does not yet achieve the gravity that arises when the full covenantal height of the passage — a God who remembers a 400-year-old promise in the groaning of slaves — is proclaimed in its proper connection to the cross.

---

### 17. Rubric Fit Note
This is a narrative/historical passage serving as the hinge of a redemptive-historical sequence. The rubric applies well with one calibration: the Exposition category must account for the fact that narrative passages communicate theology through plot and divine action rather than propositional argument. The sermon's exegetical weakness is not that it failed to parse propositions, but that it failed to read the narrative's own climax — the four divine verbs in v.24-25 — as the text's theological center. The rubric's Redemptive-Historical Awareness subcategory is especially load-bearing for this genre and was appropriately weighted in the assessment.
