# The Crucible
## Exodus 14

---

We talked a couple of weeks ago about how different contexts are important. Today's passage in Exodus 14 — if you've brought your Bibles, and I hope you have; if not, open to page _____ in your pew Bibles — gains new depths when you consider the geographic, historic, and archaeological contexts.

As you may already know, Exodus 14 is the story of the crossing of the Red Sea — this is after the plagues in Egypt. You remember that story; I think everyone has seen Charlton Heston's *Moses* [*The Ten Commandments*, 1956]. Hopefully you all read this story recently too. Did you make it through your weekly reading? I hope so. If not, there is always next week — it's hard to establish new habits.

So where we are in our story today is right after the tenth plague. Every Egyptian was grieving and mourning the loss of their firstborn. In their grief they sent the Israelites off with lavish gifts. It was worth any cost to get these people — whose God was so evidently angry with Egypt — out of their city. So they loaded the Israelites up with everything they could carry and sent them off.

But as we will read, something happens. Scripture says God hardens the heart of Egypt. When we read our passage, please make note of that in your Bible. There are entire denominations founded around the idea that man's will is sovereign over God. Yet time after time after time in this story we see God hardening the heart of a man, or of men. If God has the power to harden Pharaoh's heart, does he not have the power to soften people's hearts? To draw people to himself? Absolutely — and praise God for it. No one is ever too far gone for God to reach and woo back to himself.

So when you hear people say God cannot change the heart of man, or that God always respects man's free will, you can say: "Yeah, that's true — but that doesn't keep him from acting so that he can accomplish his will. Look with me at Exodus 14."

---

*[Read: Exodus 14:1–10]*

---

The first thing I want you to notice is the emphasis on location. In verse one they mention several landmarks and even what direction they were supposed to be facing. That caught my eye, and you know me — that triggered some research.

I found some navigational charts for the Red Sea and found where others before me had figured out where the Israelites would have crossed. It turns out there is a place — forgive me for butchering Arabic — called Nuweiba. If you look on maps from more than a hundred years or so ago, you find that it was called "Nuweiba al Muzayyinah" — and some have translated that as "waters of Moses opening," or the place where Moses crossed.

There are a lot of things whose real location has been changed or obscured over the years — not only geographical markers, but dates. Look at Christmas: Jesus was probably born in the spring as far as we can tell. Over the years the church co-opted the date of Christ's birth to displace a pagan holiday. I don't think Christ minds — it's more important that we celebrate Christ than that we get the day exactly right. But this kind of thing has happened with historic locations as well.

So the name alone wasn't enough to convince me. I did some more digging and found that there is an obelisk on the Egyptian side of the crossing site that has been defaced — it had writing on it, but little remains. Across the sea there is reportedly another pillar that still has writing. According to some researchers, it commemorates the crossing of Moses and was placed by Solomon to honor God and the miracle he performed at the crossing, and the destruction of Pharaoh's armies. Little wonder the one on the opposite shore was defaced.

Next I turned to navigation charts of the area — the Gulf of Aqaba. Just a little bit north and south, the sea drops off very steeply. Where the Israelites likely crossed is about 90 to 100 feet deep at its deepest. To the north and south of this place, the sea drops to just under 2,000 meters — just short of 6,000 feet deep.

I don't know about you, but whenever I pictured this crossing I had the Bible storybook image of people walking between walls of water that came to their shoulders. Inconvenient, but not dangerous. But no — they would have been surrounded by walls of water up to a hundred feet high. And not only that: the crossing at that point is around ten miles wide, by my best estimate using Google Maps. And they have found enormous quantities of broken chariots. They found the bones of men and horses, all of which have been preserved and encrusted in coral.

By contrast, they really haven't found anything remotely like this anywhere else along the sea. Precious few locations are that shallow; none of them are littered with archaeological evidence; none of them bear a name like "where Moses crossed"; and none of them were recognized by the ancient Israelites as the actual site. It's pretty clear this is *the* spot where it happened. Isn't that something? Science confirming events recorded in Scripture.

See the difference geographic context can make? You go from picturing two million people in chest-deep water crossing a short stretch of water, to two million people crossing a supernaturally made channel with walls of water towering a hundred feet on either side over a ten-mile crossing. That's significant, isn't it? A lot of the time when you reach something that feels difficult or dry, that's just because you need to go deeper.

---

The next thing I noticed was in verses 6 and 7: *all* his army, and 600 choice chariots. What we know of Egyptian military design is that there were at least two types of chariots. The standard, mass-produced kind — each wheel had four spokes. Then there was the gold-veneered type with six-spoke wheels. The six spokes represented an advance in wheel construction: they could handle rougher terrain with less risk of damage, and they also gave more surface area for gold inlays.

Historians estimate there were tens of thousands of regular chariots, with 600 of the choice chariots — likely commanders' vehicles. There were almost certainly mercenaries as well. The Egyptians of this period were known to hire Greeks, Phoenicians, and others. This would have been toward the end of Greece's prominence — just past their prime. You remember the line about Helen of Troy — "the face that launched a thousand ships" — that's from Christopher Marlowe, but the Greeks may well have had something close to that number in their fleet. The Spartans were famously militaristic: their whole way of life celebrated the warrior. More than likely they were a terrifying force on foot while the Egyptians operated from chariots and horseback.

At this point in history Egypt was the equivalent of the world's superpower. Could you imagine being unarmed, heavily laden, carrying your children and your belongings — and then having the full might of that army deployed against you? It would be a terrifying thing.

Egyptian chariot crews typically had at least two men — a driver and a warrior — and there were runners alongside on foot. Tens of thousands of chariots, each with trained soldiers. That is an overwhelming force.

Even with two million Israelites, the Egyptians and their mercenaries would have slaughtered them. The casualties would have been in the hundreds of thousands, if not over a million. These people were strong from a lifetime of hard labor — but they were not soldiers. They were not armed. They were carrying their children and their belongings. Apart from the direct intervention of God, they would have been massacred.

The Israelites knew exactly how vulnerable they were. They knew that short of God's divine intervention, all was lost.

---

I think that was precisely the point God needed to bring them to — in order to deepen their faith. Look with me at verses 11–13:

> "They said to Moses, 'Is it because there are no graves in Egypt that you have taken us away to die in the wilderness? What have you done to us in bringing us out of Egypt? Is not this what we said to you in Egypt: "Leave us alone that we may serve the Egyptians"? For it would have been better for us to serve the Egyptians than to die in the wilderness.' And Moses said to the people, 'Fear not, stand firm, and see the salvation of the LORD, which he will work for you today. For the Egyptians whom you see today, you shall never see again. The LORD will fight for you, and you have only to be silent.'"
> — Exodus 14:11–13, ESV

Have you ever been at a point in your life where you asked God *why*? Everything seemed to be going the wrong direction. Maybe your enemies are encamped around you. Maybe you have an unstoppable force on one side and an uncrossable sea on the other.

I forget who said it, but someone once said: sometimes you're the hammer, sometimes you're the anvil, and sometimes you're the iron. The raw lump of metal being tossed into the crucible — heated and scraped, hammered and shaped — being forged from a useless lump into a useful tool.

Like any of us, the Israelites were a stubborn and forgetful people. They had to learn this lesson over and over and over. I like to think I'd do better, but for almost all of us — that wouldn't be true. Do we not have to relearn the same lessons sometimes, too? Especially the lesson of trust in God, of faith in God, of dependence upon God.

We — especially we Americans — are an independent lot. We have this idea that *we* can pull *ourselves* up by *our* bootstraps. That we are sufficient for anything we set our minds to. We think we know better, work harder, lead better, come up with better solutions than anyone else. The problem is we are idiots compared to God. We can barely see past the end of our face, and yet God sees everything — past, present, and future. His higher perspective means he *knows* what's coming, where we only *think* we know.

The Israelites had to have this lesson driven home. And sometimes we do too.

When your problems surround you — when everything looks hopeless — frequently that is when God chooses to move.

All the Israelites could see was death behind them and death in front.

But look at what God was doing:

---

*[Read: Exodus 14:15–20]*

---

Geography plays a role here again. If you look at the place God sent them on a map, you will see that there is only one way onto that beach — through a narrow canyon. It's wide enough for a two-lane road, but it is a definite pinch point. It was the perfect spot. When God shaped that place, he knew what would come centuries later. He made it easily defensible and positioned his people where they could cross. With the canyon being the only way in, it was a simple thing for God to place a single pillar of fire and smoke in that one spot — blinding and terrifying the Egyptians into not entering.

Could you imagine what it would have been like to be an Egyptian soldier at that moment? Right before this there had been the plagues — frogs, boils, insects, a river of blood. All ten plagues, culminating in the death of every firstborn that wasn't covered by the blood of the Passover lamb. Then, right after all that, you start chasing this large group of civilians to slaughter them — and suddenly a pillar of fire appears, blocking your path. Their hair must have been standing on end. There may have been considerable convincing required to get them to follow Pharaoh any further. And yet — we don't hear of any staying behind.

What we read is:

---

*[Read: Exodus 14:21–31]*

---

Total and complete victory over the mighty army of Egypt.

Verse 31 is the key:

> "Israel saw the great power that the LORD used against the Egyptians, so the people feared the LORD, and they believed in the LORD and in his servant Moses."
> — Exodus 14:31, ESV

God allowed all of this to happen so that they would believe in him more deeply. So they would trust him more fully. So they would rely upon him — rather than themselves — when things get hard.

God brought his people into the crucible and made them stare at what looked like certain doom. He made them face the worst thing they could possibly imagine — and brought them through it so that he and they could have a deeper relationship.

Sometimes people have to truly reach bottom before they are willing to put their entire will in God's hands.

---

I once read a story about a woman who was born into a troubled family. Her father was largely absent. She developed an eating disorder, convinced she wasn't pretty enough or thin enough for her father to love her. She started trying to find love anywhere she could — in the arms of men, anywhere. Finally, at eighteen, she asked her mother to send her somewhere she could get help with her eating disorder.

She got counseling, and like so many young people who need counseling, decided she should become a counselor. She went to college, then to grad school, struggling on and off with her eating disorder all the while. She graduated, got a decent job at a hospital, and had her own private practice besides.

Two years later she was at work when the police showed up at her door and told her that one of her patients had committed suicide. She was shattered. A week after the funeral, she received notice that she was being sued — the family blamed her for her patient's death. The police collected all of her records. It took four years to get through the trial, and at the end of it all, the court found her negligent. She was devastated. She threw herself back into her eating disorder with a vengeance — punishing herself. She became increasingly depressed. She lost her job, her practice, her license. She found the bottom of the bottle almost every night. Then her husband left.

Finally it all came to a head. She bought rat poison. She had decided to take her life, and she had stayed sober that night — if she was going to do it, she wanted to be clear-headed.

That night, out of the blue, one of her friends dropped by. They had a short conversation, and at the end of it her friend gave her a devotional with a gospel presentation in it. She figured reading couldn't hurt. She opened it, read a clear presentation of the gospel, and ended up weeping on her knees on the floor, praying for Christ to save her.

The next day she joined Alcoholics Anonymous, and has been sober for something like twenty years. She said through the whole process her eating disorder simply melted away. She developed relationships that hold her accountable in the areas where she's weak.

But don't miss this: it wasn't until she entered the crucible — until she was stuck in the fires of the forge — that she reached the point where she was willing to have a relationship with God.

---

As believers, sometimes we still have to go into the crucible and into the fire. But it's because God is refining us. He's growing us. He's deepening our capacity to know him, to trust him, to depend upon him.

And just as with Moses and the Israelites — God had the plan before he ever allowed them to enter the situation. He had planned the battlefield, shaped the canyon, shaped the sea floor. He had orchestrated their release from Egypt and everything else we read tonight — all of it — to woo them back to himself.

I read a piece once by a woman who said the reason she loves rollercoasters is the thrill of *feeling* like things are out of control while knowing she is actually safe. That strikes me as exactly right for the believer in the crucible. We may feel like everything is out of control. And in one sense, it is — it's out of *our* control. But we are safe, because all things are in God's control.

---

## Preacher's Notes

*These notes are for preparation and verification — not for reading aloud.*

**On "Nuweiba al Muzayyinah" and its translation (line: "some have translated that as 'waters of Moses opening'"):**
This translation is associated primarily with the alternative archaeological research of Ron Wyatt (1978) and those who follow his work. Mainstream scholars attribute "al Muzayyinah" to the Muzayna Bedouin tribe of southern Sinai — meaning the full name is more accurately rendered "springs of the Muzayna [tribe]," with no direct linguistic connection to Moses. The "waters of Moses" translation is a minority and disputed reading. The body text uses "some have translated" deliberately. Do not preach this as established etymology; preach it as a tradition that some find compelling. The geographic features that follow (the shallow underwater ridge, the canyon, the depth of the Gulf of Aqaba) are real and independently verifiable.

**On the commemorative pillars attributed to Solomon:**
This claim is also from Ron Wyatt's research. Wyatt reported finding a pillar on the Egyptian side (1978) with inscriptions worn away, and a matching pillar on the Saudi side with Proto-Hebrew inscriptions including the words *Mitzraim* (Egypt), *Shlomo* (Solomon), *Moshe* (Moses), and *Yahweh*. The Saudi pillar has never been independently verified — Wyatt reported it was removed before photographs could be taken. No peer-reviewed archaeological confirmation exists for either pillar as described. The body text uses "reportedly" and "according to some researchers" deliberately. Preach this as an intriguing but unverified tradition, not as established archaeology.

**On Ron Wyatt generally:**
Wyatt was an amateur archaeologist whose claims (Red Sea chariot wheels, Noah's Ark, the Ark of the Covenant) were widely disputed by professional archaeologists. His findings were not peer-reviewed and lacked verifiable chains of custody. His work is popular in some Christian circles and has been repeated extensively online. Use it with appropriate humility. The argument of this sermon does not depend on Wyatt being right — the sovereignty of God and the geographical improbability of the crossing stand on their own.

**On the chariot remains and coral-encrusted bones:**
Wyatt also claimed to have found chariot wheels and fossilized human and horse bones encrusted in coral at the Nuweiba crossing site. These claims are disputed. Some photographs he published have been challenged as misidentified coral formations. The body text uses the language of the original sermon ("they have found enormous quantities...") — if this claim cannot be verified to your satisfaction, consider softening it further to "some researchers report finding..."

**On the chariot crew numbers:**
The standard Egyptian chariot of this period typically carried two men — a driver (*auriya*) and a warrior. The three-man crew design (driver, archer, runner with shield) was characteristic of Hittite chariots, not Egyptian. An earlier version of this sermon cited the Reformation Study Bible for a three-man Egyptian crew; that claim could not be verified and has been removed. The body now reflects historical consensus: two men per chariot, with infantry runners alongside.

**On the total chariot count:**
The figure of "around 20,000 regular chariots" appeared in an earlier draft. Ancient Egyptian military records do not give us a precise number for chariots at the time of the Exodus, and there is no consensus scholarly estimate. The body now uses "tens of thousands" to reflect honest uncertainty. Do not cite a specific number from the pulpit unless you can verify the source.

**On the Marlowe quotation:**
"The face that launched a thousand ships" is from Christopher Marlowe, *Doctor Faustus*, Act V, Scene 1 (written c. 1592, first published 1604): "Was this the face that launch'd a thousand ships / And burnt the topless towers of Ilium?" The quotation is accurately attributed.
